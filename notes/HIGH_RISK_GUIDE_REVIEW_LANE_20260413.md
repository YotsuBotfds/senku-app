# High-Risk Guide Review Lane - 2026-04-13

## Purpose

Some remaining guide gaps are real, but they are too risky for bulk drafting.

This note is the review gate for:
- suicide prevention / crisis intervention
- domestic violence / intimate partner violence response
- sexual assault / survivor-support response

Use this note before anyone drafts, expands, or "quickly fills" those topics.

## Current Rule

Do not treat these as normal guide-direction work.

For these topics:
- do not draft in a broad wave with unrelated practical guides
- do not rely only on adjacent repo content
- do not improvise legal, forensic, or mental-health crisis instructions
- do not frame community punishment or confrontation as a simple checklist

These must stay in a dedicated review lane until source-backed, conservative drafts are ready.

## Required Source Classes

### Suicide Prevention / Crisis Intervention

Use official or primary public-health sources first:
- CDC suicide prevention pages
- 988 / SAMHSA crisis-support guidance

Why:
- warning signs, crisis response, and escalation language must be current and careful
- the guide should emphasize immediate connection to crisis help where available, reduction of immediate danger, and not leaving a high-risk person isolated

Current official anchors checked:
- CDC `Risk and Protective Factors for Suicide`
- CDC `Preventing Suicide`
- CDC `About Suicide Prevention`

### Domestic Violence / Intimate Partner Violence / Sexual Assault

Use trauma-informed public-health or survivor-support sources first:
- WHO violence-against-women / survivor-support guidance
- RAINN survivor-support resources

Why:
- survivor safety, privacy, support, referral, and anti-retaliation framing matter more than amateur incident-management theory
- evidence preservation and medical follow-up language must stay conservative

Current official anchors checked:
- WHO `Violence against women`
- WHO `Health care for women subjected to intimate partner violence or sexual violence: a clinical handbook`
- RAINN support and hotline/service pages

## Drafting Boundaries

### For Suicide Prevention

Safe scope for a future guide:
- warning signs
- immediate safety steps
- staying with the person or arranging trusted supervision
- removing or separating from obvious lethal means when feasible
- contacting crisis support or emergency help where available
- community follow-up and watchfulness

Avoid or review very carefully:
- pseudo-therapy scripts presented as sufficient care
- detailed method language
- promises that community support alone is enough
- "one conversation fixes it" framing

### For Domestic Violence / Sexual Assault

Safe scope for a future guide:
- immediate safety and separation from ongoing harm
- survivor-centered support
- privacy, consent, and choice where possible
- medical follow-up / urgent health concerns
- conservative evidence-preservation guidance
- community anti-retaliation and anti-harassment boundaries
- referral to trained support where available

Avoid or review very carefully:
- vigilante or punishment-first checklists
- coercive interrogation framing
- overconfident forensic instructions
- advice that pressures disclosure or reporting
- language that shifts burden onto the survivor

## Minimum Review Checklist Before Drafting

1. collect current official sources first
2. define the guide scope narrowly
3. write a conservative draft
4. do a second-pass safety review focused on escalation wording, survivor autonomy, and emergency boundaries
5. keep the first published version shorter and more conservative than a normal practical guide
