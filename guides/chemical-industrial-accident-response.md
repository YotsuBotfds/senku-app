## Start Here: industrial incidents and public exposure

This guide is for response, not identification. Use it when the problem is a facility leak, transportation release, pipeline failure, explosion, or multi-person exposure and you need evacuation, sheltering, decontamination, or triage.

If you are dealing with a household cleaner, an unknown powder or liquid in a home or garage, or a question about whether two products can be mixed, start with [`chemical-safety.md`](chemical-safety.md) for prevention and compatibility. ## Understanding Industrial Chemical Hazards

![Chemical hazard zones and decontamination procedures](../assets/svgs/chemical-industrial-accident-1.svg)

Chemical accidents occur through transportation accidents, facility explosions or leaks, or pipeline failures. Understanding the hazards and how chemicals behave allows you to respond effectively and avoid exposure.

### Types of Industrial Chemicals and Hazards

**Corrosive chemicals** (acids, bases) cause chemical burns on contact with skin or eyes and damage respiratory tissue if inhaled. Common corrosives include sulfuric acid (batteries, metal processing), hydrochloric acid (metal pickling, cleaning), and ammonia (refrigeration, fertilizer).

**Toxic gases** cause respiratory and systemic poisoning. Common toxic gases include:
- **Chlorine:** Heavier than air, accumulates in low areas. Causes respiratory damage, burns airways.
- **Ammonia:** Lighter than air, spreads upward. Causes respiratory irritation and eye damage.
- **Hydrogen sulfide:** Heavier than air, accumulates in low spots. Smells like rotten eggs at low concentrations, numbs smell at high concentrations (dangerous—you can't smell it when exposure is worst).
- **Sulfur dioxide:** Acidic gas causing respiratory irritation.
- **Phosgene:** Odorless, delayed symptoms (may feel fine for 24 hours then acute respiratory failure). Historically a chemical weapon.

**Volatile organic compounds (VOCs)** (benzene, toluene, etc.) cause acute neurological symptoms (dizziness, disorientation, headache) and long-term organ damage with chronic exposure.

**Particulates** (dust, fumes) include asbestos, fine metal oxides, and silica dust. These cause lung disease through chronic inhalation.

**Reactive chemicals** (oxidizers, fuel sources) can spontaneously ignite or react violently with water or other chemicals. Phosphorus compounds, peroxides, and alkali metals are highly reactive.

### Exposure Pathways

Chemical exposure occurs through:

1. **Inhalation:** Breathing air containing gaseous or particulate chemicals. This is the fastest exposure route and most common in accidents.
2. **Skin contact:** Direct exposure to liquid chemicals or chemical dust. Absorption rate depends on chemical and skin condition.
3. **Eye contact:** Burns and irritation from liquid or particulate chemicals.
4. **Ingestion:** Swallowing contaminated food, water, or dust. Less common in acute accidents but important in chronic contamination.

## Recognizing Chemical Exposure

Acute chemical exposure symptoms appear within seconds to hours depending on chemical type and concentration.

### Inhalation Injury Symptoms

- **Respiratory irritation:** Coughing, throat burning, shortness of breath.
- **Pulmonary edema** (fluid in lungs): Wheezing, pink or bloody sputum, severe shortness of breath.
- **Systemic poisoning:** Disorientation, confusion, loss of consciousness (from gases like hydrogen sulfide or carbon monoxide).
- **Delayed symptoms:** Some chemicals (like phosgene) cause minimal symptoms initially, then severe respiratory failure 12–24 hours later.

### Skin and Eye Exposure

- **Chemical burns:** Pain, redness, blistering, tissue destruction.
- **Eye irritation:** Tearing, pain, redness, vision loss.
- **Absorption symptoms:** Systemic effects if chemicals penetrate through skin (organophosphate pesticides, hydrofluoric acid).

### Specific Chemical Symptom Patterns

**Chlorine exposure:** Coughing, throat burning, wheezing, eye irritation (typically within seconds of exposure).

**Ammonia exposure:** Eye and respiratory irritation, skin burns if concentrated, strong pungent odor.

**Hydrogen sulfide:** Initial rotten egg smell, then rapid loss of smell sense (dangerous—high exposure is undetectable), respiratory irritation, loss of consciousness.

**Organophosphate pesticides:** Excessive salivation and sweating, muscle twitching, pinpoint pupils, muscle paralysis, respiratory failure.

**Hydrofluoric acid:** Burns are deceptively painless initially but cause deep tissue damage and cardiac arrhythmias from fluoride ion absorption.

:::warning
**Delayed Symptoms Are Dangerous.** Chemicals like phosgene, some VOCs, and strong oxidizers cause minimal acute symptoms but severe delayed effects 12–24 hours after exposure. Do not assume lack of acute symptoms means you're safe—seek evaluation if you were in a chemical accident.
:::

## Shelter-in-Place Procedures

If chemical contamination is detected but evacuation is not immediately possible or safe, sheltering in place (remaining inside a sealed building) provides protection.

### Shelter-in-Place Triggers

- **Chemical release is detected near your location.**
- **Evacuation routes are blocked or impassable.**
- **You are unable to evacuate** (disabled, caring for vulnerable persons, or evacuation zones are unclear).
- **Outdoor conditions are worse than indoors** (e.g., heavily contaminated wind is sweeping the area).

### Sealing Rooms

The goal is to seal the room to prevent outside air from entering:

1. **Close all windows and doors.**
2. **Turn off HVAC, air conditioning, or fans** that bring outside air into the building. Leave only interior recirculation running if your system has that capability.
3. **Seal gaps around doors and windows** using duct tape, plastic sheeting, or towels stuffed in gaps.
4. **Seal electrical outlets, switches, and other wall penetrations** with duct tape and plastic.
5. **Seal around pipes and vents** that penetrate walls.
6. **Tape plastic sheeting over windows** if you're concerned about broken glass or airborne particles.

### Shelter Location

- **Choose a room on the side of the building away from the wind direction** of contamination.
- **Select interior rooms on upper floors** if the building has multiple floors (toxic gases are heavier and denser than air; upper floors are safer).
- **Avoid basements** in cases of heavier-than-air gas exposure (chlorine, hydrogen sulfide accumulate in low areas).
- **Select a room with plumbing** so you have access to water for decontamination if needed.

### Duration

Most chemical clouds disperse within 30 minutes to a few hours depending on wind conditions and chemical volatility. Monitor emergency broadcasts or weather alerts for "all-clear" signals. If authorities don't provide updates, wait at least 4–6 hours after you last noticed chemical smell or visibility.

:::tip
**Pre-Made Shelter-in-Place Kits.** Assemble kits in advance containing duct tape, plastic sheeting, and masking tape. During emergencies, you won't have time to search for supplies. Store kits accessibly in your home and workplace.
:::

## Evacuation Zone Estimation

When chemical release happens, you need to estimate the hazardous zone and evacuate accordingly.

### Wind Direction and Downwind Zone

The primary hazard zone extends downwind from the release point. Chemicals distribute into the air and flow with prevailing wind.

- **Determine wind direction.** Watch smoke, clouds, or trees to see which way wind is blowing.
- **Position yourself upwind.** Move perpendicular to the wind, then upwind of the release point if possible.
- **Estimate distance.** Distance depends on chemical type and amount. Rough estimates: most volatile chemicals affect areas within 1–3 miles downwind; heavier gases affect areas 0.5–2 miles downwind; lighter-than-air gases disperse more quickly upward.

### Terrain Effects

- **Valleys concentrate chemicals.** Chemicals flowing downhill collect in valleys. Move to higher ground.
- **Water bodies can trap chemicals.** Some chemicals are denser than air and sink, pooling near water bodies. Move away from water if possible.
- **Buildings upwind provide shelter** from the initial chemical release, but are downwind of the release source and may become contaminated afterward.

### Safe Distance

In most industrial chemical accidents, evacuation to 3+ miles from the release point is adequate for safety. Chemical clouds dilute as they move downwind; concentrations that cause acute symptoms are typically limited to closer distances.

However, **evacuate immediately if:**
- A facility is burning or exploding.
- You see visible chemical clouds or smell strong chemical odor.
- Emergency sirens or alerts are activated.
- You experience any acute symptoms (respiratory irritation, eye burning, dizziness).

## Decontamination Procedures

Decontamination removes chemical contamination from people, equipment, and buildings. Proper decontamination is critical for preventing ongoing exposure and absorption.

### Dry Decontamination

Dry decontamination (removal of contaminated clothing and items without water) is the first step.

1. **Remove all outer clothing** that may be contaminated. Place it in a plastic bag or outside area.
2. **Remove shoes and socks.** Place them in contamination bag.
3. **Wipe exposed skin** with clean towels, dry cloth, or leaves to remove particulate chemicals.
4. **Avoid touching the face, eyes, or mouth** while contaminated.

Dry decontamination removes particulates and some liquid chemical residues without spreading contamination through water (which some chemicals require).

### Wet Decontamination

Wet decontamination (washing with water) removes many chemical residues but can activate certain reactive chemicals.

1. **Remove additional clothing** beyond what was removed in dry decontamination.
2. **Rinse with copious water.** Use a shower, hose, or faucet. Water should be lukewarm to cool, not hot (hot water increases skin absorption).
3. **Wash thoroughly** skin, hair, under fingernails, and between toes.
4. **Do not rub vigorously.** Gentle washing is sufficient. Aggressive rubbing can increase skin absorption.
5. **Rinse for at least 5–15 minutes** depending on chemical type. Longer rinsing is safer.
6. **If eyes are contaminated**, rinse eyes for at least 15 minutes with gentle stream of water or saline solution.

### Chemical-Specific Decontamination Modifications

**Alkali metals** (sodium, potassium) react violently with water. Do NOT use water for decontamination of these chemicals. Remove clothing and brush off residues, then soak in mineral oil or kerosene if available, or leave for professional decontamination.

**Phosphorus compounds** may spontaneously combust if wet. Remove clothing immediately and do NOT apply water. Immerse in water only if the material is burning (paradoxically, water prevents re-ignition when submerged).

**Hydrofluoric acid** is highly toxic through skin absorption. Immediate decontamination and medical treatment is critical.

**Organophosphate pesticides** inhibit acetylcholinesterase and cause systemic poisoning. Rapid decontamination and atropine treatment are essential.

## Triage and First Aid During Chemical Exposure

In mass casualty incidents, triage categorizes patients by severity.

### Triage Categories

- **Immediate (Red):** Severe symptoms, altered consciousness, difficulty breathing, chemical burns. These patients need medical attention right now.
- **Delayed (Yellow):** Moderate symptoms, stable vital signs, no respiratory compromise. These patients can wait 1–4 hours for treatment.
- **Minor (Green):** Mild or no symptoms, walking and alert. These patients can care for themselves.
- **Expectant (Black):** Unsurvivable injuries or no vital signs. In resource-limited scenarios, focus resources on survivors.

Assign colors using colored tape, spray paint, or marker on skin or clothing.

### Chemical Burn Treatment

- **Dry decontamination first** to remove dry chemical residues.
- **Rinse thoroughly** with water for 5–15 minutes.
- **Do not apply ice or ice water** (increases tissue damage through vasoconstriction).
- **Cover burns with clean dry dressing** after rinsing.
- **Elevate burned areas** to reduce swelling.
- **Pain control:** Ibuprofen or other pain relievers, but do not apply topical anesthetics (they can increase absorption).
- **Seek medical attention** for burns larger than a few square inches or deep burns.

### Inhalation Injury Management

- **Move to fresh air immediately** if breathing is difficult or you experienced chemical inhalation.
- **Sit upright** to ease breathing.
- **High-flow oxygen** if available (critical for CO poisoning, significant inhalation injury).
- **Do not lie flat** (worsens respiratory symptoms).
- **Seek medical attention** for any respiratory symptoms.

## Improvised PPE (Personal Protective Equipment)

When commercial PPE is unavailable, improvised protection reduces exposure.

### Respiratory Protection

**N95 or surgical masks** provide limited protection against particulates and some chemical mists but do not protect against vapors.

**Improvised respirator:**
- Layers of cotton fabric (cloth mask, bandana, multiple layers of cheesecloth).
- Tape multiple layers over the nose and mouth to create a seal.
- Dampen the fabric with water to improve filtration of particulates and some water-soluble gases.
- This provides basic particulate protection but not vapor protection.

**Activated charcoal filter:**
- If activated charcoal is available, layer it between fabric layers.
- Activated charcoal adsorbs many organic compounds and some gases.
- Construct a mask with charcoal sandwiched between fabric and sealed around the face.

**Limitations:** Improvised respirators protect against particulates and some gases but do NOT protect against all chemicals. Heavy vapor exposure requires commercial respirators or escape from the area.

### Skin Protection

**Plastic sheeting or garbage bags:**
- Cover exposed skin with plastic sheeting sealed with duct tape.
- This prevents liquid chemical contact and limits vapor absorption.
- Not ideal (uncomfortable, hot) but adequate for short-term protection.

**Heavy protective clothing:**
- Wear multiple layers of heavy cotton or synthetic clothing.
- Tuck pant legs into socks, sleeves into gloves, shirt into pants.
- This limits skin contact but is not a perfect barrier.

**Protective gloves:**
- Thick rubber or nitrile gloves provide protection against most liquid chemicals.
- Latex gloves are inadequate for chemical protection.
- Multiple layers of heavy fabric or plastic bags can substitute if gloves are unavailable.

## Common Industrial Chemicals and Hazards

### Ammonia (NH₃)

**Sources:** Refrigeration systems, fertilizer plants, cleaning products.
**Hazard:** Corrosive gas, causes respiratory and eye irritation, burns skin.
**Exposure symptoms:** Strong pungent odor (can smell at 5 ppm; causes damage at 300+ ppm), respiratory irritation, eye burning, skin irritation.
**Response:** Shelter in place (ammonia is lighter than air, accumulates upward). Move to lower floors if possible. Evacuate if respiratory symptoms develop. Decontaminate with copious water.

### Chlorine (Cl₂)

**Sources:** Water treatment plants, industrial bleach production, chemical manufacturing.
**Hazard:** Toxic, dense gas, causes severe respiratory damage.
**Exposure symptoms:** Pungent odor, respiratory irritation, wheezing, pulmonary edema (fluid in lungs), loss of consciousness in high concentrations.
**Response:** Shelter upwind and uphill from release. If downwind, evacuate perpendicular to wind, then away. Seek high ground. Decontaminate with water; oxygen if available for respiratory effects.

### Hydrogen Sulfide (H₂S)

**Sources:** Oil and gas facilities, wastewater treatment, industrial processes.
**Hazard:** Toxic, dense gas, smells like rotten eggs at low concentrations, but numbs smell at high concentrations (very dangerous—high exposures are undetectable by smell).
**Exposure symptoms:** Rotten egg smell initially, loss of smell, rapid onset of respiratory symptoms, loss of consciousness, sudden death in very high concentrations.
**Response:** DO NOT rely on smell to gauge safety. Evacuate upwind if you smell hydrogen sulfide even faintly. Move to high ground. Rapid evacuation is critical. High-flow oxygen essential for victims.

### Sulfur Dioxide (SO₂)

**Sources:** Power plants, refineries, chemical manufacturing.
**Hazard:** Acidic gas, causes respiratory and eye irritation.
**Exposure symptoms:** Irritating smell, respiratory irritation, eye irritation, asthma attacks in sensitive individuals.
**Response:** Shelter in place or evacuate upwind. Wet cloth over mouth/nose provides some protection. Decontaminate with water.

### Hydrofluoric Acid (HF)

**Sources:** Petroleum refining, uranium enrichment, various industrial processes.
**Hazard:** Highly corrosive liquid and vapor, causes deep tissue damage, systemic fluoride poisoning causing cardiac arrhythmias.
**Exposure symptoms:** Deceptively mild pain initially (skin exposure), but deep tissue damage continues for hours. Cardiac arrhythmias, severe electrolyte imbalance.
**Response:** Immediate decontamination with water (at least 5 minutes). Calcium gluconate gel applied to burns reduces fluoride absorption. IMMEDIATE medical attention is critical—even small exposures can be fatal. Do not delay seeking help.

### Phosgene (COCl₂)

**Sources:** Chemical manufacturing, formed when chlorine reacts with organic compounds or when chlorine-based cleaner is mixed with ammonia.
**Hazard:** Odorless or faintly sweet-smelling gas. Causes minimal acute symptoms but severe delayed pulmonary edema (12–24 hours after exposure).
**Exposure symptoms:** May feel fine initially, then 12–24 hours later: shortness of breath, pink or bloody sputum, respiratory failure.
**Response:** If phosgene exposure is suspected (unknown chemical release or chlorine+ammonia mixing), evacuate and seek medical observation even if symptoms are absent. High-flow oxygen and medical monitoring essential. Elevation of head and positive pressure ventilation if available.

### Organophosphate Pesticides

**Sources:** Agricultural areas, pesticide storage, industrial spraying.
**Hazard:** Inhibit acetylcholinesterase enzyme, causing cholinergic crisis (excessive salivation, sweating, muscle twitching, respiratory paralysis).
**Exposure symptoms:** Excessive salivation, sweating, muscle twitching, pinpoint pupils, muscle weakness, respiratory paralysis, loss of consciousness.
**Response:** Decontamination (remove clothing, wash with water) is primary treatment. Atropine (antidote) is critical—seek medical attention immediately. Do not touch contaminated people without gloves.

:::danger
**Some Chemical Combinations Are Dangerous.** Mixing ammonia and bleach produces toxic chloramine gas. Mixing ammonia and pesticides produces deadly mixtures. Store chemicals separately and never mix or combine household or industrial chemicals.
:::

## Long-Term Contamination Monitoring

After a chemical accident, ongoing monitoring assesses whether areas are safe for habitation.

### Soil and Groundwater Contamination

- **Many chemicals persist in soil** for months or years.
- **Groundwater contamination spreads** and can render wells unsafe for extended periods.
- **Professional testing** by environmental labs is required to determine contamination levels and safe areas for exposure.
- **Avoid hand-dug wells** or gardens in contaminated areas until soil testing shows safety.

### Building Decontamination

- **Ventilate buildings** thoroughly for days or weeks depending on chemical type.
- **Replace contaminated materials** (insulation, carpeting, drywall, etc.).
- **HVAC systems** may require professional cleaning or replacement.
- **Professional assessment** determines whether buildings are safe to reoccupy.

## Community Notification Protocols

In chemical accidents, rapid notification of affected populations is critical.

### Warning Systems

- **Sirens:** Outdoor sirens alert people to take shelter or evacuate.
- **Reverse 911 / Emergency alerts:** Text and phone alerts to specific geographic areas.
- **Local media:** Radio and TV stations broadcast emergency information.
- **Social media and apps:** Real-time location-based alerts.

### Community Roles

- **Emergency management** issues evacuation orders and provides guidance.
- **Fire departments** assess hazard zones and guide evacuees.
- **Health departments** provide decontamination guidance and medical oversight.
- **Law enforcement** enforces evacuation orders and prevents unauthorized entry to hazard zones.

### Individual Responsibilities

- **Register in advance** with emergency notification systems.
- **Maintain functional** radio, phone, and communication devices.
- **Know your evacuation zones** and routes.
- **Share information** with neighbors, especially those with disabilities or who live alone.
- **Follow official guidance** and avoid spreading rumors or inaccurate information.

## Summary and Action Items

Chemical accidents are low-probability, high-consequence events. Preparation focuses on early recognition, rapid evacuation, and proper decontamination.

**Immediate priorities:**
1. Identify chemical facilities, transportation routes, and accident risk in your area.
2. Learn your community's chemical emergency alert system.
3. Locate your evacuation zone and routes.
4. Assemble a shelter-in-place kit (duct tape, plastic sheeting, tape).

**Before incidents occur:**
1. Know symptoms of chemical exposure.
2. Practice evacuation routes.
3. Learn decontamination procedures.
4. Identify high-ground locations upwind of chemical facilities.
