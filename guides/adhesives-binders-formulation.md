:::warning
**Chemical Exposure and Burn Hazard**
Adhesive formulation involves heating organic materials, handling caustic substances, and working with volatile solvents. Animal glue rendering produces strong fumes — work outdoors or in well-ventilated spaces. Lime-based binders (quicklime, slaked lime) cause severe chemical burns; always add lime to water, never water to lime. Pine pitch and shellac processing involves flammable solvents and high temperatures. Cement mixing generates alkaline dust that damages lungs and skin. Wear gloves, eye protection, and a dust mask for all formulation work. This guide is educational reference material — not a substitute for formal chemistry safety training.
:::

Adhesives and binders join materials, hold coatings, and bind particles together. Understanding formulation allows you to make suitable adhesives for your materials and conditions.

## Animal Glues

Collagen-based glues, historically the standard for woodworking and binding.

### Hide Glue (Leather Scrap Glue)

**Source:** Collagen from leather scraps, hides, bones

**Preparation:**
1. Soak leather/hide scraps in water (softens)
2. Simmer gently (don't boil; heat breaks down collagen)
3. Strain through cloth
4. Cool; sets to jelly
5. Reheat slightly before use (becomes liquid)

**Properties:**
- Strength: Moderate (suitable for woodworking, binding)
- Set time: Slow (12–24 hours curing)
- Soluble in water: Yes (can be softened by moisture)
- Reversible: Can be softened by heating (useful for repairs)
- Cost: Very low if made from scraps

**Applications:**
- Woodworking (best for furniture, musical instruments)
- Bookbinding
- Veneer gluing
- NOT for wet environments or exterior

**Improving hide glue:**
- Add chalk or ground fish bone (filler; increases strength)
- Add a bit of salt (helps set)
- Ratio: 100 parts glue + 5–10 parts filler + pinch salt

### Bone Glue

**Source:** Animal bones (cattle bones, fish bones)

**Preparation:**
1. Boil bones in water (extracts collagen and gelatin)
2. Simmer slowly; don't scorch
3. Strain; cool to jelly
4. Scrape off fat (floats on top)
5. Use as hide glue

**Properties:**
- Stronger than hide glue (higher collagen content in bones)
- More brittle than hide glue
- Very slow set (24–48 hours)
- Can be made into bars for storage (boil down more, pour on greased surface, dry)

**Bars for storage:**
- Boil down to thick syrup
- Pour on greased baking sheet
- Dry thoroughly
- Cut into chunks
- Store dry; reheat when needed

### Fish Glue

**Source:** Fish bladders, fish skins

**Preparation:**
1. Soak fish bladders/skins
2. Simmer with water (less aggressive heating than bones)
3. Strain; cool

**Properties:**
- Fast setting (2–4 hours)
- Water-resistant (better than hide glue)
- Penetrates wood well
- Pleasant fishy smell (some find it unpleasant)
- Good for marine work (resistant to moisture)

## Plant-Based Adhesives

### Starch Paste

Simplest adhesive; flour or starch mixed with water.

**Basic recipe:**

<div class="recipe-box">
FLOUR PASTE (works for paper, light wood, fabric)
- 1 part wheat flour
- 3 parts water
- Pinch salt
- Pinch alum or citric acid (preservative)

Method:
1. Mix flour with small amount of cold water to avoid lumps
2. Heat remaining water to boiling
3. Add flour mixture slowly, stirring constantly
4. Simmer 3-5 minutes (thickens)
5. Cool slightly before use
6. Strain if lumpy

Properties:
- Sets slowly (overnight for full cure)
- Water-soluble (not for wet use)
- Very inexpensive
- Edible; non-toxic
- Can mold/rot if not protected
</div>

**Improvements:**
- Add gelatin powder (1–2 tbsp per batch): Faster setting, more strength
- Add animal glue powder: Much stronger; less soluble
- Casein (powdered milk protein): Makes starch more water-resistant

### Casein Glue

Milk protein-based adhesive; strong and water-resistant when dry.

**Recipe:**

<div class="recipe-box">
CASEIN GLUE (strong for woodworking, paper)
- 1 part casein powder (curd from soured milk)
- 2 parts water
- 1/2 part slaked lime (calcium hydroxide) or borax
- Fillers optional: chalk, sawdust, clay

Method:
1. Mix casein with small amount of cold water to dissolve
2. Add lime or borax; mixture thickens as pH changes
3. Use within 30 minutes (sets by chemical hardening)
4. Can be stored dry; activate by adding water
5. NOT reversible (hardens irreversibly)

Properties:
- Very strong (stronger than hide glue)
- Water-resistant when dry
- Must be mixed fresh (hardens in pot if left)
- Fast setting (4–8 hours)
- Suitable for exterior/damp conditions
</div>

### Vegetable/Plant Gums

**Gum Arabic:**
- From acacia tree sap
- Historical adhesive for painting, binding
- Soluble in water
- Slow setting
- Used for watercolor painting, calligraphy inks

**Tragacanth:**
- From astragalus shrub
- Very sticky, slow-drying
- Used for binding, pasting light materials

**Dextrin (starch-based):**
- Partially hydrolyzed starch
- Thicker, stronger than flour paste
- Better adhesion than flour paste alone

<section id="resin-adhesives">

## Natural Resin Adhesives

### Pine Pitch (Rosin)

Distilled resin from pine trees; sticky when warm, hard when cool.

**Obtaining:**
- Heat pine wood low-oxygen environment; collect condensing vapor (pitch)
- Or purchase as powder/solid

**Properties:**
- Strong when cold and hardened
- Sticky/plastic when warm
- Very water-resistant
- Brittle (can crack if stressed sharply)
- Long shelf life if kept dry

**Uses:**
- Violin bow rosin (musician's bow)
- Joinery (medieval woodworking; pre-glue modern joints)
- Adhesive for stone tiles

**Application:**
1. Heat pitch to workable consistency
2. Apply to joint
3. Clamp (pressure accelerates setting)
4. Cool; pitch hardens

### Shellac

Lac resin from lac beetle; dissolved in alcohol (see Interior Finishes guide).

**As adhesive:**
- Less common than as finish
- Can be used as binder/glue between wood and other materials
- Shellac wafers can be melted and used as hot-melt adhesive (before modern hot-glue guns)

**Application:**
- Melt solid shellac
- Apply to joint
- Press together; cool to set

## Cement and Mortar Formulations

Hydraulic cements set by chemical reaction with water; strong and permanent.

### Lime Mortar (Non-Hydraulic)

**Composition:**
- Lime (calcium hydroxide): Binder
- Sand: Aggregate (provides body)
- Water: Activator

**Ratio typical:** 1 part lime : 2–3 parts sand

**Setting mechanism:**
- Lime absorbs CO₂ from air
- Hardens to calcium carbonate
- Slow process (months to years for full strength)

**Properties:**
- Breathable (lime mortar allows vapor movement)
- Flexible (accommodates slight movement)
- Softer than concrete (sacrificial; protective of masonry)
- Can be repointed (older mortar removed, new applied)

**Uses:**
- Historic masonry (brick, stone)
- Areas where flexibility needed
- Restoration work

### Hydraulic Lime Mortar

Lime mortar with small amount of silicate content (naturally occurring or added).

**Sets faster than pure lime:** Days to weeks instead of months

**Composition:**
- Lime + silica-rich sand (clay + sand)
- Or: Lime + clay + sand

**Properties:**
- Still breathable
- Faster than pure lime; slower than cement
- Better for exterior/damp conditions

### Portland Cement Mortar

Modern standard; cement + sand + water.

**Ratio typical:** 1 part cement : 3–4 parts sand

**Setting mechanism:**
- Cement minerals react with water (hydration)
- Very strong bond
- Fast set (24–48 hours workable; full strength weeks)

**Properties:**
- Very strong; durable; long life
- NOT breathable (can trap moisture in masonry)
- Harder than masonry (can damage old brick if used on historic buildings)
- Not reversible (cannot be removed and reapplied)

**Uses:**
- Modern construction
- Exterior masonry
- High-stress applications
- Underwater use (water-resistant)

</section>

<section id="adhesive-selection">

## Adhesive Selection Guide

| Application | Best Adhesive | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Wood furniture | Hide glue or casein | Strong, reversible (hide), or fast (casein) |
| Paper/bookbinding | Flour paste or starch | Reversible, moisture-sensitive safe |
| Exterior woodwork | Casein or modern epoxy | Weather-resistant; permanent |
| Stone/tile masonry | Lime or cement mortar | Permanent, breathable (lime) or strong (cement) |
| Leather | Animal glue or casein | Flexible; water-resistant |
| Porous ceramics | Flour paste or starch | Moisture-absorption accommodated |
| Glass (non-structural) | Shellac or pitch | Transparent/clean appearance |
| Joint sealing (permanent) | Portland cement | Very strong, waterproof |

</section>

<section id="application">

## Application Techniques and Curing

### Surface Preparation

**Critical for adhesive success:**
1. **Clean:** Remove dust, oil, old finish
   - Sand lightly if smooth/glossy (improves adhesion)
   - Vacuum dust
   - Wipe with damp cloth; allow to dry
2. **Moisture content:** Wood should be dry (~12% moisture ideal); too wet = poor adhesion
3. **Fit:** Surfaces should fit well (gaps = weak joints)

### Application Method

**Brush or spreader:**
- Thin, even coat
- Not too much (squeezes out; leaves gaps) or too little (starved joint)
- "Good squeeze-out" indicates adequate glue with proper pressure

**Clamping:**
- Provides pressure for adhesive to flow and fill voids
- Duration: Per adhesive instructions (hide glue 12–24 hrs; casein 4–8 hrs)
- Pressure: Moderate (don't crush wood); just enough to ensure contact

**Temperature and humidity:**
- Hide glue: Works best 65–75°F; slow at lower temps
- Casein: Works best 65–70°F; too warm = too fast set
- Starch: Best in dry conditions (humidity slows set)
- Cement: Best in moderate temp; too hot = too fast

### Curing (After Assembly)

**Time before use:**
- Hide glue: 24–48 hours full strength
- Casein: 4–8 hours to handle; 24–48 hours for stress
- Starch: 24+ hours
- Cement: 3–7 days (continues hardening)

**Keep assembled:**
- Don't move until glue set
- Keep clean (remove squeeze-out before it hardens if desired)
- Allow full ventilation (helps drying in humid conditions)

### Testing Joint Strength

Once cured, test by:
- Flexing joint gently (should be solid; no movement)
- Attempting to pry apart (good adhesive makes parting difficult)
- For critical assemblies: Destructive test on sample joint

</section>

<section id="troubleshooting">

## Troubleshooting Adhesive Failures

| Problem | Likely Cause | Solution |
|---|---|---|
| Joint separated during curing | Insufficient pressure | Increase clamp pressure; ensure even contact |
| Glue didn't set (still soft after time) | Wrong temperature; contaminated adhesive | Warm joint slightly; ensure adhesive not old/degraded |
| Glue set too fast | Warm environment; low humidity (starch) | Work faster; warm adhesive slightly to liquefy |
| Weak joint (breaks under pressure) | Starved joint (too little glue) | Use more glue; ensure good contact and pressure |
| Glue squeezed out completely | Too much glue; not enough pressure needed | Use less glue; ensure surfaces fit well |
| Joint gaps visible | Poor surface fit | Replane/sand surfaces; ensure they mate well |

</section>

<section id="conclusion">

## Conclusion

Understanding adhesive chemistry allows you to make or select suitable binders for your work. Animal glues are strong, reversible, and historically proven; plant-based are inexpensive and non-toxic; hydraulic cements are permanent and strong. The best adhesive balances strength, durability, working time, and reversibility based on your application. Proper surface preparation and clamping technique matter as much as the adhesive itself.

</section>
