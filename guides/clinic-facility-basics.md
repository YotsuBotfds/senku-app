## What A Low-Resource Clinic Is For

A clinic is the place where people come for:
- first contact and triage
- basic exam and observation
- minor procedures and dressings
- simple testing if available
- recordkeeping and referral
- short isolation of potentially infectious patients

It is not a hospital, and it should not try to behave like one. In a rebuild setting, the safest clinic is usually small, clear, and easy to clean.

## Set Up Order When Space Is Limited

If you only have one room or one shelter, set up in this order:
1. Handwashing point at the entrance
2. Triage table or chair near the entrance
3. Waiting area that is separate from the exam space
4. Privacy screen or curtain for exam and treatment
5. Clean supply shelf
6. Dirty waste bin or bag
7. Simple records table
8. Isolation corner or separate room if any infectious patient is expected

If you have only one usable room, divide it into clean and dirty zones with furniture, rope, cloth, or chalk marks. The key is not the material. The key is that people can tell where they are supposed to go.

## Basic Clinic Flow

The safest flow is simple and one-way:

Arrival -> hand hygiene -> registration or quick check-in -> triage -> wait or isolate -> exam -> treatment -> discharge or referral -> waste handling and room cleaning

Keep the flow obvious:
- Patients should not wander through storage areas.
- Dirty items should not move back through clean areas.
- Infectious patients should not sit in the main waiting line if it can be avoided.
- Staff should know where to enter, where to change, and where to dispose of waste.
- If crowding grows, move screening or first contact closer to the entrance or outside under cover rather than letting a large mixed waiting room build up indoors.

## Zones To Create

### 1. Arrival and Triage Zone

This is the first contact point. It should be near the entrance so people are sorted before they enter the rest of the clinic.

Put here:
- chair or bench
- simple registration surface
- thermometer or vital-sign tools if available
- handwashing point
- mask or cloth covering supplies if your setting uses them

Purpose:
- identify urgent cases quickly
- separate patients who may be infectious
- avoid crowding the rest of the clinic

### 2. Waiting Zone and Triage Holding

Keep the waiting area separate from triage, exam, and storage areas.

Good waiting areas for patients after triage:
- are shaded
- have airflow
- keep people dry
- do not block the entrance or waste route

Keep the waiting area small. In a low-resource clinic, a large waiting crowd usually means the clinic is doing too much at once.

If respiratory illness is spreading, reduce crowding first. Shorter waits, staggered arrivals, or an outside waiting option are usually safer than packing more people into the same room.

### 3. Exam and Treatment Zone

This should be the cleanest working space after triage.

Put here:
- exam table or clean mat
- privacy screen or curtain
- cleanable chair
- basic lighting
- supplies for minor procedures and dressing changes

Do not store general household items here. Do not use this space as a passageway.

### 4. Isolation Zone

If possible, separate patients with fever, cough, vomiting, diarrhea, rash, or other potentially infectious symptoms.

Minimum isolation setup:
- separate room, corner, tent, or screened area
- its own handwashing point if possible
- its own waste container
- simple sign or marker so other people know it is restricted

If you cannot isolate fully, increase distance, improve ventilation, and reduce the time the patient spends in common areas.

### 5. Clean Supply Zone

Keep clean supplies together and protected from dust, moisture, rodents, and casual handling.

Store here:
- dressings
- sterile or clean instruments
- soap and alcohol-based hand rub if available
- gloves or other protective items
- clean paper forms and pens
- flashlight, batteries, and small tools

Keep this area dry, labeled, and off the floor.

### 6. Dirty Utility and Waste Zone

This is the place for contaminated materials, used dressings, dirty linens, sharps, body-fluid waste, and cleaning supplies after use.

The dirty zone should be:
- away from the clean supply shelf
- easy to reach from treatment areas
- easy to exit without crossing clean flow
- closed off from patients when possible

## Handwashing And Cleaning Points

The clinic should have handwashing points at the places where contamination is most likely.

Place handwashing at:
- the entrance
- triage
- exam room
- isolation area
- waste handling area
- staff exit or transition point

Use soap and water first if available. If water is limited, use a small, controlled station rather than an open bucket that can be contaminated quickly.

Important habits:
- wash before and after each patient contact
- wash after handling waste, body fluids, or dirty linens
- clean the exam surface between patients
- clean high-touch surfaces more often than low-touch surfaces

## Ventilation

Ventilation matters because stale indoor air increases spread of respiratory illness and makes the clinic harder to tolerate.

Practical rules:
- use windows that open on opposite sides if possible
- keep air moving through the clinic, not just trapped inside it
- avoid crowding beds or chairs tightly together
- keep isolation space as separate and as airy as possible

If fans exist, use them to move air out of patient spaces and away from clean zones. Do not place dirty air so it blows directly across waiting people or clean supplies.

## Storage Rules

Good clinic storage is simple:
- clean
- dry
- labeled
- locked or controlled
- protected from heat, rodents, and casual contamination

Separate storage by purpose:
- clean medical supplies
- medications
- records and forms
- cleaning supplies
- waste supplies

Never store contaminated items with clean dressings or paperwork. Never use the floor as a long-term storage shelf if you can avoid it.

## Records

Even a small clinic needs records. Without them, repeat visits become guesswork.

Minimum record set:
- patient name or identifier
- date and time
- reason for visit
- triage category or urgency
- basic findings
- what was done
- what was sent home
- referral or follow-up plan

Keep records where staff can find them quickly but patients cannot casually alter them. If privacy is limited, keep names and sensitive details out of public view.

## Waste Flow

Waste should move one direction only: from patient area to disposal area.

Separate waste if you can:
- general trash
- contaminated dressings and linens
- sharps
- body-fluid waste
- cleaning waste

Never let contaminated waste sit in the waiting area or on the exam table after use.

Basic waste handling rules:
- use a lidded bin or bag if possible
- close waste containers before moving them
- keep sharps in puncture-resistant containers
- follow the sanitation guide for burial, incineration, or treatment options

## Staff Movement

Staff movement should protect the clean area from the dirty area.

Good practice:
- start with clean tasks and move toward dirtier tasks
- avoid going from isolation back to clean supply handling without washing and changing as needed
- keep one person focused on records if possible
- keep one person focused on cleaning and waste if possible

If staffing is small, assign one person to one patient zone at a time. Switching back and forth too often creates mistakes.

## Privacy

Privacy is not only respectful. It helps patients speak honestly.

Use:
- curtain
- screen
- separate corner
- low-traffic exam area

Privacy matters especially for:
- reproductive health
- injuries
- children
- elders
- anyone describing trauma or abuse

## What To Build First

If you are starting from almost nothing, build in this order:
1. Handwashing point
2. Triage point
3. Privacy screen
4. Clean supply shelf
5. Waste container
6. Records station
7. Ventilated exam space
8. Isolation corner or room

That order gives you a usable clinic faster than trying to build the whole ideal layout at once.

## Common Mistakes

- Putting the waiting line through the exam space
- Storing clean supplies beside waste
- Using one room for everything with no visual separation
- Forgetting ventilation because the room feels small
- Failing to create a place for records
- Allowing staff to cross from dirty tasks into clean tasks without handwashing
- Treating the clinic like a treatment center instead of a flow-control space

## Practical Minimum Standard

If you want a simple rule, use this:
- one clear entrance
- one triage point
- one clean exam area
- one isolation option
- one waste route
- one records area
- one handwashing point for every major work zone

That is enough to make a low-resource clinic safer than a room full of medical supplies with no system.
