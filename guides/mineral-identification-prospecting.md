## Overview

Mineral identification is the foundation of prospecting, resource assessment, and survival-critical material location. Whether finding flint for fire-starting, iron ore for smelting, limestone for construction, or clay for pottery, the ability to identify minerals in the field using simple tests ensures efficient resource utilization. This guide covers systematic identification methods, common minerals with survival applications, geological environments where specific minerals occur, panning techniques, sample collection, and field safety.

<section id="identification-methods">

## Mineral Identification Methods

![Mineral identification tests and classification chart](../assets/svgs/mineral-identification.svg)

### Streak Test

The streak test reveals the true color of a mineral's powder, which often differs from the specimen's visible color.

**Procedure:**
1. Use a ceramic tile (unglazed porcelain), brick, or concrete surface.
2. Scratch the mineral across the surface with moderate pressure (firm but not excessive force).
3. Observe the color of the powder left behind.

**Examples:**
- **Magnetite (iron oxide):** Black mineral, leaves black streak.
- **Hematite (iron oxide):** Red-brown mineral, leaves red-brown or black streak (specular hematite leaves shiny black).
- **Pyrite (iron sulfide):** Brassy yellow mineral, leaves greenish-black streak.
- **Chalcopyrite (copper iron sulfide):** Golden mineral, leaves greenish-black streak.

**Why it matters:** Streak is more reliable than color for identification because color can vary based on crystal size, impurities, and surface weathering.

### Hardness (Mohs Scale)

Hardness tests rank mineral scratch resistance, indicating composition.

**Mohs hardness scale (1–10):**
1. Talc (scratched by fingernail)
2. Gypsum (scratched by fingernail easily)
3. Calcite (scratched by copper penny, barely scratched by fingernail)
4. Fluorite (not easily scratched by knife, scratched by steel nail)
5. Apatite (scratched by steel knife with some pressure)
6. Feldspar (not scratched by knife; scratches glass)
7. Quartz (scratches glass; not easily scratched)
8. Topaz (scratches quartz; uncommon in common rocks)
9. Corundum (scratches topaz; uncommon in common rocks)
10. Diamond (scratches all others; extremely rare)

**Field test procedure:**
1. Try to scratch with fingernail (marks hardness < 2–3).
2. Try to scratch with copper penny (marks hardness < 3–4).
3. Try to scratch with steel knife or nail (marks hardness < 5–6).
4. Try to scratch glass or quartz (marks hardness > 6–7).
5. Compare results to scale.

**Survival minerals and hardness:**
- **Flint (quartz):** Hardness 7; cannot be scratched with knife; breaks with conchoidal fracture.
- **Hematite (iron ore):** Hardness 5–6; leaves red streak; heavy.
- **Magnetite (iron ore):** Hardness 5.5–6.5; magnetic; leaves black streak.
- **Limestone (calcite):** Hardness 3; scratched easily with knife; reacts with acid (fizzes).
- **Clay minerals:** Hardness 1–2; soft, readily scratched.

### Crystal System

Crystal structures reflect atomic arrangement and indicate mineral type. However, field-observable crystal form is less reliable than tested properties (streak, hardness) for quick identification.

**Common crystal systems:**
- **Cubic:** Cubes, octahedra; examples: magnetite, halite (salt).
- **Hexagonal:** Hexagons, pyramids; examples: quartz, calcite (trigonal, related to hexagonal).
- **Monoclinic:** Slanted parallelograms; example: feldspar.
- **Orthorhombic:** Rectangles; example: olivine.

**Practical use:** In the field, most minerals have broken or poorly formed crystals, making crystal system difficult to determine visually. Focus on streak and hardness instead.

### Luster (Surface Shininess)

Luster describes how light reflects from the mineral surface.

**Types:**
- **Metallic:** Shiny like polished metal (magnetite, hematite, pyrite).
- **Submetallic:** Less shiny than metallic (some iron oxides).
- **Vitreous (glassy):** Shiny like glass (quartz, feldspar).
- **Resinous:** Like amber or resin (some sulfides, sulfates).
- **Pearly:** Like mother-of-pearl (mica, some feldspars).
- **Silky:** Like silk (some fibrous minerals).
- **Dull or earthy:** No shine (clay, chalk, some oxides).

**Practical use:** Luster helps narrow down possibilities; metallic luster suggests ore minerals (oxides, sulfides); vitreous luster suggests silicates (quartz, feldspar).

### Cleavage and Fracture

Cleavage is how minerals break along atomic planes (clean breaks); fracture is irregular breaking.

**Cleavage types:**
- **Perfect cleavage:** Breaks easily into flat sheets or clean fragments; example: mica (peels into thin sheets), halite (salt, breaks into cubes).
- **Good cleavage:** Breaks with some effort into fragments; example: feldspar.
- **Imperfect cleavage:** Breaks with difficulty; example: olivine.

**Fracture types:**
- **Conchoidal (shell-like):** Smooth, curved breaks; example: flint, obsidian (both forms of quartz), glass-like fracture pattern.
- **Uneven:** Rough, irregular breaks; example: most minerals.
- **Splintery or fibrous:** Breaks into splinters; example: some minerals with needle-like crystals.

**Survival application:** Flint's conchoidal fracture is why it's excellent for tool-making (the curved break creates sharp edges).

### Specific Gravity (Relative Density)

Specific gravity is the ratio of a mineral's density to water's density. Heavy minerals feel surprisingly heavy for their size.

**In the field:**
- Hold a specimen in each hand; heavy-for-size minerals likely have high specific gravity.
- **High gravity (> 4):** Iron ores (magnetite, hematite), gold, other metals and heavy ores.
- **Moderate (2.5–4):** Most silicates (quartz, feldspar), calcite.
- **Low (< 2.5):** Rare; mostly very light minerals like halite or certain sulfates.

**Prospecting:** Heavy minerals (gold, cassiterite, magnetite) concentrate in stream beds and can be separated by panning due to density difference.

### Acid Reaction Test

Some minerals (carbonates, sulfates) react with dilute acid.

**Procedure:**
1. Use weak acid (vinegar is sufficient; hydrochloric acid is stronger).
2. Apply a drop to the mineral surface.
3. Observe fizzing, color change, or dissolution.

**Results:**
- **Vigorous fizzing:** Calcite (calcium carbonate) or dolomite; indicates limestone or marble.
- **No reaction:** Silicates (quartz, feldspar), oxides (magnetite, hematite).

**Survival application:** Limestone (reacts with acid) is useful for mortar, plaster, and alkali production.

### Magnetism Test

Some minerals (iron oxides) are magnetic.

**Procedure:**
1. Use a compass or magnet.
2. Place near specimen; observe if needle deflects or if specimen moves toward magnet.

**Results:**
- **Strongly magnetic:** Magnetite (most magnetic ore mineral).
- **Weakly magnetic:** Some hematite and other iron minerals.
- **Non-magnetic:** Most silicates, carbonates.

**Survival application:** Magnetite is prized for fire-starting (using it in a bow-drill set with flint) and as a compass orientation aid.

## Common Minerals with Survival Applications

### Flint (Microcrystalline Quartz)

**Appearance:** Dark gray, brown, or black opaque stone; glassy luster when fresh.

**Properties:**
- Hardness: 7 (cannot be scratched with steel).
- Streak: White.
- Fracture: Conchoidal (curved, shell-like breaks).
- Luster: Waxy to glassy.

**Identification:** Found in chalk beds, limestone, and certain sedimentary rocks. Breaks with sharp edges suitable for cutting and fire-starting (when struck against steel or other flint).

**Applications:**
- Fire-starting (striking against steel creates sparks).
- Tool-making (blades, scrapers).
- Cutting edges for food processing.

**Where to find:** Limestone or chalk deposits; stream beds in areas with these formations; often appears as dark nodules in white chalk.

### Hematite (Iron(III) Oxide)

**Appearance:** Gray, red-brown, or black; shiny if crystalline (specular hematite), dull if earthy (red ochre).

**Properties:**
- Hardness: 5–6 (scratched with knife).
- Streak: Red-brown or black (very distinctive).
- Luster: Metallic to dull.
- Specific gravity: High (5.3; notably heavy).

**Identification:** Streak test (red-brown) is diagnostic. Often found in banded iron formations or in soils and sediments.

**Applications:**
- Primary iron ore (35–60% iron content when pure).
- Red pigment (ochre) for art and marking.
- Flux in smelting operations.

**Where to find:** Banded iron formations (widespread in ancient mountain belts); seashores (magnetic sands); stream beds and soils in iron-rich regions.

### Magnetite (Iron(II,III) Oxide)

**Appearance:** Black, metallic, cubic or octahedral crystals; sometimes with fine striations.

**Properties:**
- Hardness: 5.5–6.5.
- Streak: Black.
- Luster: Metallic.
- Magnetic: Strongly magnetic; can move compass needle or be picked up by magnet.
- Specific gravity: High (5.2; notably heavy).

**Identification:** Magnetism test is diagnostic; also streak (black) and weight.

**Applications:**
- Premium iron ore (45–70% iron content).
- Compass calibration (aligns with magnetic north).
- Fire-starting aid in bow-drill sets (creates sparks in certain configurations).

**Where to find:** Banded iron formations; magnetite beaches (magnetic black sand); stream beds; some igneous rocks (basalt).

### Limestone (Calcium Carbonate, Calcite)

**Appearance:** White, gray, or tan; often massive (no clear crystals); sedimentary layers visible.

**Properties:**
- Hardness: 3 (scratched with knife).
- Streak: White.
- Luster: Vitreous or dull (earthy).
- Acid reaction: Fizzes vigorously with weak acid (vinegar).
- Specific gravity: Low to moderate (2.7).

**Identification:** Acid reaction test is diagnostic; white color and low hardness are supportive.

**Applications:**
- Building material (blocks, crushed stone).
- Mortar and plaster (heated to produce quicklime, mixed with sand and water).
- Flux in smelting (aids melting of ore).
- Alkali production (when heated to quicklime, then reacted with water and used in various chemical processes).

**Where to find:** Limestone quarries (often visible as white or gray cliffs); stream beds in limestone regions; caves (calcium carbonate deposits).

### Clay Minerals

**Appearance:** Fine-grained, soft, moldable; white, gray, tan, or red depending on iron content.

**Properties:**
- Hardness: 1–2 (soft, easily scratched by fingernail).
- Streak: White or pale.
- Luster: Dull or earthy.
- Water absorption: Swells when wet; becomes moldable.

**Identification:** Softness and moldability when wet are diagnostic.

**Applications:**
- Pottery and ceramics (fired clay becomes hard and durable).
- Insulation.
- Construction material (adobe bricks, rammed earth).
- Medicinal clay (certain clays used traditionally for health purposes).

**Where to find:** Stream banks, lake beds, river deltas; often mixed with sand (clay content visible as fine silt settling slowly in water).

### Salt (Halite, Sodium Chloride)

**Appearance:** Clear, white, or pink cubic crystals; sometimes massive (rock salt).

**Properties:**
- Hardness: 2–2.5 (scratched by fingernail or knife).
- Streak: White.
- Luster: Vitreous (glassy).
- Taste: Salty (but only taste in controlled situations when source is known).
- Specific gravity: Low (2.16).

**Identification:** Taste (if safe), crystal form (cubic), and low hardness are diagnostic.

**Applications:**
- Food preservation (as salt brine or dry salt).
- Electrolyte supplementation.
- Chemical processes (alkali production, chlor-alkali process).
- De-icing.

**Where to find:** Salt mines and deposits (evaporite formations); salt springs and salt lakes; seawater (requires evaporation for extraction).

### Other Useful Minerals

**Copper minerals (malachite, azurite, chalcopyrite):**
- Distinctive green (malachite) or blue (azurite) colors.
- Hardness: 3–4.
- Applications: Copper smelting, jewelry, tools.
- Found in copper ore deposits, often associated with iron ore.

**Tin oxide (cassiterite):**
- Brown-black heavy mineral.
- Hardness: 6–7.
- Applications: Tin production (used with copper to make bronze).
- Found in stream beds (due to high density); panned from placer deposits.

## Panning for Heavy Minerals

Panning exploits density differences; heavy minerals (gold, magnetite, tin) concentrate at the bottom of a pan as lighter material washes away.

### Basic Panning Procedure

1. **Fill pan:** Load with stream sediment (sand, gravel, clay).
2. **Add water:** Submerge the material completely.
3. **Break up:** Crush large clay lumps with your hands; disperse material.
4. **Initial rough wash:** Tilt pan slightly and wash away very light (non-mineral) clay and silt.
5. **Swirl and settle:** Gentle circular motion settles heavy minerals to the bottom.
6. **Remove light material:** Carefully pour off light sand and silt while tilting the pan.
7. **Repeat:** Continue washing off light materials in steps 4–6.
8. **Concentrate:** After 5–10 washes, only heavy material remains (magnetite, gold, tin oxide, etc.).
9. **Inspect:** Look for shiny specks (gold), black magnetic particles (magnetite), or brown/black grains (tin).

### Identifying Concentrate

**Gold:** Distinctive bright yellow, very dense, small flakes or tiny nuggets. Follows magnetic concentration (after removing magnetite).

**Magnetite:** Black, magnetic, dominant heavy mineral in most locations with iron deposits.

**Cassiterite (tin oxide):** Brown-black, slightly magnetic or non-magnetic, high density, found in tin-rich regions.

**Other heavy minerals:** Garnets (red), zircon (brown), ilmenite (black, slightly magnetic).

### Pan Selection

**Size:** 12–16 inch diameter pans are standard; larger pans (18–20 inches) capture more material but are heavier.

**Depth:** 2–3 inches deep; shallow pans make panning easier.

**Color:** Lighter-colored pans (white, yellow) make it easier to see gold or other minerals.

**Material:** Plastic pans are lightweight; steel pans are more durable but heavier and can rust.

## Geological Indicators of Mineral Deposits

### Ore Deposit Environments

**Banded iron formations (BIF):**
- Layers of iron-rich rocks (hematite, magnetite) alternating with silica-rich bands.
- Formed in ancient (> 1.5 billion years old) ocean sediments.
- Often occur in mountain belts.
- Diagnostic: Distinctive layering visible in road cuts, quarries, or cliff faces.

**Copper/sulfide deposits:**
- Often associated with igneous rocks (granite, diorite).
- Surface oxidation produces green (malachite) or blue (azurite) staining.
- Diagnostic: Greenish staining on rocks in specific areas; Copper minerals develop "gossan" (oxidized zone) on surface.

**Salt deposits:**
- Evaporite formations (ancient salt lakes, salt domes).
- Located in arid regions or geologically recent salt mines.
- Diagnostic: White deposits; salt springs; saline groundwater.

**Clay deposits:**
- River deltas, floodplains, ancient lake beds.
- Fine, smooth, cohesive material; swells when wet.
- Diagnostic: Pottery-quality clay is found in specific geologic contexts; test by kneading wet material.

### Indicator Minerals (Pathfinder Minerals)

Some minerals (indicator or pathfinder minerals) often occur near valuable deposits:

- **Garnet:** Often found near gold deposits (in certain rock types).
- **Tourmaline:** Associated with tin and tungsten deposits.
- **Magnetite:** Indicates iron ore potential; also pathfinder for certain gold deposits.
- **Green or blue minerals (malachite, azurite, chrysocolla):** Indicate copper mineralization.

## Fieldwork Safety

:::warning
**Prospecting hazards:** Be aware of abandoned mines, unstable terrain, weather, and dehydration.
:::

### Mine Hazard Awareness

**Abandoned mines:**
- Unstable shafts and tunnels; do not enter.
- Underground water; flooding risk.
- Toxic gases (methane, hydrogen sulfide, carbon dioxide).
- Old, unsafe equipment and hazardous waste.

**Safe alternatives:**
- Sample from exposed surface rocks and stream beds.
- Use modern quarries or mines (if access is permitted).
- Study geological maps and reports to understand mineralization without entering closed mines.

### Environmental Hazards

**Weather:**
- Carry water (at least 3–5 liters).
- Wear sun protection (hat, sunscreen).
- Know weather forecasts before heading out.

**Terrain:**
- Use sturdy footwear.
- Inform someone of your location and return time.
- Carry map, compass, and first-aid kit.
- Avoid cliffs and unstable banks when panning streams.

### Sample Collection

**Responsible collecting:**
- Do not remove large amounts from public lands (many areas require permits).
- Respect private property; ask permission before collecting.
- Leave geological features intact for others.
- Pack out all trash; minimize environmental impact.

## Sample Collection and Labeling

### Field Documentation

1. **Sample number:** Assign a unique ID (e.g., ML-2026-001).
2. **Location:** GPS coordinates (if available) or detailed description (landmark, distance, direction).
3. **Date:** When collected.
4. **Description:** Rock type, mineral composition (observed), color, size.
5. **Depth/context:** From surface exposure, stream bed, excavated pit.
6. **Photo:** Optional but helpful; include scale object (coin, ruler).

### Sample Organization

- Store samples in cloth bags or boxes, labeled clearly.
- Keep field notebook with corresponding sample numbers.
- Photograph samples in good light for reference.
- If analyzing further, take a small portion to a local university, mining office, or laboratory for identification confirmation.

## Mineral Applications in Survival

### Flint and Fire-Starting

Flint is superior to most rocks for fire-starting because its conchoidal fracture creates naturally sharp edges that shed sparks when struck against steel or another flint rock.

### Iron Smelting and Tool-Making

Magnetite and hematite are primary iron ores. Even basic understanding of these minerals allows assessment of iron-ore availability for smelting operations (see <a href="../metalworking.html">Metalworking</a> for smelting details).

### Limestone and Mortar Production

Limestone can be heated to quicklime (calcium oxide), mixed with water and sand to form mortar, essential for building.

### Clay for Pottery

Clay deposits provide material for ceramic vessels, crucial for water storage, cooking, and other survival applications.

### Salt Extraction

Salt deposits or salt springs provide an essential nutrient and food preservation agent.
