:::info-box
**Step 1 — What should you grab first before it decays? Quick triage order:**

- **Now (days):** Liquid medications, insulin, fresh food, opened chemicals, wet-cell batteries — grab immediately or mark the location and return within 1–2 days.
- **This week (weeks):** Gasoline, opened solvents/paints, unprotected seeds, fresh batteries under load, animal feed.
- **This month (months):** Diesel, rubber products (if stored outdoors), exposed steel tools, leather in wet storage, opened lubricants.
- **Later (years–decades):** Canned food, sealed medications, sealed batteries, hand tools, stone, glass, precious metals — salvage systematically, not urgently.

When in doubt, treat the item as more urgent than it appears. Overestimating urgency costs time; underestimating costs the item. Before entering any building to retrieve materials, confirm it is structurally safe: [Structural Safety for Building Entry](../structural-safety-building-entry.html).

<section id="overview">

## Degradation Timeline Overview

The fundamental principle underlying all strategic scavenging is this: different materials degrade at dramatically different rates. Some items become worthless within days; others remain valuable for decades. Your scavenging priority should directly correspond to degradation rate. Grab fast-degrading items first, knowing you can return for slow-degrading items later.

This framework isn't about grabbing everything (an impossible task in most scenarios), but about intelligent prioritization. If you have limited carrying capacity or limited scavenging time, you must choose which items to salvage. Items that remain viable for decades can be postponed; items that degrade in days cannot.

The timeline categories below use realistic degradation rates under typical post-collapse conditions: room temperature (roughly 60-75°F / 15-24°C), moderate humidity (not in water, but not in a dry desert), and protection from extreme weather (in buildings, basements, or covered areas). Exposure to heat, moisture, UV light, or extreme cold will dramatically accelerate degradation. Conversely, ideal storage conditions (cool, dry, dark) will extend timelines significantly.

Use these timelines as guides, not absolute rules. Assess each item individually based on its specific storage conditions and condition when you encounter it.

</section>

<section id="immediate">

## Immediate (Days)

These items become worthless within days of infrastructure collapse. They must be prioritized first because your window of opportunity is closing constantly. If you encounter one of these items and you have carrying capacity, stop and grab it—you cannot return for it later.

### Medications—Especially Critical Formulations

Liquid antibiotics (penicillin, amoxicillin suspension) degrade rapidly. Room temperature storage reduces potency significantly within 2-4 weeks. Insulin loses potency much faster; refrigerated insulin becomes unreliable within days of losing temperature control. Nitroglycerin tablets for heart conditions become inert in 2-3 months after the bottle is opened. Liquid medications (cough syrup, antibiotic suspensions, etc.) are extremely time-sensitive.

Solid medications are more stable, but those requiring refrigeration (some antibiotics, inhalers with certain propellants) deteriorate rapidly without cold. Any medication requiring specific storage conditions becomes unreliable when those conditions fail. Prioritize salvaging these above almost everything else.

### Fresh Food

Unpackaged food—fresh vegetables, fruits, meat, dairy—becomes unsafe or spoiled within 1-3 days at room temperature. Milk spoils within 4-8 hours of temperature loss. Meat becomes unsafe after 4-6 hours. Greens wilt within hours. If you encounter unpackaged fresh food, consume it immediately or process it (cook, salt-preserve, or dry). You cannot defer these salvage priorities.

### Opened Chemicals

Opened solvents, paint thinners, and volatile compounds evaporate and degrade rapidly. An opened can of solvent loses potency within 1-2 weeks; within a month, it's mostly worthless. Salvage opened chemicals immediately for their remaining utility.

### Wet-Cell Batteries

Car batteries, battery banks with liquid electrolyte, and old-style wet batteries leak and degrade rapidly. The electrolyte solution breaks down, reducing capacity significantly within days. If you need the battery, salvage it immediately. Dry-cell batteries (alkaline, lithium) are more stable; wet-cell batteries are time-critical.

### Exposed Film and Photography

Exposed photographic film (undeveloped) begins degrading immediately after exposure. Ambient temperature and humidity cause color shifts and contrast loss. Within days, degradation becomes visible. If you have undeveloped film and want to preserve the images, develop it immediately or store it in extreme cold (if possible). Otherwise, the images are lost.

### Biological Specimens

Blood products, serum, tissue samples, and biological materials become unusable within hours to days. Vaccines are highly temperature-sensitive; most lose viability within 24-48 hours if not refrigerated. If you're salvaging a medical facility or research lab, biological materials are your absolute priority.

</section>

<section id="weeks">

## Weeks

Items in this category remain viable for roughly 2-8 weeks before significant degradation. These should be your second priority after immediate items. You have somewhat more flexibility here, but these still represent relatively short salvage windows.

### Gasoline and Fuel Stabilization

Gasoline begins degrading within 30 days without stabilizer. Gum formation, varnish deposits, and oxidation reduce fuel quality and engine performance. Fuel stored in heat-prone locations (cars in the sun, fuel cans in garages) degrades faster—2-3 weeks is realistic. Diesel is slightly more stable (2-3 months untreated). Stabilized fuel with additives extends life to 6-12 months, but untreated fuel from a salvage source is degrading now.

If you need fuel for transportation or generators, prioritize salvaging gasoline from vehicles and storage tanks early. Store salvaged gasoline in sealed, opaque containers in cool, dark locations. Strain it through cloth to remove any water or debris.

### Opened Solvents and Paints

Beyond the immediate category, already-opened paint cans, lacquer, varnish, and solvents remain somewhat useful for 3-8 weeks before they thicken, separate, or become unusable. If you need solvents for cleaning, degreasing, or other purposes, salvage opened containers while they're still mostly functional.

### Unprotected Seeds

Seeds stored at room temperature begin losing viability within 4-8 weeks if not in ideal conditions. Seeds in paper packets or cloth bags absorb moisture and degrade faster. If you find seeds and need to plant them, prioritize them over longer-term items. Seeds in sealed containers remain viable longer, but unprotected seeds need immediate use. Test germination before planting large amounts—soak a small sample and watch for sprouting within 3-7 days.

### Animal Feed

Dry livestock feed (chicken feed, horse grain, dog kibble) begins degrading within 3-6 weeks if stored in moderate humidity. Grain attracts insects and mold. If you're raising animals, prioritize fresh feed salvage. Opened or bulk feed is your first priority; sealed bags remain viable longer.

### Fresh Batteries Under Load

Alkaline batteries supplying power to devices (flashlights, radios, electronics) lose capacity within 2-4 weeks depending on usage intensity. Fresh salvaged batteries remain viable much longer, but batteries actively powering devices degrade noticeably within weeks. If you have battery-powered essential devices, prioritize fresh battery salvage.

### Refrigerated Medical Supplies

Beyond medications, refrigerated medical supplies include serological products, plasma substitutes, and certain biological-derived medications. Once removed from refrigeration, these degrade within 3-7 days in most cases. If you're salvaging a medical facility, prioritize refrigerated items on your initial pass.

</section>

<section id="months">

## Months

Items degrading on a monthly timeline give you greater flexibility. These are valuable to salvage but less urgent than faster-degrading items. You can reasonably defer salvaging these items if immediate and weekly items demand your attention first.

### Seeds and Plant Viability

Seeds stored in suboptimal conditions (room temperature, moderate humidity) lose viability over months. Most vegetable seeds remain germination-viable for 2-4 months in poor conditions, 6-12 months in moderate conditions. If you're establishing a garden or planning to plant, prioritize seed salvage within months of discovering the seeds. After 6-12 months, germination rates drop noticeably.

### Rubber Products and UV Exposure

Rubber degrades under UV exposure. Tires left in sunlight, rubber hoses, rubber seals, and rubber-based tools begin cracking and losing flexibility within 2-4 months of sun exposure. Rubber stored indoors remains viable far longer. If you're salvaging rubber products, items stored outside are your priority; items in dark storage can wait.

### Exposed Metals and Corrosion

Unprotected steel and iron begin visible rust formation within weeks, but significant structural weakening takes months. Carbon steel tools begin developing rust within 1-2 weeks of exposure to humidity but remain functionally viable for several months. Stainless steel and brass resist corrosion much longer. If you're salvaging metal tools, prioritize those showing early rust over those still bright; the rusty ones are degrading faster. Corrosion accelerates in salt-air or acidic environments.

### Unstabilized Diesel Fuel

Diesel fuel is more stable than gasoline but still degrades. Untreated diesel remains usable for 2-3 months; after that, gum formation and water contamination become significant problems. Salvage diesel within the first few months or add stabilizers to extend life.

### Lubricants in Opened Containers

Motor oil, synthetic oils, grease, and lubricants in opened containers begin oxidizing and losing properties within 2-4 months of opening. Sealed containers remain viable for years. If you find opened lubricant containers, use them relatively soon; sealed containers can be deferred.

### Leather Goods in Wet Conditions

Leather begins mold growth and rotting within 1-3 months if stored in wet, humid conditions. Leather in dry storage remains viable for years or decades. If you encounter leather goods (shoes, belts, jackets, tools) stored in damp areas, prioritize salvaging them over leather stored in dry locations. Moldy leather is salvageable if dried and cleaned, but the window is limited.

</section>

<section id="years">

## Years

Items with multi-year viability can be systematically salvaged once you've addressed faster-degrading categories. These represent significant value and should be collected, but you have years to gather them.

### Canned Food

Properly canned food remains safe for 5-30+ years if the seal is intact. The shelf life depends on food type (acidic vs. low-acid), storage temperature, and can condition. Assess cans for seal integrity and container damage, then store. You can defer canned food salvage for months because the timelines are so long. However, begin collecting them in your second scavenging phase.

### Sealed Electronics and Circuit Boards

Electronics in sealed, dry storage remain functional for many years. Capacitors begin drying out after 5-10 years of storage without power, but the devices remain repairable. Microprocessors, memory chips, and solid-state components are stable for decades. If you're collecting electronics for repair or repurposing, years-long timelines apply. Moisture and heat are the primary enemies; devices stored cool and dry remain viable for years.

### Sealed Batteries

Sealed alkaline batteries stored in cool, dry conditions remain viable for 7-10 years, sometimes longer. Lithium batteries last 10-15+ years. Sealed rechargeable batteries (NiMH, NiCd) self-discharge but remain viable for 10+ years. Prioritize sealed batteries over opened batteries; sealed items don't need immediate salvaging.

### Quality Hand Tools

Steel hammers, wrenches, saws, screwdrivers, and quality hand tools remain viable for many years even with moderate rust. Rust is surface-level and can be cleaned. Functionality isn't significantly compromised by rust unless it's extensive. Salvage hand tools opportunistically; they're not time-critical. However, if you see them exposed to harsh conditions (outdoors, in salt-air environments), prioritize them slightly.

### Sealed Medications

Most sealed medications remain effective well past their printed expiration dates. Efficacy decreases gradually—a 10-year-old antibiotic might be 60-80% potency but still useful. Certain medications (tetracyclines) become dangerous with age, but most are simply less effective. Sealed medications stored in cool, dry locations remain viable for years. You can safely defer sealed medication salvage for months or years.

### Properly Stored Ammunition

Factory ammunition stored in sealed containers in cool, dry conditions remains viable for decades. Powder doesn't degrade significantly, brass doesn't corrode in dry storage, and primers remain reliable. Ammunition stored in damp conditions begins corroding within years. Salvage ammunition based on storage condition: outdoor ammunition is more time-critical than indoor ammunition.

### Sealed Chemicals and Pesticides

Sealed containers of pesticides, herbicides, and industrial chemicals remain viable for many years if the seal is intact. Opened containers degrade faster (see weeks category). Sealed chemicals don't require urgent salvaging; you can collect them systematically over months.

</section>

<section id="decades">

## Decades

These materials have effectively indefinite shelf life under normal storage conditions. They're valuable but absolutely not time-critical. Salvage them opportunistically when you have excess capacity.

### Stone and Masonry

Properly worked stone (blocks, bricks, tiles) lasts hundreds of years or longer. Salvaged stone from buildings or structures can be repurposed. No time urgency applies.

### Quality Steel Tools and Equipment

Well-made steel tools, machine parts, and equipment remain functional for decades even with rust. Surface rust doesn't compromise functionality. Salvage steel opportunistically; it won't degrade significantly within your scavenging window.

### Precious Metals

Gold, silver, and platinum don't corrode or degrade. Salvaged precious metals remain unchanged indefinitely. No time urgency applies; these are salvage goals but not time-critical priorities.

### Ceramics and Porcelain

Undamaged ceramic and porcelain items remain functional indefinitely. Glaze doesn't degrade, and the material is inert. Salvage ceramics opportunistically but not urgently.

### Glass

Glass remains chemically stable indefinitely. Clear glass doesn't cloud or degrade with age. Glassware, containers, and windows salvaged decades ago are perfectly functional. No time urgency applies.

### Properly Stored Lumber

Lumber stored indoors and kept relatively dry remains functional for decades. Wood doesn't significantly degrade if protected from rot-promoting moisture and insect damage. Salvaged lumber from buildings remains usable. Rot develops over years of exposure to moisture, not within your immediate scavenging timeline. Prioritize lumber stored indoors over exposed lumber, but neither is time-critical.

### Cast Iron and Stainless Steel

Cast iron develops rust easily but the underlying metal remains sound. Surface rust doesn't prevent functionality. Stainless steel resists corrosion for decades. Both remain viable indefinitely. Salvage based on convenience, not time urgency.

### Copper and Brass

Copper develops a patina (green oxidation) but remains sound indefinitely. Brass (copper-zinc alloy) also resists degradation. Both materials remain viable for decades with negligible functional loss. Salvage opportunistically.

### Concrete Structures

Concrete lasts decades or longer without significant degradation (centuries in some cases). Salvaged concrete blocks, slabs, or aggregate remain viable indefinitely. No time urgency applies; concrete becomes valuable in your secondary and tertiary salvage phases.

</section>

<section id="seasonal">

## Seasonal Scavenging Calendar

Seasonal changes affect both degradation rates and your access to items. Plan your scavenging calendar around seasons to maximize efficiency.

### Spring (March-May)

Focus on heat-sensitive and moisture-sensitive items. Seeds are becoming viable for spring planting; if you haven't salvaged seeds, do so now. Winter storage has ended; items that survived cold are still viable. Temperatures begin rising, accelerating degradation of heat-sensitive items like fuel and paint.

Priorities: seeds (diminishing viability as spring advances), fuel (gather before summer heat degrades it), medications requiring cool storage (temperatures rising), unopened electronics (before summer heat damages capacitors), lubricants (oxidation accelerates with heat).

The window between winter (slow degradation) and summer (accelerated degradation) is your chance to salvage temperature-sensitive items effectively.

### Summer (June-August)

Heat accelerates degradation across nearly all material categories. Outdoor items degrade rapidly due to UV exposure and heat. Focus on sheltered, indoor salvage. Summer access to locations is easiest due to weather, but outdoor items are degrading fastest.

Priorities: sealed items stored indoors (taking advantage of weather access while items remain viable), items with summer-specific utility (water storage containers, shade structures, light-reflecting materials), non-perishable foods (degrading slower in summer than perishables, but still time-sensitive).

Defer outdoor items with long shelf life to cooler seasons; prioritize items that actually need salvaging now before summer heat finishes them.

### Fall (September-November)

Temperatures drop; this is your chance to salvage heat-sensitive items that have survived. Winter access will become difficult in many climates. Prepare for winter by salvaging items you'll need when access is restricted.

Priorities: leather goods (mold growth slowing as humidity drops), lubricants and oils (heat-stress declining), medications requiring cool storage (returning to better conditions), items for winter utility (heated storage items, insulation, heavy clothing), fuel for winter heating.

This season is about preparing for winter scarcity. Gather winter-critical items while weather still permits access.

### Winter (December-February)

Cold slows degradation dramatically. Frozen conditions provide excellent preservation for some items, though access becomes difficult. Frozen items (meat, food) remain preserved. Sealed items stored outdoors are protected by cold.

Priorities: outdoor items that benefit from cold storage (frozen food becomes self-preserved), sealed items that can tolerate freezing (electronics, ammunition, canned goods), items stored in locations that are now inaccessible due to snow (salvage before access is lost).

Winter salvaging is hard but valuable for items that benefit from cold storage. Plan for difficult access and shorter working windows.

</section>

<section id="decision-matrix">

## Decision Matrix

Use this flowchart-style decision framework when you encounter items during scavenging and must decide whether to salvage them given your current priorities and carrying capacity.

### The Basic Question

When you encounter an item, ask: "How quickly will this become worthless?" Then cross-reference with your current situation using this matrix.

### Decision Framework

**If item degrades in DAYS:** Salvage immediately if you have any carrying capacity at all. This is your highest priority. If truly over capacity, mark the location and return within 1-2 days.

**If item degrades in WEEKS:** Salvage within the week if possible. If you're over capacity, prioritize this over longer-term items. Plan to return within 1-2 weeks if you must defer.

**If item degrades in MONTHS:** Salvage within the month if convenient. These aren't as urgent as weekly items but more urgent than yearly items. You can defer these for a few weeks if immediate/weekly items demand attention.

**If item degrades in YEARS:** Salvage within your broader strategy but not urgently. These can be deferred for months. Build these into your systematic salvage route; don't rush.

**If item degrades in DECADES:** Salvage opportunistically when you have extra carrying capacity or when passing nearby. Zero time urgency. These are your filler items—use them to bulk out loads when higher-priority items aren't available.

### Scenario Examples

**Scenario 1: Pharmacy Disaster** You discover an abandoned pharmacy with medication spilled in aisles. Liquid antibiotics are exposed (days priority), sealed medications are visible on shelves (years priority), and you have a vehicle with limited capacity. Decision: Load the liquid antibiotics and sealed medications that are most potent/useful first. The sealed medications can wait if necessary; the liquid antibiotics cannot. Carry liquid items out first.

**Scenario 2: Hardware Store Salvage** You find opened paint cans (weeks), sealed tools (decades), and loose screws/nails (decades). You're on foot with limited carrying capacity. Decision: Skip the loose hardware (decades, replaceable). Take sealed tools if you have room; they're useful and stable. Opened paint gets lower priority than sealed items but higher than loose materials. Load sealed tools, grab opened paint if room remains.

**Scenario 3: Fuel Depot** You find gasoline cans (weeks without stabilizer), diesel in drums (months), and metal storage equipment (decades). Your priority is fuel for vehicles. Decision: Gasoline is weeks-critical; it's your first load. Diesel is months-critical; grab it in your second trip. Storage equipment is low priority; salvage if you have dedicated vehicle capacity later.

**Scenario 4: Greenhouse or Garden Center** Seeds are everywhere (weeks to months), potting soil is plentiful (years), and tools are present (decades). You're establishing a homestead and need plants. Decision: Seeds are months-critical if planting season is approaching; prioritize them. Potting soil is years-critical; grab it if you have transport but don't rush. Tools are decades-critical; salvage them opportunistically when passing.

**Scenario 5: Abandoned Residence (Cold Storage)** You find canned food (years if sealed), medications (years if sealed), and electronics (years in cold storage). Items are stored in a cool basement. Time urgency is low for all items due to cold storage. Decision: Salvage systematically. Medications first (most valuable). Canned food next (most useful quantity). Electronics last (least immediate utility but still valuable). Cold storage buys you time—there's no rush.

</section>

<section id="location-assessment">

## Location Assessment Framework

Evaluating salvage locations determines which materials are available and how to prioritize effort.

### Residential Properties

Homes contain distributed resources across multiple rooms. Priority assessment: (1) Kitchen (food, cooking equipment, stored supplies); (2) Bathrooms (medications, first aid, soap, cleaning supplies); (3) Bedrooms (textiles, mattresses, clothing); (4) Basement/storage (preserved items, bulk supplies, tools); (5) Attic (seasonal clothing, old equipment, insulation materials). Typical residence yield: 5-20 lbs of time-critical items, 50-200 lbs of durable items. Time investment: 1-2 hours for thorough assessment.

### Retail and Commercial

Stores contain organized, high-density resources. Assessment: (1) Shelves (sealed, long-shelf-life items first); (2) Storage rooms (bulk items, employee areas); (3) Dumpsters (rejected goods often safe); (4) Refrigeration areas (time-critical if power compromised). Yield: 100-1,000+ lbs depending on store size. Time investment: 30 minutes-2 hours.

### Vehicles

Cars and trucks contain multiple salvage categories. Assessment: (1) Fuel tank (weeks-critical); (2) Engine fluids (oil, coolant—months if sealed); (3) Electrical (batteries, wiring—years); (4) Interior (textiles, upholstery—years); (5) Structural (metal, glass—decades). Yield: 20-100 lbs depending on vehicle type. Time investment: 30-60 minutes per vehicle.

### Medical Facilities

Hospitals, clinics, and pharmacies are highest-value salvage locations. Assessment: (1) Pharmacy stock (days to years depending on temperature control); (2) Refrigerated supplies (days-critical if power lost); (3) Sterilization equipment (years); (4) Surgical supplies (years); (5) Records/reference materials (indefinite). Yield: extremely high value despite lower weight. Time investment: 1-4 hours depending on size and hazards.

### Agricultural Facilities

Greenhouses, storage sheds, and farm buildings contain seasonal materials. Assessment: (1) Seeds (months to years); (2) Stored crops (months if cool); (3) Tools and equipment (decades); (4) Feed and supplies (weeks to months); (5) Fertilizers and soil amendments (indefinite). Yield: moderate to high. Time investment: 1-2 hours.

## Tools & Requirements

Salvage operations require minimal specialized equipment but specific safety items.

### Essential Items

- Flashlight with spare batteries (dark basements, attics, interiors)
- Gloves (chemical protection, contamination prevention, grip)
- Heavy-duty bags or containers (transport, sorting)
- Notebook (record locations, conditions, dates)
- Respirator or dust mask (mold, dust, contamination)
- First aid kit (cuts, scrapes, burns)

### Recommended Items

- Dolly or hand truck (reduce injury, improve transport)
- Rope or tie-downs (secure loads)
- Tool kit (disassembly when needed)
- Scale or weighing method (estimate value)
- Camera (document locations for community intelligence)

<section id="risk-assessment">

## Risk Assessment

Salvage operations carry inherent risks. Systematic risk assessment prevents injury and resource loss.

### Structural Hazards

Building integrity assessment: (1) Observe exterior for sagging roofs, leaning walls, visible foundation cracks; (2) Listen for creaking or settling sounds when entering; (3) Test floors for stability before putting full weight on them; (4) Look for water damage (soft spots, mold) indicating structural failure; (5) Never enter alone—always have a partner. If safety is questionable, abandon the location. No material is worth structural collapse injury.

### Contamination Hazards

Unknown contents may include: (1) Hazardous chemicals (pools of liquid, fumes, odors); (2) Biological hazards (mold, rodent droppings, blood); (3) Radiation (unlabeled medical or industrial equipment); (4) Pathogens (blood, body fluids). Respiratory protection is mandatory in moldy or contaminated environments. Gloves required for any contact with unknown materials. Wash hands and face after every salvage operation.

### Violence and Territorial Risk

Other scavengers may view locations as territory. Risk factors: (1) signs of other salvagers (recent damage, tools left behind, fresh footprints); (2) organized group operations (signs of systematic salvage); (3) defended materials (booby traps, warnings, blocked access). De-escalation strategy: (1) avoid confrontation; (2) salvage different locations; (3) salvage during different times; (4) if confronted, prioritize safety over material. Material is replaceable; injuries and deaths are not.

### Environmental Hazards

Weather, temperature, and environmental conditions: (1) Never salvage during active weather (storms, heat waves); (2) Hypothermia risk in cold, wet conditions—limit time and wear appropriate clothing; (3) Heat stress in hot environments—drink water, take breaks, work in cooler parts of day; (4) Darkness hazard—use flashlight and avoid working in poor visibility. Operate within your physical limits; pushing beyond capacity causes injury.

:::warning
Salvage operations frequently result in injury (cuts, burns, falls, crush injuries, chemical exposure). Prevention through planning is critical. Conservative safety choices prevent temporary discomfort and permanent disability.
:::

</section>

<section id="documentation">

## Documentation & Community Intelligence

Recording salvage information benefits your planning and serves the broader community.

### Location Records

Document each salvage location: (1) Address and geographic coordinates (if possible); (2) Building type and condition; (3) Materials found and approximate quantity; (4) Items remaining (for future reference); (5) Structural condition and hazards noted; (6) Access points and security concerns; (7) Date salvaged; (8) Estimated return value (if recycling vs. functional use). Simple notebook entries are sufficient.

### Degradation Observations

Track your observations about actual degradation rates: (1) What materials did you find in good condition after expected degradation period?; (2) What degraded faster than expected?; (3) Storage condition effects (hot vs. cold, wet vs. dry, sealed vs. exposed); (4) Mold and insect activity timelines; (5) Specific location conditions that extended viability. Over time, your personal database improves estimation accuracy.

### Community Sharing

In group situations, intelligence sharing prevents wasted effort: (1) Share location finds with community members (with security caveats); (2) Record material availability patterns (residential areas better for textiles, commercial better for bulk goods, etc.); (3) Communicate time-critical findings immediately (spoiled food requiring processing, medications with degrading potency, fuel before winter); (4) Create maps of salvage locations for systematic coverage. Community coordination multiplies individual effort.

</section>

## Planning Checklist

Before each salvage operation, use this checklist to ensure readiness and safety.

### Pre-Salvage

- [ ] Location identified and assessed (exterior safety check)
- [ ] Time window determined (hours available, daylight remaining)
- [ ] Partner(s) assigned with communication plan established
- [ ] Equipment gathered (bags, gloves, flashlight, tools, first aid)
- [ ] Emergency route planned (exit strategy if emergency occurs)
- [ ] Inventory expectations determined (priority order for carrying capacity)
- [ ] Weather assessed (no active storms, safe temperature range)
- [ ] Documentation plan (method to record findings)

### During Salvage

- [ ] Time limit enforced (don't extend beyond planned window)
- [ ] Partner communication maintained (regular check-ins)
- [ ] Contamination protocol followed (gloves, hand washing, separation)
- [ ] Priority system executed (time-critical items first)

## Seasonal Optimization Strategy

Aligning salvage operations with seasonal patterns maximizes efficiency and safety.

### Spring Strategy

Focus on heat-sensitive and moisture-sensitive items before summer heat accelerates degradation. Seeds need priority if planting season is imminent. Winter-stored items may have survived freeze-thaw cycles; assess stability. Access to outdoor locations improves; prioritize items exposed to weather. Target locations: gardens (seeds), attics (winter storage items), unheated structures.

### Summer Strategy

Heat accelerates degradation across all categories. Focus on sheltered indoor salvage. Access to outdoor locations is easiest; use good weather window for items that require outdoor salvage (structural recovery, bulk materials). Prioritize sealed, protected items; defer outdoor items with long shelf lives to cooler seasons. Target locations: basements (cool, well-preserved), commercial storage (climate controlled), underground areas.

### Fall Strategy

Temperatures moderating; prepare for winter. Salvage items for winter utility (insulation, heating materials, heavy clothing). Leather goods should be prioritized before winter humidity. Winter access will be difficult; gather items you'll need when weather restricts movement. Target locations: textile sources (clothing, blankets), waterproofing materials, fuel storage.

### Winter Strategy

Cold preservation is advantage for some items (frozen food, medications, seeds). Access is most difficult; limit operations to essential items. Use extended cold to advantage—items frozen are well-preserved. Outdoor salvage is hazardous; focus on accessible, nearby locations. Target locations: frozen food sources, sealed items suitable for cold storage, medicinal plants (harvesting from frozen state).

## Troubleshooting Common Challenges

### Problem: Uncertain About Item's Degradation Status

Solution: When in doubt, treat as more urgent than assessment suggests. Overestimating urgency costs extra time; underestimating costs lost material. Conservative approach prevents regret.

### Problem: Multiple Time-Critical Items Exceed Carrying Capacity

Solution: (1) Make multiple trips if location is safe and stable; (2) Prioritize within time-critical tier (liquid meds > fresh food > fuel); (3) Leave some items behind for later retrieval if location is accessible.

### Problem: Location Conditions Change (Weather, Structural Concerns, Safety)

Solution: Abandon operation immediately. Material is replaceable; injury or death is not. Return when conditions stabilize. Safety trump priorities every time.

### Problem: Contamination Suspected in Salvage Items

Solution: Isolate immediately from clean inventory. Process separately (wash, air-dry, inspect). Don't mix until confirmed safe. If severe contamination suspected, discard rather than risk spreading to other materials.

### Problem: Energy and Time Running Out Before Completing Planned Salvage

Solution: Prioritize what remains. Take highest-value or most-needed items. Leave detailed notes about remaining materials for next visit. Fatigue leads to accidents—stop before exhaustion.

## Safety Considerations

### Physical Injury Prevention

- Wear appropriate clothing (long sleeves, sturdy shoes, gloves)
- Use proper lifting technique (bend knees, keep back straight)
- Never attempt to move objects alone if they're heavy (ask for help)
- Stay aware of surroundings (watch for hazards, trip hazards, sharp objects)
- Use tools appropriately (tools are not toys; sharp and heavy)

### Health Hazard Prevention

- Respiratory protection in moldy or dusty environments
- Avoid inhalation of unknown fumes or dust
- Wash hands and face after every operation
- Shower when possible to remove contaminants
- Seek medical attention for cuts, bites, or unknown exposures

### Mental and Social Health

- Work in pairs or groups (safety and morale)
- Take breaks (avoid exhaustion and decision fatigue)
- Maintain community connection (share intelligence, build cooperation)
- Process stressful situations (salvage operations can be psychologically difficult)
