## Overview

Pressure canning is the only safe method for preserving low-acid foods (meat, fish, vegetables) without refrigeration. Heat alone cannot kill Clostridium botulinum spores unless temperatures exceed boiling (212°F at sea level); pressure cookers achieve 240–250°F at 10–15 PSI, sufficient to destroy botulinum spores and ensure long-term safety. This guide covers the microbiology of botulism, pressure canner operation, processing time calculations by altitude, equipment calibration and maintenance, processing specific foods, troubleshooting failures, and improvised high-pressure sterilization for other applications (medical equipment, water sterilization).

## Botulism Fundamentals

### Clostridium botulinum: The Hazard

Clostridium botulinum is an anaerobic (oxygen-hating) bacterium that produces botulinum toxin, one of the most potent toxins known. It is found in soil and water worldwide.

**Key properties:**
- **Spores**: Dormant forms survive harsh conditions (heat, cold, salt, acid).
- **Vegetative growth**: Occurs in anaerobic (oxygen-free) environments with pH >4.6 and temperature 40–120°F.
- **Toxin production**: Occurs during vegetative growth; toxin is heat-stable (survives boiling after production).

### Safety by pH and Acidity

**pH 4.6 cutoff** (critical):
- **pH ≤4.6** (acidic): Botulinum spores cannot germinate or produce toxin. Water bath canning (boiling, 212°F) is safe.
- **pH >4.6** (low-acid): Spores can germinate and produce toxin. Only pressure canning (240–250°F) kills spores reliably.

**Examples:**
- **High-acid foods** (water bath safe): Pickles, jams, jellies, fruit, tomato sauce with added acid.
- **Low-acid foods** (pressure canning required): Meat, fish, poultry, vegetables, beans, soups, stews.

:::danger
Pressure canning is mandatory for low-acid foods. Water bath canning or underprocessing can result in botulism, a potentially fatal foodborne illness. Botulinum toxin causes paralysis and death if untreated.
:::

### Botulism Symptoms

Foodborne botulism occurs 18–36 hours after consuming contaminated food. Symptoms:
- Blurred vision, difficulty swallowing, dry mouth.
- Muscle weakness, paralysis (descending: face → neck → arms → trunk → respiratory muscles).
- Respiratory failure (death if untreated).

**Treatment**: Immediate hospitalization for supportive care (mechanical ventilation) and antitoxin (stops progression but does not reverse existing paralysis).

## Pressure Canner Basics

### Types of Pressure Canners

**Dial-gauge canner:**
- Manual pressure control (turn burner up/down to maintain pressure on gauge).
- Dial gauge reading (0–15 PSI typical).
- Cost: $50–150.
- Disadvantage: Gauge loses calibration over time; requires annual testing.

**Weighted-gauge canner:**
- Automatic pressure regulation (weights sit on vent pipe; hiss audibly when pressure is correct).
- Three weights (5, 10, 15 PSI) added as needed.
- No gauge to calibrate; more reliable over time.
- Cost: $50–150.
- Advantage: Simple, accurate, durable.

**Large commercial canners:**
- Steam-jacketed or electric; 20+ quart capacity.
- For large operations; $500+.

**Choice for home use:** Weighted-gauge canners are more reliable; dial-gauge canners require annual gauge testing.

![Pressure canner cross-section showing components and pressure buildup](../assets/svgs/pressure-canning-sterilization-2.svg)

### Pressure vs. Temperature

Higher pressure = higher temperature = faster spore destruction.

| **PSI** | **Temperature (°F)** | **Typical Use** |
|---------|-------------------|-----------------|
| 5 | 227°F | Rare (some high-altitude locations) |
| 10 | 240°F | Mild climates; sea level to 1000 ft elevation |
| 15 | 250°F | Most US locations and higher elevations |

**Rule of thumb:** At sea level and up to 1000 ft elevation, process at 10 PSI. Above 1000 ft, increase to 15 PSI.

### Essential Equipment

- **Canner**: Pressure cooker designed for canning (NOT a stovetop pressure cooker; those lack venting and safety features).
- **Rack**: Prevents jars from touching canner bottom (holds jars off direct heat).
- **Jars**: Quart (32 oz) or pint (16 oz); Mason jars designed for canning.
- **Lids and bands**: Self-sealing lids (used once); metal bands (reusable).
- **Jar lifter**: Long-handled tool for removing hot jars safely.
- **Funnel**: Wide-mouth plastic funnel for filling jars.
- **Bubble remover**: Plastic stick for removing air bubbles from filled jars.
- **Magnetic lid lifter**: Retrieves lids from hot water before use.

## Pressure Gauge Calibration

Dial gauges lose accuracy over time and require annual calibration.

### Testing Dial Gauge Accuracy

**Method 1: Boiling water (at your elevation):**
1. Fill canner with water; bring to a boil with vent open (let steam escape for 10 minutes).
2. Close vent; allow pressure to rise on dial gauge.
3. At sea level, pressure should read 0 PSI when water is boiling (212°F). If dial reads 5 PSI, gauge is off by 5 PSI (dangerous; it will underprocess).
4. Correction: If gauge is off, add/subtract the difference from recipe times (e.g., if gauge reads 5 PSI at boiling, subtract 5 PSI from recipe pressure).

**Method 2: Submitting to a testing center:**
- Cooperative Extension offices (county or state) test gauges annually for free or nominal fee (~$3–5).
- Mail or drop off dial gauge; replaced if error exceeds ±1 PSI.

**Recommendation:** Check gauge annually. If error is >±1 PSI, have it serviced or replaced.

:::warning
An inaccurate dial gauge is a serious food safety risk. Underprocessing (lower pressure than required) leaves botulinum spores alive; overprocessing (higher pressure) wastes time and fuel but does not harm safety.
:::

<section id="processing-times">

## Processing Times by Altitude

Processing time increases with altitude (lower atmospheric pressure).

### USDA Pressure Adjustment Table

The USDA Complete Guide to Home Canning specifies pressure adjustments for altitude. Use the dial gauge PSI (for dial-gauge canners) or weighted gauge PSI (for weighted-gauge canners) that corresponds to your elevation.

| **Elevation (ft)** | **Dial Gauge PSI** | **Weighted Gauge PSI** |
|---|---|---|
| 0–1,000 | 10 | 10 |
| 1,001–2,000 | 11 | 15 |
| 2,001–4,000 | 12 | 15 |
| 4,001–6,000 | 13 | 15 |
| 6,001–8,000 | 14 | 15 |
| 8,001+ | 15 | 15 |

**Processing Time Adjustment:** USDA guidance states that when canning at altitude, process time increases as pressure decreases. Consult tested recipes for specific time adjustments based on food type and jar size. For dial-gauge canners above 2,000 ft, add 1 PSI per 1,000 feet of elevation above 2,000 ft. Refer to the USDA Complete Guide to Home Canning for detailed altitude-specific processing times for each food category.

:::danger
**ALTITUDE BOTULISM RISK:** Insufficient pressure at altitude allows Clostridium botulinum spores to survive processing. At higher elevations, atmospheric pressure is lower, which means water boils at a lower temperature — a canner at 10 PSI produces lower internal temperatures than at sea level. Always increase pressure according to the table above: use at least 11 PSI (dial gauge) or 15 PSI (weighted gauge) above 1,000 ft. As a general rule, increase pressure by 1 PSI per 1,000 feet above 2,000 ft elevation for dial gauges. Failure to adjust pressure for altitude causes botulism — a potentially fatal toxin that is colorless, odorless, and undetectable by visual inspection of the jar.
:::

**Example**: Recipe calls for 75 minutes at 10 PSI at sea level. At 3000 ft elevation:
- Use 12 PSI on dial gauge (or 15 PSI on weighted gauge).
- Time: 75 × 1.1 = 82.5 minutes (round to 85 minutes).

**Determine your elevation:**
- Topographic map, GPS, smartphone app, or weather station.
- NOAA or USGS websites (search "[your town] elevation").

### Processing Time Table (Examples, 10 PSI, Sea Level)

| **Food** | **Jar Size** | **Raw/Hot Pack** | **Time** |
|---------|-----------|---|---|
| **Meat** (ground, chunks) | Pint | Hot | 75 min |
| | Quart | Hot | 90 min |
| **Poultry** (pieces, chunks) | Pint | Hot | 75 min |
| | Quart | Hot | 90 min |
| **Fish** (plain, no liquid) | Pint | Hot | 100 min |
| | Quart | Hot | 110 min |
| **Green beans** | Pint | Hot | 20 min |
| | Quart | Hot | 25 min |
| **Carrots** | Pint | Hot | 25 min |
| | Quart | Hot | 30 min |
| **Corn** | Pint | Hot | 55 min |
| | Quart | Hot | 85 min |
| **Peas** | Pint | Hot | 40 min |
| | Quart | Hot | 50 min |

**Key points:**
- Use times from USDA or "Ball Blue Book" (official reference).
- Hot pack (food heated before filling jars) is faster than raw pack.
- Quarts take longer than pints (more heat must penetrate center).
- Cold (high) elevations require longer times.

## Step-by-Step Canning Process

### Preparation

1. **Inspect jars**: Look for cracks or chips; discard damaged jars.
2. **Prepare jars**: Wash in hot soapy water; rinse. Keep warm (in hot water or oven at 180°F).
3. **Prepare lids**: Pour boiling water over lids and bands; let soften (~5 minutes). Don't boil (damages sealing compound).
4. **Prepare canner**: Fill with 2–3 inches of water; place rack inside. Bring to boil.

### Filling Jars

1. **Prepare food**: Cook/heat food as recipe directs (hot pack = food is hot when jarred).
2. **Fill jars**: Using wide-mouth funnel, ladle hot food into warm jars. Leave headspace:
   - **Pint jars**: 1 inch headspace (top of liquid should be 1 inch below jar rim).
   - **Quart jars**: 1.25 inches headspace.
3. **Remove air bubbles**: Insert bubble remover (plastic stick) along inside of jar, moving it in a circle to release trapped air.
4. **Wipe rims**: Use damp cloth to wipe jar rims clean (any food residue prevents seal).
5. **Add lids**: Place hot lid (from water) on jar; screw band on finger-tight (not over-tightened).

### Processing (Pressure Canning)

1. **Load jars**: Place filled jars on rack in canner, standing upright. Do not let jars touch each other or canner walls.
2. **Close vent**: Close canner lid; ensure gasket (rubber seal) is clean and seated.
3. **Vent the canner** (critical): Open vent pipe (petcock); let steam escape for 10 minutes. This removes air from the canner (air prevents proper pressure/temperature).
4. **Close vent**: After 10 minutes, close vent pipe.
5. **Bring to pressure**: Increase heat; watch dial gauge (or listen for weight to start hissing). When gauge reaches target PSI (adjusted for elevation), start timing.
6. **Maintain pressure**: Keep pressure stable for entire processing time. Adjust burner heat to prevent pressure from fluctuating (if pressure drops, processing time begins over).
7. **End processing**: When time is complete, turn off burner and let canner cool naturally (15–30 minutes). Do NOT lower pressure rapidly (risk of seal failure).
8. **Remove jars**: When canner is cool enough to touch (not hot), open vent slowly, then open lid (open lid away from face to avoid steam).
9. **Cool jars**: Remove jars with jar lifter; place on cloth or wire rack at room temperature. Do NOT retighten bands.

### Cooling and Sealing

- **Sealing**: As jars cool, lids seal with a "pop" sound (center of lid moves downward). This indicates a vacuum seal formed.
- **Timing**: Cooling takes 12–24 hours. Seals form during this period; do not disturb jars.
- **Testing seals**: After 12–24 hours, press center of each lid. If it springs back up, seal failed; jars must be reprocessed or refrigerated.

### Storage

- **Sealed jars**: Remove bands, wash jars, label with date and contents. Store in a cool, dark place (50–70°F ideal).
- **Shelf life**: 1–2 years at room temperature; 3–5 years if stored cool and dark.
- **Unsealed jars**: Discard or refrigerate and use within 2 weeks.

:::info-box
The "pop" sound during cooling is a seal forming as the jar cools and internal pressure drops below atmospheric, drawing the lid tight. This is a good sign of proper processing.
:::

<section id="troubleshooting">

## Troubleshooting Common Problems

### Seal Failure (Lid Springs Back)

**Causes:**
- Underprocessing (pressure too low or time too short).
- Food residue on jar rim (prevents seal).
- Defective jars or lids.
- Overheated jars before processing (dissolved gases).

**Solution:**
1. Reprocess immediately: Remove lid, reheat food, add fresh hot lid, reprocess.
2. Or: Cool, refrigerate, and use within 2 weeks.

### Siphoning (Liquid Leaks During Processing)

**Causes:**
- Overfilled jars (too little headspace).
- Pressure increased too rapidly (internal pressure pushes liquid out).
- Jars not vented properly (air in jars expands during heating).

**Solution:**
- Vent canner for 10 minutes before closing vent (releases air).
- Use correct headspace (1–1.25 inches).
- Increase pressure gradually (adjust heat slowly).
- Do not reuse siphoned jars; discard or refrigerate.

### Underprocessing (Spores Survive)

**Causes:**
- Pressure too low (dial gauge inaccurate; PSI too low for altitude).
- Time too short.
- Large jar size with insufficient heat penetration.

**Risk:** Botulinum spores survive; toxin forms during storage. Jar may appear normal; toxin is colorless, odorless.

**Prevention:**
- Verify dial gauge accuracy annually.
- Use correct PSI and time for elevation.
- Follow USDA times, not improvised recipes.
- Do NOT use water bath canning for low-acid foods.

:::danger
Underprocessed jars are invisible hazards. Botulism may develop during storage without obvious spoilage signs. Never guess on processing times; always use tested recipes.
:::

### Darkening (Food Discoloration)

**Causes:**
- Oxidation during cooking/storage.
- Natural chemical reactions in food (harmless).

**Impact:** Appearance is poor but food is safe. Prevents only if jars are not sealed; if sealed, oxidation is minimal.

### Spurting When Opening Jar

**Cause:**
- Some spoilage or gas production during storage (indicates underprocessing or seal failure during storage).

**Action:** Discard jar; do not taste or smell.

## Processing Specific Low-Acid Foods

### Meat (Ground or Chunks)

**Preparation:**
1. Use fresh meat, free of excess fat (fat can form seal barriers).
2. Cook meat until done (no pink).
3. Cut into chunks if using whole meat pieces.
4. Pack hot into jars; cover with boiling broth (leaving 1.25 inches headspace).

**Processing:**
- Pints: 75 minutes at 10 PSI.
- Quarts: 90 minutes at 10 PSI.

### Fish

**Preparation:**
1. Use fresh, high-quality fish.
2. Pack raw into jars (do not cook beforehand).
3. Add 1/2 tsp salt per pint (optional, for flavor).
4. No liquid added (fish releases its own).
5. Leave 1.25 inches headspace.

**Processing:**
- Pints: 100 minutes at 10 PSI.
- Quarts: 110 minutes at 10 PSI.

### Vegetables (Green Beans, Carrots, etc.)

**Preparation:**
1. Use young, tender vegetables.
2. Wash, trim, cut into uniform pieces.
3. Hot pack: Boil vegetables 2–5 minutes; pack hot with boiling water.
4. Leave 1 inch headspace.

**Processing (hot pack):**
- Green beans: Pints 20 min, Quarts 25 min @ 10 PSI.
- Carrots: Pints 25 min, Quarts 30 min @ 10 PSI.

### Corn (Whole Kernel)

**Preparation:**
1. Use fresh corn, kernel-cut from cob.
2. Boil 3 minutes; pack hot with boiling liquid (not cob cooking liquid; use fresh water or broth).
3. Leave 1 inch headspace.

**Processing:**
- Pints: 55 minutes @ 10 PSI.
- Quarts: 85 minutes @ 10 PSI.

## Improvised High-Pressure Sterilization

Pressure canning principles apply to other sterilization needs (medical equipment, water supplies, etc.). Improvised systems are risky but possible in emergency conditions.

### Improvised Pressure Vessel (Not Recommended)

**Materials:**
- Heavy-duty pressure cooker or steel drum with fitted top.
- Pressure gauge (manometer or improvised water column).
- Safety relief valve (critical; prevents over-pressurization).
- Thermometer.

**Safety critical warning:**
- Over-pressurization risks explosion (potentially fatal).
- Only attempt if trained in pressure vessel operation.
- Always include pressure relief valve.

**Water column manometer** (improvised pressure measurement):
- Fill a tube with water; height equals water pressure.
- 1 PSI = 2.31 feet of water column (27.7 inches).
- 10 PSI = 23 feet; 15 PSI = 35 feet (very tall; impractical).
- Alternative: Mercury column (1 PSI = 2 inches mercury), but mercury is toxic.

### Water Sterilization via Pressure/Temperature

Pressure-boil water at 15 PSI, 250°F for 30 minutes to sterilize (kills bacteria, viruses, some parasites; not all chemical contaminants).

**Limitations:**
- Does not remove chemical contaminants (arsenic, nitrates, pesticides).
- Does not kill prions (infectious proteins causing diseases like CJD).
- Pressure sterilization is reliable only in equipment designed for it.

### Alternative: Boiling

Simple boiling (rolling boil for 1 minute at sea level; 3 minutes above 6500 ft) kills most pathogens but not spores. Use for drinking water safety when pressure is not available.

## Equipment Maintenance and Safety

### Canner Gasket (Rubber Seal)

**Maintenance:**
- Inspect before each use (look for cracks, discoloration, hardening).
- Clean with warm soapy water; dry completely.
- If gasket is hard, cracked, or does not seat properly, replace (cost ~$10–20).

**Replacement frequency:** Every 2–5 years (depending on use and material).

### Vent Pipe Cleaning

**Maintenance:**
- After each use, clear vent pipe of food debris (blockage prevents pressure equalization).
- Use a thin wire or pipe cleaner to clear obstruction.
- Boil vent pipe briefly to remove residue.

### Pressure Gauge (Dial)

**Maintenance:**
- Check calibration annually (see "Gauge Calibration" section).
- Keep clean (tap gently to remove debris).
- If gauge is cracked, replace immediately.

### Jar Lids and Bands

**Reusability:**
- **Lids**: Single-use only (sealing compound is consumed after one use).
- **Bands**: Reusable; wash after use, dry thoroughly. Replace if bent or rusted.

**Storage:**
- Keep new lids in a cool, dry place (moisture degrades sealing compound).
- Discard lids if sealing compound is discolored or hardened.

### Safety Precautions

- **Never open canner under pressure**: Risk of explosion/scalding.
- **Never force canner lid open**: Wait for pressure to equalize; vent pipe can be opened to release pressure slowly.
- **Always vent for 10 minutes** before closing vent (releases air; ensures proper pressure).
- **Never leave canner unattended** during processing (fire/pressure hazard).
- **Keep children and pets away** from canning area (risk of scalding).

## Summary Checklist

1. **Understand the hazard**: Botulinum spores are deadly; pressure canning is the only safe method for low-acid foods.
2. **Use proper equipment**: Pressure canner (not pressure cooker), validated recipes, correct jars/lids.
3. **Vent the canner** for 10 minutes before closing vent (removes air).
4. **Maintain pressure** throughout processing time; do not allow pressure to drop.
5. **Use correct processing times** based on jar size, altitude, and food type.
6. **Check dial gauge annually** for calibration accuracy.
7. **Cool jars slowly** after processing; seals form during cooling.
8. **Test seals** after 12–24 hours; discard or reprocess if seal failed.
9. **Store sealed jars** in cool, dark place; use within 1–2 years.
10. **Follow USDA/tested recipes only**: Never guess on processing times or methods.

Pressure canning is safe, reliable, and essential for preserving meat and vegetables without refrigeration. Proper technique and equipment maintenance ensure both safety and quality.
