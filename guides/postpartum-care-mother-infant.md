## Overview

The postpartum period is a critical and dangerous time for both mother and newborn. In the first 24 hours, uterine atony and hemorrhage cause most direct maternal deaths. In the first week, infection and overwhelming complications claim more lives than any other time in pregnancy and birth. Yet most postpartum deaths are preventable with basic monitoring, early recognition of danger signs, and prompt intervention.

This guide provides protocols for community health workers and birth attendants managing mother and infant in low-resource settings—without hospital backup. Immediate recognition of red flags and knowledge of when to seek help can mean the difference between life and death.

**Key principle:** Postpartum care is not an afterthought. The "fourth trimester" (first 12 weeks) requires as much vigilance as pregnancy and labor.

<section id="immediate-postpartum-first-2-hours">

## Immediate Postpartum (First 2 Hours)

The first 2 hours after delivery are the golden window for preventing postpartum hemorrhage and assessing newborn viability.

### Maternal Management (First 2 Hours)

**Position & Observation:**
- Keep mother supine or semi-upright for the first 30 minutes
- Check uterine firmness every 5 minutes: the fundus should be firm (hard like a fist), at or just below umbilicus level
- If the fundus is soft or boggy, perform immediate uterine massage: place one hand on lower abdomen, other hand inside vagina at the cervix, massage fundus in circular motions until firm (30 seconds to 2 minutes)
- Empty the bladder: a full bladder prevents uterine contraction and causes hemorrhage

**Bleeding Assessment:**
- Estimate blood loss by weighing soaked pads/cloths: 1 gram saturated pad = ~1 mL blood
- Normal vaginal bleeding in first 2 hours: up to 500 mL
- Weigh blood loss against maternal weight and hemoglobin status

:::warning
**Red flags for hemorrhage (first 2 hours):**
- Fundus soft, boggy, or displaced upward (suggests blood clot in uterus)
- Soaking more than one pad per 15 minutes
- Bright red, continuous flow that does not slow
- Maternal dizziness, rapid pulse (>110), cold skin, confusion
- Visible blood clots larger than a plum
:::

**Third Stage Management (if not yet complete):**
- Do NOT pull on umbilical cord aggressively before delivery of placenta—this risks uterine inversion
- When placenta delivers, place it in a clean container and inspect: all lobes should be present, no tears in membranes
- If placenta has not delivered within 30 minutes of infant delivery, this is retained placenta—urgent action needed (see <a href="../gynecological-emergencies-womens-health.html">Gynecological Emergencies & Women's Health</a>)

**Early Bonding & Breastfeeding:**
- Immediate skin-to-skin contact (naked infant on mother's bare chest) for at least 1 hour
- This stabilizes infant temperature, reduces stress, and stimulates mother's oxytocin (promotes uterine contraction)
- Offer breast within first 30 minutes if mother is alert and willing

### Newborn Assessment (First 2 Hours)

**APGAR Score (at 1 minute and 5 minutes):**

| Component | Score 0 | Score 1 | Score 2 |
|-----------|---------|---------|---------|
| **A**ppearance (skin color) | Blue, pale | Body pink, extremities blue | Completely pink |
| **P**ulse (heart rate) | Absent | <100 bpm | >100 bpm |
| **G**rimace (reflex irritability/cry) | No response | Weak cry, grimace | Strong cry |
| **A**ctivity (muscle tone) | Limp, flaccid | Some flexion | Active motion, good tone |
| **R**espiration (breathing effort) | Absent | Weak, irregular | Strong, regular |

**Scoring:** 7–10 = Normal (minimal intervention). 4–6 = Moderate distress (monitor closely, may need assistance). 0–3 = Severe distress (resuscitation needed).

**Immediate Assessment:**
- Dry infant thoroughly with clean, warm cloth
- Place skin-to-skin with mother immediately (passive external warming)
- If breathing irregular or grunting, dry again and stimulate gently by rubbing back
- Check for major birth defects: cleft lip/palate, limb position, abdominal distention, ambiguous genitalia
- Count respiratory rate: normal is 40–60 breaths/min. Rates >60 suggest respiratory distress

:::tip
**Temperature control is critical:** Newborns lose heat 4× faster than adults. Keep infant against mother's skin, cover with dry cloth, keep head covered. Hypothermia (T <36.5°C) is a leading cause of neonatal death in low-resource settings.
:::

**Cord Clamping & Cutting:**
- Clamp cord 1 to 3 minutes after delivery (delayed clamping allows placental blood to transfuse infant, boosting iron stores)
- Use two clamps 6 inches apart, or tie with clean string
- Cut between clamps with sterile scissors
- Do NOT apply anything to the cord stump yet (keep it dry and open to air)

**Fundal Assessment Technique:**
1. Have mother void bladder first
2. Place one hand on lower abdomen to stabilize
3. With other hand, palpate just below umbilicus and trace downward until you feel the firm mass of the uterus
4. Measure the distance from umbilicus to fundal top (in cm); this decreases ~1 cm/day
5. At day 2: fundus should be 1 cm below umbilicus
6. At day 7: fundus should be 2 cm above pubic symphysis (palpable only at lower abdomen)
7. At day 14: fundus should be non-palpable

### Lochia (Postpartum Bleeding) Patterns

| Timeline | Expected Bleeding | Odor | Composition | Red Flag |
|----------|-------------------|------|-------------|----------|
| Days 1–3 | Heavy (changing 1–2 pads/hr) | Meaty, not foul | Red blood, clots <plum size | Clots >plum, continuous soaking, foul odor |
| Days 4–7 | Moderate (1 pad per few hrs) | Metallic | Red-brown, fewer clots | Bright red again, heavy flow, fever |
| Days 8–21 | Light (spotting) | Metallic | Brown-yellow | Increased flow, green discharge |
| After day 21 | Minimal or stops | None | Clear/serous | Any fresh bleeding after apparent stopping |

:::warning
**Postpartum hemorrhage red flags (days 1–42):**
- Heavy vaginal bleeding (>1 pad soaked per hour for >2 hours) at any time
- Sudden gush of blood or large clots (>plum-sized)
- Foul-smelling lochia (suggests infection/retained placenta)
- Fundus becomes soft, boggy, or rises after previously contracting
- Signs of anemia: dizziness, shortness of breath, palpitations, extreme fatigue, pale mucous membranes
:::

**Management of Excessive Bleeding:**
- Perform uterine massage immediately
- Ensure bladder is empty
- Encourage frequent breastfeeding (oxytocin promotes contraction)
- Administer ergot alkaloid if available (methylergonovine 0.2 mg IM, or ergotamine tablet 0.5 mg SL—do NOT use if hypertensive)
- If bleeding does not slow, transport urgently for uterine evacuation or transfusion

<section id="postpartum-hemorrhage-prevention-management">

## Postpartum Hemorrhage Prevention & Management

Postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) is the leading direct cause of maternal death globally. Most deaths occur within 24 hours and are preventable.

### Prevention Strategies

**Active Management of Third Stage:**
1. Administer uterotonic immediately after infant delivery (before placenta):
   - Oxytocin 10 IU IM (preferred), or
   - Ergotamine 0.5 mg sublingual, or
   - Misoprostol 600 mcg oral or rectal (if others unavailable)
2. Controlled cord traction: hold uterine fundus stable, apply gentle downward traction on cord once placenta separates (do NOT pull before separation—risk of inversion)
3. Fundal massage after placenta delivery until firm

**Ongoing Prevention (first 6 weeks):**
- Keep bladder empty (full bladder prevents contraction)
- Encourage frequent, effective breastfeeding (stimulates oxytocin)
- Inspect lochia and fundus daily
- Manage anemia: iron supplementation 30–60 mg elemental iron daily, dietary iron (red meat, legumes, leafy greens)
- Adequate nutrition and hydration

### Severity and Immediate Response

**Primary PPH (within 24 hours):**

:::warning
**If bleeding is >500 mL total or signs of shock appear:**
1. Keep mother flat or in left lateral decubitus position
2. Start two large-bore IV lines (18-20G) if available; begin normal saline infusion rapidly
3. Perform firm, continuous uterine massage until fundus becomes hard
4. Empty bladder (catheter if unable to void)
5. Administer oxytocin 10 IU IM (or IV drip if IV access available: 20 IU in 1 L normal saline, infuse 200 mL/min)
6. If bleeding continues: ergotamine 0.5 mg SL or IM (NOT if hypertensive)
7. If access to antibiotics: start broad-spectrum (cefazolin 1 g IV or ceftriaxone 1 g IM)
8. **Arrange urgent evacuation for blood transfusion, uterine packing, or surgical evacuation**
9. Monitor vital signs every 15 minutes
:::

**Secondary PPH (after 24 hours to 6 weeks):**
Usually caused by infection, retained products, or subinvolution (slow shrinking). See Infection section. Urgent evacuation may be needed for curettage.

### Risk Factors for PPH

Identify these before delivery when possible:
- Multiparity (>5 births): increased atony risk
- Rapid or prolonged labor
- Large infant (macrosomia)
- Multiple pregnancy
- Polyhydramnios (excessive amniotic fluid)
- Fever or infection during labor
- Previous PPH

Women with risk factors need closer monitoring, ready access to uterotonics, and low threshold for evacuation.

## Infection Signs & Prevention

Postpartum sepsis can develop rapidly and is often missed because fever is attributed to "normal postpartum" changes. Infection kills more women beyond 24 hours postpartum than hemorrhage.

### Prevention: Hygiene & Asepsis

**Immediate postpartum (first 48 hours):**
- Use clean/sterile gloves for all vaginal exams
- Clean perineum with clean water and soap after each void and bowel movement; pat dry
- Change sanitary pads frequently; use clean, not reusable pads if possible (boil and dry if reusable)
- Ensure adequate clean water for bathing; delay full baths if perineal lacerations until bleeding slows
- Encourage short showers instead if feasible

**Ongoing (first 6 weeks):**
- Continue perineal hygiene
- Avoid vaginal douches
- Do not insert tampons (use pads only)
- Change pads after urination and defecation
- Avoid sexual intercourse until lochia stops (day 21) and perineal healing complete
- Encourage hand-washing before handling baby and before eating

**Breast Care:**
- Wash breasts with water only (not soap, which dries skin)
- Air-dry nipples after each feeding
- Inspect for cracks or blisters; treat with lanolin or expressed breast milk
- If mother is unwell, express and feed via clean cup/spoon rather than direct feeding (prevents infant exposure if not yet diagnosed with infection)

### Infection Red Flags

:::warning
**Seek urgent medical evaluation if mother has any of:**
- **Temperature >38.5°C** on a single reading or >38°C on two separate readings 4+ hours apart
- **Foul-smelling lochia** (putrid, not just "meaty" smell)
- **Abdominal pain** beyond normal afterpains (more than mild cramping)
- **Wound discharge:** pus, opening of perineal tear or cesarean incision, redness, warmth, swelling
- **Breast redness, hardness, or abscess** (localized warmth, swelling, fluctuance)
- **Calf pain, swelling, or warmth** (thrombophlebitis risk)
- **Severe headache, visual changes, or confusion** (sepsis/encephalitis)
- **Rapid pulse (>110 bpm) without explanation** or persistent tachypnea (>25 breaths/min)
- **Maternal confusion, lethargy, or unresponsiveness**
:::

### Common Postpartum Infections

**Endometritis (infection of uterine lining):**
- Most common postpartum infection
- Onset: typically days 2–7
- Signs: fever, foul lochia, lower abdominal tenderness, fundal tenderness
- Risk factors: prolonged labor, repeated vaginal exams, manual removal of placenta, cesarean delivery
- Treatment: broad-spectrum antibiotics (cefazolin + gentamicin, or cefoxitin). Transport for IV antibiotics if systemic signs.

**Perineal Wound Infection (episiotomy/tear):**
- Onset: days 3–10
- Signs: redness, swelling, purulent discharge, separation of wound edges, fever
- Inspect wound daily under good light
- Treatment: clean twice daily with normal saline, keep dry. Antibiotics if systemic signs. May need surgical drainage if abscess forms.

**Mastitis (breast infection):**
- Onset: typically days 3–14
- Signs: localized breast redness, hardness, heat, pain; may have fever and flulike symptoms
- Cause: milk duct obstruction + bacterial colonization (usually Staphylococcus aureus)
- Prevention: frequent feeding, correct latch, expression of breast if engorgement
- Treatment: continue breastfeeding (safe for infant), warm compresses, analgesics. Antibiotics if fever or not resolving in 24–48 hours.

**Urinary Tract Infection (UTI) / Cystitis:**
- Onset: anytime in first 6 weeks
- Signs: dysuria (painful urination), frequency, suprapubic tenderness, fever (if pyelonephritis)
- Risk factors: catheterization, prolonged catheter use, incomplete bladder emptying
- Treatment: increased hydration, analgesics, antibiotics if fever or symptoms persist >48 hours

## Breastfeeding Initiation & Support

Exclusive breastfeeding is the gold standard for infant nutrition and provides immunological protection—critical in low-resource settings with contaminated water. Most breastfeeding problems are preventable or treatable with early intervention.

### First Latch (Within 30 Minutes)

**Positioning mother:**
- Ensure mother is comfortable: upright, semi-recumbent, or side-lying depending on delivery type
- Use pillows for support
- Ensure mother is calm and not in acute pain

**Correct Latch:**
1. Mother holds breast: thumb on top, fingers underneath, hand off the areola
2. Infant's mouth opens wide (gaping mouth like a yawn)
3. Bring infant's lower lip first to breast, aiming nipple upward toward roof of mouth
4. Entire areola (dark ring around nipple) should be in infant's mouth, not just nipple
5. Infant's chin touches breast, nose clears breast for breathing
6. Infant's cheeks are rounded (not dimpled), sucking is strong and audible

**Signs of Good Latch:**
- Infant's mouth covers most of areola
- No pain for mother (mild sensation only, no pinching)
- Audible swallowing (after several sucks)
- Milk visible in infant's mouth or dripping from opposite breast
- Infant's head tilted slightly back, neck extended

:::tip
**If initial latch is difficult:** Do not force it. Allow infant to rest, skin-to-skin. Try again in 5–10 minutes. Patience is key—many infants latch successfully on the second or third attempt.
:::

### Feeding Frequency and Duration

- Newborns should feed 8–12 times per 24 hours in first days
- Each side for 15–20 minutes, or until infant releases the breast naturally
- Feed on demand in the first week; do not restrict frequency
- Most newborns lose 7–10% body weight by day 3 (normal), regain by day 10

### Common Breastfeeding Problems & Solutions

| Problem | Signs | Solution |
|---------|-------|----------|
| Engorgement | Breasts swollen, hard, red, painful | Express milk manually or with pump to soften. Warm compress before feeding. Frequent feeding. Cabbage leaf compress between feedings. |
| Sore nipples | Pain, cracking, bleeding | Check latch (most common cause). Lanolin after each feeding. Express and feed via cup if too painful. Air-dry nipples. |
| Plugged duct | Localized breast lump, pain | Warm compress, massage area toward nipple, frequent feeding from that side. Position infant's chin toward the lump. |
| Low milk supply | Infant weight loss >10%, few wet diapers | Increase feeding frequency to 10–12 times/day. Ensure latch is correct. Manual expression after feeds. Adequate mother nutrition and hydration. |
| Flat or inverted nipples | Nipple does not protrude | Use nipple shield or breast shells (worn inside bra between feeds) to draw out nipple. Manual expression may be needed initially. |

### Exclusive Breastfeeding

- No water, formula, or other foods for first 6 months if possible
- Even small amounts of formula reduce exclusive breastfeeding benefits
- In water-insecure settings, breastfeeding alone is safest (no contaminated water needed)
- Vitamin D supplementation (400 IU daily) if no sun exposure; vitamin K prophylaxis at birth if available

:::warning
**Stop breastfeeding and seek urgent care if:**
- Mother has fever >39°C with breast symptoms (possible abscess)
- Infant has difficulty swallowing, weak suck, or respiratory distress
- Infant has projectile vomiting after every feed
- Infant is lethargic, difficult to wake, or not feeding
:::

## Neonatal Assessment (APGAR, Reflexes)

Systematic assessment of the newborn in the first minutes and hours detects life-threatening problems early.

### APGAR Score (at 1 min and 5 min)

(See Immediate Postpartum section for full table and scoring.)

**Interpretation:**
- **7–10:** Vigorous infant, minimal intervention usually needed. Reassess at 5 minutes.
- **4–6:** Moderate distress. Keep in warm, dry environment. Dry skin, stimulate by rubbing back and feet. Keep airway clear. Reassess frequently.
- **0–3:** Severe distress / non-responsive. Prepare for resuscitation: suction mouth and nose, provide gentle stimulation, dry and warm, position on back with head slightly extended, prepare to give positive pressure ventilation if available.

If APGAR remains 0–3 at 5 minutes, outlook is poor without advanced resuscitation capability. Focus on comfort care and supporting parents.

### Reflex Assessment (First Hour)

Primitive reflexes indicate intact nervous system. Absence may suggest birth injury or serious illness.

| Reflex | How to Elicit | Normal Response | Abnormality |
|--------|---------------|-----------------|-------------|
| **Moro** | Sudden noise, drop head back ~5 cm | Arms spread, fingers extend, then arms come back, may cry | Absent (neurological injury), one-sided (brachial plexus injury) |
| **Rooting** | Stroke cheek near mouth | Head turns toward touch, mouth opens | Absent on one side (trigeminal nerve, facial nerve) |
| **Sucking** | Place finger in mouth | Strong suck | Weak or absent (CNS depression, nerve injury) |
| **Grasp** | Place finger in palm | Strong grip, infant may be lifted briefly | Weak or absent (neurological injury) |
| **Stepping** | Hold infant upright, feet touching surface | Alternating stepping motion | Rigid or floppy (tone abnormality) |
| **Plantar response** | Stroke sole of foot from heel toward toes | Toes curl downward | Toes extend upward (Babinski sign—normal in newborns but check bilaterally) |

**Tone Assessment:**
- Newborn should have flexed posture at rest (arms and legs bent)
- When head is moved side to side, infant should follow briefly
- Extreme floppiness (hypotonia) or extreme stiffness (hypertonia) is abnormal

## Newborn Danger Signs

Many newborn emergencies develop within the first 48 hours. Early recognition can be lifesaving.

:::warning
**Seek urgent medical evaluation immediately if infant has:**

**Breathing Problems:**
- Respiratory rate >60 breaths/min at rest (tachypnea) or <30 breaths/min (bradypnea)
- Grunting sound with each breath (sign of respiratory distress)
- Flaring of nostrils with each breath
- Retractions: skin pulls in around ribs or above collarbones with each breath
- Apnea: infant stops breathing for >15 seconds (cyanosis may or may not be visible)
- Inability to cry or cry is very weak

**Circulation & Perfusion:**
- Cyanosis (blue/purple discoloration) that does not resolve with drying and warming
- Pallor (very pale skin, white appearance)
- Mottled skin (lacy red-and-white pattern) that persists
- Capillary refill >3 seconds (press fingernail, color returns slowly)
- Weak or absent pulses
- Very fast heart rate (>160 bpm) or slow (<120 bpm)

**Feeding & Hydration:**
- Refuses to feed or cannot coordinate suck-swallow
- Projectile vomiting after feeds
- Excessive drowsiness (difficult to wake)
- Seizures or twitching
- Weak cry or "whimper"

**Temperature Instability:**
- Temperature <36°C (hypothermia)—most critical in first hours
- Temperature >38.5°C (fever in first 48 hours is concerning for sepsis)

**Skin & Umbilicus:**
- Pustules, rash, or areas of breakdown (infection risk)
- Umbilical cord stump red, swollen, draining pus or foul odor (omphalitis)
- Bleeding from umbilicus that does not stop with gentle pressure for 10 minutes

**Abdomen:**
- Distention, hardness, or visible loops of bowel
- Vomiting with inability to feed
- No stool within first 24 hours OR excessive diarrhea
- No urine within first 48 hours or very few wet diapers

**Level of Consciousness:**
- Extreme lethargy, inability to wake
- Irritability with high-pitched cry (possible meningitis)
- Seizures or tremors
- Poor response to stimulation
:::

### Danger Sign Follow-Up

If any danger sign is noted:
1. Keep infant warm (skin-to-skin or blanket)
2. Do NOT delay seeking medical help while trying home remedies
3. Document what was observed and when
4. If transport is necessary, keep infant warm and continue feeding attempts if possible
5. If transport impossible, focus on comfort measures and monitoring

## Jaundice Detection & Management

Jaundice (yellowish skin) is common in newborns but can indicate serious disease. Severe untreated jaundice causes bilirubin encephalopathy and permanent brain damage.

### Physiological Jaundice vs. Pathological

**Physiological Jaundice:**
- Appears after 24 hours (usually days 2–5)
- Caused by normal breakdown of fetal hemoglobin
- Resolves by 2 weeks in term infants
- Bilirubin rises slowly, <0.2 mg/dL per hour
- No other signs of illness

**Pathological Jaundice (requires urgent evaluation):**
- Appears in first 24 hours
- Rises rapidly (>0.2 mg/dL per hour)
- Associated with poor feeding, lethargy, or fever
- History of maternal infection, hemolytic disease, or sepsis risk

### Detection Method (Visual Assessment)

In first 24–48 hours, perform serial visual assessments:

1. **Blanch test:** Press thumb on baby's nose or forehead. Release and observe color return.
   - If yellow color remains after blanching, jaundice is present
   - If color returns to normal, no clinical jaundice yet

2. **Progression of jaundice:** Use Kramer's zones to estimate severity
   - Zone 1 (face, trunk): mild jaundice
   - Zone 2 (arms, thighs to knees): moderate
   - Zone 3 (below knees, below elbows): significant
   - Zone 4 (soles, palms): severe (risk of bilirubin encephalopathy)

3. **Photo-period:** If jaundice appears in first 24 hours or extends to zones 3–4, urgent evaluation and treatment are needed

### Risk Stratification by Age & Bilirubin Level

(Use 24, 48, 72, and 96-hour bilirubin levels if available; otherwise use visual assessment and risk factors)

**High-risk factors:**
- Isoimmune hemolytic disease (Rh incompatibility, ABO incompatibility)
- Asphyxia, sepsis
- Significant lethargy or temperature instability
- Sibling with severe neonatal jaundice
- Exclusive breastfeeding with poor latch/weight loss

### Management of Breastfeeding Jaundice

**Caused by inadequate milk transfer (not breast milk jaundice—which is different):**
- Ensure correct latch and frequent feeds (at least 10–12 times per 24 hours)
- Assess for wet diapers (at least 6 per 24 hours by day 4) and stools (at least 3–4 per 24 hours)
- Monitor infant weight loss; should not exceed 10% by day 3
- If weight loss >10% or inadequate feeds, express breast milk and supplement via cup, dropper, or spoon
- After each supplemental feed, place infant back on breast to maintain stimulation

### Phototherapy & Exchange Transfusion

**Phototherapy:**
- Requires bilirubin levels above treatment thresholds (use age and risk category to determine)
- Uses visible light (blue-green wavelength, 425–475 nm) to convert unconjugated bilirubin to water-soluble photoisomers excreted in urine
- Position: naked infant under lights or under fiberoptic blanket; cover eyes
- Duration: continuous for 24+ hours depending on bilirubin level and response
- Continue breastfeeding every 2–3 hours
- Monitor: serum bilirubin every 12–24 hours initially, then based on trend

**Exchange Transfusion:**
- Indicated if bilirubin levels pose imminent risk of encephalopathy
- Last resort in low-resource settings; requires O-negative or matched, CMV-negative, freshly typed blood
- High mortality without this intervention in severe cases

:::warning
**Signs of bilirubin encephalopathy (acute phase):**
- Lethargy, poor feeding
- High-pitched cry (unusual)
- Hypotonia (floppy) or hypertonia (stiff)
- Arching of back (opisthotonos)
- Seizures

**Chronic sequelae (if survived):** Cerebral palsy, deafness, intellectual disability—permanent.

**Prevention is key:** Early, frequent breastfeeding and phototherapy when indicated save lives.
:::

## Umbilical Care

The umbilical cord stump is a potential entry point for infection and must be kept clean and dry until it separates (day 5–15) and the site fully heals.

### First 24 Hours

- Do NOT clamp the cord stump too tightly (venous engorgement risks infection)
- Leave cord stump uncovered to air-dry (exposure to air promotes drying and healing)
- Do NOT apply routine antiseptics unless resources are very limited or infection risk is high
- Keep area dry: change soiled diapers promptly, fold diaper below cord site to keep stump exposed

### Cord Care: Dry vs. Antiseptic Method

**Dry Cord Care (preferred):**
- Proven equally effective to antiseptic in clean environments
- Simply keep clean and dry
- Accelerates separation and healing
- No additional cost

**Antiseptic Method (if infection risk is very high):**
- Use chlorhexidine 4% solution (preferred over alcohol, which delays drying)
- OR isopropyl alcohol 70%
- Clean with applicator once daily or after soiling
- Allow to air-dry between applications
- Less evidence of benefit in clean settings but may reduce infection if hygiene is poor

### Daily Inspection

- Check cord color and odor
- Normal: progresses from pale/grayish to brownish to dark brown, then separates
- Inspect for redness, swelling, drainage, or odor

:::warning
**Red flags for umbilical infection (omphalitis):**
- Redness extending beyond cord insertion onto skin
- Swelling or induration
- Purulent (pus-like) drainage
- Foul odor (not just the normal musty smell of a drying cord)
- Fever in infant
- Abdominal wall cellulitis (spreading redness)
- Sepsis signs (lethargy, poor feeding, temperature instability)

**If omphalitis suspected:** Start broad-spectrum antibiotics immediately (cephalosporin or amoxicillin-clavulanate if available), keep area clean and dry. Seek urgent medical evaluation if systemic signs or spreading cellulitis.
:::

### After Separation

- Cord stump typically separates by day 10–15
- After separation, small amount of bleeding may occur; apply gentle pressure with clean cloth for 5–10 minutes
- Granulation tissue (red, moist appearance) is normal; may persist for a few weeks
- Keep area clean and dry until fully healed (usually by week 3–4)
- If persistent oozing or pus, evaluate for granuloma or persistent infection

</section>

## Postpartum Mental Health

Postpartum depression and anxiety are common (10–20% of mothers) and often unrecognized. Without support, they impact mother-infant bonding, breastfeeding, and infant care safety.

### Baby Blues vs. Postpartum Depression

**Baby Blues (temporary, self-limiting):**
- Onset: days 2–5
- Duration: usually resolves by week 2
- Symptoms: mild sadness, mood swings, anxiety, tearfulness, irritability
- No functional impairment; mother can still care for infant
- Caused by hormonal shifts; reassurance and support are treatment

**Postpartum Depression (pathological, requires treatment):**
- Onset: days to weeks postpartum, can occur months later
- Duration: weeks to months if untreated
- Symptoms: persistent sadness, hopelessness, worthlessness, sleep disturbance (beyond normal newborn-related disruption), appetite changes, difficulty concentrating, lack of interest in baby or activities (anhedonia), fatigue, guilt
- Severe cases: thoughts of harming self or infant, suicidal ideation
- Requires mental health intervention

:::warning
**Seek immediate help if mother reports:**
- Thoughts of harming herself or infant
- Suicidal ideation or planning
- Inability to care for infant due to mental state
- Severe anxiety, panic attacks, or obsessive thoughts about harm
:::

### Screening & Support

**Screen at each postpartum visit (especially days 7, 14, 28):**
- "How are you feeling emotionally since delivery?"
- "Do you feel sad, empty, or without hope?"
- "Have you had thoughts about hurting yourself or the baby?"
- Use simple questionnaires if available (PHQ-2, EPDS)

**Supportive Interventions:**
- Normalize postpartum emotions; educate about baby blues vs. depression
- Encourage sleep (coordinate with partner/family to allow rest blocks)
- Facilitate social support; reduce isolation
- Reassure that depression is medical, not a personal failure
- Encourage self-care and physical activity
- Ensure adequate nutrition
- Refer for <a href="../psychological-first-aid-peer-support.html">mental health counseling</a> or antidepressant medication if available (sertraline, paroxetine, or tricyclic antidepressants are safe in breastfeeding)

### Postpartum Psychosis (Rare, Medical Emergency)

- Onset: typically within 2 weeks
- Symptoms: hallucinations, delusions, severe mood swings, confusion, disorganized behavior
- Risk of infanticide or suicide
- **Requires psychiatric evaluation and hospitalization**; antipsychotic medication

## Recovery Timeline: What to Expect

Understanding normal recovery patterns helps distinguish normal healing from complications.

### Week 1 (Days 1–7)

**Mother:**
- Fundus shrinks from umbilical level to below umbilicus by day 7
- Heavy lochia (red); may have clots <plum size
- Afterpains (uterine contractions): cramping, especially with breastfeeding (normal)
- Pain at perineum if lacerations or episiotomy; swelling peaks days 2–3, then improves
- Sleep disruption due to newborn demands
- Emotional shifts; may feel emotional, overwhelmed, or tearful
- Appetite returns; hunger and thirst may be intense
- Constipation common despite adequate fiber/fluids (fear of straining on sore perineum)

**Infant:**
- Weight loss of 7–10% (normal); regain starts by day 4, completed by day 10
- Increasing alertness and responsiveness
- Jaundice may appear (peak day 3–5)
- Cord stump drying; separation by day 5–10
- Frequent feeding (8–12 times/day)
- Stooling pattern: meconium (black, tar-like) for days 1–3; transitional (green, brown) days 3–5; then milk stools (yellow, seedy) after day 5

### Weeks 2–4

**Mother:**
- Lochia transitions from red to pink/brown (days 5–14) to yellow-white (after day 14)
- Fundus no longer palpable by day 14
- Perineal pain should be minimal by day 10; healing mostly complete by week 4
- Energy begins to return if not sleep-deprived; fatigue is normal
- Sexual interest may be low; hormonal changes and sleep deprivation suppress libido
- Milk supply stabilizes (softer breasts after engorgement phase)

**Infant:**
- Weight gain of ~20 g/day by week 2–4
- More organized feeding pattern (cluster feeding in evenings is normal)
- Alert, interactive, beginning social smiles by week 4
- Cord stump should have separated; umbilicus fully healed or nearly so

### Weeks 5–6

**Mother:**
- Lochia stops by week 5–6; if bleeding continues, investigate retained placental tissue
- Perineal healing complete
- Episiotomy scar may still be tender but functional
- Return of menses may be delayed, especially if breastfeeding (lactational amenorrhea)
- Postpartum check-up: final assessment of recovery

**Infant:**
- Established feeding rhythm
- Rooting, grasping, and social interaction more pronounced
- Sleeping longer stretches at night possible (highly variable; many still wake frequently)
- Jaundice should have resolved

### When to Expect Complications

**Day 1–3:** Hemorrhage (most dangerous)
**Day 2–14:** Infection (endometritis, wound infection, mastitis)
**Day 5–42:** Thromboembolism (DVT/PE), secondary hemorrhage
**Week 1+:** Psychological symptoms (depression, anxiety, psychosis)

:::tip
**Arrange follow-up visits at:**
- Day 2 or 3 (check for jaundice, feeding assessment, vital signs)
- Day 7 (fundal assessment, lochia check, infection screening)
- Day 14 (healing progress, infant weight and development)
- Day 28–42 (final postpartum assessment, contraception discussion, mental health screening)

If home visits are impossible, teach mother danger signs and instruct to return if any develop.
:::

<section id="key-takeaways">

## Key Takeaways

- **The postpartum period is high-risk:** Most maternal and neonatal deaths occur in the first week. Do not assume recovery is automatic.
- **Prevention is paramount:** Active management of third stage, uterine massage, infection prevention, frequent breastfeeding assessment, and daily monitoring catch 80% of problems early.
- **Know the red flags:** Excessive bleeding, fever, infection signs, newborn danger signs, and maternal mental health warning signs require urgent action or evacuation.
- **Breastfeeding saves lives:** Exclusive breastfeeding provides nutrition, immunological protection, and promotes uterine contraction. Support and correct latching are essential.
- **Newborn assessment must be systematic:** APGAR, reflexes, danger sign screening, and jaundice monitoring detect serious problems.
- **Communication and planning matter:** Discuss danger signs with mother and family before discharge. Arrange follow-up visits. Know how and when to access help.
