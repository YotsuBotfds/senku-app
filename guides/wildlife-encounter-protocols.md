## Overview

Wildlife encounters are a leading cause of injury and death in wilderness survival situations. The correct response depends entirely on the species and the type of encounter. **Applying the wrong protocol can be fatal** — for example, playing dead is correct for a defensive grizzly attack but will get you killed by a predatory black bear.

This guide provides species-specific protocols. When in doubt, identify the species first, then respond accordingly.

:::info-box
**Routing note:** If you only have tracks, scat, scrapes, trails, or movement patterns and do not have the animal in sight, start with [Animal Tracking & Wilderness Signs](animal-tracking.md). Use this guide when the animal is present, the encounter is escalating, or the sign points to an immediate safety decision.
:::

### Decision Routing

- **Sign only, no animal in sight:** Use the tracking guide first to identify species, direction, freshness, and likely intent.
- **Animal sighted but not charging:** Create space, identify the species, and follow the prevention and retreat steps below.
- **Animal charging, stalking, or entering camp:** Move directly to the species-specific attack or campsite response.
- **Species uncertain and the animal is close:** Prioritize distance, barriers, and calm movement. Do not improvise a close approach or assume the animal will leave on its own.

## Bear Encounters

Bears are the most dangerous large predator encountered in North American, European, and Asian wilderness. There are three species with **fundamentally different** attack behaviors requiring different responses.

### Identifying Bear Species

| Feature | Grizzly/Brown Bear | Black Bear | Polar Bear |
|---------|-------------------|------------|------------|
| **Size** | 200-360 kg (400-800 lbs) | 60-225 kg (130-500 lbs) | 350-700 kg (770-1540 lbs) |
| **Shoulder** | Prominent hump | No hump | Sloping, no hump |
| **Face profile** | Concave (dish-shaped) | Straight | Long, narrow |
| **Ears** | Short, rounded | Tall, pointed | Small, rounded |
| **Claws** | Long (5-10 cm), light-colored | Short (3-4 cm), dark | Long, curved |
| **Color** | Brown, blonde, dark brown | Black, brown, cinnamon, blonde | White/yellowish |
| **Habitat** | Mountains, forests, tundra | Forests, swamps | Arctic coast, sea ice |

**Critical note:** Black bears can be brown, cinnamon, or blonde. Do NOT identify by color alone. Use the shoulder hump and face profile.

### Prevention (All Species)

- Store food in bear-proof containers or hang from a tree branch at least 4 meters high and 1.5 meters from the trunk
- Cook and store food at least 100 meters downwind from your sleeping area
- Never keep food, toothpaste, or scented items in your shelter
- Make noise while traveling (talking, clapping, bells) to avoid surprising bears
- Travel in groups of 3 or more — bears rarely approach groups
- Be especially alert near berry patches, salmon streams, animal carcasses, and dense brush
- Carry bear spray if available — it is more effective than firearms in most encounters

### Encounter Protocol (Bear Aware But Not Charging)

**All species:**
1. Stop. Do not run. Running triggers pursuit instinct in all bear species.
2. Speak in a calm, low voice so the bear knows you are human.
3. Make yourself appear large — raise arms, stand on a log if available.
4. Back away slowly while facing the bear. Do not turn your back.
5. Give the bear an escape route. Never corner a bear.
6. If the bear stands on hind legs, it is trying to identify you — this is curiosity, not aggression.

### Attack Response: GRIZZLY/BROWN BEAR

**Defensive attack (most common):** The bear charges because it feels threatened — you surprised it, you are near its cubs, or you are near its food cache.

**Response: PLAY DEAD.**
1. Drop to the ground face-down.
2. Clasp your hands behind your neck to protect it.
3. Spread your legs to make it harder for the bear to flip you.
4. Keep your backpack on — it provides protection.
5. Remain still and silent. Do not scream or struggle.
6. The bear will typically bite, swat, and leave once it determines you are not a threat.
7. **Do not move until you are certain the bear has left the area.** Grizzlies often watch from a distance and will return if you move too soon.

**Predatory attack (rare):** The bear stalks you silently, approaches at night, or continues attacking after you play dead for several minutes.

**Response: FIGHT BACK.**
- Use any weapon available — rocks, sticks, knife, bear spray.
- Target the eyes and nose.
- A predatory grizzly attack is a life-or-death situation. Do not play dead.

### Attack Response: BLACK BEAR

**Black bears are almost always predatory when they attack.** Defensive attacks are rare because black bears prefer to flee rather than confront humans.

**Response: ALWAYS FIGHT BACK.**
1. Do NOT play dead. A black bear attacking you is trying to eat you.
2. Make yourself as large and loud as possible.
3. Fight aggressively — punch, kick, use weapons.
4. Target the eyes and nose.
5. Use bear spray if available.
6. Do not climb a tree — black bears are excellent climbers.

**Key distinction:** If a black bear enters your camp at night and approaches you, this is predatory behavior. Fight back immediately and aggressively.

### Attack Response: POLAR BEAR

**Polar bears are apex predators that view humans as prey.** All polar bear approaches should be treated as predatory.

**Response: FIGHT BACK AND ESCAPE.**
1. Do NOT play dead. Polar bears will continue attacking.
2. Use bear spray, firearms, flares, or any deterrent.
3. Fight aggressively if contact occurs.
4. Try to get inside a vehicle, building, or other shelter.
5. Group together with others — polar bears may be deterred by a large group.

See the Arctic Survival & Boreal Adaptation guide for polar-bear-specific prevention in Arctic environments.

### Bear Spray Usage

Bear spray (capsaicin aerosol) is effective against all bear species when used correctly:
1. Remove the safety clip.
2. Aim slightly downward (spray creates a cloud — you do not need to hit the bear's face directly).
3. Begin spraying when the bear is 6-9 meters (20-30 feet) away.
4. Spray in a 2-3 second burst, creating a wall of spray between you and the bear.
5. If the bear continues through the spray, spray directly at its face.
6. Back away while continuing to spray.
7. Leave the area immediately after use — the spray dissipates and the bear may return.

**Wind:** If wind is blowing toward you, move sideways before spraying. Do not spray into a headwind.

### Bear Near Your Campsite

If a bear approaches or enters your camp, the response depends on its behavior:

1. **Bear investigating food but not approaching people:** Make loud noise, bang pots, yell. Most bears will leave. Retrieve bear spray but do not approach.
2. **Bear near your tent at night:** Make noise, use a headlamp or flashlight. If it is a black bear approaching you, fight back immediately — this is predatory. If it is a grizzly near camp, use noise and light first; play dead only if it charges and makes contact defensively.
3. **Bear has your food:** Let it have the food. Do not fight a bear over supplies. Secure remaining food at least 4 meters high and 100 meters from sleeping area after the bear leaves.
4. **Prevention is critical:** A bear that finds food at a campsite will return. Cook and store food downwind, use bear-proof containers, and never keep food in your shelter.

### Summary: Bear Attack Decision

| Situation | Grizzly/Brown | Black Bear | Polar Bear |
|-----------|--------------|------------|------------|
| **Encounter (not charging)** | Back away slowly, speak calmly | Back away slowly, make noise | Retreat to shelter immediately |
| **Defensive charge** | PLAY DEAD | FIGHT BACK | FIGHT BACK |
| **Predatory attack** | FIGHT BACK | FIGHT BACK | FIGHT BACK |
| **Night approach to camp** | Use noise/light to deter, prepare to play dead | FIGHT BACK immediately | FIGHT BACK immediately |

## Mountain Lion (Cougar) Encounters

Mountain lions are ambush predators. If you see one, it has likely been watching you.

**Prevention:**
- Travel in groups. Mountain lions target lone individuals.
- Make noise while hiking.
- Keep children close — mountain lions preferentially target small prey.

**If encountered:**
1. Do NOT run. Do not crouch or bend down.
2. Face the animal. Maintain eye contact.
3. Make yourself appear as large as possible — raise arms, open jacket wide.
4. Speak loudly and firmly.
5. Back away slowly. Do not turn your back.

**If attacked:** Fight back aggressively. Target eyes and nose. Mountain lion attacks are always predatory — never play dead.

## Moose Encounters

Moose injure more people annually than bears in North America. They are not predators but are extremely aggressive when threatened, especially cows with calves and bulls during rut (autumn).

**Signs of aggression:** Ears pinned back, hackles raised, licking lips, lowering head.

**If charged:**
1. RUN. Unlike bears, running from a moose is appropriate — they usually will not pursue far.
2. Get behind a large tree, vehicle, or building. Moose have difficulty changing direction quickly.
3. If knocked down, curl into a ball and protect your head. Do not try to get up until the moose moves away.
4. A moose may stomp repeatedly — protect your head and neck.

## Wild Boar Encounters

Wild boar are aggressive, fast (up to 50 km/h), and equipped with razor-sharp tusks. They are most dangerous when wounded, cornered, or with piglets.

**If encountered:**
1. Back away slowly. Do not approach.
2. Climb a tree if one is available — boar cannot climb.
3. If charged, dodge to the side at the last moment. Boar have difficulty turning quickly.
4. If knocked down, protect your femoral arteries (inner thighs) — boar tusks slash upward.

## Snake Encounters

Venomous snake encounters near shelters, campsites, and dwellings are common in temperate and tropical regions. Most snake bites occur when people try to kill, capture, or handle the snake.

### If You Encounter a Snake (Not Bitten)

1. Stop. Freeze in place until you know where the snake is.
2. Back away slowly to at least 6 feet (2 meters). Most snakes strike only within half their body length.
3. Do not try to kill, capture, or move the snake. Give it an escape route and it will usually leave.
4. Keep children and pets away. Watch the snake from a safe distance until it moves on.

### Snake Near Your Shelter or Dwelling

1. Keep everyone inside or away from the snake.
2. If the snake is near a door or path, use a long stick (at least 6 feet) to gently encourage it away from the area. Never pick it up or pin it.
3. If the snake is in a confined space, prop open an exit and wait. Most snakes leave within an hour.
4. Check sleeping bags, boots, and gear before use in snake country. Shake out clothing and footwear every morning.

### Identifying Venomous Snakes (North America)

| Feature | Venomous Pit Vipers | Non-Venomous |
|---------|-------------------|--------------|
| **Head** | Triangular, wide | Oval, narrow |
| **Pupils** | Vertical (cat-eye) | Round |
| **Pits** | Heat-sensing pits between eye and nostril | No pits |
| **Rattle** | Rattlesnakes have a rattle | No rattle |

**Coral snakes (North America):** Red and yellow bands touching — "red on yellow, kill a fellow; red on black, venom lack." Small, shy, but highly venomous. Bites usually occur when handled.

**If bitten:** See the First Aid guide (GD-054) and the snake-bite special case for emergency response.

## Rabies and Strange Animal Behavior

An animal acting strangely — stumbling, approaching humans without fear, aggressive without provocation, active at unusual hours, drooling excessively, or circling — may have rabies or another neurological disease. **Rabies is nearly 100% fatal once symptoms appear.** Treat any suspicious animal as a rabies risk.

### Rabies Warning Signs

- Nocturnal animals active in daylight (raccoons, skunks, foxes, bats)
- Wild animals that approach humans without fear
- Stumbling, circling, or appearing drunk
- Excessive drooling or foaming at the mouth
- Unprovoked aggression or paralysis
- Bats found on the ground or in living spaces (considered exposure even without a visible bite)

### If You See an Animal Acting Strange

1. Do not approach, touch, or try to help it. Back away slowly.
2. Keep children and pets indoors. Secure food and garbage that may attract it.
3. Note the animal's location, species, and behavior for reporting.
4. Report to local animal control or health authorities if possible.

### If Bitten or Scratched by a Suspect Animal

1. Wash the wound immediately with soap and running water for at least 15 minutes. This is the single most effective first step.
2. Apply direct pressure for bleeding control.
3. Seek urgent medical evaluation for rabies post-exposure prophylaxis. **Do not wait for symptoms.**
4. If the animal can be safely captured or tested, avoid damaging the head — brain tissue is required for rabies testing.
