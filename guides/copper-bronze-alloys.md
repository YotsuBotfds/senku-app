Copper, bronze, and brass are non-ferrous metals prized for corrosion resistance, ductility, and electrical conductivity. This guide covers smelting, alloy selection, casting, and finishing techniques for non-ferrous work in survival and smithing contexts.

## Practical Entry Points

Use this guide when the starting material is copper-bearing scrap, when you already know you need a copper alloy instead of iron or steel, or when you are choosing between bronze and brass for a cast part.

### Quick alloy-selection cues

- **Pure copper:** best when conductivity, easy forming, or a base metal for later alloying matters most.
- **Tin bronze:** best for cast tools, wear parts, and general-purpose non-ferrous work.
- **Leaded bronze:** best when castability matters more than strength.
- **Phosphor bronze:** best when springiness and wear resistance matter.
- **Brass:** best for fittings, decorative work, and corrosion-resistant hardware when zinc loss is acceptable.
- **Aluminum bronze:** best when higher strength or marine exposure matters, but it is a more demanding melt.

## Copper Metallurgy

Copper is a soft, highly ductile, reddish metal. Pure copper (>99.9% Cu) conducts electricity and heat well, resists corrosion, and can be cold-worked to extreme thinness without cracking.

**Key properties:**
- **Melting point:** 1083°C (1981°F).
- **Density:** 8.96 g/cm³ (about 8.9× heavier than water).
- **Ductility:** Can be drawn into wire or hammered into thin sheet without annealing.
- **Thermal conductivity:** Excellent; second only to silver among metals.
- **Corrosion resistance:** Oxidizes to black copper oxide (Cu₂O) or green patina (basic copper carbonate) but doesn't rust like iron.

**Oxidation colors:** As copper is heated, the surface oxidation changes color:
- Black oxide (CuO) forms around 250–400°C.
- Red oxide (Cu₂O) forms below 250°C.
- These oxides reduce electrical conductivity. Annealing copper in a slightly reducing atmosphere (charcoal dust, damp wood) can preserve electrical properties.

<section id="copper-smelting">

## Copper Smelting from Ore

Copper ore minerals include malachite (CuCO₃·Cu(OH)₂, green), azurite (2CuCO₃·Cu(OH)₂, blue), and chalcopyrite (CuFeS₂, brass-yellow). The smelting process extracts metallic copper.

### Ore Preparation

**Malachite and azurite:** Crush finely and roast in a charcoal fire at 500–700°C to drive off CO₂ and H₂O, leaving copper oxide (CuO).

**Chalcopyrite and sulfide ores:** Roast at 600–800°C to convert sulfides to oxides (roasting releases SO₂ gas—work outdoors).

### Smelting Process

1. **Mix ore with charcoal:** Combine roasted ore with powdered charcoal (or charcoal dust) in a 1:2 to 1:3 ratio.
2. **Load into crucible:** Pack the mixture tightly into a clay crucible.
3. **Heat in forge:** Bring to 1100–1150°C over 1–2 hours. The charcoal reduces copper oxide (CuO) to metallic copper (Cu).
4. **Extract:** Once the charge is fully molten and the ore has reduced, pour into a mold or skim the copper from the surface with a ceramic ladle.
5. **Cool:** Allow to solidify. The copper will sink; impurities (slag) float on top or remain in the crucible.

**Yield:** Expect 40–70% recovery of copper from ore, depending on ore grade and process efficiency.

:::warning
Sulfide ore smelting produces SO₂ gas (toxic, pungent). Work in a well-ventilated area or outdoors. Do not inhale the fumes.
:::

### Refining (Optional but Recommended)

Smelted copper often contains oxygen, which makes it brittle. Refining removes oxygen:

1. **Poling:** While the copper is still molten, dip a green wood pole into the melt. The pole burns, producing CO gas, which reduces residual oxides to metal.
2. **Annealing:** Cool the ingot, then reheat to red heat and cool in air. Repeat 2–3 times. This softens the metal and drives off some oxygen.

Refined copper is softer, more ductile, and conducts electricity better than smelted copper.

<section id="bronze-alloys">

## Bronze Alloys

Bronze is primarily copper with tin (Sn) added, historically ranging from 8–12% tin. The tin hardens the copper without making it brittle like iron.

### Tin Sources and Preparation

Tin ore (cassiterite, SnO₂) is rare in many regions. Tin sources:
- **Primary ore:** Cassiterite, usually found in alluvial deposits (stream beds, erosion).
- **Historical trade:** Bronze Age cultures traded tin over vast distances, suggesting scarcity.
- **Salvage:** Reclaimed tin plate, tin cans (though modern cans are often steel with tin coating).

**Roasting and smelting tin ore:**
1. Roast cassiterite at 500–700°C to drive off volatile compounds.
2. Mix with charcoal and heat to 1200°C in a crucible.
3. Tin melts at 232°C, so molten tin separates easily from the crucible.
4. Tin is denser than most slag, so it sinks and can be skimmed off.

### Bronze Alloy Composition

| Alloy Type | Copper | Tin | Other | Uses |
|------------|--------|-----|-------|------|
| Leaded bronze | 85% | 10% | 5% Pb | Bearings, ease of casting |
| Tin bronze | 88–92% | 8–12% | — | Tools, weapons, bells |
| Phosphor bronze | 88–95% | 10–12% | <1% P | Springs, corrosion resistance |
| Aluminum bronze | 88–92% | — | 8–12% Al | High strength, marine use |

**Why tin hardening works:** Tin atoms don't dissolve completely in copper. Instead, they form intermetallic compounds (Cu₃Sn, Cu₆Sn5) that provide strength and hardness. Higher tin content = harder but more brittle.

**Common ratio:** 88–92% copper with 8–12% tin produces a sweet spot: hard enough for tool edges, ductile enough to shape without cracking.

### Making Bronze

1. **Charge the crucible:** Weigh out copper and tin (total ~1 kg for a test batch).
2. **Melt copper first:** Bring copper to 1150°C until fully molten (glowing orange).
3. **Add tin slowly:** Sprinkle powdered tin or add small pieces into the molten copper. Tin melts at 232°C and readily dissolves.
4. **Stir gently:** Use a ceramic rod to mix. Avoid excessive stirring, which traps air bubbles.
5. **Pour:** Once uniform in color (no streaks), pour into a mold.
6. **Cool slowly:** Allow to solidify without disturbance. Rapid cooling can cause segregation (uneven composition).

:::tip
If making leaded bronze, add lead after the copper and tin are mixed. Lead sinks and improves castability but reduces corrosion resistance and tensile strength.
:::

<section id="brass-making">

## Brass (Copper-Zinc Alloys)

Brass is copper with zinc (Zn) added. Zinc is volatile at high temperatures (boiling point 906°C), making it tricky to work with but valuable for corrosion resistance and aesthetic appeal.

**Brass composition:** Typical brasses range from 60–90% copper and 10–40% zinc.

| Brass Type | Copper | Zinc | Uses |
|------------|--------|------|------|
| Cartridge brass | 70% | 30% | Ammunition cases, sheet metal |
| Yellow brass | 65% | 35% | Hardware, plumbing fittings |
| Red brass | 85% | 15% | Coins, decorative items |
| Muntz metal | 60% | 40% | Marine components (dezincification-resistant) |

**Making brass:**
1. **Melt copper** to 1150–1200°C in a crucible.
2. **Add zinc oxide (ZnO) or metallic zinc** carefully. Zinc evaporates rapidly, so cover the melt with charcoal dust to reduce loss.
3. **Stir gently** and allow 2–3 minutes for the zinc to fully dissolve.
4. **Pour quickly** before zinc evaporates further.

:::warning
Zinc vapors (white smoke) are toxic in high concentrations. Work outdoors or in a well-ventilated forge. Do not inhale the fumes directly.
:::

**Dezincification:** If brass is used in marine or corrosive environments, zinc can selectively corrode out, leaving a weak, porous copper structure. High-copper (>85% Cu) brasses resist this better than high-zinc brasses.

## Casting Techniques for Non-Ferrous Metals

Non-ferrous metals (copper, bronze, brass) have lower melting points than steel, making them easier to cast in simple molds.

### Mold Materials

- **Sand molds:** Fine, damp sand compressed around a wooden or ceramic form. Good for one-time casts; the mold is destroyed during extraction.
- **Clay molds:** Baked clay provides better heat retention and reusability. Used for multiple casts.
- **Stone molds:** Natural stone (soapstone, talc) or slate can be carved and reused many times.
- **Ceramic crucibles:** For holding molten metal; disposable or reusable depending on clay quality.

### Investment Casting (Lost-Wax Method, Simplified)

1. **Carve or mold wax:** Create the desired shape in wax (beeswax or tallow candle wax).
2. **Invest in clay:** Bury the wax form in a thick clay paste (clay + sand + straw, ~2 cm thick on all sides).
3. **Dry:** Allow the clay investment to dry fully in air or in a gentle oven (not too hot).
4. **Burn out:** Heat the investment slowly to 400–600°C over several hours. The wax melts and burns away, leaving a cavity.
5. **Pour:** Heat the metal to 1150–1200°C and pour into the cavity. The metal cools in the clay investment.
6. **Break out:** Once cool, break apart the clay to extract the casting.

**Advantages:** Excellent surface detail; no draft required; complex shapes possible.

**Disadvantages:** Slow; requires investment material preparation; air bubbles can form.

<section id="cold-working">

## Cold Working and Annealing

Copper, bronze, and brass can be shaped by cold hammering or bending, but the metal work-hardens and becomes brittle.

**Annealing (softening):**
1. **Heat to red heat (600–750°C)** in a charcoal forge.
2. **Cool slowly** in air or embed in ash/sand to slow cooling.
3. **The metal becomes soft** and can be reshaped.

**Repeat as needed:** A copper object can be annealed multiple times throughout its working life without degradation.

**Hardness progression:** Soft (annealed) → 1/4 hard (light hammering) → 1/2 hard (moderate hammering) → 3/4 hard (heavy hammering) → full hard (maximum work hardening).

<section id="patina-protection">

## Patina and Corrosion Protection

Unlike iron, copper doesn't rust, but it oxidizes. The oxidation can be controlled or used aesthetically.

**Natural patina formation:**
- **Black oxide:** Forms quickly in the first days, from CuO formation in air/moisture.
- **Green patina:** Forms over months/years in moist conditions, from basic copper carbonate (CuCO₃·Cu(OH)₂).

**Preventing patina (keeping bright):**
- **Lacquer:** Apply a thin coat of clear lacquer to seal the surface.
- **Wax:** Melt beeswax and rub onto the object, then buff.
- **Oil:** Regular oiling (linseed oil) prevents oxidation but requires reapplication.

**Accelerating patina (for appearance):**
- **Ammonia fumes:** Suspend the object over ammonia solution in a sealed box; green patina forms in hours.
- **Salt water:** Spray with saltwater solution and allow to oxidize naturally.
- **Heat:** Heating in air produces darker oxides; oxidation color follows temperature (as in tool tempering).

:::tip
A thin layer of black oxide (Cu₂O) actually protects deeper copper from further oxidation. It's only when water and air penetrate that deep green patina forms. A sealed copper object can last centuries with minimal corrosion.
:::

## Historical Importance

Bronze was humanity's first intentionally created alloy (Bronze Age, ~3500–1200 BCE). Before iron, bronze provided:
- **Superior edge retention:** Harder than pure copper, able to hold a sharper edge than stone.
- **Trade networks:** Tin's rarity created vast trade routes.
- **Prestige:** Bronze items signified wealth and craftsmanship.

Brass became important later (Roman period), prized for its golden color and resistance to corrosion in seawater.

In survival contexts, understanding non-ferrous working extends your tool-making options when iron is unavailable. Copper scavenging, local tin ore, and simple casting techniques can produce functional tools, decorative items, and electrical components.

## Practical Example: Casting a Bronze Tool Head

1. **Prepare materials:** 900g copper, 100g tin (approximately 10% tin bronze).
2. **Make a clay mold:** Press a wooden axe-head shape into damp sand. Create a pour gate (funnel) and air vents.
3. **Melt copper:** Heat in a crucible to 1150°C (bright orange glow).
4. **Add tin:** Sprinkle 100g powdered tin into the molten copper.
5. **Mix:** Stir gently with a ceramic rod for 2 minutes.
6. **Pour:** Quickly pour the molten bronze into the sand mold. Work fast before it cools.
7. **Cool:** Let the mold cool undisturbed for 30 minutes.
8. **Extract:** Carefully break apart the sand mold to reveal the casting.
9. **Finish:** File away excess metal (gates, vents) and sand-smooth the surface.
10. **Anneal:** Heat to red heat and cool to soften any work-hardened sections, if needed.

The resulting tool head can be hafted onto a wooden handle and used for generations with proper maintenance.
