## Introduction

If you need a temporary toilet, portable latrine, or emergency bathroom while a sewer, septic, or permanent latrine is being built or repaired, the goal is simple: keep feces and urine away from hands, water, food, insects, and living space. A good temporary setup is private, easy to clean, easy to empty, and easy to wash hands after use.
This guide is for stopgap toilets and repair-site sanitation. If you are choosing where to put a new permanent latrine, deciding safe distances from wells or kitchens, or planning pit layout and separation, use [Latrine & Sanitation Design](./latrine-sanitation-design.md).

## What Makes a Good Temporary Toilet

A temporary toilet only needs to do a few things well:

- Hold waste safely without leaking
- Keep users from being exposed to waste
- Prevent smell, flies, and spills as much as possible
- Be easy to empty or replace
- Support handwashing every time

If a setup cannot do those things, it is not a toilet, just a contamination problem with a seat on it.

### Good use cases

- Power outage or water outage
- Septic repair or sewer backup
- Construction site or temporary work camp
- Flooding, freezing weather, or frozen ground
- Isolated cabin, shed, or off-grid build site

### Poor use cases

- Long-term dense housing without a disposal plan
- Any setup near wells, surface water, or drainage channels
- Open containers with no cover
- Any plan that depends on "we'll figure out the waste later"

## Temporary Toilet Options

| Option | Best For | Pros | Limits |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bucket toilet | Fast emergency use, low cost | Simple, portable, easy to build | Needs frequent emptying and careful odor control |
| Lined pit or trench | Short-term camps with proper siting | Lower daily handling | Hard to move, can fail in wet ground or winter |
| Bag-lined toilet | Very short-term use or transport | Fast cleanup, useful when mobility matters | Ongoing bag supply needed |
| Commercial portable toilet | Events, job sites, rentals | Familiar, cleanable, low effort | Requires service or disposal access |

For most emergency household situations, a covered bucket toilet is the fastest practical choice.

## Bucket Toilet Basics

A bucket toilet is the simplest temporary toilet: a sturdy bucket, a seat or lid, absorbent cover material, and a plan for waste disposal. It works best when the container is treated as a sealed waste vessel, not a trash can.

### Materials

- 5-gallon bucket or similar sturdy container
- Tight-fitting lid
- Seat ring, toilet seat, or improvised wooden top with a hole
- Absorbent cover material such as sawdust, dry leaves, shredded paper, or wood shavings
- Heavy-duty liner bags if you want faster cleanup
- Gloves, cleaning supplies, and a dedicated waste container

### Setup

1. Place the bucket on a stable, level surface.
2. Add a few centimeters of dry absorbent material to the bottom.
3. Mount a seat or lid that is comfortable and closes tightly.
4. Keep more absorbent cover material beside the toilet.
5. Label the bucket or keep it visually separate from food, water, and cleaning supplies.

### Use

- Urinate into the bucket only if that is your chosen setup, or use a separate urinal container if practical.
- After each solid use, cover the waste with dry material.
- Close the lid immediately.
- Clean visible contamination as soon as possible.

The absorbent cover material reduces odor, slows flies, and makes later disposal easier.

## Privacy and Placement

Temporary toilets work better when they feel private and predictable. People are more likely to use them correctly when the path is obvious and the setup is not awkward.

### Placement rules

- Put the toilet far enough from kitchens and food storage to feel separate
- Keep it out of flood paths and away from standing water
- Do not place it downhill from a well or spring
- Choose a spot that is easy to reach at night
- Add a light if the toilet will be used after dark

### Privacy options

- Tarp wall, shower curtain, or tarp-and-pole enclosure
- Simple shed or tent frame
- Screened corner inside a worksite or camp
- Frost-proof or wind-blocked corner in winter

For emergency use, even a rough privacy screen is better than forcing people to improvise in the open.

## Waste Handling and Disposal

The most important part of a temporary toilet is what happens after use. Waste should be managed on a schedule, not when the bucket overflows.

:::warning
**Contamination control:** Human waste carries bacteria, viruses, and parasites that cause cholera, typhoid, dysentery, and hepatitis. Every surface the waste touches (bucket, lid, gloves, tools) is contaminated until cleaned with soap and safe water. Never let waste contact drinking water, food, cooking areas, or bare hands. If waste spills, clean the area immediately and wash your hands.
:::

### Safe handling workflow

1. Put on gloves before emptying or moving the container.
2. Close the lid before transport.
3. Move the waste container carefully so it does not slosh or spill.
4. Empty only into an approved disposal or treatment system.
5. Wash the container with soap and safe water.
6. Dry it before reusing if possible.
7. Remove gloves and wash hands with soap immediately.

### Cover materials

Good cover materials reduce odor and make emptying easier:

- Sawdust
- Wood shavings
- Dry leaves
- Shredded paper
- Straw or chopped plant fiber

Avoid dumping loose food scraps, liquids, or wet trash into the toilet. They increase smell and make disposal harder.

### Do not do this

- Do not leave an open bucket uncovered indoors
- Do not pour raw waste onto soil where people walk or where runoff can carry it away
- Do not empty into storm drains
- Do not store waste where children or animals can reach it

If you are in a camp or repair site, set a single clear waste handling point so every user does the same thing.

## Handwashing Station

Handwashing is not optional. A toilet setup without handwashing is only a disease-transfer system.

### Simple handwashing options

- Jug or bucket with a tap, spigot, or pinched outlet
- Soap tied to the station so it is always nearby
- Basin or catch container to keep the ground from turning muddy
- Paper towels, reusable cloth towels, or air drying

### Minimum handwashing steps

1. Wet hands.
2. Apply soap.
3. Scrub palms, backs of hands, between fingers, thumbs, and under nails.
4. Rinse with clean water.
5. Dry with a clean towel or air dry.

### Where to place it

- Right next to the toilet
- At the waste emptying point
- Near the entrance to any latrine shelter

If soap is scarce, use it for handwashing before and after waste handling first. That is the highest-value use.

## Cold-Weather and Winter Use

In winter, the problems change: waste can freeze, lids can stick, hands get colder faster, and people avoid walking outside. A winter temporary toilet needs wind protection, insulation, and a disposal plan that still works when the ground is hard.

### Winter adjustments

- Put the toilet inside a sheltered but ventilated structure if possible
- Keep the seat cover closed to reduce heat loss and odor
- Store absorbent cover material where it stays dry
- Use an insulating ring, foam, or wood around the bucket if freezing is severe
- Keep a small shovel, scraper, or de-icing plan for the path to the toilet
- Add lighting so people do not avoid the toilet because the route is unpleasant

### Cold-weather waste handling

- Empty buckets before they become too full and heavy
- If waste is freezing to the container, clean sooner and more often
- Use a transport container with a lid
- Keep handwashing water from freezing if possible by storing it indoors or using a warmed supply

### Winter mistakes to avoid

- Letting waste accumulate until the bucket freezes solid
- Putting the toilet too far away just because it is private
- Skipping handwashing because water is cold
- Carrying uncovered waste through snow or ice

When conditions are harsh, convenience matters. The easier it is to use the toilet correctly, the more likely people are to keep using it.

## Transition to a Better System

Temporary toilets should not become permanent by accident. Start planning the next system as soon as the emergency passes.

### Signs it is time to upgrade

- The bucket is being emptied too often
- Odor control is failing
- You have a stable site and enough space for a better system
- Users complain about privacy, smell, or winter access
- You now have better access to water, materials, or labor
- You are no longer improvising and need permanent siting, setbacks, or pit design

### Common next steps

- Build a proper pit latrine if soil and groundwater allow it
- Move to a composting toilet if water is limited and you can manage cover material
- Connect to septic or sewer repair as soon as infrastructure is restored
- Set up a small waste area or service routine for a worksite or camp
- If the next question is "where should the permanent latrine go?" or "how far from a well or kitchen?", hand off to [Latrine & Sanitation Design](./latrine-sanitation-design.md)

### Transition checklist

- Choose the long-term toilet type
- Identify safe siting and setback distances
- Reserve tools and materials
- Keep the temporary toilet operating until the new one is usable
- Clean and retire the temporary equipment without leaving waste behind

## Troubleshooting

### Bad smell

**Field fix:** Add a thick layer of dry cover material right now and close the lid. Empty the bucket today if smell persists.
- Add more dry cover material
- Close the lid immediately after use
- Empty more often
- Check for leakage or a cracked bucket

### Flies

**Field fix:** Cover all visible waste with dry material and close the lid. Empty the bucket and clean it today.
- Keep the lid closed
- Cover waste with dry material
- Remove waste on a tighter schedule
- Improve screening around the toilet area

### Spills or leaks

**Field fix:** Replace or double-bag the container immediately. Clean the spill area with soap and safe water. Wash your hands.
- Replace the container
- Use a liner bag or secondary bucket
- Keep the toilet on level ground
- Move the disposal route so the container is not carried through crowded spaces

### Users avoid the toilet

**Field fix:** Ask what is keeping people away — usually privacy, smell, distance, or lighting.
- Improve privacy
- Improve lighting
- Shorten the walking path
- Make the seat more stable and comfortable

## Quick Start Checklist

If you need a temporary toilet today:

1. Get a bucket or other sturdy container with a lid.
2. Add dry absorbent material.
3. Add a seat or stable top.
4. Place it in a private, sheltered spot.
5. Set out soap and a handwashing station.
6. Decide how waste will be emptied and where it will go.
7. Keep the setup simple enough that everyone can use it the same way every time.

## Bottom Line

A good temporary toilet is boring, private, and easy to clean. The safest improvised system is the one people actually use, handwash after, and empty on schedule. Build for the next few days first, then keep improving it until a better sanitation system is ready.
