## Introduction: Why Inventory Your People

A community is its people. In collapse, you need to know:

- **Who are we?** Age, family ties, current location
- **What can we do?** Skills, education, physical capability
- **What's our condition?** Health, injury, dependency needs
- **What's our capacity?** Labor hours available, defense capability, consumption needs

Without this data, you're flying blind. You can't ration fairly, assign work efficiently, prepare for defense, or plan for the future.

Yet registration is also **dangerous**. Data can be stolen, misused for oppression, or seized by hostile groups. This guide shows how to build a registration system that's useful but also secure and ethical.

:::warning
**The Privacy Paradox**
You need information to survive, but information concentrated in one place creates vulnerability. Someone with the registry could blackmail people, identify targets for violence, or enforce totalitarian control. Balance transparency with security.
:::

## Part 1: What Information to Collect

### The Core Registry

Every person in your community should be recorded with:

**Identity & Location**
- Full name (and any nicknames/aliases used locally)
- Age (date of birth if known, approximate age if not)
- Gender identity (used for privacy in shared spaces, bathroom allocation, etc.)
- Family/household relationships (who is kin to whom, living unit)
- Current primary location (house, tent, barn, etc.)

**Health & Dependency**
- Current health status (healthy, injured, ill, chronic condition)
- Known medical conditions or allergies
- Mobility (fully mobile, uses cane/crutches, wheelchair-bound, bedridden)
- Pregnancy or postpartum status (last 1 year)
- Dependency level (fully independent, needs assistance, requires constant care)
- Dietary restrictions (allergy, intolerance, religious, ethical)

**Skills & Labor Capacity**
- Primary occupation/skill (healer, farmer, builder, scout, cook, teacher, mechanic)
- Secondary skills (languages, literacy, special knowledge)
- Current physical capacity (can work full days, part-time, light duty only)
- Years of experience in primary skill
- Willingness to teach others (training capacity)

**Household & Consumption**
- Dependents (how many children, elderly, or disabled people rely on this person)
- Consumption rate (eats full ration, half ration, special diet)
- Water needs (normal, high due to labor/illness, low)
- Any special supplies needed (medication, tools, materials for their skill)

**Social & Demographic**
- Social role (leader, council member, trainer, caretaker, etc.)
- Known conflicts or alliances (who do they work well with, who do they clash with)
- Trustworthiness rating (internal assessment: reliable, questionable, unreliable)
- Outside connections (family members in other communities, trade contacts)

### Optional But Useful

**Education & Literacy**
- Can read and write (local language)
- Languages spoken
- Educational background (formal schooling, apprenticeship, self-taught)
- Teaching capacity (can train others in their skill)

**Mental & Psychological**
- Known mental health conditions (anxiety, depression, trauma)
- Substance dependencies (alcohol, drugs)
- Behavioral flags (aggressive under stress, hoarder, compulsive liar)
- Coping mechanisms (what helps them stabilize during crisis)

**Historical**
- When did they join the community (founding member, refugee, newcomer)
- Previous conflicts or violations (what have they done wrong, how were they resolved)
- What skills have they acquired since arrival
- Commitment level (planning to stay, uncertain, wants to leave)

:::info-box
**Privacy vs Utility**
Collect what's genuinely useful for survival. "Attractiveness" or "sexual preferences" or "political views" are not useful; they're surveillance. Only collect information that directly helps with resource allocation, work assignment, or health care.
:::

### Data Entry Notes

**Keep it concise:**
- Use checkboxes; avoid long text fields
- "Primary skill" is one thing; secondary skills can be a short list
- Mental health information is sensitive; brief flags only, details in separate secure file
- Trust rating is internal assessment, not shared with the person being registered

**Consistency matters:**
- Use the same form for everyone
- Train the registrar to ask questions the same way
- Review forms quarterly to catch inconsistencies

**Update schedule:**
- New people: register within 48 hours of arrival
- Changes: update within 1 week of significant change (injury, birth, departure)
- Full audit: once per year (health status, skills, location changes)

## Part 3: Privacy and Security of Records

### Who Has Access

**Tier 1 (Full Access):**
- Primary leader/council
- Registrar (the person maintaining forms)
- Health care provider (their own health section only)
- Defense coordinator (defense-relevant sections)

**Tier 2 (Limited Access):**
- Work assignment coordinator (skills and capacity only)
- Food coordinator (household/consumption section only)
- Council members (summary data only, not details on individuals)

**Tier 3 (No Access):**
- General community members (cannot see other people's detailed data)
- Children (access restricted until age 16+)
- Newcomers (not given access until they've been in community 6+ months)

### Storage Security

**Physical:**
- Original forms stored in locked box or cabinet
- Only registrar and leader have keys
- Backup copy stored in separate secure location (fireproof if possible)
- Destroy when no longer needed (see below)

**Digital (if you have computers):**
- Password-protected spreadsheet or database
- Encrypted if possible
- Backup on separate physical location
- Restrict printing and copying

**Oral (in non-literate communities):**
- Designated keeper of information (often the leader or a trusted elder)
- Information is memorized, not written
- Secondary keeper trained as backup
- Sensitive information is only shared when necessary

:::warning
**Do Not Let Data Concentrate in One Person**
If only one person knows everything about everyone, that person becomes a target. They can be blackmailed, coerced, or kidnapped. Distribute knowledge: registrar knows identity/location, healer knows health, defense coordinator knows defense capacity. No single person holds everything.
:::

### What Not to Record

Avoid collecting information that creates vulnerability:

- **Precise location of hidden resources**: Don't record where families keep private stashes
- **Names of children separately from parents**: Predators could target specific children
- **Sexual history or orientation**: Creates basis for coercion and blackmail
- **Past atrocities or war crimes**: Can be used to hunt individuals or create blood feuds
- **Debt or grievances**: Creates basis for internal violence

### Destroying Records

Old records create risks (data breach, identity theft, blackmail). Destroy them periodically:

**Policy:**
- Records older than 5 years: destroy by burning if paper, secure deletion if digital
- Records of people who have left: destroy after 1 year
- Medical information: destroy after person recovers (except chronic conditions that need tracking)
- Conflict/behavioral notes: destroy after 3 years if no ongoing issue

**Ceremony:**
- Some communities burn old records publicly (shows transparency, ensures they're really gone)
- Make it clear: "We're destroying information we no longer need"
- This builds trust that the registry is not a permanent surveillance tool

## Part 4: Census Procedures

A **census** is a full community count and recount. Do this at least once per year.

### Census Phases

**Phase 1: Preparation (1 week before)**
- Leader announces census date
- Explain why: "We need accurate count for planning resources"
- Ask people to gather any relevant information (birth dates, dependents, health changes)
- Organize registrars (pick 2-3 trusted people to help collect forms)
- Prepare blank forms

**Phase 2: Collection (1 week)**
- Registrars visit each household
- Re-interview each person using the form
- Update any changes since last census (birth, death, injury, skill gains)
- Record the census date on each form

**Phase 3: Verification (3 days)**
- Tally the total: how many people do we have?
- Check for duplicates (same person registered twice)
- Check for missing people (anyone who wasn't found, who moved, who died recently)
- Verify household counts

**Phase 4: Review (1 day)**
- Leader and council review census results
- Identify significant changes: population growth? Deaths? Major disability? New skills?
- Discuss implications for resource planning

**Phase 5: Announcement (evening assembly)**
- Leader announces total population, major changes
- Share useful summary: "We have 47 people: 12 children, 8 elderly, 27 adults. We gained 3 people and lost 2 to illness."
- Thank people for participation
- Explain how the data will be used (rationing, work assignment, planning)

### Counting the Vulnerable

**Children** (age 0-12):
- Count with their household
- Record age as accurately as possible
- Track developmental milestones (which kids can do adult work yet)
- Monitor for abuse/malnourishment

**Elderly** (age 65+):
- Count and note dependency level (independent, assisted, fully dependent)
- Assess cultural status (are they respected advisors or burdensome?)
- Plan for potential death within 1-3 years
- Involve them in census if possible (maintains dignity)

**Disabled or chronically ill:**
- Record care needs clearly
- Assess whether their condition is stable or worsening
- Plan for potential loss of their labor (they may die or become fully dependent)
- Protect their identity data (if hostile group gets roster, disabled people are vulnerable to harm)

**Recent refugees or newcomers:**
- Census within first month of arrival
- Use this to identify if they're fleeing with dependents (children, elderly)
- Track if they have outside connections (family in hostile territory, etc.)

## Part 5: Using Demographic Data for Planning

### Rationing Calculations

Once you know population, you can calculate food consumption:

**Example:**
- 45 people total
- 12 children (half ration each) = 6 adult-equivalent consumption
- 27 healthy adults (full ration) = 27 adult-equivalent consumption
- 6 elderly (three-quarter ration) = 4.5 adult-equivalent consumption
- **Total consumption**: 37.5 adult-equivalent rations/day

If you have 2000 kg of grain:
- At 1 kg per person-equivalent per day: 2000 ÷ 37.5 = 53 days of full rations
- If rationing to 70%: 76 days
- If severe rationing to 50%: 114 days

This data lets you **plan rationing before crisis** rather than improvise when food runs out.

### Labor Capacity Assessment

After census, calculate available labor:

**Example (continued):**
- 27 healthy adults, 1.0 work capacity = 27 full work-days available
- 6 elderly, 0.25 work capacity (light tasks) = 1.5 work-days
- 8 youths age 13-17, 0.75 capacity = 6 work-days
- 12 children, 0.2 capacity (light tasks, training) = 2.4 work-days
- **Total capacity**: ~37 work-days/day available

**Allocation example:**
- Food production (planting, harvesting, water): 15 work-days
- Building/maintenance (shelters, tools): 8 work-days
- Defense (watch, perimeter): 6 work-days
- Childcare (supervision, education): 5 work-days
- Administration (record-keeping, council): 2 work-days
- **Total**: 36 work-days (sustainable, with 1 day slack for emergencies/rest)

Without demographic data, you're guessing. With it, you can balance work assignments realistically.

:::tip
**Account for the Unseen Work**
Census tracks official roles, but some work is invisible: childcare, elder care, household maintenance, emotional labor. Assign value to this work in your labor calculations. A woman caring for 3 grandchildren isn't "not working"—she's doing critical work.
:::

### Defense Planning

Demographics inform defense capacity:

**By age:**
- Adults 18-45: full combat capability
- Adults 45-65: reduced combat, strong support/logistics roles
- Elderly 65+: defense from fortified positions only
- Youths 13-17: scouts, runners, defensive support
- Children: evacuation, non-combat roles

**By health:**
- Healthy: assigned to high-risk roles
- Injured/disabled: assigned to defensive positions, supply work, or civilian roles
- Chronically ill: civilian roles, support positions

**Example (continued):**
- 20 healthy adults age 18-45: primary combat/active defense
- 7 adults age 45-65: secondary defense, fortified positions, leadership
- 6 elderly: evacuation support, supply delivery from rear
- 8 youths: scouts, runners, perimeter watch
- **Defense capability**: Can mount ~20 active defenders, with 15-20 in support roles

This tells you: are you strong enough to defend the perimeter? Can you form an offensive force if needed? Do you need to prioritize defense improvements (walls, better positions)?

### Taxation or Labor Obligation

In some communities, tax is calculated based on demographics:

**Progressive labor tax** (percentage of labor owed to community):
- Healthy adult: 40% of labor (60% for self/family)
- Reduced capacity: 25% of labor
- Dependent (child, elderly, disabled): 0% tax
- Specialist (healer, teacher): 50% tax (for community benefit)

**Progressive consumption tax** (percentage of ration above survival):
- Survival ration: untaxed
- Above survival: taxed at 10-30%
- Example: if basic ration is 1 kg grain/day and someone has 1.5 kg, the extra 0.5 kg is taxed

Demographics tell you who pays and how much the community can collectively afford to tax.

### Identifying Vulnerabilities

Census data reveals risks:

**Dependency ratio too high:**
- If 15+ people are fully dependent (children, elderly, disabled) and only 25 can work
- You have 1 dependent per 1.67 workers (risky; less than 1:2 is unsustainable)
- Plan: recruit, train youths to increase worker count, or reduce dependency through difficult choices

**Skills concentrated in one person:**
- Only 1 healer, 1 engineer, 1 teacher
- If that person dies, knowledge is lost
- Plan: identify successors, begin training immediately

**Unbalanced age distribution:**
- Too many young (high food consumption, many years of dependency ahead)
- Too many old (declining labor, approaching deaths)
- Plan: recruitment focus on working-age adults, family planning discussions

**Health crisis signal:**
- Census shows 8 people with respiratory illness, trend upward from last year
- Plan: improve shelter hygiene, increase medical supplies, prepare quarantine

## Part 6: Newcomer Processing

When a refugee or new settler arrives, register them quickly:

**Initial Registration (Day 1):**
- Name, age, family ties
- Health status (are they injured or ill?)
- Immediate needs (food, shelter, medical care)
- Basic skills inventory
- Any outside connections or threats
- Origin story (where are they from, why are they here?)

**Secondary Registration (Days 2-5):**
- Full form as people adjust
- Extended interviews about skills
- Health assessment by healer
- Mental health check-in (are they stable, traumatized, dangerous?)
- Background check (council discusses whether to trust them with information)

**Provisional Membership (1-3 months):**
- They participate in work assignments
- They eat community rations
- They live in community spaces
- They cannot access sensitive information or make decisions
- Council assesses whether they're reliable

**Full Membership (after assessment):**
- They're added to succession plans, council decisions
- They can access skilled training
- They can own property or establish permanent housing
- They're bound by community rules fully

:::warning
**Beware the Infiltrator**
A refugee may be a scout for a hostile group, or an escaped slave carrying disease, or a person fleeing justice for crimes. Use the provisional period to vet newcomers thoroughly. Don't give them trust immediately.
:::

## Part 7: Missing Persons & Death Tracking

### Missing Persons Protocol

**When someone is reported missing:**
1. **Verify they're actually missing** (not just in another part of community, sleeping late, etc.)
2. **Check known locations**: friends' houses, work sites, their known destinations
3. **Alert the defense/security team**: there may be a threat (attacker, predator)
4. **Organize a search party** (within 2-4 hours if possible)
5. **Update the registry**: note date/time missing, last known location, circumstances
6. **Continue searches** for 48-72 hours with declining intensity
7. **Reopen the case** if evidence emerges later (body found, hostile group confirms taking them)

**After 1 week missing:**
- Mark status as "presumed lost" in registry
- Update dependents and households
- If person had critical role, begin succession/reassignment
- Look for body (check dangerous areas, water sources, known attack sites)

### Death Tracking

When someone dies:

1. **Confirm death** (not just unconscious/comatose)
2. **Record in registry**: death date, probable cause, who confirmed
3. **Note dependents**: who is now without support, who needs reassignment
4. **Note skills lost**: if they had unique knowledge, what do we lose
5. **Update census counts**: total population decreases
6. **Ritual/burial**: community acknowledges the death
7. **Reassignment**: dependents, their property, their roles redistribute

**Tracking patterns:**
- If deaths cluster (all children, all elderly, all one group), there may be a cause
- Epidemic? Bad harvests? Poisoning? Investigate
- Age-related deaths are expected; unexpected deaths need explanation

### Sample Death Entry

```
Name: [John Smith]
Death date: 2026-02-15
Age at death: 72
Probable cause: Infection/sepsis (following injury)
Confirmed by: [Healer Name]
Dependents affected: None (adult, no children)
Household affected: Wife [Jane Smith] (now widow, support needed)
Skills lost: Advanced carpentry, tool maintenance
Succession: None (secondary carpenter is [Tom Jones])
Notes: Injury occurred 2-15-2026, deteriorated rapidly, died 48 hours later
```

## Part 8: Registry Ethics and Governance

### When to Share Data vs Protect Privacy

**Share with community:**
- Total population count (useful for morale, planning)
- Summary statistics (X children, Y adults, Z elderly)
- Aggregated skills (we have 3 healers, 5 farmers, 2 educators)
- Aggregated health (Y people currently ill, Z injured)

**Share with council/leadership:**
- Individual-level data on all people
- Dependency ratios and labor capacity
- Specific skills and training capacity
- Defense capacity
- Health crises and vulnerable individuals

**Keep strictly confidential:**
- Medical details (specific diagnoses, medications)
- Mental health information
- Behavioral flags and trust ratings
- Outside family/connections
- History of conflict or rule-breaking

### Preventing Misuse

**Safeguard against authoritarian control:**
- Council must approve any use of data for punishment/exile
- Registry cannot be used to discriminate (ration fewer to a group based on identity)
- Mental health information cannot be weaponized (spreading rumors that someone is "crazy")

**Prevent economic abuse:**
- Data on skills cannot be used to force people into roles
- Data on dependents cannot be used to threaten children
- Health information cannot be shared to stigmatize people
- Outside connections cannot be disclosed to enemies

**Accountability:**
- Anyone accessing sensitive data must sign an oath not to misuse it
- Breaches (sharing confidential information) are punished
- Regular audits (quarterly check: who accessed what, were there breaches)

### Community Trust

The registry will be trusted **only if**:
1. People can see their own information (and correct errors)
2. They understand why information is being collected
3. They see the data being used fairly (for good decisions, not abuse)
4. They know who has access and why
5. Old information is destroyed as promised

Host a quarterly "registry review" meeting:
- Show community the aggregate summary
- Explain any policy changes
- Invite questions and concerns
- Make changes if people feel data is being misused

## Conclusion: Registry as Foundation

A good population registry is foundational to fair, effective governance. It prevents:
- Ration fraud (knowing exactly how many people means you catch hoarding)
- Unfair work assignments (knowing skills means everyone does what they do best)
- Health crises spreading (knowing who's ill helps you quarantine)
- Defense failures (knowing capacity means realistic planning)
- Succession chaos (knowing people means identifying good leaders)

A bad registry becomes a tool of oppression. Protect it carefully, use it ethically, and remember: the registry serves the people, not the other way around.
