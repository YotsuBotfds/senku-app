## Overview

Avalanches are rapid, flowing masses of snow that accelerate downslope, reaching speeds of 200+ mph and generating tremendous force. They kill hundreds of people worldwide annually, many of them experienced mountaineers. This guide covers preventing avalanche involvement through terrain assessment and snowpack reading, executing self-rescue if caught, and rescuing companions buried in snow. The goal is threefold: avoid triggering slides, survive if caught, and rescue others efficiently.

Avalanche survival depends on preparation and knowledge rather than luck. Most avalanche deaths are preventable through understanding slope hazard, route selection, and party discipline. Burial survival depends on staying near the surface, self-rescue technique, and rapid companion response. This guide provides the no-tech foundation; in real avalanche terrain, beacon-based search should be considered standard.

## Terrain Assessment and Avalanche Hazard Recognition

The first defense against avalanches is avoiding hazardous terrain. Learning to assess slope danger saves lives before anything else.

### Slope Angle Evaluation

**Critical angle range**: Slopes between 30° and 45° are most prone to avalanche. Slopes under 30° rarely slide (unless very wet or heavily loaded). Slopes over 50° are generally too steep to accumulate snow thick enough to slide (snow sloughs off in small releases).

**Slope angle measurement without instruments**:

1. **Hold your arm straight out at 90° to your body** (perpendicular to your torso). Your arm points at roughly 90° to flat ground.

2. **Lower your arm until it aligns with the slope** you're assessing. The angle from vertical to your arm equals the slope's steepness. A slope that aligns with your arm at 45° down is approximately 45°.

3. **Alternatively, observe if you can walk upslope without using hands**. If hands are needed, slope is around 30°. If scrambling is required, it's 40°+.

Critically dangerous slopes are those where you must grip with hands to ascend but the angle is gentle enough to accumulate thick snow—these are the sweet spot for large, dangerous slides.

### Aspect and Sun Exposure

In the Northern Hemisphere, south-facing slopes receive more solar heating and are more prone to wet-slab avalanches in spring/early summer. North-facing slopes remain colder longer, maintaining dry-snow conditions but are prone to wind-slab formation in winter. Aspect alone doesn't determine hazard, but it modifies it based on season and weather.

### Wind Loading

**Wind-loaded slopes** are extremely hazardous. When wind blows snow across a ridge, it deposits snow on the lee (downwind) slope faster than gravity can consolidate it. These wind slabs are unstable and prone to triggering. Identify wind-loaded slopes by:

- **Looking for cornices** (overhanging snow formations) on ridge crests—these indicate wind deposition on the lee slope
- **Observing smooth, uniform snow surfaces** on lee slopes (compared to textured, wind-scoured surfaces on windward slopes)
- **Noting slope elevation**—wind loading is most severe just below ridgetops

Avoid crossing directly below cornices; they collapse and can trigger slides. Avoid crossing wind-loaded lee slopes without necessity.

### Terrain Traps

Even small slides become deadly in terrain traps—areas where sliding snow concentrates:

- **Gullies and couloirs** (ravines): Snow funnels into these and accumulates deeply. A small slide that seems harmless on the slope above becomes a deadly burial pit in the gully.
- **Cliff bands**: Terrain that steepens dramatically funnels slides toward cliffs. Terrain below cliffs is extremely hazardous during slide activity.
- **Stream channels**: Snow sliding into stream channels can be compacted by banks and terrain.

Whenever possible, route-plan to avoid terrain traps entirely.

### Slope History and Evidence

**Signs of past slides**:
- **Sparse vegetation or missing trees** in avalanche paths (vegetation is swept away and doesn't regenerate quickly)
- **Debris piles** (broken tree trunks, boulders) at slope bases—evidence of old slides
- **Scarps** (fresh breaks in snow showing older layers) indicate slides occurred recently

If a slope has slid recently, it's unstable. If a slope shows evidence of frequent slides, approach with extreme caution.

## Snowpack Evaluation and Stability Assessment

The snowpack is a layered structure—each storm adds a new layer, and these layers can fail along weak interfaces.

### Snowpack Stability Indicators

**Hand shear test**:
1. **Dig a small pit (3 feet wide, 2 feet deep) on a slope identical to your intended route** but slightly off to the side.
2. **Clear the snow to create a vertical wall** showing all layers clearly.
3. **Insert your hand into the snowpack and apply sideways force**, trying to shear a vertical column of snow. If snow shears cleanly with minimal resistance, stability is poor. If it compacts and holds, stability is better.

**Slope stability observation**:
- **Recent slides nearby** on similar slopes indicate current instability
- **Rapid settling or collapse under your boots** indicates weak bonding
- **Cracking radiating from your feet as you walk** (shooting cracks) indicates unstable snow under stress

:::warning
Shooting cracks or collapsing snow under your weight are red flags. Do not continue ascending. Descend to safer terrain immediately.
:::

### Weak Layer Identification

Snowpacks contain weak layers—thin zones of poor bonding that can fail:

- **Surface hoar**: Delicate crystals that form on the snow surface at night. If buried under new snow, they create a weak interface.
- **Depth hoar**: Coarse crystals that form in the lower snowpack during periods of temperature gradient (cold air above, warm below). These bond poorly and are a common failure layer.
- **Wind slab on weak layer**: A well-bonded slab of wind-compressed snow over a weak layer is extremely prone to failure as a unit.

Visual identification of weak layers requires a pit inspection. Look for layers that differ visually—coarser, darker, or structurally distinct—as indicators of potential instability.

### Temperature Effects

**Warming cycles**: Wet-snow avalanches are most common in spring when melting weakens the snowpack. After warm days, slope hazard increases markedly. Descend or avoid hazardous slopes during peak warming hours (midday).

**Refreezing at night**: Nighttime refreezing strengthens the snowpack. Early morning travel, before sun warming, is safest in spring conditions.

**Storm cycling**: During and immediately after storms, slopes are unstable from new loading. Wait 24-48 hours after storms when possible before traversing dangerous slopes.

## Self-Arrest Techniques and Survival If Caught

If you're caught in an avalanche and can't escape it, your immediate goal is staying near the surface.

### Self-Arrest Technique

If you fall on a steep slope (avalanche or otherwise) and begin to slide:

1. **Turn yourself face-down**, oriented head-upslope, digging your toes and hands into the snow for friction.

2. **Drive the shaft of an ice axe (if you have one) across your body into the snow**, using the pick for greatest penetration. Simultaneously press your body weight against the axe.

3. **Spread your legs wide** with toes dug in to increase friction and prevent tumbling.

4. **Arrest the slide and come to a stop** if possible. If the slope is too steep or snow too smooth, this may not be fully effective, but it reduces speed and gives you a chance.

Without an axe, friction from your body and limbs is limited. Do not attempt self-arrest without training—improper technique may accelerate sliding.

### Surviving Avalanche Burial

If you're fully buried:

1. **Remain calm and assess your position**. If you can move, create a small air pocket in front of your face immediately—suffocation is the primary killer of buried victims.

2. **Mark your position mentally if you sense any ground movement or compression** stopping. Note which direction is "up"—sometimes this is disorienting.

3. **Do not shout immediately**. Conserve energy; rescuers will search regardless. Shouting depletes oxygen and causes panic.

4. **If you can move, attempt to extricate yourself** by pushing upward, but assess the overlying snow depth first. Digging out from under 10+ feet of snow is nearly impossible alone.

5. **If you cannot move, establish an air pocket, control breathing, and wait** for rescue. Burial survival time varies from 15 minutes (if fully compressed with no air) to several hours if space allows breathing.

### Burial Depth and Survival Correlation

- **Buried less than 1 meter**: ~90% survival if rescued within 1 hour
- **Buried 1-2 meters**: ~70-80% survival with quick rescue
- **Buried over 2 meters**: Survival drops rapidly; most victims are fatal after 30 minutes

This emphasizes the importance of immediate companion rescue.

## Companion Burial Rescue Without Beacons

If a companion is buried in an avalanche and you have no beacon, rescue depends on visual clues, organized search, and quick action.

### Visual Clue Search (First 5 Minutes)

1. **Watch the person as they're caught**. Try to mark their last visible location mentally.

2. **Scan the avalanche debris immediately** for any visible parts (arm, leg, pack, hat).

3. **Look for depressions or disturbed snow** indicating burial depth or location.

4. **Shout to alert the buried person that rescue is coming** (optional; primarily for your communication).

If the burial is shallow and visible clues exist, hasty excavation may save the victim within minutes.

### Probe Line Search (Organized Rescue)

If no visual clues exist, conduct a systematic probe line search:

1. **Establish a search line** across the avalanche path at the estimated burial site. The line should be perpendicular to the fall line (the upslope/downslope direction).

2. **Each rescuer probes** with a pole, ski pole, or sturdy stick, inserting it into the snow every 30-50 cm as you walk. Probe vertically, at an angle, and at different depths.

3. **Move the entire line downslope together**, spacing rescuers about 1 meter apart. As you progress, overlap your probe locations.

4. **If a probe strikes something solid and resistance is different from normal snow** (resistance that feels like a body or pack), stop immediately. This is your burial location.

5. **Mark the probe location and begin excavation**, digging downward from above rather than sideways, to avoid injuring the buried person.

Probe line searches are laborious but systematic. Success depends on having enough searchers to cover the likely burial area thoroughly.

### Excavation Technique

Once you've located a burial:

1. **Create a rescue pit** surrounding the burial location. Excavate downslope and to the sides, creating a safe working area.

2. **Dig carefully as you approach the body**, using hands and small tools to avoid striking the buried person.

3. **Clear the face and head first** to establish an airway if the person is alive.

4. **Check for pulse and breathing**. If absent, assess burial depth and time—deep burial after 45+ minutes has very low survival probability.

5. **Remove the person from the snow** and provide first aid for injuries, hypothermia, or suffocation effects.

## Group Dynamics and Avalanche Prevention Protocol

The most effective avalanche safety strategy is avoiding hazardous terrain entirely through disciplined group decision-making.

### Pre-Trip Planning

Before traveling in avalanche terrain:

1. **Check avalanche forecasts** from local services. Forecasts rate danger from Low to Extreme.

2. **Discuss terrain hazards**, identifying slopes to avoid based on angle, aspect, and history.

3. **Establish route alternates** that avoid dangerous slopes or expose fewer people to risk.

4. **Brief everyone on the plan**, including decision points and abort conditions.

### Slope Traversal Discipline

When crossing potentially hazardous slopes:

1. **One person crosses at a time** while others watch from safe positions. This limits the number of people exposed if a slide initiates.

2. **Observe the crossing person for signs of instability** (cracking, collapsing, sounds of settling).

3. **If the first person safely reaches the opposite side**, others follow one at a time.

4. **Remove heavy packs if available** to reduce snow loading before crossing.

5. **Avoid grouping in the middle of slopes**. Stay near edges or terrain features when possible.

### Decision Points and Turnaround Criteria

Establish clear turnaround criteria before entering terrain:

- **If slope angle exceeds safe parameters** (steeper than 45° or steeper than 30° if snow conditions are poor), turn around
- **If new snow accumulation exceeds 12+ inches**, delay travel to allow settling
- **If visible signs of instability** (cracking, recent slides) appear, abandon the route
- **If forecast danger rating is "considerable" or higher**, avoid anything above 30° angle

Clear, pre-established criteria remove ego and debate from the decision—if criteria are met, you turnaround, period.

## Improvised Rescue Without Commercial Equipment

Commercial avalanche transceivers and probes are gold standard, but improvisation is possible:

**Probe alternatives**: Ski poles (removed from feet and extended by connecting), tent poles lashed together, or sturdy branches provide functional probes. Less efficient than commercial probes but serviceable.

**Beacon alternatives**: None exist. Beacon location requires electronic detection and cannot be improvised. Without a beacon, rescue depends entirely on visual clues and probe lines.

**Shovels**: Snow can be excavated with hands, ski edges, or axes. Slower than purpose-made shovels but functional.

**Rescue sleds or stretchers**: Use a pack, tarp, or improvised sled to lower an injured person from steep terrain if extraction is required.

In true avalanche terrain, carrying commercial safety gear (beacon, probe, shovel) per person is strongly recommended. Modern devices are lightweight and relatively inexpensive compared to the lives they save.

<section id="psychological-factors">

## Psychological Factors and Decision-Making

Avalanche accidents often result from poor judgment rather than unavoidable chance. Understanding psychological traps improves decision-making:

**Summit fever**: The drive to reach a goal (summit, campsite) despite deteriorating conditions. Counteract this by establishing turnaround times or points, independent of desire to proceed.

**Social proof**: Following others because they're proceeding. Examine conditions independently; others' choices don't confirm safety.

**Overconfidence**: Believing you're more experienced or lucky than you are. Maintain humility—experienced climbers trigger slides regularly.

**Groupthink**: Groups often make riskier decisions than individuals. Designate a "devil's advocate" to challenge consensus and evaluate hazards skeptically.

**Sunk cost fallacy**: Continuing because you've already invested time/effort. If conditions become unsafe, the prior investment is irrelevant—turn around.

Effective groups discuss hazards explicitly, challenge risky decisions, and maintain discipline even when tempted to proceed. This culture saves lives.
