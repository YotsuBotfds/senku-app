## Overview

Hemorrhoids are swollen veins in or around the anus and lower rectum. They can itch, burn, ache, or bleed a little, especially after straining or passing hard stool. They are common, and many cases improve with simple home care.

This guide is for anus and rectum symptoms. If the main complaint is burning behind the breastbone, reflux, or sour stomach after eating or lying down, route to heartburn-reflux-basic-care instead.
Small amounts of bright red blood after straining may fit hemorrhoids, but black tarry / sticky stool with dizziness or weakness, upper-GI bleed symptoms, or any bleeding with dizziness, weakness, pale skin, or fainting do not.
If the belly is getting hard or swollen, pain is moving higher into the abdomen, the person is vomiting, or stool and gas stop passing, do not keep this in hemorrhoid care.

This guide is for practical questions like:

- "My hemorrhoids hurt, what can I do?"
- "How do I stop making them worse?"
- "What helps when constipation keeps triggering them?"
- "When is rectal bleeding dangerous?"

This guide is for anal or rectal pain, soreness, burning, or bleeding questions. Urinary frequency or urgency without rectal or bowel-focused symptoms should route to the UTI section in common-ailments instead.

The short version:

1. Make stools softer and easier to pass.
2. Avoid straining and long sitting on the toilet.
3. Use gentle cleaning and comfort measures.
4. Do not assume every rectal bleed is a hemorrhoid.

## What Hemorrhoids Often Feel Like

Common hemorrhoid symptoms include:

- Itching or irritation around the anus
- Burning or soreness after a bowel movement
- A tender lump or swelling
- Small amounts of bright red blood on toilet paper or on the stool surface
- A feeling of pressure or incomplete emptying
- Discomfort worse after straining, lifting, or sitting a long time

Hemorrhoids often overlap with constipation. Hard stool and repeated straining are common triggers.

## What To Do Right Now

If symptoms are mild and you do not have red flags, start with these steps:

1. **Soften the stool.** Drink water, eat fiber-rich foods, and use the constipation guide if needed.
2. **Stop straining.** Do not sit on the toilet and push for a long time.
3. **Use a footstool.** Knees higher than hips can make bowel movements easier.
4. **Try a warm sitz bath or warm soak.** Sit in plain warm water for 10-15 minutes, especially after a bowel movement.
5. **Clean gently.** Use soft toilet paper, a damp cloth, or a rinse with water. Do not scrub.
6. **Pat dry.** Rubbing can make swelling and itching worse.
7. **Use comfort measures if available.** A cold pack wrapped in cloth can help swelling for short periods. Over-the-counter hemorrhoid creams or pads may help temporarily.

If you are using a cream or suppository, follow the label and do not use it longer than directed unless a clinician tells you to.

## Gentle Hygiene

The area should be kept clean, but aggressive washing can make irritation worse.

Good habits:

- Clean with water after a bowel movement if possible
- Use mild, unscented soap only on the outer skin if you need soap
- Avoid perfumed wipes, harsh cleansers, or strong scrubbing
- Change sweaty underwear when you can
- Wear loose, breathable clothing if tight clothes worsen discomfort
- Keep the skin dry with gentle patting instead of rubbing

If wiping is painful, rinsing with water and patting dry is often gentler than repeated dry wiping.

## Bowel Habits That Help

The most important long-term treatment is avoiding hard stool and straining.

Helpful habits:

- Do not ignore the urge to go
- Try to go around the same time each day, often after meals
- Sit only a few minutes if nothing happens
- Use a small footstool or squat-like position
- Increase fiber slowly so you do not create extra gas or cramping
- Drink enough water for the fiber to work
- Walk or move daily if possible

If constipation is the main trigger, treat the constipation directly. Hemorrhoids usually keep coming back if the stool stays hard.

## Foods And Fluids That Often Help

These are simple, practical options:

- Water through the day
- Prunes or prune juice
- Pears, kiwi, apples, and berries
- Oatmeal or other whole grains
- Beans, lentils, and vegetables if you tolerate them
- Soups and stews that add fluid plus fiber

Foods that can make things worse for some people:

- Very low-fiber meals
- Large amounts of cheese
- Too much alcohol
- Dry snacks without enough fluid

## Pain Relief And Comfort

For mild hemorrhoid pain or swelling:

- **Warm water** often helps more than cold for anal sphincter tension and soreness
- **Cold packs** can reduce swelling for a short time
- **Pain medicine** such as acetaminophen may help if you normally can take it safely
- Be more cautious with ibuprofen, naproxen, aspirin, and similar medicines if bleeding is active, you have stomach ulcers, kidney disease, are on blood thinners, or you are not sure the bleeding is truly from hemorrhoids
- If those medicines cause upper stomach burning, sour stomach, or reflux, use the heartburn guide or ask a clinician before keeping them up
- **Rest from heavy lifting** if lifting makes symptoms worse

If the pain is sharp, severe, or suddenly much worse than before, do not assume it is a simple hemorrhoid.

## What Makes Hemorrhoids Worse

Common triggers include:

- Straining on the toilet
- Constipation
- Sitting for long periods
- Heavy lifting
- Pregnancy
- Chronic cough
- Repeated diarrhea or frequent wiping

If you have a cough or a job that involves repeated lifting, reducing strain may help prevent flare-ups.

## When It Might Not Be Hemorrhoids

Some problems can look similar:

- **Anal fissure:** a small tear that can cause sharp pain during bowel movements
- **Rectal prolapse:** tissue that comes out and may not stay in
- **Abscess or infection:** swelling, warmth, fever, or worsening pain
- **Inflammation or bowel disease:** bleeding with diarrhea, weight loss, or ongoing symptoms
- **Other causes of rectal bleeding:** especially if you are older or the bleeding is new

Do not self-diagnose every case of rectal bleeding as hemorrhoids.

## When To Worry

These warning signs apply to anal or rectal bleeding, pain, lumps, or bowel-movement complaints. Urinary frequency or urgency by itself should stay in urinary guidance unless rectal or bowel symptoms are also present.

Get urgent medical help if you have:

- Heavy bleeding
- Bleeding that does not stop or keeps coming back
- Black, sticky, or tar-like stool, especially with dizziness, weakness, fainting, or pale skin
- Bright red bleeding with dizziness, weakness, fainting, pale skin, or shortness of breath
- Vomiting blood, coffee-ground vomit, or throwing up blood after alcohol
- Severe pain
- A lump or tissue that protrudes and will not go back in
- Fever, pus, or increasing redness/swelling
- Trouble passing stool or gas
- Trouble passing stool or gas, especially with vomiting or a swollen belly
- Dizziness, fainting, weakness, or shortness of breath
- Unexplained weight loss or major change in bowel habits

Also get checked if:

- This is the first time you have rectal bleeding
- You are older and new bleeding has started
- Blood seems mixed through the stool instead of just on the paper or stool surface
- Bleeding comes with abdominal pain, ongoing diarrhea, or a major change in bowel pattern
- Belly pain becomes localized to one tender spot, especially with fever, guarding, or worsening pain with movement
- Home care does not help after several days
- You keep having episodes despite soft stools

## Simple Prevention Pattern

If hemorrhoids keep coming back, this routine often helps:

1. Drink water regularly.
2. Eat fiber at most meals.
3. Treat constipation early.
4. Do not strain on the toilet.
5. Use gentle cleaning instead of scrubbing.
6. Keep heavy lifting and prolonged sitting in check when you can.

## Quick Rule

If the problem is mild itching, soreness, or a small amount of bright red blood after straining, start with stool softening, gentle hygiene, and warm soaking.

If the problem is heavy bleeding, severe pain, a prolapsed lump, fever, or anything that seems unlike your usual hemorrhoids, stop treating it as routine and get medical care.
