## Understanding Storm Systems

Severe storms range from isolated thunderstorms to large-scale hurricane and tornado systems. Understanding the structure and behavior of these systems allows you to recognize danger early and respond appropriately.

### Barometric Pressure and Weather Changes

Barometric pressure is one of the most reliable indicators of approaching severe weather. A barometer measures atmospheric pressure in millibars or inches of mercury.

- **Normal atmospheric pressure** at sea level is 1013 millibars (29.92 inches).
- **Rapid pressure drop** (more than 2 millibars per hour) indicates a strong storm system approaching.
- **Very low pressure** (below 980 millibars) indicates severe weather—hurricanes, tornadoes, or extreme thunderstorms.

**Without a barometer:** Watch for pressure-related body symptoms. Some people experience joint pain, sinus pressure, or ear discomfort when pressure drops. These sensations often precede visible weather changes.

### Cloud Formations and Weather Signs

Different cloud types indicate different weather conditions:

- **Cumulonimbus clouds** (towering, anvil-shaped) are thunderstorm clouds. They form rapidly and indicate hail, strong wind, and lightning risk.
- **Lenticular or lens-shaped clouds** near mountains or hills can indicate wind shear and severe updrafts on the downwind side.
- **Mammatus clouds** (pouch-like formations on cloud bases) often precede severe thunderstorms and hail.
- **Shelf clouds** (low, horizontal clouds at the leading edge of storms) indicate strong wind gusts are imminent.
- **Rotation or lowering** of cloud bases indicates tornado potential.

### Animal Behavior

Animals sense pressure and electrical changes before humans do:

- **Birds flee** rapidly when pressure drops. Sudden silence and departure of birds indicates approaching severe weather.
- **Insects** become more active, congregate near lights, or disappear into shelter before storms.
- **Dogs and cats** become anxious, hide, or pace before severe weather.
- **Livestock** huddle together, face wind, or move to higher ground before storms arrive.

### Wind Pattern Changes

- **Gusty, rotating winds** from changing directions indicate wind shear and tornado risk.
- **Sustained high winds** from a single direction that increase in speed indicate strong gust fronts from severe thunderstorms.
- **Sudden wind cessation** before extreme violence indicates a brief lull before the strongest winds arrive.

## Storm Shelter Hardening

Hardening a shelter against severe winds and flying debris significantly increases survival odds. Wind pressure increases with the square of wind speed—a 100 mph wind exerts four times the pressure of a 50 mph wind.

### Structural Assessment

Before hardening your home, assess its vulnerability:

- **Inspect roof attachment.** Is the roof bolted or simply nailed to the frame? Bolted roofs withstand much stronger winds.
- **Check for roof damage.** Missing shingles, loose flashing, or deteriorated fascia create entry points for wind.
- **Evaluate wall bracing.** Older homes without diagonal bracing in walls are vulnerable to collapse under wind pressure.
- **Examine foundation attachment.** Is the house bolted to the foundation or simply sitting on it? Bolting dramatically improves wind resistance.
- **Identify weak points.** Garage door openings, corner areas, and roof edges fail first under wind pressure.

### Roof Improvement

The roof is the first line of defense against wind and water:

- **Install hurricane ties or roof straps.** These metal connectors bridge the roof and wall, preventing roof separation. Installation costs ~$1000–3000 for an average home and is highly cost-effective.
- **Upgrade roof covering.** Asphalt shingles rated for 130+ mph wind speeds and Class A fire rating are standard. Metal roofing is even stronger.
- **Seal roof penetrations.** Vents, chimneys, and antenna bases create leak and structural weak points. Seal with metal flashing and roofing cement.
- **Reinforce gable ends** if your roof has overhanging gables. Gable failure is common in high winds. Brace or support overhanging gables.
- **Address sagging.** If your roof sags or dips, it indicates structural compromise. Reinforce or rebuild before storm season.

### Shutter and Window Hardening

Windows are vulnerability points. Wind entering through broken windows increases interior pressure, which can blow off the roof.

- **Install permanent storm shutters** (hurricane or impact-resistant). Metal panels, board-ups, or shutter systems prevent window penetration.
- **Upgrade to impact-resistant windows** rated for hurricane-force winds. These are expensive but dramatically improve performance.
- **Install plywood sheets** on windows if permanent shutters are not feasible. Pre-cut, labeled sheets nailed over windows provide reasonable protection. Store plywood and fasteners accessibly.
- **Protect glass doors.** Sliding glass doors and French doors fail readily. Reinforce with temporary shutters or plywood, or upgrade to impact-resistant doors.

### Garage Door Strengthening

A garage door failure allows wind inside the home, pressurizing it and increasing roof and wall failure risk.

- **Install a reinforcement bar system.** Horizontal and diagonal bracing on the inside of the garage door resists buckling.
- **Upgrade to a wind-rated garage door.** New doors rated for 130+ mph winds are much stronger than older designs.
- **Install storm shutters over garage doors** if replacement is not feasible.

### Water Intrusion Prevention

Severe storms bring heavy rainfall and wind-driven rain. Water infiltration damages structures and causes mold.

- **Caulk and seal gaps** around windows, doors, and exterior trim.
- **Install gutters and downspouts** that direct water away from the foundation.
- **Clear gutters** before storm season so water drains properly.
- **Seal roof penetrations** (vents, flashing) to prevent leaks.
- **Check for existing roof leaks.** Get them fixed before storm season.
- **Inspect and repair siding.** Loose or damaged siding allows water intrusion.

## Storm Surge Preparation

Storm surge is an extreme rise in sea level caused by a hurricane's wind pushing ocean water ashore. Storm surge can reach 20+ feet in extreme hurricanes and inundates coastal areas with saltwater that destroys structures, contaminates freshwater, and makes areas uninhabitable for months.

### Storm Surge Risk Assessment

- **Know your elevation and storm surge risk.** NOAA and FEMA provide surge inundation maps showing surge risk at different hurricane categories.
- **If you're in a surge zone**, evacuation is the only reliable defense. You cannot shelter in place during extreme surge.
- **Understand evacuation zone designations.** Most coastal areas have A, B, C, and sometimes D zones based on surge risk. Know your zone and evacuation order triggers.

### Securing Property Against Surge

If you remain in a lower-risk surge zone:

- **Elevate utilities** (electrical panels, HVAC, water heaters, generators) above anticipated surge levels.
- **Install check valves** in plumbing to prevent backflow of contaminated water into supply lines.
- **Create waterproof barriers** (sandbags, temporary barriers) around the foundation if surge is expected but minor.
- **Move vehicles and heavy items** away from the coast before storms approach.

### Post-Surge Contamination

Saltwater and sewage mixing in surge water creates severe contamination:

- **Do not drink or cook with surge-contaminated water.** The salt content is toxic and chemical contamination is extensive.
- **Do not enter floodwaters** if they contact utilities or fuel tanks—electrocution and chemical exposure risk are extreme.
- **Treat all water as contaminated** post-storm until supply integrity is verified by water authorities.

:::warning
**Evacuation from Storm Surge Is Difficult After Wind Increases.** Once tropical-force winds (39+ mph) arrive, evacuation becomes extremely dangerous due to flying debris and visibility loss. Evacuate before winds reach this threshold (usually 24–36 hours before landfall for hurricanes).
:::

## Flooding Response and Preparation

Storms cause flooding through heavy rainfall, storm surge, and drainage system failure. Urban and suburban areas prone to flooding require specific preparations.

### Flooding Risk Assessment

- **Understand flood zones.** FEMA flood maps show 100-year and 500-year flood zones. Properties in these zones require flood insurance.
- **Check property elevation.** If you're in a flood-prone area, elevation relative to rivers, streams, and drainage is critical.
- **Map evacuation routes** for flooding. Know which roads flood first and which remain passable longest.

### Flood-Resistant Improvements

- **Elevate critical systems** (electrical panels, HVAC, utilities) above expected flood level.
- **Install sump pumps** in basements or low areas to remove accumulated water during floods.
- **Use flood vents** in basement walls to allow water to drain rather than build pressure against walls.
- **Waterproof basement walls** with sealers and drainage systems.
- **Avoid finished basements** in flood zones—they will flood, and finishing materials are destroyed.

### Flood Evacuation

- **Leave early.** Don't wait for water to rise. Moving water is dangerous and can sweep vehicles away in surprisingly shallow depth (12–18 inches of moving water can destabilize a car).
- **Never drive through flooded roads.** The road may be washed out beneath water, or currents may be stronger than you anticipate.
- **Walk to higher ground if evacuation by vehicle is not possible.** Climb to upper floors or roofs in your immediate area. Rescue teams will find you.

### Post-Flood Safety

- **The water is contaminated.** It contains sewage, chemicals, and pathogens.
- **Do not enter** unless necessary for rescue. If you must enter, wear protective gear (gloves, boots, eye protection, respiratory protection if mold is present).
- **Remove water-damaged materials.** Drywall, insulation, and carpeting harbor mold and must be removed and replaced.
- **Disinfect surfaces** with bleach solution (1:10 ratio) after water is removed.
- **Run dehumidifiers and fans** to dry the structure. Mold growth begins within 24–48 hours of water intrusion.

## Tornado Safety

Tornadoes are violent rotating columns of air that form rapidly and cause extreme localized damage. Unlike hurricanes, tornado warning time is measured in minutes.

### Tornado Warning Signs

- **Dark, greenish sky** indicates hail and strong updrafts in storm clouds above.
- **Large hail** is a strong tornado indicator.
- **Loud roar** sounds like a freight train or jet engine—a tornado's wind signature.
- **Dust or debris** swirling at the base of a cloud indicates a rotating vortex.
- **Sudden wind changes** and wind direction rotation.

### Shelter During Tornadoes

- **Identify a tornado shelter.** Interior rooms on the lowest floor with no windows are safest: bathrooms (plumbing braces walls), closets, or purpose-built safe rooms.
- **Avoid gyms, auditoriums, or large open areas.** Roofs over large spans collapse more readily.
- **If outside with no shelter**, move perpendicular to the tornado's path. Tornadoes typically move northeast; moving north or south increases your chance of the tornado passing parallel to you rather than hitting you.
- **If trapped outside in open ground**, lie flat in a ditch or low area. Don't try to outrun a tornado by car if it's close.

### Safe Room Construction

A purpose-built safe room provides the best tornado protection:

- **Location:** Interior room on the lowest floor, no windows.
- **Dimensions:** 8–10 feet by 10–12 feet is adequate for a family.
- **Wall construction:** Reinforced concrete or concrete blocks, or wood frame with plywood and diagonal bracing.
- **Door:** Solid-core or metal door opening inward, with strong locks.
- **Shelter contents:** First aid kit, water, battery-powered radio, flashlights, blankets, documents.

Cost for a safe room is typically $2,000–8,000 depending on whether it's incorporated during construction or added afterward.

:::tip
**Practice Tornado Drills.** Teach children where the tornado shelter is and practice moving there quickly. During actual tornado warnings, you'll only have minutes to respond.
:::

## Post-Storm Assessment and Recovery

After a storm passes, assess damage and hazards before re-entering the home.

### Structural Assessment

- **Check for obvious damage.** Roof damage, wall collapse, broken windows, debris inside.
- **Look for foundation damage.** Cracks, shifting, or separation of the house from the foundation indicate serious damage.
- **Inspect electrical systems.** If the house is flooded or the electrical panel is damaged, turn off the main breaker and call an electrician before restoring power.
- **Check gas lines.** If you smell gas, leave the area, go to a neighbor's house, and call the gas company.

### Electrical Hazards

- **Do not restore power** if the house is wet or has flooding. Water and electricity are lethal.
- **Replace electrical panels, outlets, and switches** damaged by water or debris.
- **Do not use electrical appliances** in wet areas until they are completely dry and inspected.
- **Be aware of downed power lines.** Stay at least 50 feet away; assume all downed lines are energized.

### Water Damage Management

- **Shut off water at the main if leaks are visible.** Water damage worsens minute by minute.
- **Remove standing water** using pumps, wet vacuums, or manual removal. The sooner water is removed, the less structural and mold damage occurs.
- **Dry the structure** using fans, dehumidifiers, and ventilation. Open windows if weather allows.
- **Remove water-damaged materials.** Drywall, insulation, flooring, and carpeting damaged by water must be removed entirely—they cannot be saved.
- **Begin mold remediation immediately.** Mold starts growing within 24–48 hours. Dry quickly.

### Debris and Hazard Cleanup

- **Wear protective gear.** Heavy work gloves, steel-toed boots, eye protection, and respiratory protection (N95 or P100) are essential when handling storm debris.
- **Clear downed branches and debris** from around the house to improve visibility and prevent injury.
- **Check for contaminated materials.** Asbestos and lead paint are common in older homes. Hire professionals for removal.
- **Dispose of debris** according to local guidelines. Many areas have temporary debris collection following storms.

## Water Safety After Flooding

Floodwater is severely contaminated and flooding damages water infrastructure.

### Water Contamination

- **Floodwater contains sewage, chemicals, heavy metals, and pathogens.**
- **Do not drink flood-affected water** under any circumstances. Even brief exposure to contaminated water can cause serious illness.
- **Boiling floodwater does not remove chemical contamination.** It only kills pathogens.
- **Water systems may be compromised** for weeks after flooding due to treatment facility damage and line contamination.

### Water Restoration

- **Use bottled water** until local authorities declare the water system safe.
- **Flush your water lines** for several minutes once system integrity is restored.
- **Clean faucet aerators** and screens—they trap sediment.
- **Disinfect your water tanks and lines** if you have a private well system or storage tanks.
- **Test your well water** through a certified lab before resuming use of private wells.

## Emergency Communication Plans

During storms and post-storm chaos, communication systems fail. Advance planning helps you stay connected.

### Communication Tools and Redundancy

- **Designate an out-of-area contact person** (friend or relative not in the storm zone). This person is easier to reach than local emergency services. Everyone in your household calls them to report status.
- **Keep a physical list** of important phone numbers. Cell phones die and contacts are lost; written lists survive when electronics don't.
- **Battery-powered or hand-crank radio** allows you to hear emergency broadcasts.
- **NOAA weather radio** with battery backup provides weather alerts and emergency notifications.
- **Satellite communicator** or personal locator beacon allows you to call for help if infrastructure is down.

### Social Media and Online Notification

- **Use social media to post status updates** visible to friends and family. Facebook's "Safety Check" feature is automatically activated during major disasters.
- **Text messages are more reliable than phone calls** during network congestion. Prioritize texting your out-of-area contact.

## Supply Caching and Preparedness

Storm-damaged areas can be isolated for weeks. Supplies must be cached (stored locally) before storms arrive.

### Essential Supply List

- **Water:** 1 gallon per person per day for at least 2 weeks (14+ gallons for a family of 4).
- **Food:** Non-perishable food requiring no refrigeration or cooking (canned goods, crackers, nuts, protein bars, baby formula if applicable).
- **Medications:** At least 2 weeks of prescription medications in original labeled containers.
- **First aid kit:** Bandages, antiseptic, pain relievers, anti-diarrhea, anti-nausea, thermometer, tweezers.
- **Lighting:** Flashlights with fresh batteries, candles, matches.
- **Fuel:** Generator fuel, chainsaw fuel (if you have a generator or chainsaw).
- **Tools:** Hand tools (hammer, saw, pry bar, axe), heavy-duty tarps and rope for temporary repairs.
- **Sanitation:** Portable toilet, trash bags, hand sanitizer, hygiene products.
- **Documents:** Copies of insurance policies, deeds, IDs in a waterproof container.

### Cache Location

- **Store supplies in multiple locations.** A garage, basement, and shed each holding separate caches increases odds of survival if part of your property is destroyed.
- **Use waterproof containers.** Heavy plastic bins with lids protect supplies from water and pests.
- **Rotate stock.** Food and medication expire. Replace supplies annually or on expiration dates.

## Community Storm Shelter Design

In areas subject to frequent severe storms, community shelters provide refuge for the homeless, those in inadequate shelter, or those with medical conditions requiring electricity.

### Shelter Specifications

- **Location:** Central and accessible to most of the community. Avoid flood zones and areas prone to wind tunneling.
- **Structure:** Reinforced concrete, or solid construction rated for 150+ mph winds.
- **Interior design:** Interior rooms without windows, multiple restrooms, water supply, capacity for generators.
- **Capacity:** Sized to shelter 5–10% of community population initially; can expand if needed.
- **Supplies:** Water, food, first aid, medications, battery-powered fans and lighting, blankets.
- **Accessibility:** ADA-compliant (elevators with backup power, accessible restrooms), accessible parking.

### Community Coordination

- **Pre-storm registration.** Identify those who need shelter (medically dependent, homeless, those in mobile homes) and pre-register them before storm season.
- **Communication plan.** Notify shelter-eligible people when shelters open.
- **Staff and volunteers.** Train and schedule staff and volunteers to operate the shelter 24 hours during activation.
- **Supply management.** Pre-position water, food, and supplies before storm season; rotate to maintain freshness.

## Summary and Action Items

Storm preparedness involves understanding warning signs, hardening your shelter against wind and water, planning evacuation, and caching supplies. No single action prevents disaster, but combined measures dramatically improve survival odds and post-storm recovery speed.

**Immediate priorities:**
1. Assess storm risk for your location (FEMA flood maps, local hazard assessment).
2. Identify tornado shelter and evacuation routes.
3. Cache water, food, medications, and supplies for 2 weeks.
4. Install window protection or upgrade to impact-resistant windows.

**Before storm season:**
1. Clear gutters and trim trees to prevent wind damage.
2. Test emergency communication plan with out-of-area contact.
3. Verify emergency supply stock and rotate expired items.
4. Review insurance coverage and confirm documents are accessible.
