## Overview

Lice, scabies, bed bugs, and fleas are all small parasites that make people miserable, keep them from sleeping, and spread fast in crowded or low-water living conditions.

They are not the same problem:
- **Lice** live on hair or clothing and spread by close contact, hats, combs, bedding, and shared clothing.
- **Scabies** burrow into skin and spread by prolonged skin contact, bedding, and close household contact.
- **Bed bugs** hide in furniture, seams, and sleeping areas and bite at night.
- **Fleas** usually come from pets, bedding, or animals and jump onto people.

The first goal is not perfection. The first goal is to stop spread, reduce biting, and get clean clothes and clean bedding back into use.

:::tip
If several people in the same house are itching at the same time, think beyond "bug bites" and check for lice, scabies, bed bugs, or fleas together.
:::

### Quick routing for parasite and skin infestation questions

- "everyone in the house is itching" -> check all four parasites; start with the identification table
- "how to prevent lice spread" -> stop sharing combs, hats, and bedding; wet-comb affected hair
- "how to avoid getting scabies" -> avoid prolonged skin contact with affected people; wash bedding
- "bed bugs how to stop them spreading" -> reduce clutter near beds, wash bedding, inspect seams
- "fleas from pets getting on people" -> treat the pet first, then wash pet bedding and vacuum
- "parasite exposure in shared living space" -> separate clean and dirty items, then treat contacts together
- "bed bugs in bedding, mattress seams, or sleeping areas" -> bag soft items, wash and dry them, and inspect seams repeatedly
- "dirty blankets, pillows, or clothing from an infested room" -> separate them from clean items first, then wash or bag for later treatment
- "rodent droppings or chewed food in a pantry or grain room" -> hand off to `rodent-control-food-storage.md`

### Preventing parasite exposure and containing spread

Parasite exposure usually starts from close contact with an affected person, contaminated bedding, or an infested pet. In crowded or shared housing, lice, scabies, and bed bugs can move between rooms before anyone realizes the problem. Prevention means not sharing bedding, clothing, or grooming items; keeping sleeping areas clean and uncluttered; and checking pets regularly. Containment means treating all affected people at the same time, washing bedding and clothing, and isolating items that cannot be washed right away. The sections below cover recognition, first-response steps, and specific control for each parasite.

## Quick Identification

| Problem | Common signs | Where to look | Typical clue |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Head lice | Itching, scratch marks, visible nits stuck to hair shafts | Behind ears, nape of neck, scalp hair | Tiny eggs glued to hair, not shaken off easily |
| Body lice | Itching, rash, scratch marks, egg cases in clothing seams | Clothing seams, waistbands, bedding | Itch is often worse where clothes fit tightly |
| Scabies | Intense itching, often worse at night; small bumps; burrows | Between fingers, wrists, elbows, waist, groin, ankles | Other close contacts itch too |
| Bed bugs | Bites after sleeping, small blood spots, dark specks in seams | Mattress seams, bed frame, headboard, cracks near bed | You see signs in the bed, not on the skin |
| Fleas | Itchy bites, often ankles or lower legs; pets scratching | Pet bedding, rugs, cracks, animal resting spots | Pets are often the source |

Do not rely on bite patterns alone. Skin reactions look similar across different causes. The environment matters:
- Hair and scalp point toward lice.
- Clothing seams point toward body lice.
- Nighttime bedroom signs point toward bed bugs.
- Pets and pet bedding point toward fleas.
- Widespread nighttime itch with household spread points toward scabies.

<section id="first-24-hours">

## First 24 Hours: Stop Spread First

When you suspect any of these parasites, act the same day.

1. Put worn clothing, bedding, and towels into a separate pile.
2. Do not share hats, combs, brushes, bedding, or coats.
3. Change into clean clothing if you have it.
4. Wash hands after touching itchy skin, bedding, or infested clothing.
5. Trim fingernails short to reduce skin damage from scratching.
6. Keep the sleeping area uncluttered so you can inspect and clean it.
7. Mark which people and which beds may be involved so you do not lose track.

If only one person is affected, still check close household contacts. Lice and scabies often spread before the first person realizes what is happening.

:::warning
Do not keep sleeping in the same untreated bedding if you can avoid it. Re-exposure will make it feel like nothing is working.
:::

## Lice: Recognition and Practical Control

Lice are tiny insects that live on the body. Head lice are the most common. They feed on blood and glue their eggs, called nits, to hair shafts.

### Signs of Head Lice

- Itching on the scalp, especially behind the ears and at the nape of the neck
- Scratch marks and irritated skin
- Small tan, brown, or white eggs stuck to hair close to the scalp
- Live lice moving quickly through hair, though they can be hard to see

### Nit Removal

Nit removal is one of the most useful low-tech tools.

What you need:
- A fine metal lice comb if possible
- Conditioner or water to slow the lice and help the comb pass through hair
- A bright light
- A towel or white cloth

Steps:
1. Wet the hair or coat it lightly with conditioner.
2. Part the hair into small sections.
3. Comb from scalp to hair ends slowly.
4. Wipe the comb on a towel or rinse it after each pass.
5. Check the comb and towel for lice and nits.
6. Repeat section by section until the whole scalp is done.

Repeat combing every 2 to 3 days for 2 weeks if possible. One combing is usually not enough.

### Washing Routine for Lice

- Wash hair with regular shampoo or soap if available.
- Wash combs, brushes, hats, pillowcases, and recently worn clothes.
- Dry hair fully before sleep if possible.
- Recheck the scalp in bright light after treatment.

### Clothing and Bedding

For body lice, clothing matters as much as the body:
- Wash the clothing the person wore recently.
- Focus on seams, waistbands, cuffs, and collars.
- Change into clean clothes after washing.
- Do not re-wear the same unwashed clothing day after day.

### What Helps

- Wet combing
- Regular washing
- Clean clothing and bedding
- Treating all close contacts at the same time if lice are confirmed

### What Does Not Solve It Alone

- A single quick combing
- Spraying the room while leaving dirty clothes in use
- Washing the hair but not the bedding or hats

<section id="scabies">

## Scabies: Recognition and Control

Scabies is caused by a mite that burrows into skin. It usually causes intense itching, often worse at night. It spreads easily in close living quarters.

### Common Signs

- Intense itching, often worse after dark
- Small red bumps, scratch marks, and crusts
- Burrow-like lines or tiny tracks in the skin
- Itch between fingers, on wrists, elbows, waist, buttocks, groin, or ankles
- Several people in the same home itching at the same time

### Important Reality Check

Scabies usually needs a real medicine treatment to cure it. Cleaning alone helps limit spread, but it usually does not eliminate the mite from the skin.

If you have access to a clinician, pharmacy, or clinic, use that path.

Also remember that **itching can continue after treatment**. Ongoing itch by itself does not always mean the mites are still active. Look for new burrows, new household cases, or clearly worsening rash before assuming treatment failed.

### Low-Tech Supportive Steps

These steps do not replace treatment, but they help:
- Keep fingernails short.
- Wash bedding, pajamas, and recently worn clothes.
- Avoid close skin-to-skin contact until treatment is underway.
- Change into clean clothes after washing.
- Reduce scratching so the skin does not get infected.

### Treating Contacts

If one person in a household has confirmed scabies, close contacts often need treatment too. If only one person is treated, the infestation can keep cycling through the group.

### Crusted Scabies Warning

Crusted scabies is a severe form with very heavy mite burden and high spread risk. Think about it when the skin is thickly crusted, scaly, or widespread, especially in a person who is elderly, debilitated, or immunocompromised. This needs prompt medical care if at all possible.

### Cleaning the Environment

Scabies mites do not live long away from human skin, so focus on clothing and bedding rather than spraying the house.

Useful steps:
- Strip bedding carefully so loose skin flakes, lint, and bugs do not spread through the room.
- Wash and dry bedding, towels, and clothing used recently.
- Bag items that cannot be washed until they can be treated.
- Clean sleeping areas and discard lint and skin flakes.
- Vacuum sleeping surfaces if you have a vacuum.

Avoid turning your entire shelter upside down with random pesticides. That usually adds risk without solving the real problem.

## Bed Bugs: Recognition and Control

Bed bugs hide where people sleep and come out at night to feed. They do not live on the skin like lice and do not burrow like scabies.

### Signs

- Bites that appear after sleeping
- Blood spots on sheets or pillowcases
- Dark specks along mattress seams, bed frames, or headboards
- Small flat brown insects hidden in cracks and seams
- Repeated bites in the same sleeping place

### Bed Bug Inspection

Inspect:
- Mattress seams
- Box springs or sleeping mats
- Bed frame joints
- Headboards
- Cracks in nearby walls or furniture
- Behind loose trim, pictures, or clutter near the bed

### Practical Control

1. Reduce clutter near sleeping areas.
2. Pull beds away from walls if possible.
3. Wash bedding and sleepwear.
4. Vacuum seams, cracks, and edges if you have a vacuum.
5. Dry heat-treated items thoroughly if you can do so safely.
6. Inspect and repeat, because bed bugs hide well.

Expect to recheck sleeping areas repeatedly over days to weeks. One clean-up round rarely proves the problem is gone.

### Heat and Heat Caution

Heat can help kill bed bugs, but only if the item gets hot enough all the way through. Surface warmth is not enough.

Do not:
- Place flammable items near open flame
- Guess at heat treatment with bedding that may smolder
- Move infested furniture through other sleeping areas without checking it first

If you can safely use a dryer or controlled heat source, follow the appliance instructions. If you cannot, focus on washing, inspection, and containment.

</section>

<section id="fleas">

## Fleas: Recognition and Pet Crossover

Fleas often come from dogs, cats, rodents, bedding, or areas where animals rest. They are a human problem and an animal problem at the same time.

### Signs

- Itchy bites, often on ankles or lower legs
- Pets scratching, biting, or grooming constantly
- Tiny dark specks in pet bedding or where the animal sleeps
- Bites that keep returning after you thought the problem was gone

### Household Control

- Wash pet bedding, blankets, and resting mats.
- Vacuum rugs, furniture, and cracks near pet areas.
- Keep animals out of sleeping areas until the infestation is under control.
- Check for fleas in the place where the pet spends the most time.
- Keep rechecking pet areas and bedding over the next several days to weeks so you do not stop too early.

### Pet Safety

Use species-appropriate flea control only.

Do not:
- Put dog flea products on cats unless the product is specifically labeled for cats
- Use household insecticides on an animal's skin
- Guess at a homemade dip or bath that could poison the pet

If the animal is weak, very young, pregnant, or has a large flea burden, get veterinary help if possible. Heavy flea infestations can cause anemia, especially in small animals and kittens.

## Laundry, Bedding, and Clothing Treatment

Laundry is one of the most important control tools for lice, body lice, and fleas, and it helps with scabies and bed bugs too.

### Best Available Option

If you have access to a washer and dryer:
1. Wash clothing, bedding, and towels in hot water if the fabric can handle it.
2. Dry on high heat if the fabric can handle it.
3. Put clean items straight into a clean container or bag.

### If You Do Not Have a Washer

- Hand wash clothing and bedding with soap and clean water.
- Rinse well.
- Dry fully in sun, wind, or near a safe heat source.
- Keep clean items separate from dirty items.

### Bagging Items

For items that cannot be washed right away:
- Seal them in a bag or container.
- Keep them separate from clean clothing.
- Label the bag with the date.

This is useful for bed bugs and for clothing that may need later treatment.

### Reuse Order

If supplies are limited, prioritize:
- Underwear and socks
- Sleepwear and bedding
- Hats, scarves, and coats that touch the head or body closely
- Towels

### Combs, Brushes, and Shared Items

Clean combs and brushes after use.
- Remove visible hair first.
- Wash in hot soapy water if possible.
- Do not share them between people until the infestation is controlled.

<section id="low-tech-measures">

## Safer Low-Tech Control Measures

These steps are useful when commercial products are limited.

- Wet comb lice out of hair with a fine comb.
- Keep nails short to reduce skin damage from scratching.
- Wash bedding and clothing consistently.
- Vacuum sleeping areas if available.
- Reduce clutter around beds and seating areas.
- Separate clean clothes from dirty clothes.
- Keep pets out of beds until fleas are controlled.
- Check household contacts, not just the first person who complained.

Low-tech does not mean "home remedy at any cost." It means using cleaning, separation, inspection, and repeatable routines instead of dangerous shortcuts.

</section>

<section id="what-not-to-do">

## What Not to Do

Do not:
- Put gasoline, kerosene, paint thinner, bleach, or strong solvents on skin or hair
- Use pesticide sprays, foggers, or dusts on people
- Rub harsh chemicals into a child's scalp
- Assume one treatment is enough for lice or bed bugs
- Ignore bedding and clothing while only treating the body
- Share hats, brushes, pillows, coats, or towels during an infestation
- Scratch until the skin breaks open
- Use dog flea products on cats
- Use essential oils as a cure, especially on infants or sensitive skin
- Throw away every belonging before you have tried cleaning and containment

If a product is not labeled for human skin or for the animal you are treating, do not improvise.

</section>

<section id="red-flags">

## Red Flags: Get Help Promptly

Seek medical or veterinary help if you can when you see:
- Fever
- Pus, yellow crusting, or spreading redness
- Skin that is hot, swollen, very painful, or rapidly worsening
- Sores from scratching that are not healing
- Trouble sleeping for days because of the itching
- Scabies in an infant, or a rash in a very young child
- Infestation in a pregnant person or someone with immune problems
- Severe flea burden in a small pet, kitten, or puppy
- Signs of anemia in a pet such as weakness, pale gums, or collapse
- Unclear rash that is spreading or not matching the usual patterns

:::danger
If someone has a fever plus skin infection signs, treat it as more than a simple infestation. Scratching can open the door to a serious bacterial infection.
:::

</section>

<section id="simple-routines">

## Simple Routines That Actually Help

If you need a short routine to follow, use this:

### Daily

- Check the itching person or pet for worsening signs.
- Change into clean underwear and sleepwear.
- Keep nails short.
- Keep bedding and sleeping areas tidy.

### Every 2 to 3 Days

- Comb lice if head lice are present.
- Recheck bedding seams and clothing seams.
- Wash recently used linens and clothing as needed.

### Weekly

- Vacuum sleeping areas and pet resting areas if possible.
- Reinspect beds, couches, and mats.
- Confirm that pets are on species-appropriate parasite control.

Consistency matters more than dramatic action. Most infestations persist because people do one clean-up and then stop too early.

</section>

<section id="summary">

## Summary

For lice, focus on nit combing, clean hair, and clean clothing.
For scabies, focus on real treatment if available, plus washing and contact control.
For bed bugs, focus on the bed, seams, clutter, and repeat inspection.
For fleas, focus on pets, pet bedding, and the living area together.

When in doubt, stop sharing items, wash what was recently used, and look for the source instead of guessing at a cure.
