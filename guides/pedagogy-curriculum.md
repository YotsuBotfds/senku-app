<section id="why">

## Why Pedagogy Matters

Knowledge is survival. In collapse scenarios, societies that preserve and transmit knowledge avoid descent into a new dark age. The difference between a community that rebuilds and one that merely persists lies in effective knowledge transfer across generations.

In offline contexts, you cannot rely on digital tools, institutional credentials, or external validation. You must become the repository of knowledge, then teach others to teach. The methods you employ will shape whether your knowledge multiplies or dies with you.

## Quick Routing For Mixed-Age Classroom Questions

- `how do we teach in one room with mixed ages` or `one-room schoolhouse` -> `education-teaching`
- `how should classroom space be laid out` -> `education-system-design` and `accessible-shelter-design`
- `how do we make a learning space accessible and usable` -> `accessible-shelter-design`
- `how do we plan lessons for different ages` -> `teacher-training-non-educators`

:::warning
**Critical truth:** The greatest vulnerability of knowledge systems is a single point of failure. One teacher dying, one book burning, or one person forgetting—and entire skill domains vanish. Distribute knowledge widely through multiple learners and teachers.
:::

<section id="learning-theory">

## Learning Theory Fundamentals

### Behaviorism: Practice & Reinforcement

**Principle:** People learn through stimulus, response, and reinforcement. Repeat a behavior, reward it, and it becomes ingrained. Excellent for skill acquisition and habit formation.

**Offline advantage:** Requires no technology. Works perfectly through repetitive practice, immediate feedback, and rewards.

### Constructivism: Active Building

**Principle:** People actively construct understanding through experience and reflection, not absorbing knowledge passively. Produces deeper understanding and longer retention.

**Offline advantage:** Perfectly suited to resource-limited contexts. A child learns fractions by dividing a pie and eating pieces.

### Cognitive Load Theory: Working Memory Limits

**Principle:** Human working memory is limited (~7 items at once). Instruction should not overload this capacity. Break complex material into manageable chunks.

**Application:** Ten simple, focused lessons work better than one overwhelming lecture.

<section id="scaffolding">

## Scaffolding & Zone of Proximal Development

**Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD):** The gap between what a learner can do alone and what they can do with expert help. **Scaffolding:** is the temporary support you provide to bridge that gap.

### Scaffolding Techniques

-   **Modeling:** Show exactly how to do it first. "Watch me form this letter. Now you try."
-   **Guided practice:** Do it together, gradually reducing support
-   **Questioning:** Ask questions that lead toward understanding. "What do you notice? What comes next? Why?"
-   **Breaking down tasks:** Divide complex tasks into smaller steps
-   **Removing support gradually:** As competence grows, remove scaffolds
-   **Increasing complexity:** Master simple before complex

**Common mistakes:** Starting too high, removing support too quickly, never removing support, teaching to the middle and ignoring individual differences.

</section>

<section id="spaced">

## Spaced Repetition & Retrieval Practice

**Core principle:** Memory is retrieval. A fact you never retrieve is a fact you've forgotten. Spacing repetition over time and forcing retrieval dramatically improves long-term retention.

### Optimal Spacing Pattern

Review after: 1 day, 3 days, 7 days, 14 days, 30 days, then monthly. By the 6th review, information is typically permanent.

### Retrieval Methods

-   Flashcards (physical cards with question on one side, answer on the other)
-   Oral quizzing (ask learners questions without advance warning)
-   Written tests and quizzes
-   Practical demonstrations ("Show me how to filter water")
-   Free recall (learner writes what they remember about a topic)
-   Elaboration questions ("Explain why this works")

**Offline implementation:** Create a simple review schedule. Keep a calendar or notebook. Each skill has scheduled review dates.

<section id="literacy">

## Literacy Instruction

### Phonics-Based Sequence

1.  Letter recognition and sound
2.  Letter formation (handwriting)
3.  Basic phonemes (common sounds)
4.  Blending (sound-by-sound)
5.  Sight words (irregular words: the, said, of)
6.  Reading simple texts (decodable readers)
7.  Fluency (repeated reading until automatic)
8.  Comprehension (literal to inference to analysis)

### Adult Literacy

Adults learn faster than children but need:

-   **Practical motivation:** Anchor learning in immediate goals (read medicine labels, write letters)
-   **Safe environment:** Private learning; never shame an adult
-   **Sentence-based method:** Meaningful sentences often work better than abstract phonics
-   **Frequent short sessions:** 30 minutes daily better than 2-hour weekend sessions

</section>

<section id="numeracy">

## Numeracy Progression

Mathematical thinking progresses from concrete to abstract:

-   **Counting (2-4):** Rote memorization of number words. Fingers, objects, songs.
-   **Cardinality (3-5):** Understanding that the last number counted = total quantity
-   **Arithmetic (5-8):** Addition and subtraction with physical objects, then drawings, then symbols
-   **Place value (6-8):** Understanding that 13 = 1 ten and 3 ones. Use bundled sticks or coins.
-   **Multiplication (7-9):** Repeated addition and arrays. 3 groups of 4 visualized as blocks in rows and columns.
-   **Division (8-10):** Sharing fairly and grouping
-   **Fractions (8-10):** Break a pie into pieces, share food, measure land. 1/2 is half a pie first, then the symbol.
-   **Algebra (10-14):** Finding unknowns. Use balance scales: "What weight balances?"
-   **Geometry (6+):** Shapes, area, perimeter. Start with physical shapes, measure real spaces.

**Key principle:** Always start with objects or drawings. Symbols come last. Speed doesn't matter; understanding does.

</section>

<section id="apprenticeship">

## Apprenticeship Structures

**Most resource-efficient way to teach practical skills.** A skilled person teaches a less-skilled person through imitation, guided practice, and gradual responsibility increase.

### Progression

1.  **Foundation (months 1-6):** Learn tool handling, safety, terminology. Watch constantly. Perform only simplest tasks.
2.  **Assisted Practice (months 6-18):** Attempt simple tasks under close supervision. Immediate feedback.
3.  **Guided Independence (months 18-36):** Work with decreasing supervision. Make mistakes and correct them.
4.  **Independent Work (years 3-5):** Produce work independently. Begin teaching newer apprentices.
5.  **Mastery (year 5+):** Work independently, manage others, innovate, train.

</section>

<section id="assessment">

## Competency-Based Assessment

**Measure whether someone can actually do the thing, not relative performance.**

### Assessment Methods

-   **Practical demonstrations:** Learner performs the skill while observer watches
-   **Rubrics:** Detailed scoring guides describing levels of competency (novice, developing, proficient, advanced)
-   **Oral exams:** Questions asked aloud; flexible and allows follow-up
-   **Portfolio assessment:** Collect evidence over time: writing samples, drawings, photographs, objects created
-   **Self/peer assessment:** Learners evaluate themselves or each other against rubrics

</section>

<section id="common-mistakes">

## Common Mistakes in Education

-   **Confusing activity with learning:** A busy classroom feels productive but may not produce learning. Assess frequently.
-   **Teaching to the middle:** Ignoring advanced and struggling learners. Use differentiation and multi-age structures.
-   **Speed over understanding:** Fast learning is often shallow. Mastery matters more than speed.
-   **Teaching in isolation:** Always show application. "When will I use this?"
-   **Hoarding knowledge:** Every master should have apprentices. Document knowledge. Create redundancy.
-   **Not assessing frequently:** Catch misunderstandings early, not after weeks of wasted time.
-   **Punishment without understanding:** Investigate root causes before reacting. A learner who doesn't complete work may be confused, not defiant.
-   **Not recording progress:** Without records, progress is unknown. Maintain a ledger for each learner.

</section>

<section id="differentiation">

## Differentiation & Multi-Age Teaching

In resource-limited settings, you cannot run separate classes for each ability level. Multi-age teaching (combined age groups in single classroom) is historically normal and surprisingly effective.

### Differentiation Strategies

**By task:** Assign same topic, different depth. For a lesson on plants: beginners identify 3 common plants + draw them; intermediate students identify 10 plants + describe growing conditions; advanced students identify 15 plants + record seasonal changes over time.

**By pace:** Allow students to progress at own speed through curriculum rather than fixed pace. A student who masters fractions in 8 weeks continues to decimals; another student takes 12 weeks—both move forward.

**By support:** Struggling learners get more scaffolding (guided practice, questioning, modeling). Proficient learners get independence. Use mixed-ability peer tutoring: struggling student paired with proficient student; proficient student explains (teaching deepens understanding), struggling student gets peer support.

**By groups:** Flexible grouping by topic/skill, not fixed "high/low groups." A student advanced in mathematics might need support in writing—placed differently in different subjects.

:::tip
Multi-age classrooms naturally allow differentiation. Younger students observe and learn from older students. Older students reinforce learning by teaching younger students. A 10-year-old reteaching a concept to a 7-year-old deepens their own understanding.
:::

:::info-box
**Mixed-age research insight:** Studies of historical one-room schoolhouses and modern mixed-age classrooms show academic outcomes equal or better than age-segregated classrooms, plus additional social benefits: younger students learn from older, older students develop leadership skills, and the class feels more like a family than an institution.
:::

### One-Room Schoolhouse Model

Historical effectiveness provides a template for post-collapse education:

1. **Vertical curriculum:** Younger students (5-7) focus on literacy and numeracy foundations. Older students (8-10) extend into science, geography, history. Oldest (11-14) work toward specialization and mentoring.

2. **Simultaneous instruction:** Teacher manages multiple lesson stations. Students rotate or work on assignment while teacher works with one group intensively. 30-45 minute blocks per subject before rotation.

3. **Student responsibility:** Older students get assigned learning tasks that occupy them (reading assignments, workbooks, project work) while teacher instructs younger students. Accountability system: daily check-in or test on work completed.

4. **Peer instruction:** Older students review younger students' work, provide immediate feedback, answer questions. Codifies knowledge (better understanding through teaching) and frees teacher time.

5. **Shared resources:** Limited books rotated by group. Wall charts, blackboard, slate tablets reduce need for individual materials.

</section>

<section id="assessment-practical">

## Practical Assessment Methods

Assessment serves two purposes: (1) gather evidence of what learner can do (summative), (2) identify what to teach next (formative).

### Quick Daily Assessments (Formative)

-   **Exit ticket (5 minutes):** At lesson end, each student answers 1-2 questions on paper or slate. Collect and review. Reveals gaps quickly.
-   **Thumbs check:** Teacher asks "Do you understand?" Students respond with thumbs (up=yes, middle=sorta, down=no). Instant feedback on pacing.
-   **Pair-share:** Students pair, explain concept to partner, then pairs share key insights with class. Catches misconceptions immediately.
-   **Whiteboard responses:** All students show answer simultaneously (small boards or paper). Reveals which students are struggling.

### Detailed Demonstrations (Summative)

**Practical skill assessment:** Learner demonstrates skill with materials/tools while observer watches. Scoring rubric guides assessment: Does learner demonstrate safe handling? Correct sequence? Acceptable output quality? Record date, skill, proficiency level, notes.

**Portfolio assessment:** Collect work samples over time (writing pieces, drawings, test scores, photographs of projects). Portfolio shows growth trajectory better than single snapshot. Useful for motivation: "Remember this drawing from 3 months ago? Look at your improvement!"

**Oral exam:** Especially valuable in low-literacy contexts. Teacher asks questions; learner responds verbally. Can probe deeper: "Explain why" and "What if..." questions reveal understanding vs. rote memorization.

### Recording Progress

Create simple ledger for each learner:

| Date | Skill | Proficiency | Notes |
|------|-------|-------------|-------|
| 2026-02-01 | Phoneme blending | Developing | Blends simple CVC words, struggles with digraphs |
| 2026-02-15 | Phoneme blending | Proficient | Accurately blends all CVC and simple digraph words |
| 2026-02-20 | Simple addition (within 10) | Beginning | Counts on fingers, accurate with objects present |
| 2026-03-05 | Simple addition (within 10) | Proficient | Quick recall, rarely counts fingers |

Progress visible to both teacher and learner. Builds motivation when learner sees documented improvement.

:::info-box
**Key insight:** In survival contexts, you track not to assign grades (which matter only for institutional filtering) but to optimize teaching. You need to know who understands what, so you can provide appropriate help. Records are tools for learning, not punishment instruments.
:::

<section id="teaching-subjects">

## Subject-Specific Teaching Strategies

### Reading Comprehension (Beyond Decoding)

Once students can decode, teaching must move to comprehension:

1. **Before reading:** Activate prior knowledge ("What do you know about farmers?"). Set purpose ("Read to find 3 problems the farmer faced").

2. **During reading:** Pause to check understanding. Ask questions. Allow think-aloud (teacher models internal thoughts: "I'm confused here because... let me reread").

3. **After reading:** Comprehension questions (literal: "What did the farmer do?" inferential: "Why did the farmer do this?" evaluative: "Was the farmer's decision wise?").

4. **Vocabulary:** New words learned in context better than memorization. Discuss word meanings when encountered. Create word walls (written list on classroom wall) for reference.

### Mathematics Progression (Beyond Rote)

Problem-solving requires understanding, not procedure memorization.

**Concrete-representational-abstract (CRA) progression:**

- **Concrete:** Use physical objects (sticks, stones, coins). "Here are 5 stones and 3 stones. Push them together. How many?" Child counts: 8.

- **Representational:** Use drawings. "I'll draw 5 circles and 3 circles. How many?" Child counts or uses known fact.

- **Abstract:** Use symbols only. "5 + 3 = ?" Child retrieves fact or decomposes: 5 + 3 = 5 + 2 + 1 = 8.

Each learner progresses at own pace through levels. Struggle at abstract? Return to concrete/representational. No shame—understanding is the goal.

**Word problems:** Vital application of mathematics. Simple: "You have 8 apples. You eat 2. How many left?" Complex: "Apples cost 2 coins each. You have 15 coins. How many can you buy? How much change?" Complex problems integrate reading, reasoning, and math.

### Science & Nature Study

Offline contexts advantage observation-based learning:

1. **Local study:** Focus on plants, animals, weather, water, soil locally available. Identification, observation, recording.

2. **Seasonal patterns:** Track seasonal changes in phenology (leaf emergence, flowering, fruiting, migration). Simple chart: "When did apple trees flower this year? Last year? Why the difference?"

3. **Hands-on experiments:** Growing plants from seeds, fermentation, water filtration, soil composition. Learner controls variables: "Does plant height differ in sunlight vs. shade? Why?"

4. **Documentation:** Drawings, pressed plants, specimen collection (leaves, seeds), written descriptions. Portfolio becomes field guide.

:::note
Science without laboratory equipment remains science. Observation, hypothesis, testing, recording = scientific method. Modern equipment amplifies capacity but isn't required for understanding.
:::

### History & Social Understanding

Oral history, local artifacts, and documentary evidence substitute for textbooks:

1. **Primary sources:** Letters, photographs, tools, structures—physical evidence of what people did/believed. Interpretation develops critical thinking.

2. **Oral interviews:** Local elders remember decades. Record (write down) memories, stories, skills. Preserves knowledge before loss.

3. **Timeline construction:** Learners place events chronologically, see patterns. "What happened before agriculture? After? What changed?"

4. **Map-making:** Local maps, regional maps. Students create maps from exploration or description. Geography as lived experience, not memorized capitals.

</section>

<section id="special-needs">

## Supporting Struggling & Advanced Learners

### Struggling Learners

Common underlying causes: (1) prerequisite skills missing, (2) learning disability (dyslexia, dyscalculia), (3) insufficient engagement/motivation, (4) language barrier.

**Interventions:**

- **Reteach prerequisite:** Cannot learn fractions without division understanding. Backtrack. No punishment—just efficient learning path.

- **Multisensory approach:** Some learners visual, some auditory, some kinesthetic. Use all modalities: show (visual), explain (auditory), manipulate (kinesthetic). Reaches more learners.

- **Reduce load:** Complex task broken smaller. Teach one step, practice, reinforce before next step. "Your job is just to decode this word today. Tomorrow we'll read it in a sentence."

- **Frequent review:** Struggling learners forget faster. Daily brief review of recently learned content. Spaced repetition rhythm: same day, next day, 3 days, 1 week.

- **Confidence building:** Arrange for success experiences. Assign tasks learner can do successfully. Celebrate progress: "Look at your progress over 2 weeks!"

### Advanced Learners

Bored learners become discipline problems. Enrichment activities keep advanced learners engaged:

- **Deeper investigation:** Instead of 3 plant identifications, advanced student catalogs 20 local plants + researches medicinal uses + grows samples from cuttings.

- **Mentorship:** Advanced student assists teacher with younger students. "Can you listen to Sarah read and help with hard words?" Teaching deepens understanding.

- **Projects & investigations:** Open-ended investigations: "Research and report on water sources in our region" or "Design a tool to solve X problem." Requires planning, research, creation.

- **Acceleration:** Ready for next level? Let them progress. No need to artificially hold back. Mixed-age classroom enables this naturally.

## Learning Progressions by Subject Area

Knowledge is not random. Every discipline follows logical progressions where foundational concepts must precede advanced ones. Map progressions explicitly so you don't waste time teaching prerequisites out of order.

### Literacy Progression (Full Scope)

**Phase 1 (Ages 4-5): Pre-literacy (6-12 months)**
- Letter recognition (visual shape identification)
- Letter sound associations (beginning sounds)
- Directionality (left-to-right reading)
- Print concepts (word boundaries, capital letters)
- Rhyme awareness and alliteration
- Oral language development (vocabulary, sentence structure)

**Phase 2 (Ages 5-6): Phonetic Decoding (8-12 months)**
- CVC (consonant-vowel-consonant) words: cat, dog, sit
- Initial consonant blends: st, bl, fr
- Sight word recognition: the, is, and, that
- Short vowel patterns
- First simple decodable readers (structured texts with taught phonemes only)

**Phase 3 (Ages 6-7): Fluency Building (6-12 months)**
- Longer vowel patterns: silent-e, vowel digraphs (ea, oa)
- Consonant digraphs: ch, sh, th, wh
- Multi-syllable words (breaking into parts)
- Sight words expansion (100+ common words)
- Beginning chapter books
- Reading at own pace becomes natural (not labored)

**Phase 4 (Ages 7-8): Comprehension Focus (12+ months)**
- Reading becomes primarily silent (previously aloud/echo reading)
- Literal comprehension: "What happened?" questions
- Inferential comprehension: "Why did the character do that?"
- Vocabulary expansion through context and direct teaching
- Genre introduction (fiction, informational, poetry)
- Simple book reports or story retelling

**Phase 5 (Ages 8-10): Analytical Reading (12+ months)**
- Compare/contrast across texts
- Identifying main idea and supporting details
- Introduction to literary devices (metaphor, simile, symbolism)
- Non-fiction reading for information gathering
- Building background knowledge across topics
- Beginning note-taking and summarization

**Phase 6 (Ages 10-14): Critical Reading (ongoing)**
- Author's purpose and perspective analysis
- Fact vs. opinion distinction
- Source evaluation and credibility
- Reading for different purposes (entertainment, learning, research)
- Advanced literary analysis
- Reading across multiple texts on same topic

:::tip
**Progression rule:** A student not ready for Phase 3 should NOT be given Phase 3 materials. Return to Phase 2, re-teach, build fluency. Pushing students forward before foundational competency creates discouraged readers who avoid reading. Better to spend 18 months in early phases and create confident readers than 10 months of struggle.
:::

### Numeracy and Mathematics Progression

**Age 3-4: Quantity Sense (Subitizing)**
- Recognize small quantities without counting (1, 2, 3 groups)
- Match sets (same number of objects)
- Rote counting to 10

**Age 4-5: Counting & Cardinality**
- Stable counting sequence (order stays same each time)
- One-to-one correspondence (count object once, touch once)
- Cardinal principle (last number = total amount)
- Recognize numerals 1-10
- Compare sets (more/less/same)

**Age 5-6: Simple Arithmetic Within 10**
- Addition within 10 using objects: 3 + 2 = 5 (count all, then count on)
- Subtraction within 10: 7 - 2 = 5
- Missing number problems: 3 + ? = 5
- Compose/decompose numbers (5 = 2 + 3)

**Age 6-7: Place Value & Arithmetic Within 20**
- Understand 10 as unit (10 ones = 1 ten)
- Two-digit numbers: 15 = 1 ten + 5 ones
- Addition/subtraction within 20 (can still use objects for support)
- Word problems with simple story problems
- Begin subitizing within 5 and 10 automatically (no counting)

**Age 7-8: Fluency Within 20, Introduction to Larger Numbers**
- Automatic recall of addition facts to 10 + 10
- Strategies for unknown facts (count on, use related facts)
- Numbers to 100: place value concepts
- Represent place value with tens and ones (bundles of sticks)
- Measurement concepts (length, weight, capacity)

**Age 8-9: Fluency Within 100, Multiplication Concepts**
- Addition/subtraction facts to 20 automatic
- Two-digit addition: 23 + 15 (understanding place value in calculation)
- Multiplication introduction: repeated addition and arrays
- Equal groups concept (3 groups of 4)
- Introduction to fractions: halves and quarters (visual, using food/objects)

**Age 9-10: Multiplication/Division Fluency, Fractions**
- Multiplication facts to 10x10 (some automatic, some strategized)
- Division as inverse of multiplication
- Fractions with visual support: 1/2, 1/3, 1/4, 3/4 (part of whole)
- Fraction comparison and equivalence (visually)
- Decimal introduction through money or measurement

**Age 10-12: Multi-Digit Operations, Fraction Operations**
- Multi-digit multiplication: 23 x 14 (area model or algorithm)
- Multi-digit division: 156 ÷ 12 (inverse thinking or standard algorithm)
- Fraction addition/subtraction with like denominators (using visuals)
- Fraction multiplication (more abstract)
- Algebra introduction: finding unknown values, simple equations

**Age 12-14: Abstract Algebra, Proportional Reasoning**
- Solving equations with variables: 3x + 2 = 14
- Proportional relationships: if 3 apples cost 6 coins, 9 apples cost ?
- Ratios and rates
- Introduction to functions and graphing
- Negative numbers and operations

:::warning
**Critical bottleneck:** Fractions. Most students struggle because fractions taught without sufficient visual/manipulative support. Spend 4-6 weeks on fractions using pies, rods, number lines, before moving to abstract operations.
:::

### Practical Skill Progressions

**Agriculture/Farming Progression (ages 6+)**
1. Seed germination observation (watch seeds grow in controlled setting)
2. Garden plot preparation (digging, soil preparation)
3. Planting from seed under guidance
4. Watering and maintenance
5. Identifying weeds and pests
6. Harvesting and food preservation
7. Soil amendment and composting
8. Crop rotation planning
9. Dealing with crop failure and troubleshooting

**Tool Use Progression (ages 5+)**
1. Observation of master using tool
2. Assisted handling (master guides hands)
3. Supervised use (close observation, immediate feedback)
4. Independent use of simple tasks
5. Complex tasks with supervision
6. Complex tasks with increasing independence
7. Teaching others how to use tool safely

<section id="accommodations">

## Age-Specific Teaching Approaches

### Early Childhood (Ages 3-6)

**Characteristics:**
- Short attention span (5-10 minutes maximum for focused instruction)
- Learn through play, hands-on exploration, sensory input
- Egocentric perspective (difficulty understanding others' viewpoints)
- Concrete thinking (cannot understand abstract concepts)
- Physical activity need is high

**Teaching strategies:**
- Frequent short lessons (5-10 minutes), multiple times daily
- Hands-on, manipulative-rich instruction
- Lots of movement breaks
- Sing, rhyme, and rhythmic language
- Concrete examples ("2 apples + 1 apple" not "2 + 1")
- Patience with repetition (say same thing many times)
- Make it play, not "work"
- Social-emotional support (comfort, reassurance)

### Middle Childhood (Ages 6-10)

**Characteristics:**
- Attention span growing (15-30 minutes for focused instruction)
- Still primarily concrete learners but beginning abstract thinking
- Peer relationships increasingly important
- Can understand rules and fairness
- Want to feel competent and successful

**Teaching strategies:**
- Mix concrete and representational (pictures/drawings) before abstract
- Provide lots of practice and immediate feedback
- Use games and peer learning activities
- Acknowledge effort and progress
- Explain the "why" not just "how"
- Create opportunities for success
- Build in competition and achievement recognition
- Collaborative projects

### Adolescents (Ages 11-14)

**Characteristics:**
- Attention span: 30-45 minutes for single topic
- Increasingly abstract thinking (can manipulate ideas)
- Peer group is primary social focus
- Questioning of authority and rules
- Beginning of specialization interests

**Teaching strategies:**
- Provide rationale/explain relevance ("why does this matter?")
- Provide choice when possible
- Appeal to emerging independence
- Acknowledge budding expertise
- Allow discussion and debate
- Incorporate social relevance
- Project-based learning
- Introduce specialization paths

### Adult Learners

**Characteristics:**
- High motivation if literacy is immediate goal
- Experience-based (relate new learning to what they know)
- Self-directed preference
- May have negative school history/anxiety
- Practical focus (what can I DO with this?)

**Teaching strategies:**
- Make purpose clear and relevant
- Connect to their experience and goals
- Avoid shame (create psychologically safe environment)
- Use sentence-based, meaning-focused methods (not abstract phonics)
- Private one-on-one preferred (not group)
- Frequent success experiences
- Respect their intelligence even if they're illiterate

### Learners with Specific Differences

**Dyslexia (difficulty with phonological processing)**
- Approach: Multisensory, systematic phonics with multisensory input
- Use letter tiles they can touch, trace letters in sand, large motor movements (trace in air)
- Slower pace, more repetition required
- Strength: often good at reasoning, spatial thinking, big-picture understanding
- Do NOT assume lack of intelligence

**Dyscalculia (difficulty with numerical processing)**
- Approach: Extended use of concrete manipulatives, never rush to abstract
- Base-10 blocks, Cuisenaire rods, bundled sticks essential
- Slower progression through numeracy levels
- Focus on number sense and relationships, not speed or memorization
- Strength: often good language skills, compensation strategies

**ADHD (Attention difficulties)**
- Approach: Frequent movement breaks, reduce distractions, fidget-friendly environment
- Shorter, more frequent lessons rather than long blocks
- Physical activity before or between lessons (improves focus)
- Clear structure and expectations
- Positive reinforcement for attention/behavior
- Movement integrated into learning when possible

**Autism/Sensory sensitivities**
- Approach: Explicit instruction (don't assume they "understand" socially), respect sensory needs
- Quiet learning spaces, avoid strong scents, manage lighting/noise
- Literal instruction ("sit in your chair" not "settle down")
- Transition warnings ("in 5 minutes we'll switch activities")
- Special interests can be leverage point (teach math using their interest)
- Strength: often excellent pattern recognition, memory for specific knowledge

:::info-box
**Key principle:** Differences in how people learn are not deficits in potential. A dyslexic learner can become an excellent reader; it may just take longer and require different methods. Avoid sorting learners into "smart" and "not smart" categories. Everyone can learn—the question is what method works for them.
:::

## Teaching in Resource-Limited Settings

You may have no books, no paper, limited teacher time. Design for this reality.

**If you have NO books:**
- Teach literacy from dictated texts (community members tell stories, you write them, children read them)
- Create teaching books: copy simple texts by hand repeatedly
- Use natural text (signs, labels, any written material available)
- <a href="../oral-tradition-techniques.html">Oral tradition</a> becomes primary - stories told, learned by heart, performed

**If you have LIMITED paper:**
- Reusable learning surfaces: slate tablets, sand trays (write/erase repeatedly)
- Cloth with markers (washable)
- Soil ground flat (write with stick)
- Memorization-based learning (less need for written notes)
- Shared materials (one book for 10 children, shared in circles)

**If you have limited teacher time:**
- Peer teaching: older students teach younger students
- Self-directed learning with clear tasks: "Work on your reading list today"
- Recorded stories/songs (if audio available): listen and repeat
- Community volunteers teach specialty skills (elder teaches farming, healer teaches plant knowledge)
- Apprenticeship structures where learner goes to skilled person for knowledge

**If you have limited materials for math:**
- Natural objects: stones, sticks, seeds, beans for counting/calculating
- Body-based: fingers, paces, jumps
- Indigenous games often embed math (strategies, counting, pattern recognition)
- Food and cooking (measuring, fractions, ratios)
- Land measurement and construction projects
