:::danger
Diabetes is manageable in austere settings with discipline and knowledge, but emergencies (DKA, severe hypoglycemia) are immediately life-threatening. Insulin is critical for type 1 diabetes; without it, patients die within days. Resource constraints demand careful prioritization and rationing.
:::

<section id="type-1-vs-2">

## Type 1 vs. Type 2 Differentiation

Understanding the type is essential because management differs fundamentally.

<div class="diabetes-section">

### Type 1 Diabetes (Insulin-Dependent)

**Pathophysiology:** Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells; pancreas cannot produce insulin.

**Typical presentation:**
- Age of onset: Usually <30 years (can occur at any age; "juvenile diabetes")
- Speed of onset: Rapid (weeks to months)
- Body habitus: Lean, not overweight
- Family history: 5–10% of relatives affected (autoimmune pattern)

**Initial symptoms:**
- Polyuria (frequent urination)
- Polydipsia (excessive thirst)
- Polyphagia (excessive hunger) despite eating
- Weight loss (rapid, 5–10 lbs over weeks)
- Fatigue, weakness
- Fruity breath odor (ketones; suggests DKA risk)

**Diagnostic labs (if available):**
- Fasting glucose >126 mg/dL (or random >200 mg/dL)
- Hemoglobin A1C >6.5% (average blood sugar past 3 months)
- C-peptide LOW (<0.6 ng/mL indicates minimal beta cell function)
- Positive anti-GAD65, anti-IA2, or ICA antibodies (autoimmune markers)

**Management:** INSULIN REQUIRED. Without insulin, DKA develops within days and is fatal.

### Type 2 Diabetes (Insulin-Resistant)

**Pathophysiology:** Insulin resistance (body's cells don't respond to insulin) + eventual pancreatic exhaustion (beta cells fail over time).

**Typical presentation:**
- Age of onset: Usually >45 years (increasingly common in young adults)
- Speed of onset: Gradual (years)
- Body habitus: Overweight or obese
- Family history: 40–50% of relatives affected (genetic + environmental)

**Initial symptoms:** Often asymptomatic; discovered incidentally on blood tests.
- Mild polyuria/polydipsia
- Fatigue
- Blurred vision
- Numbness in extremities (neuropathy from chronically elevated glucose)

**Diagnostic labs:**
- Fasting glucose >126 mg/dL (or random >200 mg/dL)
- Hemoglobin A1C >6.5%
- C-peptide NORMAL or HIGH (pancreas still producing insulin, but body's cells resistant)
- Negative autoimmune antibodies

**Management:** Lifestyle + oral medications (if available), then insulin if glucose uncontrolled.

### Differentiation in Austere Settings

Without labs, clinical clues:
- **Young + rapid onset + weight loss:** Type 1; needs insulin urgently
- **Older + gradual onset + overweight:** Type 2; try diet/exercise first, then medication

## Blood Glucose Monitoring Alternatives

### Standard Glucose Meter (If Available)

- **Finger-stick:** Prick fingertip with lancet; apply blood to test strip; meter displays glucose within 5 seconds
- **Normal fasting:** 70–100 mg/dL
- **Normal 2 hours after meal:** <140 mg/dL
- **Target ranges:** Fasting 80–130 mg/dL (diabetic); 2-hour post-meal <180 mg/dL

### Urine Testing (Ketones and Glucose)

**Urine glucose test:**
- Glucose appears in urine when blood glucose >180 mg/dL (renal threshold)
- Positive test = blood glucose definitely elevated, but negative doesn't mean normal (may be normal or moderately elevated)
- **Utility:** Screening only; less precise than blood testing

**Urine ketone test:**
- Ketones appear in urine during DKA or starvation
- Positive ketones = urgency (DKA risk)
- **Critical finding:** Positive urine ketones + elevated glucose = assume DKA until proven otherwise

### Clinical Monitoring (No Tests Available)

**Hyperglycemia symptoms:**
- Polyuria (frequent urination)
- Polydipsia (excessive thirst)
- Blurred vision
- Fatigue
- Fruity breath (ketones; DKA sign)

**Hypoglycemia symptoms:**
- Shakiness, tremor
- Sweating
- Anxiety, nervousness
- Rapid heartbeat
- Hunger (intense)
- Confusion, disorientation, loss of consciousness (severe)

**Monitor trends:** Patients know their own body. If symptoms worsen, glucose likely rising; if improved after eating, confirmed hypoglycemia.

## Insulin Storage Without Refrigeration

Insulin is temperature-sensitive. Optimal storage is 2–8°C (refrigerator). In austere settings without refrigeration, alternative storage extends viability.

### Normal Storage (Refrigerated)

- **Unopened vials:** 2–8°C; stable until expiration date (typically 1–2 years)
- **Opened vials:** 2–8°C; stable for 28 days (some types 42 days)
- **Once removed from refrigeration:** Insulin gradually loses potency; estimate 25–50% loss per week at room temperature

### Room Temperature Storage (No Refrigeration)

- **Inject immediately after removing from refrigerator:** Once removed, use within 28 days at room temperature (68–77°F / 20–25°C)
- **Extend viability with cool packs:** Insulated bag with ice packs keeps insulin cooler longer; replace ice packs 2–3× daily
- **Evaporative cooling:** Wrap insulin bottle in damp cloth; evaporation cools bottle below ambient temperature (effective in dry climates; loses effectiveness in humid environments)

### DIY Evaporative Cooler (Clay Pot Cooler)

For extremely austere settings:

1. **Obtain two clay pots:** One smaller, fits inside larger
2. **Create gap:** Place smaller pot inside larger, creating 2–3 cm gap between walls
3. **Fill gap with sand:** Pour wet sand into gap between pots
4. **Place insulin in inner pot:** Close with lid
5. **Keep sand damp:** Add water daily
6. **Mechanism:** Water evaporation from sand cools inner pot; can achieve 10–20°C cooler than ambient temperature

**Effectiveness:** In dry climates, can keep insulin viable several weeks longer than unrefrigerated storage.

### Monitoring Insulin Viability

- **Visual inspection:** Cloudy insulin (NPH, long-acting) should be uniform when gently rolled; clear insulins (rapid-acting, basal) should be clear. Precipitation, clumping, or discoloration = discarded
- **Function test:** Patient notices less effect of insulin (blood glucose rises despite normal dose) = insulin weakened; increase dose by 10–20% or consider it expired
- **Date marking:** Mark date when vial opened; discard after 28 days regardless of appearance

<section id="dietary-management">

## Dietary Management

Diet is cornerstone of diabetes control. Without medication, diet alone can manage many type 2 cases.

### Carbohydrate Management

**Carbohydrate counting:**
- **Goal:** Consistent carbohydrate intake per meal (stabilizes blood glucose)
- **Typical targets:** 45–60g carbs per meal (type 2), adjusted per insulin dose (type 1)
- **Sources to limit:** Refined carbs (white bread, sugar, sweets); favor complex carbs (whole grains, legumes, vegetables)

**Glycemic index (GI):**
- **Low GI foods:** Slow glucose rise; better for blood sugar control
- **Examples:** Oatmeal, beans, nuts, most vegetables
- **High GI foods:** Rapid glucose spike; avoid
- **Examples:** White bread, sugary drinks, processed foods

### Protein and Fat

- **Protein:** 25–30% of calories; slows carbohydrate digestion, reduces glucose spike
- **Fat:** Limit saturated fat; emphasize unsaturated (olive oil, avocado, nuts) to protect cardiovascular health

### Practical Diet (Limited Resources)

In austere settings, diet simplification:
1. **Emphasize starches:** Rice, beans, lentils, potatoes (complex carbs)
2. **Add protein:** Eggs, legumes, fish, meat (when available)
3. **Include vegetables:** Leafy greens, carrots, tomatoes (non-starchy)
4. **Avoid sugar:** No sweets, sugared beverages, refined flour

**Example day:**
- **Breakfast:** Oatmeal + eggs + vegetable
- **Lunch:** Beans + rice + vegetables
- **Dinner:** Fish/meat + legumes + vegetables
- **Snacks:** Nuts, fruit (limited), unsweetened tea

**Effect:** Consistent, simple diet maintains stable blood glucose with less insulin.

<section id="dka-recognition">

## Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA): Recognition and Management

DKA is life-threatening metabolic emergency. Without treatment, mortality approaches 100%; with treatment, <5%.

<div class="emergency-box">

### Recognition

**Classic presentation:**
- **Symptoms:** Polyuria, polydipsia, fatigue, nausea/vomiting, abdominal pain
- **Respiratory:** Kussmaul breathing (deep, rapid, fruity-smelling breath)
- **Mental status:** Confusion, disorientation, loss of consciousness (severe cases)
- **Physical findings:** Dehydration signs (dry mouth, poor skin turgor), fruity breath odor

**Risk factors:**
- Type 1 diabetes (most common)
- Infection (urinary, respiratory, other)
- Insulin omission or inadequate dose
- Acute illness or stress

**Lab findings (if available):**
- **Glucose:** Usually >250 mg/dL (can be <250 in euglycemic DKA; diagnosis tricky)
- **pH:** <7.35 (acidemia)
- **Bicarbonate:** <18 mEq/L (low; metabolic acidosis)
- **Anion gap:** >12 (wide, due to ketone accumulation)
- **Beta-hydroxybutyrate or acetoacetate:** Elevated (ketones)

**Clinical diagnosis (without labs):**
- Patient with known type 1 diabetes (or undiagnosed, presenting acutely) + polyuria + polydi... + fruity breath + respiratory distress = assume DKA until proven otherwise

### Immediate Management

**Airway/Breathing/Circulation:**
1. **Airway:** Ensure patent airway; patient may be drowsy or unconscious
2. **Breathing:** Observe for Kussmaul breathing (deep, rapid)
3. **Circulation:** Establish large-bore IV; begin normal saline bolus (dehydration is severe; DKA patients 5–10 liters fluid deficit)

**Insulin and Fluids:**
1. **IV fluids:** 1–2 liters normal saline over 1–2 hours; then slower infusion
2. **Insulin:** 0.1 units/kg IV bolus (e.g., 70 kg person = 7 units), then 0.1 units/kg/hr infusion
   - **Critical:** Give insulin AFTER initiating fluids (fluids alone don't cause dangerous hypoglycemia; insulin does)
   - **Purpose:** Stops ketone production; reverses acidosis
3. **Electrolytes:** Potassium often dangerously depleted; add to IV fluids when urine output confirmed (typically 20–30 mEq/L)

**Monitoring:**
- **Glucose:** Check every 1–2 hours; goal is slow decline (100 mg/dL every 1–2 hours)
- **Electrolytes:** Recheck every 4–6 hours
- **pH:** Monitor improvement in acidosis

**Resolution:** When pH >7.35, glucose <250 mg/dL, and nausea subsided = DKA resolved. Transition to subcutaneous insulin.

**Without hospital resources:**
- If IV fluids/insulin unavailable, patient will likely die
- Field management limited to oral fluids if conscious (delays death but not curative)

## Hypoglycemia Management

Low blood glucose is immediately symptomatic and dangerous. Rapid treatment essential.

### Recognition

| Mild-Moderate | Severe |
|---------------|--------|
| Shakiness, tremor | Loss of consciousness |
| Sweating | Seizures |
| Anxiety, nervousness | Inability to swallow or drink |
| Hunger (intense) | Glucagon injection needed |
| Difficulty concentrating | |
| Rapid heartbeat | |

### Treatment (Conscious Patient)

**"Rule of 15":**
1. Give 15g fast-acting carbohydrate (glucose tablet, sugar, juice)
2. Wait 15 minutes
3. Recheck glucose (if available) or reassess symptoms
4. Repeat if still low

**Fast carbs (15g):**
- 3–4 glucose tablets
- 1 tablespoon honey/sugar
- 4 ounces fruit juice (not diet)
- 1 small piece fruit

**Follow-up:**
- After symptoms resolve, eat complex carbohydrate + protein (peanut butter on bread, cheese, nuts) to prevent relapse

### Severe Hypoglycemia (Unconscious)

**Glucagon injection (if available):**
- **Dose:** 1 mg IM or SC
- **Effect:** Raises blood glucose within 10–15 minutes
- **Procedure:** Inject into deltoid or thigh muscle
- **Follow-up:** Once patient conscious, give fast carbs + complex carbs

**Without glucagon:**
- **If patient can swallow:** Place sugar/honey inside cheek; monitor for aspiration
- **If unconscious:** Do NOT force food/liquid (aspiration risk). Place in recovery position; monitor airway.
- **Evacuate:** Severe hypoglycemia without reversible treatment is medical emergency

### Prevention

- **Carry fast carbs:** Always have glucose tablets, candy, juice box
- **Never skip meals:** Regular eating maintains stable glucose
- **Identify patterns:** If low-glucose episodes occur at specific times, adjust insulin dose or meal timing
- **Alcohol reduction:** Alcohol impairs liver's ability to release stored glucose; increases hypoglycemia risk

## Diabetic Foot Care and Wound Management

High blood glucose causes nerve damage (neuropathy) and poor circulation, increasing infection risk. Foot ulcers are leading cause of non-traumatic lower limb amputation in diabetics.

### Neuropathy (Nerve Damage)

**Signs:**
- Numbness or tingling in toes/feet
- Loss of sensation to pain/temperature
- Weakness in legs

**Result:** Patient unaware of injuries; small cuts become infected and progress to ulceration.

### Circulation Problems

High glucose damages blood vessels; poor circulation reduces ability to heal.

### Foot Care Protocol

1. **Daily inspection:** Look at feet daily. Check between toes, heels, soles for cuts, blisters, redness, swelling
2. **Wash daily:** Warm (not hot) water and soap; pat dry (moisture promotes fungal infection)
3. **Moisturize:** Apply lotion to dry areas (not between toes; moisture there is harmful)
4. **Trim toenails:** Cut straight across; don't cut too short (risk of infection)
5. **Wear proper shoes:** Comfortable, protective footwear; avoid walking barefoot
6. **Protect from trauma:** Avoid stubbing, stepping on sharp objects

### Diabetic Foot Wound Management

**For any cut/blister:**
1. **Clean immediately:** Wash with soap and clean water
2. **Apply antibiotic ointment:** Neomycin, polymyxin B, bacitracin (if available)
3. **Cover loosely:** Sterile bandage; change daily
4. **Monitor closely:** Any sign of infection (redness, warmth, pus, increasing pain) = serious; needs aggressive treatment
5. **Offload pressure:** If on foot, use crutches to avoid pressure on wound
6. **Elevation:** Reduces swelling, improves circulation

**Infection signs (severe wounds):**
- Spreading redness
- Warmth, swelling
- Pus or malodorous discharge
- Fever, chills
- Black/dead tissue (gangrene)

**If infected:**
- Antibiotics (if available): Dicloxacillin or cephalexin (Staph/Strep coverage)
- Surgical debridement: Dead tissue must be removed; risk of amputation if infection spreads to bone

<section id="long-term-complications">

## Long-Term Complications

High blood glucose, over years, damages organs. Prevention through good glucose control is essential.

### Retinopathy (Eye Damage)

- High glucose damages retinal blood vessels
- May cause blindness if untreated
- Prevention: Tight glucose control, yearly eye exams
- Treatment: Laser surgery (if access to ophthalmology)

### Nephropathy (Kidney Damage)

- Damages kidney filtering system (glomeruli)
- Early: Protein leakage into urine (albuminuria)
- Late: Kidney failure, need for dialysis
- Prevention: Tight glucose control, blood pressure control, ACE inhibitor use (protects kidneys)
- Monitoring: Check urine for protein annually (dipstick test)

### Neuropathy (Nerve Damage)

- Damages peripheral nerves (feet, hands primarily)
- Causes numbness, pain, weakness
- Irreversible once established; prevention crucial
- Consequences: Foot ulcers (infection risk), falls, injury

### Cardiovascular Disease

- High glucose + hypertension + high cholesterol accelerates atherosclerosis
- Increases risk of heart attack, stroke
- Prevention: Tight glucose control, blood pressure management, low-fat diet, exercise

### Immune Dysfunction

- High glucose impairs white blood cell function
- Increases infection risk (especially fungal, urinary, skin)
- Prevention: Tight glucose control

</section>

<section id="rationing-strategies">

## Insulin Rationing in Austere Settings

If insulin supply is limited, rationing strategies can extend supply while minimizing harm.

### Baseline Calculation

- **Typical daily insulin:** 0.5–1 unit/kg body weight (varies by individual, type of diabetes, diet)
- **Example:** 70 kg person = 35–70 units/day baseline

### Rationing Priorities

1. **Type 1 patients first:** Insulin is life-sustaining; without it, death within days
2. **Children second:** Growth, development, and future life years
3. **Type 2 patients:** Lower priority if oral medications available as alternative

### Dose Reduction Strategies

**Modest reduction (75% of normal dose):**
- Provides 75% glucose control
- Acceptable if supply shortage temporary
- Monitor for DKA signs (fruity breath, polyuria); if appear, restart full dose

**Aggressive reduction (50% of normal dose):**
- Provides 50% glucose control
- Higher DKA risk
- Only if supply extremely limited and temporary

**Temporary cessation (high-risk):**
- If oral medications available for type 2: Switch to dietary management + oral meds (insulin sparing)
- Type 1 without insulin: Will develop DKA within 3–7 days; death likely if continues

### Monitoring During Rationing

- **Daily glucose testing:** Track glucose trends; if rising rapidly, DKA risk increases
- **Symptom monitoring:** Fruity breath, polyuria, fatigue = warning signs
- **Keto strips:** Positive urine ketones = DKA risk; increase insulin immediately if available
