## 1\. Normal Labor Review: Stages, Timing, and Safety

Understanding normal labor is essential to recognize abnormalities requiring intervention.

### The Three Stages of Labor

#### Stage 1: Latent, Active, and Transition Phases

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Phase</th><th scope="row">Dilation</th><th scope="row">Contraction Pattern</th><th scope="row">Duration (Primigravida)</th><th scope="row">Duration (Multipara)</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td><strong>Latent</strong></td><td>0-3 cm</td><td>Irregular 15-20 min apart</td><td>6-12 hours</td><td>3-6 hours</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Active</strong></td><td>3-7 cm</td><td>Regular 3-5 min apart, strong</td><td>3-6 hours</td><td>1-3 hours</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Transition</strong></td><td>7-10 cm</td><td>Very strong 1-2 min apart</td><td>15 min-1 hour</td><td>10-30 min</td></tr></tbody></table>

#### Stage 2: Pushing and Delivery

-   **From:** Complete cervical dilation (10 cm)
-   **To:** Delivery of baby
-   **Duration:** 1-2 hours in first-time mothers, 15-30 minutes in experienced mothers
-   **Mechanism:** Involuntary and voluntary contractions push baby down birth canal. Mother feels urge to push, which is normally helpful signal to bear down with contractions.

#### Stage 3: Placental Delivery

-   **From:** Delivery of baby
-   **To:** Delivery of placenta
-   **Duration:** 5-30 minutes normally
-   **Signs of placental separation:** Fresh gush of blood, uterus rises and becomes more globular, umbilical cord extends further out (not retracting)
-   **Delivery:** Mother experiences contractions that push placenta out. May push gently or use Brandt-Andrews maneuver (see below)

### When to Seek Help

-   **Labor does not progress:** No dilation despite strong contractions for 2+ hours
-   **Excessive bleeding:** Soaking more than one pad per hour during labor (usually blood-stained, not bright red)
-   **Severe pain:** Localized severe pain between contractions (suggests uterine rupture or other complication)
-   **Fetal distress:** No fetal heart tones, meconium-stained fluid (baby's stool in amniotic fluid), or severe decelerations if monitoring available
-   **Fever:** Temperature >38.5°C suggests infection (chorioamnionitis)
-   **Abnormal position:** Breech, transverse lie, face/brow presentation

### Supportive Care in Labor

-   **Hydrate:** Give small frequent sips of water, electrolyte drinks, or juice
-   **Provide nutrition:** Light snacks acceptable in early labor (crackers, broth); later restrict to clear liquids to prevent aspiration if anesthesia needed
-   **Encourage movement:** Upright positions (standing, walking, squatting, hands-knees) facilitate labor descent. Change position every hour
-   **Provide emotional support:** Continuous presence of support person, encouragement, reassurance crucial for psychological well-being and labor progress
-   **Manage pain:** Use breathing techniques, massage, counterpressure, position changes; herbal teas (raspberry leaf, chamomile); heat application

:::warning
Do NOT apply fundal pressure (pushing on the top of the uterus) during active labor without medical supervision. This can rupture the uterus or cause placental abruption. Support the mother's efforts to push naturally.
:::

:::tip
Labor progress varies greatly between individuals. A first-time mother may take 12-18 hours; experienced mothers often progress faster. Be patient and monitor for signs of distress rather than strict timing alone.
:::

## 2\. Breech Presentation

Breech occurs in 3-4% of term pregnancies. Baby's feet/buttocks present first instead of head. High risk of entrapment of head.

### Types of Breech

![Obstetric Emergencies diagram 1](../assets/svgs/obstetric-emergencies-1.svg)

Three types of breech presentation have different delivery challenges and risks.

### Management

#### External Cephalic Version (ECV)

Attempt to turn baby to head-down position manually by pushing through abdominal wall. Success rate 40-60%.

1.  **Check contraindications:** Do NOT attempt if: rupture of membranes, vaginal bleeding, abnormal fetal heart rate, multiple pregnancy, previous uterine surgery, small pelvis, or labor already started.
2.  **Prepare:** Give tocolytics (uterine relaxants) if available to improve success and relax uterus before attempt.
3.  **Ultrasound check:** If available, confirm breech position, rule out placenta previa, assess amniotic fluid.
4.  **Position:** Mother semi-recumbent, relaxed. Operator stands at mother's side.
5.  **Technique:** Place hands on mother's abdomen. Identify baby's buttocks. Apply gentle sustained pressure to roll baby forward (or backward, depending on direction). Takes 1-3 minutes of patient steady pressure.
6.  **Success indicators:** Baby rotates to vertex (head-down) position. Fetal heart rate monitored—should remain normal.
7.  **Failure:** If baby will not turn after 3-5 minutes gentle attempt, stop. Forcing causes placental abruption or rupture of membranes.

#### Vaginal Breech Delivery

If ECV fails or not attempted, breech vaginal delivery requires meticulous technique. "Hands-off" approach until head delivery.

:::danger
Do NOT pull on the baby during breech delivery before the umbilicus is visible. Premature traction causes spinal cord injury and death. Allow gravity and maternal contractions to deliver the baby naturally. This is non-negotiable.
:::

1.  **Position mother:** Place mother supine with legs in stirrups (or supported by attendants). Have suction, oxygen, and resuscitation ready. Station assistant at the head to provide suprapubic pressure when needed.
2.  **Deliver feet to umbilicus:** Use hands-off approach. Allow baby to deliver by gravity and mother's contractions alone. Do NOT pull on baby. Baby will deliver feet, legs, buttocks, and torso with mother's pushing. This stage typically takes 3-5 minutes.
3.  **Deliver trunk:** Once buttocks visible and contracting, baby usually delivers trunk without assistance. The arms typically deliver with the trunk if baby is not pulled.
4.  **Deliver arms:** Once baby is delivered to axillae (armpits), arms usually disengage spontaneously. If not, gently sweep arm across baby's chest. Do NOT apply traction.
5.  **Deliver head (Mauriceau maneuver):** Once entire body delivered except head, head is usually at pelvic inlet. Have mother push with contraction. Have assistant apply suprapubic pressure (firm downward pressure just above pubic symphysis) to flex baby's head. Wrap baby in towel, support torso with one hand. Insert two fingers of other hand along baby's face to keep airway patent (mouth between fingers). Jaw hangs in flexion. Mother's straining pushes head out. If head does not deliver quickly with pushing (>2-3 minutes), may need forceps or emergency C-section.

:::info-box
The Mauriceau maneuver places two fingers in the baby's mouth to maintain airway patency while delivering the head. This prevents airway obstruction when the head is delivered last in breech presentation.
:::

## 3\. Shoulder Dystocia: Emergency Maneuvers

Baby's shoulders become stuck behind pubic bone after head is delivered. Fetus is dying (oxygen depleted) as cord is compressed. Requires rapid intervention.

### Recognition

-   **Turtle sign:** After head delivers, it retracts back into vagina like turtle—classic sign of dystocia
-   **No progress:** Head is out but shoulders do not deliver with next contraction
-   **Timeframe:** Fetus tolerates 5-10 minutes without oxygen. After that, permanent brain damage likely.

:::danger
Shoulder dystocia is a true obstetric emergency. Fetal brain damage occurs after 5 minutes without oxygen. After 10 minutes, death or severe permanent injury is likely. Act decisively and quickly through the maneuvers in sequence.
:::

### Emergency Maneuvers (In Order of Attempting)

#### 1\. McRoberts Maneuver (70% success)

1.  Flex mother's thighs sharply against abdomen (knees to chest position) — move quickly and decisively
2.  This straightens sacral promontory and increases pelvic outlet diameter by approximately 1-2 cm
3.  Often allows shoulders to deliver spontaneously within 30 seconds
4.  Easy, no harm if unsuccessful. Try first. Success rate highest when combined with suprapubic pressure.

#### 2\. Suprapubic Pressure

1.  Apply firm downward and posterior (backward) pressure just above pubic symphysis (at location of pubic hair line)
2.  Aim to rotate baby's anterior shoulder (nearest pubic bone) downward and out from under pubic bone
3.  Maintain steady pressure for 30-60 seconds while mother pushes (if still contracting)
4.  Do NOT apply fundal pressure (pushes baby downward into pelvis)—worsens dystocia and increases compression
5.  This maneuver combines with McRoberts to achieve 90% success rate

#### 3\. Rubin Maneuver

1.  Insert fingers into vagina behind posterior shoulder
2.  Push shoulder toward baby's midline (rotate baby internally)
3.  This reduces shoulder diameter passing through pelvis
4.  Allows delivery of anterior shoulder

#### 4\. Woods Screw Maneuver

1.  Insert fingers in vagina in front of posterior shoulder (the shoulder farther from pubic bone)
2.  Apply pressure rotating baby clockwise or counterclockwise 180 degrees (like turning a screw) — do not be hesitant with pressure
3.  This brings posterior shoulder to anterior position, reducing stuck shoulder diameter
4.  Then deliver anteriorly-rotated shoulder with gentle traction

#### 5\. Gaskin Maneuver (All-Fours Position)

1.  If mother able, have her flip to all-fours (hands and knees) position quickly
2.  Gravity changes pelvic mechanics, increasing outlet diameter and often allows shoulder delivery
3.  Gentle traction on baby's head during contraction may help
4.  Success rate lower than other maneuvers but worth attempting if mother mobile

#### 6\. Zavanelli Maneuver (Last Resort for Persistent Dystocia)

Pushing baby's head back into vagina and performing emergency C-section. Highest risk of maternal trauma but last option if other maneuvers fail after 5 minutes.

:::warning
The Zavanelli maneuver is controversial and rarely needed. It should only be attempted if all other maneuvers have failed and time is >5 minutes of dystocia. Maternal morbidity is significant — expect uterine damage, bladder injury possible.
:::

1.  Push baby's head back into vagina gently — apply steady backward pressure rather than forcing
2.  May need anesthesia/sedation for mother's relaxation if available
3.  Perform emergency C-section immediately (see section below)
4.  Maternal trauma extensive—causes significant uterine manipulation and increases infection risk

## 4\. Postpartum Hemorrhage: Recognition and Treatment

Leading cause of maternal death globally. Diagnosis difficult as symptoms lag behind blood loss. Recognition and immediate action critical.

### The Four T's (Causes of Postpartum Hemorrhage)

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Cause</th><th scope="row">% of Cases</th><th scope="row">Key Signs</th><th scope="row">First-Line Treatment</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td><strong>Tone (Atony)</strong></td><td>80%</td><td>Soft boggy uterus, no contractions</td><td>Uterine massage + oxytocin</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Trauma</strong></td><td>15%</td><td>Bleeding from specific laceration site</td><td>Locate and repair lacerations</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Tissue</strong></td><td>3%</td><td>Retained placental pieces, delayed delivery</td><td>Manual removal or curettage</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Thrombin</strong></td><td>1-2%</td><td>Bleeding from multiple sites, blood won't clot</td><td>Replace clotting factors, transfusion</td></tr></tbody></table>

#### Tone (Uterine Atony - 80% of cases)

-   **Cause:** Uterus fails to contract after placental delivery, leaving open blood vessels uncompressed
-   **Risk factors:** Prolonged labor (>12 hours), multiple pregnancy (twins/triplets), polyhydramnios (too much fluid — >2 liters), uterine fibroids, general anesthesia use
-   **Signs:** Soft boggy uterus on palpation (feels like a water balloon rather than firm grapefruit), heavy vaginal bleeding (>500 ml/hour in severe cases), blood pooling in vagina, mother may feel lightheaded or show signs of shock (rapid heart rate, cold skin, decreased mental clarity)
-   **Treatment:** Uterine massage (see below), oxytocin, ergot alkaloids

#### Trauma (Lacerations, Uterine Rupture)

-   **Lacerations:** Tears to vagina (first, second, third, or fourth degree), cervix, perineum during delivery
-   **Uterine rupture:** Catastrophic—uterus tears completely, usually from overcontraction with oxytocin, internal podalic version, or forceful manipulation. Baby escapes into peritoneal cavity. Maternal mortality 5-7% even with treatment.
-   **Signs:** Severe abdominal pain between contractions (key sign of rupture), vaginal bleeding, shock (rapid heart rate >120, weak pulse, cool skin, confusion), fetal distress (if rupture occurs before delivery), loss of contractions, baby palpable through abdominal wall
-   **Treatment:** Locate and repair tears with sutures if possible (first and second degree tears often heal well; third and fourth degree require careful repair or hysterectomy), emergency C-section and hysterectomy if complete rupture (stops bleeding, but causes permanent sterility and loss of childbearing capability)

:::danger
Uterine rupture is a true life-threatening emergency. If you suspect rupture (severe pain between contractions + bleeding + shock signs), prepare for immediate surgical intervention or evacuation. Delay in treatment increases maternal mortality significantly.
:::

#### Tissue (Retained Placenta or Products)

-   **Cause:** Placenta does not separate or parts retained in uterus
-   **Risk factors:** Manual removal attempts, placenta accreta (abnormal implantation)
-   **Signs:** Delay in placental delivery (>30 minutes), continued bleeding after placental delivery (indicating retained pieces)
-   **Treatment:** Manual removal of placenta/tissue (see below), currettage if fragments remain

#### Thrombin (Coagulopathy/Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation - DIC)

-   **Cause:** Massive bleeding activates clotting cascade, consumes clotting factors and platelets, paradoxically causes both clotting and bleeding
-   **Risk factors:** Severe bleeding (>1500 ml or >25% blood volume loss), placental abruption (blood leaks behind placenta), amniotic fluid embolism (amniotic fluid enters maternal circulation), HELLP syndrome (liver damage in eclampsia), infection/sepsis
-   **Signs:** Continued bleeding from all sites (nose, gums, IV sites, etc.), blood does not clot normally (oozing rather than flowing), mother may have tiny blood clots in capillaries (purpura — red/purple spots on skin)
-   **Treatment:** Replace clotting factors with fresh frozen plasma if available (thaw 30 minutes, infuse 15 ml/kg = ~1 liter for 70 kg woman), aggressive crystalloid fluid resuscitation, transfusion of packed red blood cells, treat underlying cause (deliver baby/placenta, antibiotics for infection)

:::info-box
DIC cannot be diagnosed without blood tests (platelet count, PT/INR, fibrinogen), but in field conditions, suspect it if bleeding continues despite all interventions and mother shows multiple bleeding sites. Treat presumptively with fluids and transfusion while evacuating for definitive care.
:::

### Emergency Treatment Protocol

#### Uterine Massage (for Atony)

![Obstetric Emergencies diagram 2](../assets/svgs/obstetric-emergencies-2.svg)

:::warning
Start uterine massage IMMEDIATELY upon recognition of postpartum hemorrhage. Do not wait for oxytocin to work. Massage must begin within 2-3 minutes of delivery of placenta to prevent maternal shock.
:::

1.  **Position:** Place one hand on mother's abdomen just above pubic bone (lower uterus), other hand cupped on uterine fundus (top of uterus above umbilicus, approximately at or above the level of the umbilicus)
2.  **Technique:** Apply firm downward pressure with fundal hand while massaging uterus in firm circular motions with lower hand. Like kneading bread dough — be vigorous, not gentle. Pressure should be hard enough that mother would find it uncomfortable if conscious.
3.  **Duration:** 5-10 minutes continuous. Uterus should contract and become firm (feels like a hard grapefruit or tennis ball, not a water balloon).
4.  **Goal:** Once firm and contracting, uterus compresses open blood vessels and bleeding should slow dramatically or stop
5.  **Rate:** Continue massage for 30 minutes postpartum even if bleeding stops — prevents recurrence

#### Bimanual Uterine Compression (if Massage Inadequate)

1.  **One hand:** Insert into vagina and make a fist in vaginal fornix (space above uterus inside vagina)
2.  **Other hand:** Press abdomen downward against the fist, compressing uterus between both hands
3.  **Maintain pressure:** 5-10 minutes while uterus contracts
4.  **Goal:** Mechanical compression of vessels stops bleeding until oxytocin takes effect

#### Medications

**Oxytocin (First-line for uterine atony):**  
\- IM: 10 units intramuscularly immediately after delivery (if available)  
\- IV: 10 units in 500 ml normal saline, infuse at 125 ml/hour until bleeding controlled, then slow  
\- Effect: Within 1-2 minutes, uterus contracts firmly  
\- Caution: Do not give as IV bolus (rapid injection)—causes hypertension, seizures  
  
**Ergot alkaloid (Ergotamine/Methylergonovine):**  
\- IM: 0.2-0.25 mg immediately  
\- Effect: Potent sustained uterine contraction  
\- Caution: Contraindicated if hypertension present (causes severe hypertension), do not use if placenta not yet delivered (traps placenta)  
  
**Misoprostol (Cytotec) - if above unavailable:**  
\- Sublingual: 800 mcg under tongue  
\- Rectal: 800 mcg rectally  
\- Effect: Takes 5-10 minutes, weaker than oxytocin/ergot  
\- Side effect: Fever, shivering, diarrhea

#### Manual Removal of Placenta

1.  **Indication:** Placenta not delivered 30+ minutes after baby, or heavy bleeding with placenta still in uterus
2.  **Anesthesia:** If available, use spinal or epidural. If not, may use local infiltration or proceed without (high pain)
3.  **Technique:** Gloved hand inserted into uterus along cord to placental site. Feel for plane of separation between placenta and uterine wall. Gently peel placenta away with lateral motion of hand. Once separated, withdraw placenta. Be gentle to avoid uterine perforation.
4.  **After removal:** Check that entire placenta removed (retained pieces cause hemorrhage). Examine placenta for missing cotyledons (lobes).
5.  **Compression:** After placental removal, contract uterus with oxytocin and massage as above

#### Aortic Compression (Last Resort for Massive Bleeding)

If mother in shock from massive bleeding and no other option available, aortic compression can temporarily stop lower body bleeding while awaiting evacuation:

:::danger
Aortic compression is only for use when mother is in severe shock (unresponsive, barely palpable pulse, agonal breathing) from massive hemorrhage and no other intervention possible. This is a last-resort temporizing measure only—does not treat underlying cause and causes organ ischemia.
:::

1\. Place both hands just below umbilicus (approximately at level of sacral promontory), one on top of other
2\. Press firmly downward and slightly backward toward spine with force — aim to press aorta against vertebrae
3\. Maintain compression continuously. Mother's breathing and heart rate should improve if successful.
4\. Do not compress longer than 10 minutes total (causes permanent organ damage from ischemia — intestines, kidneys, spinal cord can be damaged)
5\. Resume massage or other interventions while maintaining compression — they work together

This is emergency temporizing measure only—does not treat underlying cause. Use while preparing for emergency evacuation or C-section.

## 5\. Eclampsia: Recognition and Management

Severe hypertension in pregnancy with seizures. Fatal if untreated. Part of spectrum with preeclampsia.

### Recognition (Classic Triad)

-   **Seizures:** Usually grand mal (whole body convulsions), often during labor or immediately postpartum
-   **Hypertension:** Blood pressure >160/110 mmHg (or increase >30 mmHg systolic, >15 mmHg diastolic from baseline)
-   **Edema:** Swelling of face, hands, feet (often described as "puffy" appearance)

### Prodromal Symptoms (Before Seizure)

:::warning
If ANY of these symptoms develop during pregnancy/labor/postpartum, assume eclampsia and prepare for emergency magnesium sulfate administration. Do not wait for seizure to confirm diagnosis. Seizure prevention is the goal.
:::

-   Severe headache (often described as "worst headache of my life")
-   Visual disturbances (blurred vision, scotomas — dark spots in visual field, seeing stars)
-   Epigastric pain (upper middle abdomen under ribs)
-   Right upper quadrant pain (liver area pain)
-   Nausea/vomiting
-   Decreased urine output (<500 ml/24 hours indicates kidney involvement)
-   Restlessness or altered mental status
-   Facial flushing or feeling of impending doom

### Emergency Management

#### Immediate Actions

1.  **Protect airway:** During seizure, position on left side, keep airway clear. Do not put anything in mouth.
2.  **Prevent injury:** Remove nearby objects, pad side rails if available
3.  **Medication:** Magnesium sulfate IMMEDIATELY (see dosing below)
4.  **IV access:** Establish large-bore IV

#### Magnesium Sulfate Protocol

**Loading dose (stops seizure):**  
\- IV: 4-6 grams in 100 ml normal saline over 20 minutes (if IV access)  
\- IM: 10 grams (5 grams in each buttock) with 1% lidocaine (needle pain relief)  
  
**Maintenance dose (prevents recurrence):**  
\- IV: 1-2 grams per hour for 12-24 hours after last seizure  
\- Or IM: 5 grams every 4 hours  
  
**Monitoring:**  
\- Respiratory rate must stay >12 (magnesium slows breathing)  
\- Reflexes must be present (sign of toxicity if absent)  
\- Urine output must be >100 ml/4 hours (sign of kidney function)  
\- If respiratory depression or loss of reflexes develops, STOP magnesium  
  
**Reversal:**  
\- Calcium gluconate 1 amp (10 ml of 10% solution) IV if severe toxicity

#### Blood Pressure Management

:::info-box
Hypertension management in eclampsia is different than in non-pregnant patients. Rapid lowering of BP (>20-30 mmHg/hour) can cause stroke. Gradual lowering over hours is safer. Mild-to-moderate elevation (150-170 systolic) does not need urgent treatment if magnesium given.
:::

-   **Goal:** Lower BP to 140-150 systolic if >160/110 (not too fast, which causes stroke; reduction of 10-20 mmHg/hour is safer)
-   **Medications:** If available:
    - Labetalol 10-20 mg IV over 2 minutes, then 40-80 mg every 10 minutes (max 220 mg), OR
    - Hydralazine 5-10 mg IV repeated every 20 minutes (max 30 mg), OR
    - Immediate-release nifedipine 10-20 mg PO (if cannot give IV)
-   **Without medications:** Keep quiet, dark, cool environment; nothing by mouth in case C-section needed; elevate legs slightly to improve cerebral perfusion

### Delivery

-   **Goal:** Delivery of baby and placenta (only cure for eclampsia). If eclampsia antepartum, may need emergency C-section. If intrapartum or postpartum, support labor or manage postpartum complications.
-   **Continue magnesium:** Even during labor/delivery and for 12-24 hours postpartum

## 6\. Umbilical Cord Problems: Prolapse and Entanglement

### Cord Prolapse (Cord Comes Out Before Baby)

Cord slips through cervix into vagina or outside. Baby's circulation compressed by cervix/vaginal tissues. Emergency requiring immediate intervention.

#### Recognition

-   Cord visible hanging from vagina
-   Sudden fetal heart rate drop to very slow (bradycardia)
-   Abrupt change in fetal movement (usually none—baby losing blood supply)

#### Emergency Management

1.  **Fill bladder:** If catheter available, fill bladder with 500-750 ml normal saline (or clean water). Full bladder keeps baby high in pelvis, reduces cord compression while baby descends.
2.  **Knee-chest position:** Position mother on knees with head down, buttocks high (reverse Trendelenburg). Gravity moves baby upward away from cord.
3.  **Keep cord protected:** If cord outside vagina, wrap in sterile warm cloth but do NOT attempt to push back in (causes infection risk).
4.  **Avoid pushing:** Do not allow mother to push (moving baby down increases compression). Discourage straining.
5.  **Emergency evacuation:** This is ABSOLUTE EMERGENCY. Fetus will die without immediate delivery. Evacuate to facility for emergency C-section.

### Nuchal Cord (Cord Around Baby's Neck)

#### Recognition

-   As baby's head crowns (appears at vaginal opening), cord is visible around neck
-   Usually 1-2 loops, occasionally tighter wraps
-   Occurs in 20% of deliveries—common and usually manageable

#### Management

#### Nuchal Cord Maneuver (Somersault Technique)

1.  **Deliver head:** Allow baby's head to deliver spontaneously (do not pull)
2.  **Gentle traction:** Once head delivered, attempt to slip cord over baby's head by gentle traction
3.  **If successful:** Cord slips over face and baby can deliver normally
4.  **If unsuccessful:** Do not force. Proceed to next option.

#### Clamp and Cut Technique (If Manual Reduction Fails)

1.  **Clamp:** If cord very tight and prevents head delivery, place two clamps on cord while head still in vagina
2.  **Cut:** Cut cord between clamps
3.  **Deliver baby:** Now baby can deliver without cord obstruction
4.  **Important:** Clamp immediately—prevents blood loss through cord

## 7\. Emergency Cesarean Section

LAST RESORT ONLY when mother will die without surgery and no other option. Massive blood loss and infection risk. Requires sterile field, anesthesia, emergency resuscitation capability.

### Absolute Indications

-   Cord prolapse with trapped fetus unable to deliver vaginally
-   Placental abruption with fetal distress and unable to deliver vaginally
-   Uterine rupture (baby outside uterus)
-   Severe fetal distress with arrest of labor (not progressing)
-   Maternal eclampsia/HELLP with inability to deliver vaginally within hours

### Preoperative Preparation

-   **Anesthesia:** If available, spinal or epidural preferred (if time allows). If not, general anesthesia with rapid sequence intubation.
-   **Blood available:** If any possibility of transfusion—have blood type available or O-negative on standby
-   **IV access:** Large-bore IV × 2
-   **Bladder catheterization:** Emptied bladder prevents damage
-   **Skin preparation:** Scrub abdomen with iodine or chlorhexidine if available, or alcohol

### Surgical Technique (Simplified Cesarean)

#### Incision and Access

1.  **Incision site:** Lower transverse (Pfannenstiel) incision preferred—lower infection risk. Make horizontal incision 2-3 cm above pubic symphysis, extend 10-12 cm. Or vertical incision below umbilicus if preferred (faster but more pain postoperatively).
2.  **Layers:** Cut through skin, fascia, muscle (separate, don't cut), peritoneum
3.  **Pack bowels:** Once in peritoneal cavity, gently pack small bowel upward with clean cloth/towel to protect during surgery

#### Uterine Incision

1.  **Lower uterine segment:** Make vertical incision (3-4 cm) in lower uterus where it is thinner. Avoid placenta (listen/feel for location).
2.  **Extend incision:** Extend vertically or carefully laterally (not down into bladder)
3.  **Amniotic fluid:** Fluid rushes out—suction or have towels ready

#### Fetal Delivery

1.  **Locating baby:** Feel for baby's head or buttocks. Gently guide head (or bottom if breech) toward incision.
2.  **Extract:** Gentle traction on head/shoulders. Do not pull on body—causes injury.
3.  **Cord clamping:** Once baby delivered, immediately clamp cord in two places and cut between clamps

#### Placental Removal

1.  **Location:** Feel for placental edge in uterus
2.  **Separation:** Gently peel placenta away from uterine wall
3.  **Removal:** Once separated, withdraw gently
4.  **Check completeness:** Examine placenta to ensure all lobes present

#### Uterine Repair

1.  **Closure:** Close uterine incision in two layers with absorbable suture (chromic catgut or similar if available)
2.  **Hemostasis:** Make sure bleeding controlled. Oxytocin given IV/IM to contract uterus
3.  **Check for extensions:** Ensure incision did not extend into bladder or broad ligaments

#### Abdominal Closure

1.  **Remove packing:** Remove gauze/cloth protecting bowels
2.  **Inspect:** Check for bleeding from peritoneal surfaces
3.  **Close peritoneum:** Absorbable suture in running fashion
4.  **Close fascia:** Absorbable or permanent suture, careful to avoid blood vessels
5.  **Close skin:** Sutures or staples

### Postoperative Management

-   **Pain control:** Opioids as needed, early mobilization to prevent clots
-   **Infection prevention:** Antibiotics if available (cephalosporin or broad-spectrum penicillin)
-   **Hemorrhage monitoring:** Watch for excessive bleeding, shock
-   **Uterine contraction:** Continue oxytocin for 4-6 hours postoperatively
-   **Catheter removal:** Remove bladder catheter at 24 hours
-   **Breastfeeding:** Can initiate as soon as mother stable

## 8\. Neonatal Resuscitation and APGAR Scoring

### APGAR Score (Assess at 1 minute and 5 minutes)

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Component</th><th scope="row">Score 0</th><th scope="row">Score 1</th><th scope="row">Score 2</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td><strong>Appearance (skin color)</strong></td><td>Blue/pale</td><td>Body pink, extremities blue</td><td>Completely pink</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Pulse</strong></td><td>Absent</td><td>&lt;100 bpm</td><td>&gt;100 bpm</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Grimace (reflex irritability)</strong></td><td>No response</td><td>Grimace</td><td>Cry/vigorous</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Activity (muscle tone)</strong></td><td>Limp</td><td>Some flexion</td><td>Active motion</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Respiration</strong></td><td>Absent</td><td>Weak/gasping</td><td>Good cry</td></tr></tbody></table>

**Interpretation:** 7-10 = Normal, 4-6 = Moderate distress, 0-3 = Severe distress requiring resuscitation

### Immediate Care (All Newborns)

-   **Warming:** Dry baby immediately with clean cloth. Place skin-to-skin with mother or wrap in dry blanket. Heat loss causes hypothermia and impairs recovery.
-   **Clear airway:** Suction nose and mouth with bulb syringe if available (or wipe with clean cloth). Amniotic fluid should drain.
-   **Stimulation:** Gently rub back and dry. Stimulation usually causes baby to cry.
-   **Cord clamping:** Wait 30-60 seconds after delivery if baby breathing well (allows placental transfusion). If severely asphyxiated, clamp immediately for resuscitation.
-   **Breastfeeding:** Initiate early if baby alert and can latch

### Resuscitation if Baby Not Breathing

#### Step 1: Initial Assessment and Airway Positioning

1.  **Dry and stimulate:** Dry baby, rub back vigorously. Stimulation usually triggers breathing.
2.  **Position:** Neck slightly extended (sniffing position) to open airway
3.  **Clear airway:** Suction or wipe mouth and nose
4.  **Wait 10 seconds:** Assess if baby breathing. If yes, move to warmth and observation. If no, proceed.

#### Step 2: Bag-Valve-Mask Ventilation

1.  **Equipment:** Bag-valve-mask (Ambu bag) if available. If not, can do mouth-to-mouth (not ideal but life-saving).
2.  **Technique:** Position mask to cover nose and mouth. Seal around face. Ventilate 40-60 breaths per minute with gentle pressure (see chest rise).
3.  **Duration:** Continue for 30 seconds. Then reassess.
4.  **Response:** If heart rate increases above 100 and baby starts breathing, reduce ventilation rate gradually as baby takes own breaths.

#### Step 3: Chest Compressions (if Heart Rate <60)

1.  **Position:** Encircle chest with both thumbs on lower sternum just below nipple line
2.  **Compression:** Press down firmly at 100-120 compressions per minute. Depress chest 1/3 depth.
3.  **Coordination:** Compress and release for 3 compressions, then give 1 ventilation (3:1 ratio)
4.  **Duration:** Continue 10 minutes minimum before declaring baby dead

#### Step 4: Medications (if available)

**Epinephrine (if no response to 10 minutes resuscitation):**  
\- IV/umbilical: 0.1-0.3 ml/kg of 1:10,000 solution  
\- Can repeat every 3-5 minutes  
\- Last resort—if used, likely outcome poor

### Signs of Life vs Death

-   **Life:** Heart rate present (feel umbilical cord or chest), any spontaneous movement, response to stimulation
-   **Early death signs:** Pallor (extreme paleness), no response to stimulation, no heart rate, limp
-   **Maceration:** Skin falling off—indicates death occurred days before (cannot resuscitate). Do not attempt resuscitation in macerated baby.

## 9\. HELLP Syndrome: Recognition and Management

HELLP syndrome (Hemolysis, Elevated Liver enzymes, Low Platelets) is a severe variant of preeclampsia/eclampsia with maternal mortality 15-25% even with treatment. Occurs in 0.2-0.6% of pregnancies, typically in third trimester. Without recognition and delivery, maternal mortality approaches 100%.

### Clinical Recognition Without Laboratory Testing

HELLP diagnosis traditionally requires blood tests (hemoglobin drop, elevated transaminases, platelet count <100,000). In field conditions without labs, diagnosis is clinical:

:::warning
HELLP syndrome is a true obstetric emergency. If ANY combination of the following signs develops during pregnancy/labor/early postpartum, assume HELLP and prepare for emergency delivery. Do not delay waiting for diagnosis confirmation.
:::

**Classic Triad of Presentation:**

1. **Hemolysis signs (destruction of red blood cells):**
   - Jaundice (yellow skin/eyes) indicates bilirubin >2 mg/dL
   - Dark urine (tea-colored) from hemoglobin products
   - Pale/ashen skin appearance (from anemia)
   - Microangiopathic hemolytic anemia causes fragmented RBCs visible on blood smear if testing available

2. **Elevated Liver Enzymes signs (liver damage):**
   - Severe epigastric pain (upper middle abdomen) — hallmark finding
   - Right upper quadrant pain (over liver) — second hallmark
   - Nausea/vomiting with epigastric focus
   - Tenderness on palpation over liver area
   - Possible hepatic rupture (rare, catastrophic) with sudden severe pain and shock

3. **Thrombocytopenia signs (low platelet count):**
   - Bleeding from gums, nose, or IV sites (suggests platelets <50,000)
   - Petechiae (small red/purple dots on skin, non-blanching)
   - Purpura (larger purple areas, suggesting severe thrombocytopenia)
   - Easy bruising

**Clinical Diagnosis Criteria (Modified for Field Use):**
Assume HELLP if patient has preeclampsia signs (hypertension >160/110, headache, visual disturbance, edema) PLUS:
- Epigastric or right upper quadrant pain, AND
- Evidence of hemolysis (jaundice, dark urine, pale appearance), OR
- Evidence of thrombocytopenia (bleeding, petechiae, purpura)

### Emergency Management

#### Immediate Actions

1. **Magnesium sulfate immediately** — same protocol as eclampsia (loading dose 4-6g IV over 20 min or 10g IM)
2. **Blood pressure control** — same as eclampsia (gradual reduction, target 150-160 systolic)
3. **Prepare for emergency delivery** — HELLP requires delivery of baby/placenta for cure
4. **IV access and fluid resuscitation** — expect severe coagulopathy; have blood/fluids ready
5. **Monitor for complications** — watch for hepatic rupture (sudden worsening pain), DIC (continued bleeding despite interventions), acute kidney injury (oliguria)

#### Differential Diagnosis Considerations

HELLP overlaps with other life-threatening conditions. In field settings without labs, support treatment for all suspected conditions:

- **DIC (Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation):** If bleeding from multiple sites, suspect DIC accompanying HELLP. Treat with aggressive crystalloid resuscitation and consider transfusion if available.
- **Acute Fatty Liver of Pregnancy:** Rare (occurs in ~1 in 7,000 pregnancies), typically presents with encephalopathy (confusion, altered mental status). Treatment identical: urgent delivery.
- **Thrombotic Thrombocytopenic Purpura (TTP):** Presents with thrombocytopenia, microangiopathic hemolytic anemia, neurological symptoms. Distinguishing feature: neurological findings more prominent. Treatment: urgent delivery + plasma exchange if available.

### Delivery Approach in HELLP

**Vaginal vs. Cesarean Section Decision:**
- If cervix favorable (effaced, dilated, pelvis adequate) and mother hemodynamically stable: attempt vaginal delivery
- If cervix unfavorable (thick, closed, small pelvis) OR mother unstable (continued bleeding, shock): emergency C-section
- Labor augmentation with oxytocin acceptable if mother stable and labor progressing

**Labor Management Specifics:**
- Pain management: epidural preferred (avoids general anesthesia aspiration risk), but spinal carries bleeding risk with severe thrombocytopenia—weigh risks
- Avoid NSAIDs (worsen thrombocytopenia, kidney injury)
- Continue magnesium sulfate throughout labor and 12-24 hours postpartum
- Have blood products available; expect coagulopathy postpartum
- Monitor for hepatic rupture (sudden severe pain, shock) — immediate surgical intervention required

### Postpartum Management

Most HELLP syndrome resolves within 48-72 hours after delivery. However, complications can emerge postpartum:

- **Continued hemolysis:** Watch for worsening jaundice, dark urine, anemia
- **Liver dysfunction:** Monitor for encephalopathy (confusion), prolonged coagulopathy
- **Kidney injury:** Monitor urine output (oliguria suggests acute tubular necrosis)
- **DIC:** If bleeding continues despite delivery, treat as above
- **Placental abruption:** HELLP associated with 5-10% abruption rate; watch for vaginal bleeding, abdominal pain

:::info-box
**Key Point on HELLP Recovery:** Unlike eclampsia which begins improving within hours of delivery, HELLP may continue worsening for 48 hours postpartum before improvement. Continued monitoring essential through postpartum period.
:::

## 10\. Perimortem Cesarean Section (Perimortem C-Section)

Perimortem C-section is emergency delivery of baby when mother is in cardiac arrest or profound shock and vaginal delivery not immediately possible. This procedure is performed only when mother will certainly die without intervention and standard resuscitation failing.

### Indications

Only perform if:
- Mother in cardiac arrest (no pulse) or profound shock (systolic BP <80 mmHg unresponsive to fluids)
- AND fetus potentially viable (≥20 weeks gestation)
- AND standard resuscitation ongoing for 5+ minutes without ROSC (return of spontaneous circulation)
- AND mother will certainly die without delivery (e.g., amniotic fluid embolism, peripartum cardiomyopathy, massive hemorrhage)

Common triggers: peripartum cardiomyopathy (sudden heart failure in pregnancy), amniotic fluid embolism (anaphylaxis from fetal material), placental abruption with uncontrolled hemorrhage, uterine rupture.

:::danger
Perimortem C-section in untrained hands carries >80% mortality. Only perform if death is certain without intervention. Perform while resuscitation (chest compressions, medication) continues.
:::

### Rapid Surgical Technique

**Setup (minimal preparation required in true emergency):**
- No time for sterile preparation if mother arrested; proceed immediately
- Use whatever cutting instruments available (scalpel, knife, scissors)
- Have assistant continue chest compressions

**Incision:**
1. Rapid midline incision from xiphoid (breastbone bottom) to pubic symphysis (covers most lower abdominal structures)
2. Cut through all layers rapidly—time is critical; hemostasis secondary
3. Enter peritoneal cavity and expose uterus

**Uterine Opening:**
1. Make single vertical incision in lower uterine segment
2. Extend incision upward toward fundus if needed for access
3. Do not be concerned about contamination from amniotic fluid—this is emergency extraction

**Fetal Delivery:**
1. Reach into uterus and locate baby's head or buttocks
2. Deliver baby rapidly—gentle traction to avoid uterine tearing
3. Immediately clamp and cut cord (leave long cord segment for placental transfusion if time)
4. Hand baby to assistant for immediate neonatal resuscitation

**After Extraction:**
1. Do NOT attempt detailed placental removal or layered uterine closure
2. Pack uterus with gauze to control hemorrhage
3. Close abdominal incision minimally (goal is hemorrhage control, not perfect surgery)
4. Continue maternal resuscitation while transporting to facility for definitive care

**Expected Outcomes:**
- Perimortem C-section achieves ROSC in ~20% of mothers (varies by underlying cause)
- Of those achieving ROSC, ~40-50% survive to hospital discharge
- Fetal survival if ROSC achieved within 5-10 minutes: ~60%
- Success rates drop dramatically with delay—ideally delivered within 4-5 minutes of arrest

### Amniotic Fluid Embolism

Amniotic fluid embolism (AFE) is catastrophic, causing sudden cardiorespiratory collapse when fetal material enters maternal circulation. Mortality 60-80% even with treatment.

**Recognition:**
- Sudden onset during labor, delivery, or immediately postpartum
- Dyspnea (sudden severe shortness of breath)
- Chest pain
- Hypotension (sudden drop)
- Cardiac arrhythmias (often visible on monitor if available)
- Cyanosis (blue coloration)
- Loss of consciousness
- Seizures possible
- Coagulopathy developing rapidly (DIC)

**Management:**
1. **CPR immediately** — begin chest compressions and ventilation
2. **IV access** — large-bore IV for fluids and medications
3. **Medications** — epinephrine 1 mg IV every 3-5 minutes during resuscitation
4. **Fluid resuscitation** — aggressive saline infusion
5. **Perimortem C-section** if not already delivered — baby must be delivered to stop amniotic fluid entry
6. **Prepare for hysterectomy** — may be needed to control continued hemorrhage from DIC
7. **Blood products** — anticipate massive transfusion protocol; coagulopathy severe

**Supportive care:**
- Supplemental oxygen/ventilation
- Management of DIC — fresh frozen plasma, platelet transfusion if available
- Invasive monitoring if available (central line, arterial line for BP monitoring)

:::info-box
**Prevention:** AFE risk increases with labor augmentation, cesarean delivery, placental abruption, and operative delivery. Early recognition and rapid cesarean section if indicated (for other reasons) reduces AFE risk by controlling delivery method.
:::

## 11\. Uterine Inversion: Recognition and Management

Uterine inversion occurs when the uterus turns inside-out, with placental site inverting into the uterine cavity or even protruding through cervix. Occurs in 1 in 2,000-2,500 deliveries. Causes sudden, massive hemorrhage and shock—maternal mortality 5-15% even with treatment.

### Recognition

**Immediate Signs:**
- Sudden massive vaginal hemorrhage immediately after delivery of baby (often before placenta delivers)
- Mother goes into shock suddenly (rapid heart rate >130, hypotension, cold skin, altered mental status)
- Severe abdominal pain and cramps
- Feeling of something "coming down" from vagina

**Visual Findings:**
- Inverted fundus visible protruding through cervix (appears as large mass at vaginal opening, often purple/dark)
- Absence of uterine fundus on abdominal examination (cannot palpate firm uterus above umbilicus)
- Large vaginal mass (the inverted uterus)

### Emergency Management

:::danger
Uterine inversion is a true hemorrhagic emergency. Maternal death occurs within 10-15 minutes if uterus not replaced. This requires immediate intervention.
:::

#### Manual Replacement (Johnson Maneuver)

1. **Immediate preparation:**
   - Place mother supine with pelvis elevated on pillows
   - Establish large-bore IV access; start aggressive fluid resuscitation
   - Have oxytocin or ergot available for use AFTER replacement only

2. **Anesthesia:**
   - If available, IV sedation (morphine, benzodiazepine) for relaxation
   - If available, general anesthesia with uterine relaxant (halothane causes uterine relaxation)
   - If not available, proceed (pain secondary to hemorrhage control)

3. **Manual replacement technique:**
   - Sterile gloved hand inserted into vagina
   - Locate the inverted fundus (the deepest part of the inversion)
   - Apply steady upward pressure with palm of hand against fundus
   - Simultaneously apply downward/outward pressure on uterine body with other hand on abdomen
   - Gentle sustained pressure (takes 2-5 minutes) usually allows uterus to re-evert
   - Do not force—sustained steady pressure more effective than sudden traction

4. **Confirmation of replacement:**
   - Once uterus re-inverts, you feel normal uterine shape return
   - Fundus palpable above umbilicus on abdominal exam
   - Vaginal mass disappears

5. **Post-replacement:**
   - **DO NOT remove hand from uterus immediately** — keep hand in uterus with firm pressure for 3-5 minutes to prevent recurrent inversion
   - Give oxytocin IV/IM during this time to maintain uterine contraction
   - Slowly withdraw hand after uterus contracts firmly
   - Deliver placenta only AFTER uterus fully contracted (if not already delivered)

#### Hydrostatic Replacement (O'Sullivan Maneuver) - Alternative

If manual replacement fails, this technique uses fluid pressure:

1. Prepare warm normal saline (or clean water) — 1-2 liters at body temperature
2. Fill posterior vaginal fornix with saline using IV tubing or catheter
3. Occlude vaginal opening around the IV tubing to trap fluid inside vagina
4. Hydrostatic pressure gradually pushes inverted fundus upward
5. Takes 5-15 minutes; success rate 60-70% if manual replacement failed
6. Slower than manual but less traumatic to uterine tissue

### Prevention

- **Control placental delivery:** Do not apply excessive traction on umbilical cord
- **Support uterine fundus:** Apply suprapubic pressure while guiding placenta out (Brandt-Andrews maneuver)
- **Avoid ergot alkaloids before placental delivery:** Oxytocin acceptable, but ergot causes sustained uterine contraction that may invert if placenta not yet separated
- **Careful placental traction:** Gentle technique essential; never pull cord if resistance felt

### Postoperative Management

After successful replacement:
- **Continue oxytocin** — IV infusion for 4-6 hours to maintain contraction
- **Monitor hemorrhage** — expect continued heavy bleeding for 30 minutes post-replacement
- **IV fluids and transfusion** — aggressively replace blood loss
- **Antibiotics** — prophylactic broad-spectrum coverage (infection risk high)
- **Catheterize bladder** — monitor urine output; expect oliguria if significant blood loss

## 12\. Common Mistakes in Obstetric Emergency Management

### Breech and Shoulder Dystocia

**Mistake: Pulling on baby during breech delivery before umbilicus visible**
Result: Spinal cord injury, permanent paralysis or death. The "hands-off" approach is non-negotiable — allow gravity and contractions to work for 3-5 minutes minimum.

**Mistake: Not recognizing turtle sign in shoulder dystocia**
Result: Wasting critical 30 seconds while baby's oxygen depletes. Turtle sign (head retracting into vagina after delivery) is pathognomonic for dystocia. React immediately with McRoberts.

**Mistake: Applying fundal pressure in shoulder dystocia**
Result: Worsens dystocia by pushing baby further into pelvis. Suprapubic pressure rotates baby; fundal pressure compresses — use opposite direction.

**Mistake: Doing Mauriceau maneuver incorrectly (putting fingers in baby's mouth rather than along face)**
Result: Airway obstruction, baby cannot breathe during delivery. Fingers go along the face inside the mouth to maintain patent airway, NOT grabbing the baby's jaw.

### Postpartum Hemorrhage

**Mistake: Gentle massage instead of firm massage in uterine atony**
Result: Ineffective uterine contraction, continued massive bleeding. Uterine massage must be vigorous and sustained — the mother would find it uncomfortable if conscious.

**Mistake: Waiting for oxytocin to work before starting massage**
Result: 2-5 minutes delay while blood loss accelerates. Begin massage immediately upon delivery of placenta — do not wait.

**Mistake: Attempting placental delivery too early (before signs of separation visible)**
Result: Retained placenta, continued bleeding, increased need for manual removal. Wait for fresh gush of blood, uterus to rise, cord to advance.

**Mistake: Pulling hard on umbilical cord to deliver placenta**
Result: Uterine inversion (uterus turns inside-out) — catastrophic hemorrhage and shock. Use Brandt-Andrews maneuver: one hand supports uterus from above while guiding placenta out, other hand applies gentle downward traction on cord.

**Mistake: Not recognizing shock early (waiting until mother is unresponsive)**
Result: Point of no return reached before aggressive intervention. Early signs: rapid heart rate (>110), cool skin, anxious expression, narrow pulse pressure. Start IV fluids and consider aortic compression before mother becomes unresponsive.

### Eclampsia

**Mistake: Not giving magnesium sulfate immediately with seizure**
Result: Recurrent seizures, brain damage, maternal death. First dose must be given within 2-3 minutes of seizure onset.

**Mistake: Rapid blood pressure lowering (chasing every point of elevation)**
Result: Stroke from hypotension. Lower BP slowly over 30-60 minutes; do not treat mild elevation if magnesium given.

**Mistake: Putting objects in mouth during eclamptic seizure**
Result: Aspiration, tooth damage, airway obstruction. Position on left side, keep airway clear, protect from injury — do NOT put anything in mouth.

**Mistake: Continuing labor without treating eclampsia**
Result: Recurrent seizures, maternal death. Magnesium sulfate must be given BEFORE labor augmentation or delivery.

### Neonatal Resuscitation

**Mistake: Delayed cord clamping when baby severely asphyxiated**
Result: Continued placental transfusion but baby not receiving oxygen — wastes precious time. Clamp immediately if APGAR 0-3 and perform resuscitation.

**Mistake: Gentle ventilation in severely asphyxiated baby**
Result: Baby not ventilated adequately, hypoxia worsens. Use forceful breaths with clear observation of chest rise — you should see chest move with each breath.

**Mistake: Stopping compressions after 10 minutes if no response**
Result: Giving up too early in resuscitation. Continue 15-20 minutes minimum if available resources allow — some babies have survived prolonged resuscitation.

:::tip
In field conditions, always err on the side of prolonged resuscitation in neonates. The baby's oxygen stores are greater than adults', and some will survive after seeming hopeless. Continue until you are certain no intervention possible or evacuation available.
:::

## 10\. Troubleshooting Emergency Scenarios

### Scenario: Excessive Bleeding But Uterus Contracts Firmly

**Diagnosis:** Likely laceration (vaginal, cervical, or perineal) rather than atony.

**Action:**
1. Inspect thoroughly — place patient in good light, separate labia, look for active bleeding site
2. If bleeding from perineal laceration, locate tear (usually at posterior fourchette)
3. Attempt repair if available (use absorbable suture, repair in layers)
4. If deep laceration into muscle or sphincter, may need surgical expertise — prepare for evacuation
5. If bleeding from cervix (blood flows from cervical os despite firm uterus), may need cervical repair or packing

**Prevention:** Controlled delivery with slow extension of perineum reduces severe lacerations.

### Scenario: Uterus Relaxes Again After Contraction

**Diagnosis:** Inadequate medication dosing or recurrent atony.

**Action:**
1. Continue uterine massage continuously (not intermittently)
2. Give second dose of oxytocin if available — do not redose ergot alkaloids (toxicity)
3. Consider manual uterine compression if atony severe
4. Bladder may be full — catheterize to empty (full bladder prevents uterine contraction)

### Scenario: Cord Prolapse Recognized During Contractions

**Diagnosis:** Complete cord prolapse requiring emergency delivery.

**Action:**
1. Fill bladder immediately with 500-750 ml sterile saline (or clean water in field conditions)
2. Position mother on knees with head down (knee-chest)
3. Do NOT push cord back in — wrap loosely with sterile cloth
4. Discourage ALL pushing — this worsens compression
5. Prepare for emergency C-section or rapid vaginal delivery
6. If already in advanced labor (8-9 cm dilation and vertex presentation), may attempt rapid vaginal delivery instead of section

### Scenario: Baby Not Breathing, No Response to Stimulation

**Diagnosis:** Severe asphyxia, possible meconium aspiration.

**Action:**
1. Clear airway immediately — suction nose and mouth (if meconium visible in airway, suction before bag-mask ventilation to prevent aspiration)
2. Begin bag-mask ventilation immediately — do not wait
3. If meconium in airway, consider intubation (if provider trained) to suction below vocal cords
4. Begin chest compressions if no heart rate after 30 seconds ventilation (HR <60 or absent)
5. Continue for minimum 10-15 minutes unless obviously hopeless (maceration, decomposition)

### Scenario: Breech Not Delivering — Stuck at Level of Shoulders

**Diagnosis:** Possible entrapped head (rare with breech, but possible if small-for-dates baby with large head).

**Action:**
1. Do NOT pull — this increases entrapment
2. Ensure entire procedure performed correctly (hands-off to umbilicus, gentle delivery of arms)
3. Apply suprapubic pressure to assist (not fundal pressure)
4. Attempt Mauriceau maneuver with gentle sustained pressure (takes 2-3 minutes sometimes)
5. If head does not deliver in 3-5 minutes, prepare for emergency C-section (fetal brain damage imminent)

## 11\. Field Adaptations and Low-Resource Management

### Without Oxytocin

**Alternative for uterine atony:**
- Ergot alkaloids (ergotamine/methylergonovine) 0.2-0.25 mg IM if available — but do NOT use if hypertension present or placenta not delivered
- Misoprostol 800 mcg sublingual or rectal — slower onset (5-10 min) but effective
- Aggressive uterine massage for 30+ minutes — can work alone if absolutely necessary
- Aggressive IV crystalloid fluid resuscitation — maintain maternal perfusion while uterus contracts

### Without IV Access

**Medications via IM/sublingual:**
- Oxytocin 10 units IM (absorbed in 2-5 minutes, less predictable than IV)
- Magnesium sulfate 10 grams IM for eclampsia (dilute in 1% lidocaine to reduce pain, give 5 grams in each buttock)
- Ergot alkaloids IM (0.2-0.25 mg)
- Misoprostol SL or rectal for hemorrhage

### Without Anesthesia

**Pain management for emergency procedures:**
- Local anesthetic infiltration if available (1% lidocaine 10-20 ml)
- Systemic analgesia: morphine 5-10 mg IM if available
- Psychological support and breathing techniques
- Rapid completion of procedure minimizes suffering

### Without Blood for Transfusion

**Fluid resuscitation approach:**
- Two large-bore IVs (16-18 gauge if available)
- Aggressive crystalloid infusion — target 3 liters of normal saline for first hour
- Ringer's lactate preferred over normal saline (better electrolyte balance)
- Elevate legs (reverse Trendelenburg position) to maintain cerebral perfusion
- Accept that without transfusion capability, options limited — focus on hemorrhage control and evacuation

### Without Cesarean Section Capability

**Breech delivery:**
- Breech vaginal delivery IS possible — requires meticulous hands-off approach and patience
- Success rate high (>80%) if technique followed exactly
- Have resuscitation ready for baby

**Shoulder dystocia:**
- All emergency maneuvers must succeed — have no surgical fallback
- Success rate 99%+ if all maneuvers attempted in correct sequence
- Prepare psychologically — must deliver baby vaginally

**Cord prolapse:**
- If vaginal delivery imminent (8-9 cm dilation, vertex presentation, strong contractions), attempt rapid vaginal delivery rather than attempting C-section in field conditions
- If delivery not imminent, attempt to keep cord protected, bladder full, and evacuate urgently

:::warning
Field adaptations are NOT equivalent to facility care. The goal is patient survival, not ideal outcomes. Use these adaptations while arranging evacuation to definitive care.
:::

</section>

## 13. HELLP Syndrome - Clinical Recognition Without Labs

HELLP syndrome (Hemolysis, Elevated Liver enzymes, Low Platelets) is covered in detail in Section 9 above. This expanded section provides additional field-applicable visual recognition techniques.

### Visual Diagnosis Without Laboratory Testing

When laboratory testing is unavailable, diagnosis relies entirely on clinical pattern recognition. HELLP typically presents in the third trimester or early postpartum period with the following constellation of findings:

**Hemolysis Signs:**
- Jaundice (yellow discoloration of sclera/skin) becomes visible when bilirubin >3 mg/dL. Check sclera (whites of eyes) in natural light—yellowish tinge is diagnostic
- Tea-colored or cola-colored urine from hemoglobin breakdown products (urobilinogen)
- Pallor (pale appearance) from hemolytic anemia
- Petechiae (tiny red/purple dots that do not blanch under pressure) on trunk/extremities indicate severe thrombocytopenia
- Purpura (larger purple patches) indicates platelet count <20,000

**Hepatic Involvement Signs:**
- Severe epigastric pain (upper middle abdomen) that is reproducible on palpation
- Right upper quadrant tenderness on examination
- Hepatic enlargement palpable on abdominal examination
- Tenderness over liver edge at right costal margin

**Preeclampsia/Eclampsia Context:**
- Blood pressure >160/110 mmHg
- Severe headache (often described as "pressure" or "worst headache")
- Visual disturbances (blurred vision, scotomas)
- Edema of face, hands, feet

### Complications Requiring Immediate Recognition

**Hepatic Rupture (Rare but Catastrophic):**
Signs: Sudden severe abdominal pain (worse than baseline epigastric pain), rapidly developing shock (tachycardia >130, hypotension, pale/diaphoretic appearance), rapid deterioration. This is a surgical emergency requiring immediate hysterectomy + abdominal exploration if patient survives transport.

**DIC (Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation):**
Development of coagulopathy superimposed on HELLP indicates DIC. Signs: continued bleeding despite oxytocin (from surgical or delivery wounds), oozing from IV sites, gum bleeding, nosebleeds. Treat with aggressive crystalloid resuscitation, transfusion if available, and urgent delivery.

**Acute Kidney Injury:**
Oliguria (<400 ml/24 hours) indicates kidney involvement. HELLP causes microangiopathic hemolytic anemia that damages renal vessels. Manage with fluid restriction (not overload—paradoxically worsens kidney injury), furosemide 40-80 mg IV if available, and urgent dialysis if accessible.

:::warning
HELLP syndrome can progress from recognition to maternal death within 4-8 hours without intervention. If HELLP suspected, assume obstetric emergency and prepare for immediate delivery. Do not delay for confirmatory tests—clinical diagnosis is sufficient to mandate action.
:::

## 14. Perimortem Cesarean Section - Expanded Technique

Perimortem C-section (maternal cardiac arrest with emergency fetal delivery) is covered in Section 10. This expanded section provides additional anatomical detail and troubleshooting.

### Anatomical Landmarks for Rapid Access

When operating on a mother in cardiac arrest, time is measured in seconds. Anatomical landmarks guide rapid entry to the uterus:

**Landmarks:**
- Xiphoid process (bottom of breastbone): palpate at sternal border
- Umbilicus: landmark for horizontal entry level
- Pubic symphysis (firm cartilage union of pelvic bones): landmark for lower abdomen
- Midline: palpate between two rectus muscles

**Incision Site:** Vertical midline from xiphoid to 2 cm above pubic symphysis (rapid, direct access to uterus). Avoid lateral movement—increases bleeding from mesenteric vessels.

### Rapid Identification of Uterus in Pregnancy

In a mother in cardiac arrest, you must identify the uterus rapidly among other organs:

- **Size**: Gravid uterus is massive (melon-sized in third trimester), firm, blue-purple color from engorgement
- **Location**: Fills most of pelvis and lower abdomen
- **Edges**: Rounded edges distinguishable from intestines (which are tubular)
- **Consistency**: Firm, muscular (different from soft bowel loops)

Once identified, make vertical incision directly into uterine muscle. Do not attempt lower segment if time critical—mid-uterine incision faster and adequate for emergency delivery.

### Alternative: Destructive Delivery (Last Resort if C-section Impossible)

If maternal cardiorespiratory arrest and no surgical capability exists, destructive delivery (craniotomy or symphysiotomy) may be attempted as final resort. This carries 90%+ maternal mortality and is only attempted when mother is already dead and fetal viability possible:

**Craniotomy (Perforation of fetal skull):** If fetal head at pelvic inlet and maternal death imminent, skull can be perforated with sharp instrument to collapse head, allowing delivery through pelvis. This kills fetus but may allow delivery without maternal surgical trauma.

**Symphysiotomy:** Surgical division of pubic symphysis to increase pelvic diameter. Extremely invasive, high morbidity, not recommended in modern practice.

Both are last-resort measures only if maternal death occurs and fetal delivery otherwise impossible.

:::warning
Destructive delivery procedures are only appropriate when the mother is already dead or dying and the fetus may survive with delivery. Do not perform these procedures on a living mother—attempt standard resuscitation and C-section first.
:::

## 15. Uterine Inversion - Expanded Management

Uterine inversion is covered in Section 11. This expanded section provides additional techniques and troubleshooting.

### Prevention Strategies

Prevention is far superior to treatment. These techniques reduce inversion risk:

- **Controlled Placental Delivery:** The Brandt-Andrews maneuver is the gold standard: one hand stabilizes the uterine fundus from above via abdominal wall while the other hand applies gentle downward traction on the umbilical cord. Never pull hard on the cord alone.

- **Ergot Timing:** Ergot alkaloids cause sustained uterine contraction. If given before placental separation is complete, the contracting uterus may invert around the placenta. Always wait for signs of placental separation (fresh gush of blood, uterus rising and becoming globular, cord extending further out) before giving ergot.

- **Fundal Support:** Always have one hand supporting the uterine fundus during placental delivery. This prevents inversion as placenta delivers.

### Manual Replacement - Detailed Anatomy

The Johnson maneuver (manual replacement) requires understanding the inversion anatomy:

When uterus inverts, the fundus (normally the most muscular part) inverts into the uterine cavity, creating a configuration like a sock being turned inside-out. The deepest point of inversion is the fundus. To reverse this:

1. Insert gloved hand into vagina as far as possible
2. Locate the inverted fundus (the deepest, most distal point you can reach)
3. Place palm of hand against the fundus
4. Apply steady, continuous upward pressure (not jabbing or pulsatile pressure—smooth and constant)
5. Simultaneously, apply downward pressure on the uterine body with your other hand on the abdomen
6. This creates a "sandwich" compression that slowly re-everts the uterus
7. Takes 2-5 minutes of steady pressure typically

Once fundus re-everts and normal shape returns, keep hand in uterus with firm pressure for another 3-5 minutes to prevent recurrence. Give oxytocin during this time to maintain contraction.

### Hydrostatic Replacement - Technique Refinement

If manual replacement fails, the O'Sullivan maneuver (hydrostatic replacement) can be effective:

1. Fill vagina with warm normal saline (36-37°C if possible; warm saline absorbed better)
2. Use large-bore IV tubing or catheter to deliver fluid
3. Have an assistant occlude the vaginal opening around the tubing with their hand to seal the vagina
4. Fluid pressure gradually pushes the inverted fundus upward and outward
5. Can take 5-15 minutes; do not rush
6. Success rate approximately 60% if manual replacement failed
7. Less traumatic to uterine tissue than forced manual replacement

:::tip
If hydrostatic replacement progresses slowly, do not give up prematurely. Allow 10-15 minutes of continued gentle fluid pressure. Some inversions require prolonged patient pressure to re-evert.
:::

### Recurrent Inversion Prevention

Even after successful replacement, uterus may re-invert if muscle relaxation occurs:

- **Oxytocin maintenance:** Continue IV oxytocin infusion for 4-6 hours postpartum (not just minutes). Dose: 10 units in 500 ml normal saline, infuse at 125 ml/hr
- **Hand compression:** Consider keeping a hand in the uterus (on tamponade) for 15-30 minutes after replacement if inversion was deep
- **Avoid ergot:** Do not use ergot alkaloids in the immediate postpartum period if inversion has occurred (ergot can cause re-inversion)
- **Uterine packing:** If available, pack uterus with sterile gauze moistened with antiseptic solution and leave in place for 12-24 hours to maintain shape

## 16. Amniotic Fluid Embolism - Extended Recognition and Management

AFE is covered in Section 10. This expanded section provides pathophysiology context and recognition strategies.

### Mechanism and Risk Factors

Amniotic fluid embolism occurs when fetal material (amniotic fluid containing fetal cells, vernix, hair, meconium) enters maternal circulation through small tears in the endocervical veins or uterine vessels. Once fetal material contacts maternal blood, it triggers an anaphylactic-type reaction with complement activation, massive cytokine release, and cardiovascular collapse.

**Risk Factors Increasing AFE Probability:**
- Labor augmentation with oxytocin (increases intrauterine pressure, risk of vessel rupture)
- Cesarean delivery (placental bed exposure to maternal circulation)
- Placental abruption (large vessel exposure)
- Operative vaginal delivery (forceps/vacuum)
- Uterine rupture
- Amniotic fluid under pressure (polyhydramnios, ruptured membranes with high fluid volume)

**Timing:** Can occur during labor, delivery, immediately postpartum, or within hours after delivery. Most common within 30 minutes of delivery.

### Biphasic Presentation Pattern

AFE may present in biphasic pattern—initial cardiovascular collapse followed by coagulopathy phase:

**Phase 1 (Minutes 0-10):** Cardiovascular collapse—sudden dyspnea, chest pain, hypotension, tachycardia, respiratory distress, cyanosis, loss of consciousness. This phase is primarily mechanical (fetal material in pulmonary circulation) + anaphylactoid reaction (complement activation).

**Phase 2 (Minutes 10-30):** Coagulopathy phase—DIC develops with bleeding from all sites despite ongoing resuscitation. Platelet consumption causes thrombocytopenia. Fibrinogen consumed. Paradoxically, clotting cascade activation causes both clotting and bleeding simultaneously.

Some patients skip Phase 1 and present primarily with unexplained coagulopathy postpartum—particularly dangerous because diagnosis delayed.

### Recognition Challenges in Field Settings

AFE is easily misdiagnosed as:
- **Pulmonary embolism** (also causes sudden dyspnea, chest pain, hypotension)—but AFE occurs in pregnancy/immediate postpartum with labor/delivery trigger
- **Peripartum cardiomyopathy** (also causes sudden heart failure)—but AFE more sudden and catastrophic
- **Anaphylaxis** (also causes dyspnea, hypotension)—but AFE does not have typical allergen exposure history

**Key Distinguishing Feature:** AFE + massive coagulopathy (bleeding from all sites) in immediate peripartum period = AFE until proven otherwise. No other condition presents this way.

### Supportive Care Without ICU

AFE management in field settings:

1. **CPR immediately** — begin chest compressions and ventilation
2. **High-concentration oxygen** — if any respiratory support available
3. **IV access** — wide-open large-bore IVs (two if possible); aggressive crystalloid infusion
4. **Vasopressors** — epinephrine boluses 1 mg IV every 3-5 minutes during resuscitation (if available)
5. **Baby delivery** — if not already delivered, emergency C-section or destructive delivery to stop amniotic fluid entry into maternal circulation
6. **Hemorrhage control** — apply direct pressure to surgical wounds, uterine massage if atony contributing
7. **Hypothermia prevention** — warm IV fluids, blankets (cold worsens coagulopathy)
8. **Evacuation** — this patient requires ICU-level care (extracorporeal membrane oxygenation, massive transfusion) for any chance of survival beyond minutes

:::warning
AFE has 60-80% mortality even with modern ICU care. In field settings without transfusion capability and ICU support, outcomes approach 95%+ mortality. Immediate evacuation is critical if any chance of transport to facility with massive transfusion protocol and ECMO capability.
:::
