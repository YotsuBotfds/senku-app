## Introduction: The Hardest Conversation

Famine triage is the decision about who receives limited calories when starvation is mathematically inevitable. Some people will die. The question is: who, and by what process?

This is the hardest conversation a community will have. There is no good answer. But there are **better and worse processes**.

A process decided in secret, by one person, without accountability breeds rage and permanent damage. A process that is transparent, inclusive, and grounded in an ethical framework—while still devastating—allows a community to survive morally as well as physically.

:::danger
**The Math of Famine**
Before implementing triage, confirm the math:
- Total calories available per day
- Population size and consumption rates
- Expected duration of shortage (weeks? months?)
- Possible relief scenarios (trade, migration, new crops)

If 2000 calories/day are available and 100 people need 2000 total, you have 50 full rations or 100 half rations. Exactly who gets what determines who lives and who dies. The decision is binding.
:::

## Part 1: When Does Triage Begin?

Triage is the **last resort**, not the first response. Implement it only after:

### Exhausting Other Options

**Reduce consumption (non-lethal):**
- Switch to lower-calorie staples (switch wheat to cheaper, less nutritious grains)
- Extend meals (rice stretched with water, soup with more broth, less meat)
- Cut individual rations by 20-30% (creates malnutrition but not immediate death)
- Reduce activity (minimize labor, rest more to lower caloric burn)

**Increase resources (if possible):**
- Trade with other communities (weapons, labor, goods for food)
- Forage or hunt (mushrooms, game, tree bark, insects)
- Fish or trap (if water access exists)
- Plant emergency crops (fast-growing greens if season allows)
- Borrow against future harvest (debt to be repaid later)

**Population reduction (controversial):**
- Encourage or assist migration (people leave voluntarily to other communities)
- Release dependents to other groups (children sent to relatives elsewhere, not abandoned)
- Temporary separation (healthy adults work elsewhere, return when food situation improves)

**Internal reallocation:**
- Reduce rations equally across community (everyone gets 60% of normal)
- Tax luxury consumption (anyone hoarding personal stores must contribute)
- Redistribute stored wealth (those with private supplies donate to community pool)

**Only when these are exhausted or impossible do you move to triage.**

### The Trigger Decision

A council must formally decide: "We are now in a famine triage scenario."

**Trigger criteria:**
- Food supply will fall below survival for whole population within X weeks
- No realistic possibility of resupply or trade
- Migration is not feasible (no other communities accepting refugees, hostile territory, etc.)
- Further reduction of rations means death within weeks instead of months

**Decision process:**
1. Council analyzes food supply vs demand
2. Expert (healer, farmer, or knowledgeable person) projects survival scenarios
3. Council votes: Is triage necessary? Is it now?
4. If yes, council announces: "Triage has been activated effective [date]. This means some people will receive reduced rations."

**The announcement includes:**
- Why (be specific about the food shortage math)
- When (effective date)
- How long (estimated duration)
- Ethical framework being used (see below)
- Process for grievances/appeals

:::warning
**Do Not Pretend It's Not Happening**
Communities that drift into starvation without explicitly choosing a triage system end up with chaos: theft, violence, secret rations, family fighting. Declaring triage openly, though devastating, is better than implicit starvation.
:::

## Part 2: Ethical Frameworks for Triage

There is no right answer. Here are the main ethical approaches:

### Framework 1: Utilitarian (Save the Most Lives)

**Logic**: Maximize total survival. If triage can keep 80 people alive instead of 60, do it.

**Who receives full/reduced rations:**
- Children and young adults (most life-years ahead, highest survival potential)
- Physically healthy people (best chance to survive malnutrition)
- Productive workers (their labor may enable recovery faster)
- Exclude: elderly (few years left), chronically ill (likely to die anyway), disabled (lower caloric efficiency)

**Advantages:**
- Mathematically clear
- Maximizes community's productive capacity
- Increases chance of recovery
- Easy to explain

**Disadvantages:**
- Abandons elderly and disabled
- Creates tier system (valuable vs expendable lives)
- Can become eugenic in practice
- Deeply traumatic for families losing relatives
- Damages long-term community trust and solidarity

**Risk**: If implemented harshly, becomes a form of organized killing. Community may choose this framework but implement it with compassion (reduced rations rather than starvation).

### Framework 2: Egalitarian (Equal Sacrifice)

**Logic**: Everyone gets the same reduced ration. No tiers. If 50% of normal calories keep everyone weakened but alive, give everyone 50%.

**Who receives:**
- Everyone: equal ration
- No exemptions
- No special treatment

**Advantages:**
- Maintains dignity and equality
- Everyone shares suffering equally
- No one is blamed for death
- Preserves long-term solidarity
- Easier to explain and implement

**Disadvantages:**
- Slower recovery (no labor force to rebuild)
- Higher mortality overall (everyone becomes malnourished)
- Children suffer permanent damage (stunting, cognitive impairment)
- Elderly and disabled die equally with others
- May lead to faster starvation for all

**Risk**: If rations are truly equal but insufficient, the entire community starves together rather than some surviving to rebuild.

### Framework 3: Needs-Based (Care for Vulnerable)

**Logic**: Prioritize people who cannot care for themselves. Children, elderly, disabled receive full rations. Everyone else receives less.

**Who receives:**
- Children under 14: full ration
- Elderly 65+: full ration
- Disabled/chronically ill: full ration
- Healthy adults: reduced ration
- Healthy working-age adults: minimal ration

**Advantages:**
- Protects those who cannot advocate for themselves
- Reduces permanent harm to children
- Honors cultural values of elder care
- Maintains human dignity
- Long-term community is healthier (surviving adults + healthy children)

**Disadvantages:**
- Healthy adults may become resentful (they starve to feed others)
- Low energy workforce slows recovery
- Dependent population may exceed food supply (even giving all to dependents isn't enough)
- Requires accepting that some healthy adults will die

**Risk**: Healthy working-age adults may revolt or abandon the community if they feel sacrificed unfairly.

### Framework 4: Contribution-Based (You Get What You Give)

**Logic**: Ration allocation reflects labor contribution. Those who work more, eat more. Those who don't work, eat less.

**Who receives:**
- Heavy laborers (farm, water, security): full ration
- Moderate laborers (maintenance, childcare): 75% ration
- Light laborers (sick, injured, elderly): 50% ration
- Non-laborers (young children, severe disabled): 25% ration

**Advantages:**
- Incentivizes work (people know rations depend on output)
- Maintains productivity during crisis
- Feels fair to workers (they carry extra burden, get extra food)
- Recovers quickly (people working hard to earn rations)

**Disadvantages:**
- Punishes children and disabled for circumstances beyond their control
- Creates extreme inequality
- Can become exploitative (people work themselves to death for extra rations)
- Vulnerable people die quickly
- Permanent damage to ethical trust in community
- If someone becomes injured/ill, they lose rations immediately (no safety net)

**Risk**: Becomes a system of wage slavery or indentured servitude.

### Framework 5: Lottery (Random/Democratic)

**Logic**: No group is preferred. Everyone's name is in a hat. Random draw determines who gets full rations, who gets half, who gets quarter.

**Implementation:**
- Draw names for each tier (full ration, 3/4 ration, half ration, quarter ration)
- Redraw monthly to rotate burden
- Or draw once at the start (permanent allocation for duration of crisis)

**Advantages:**
- Truly equal (no group favored)
- No one can argue they're being discriminated against
- Removes decisions from people's hands (difficult moral burden on decision-makers is distributed)
- Fair from a game-theory perspective

**Disadvantages:**
- Innocent deaths seem random/unfair
- Doesn't account for actual need (healthy young person might starve while frail elderly person survives)
- Highly destructive to morale (people feel they're playing roulette with death)
- Can be gamed (people hide identities, use false names)
- Doesn't optimize for survival or recovery

**Risk**: People lose faith in community entirely if they feel their survival depends on luck, not action or value.

### Recommended Approach: Ethical Hybrid

Combine frameworks:

1. **Core protection**: Children under 12 and people 65+ receive adequate calories (needs-based framework)
2. **Equal base**: Everyone receives a survival minimum (egalitarian framework)
3. **Work incentive**: Those who work get additional rations (contribution-based framework, but with a safety net)
4. **Oversight**: Council reviews decisions monthly; can reallocate if approach proves disastrous (accountability)

**Example allocation:**
- Children (0-12): 100% ration (needs-based)
- Elderly (65+): 100% ration (needs-based)
- Healthy adults doing essential work (farm, water, security): 100% ration
- Healthy adults doing less essential work (maintenance, childcare, administration): 75% ration
- Disabled/chronically ill: 75% ration (needs-based + respect for capacity)
- Pregnant/nursing mothers: 120% ration (needs-based)

This approach prioritizes vulnerable groups, maintains work incentive, and avoids extremes.

## Part 3: Decision-Making Process

### Who Decides?

**Option A: Leadership + Council**
- Leader and council meet privately
- Decide the framework
- Announce decision to community

Pros: Fast, decisive
Cons: Community doesn't trust the decision; feels imposed

**Option B: Full Community Assembly**
- Entire community gathers
- Each framework is explained
- Discussion/debate
- Vote on which framework to use
- Elected triage committee implements details

Pros: Everyone has voice; decision feels legitimate
Cons: Takes time (debate can last days), may gridlock

**Option C: Stakeholder Groups**
- Elders discuss what framework protects vulnerability
- Working-age people discuss what framework keeps recovery possible
- Parents discuss what framework protects children
- Council synthesizes input and decides

Pros: Multiple perspectives heard; faster than full assembly
Cons: Groups may deadlock; council still imposes final decision

### Recommended Process

1. **Crisis declaration** (24 hours)
   - Council announces: "Food shortage is at crisis level"
   - Calls for assembly tomorrow evening

2. **Community assembly** (evening of day 1)
   - Expert explains food supply math (be specific: how many calories, for how long)
   - Each framework is explained (utilitarian, egalitarian, needs-based, contribution, hybrid)
   - Open discussion (1 hour)
   - Written vote on which framework

3. **Implementation committee** (days 2-3)
   - Council appoints triage committee (5-7 people, must include at least one non-council member)
   - Committee works out details (exact ration amounts, who goes in which tier)
   - Committee drafts ration allocation list

4. **Community ratification** (day 4)
   - Ration list is posted for 24 hours (everyone can see where they are)
   - Appeals/corrections handled (errors in tier assignment)
   - Final list approved by council vote

5. **Implementation** (day 5 onwards)
   - Rationing begins
   - Triage committee meets weekly to monitor and adjust

### Grievance Process

**People can appeal their ration tier:**

1. **Submit appeal** to triage committee (writing or oral testimony)
   - State reason for appeal (error in categorization, new information, changed circumstances)
   - Provide evidence (example: "I'm not as sick as you think—I can work full days")

2. **Committee reviews**
   - Meets within 3 days
   - Hears appeal in person
   - Can request healer or other expert assessment
   - May reallocate person to different tier

3. **Appeal decision** is made public (though specific details may be private)
   - "Appeal reviewed; tier changed from 75% to 100%" (no details on why)

4. **Unresolved dispute goes to council**
   - Council can override committee decision
   - Council vote is final

:::warning
**Do Not Allow Secret Negotiations**
A person should not be able to get a better ration by bribing the committee or appealing in private to the leader. All adjustments are transparent and documented. This prevents corruption and perception of favoritism.
:::

## Part 4: Protecting Vulnerable Groups

### Children

**Special protections:**
- No child receives less than 75% of their normal caloric needs
- Priority is given to children under 5 (highest vulnerability)
- Nursing mothers receive extra rations (so milk production doesn't fail)
- Food is distributed to children directly (not through parents who might divert it)
- Monitor carefully for malnutrition (swollen bellies, lethargy, hair changes)

**Psychological impact:**
- Children will understand that food is scarce
- Never tell a child "you're not getting food so others can live"
- Instead: "Everyone is eating less right now. It's temporary. We're going to be okay."

### Elderly

**Dignity protections:**
- Elderly are not blamed for consuming calories
- Cultural respect for elders is maintained (they're not treated as a burden)
- Elderly are involved in decisions (not excluded from assemblies, votes)
- Life expectancy is not used as an excuse to reduce rations

**Practical considerations:**
- Elderly may have difficulty chewing/digesting less nutritious food
- May need softer, pre-chewed, or easier-to-digest rations
- Medication may depend on food (some medicines require food)
- They may die even on full rations due to pre-existing conditions (not a reason to ration less)

### Disabled or Chronically Ill

**Protections:**
- Rationing is not based on productivity (disabled person gets same as non-disabled)
- Mobility aids, medications, special diets are provided regardless of ration tier
- They are not made to feel like a burden (explicitly valued in community assembly)
- If ration level worsens their condition (medication requiring food), it's adjusted

### Pregnant and Nursing Women

**High priority:**
- Pregnant women receive 120% of normal rations (fetal development needs extra calories)
- Nursing mothers receive 120% of normal rations (milk production depletes stores)
- If shortage is so severe that even 120% isn't enough, difficult triage decisions are made with full council knowledge (not hidden)

### Minorities or Unpopular Groups

**Risk**: In crisis, unpopular groups (foreign refugees, people of different ethnicity/religion, recent arrivals) may be rationed less.

**Safeguards:**
- Triage committee must include at least one member from/sympathetic to any minority group
- Rationing must be based on objective criteria (age, health, family ties), not identity
- If any group has systematically lower rations, council must publicly explain why (and justify it, which is usually impossible)
- Public voting on ration allocation prevents secret discrimination

## Part 5: Implementation and Monitoring

### Ration Distribution Method

**Daily distribution** (preferred):
- Fixed time each day (7 AM, for example)
- Each person comes to distribution point
- Receives their day's ration
- Witnesses watch (prevents theft, ensures fairness)

**Weekly distribution** (if more practical):
- Saturday morning, for example
- Each household receives their week's rations
- Same-day consumption recommended (prevents hoarding/theft)
- Households must distribute fairly to their members (family culture enforces this)

### Preventing Ration Fraud

**Challenges:**
- People hiding food from community pool
- Stronger people stealing from weaker people
- Merchants or leaders taking extra
- Children traded for rations by parents

**Safeguards:**
- Surprise audits of storage (council enters homes to verify no secret stores)
- Public weighing/counting (rations are counted in full view of witnesses)
- Clear marking of official rations (rations are marked/sealed so theft is obvious)
- Punishments for theft (confiscation, reduced rations, or exile in extreme cases)
- Education (explain that individual hoarding means the whole community fails faster)

### Weekly Monitoring

**Triage committee meets weekly to assess:**

1. **Is the ration amount working?**
   - Are people on full rations healthy and capable of work?
   - Are people on reduced rations deteriorating rapidly, slowly, or holding steady?
   - Are deaths occurring at expected rate or higher/lower?

2. **Any new food supply discovered?**
   - Hunt was successful, trade agreement came through, new crop mature
   - If supply increases 10%+, entire community benefits (increase all rations by 10%)

3. **Any unforeseen impacts?**
   - Pregnant women on reduced rations miscarrying
   - Children becoming dangerously malnourished
   - Fighting over rations occurring
   - Mental health crisis

4. **Any deaths?**
   - Document the death (who, when, cause, ration level)
   - If death rate is higher than expected, investigate why
   - If unexpected groups are dying, there may be a problem (hidden disease, theft, etc.)

5. **Any grievances?**
   - Review appeals and adjustments made
   - Are appeals being heard fairly?
   - Is there evidence of favoritism?

### Adjustment Process

If monitoring reveals problems:

**If rations are too low (people dying faster than expected):**
- Council increases all rations by 10-20%
- Council looks for new food sources
- Council discusses whether someone should be removed from triage (if supply improves enough)

**If rations are too high (food running out faster than projected):**
- Council reduces all rations by 10-20%
- Council discusses which groups take the hit (try to protect children/elderly)
- Council looks for alternative calorie sources (insects, wild plants, etc.)

**If a group is affected disproportionately:**
- Committee investigates (is someone taking more than their share, is there theft, is there disease?)
- Allocations may be adjusted for that group specifically
- If the cause is theft, thieves are punished

## Part 6: Psychological Impact on Decision-Makers

The people who decide who eats will carry this for life. They need support:

### Before Implementation

**Prepare decision-makers psychologically:**
- Acknowledge this is traumatic (don't pretend it's routine)
- Discuss ethics explicitly (why this framework, what it means)
- Allow time for questions and moral doubts
- Pair decision-makers with a mentor/healer who can listen

### During Implementation

**Support during rationing:**
- Regular check-ins with triage committee (their mental state matters)
- Healer available for psychological support
- Collective decision-making (share the burden across committee, not one person)
- Rotate committee members if possible (no one person is doing this for 6+ months)
- Clear protocols (reduce moral burden by following a predetermined process)

### After Rationing Ends

**Healing for decision-makers:**
- Formal acknowledgment of what was done
- Community thanks the committee for their sacrifice
- Counseling available for PTSD, guilt, nightmares
- Explicit discussion: "You made the least terrible choice in an impossible situation. You did what was necessary."

:::info-box
**Moral Injury**
Decision-makers may experience "moral injury"—trauma from being forced to do something that violates their values. This is different from guilt (guilt is appropriate; moral injury is wounds). Communities need to help decision-makers heal, not blame them for choices the community collectively made.
:::

## Part 7: Historical Examples

### Example 1: Dutch Winter Famine (1944-45)

**Context**: German occupation, Nazi scorched-earth policy cut off food supply to Dutch cities in winter.

**Response**:
- Official rations dropped to 400-500 calories/day (starvation level)
- Underground system organized ration distribution fairly
- No formal triage (equal starvation across population)
- ~20,000 people died, mostly elderly and children

**Outcome**:
- Community survived but with permanent damage (survivors showed lifelong health problems)
- Low morale (people felt abandoned by all authority)
- Post-war resentment (some families resented losing members to starvation while others hoarded)

**Lesson**: Equal rationing to starvation level doesn't prevent suffering; it distributes it. Better to formally triage and save some than pretend equality is fair.

### Example 2: Siege of Leningrad (1941-44)

**Context**: Nazi siege; city of 3 million surrounded, no food entering.

**Response**:
- Utilitarian triage: workers got full rations, others got minimal
- Children and elderly rapidly excluded from rations
- ~1 million died (mostly non-workers)
- Surviving workers had energy to defend and work

**Outcome**:
- City militarily survived
- Post-war trauma and guilt among survivors
- Families broke apart (some members starved while others received workers' rations)
- Permanent damage to social trust

**Lesson**: Utilitarian triage saved the city but destroyed community solidarity. People who survived felt complicit in deaths of family members.

### Example 3: Irish Famine (1845-52)

**Context**: Potato blight; crop failures; British government did not prioritize relief.

**Response**:
- No formal triage (denial that crisis was happening)
- Voluntary charity (random, inconsistent)
- Starvation and disease spreading unchecked
- ~1 million died; another million emigrated

**Outcome**:
- Community collapse (Ireland's population never recovered)
- Permanent resentment of authorities
- Loss of language, culture, knowledge
- Generational trauma

**Lesson**: Refusing to make explicit triage decisions doesn't prevent death; it ensures chaos and maximum suffering.

## Part 8: Recovery and Reconciliation

After rationing ends (food becomes available again):

### Immediate Recovery Phase (Weeks 1-4)

**Restoration to normal rations:**
- Return to pre-crisis consumption levels quickly (people are severely malnourished)
- Focus on nutrient density (protein, fats, vitamins) not just calories
- Monitor for complications (refeeding syndrome, disease)

**Economic reset:**
- Ration prices return to normal
- People who received less rations don't owe debt
- No financial punishment for having been rationed

### Healing Phase (Weeks 4-12)

**Public acknowledgment:**
- Community assembly discusses what happened
- Names of dead are honored (not swept under rug)
- Triage committee is publicly thanked for their sacrifice
- No blame is assigned to decision-makers (community made choice collectively)

**Conflict resolution:**
- Resentment between groups (some got more rations than others) surfaces
- Mediation is offered to families that fought over rations
- Restitution is considered (if there was theft/hoarding, how do we repair it?)
- Some people may want to leave community (offer aid to departing families)

### Long-Term Reconciliation (Months 3-12)

**Collective memory:**
- Document what happened (written record if literacy exists, oral record otherwise)
- Mark anniversary (ceremony remembering dead, honoring survivors)
- Teach next generation (so they understand the crisis and learn from it)

**Prevent recurrence:**
- Develop better food storage and reserves (so crisis is less likely next time)
- Plan alternative rations (if it happens again, we know what to do)
- Monitor for early warning signs (don't wait until starvation)

**Psychological healing:**
- Healer or counselor continues support for traumatized people
- Survivors share their experiences (group processing)
- Decision-makers are explicitly freed from guilt ("You saved us")
- Cultural practices help (religious services, ceremonies, music)

## Conclusion: Making the Impossible Decision

Famine triage is the worst decision a community will make. There is no good outcome. But there are better and worse processes.

A process that is **transparent, ethical, inclusive, and accountable** will preserve community after the crisis ends. A process that is **secret, arbitrary, and unaccountable** will destroy the community, even if more people physically survive.

Plan now:
1. Identify likely triggers of famine (crop failure, blockade, etc.)
2. Discuss ethical frameworks with community before crisis hits
3. Develop rationing protocols with input from all groups
4. Train a potential triage committee
5. Practice (simulations help people think through the hard parts)

When famine comes, you'll have clarity. That clarity won't make the decision less devastating, but it will make it survivable.
