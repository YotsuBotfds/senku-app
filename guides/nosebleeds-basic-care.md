:::warning
**Medical Disclaimer:** Most nosebleeds are minor and stop with simple first aid. A nosebleed can also follow an injury, come from deeper in the nose, or be a sign of a problem that needs medical care. This guide is for practical home care when the person is otherwise stable. If the bleeding is heavy, the person is weak or injured, or the nosebleed will not stop, seek medical care.
:::

<section id="overview">

## Overview

A nosebleed is bleeding from the lining inside the nose. Most are caused by small blood vessels near the front of the nose drying out, cracking, or getting irritated. Common triggers include:

- Dry air, especially in winter
- Nose picking
- Blowing the nose too hard
- A cold, allergies, or irritated nasal passages
- Minor bumps or rubbing
- Medicines or conditions that make bleeding easier

This guide is built for searches such as:

- "How do I stop a nosebleed?"
- "What should I do if my nose keeps bleeding?"
- "How do I prevent nosebleeds from dry air?"
- "When is a nosebleed serious?"

If you only need the shortest possible answer: sit upright, lean forward a little, pinch the soft part of the nose firmly for 10 to 15 minutes without checking, breathe through the mouth, and get help if the bleeding is heavy, follows an injury, or does not stop.

This nosebleed guide owns urgent-routing questions about bleeding that will not stop after 20 to 30 minutes of steady pressure, blood pouring from the nose or down the throat, a nosebleed while taking blood-thinning medicine, dizziness, paleness, weakness, or repeated heavy nosebleeds in one day. For those situations, keep leaning forward and using firm pressure while arranging urgent medical help; do not wait for perfect home control before escalating.

If the nosebleed came with a head injury, a sudden severe headache, vomiting, confusion, or other neurologic symptoms, do not treat it as a simple home bleed. Follow [Headaches: Basic Care & Red Flags](../headaches-basic-care.html) for the warning-sign route and [First Aid & Emergency Response](../first-aid.html) for injury care.

</section>

<section id="first-steps">

## First Steps

1. Sit the person upright.
2. Lean them slightly forward.
3. Tell them to breathe through the mouth and spit out blood rather than swallow it.
4. Pinch the soft, flexible part of the nose closed with thumb and finger.
5. Hold steady pressure for 10 to 15 minutes without looking to see if it has stopped.
6. If it is still bleeding, repeat another full 10 to 15 minutes of pressure.

Use a clock if you can. Many nosebleeds stop only after a full, uninterrupted pressure period.

If blood is running into the throat, keep the person leaning forward. Swallowed blood can cause nausea and vomiting.

</section>

<section id="pressure-positioning">

## Pressure and Positioning

The best home treatment is direct pressure on the soft part of the nose.

- Pinch the soft lower part of the nose, not the hard bridge.
- Keep the head up and slightly forward.
- Stay calm and still during the pressure period.
- If available, a cold pack on the bridge of the nose or cheeks may help comfort, but pressure is the main treatment.

Do not keep releasing the nose to check every minute. That usually restarts the bleed.

</section>

<section id="what-not-to-do">

## What Not To Do

- Do not tilt the head back.
- Do not lie flat while the nose is actively bleeding.
- Do not pack deep into the nose with tissues, cotton, or other objects if simple pressure is enough.
- Do not blow the nose during the bleed.
- Do not pick at clots or scabs afterward.
- Do not shove tools, swabs, or tweezers into the nose.
- Do not keep trying hard if the person has obvious facial injury, trouble breathing, or a large amount of blood.

If the nosebleed started after a blow to the face or head, treat it more cautiously.

</section>

<section id="dry-air-prevention">

## Dry-Air Prevention

Dry nasal lining is one of the most common reasons for repeat nosebleeds.

Helpful steps:

- Use a humidifier if one is available and safe to clean.
- Keep indoor air from getting overly dry when possible.
- Drink enough fluids for general hydration.
- Use a small amount of saline nasal spray or saline drops if available.
- Start with saline and humidification first. A thin smear of petroleum jelly just inside the nostril may help some people if the nose is still very dry, but do not put it deep inside the nose.
- Avoid forceful nose blowing when you have a cold or allergies.

If the person has frequent nosebleeds in winter, dry air is a common culprit.

</section>

<section id="recurrence-prevention">

## Preventing Repeat Nosebleeds

After a nosebleed stops, the inside of the nose is easy to reopen.

For the next day:

- Avoid nose blowing if possible.
- Avoid picking the nose.
- Avoid heavy lifting, straining, or hard exercise.
- Avoid very hot drinks or anything that seems to trigger renewed bleeding.
- Keep the air moist if dry air is part of the problem.

Longer term:

- Treat allergies or colds gently so the nose is not irritated repeatedly.
- Trim nails if nose picking is a recurring habit, especially in children.
- Consider whether a medication or blood-thinning medicine may be contributing if nosebleeds are frequent.
- If one side of the nose keeps bleeding, or the nosebleeds happen often, get checked if care is available.

</section>

<section id="when-to-worry">

## When To Worry

Get urgent medical help if any of the following apply:

<div class="red-flag">

- The bleeding is heavy or will not stop after 20 to 30 minutes of firm pressure.
- The person feels faint, dizzy, pale, weak, confused, or short of breath.
- The nosebleed followed a head, face, or nose injury.
- Blood is pouring down the throat or causing vomiting.
- Blood keeps draining into the throat with no clear front-of-nose source.
- The person has trouble breathing.
- The bleeding is happening after a serious fall or possible broken nose.
- The person has a bleeding disorder, takes a blood-thinning medicine, or has very frequent unexplained nosebleeds.

</div>

Also get medical advice if:

- Nosebleeds are happening often without a clear reason.
- Nosebleeds are happening every week or keep returning in dry weather despite humidification and gentle care.
- A child under 2 years old has a nosebleed.
- There are signs of anemia, such as unusual paleness, fast heartbeat, fatigue, or shortness of breath.
- One nostril keeps bleeding from the same spot again and again.
- There is a foul smell, severe congestion, or concern for a foreign object in the nose.

</section>

<section id="special-cases">

## Special Cases

For a child:

- Keep the child calm and seated forward.
- Use steady pressure on the soft part of the nose.
- Do not let them lie back with blood draining into the throat.
- Watch closely after the bleeding stops because children often start rubbing or picking again.

For a suspected foreign object:

- Do not dig around inside the nose.
- If the object is visible and easy to remove, that is one thing.
- If it is not obvious, is deep, or is a button battery or magnet, get urgent help.

For a possible severe injury:

- Treat the nosebleed as part of the injury, not as a simple everyday bleed.
- Look for deformity, swelling, heavy facial bruising, or persistent bleeding.

</section>

<section id="quick-checklist">

## Quick Checklist

1. Sit up and lean forward.
2. Pinch the soft part of the nose.
3. Hold pressure for 10 to 15 minutes without checking.
4. Repeat once if needed.
5. Keep the nose dry and calm for the next day.
6. Get help fast if the bleed is heavy, follows injury, or does not stop.

</section>
