## Overview

Non-ferrous metals—copper, tin, bronze, brass, lead, and aluminum—are invaluable in survival and rebuilding scenarios. Unlike iron, these metals melt at lower temperatures, making them accessible with primitive furnaces. Copper is native in many regions, requiring no smelting. Bronze and brass create superior tools compared to pure copper. Lead is easy to cast into weights, bullets, and radiation shielding. Aluminum, abundant in scrap, offers lightweight structural material.

:::info-box
**Why Non-Ferrous Metals Matter:** Iron requires high-temperature furnaces (1200°C+) and is brittle if improperly worked. Copper melts at 1085°C, bronze at 1150°C, and lead at just 327°C. This accessibility, combined with superior corrosion resistance, makes non-ferrous metals critical for water systems, precision tools, and long-term infrastructure.
:::

Non-ferrous metals resist oxidation better than iron, making them ideal for vessels, pipes, and maritime applications. In post-industrial settings, salvaged copper, brass, and aluminum are abundant, providing raw material without mining.

## Metallurgy Basics

Understanding the properties of each non-ferrous metal is essential for selecting the right material and processing technique.

### Copper Properties

Copper is the foundational non-ferrous metal. Its electrical and thermal conductivity are exceptional (only silver exceeds copper). Copper naturally resists corrosion through patina formation—a greenish surface layer that protects the interior.

:::tip
**Native Copper:** In ore-rich regions, copper deposits often include pure copper nuggets and veins. Cold-hammer native copper directly into shapes—no smelting required. This is the fastest path to copper working in early survival.
:::

**Key Properties:**
- Melting point: 1085°C (1985°F)
- Density: 8.96 g/cm³ (very heavy)
- Ductility: Excellent when annealed; hardens when worked
- Color: Bright orange-red when pure
- Corrosion resistance: Forms stable green patina

Copper alloys (bronze, brass) are harder and stronger than pure copper, suitable for tools and weapons.

### Tin Properties and Availability

Tin is rare but essential. Its primary use is hardening copper to create bronze. Tin ore (cassiterite) appears heavy and dark. Unlike copper deposits (which form in volcanic and hydrothermal replacement zones), tin deposits occur in granitic and alluvial environments. Finding both tin and copper nearby requires specific geological conditions where these mineral-forming environments overlap or where weathering has concentrated deposits.

**Key Properties:**
- Melting point: 232°C (450°F) — the lowest of common metals
- Density: 7.31 g/cm³
- Brittleness: Pure tin is fragile but essential as an alloy component
- Availability: Geographically concentrated; some regions have no accessible tin ore

:::danger
**Tin Pest (White-to-Gray Tin):** Below 13°C, pure tin converts to a brittle gray allotrope. This causes casting failure in cold climates. Bronze (8%+ tin) is much more stable than pure tin in cold.
:::

### Copper Alloy Types and Selection

Different copper alloys serve different purposes. Understanding alloy composition helps you choose the right material.

**Phosphor Bronze (Cu-Sn-P):**
- Composition: 90-95% copper, 5-10% tin, small amount of phosphorus
- Properties: High strength, excellent corrosion resistance, maintains hardness at temperature
- Uses: Springs, bearings, precision instruments
- Difficulty: Phosphorus is difficult to add; requires specialized knowledge

**Aluminum Bronze (Cu-Al):**
- Composition: 90% copper, 10% aluminum (or 80-90% copper, 5-10% aluminum depending on variant)
- Properties: Very strong; appearance similar to gold; excellent corrosion resistance even in saltwater
- Uses: Marine hardware, decorative items requiring high strength
- Difficulty: Aluminum is tricky to melt into copper; requires careful temperature management

**Beryllium Copper (Cu-Be):**
- Composition: 97-99% copper, 1-3% beryllium
- Properties: Extremely hard, springy, excellent electrical conductivity
- Uses: High-performance tools, springs, electrical contacts
- Difficulty: Beryllium is toxic; do not attempt this alloy without expertise and safety equipment
- **WARNING:** Beryllium dust is deadly. Never attempt to work with beryllium-copper alloys in survival settings.

**Tin Plating (electroplating thin tin onto copper):**

For food contact or corrosion prevention, coating copper with thin tin layer:

1. Create electroplating cell: Copper object (cathode) and tin electrode (anode) in tin chloride solution
2. Apply DC current: Small battery or hand-crank generator (2-6V DC, 1-2A)
3. Tin metal dissolves from anode and deposits on cathode (copper object)
4. Result: Copper object covered with thin, bright tin coating
5. Time: 15-30 minutes for useful coating thickness

**Advantages of tin-plated copper:** Tin is food-safe; prevents copper oxide discoloration; appearance is bright and attractive; easier to solder.

**Alternative (mechanical plating):** Without electroplating capability, coat copper item with thin layer of solder (lead-tin alloy) using a heated iron and flux. This achieves similar protection without electricity.

### Lead Properties and Hazards

Lead is the softest metalworking metal, with exceptional density. Its ease of casting and melting make it accessible, but toxicity requires extreme care.

**Key Properties:**
- Melting point: 327°C (620°F)
- Density: 11.34 g/cm³ (extremely heavy—denser than copper)
- Softness: Can be shaped cold with light hammering
- Toxicity: SEVERE if ingested or inhaled; stable in sealed forms

:::danger
**LEAD TOXICITY:** Lead dust and fumes are extremely toxic. Prolonged exposure causes neurological damage, especially in children. Lead oxide fumes (produced above 500°C) are acutely poisonous. MANDATORY: Use lead only outdoors or in fully ventilated spaces. Wash hands before eating. Never melt lead indoors. Wear protective gloves and a respirator when handling lead powder.
:::

### Zinc and Brass

Zinc is volatile and less common than copper or tin. Brass (copper-zinc alloy) is easier to make from salvaged brass items than from raw materials.

**Key Properties of Zinc:**
- Melting point: 420°C (788°F)
- Boiling point: 908°C—zinc vaporizes easily above this temp
- Volatility: Requires careful temperature control
- Availability: Usually from salvage rather than ore

**Brass Advantages:**
- Stronger than pure copper
- Excellent corrosion resistance (better than copper in seawater)
- Higher melting point than zinc alone
- Yellow-gold appearance (65-90% copper brass) preferred for tools and visible items

### Aluminum Properties and Post-Industrial Availability

Aluminum is extremely abundant in scrap: window frames, aircraft parts, automotive components, beverage cans, and cookware. Primary smelting (electrolysis) is impractical; salvage and recast.

**Key Properties:**
- Melting point: 660°C (1220°F)
- Density: 2.70 g/cm³ (very light—useful for weight-sensitive tools)
- Oxidation: Forms a protective oxide layer quickly; dross (oxide) is floating and skimmable
- Strength: Pure aluminum is weak; alloys (aluminum-copper, aluminum-zinc) are much stronger
- Corrosion resistance: Excellent; forms stable oxide that protects interior

:::tip
**Aluminum from Scrap:** A single aluminum can weighs ~15 grams. Melting 40-50 cans provides enough aluminum for a small tool or casting. Separate aluminum from steel (test with magnet) before melting.
:::

## Equipment Setup

Non-ferrous metals require furnaces capable of reaching 700-1200°C. The good news: they melt at lower temperatures than iron, making smaller furnaces viable.

### Furnace Design for Non-Ferrous Work

**Shaft Furnace (Recommended for Smelting):**
- Height: 6-8 feet tall, tapered
- Diameter: 18-24 inches inside
- Material: Stone or clay brick; lined with refractory clay
- Tuyere (air inlet): 2 inches diameter, 45° angled entry
- Tap hole: 1 inch diameter, 12 inches from bottom
- Fuel: Charcoal (hardwood preferred) or coke

**Crucible Furnace (For Alloying and Refining):**
- Height: 2-3 feet
- Opening: Top-loading, 12-18 inches diameter
- Material: Clay brick, iron frame
- Fuel: Charcoal or coke in surrounding chamber
- Purpose: Melting and holding non-ferrous metals, not ore smelting

### Temperature Measurement and Control

Without modern instruments, controlling temperature requires observation:

:::info-box
**Color-Based Temperature Guide:**
- 327°C (lead melting): Dull red
- 400°C: Dull cherry red
- 600°C: Bright cherry red
- 700°C: Orange-red
- 800°C: Yellow-orange
- 1000°C: Yellow-white
- 1085°C (copper melting): Bright yellow-white

Experience with repeated heating teaches recognition. Always have backup methods (test pieces, thermocouples if available).
:::

### Bellows and Air Supply

Continuous air supply is essential for reaching and maintaining temperature.

**Two-person bellows:** One person pumps while the other manages the smelt or fire. Bellows material: leather (treated hide), canvas, or wood with seals. Tuyere (air pipe): 1-2 inches diameter, angled to direct air into the furnace heart.

**Pumping rate:** Start slow (3-5 strokes/minute) to light the furnace. Increase to rapid (20-30 strokes/minute) once fuel is burning well. Continuous air maintains 1000-1200°C furnace temperatures.

### Heating Rate and Equilibration

Metal must be heated slowly to avoid cracking. Rapid heating causes:
- Uneven expansion, leading to thermal stress
- Dross formation (oxidation) that contaminates the melt
- Warping in thin-walled crucibles

**Best Practice:** Heat the furnace to target temperature first. THEN place the crucible with metal inside. Allow 10-20 minutes for the metal to equilibrate (reach furnace temperature) before pouring.

:::warning
**Overheating Copper:** Copper heated above 1200°C becomes very fluid, absorbs oxygen, and becomes brittle (oxygen embrittlement). Keep copper at 1150-1190°C. When molten copper reaches optimal temperature, it flows smoothly and pours cleanly.
:::

### Lead Temperature Control

Lead is dangerous when overheated. At 500°C, lead begins oxidizing at the surface, producing toxic fumes.

:::danger
**ZINC FUME FEVER:** Zinc heated above 600°C produces zinc oxide fumes. Inhalation causes "metal fume fever"—flu-like symptoms (chills, fever, muscle aches) onset 4-12 hours after exposure. Exposure is NOT necessarily fatal but is dangerous. ALWAYS work outdoors or in excellent ventilation when working with zinc or brass above 700°C.
:::

**Safe Lead Pouring Procedure:**
1. Heat lead in crucible to 350-400°C (glowing dull red)
2. Skim oxide layer with a stick or ladle
3. Pour immediately—do not hold at temperature
4. Cool molds completely (1-2 hours) before removing castings
5. Work entirely outdoors with downwind position

## Core Techniques: Smelting, Alloying, and Casting

### Copper Smelting Procedure (Step-by-Step)

**Step 1: Ore Preparation (Time: 1-2 hours)**
- Crush ore to powder (finer = better). Pound malachite, azurite, or chalcopyrite with a stone hammer until powder.
- Screen through cloth or sieve to remove large rock fragments.
- Collect powder; discard coarse waste.

**Step 2: Roasting (Time: 2-3 hours)**
- Spread ore powder in a clay-lined pit or kiln.
- Heat to 500-700°C for 2-3 hours. Ore changes from green (malachite) to darker red-brown.
- Roasting removes water and sulfur compounds, improving subsequent smelting.
- Cool roasted ore; it is now friable (crumbles easily).

**Step 3: Furnace Loading (Time: 30 minutes prep)**
- Ensure furnace is hot (800°C minimum).
- Create layers: 3 inches charcoal, 1 inch roasted ore, repeat.
- Target ratio: 3:1 charcoal to ore by volume.
- Continue layering until furnace is 2/3 full.

**Step 4: Smelting (Time: 2-3 hours active)**
- Begin bellows pumping (20-30 strokes/minute).
- Furnace temperature climbs to 1000-1150°C over 30-45 minutes.
- Maintain steady air supply. Copper begins dripping into the furnace bottom within 1-2 hours.
- Continue for 2-3 hours total smelting time.

**Step 5: Copper Collection and Cooling (Time: 1 hour)**
- Stop bellows; furnace cools slightly.
- Remove clay plug at tap hole (positioned 12 inches from bottom).
- Direct molten copper into an iron bowl or basin (pre-heated, placed on stone or brick).
- Copper solidifies within minutes.
- Allow 30+ minutes before handling.

**Yield:** 100 pounds of ore at 40-60% copper content yields roughly 40-60 pounds of smelted copper (mixed with oxides). Refining through poling removes oxides.

### Bronze Production Procedure (Step-by-Step)

**Composition Target:** 90% copper, 10% tin (standard bronze for tools)

**Step 1: Copper Melting (Time: 45 minutes)**
- Place crushed copper in an iron crucible.
- Heat furnace to 1150°C.
- Place crucible in furnace.
- Copper melts within 20-30 minutes.
- Stir molten copper with a wooden stick to break up any oxides.

**Step 2: Tin Addition (Time: 15 minutes)**
- Tin melts at 232°C, much lower than copper. Adding tin quickly causes vaporization and loss.
- Weigh or estimate tin quantity (for 10 lbs copper, add 1.1 lbs tin).
- Add tin in small chunks (1-2 inch pieces), one at a time.
- Stir after each addition. Metal becomes brighter yellow as tin is incorporated.
- Wait 30 seconds between additions to allow full mixing.

**Step 3: Final Stirring and Pouring (Time: 10-15 minutes)**
- Stir thoroughly for 2-3 minutes. Properly alloyed bronze appears bright and fluid.
- Check temperature (bright yellow-orange, 1150°C range).
- Pour into pre-heated molds or ingot molds immediately.

:::tip
**Tin Salvage:** In post-industrial settings, salvaged solder (used in electronics) contains tin. Melting down old circuit boards or solder joints provides tin for bronze without mining ore.
:::

### Brass Production (from Salvage)

**Step 1: Collection (Scalable)**
- Gather brass items: fittings, shell casings, decorative goods, old plumbing.
- Separate ferrous from non-ferrous (magnet test).
- Clean off dirt and debris.

**Step 2: Melting and Refining (Time: 30-45 minutes)**
- Place brass scrap in an iron crucible.
- Heat to 950-1050°C. Brass melts at 910°C but should be heated above melting for proper fluidity.
- Skim gray-white oxide (dross) from surface with a stick.
- If zinc smell is strong (sharp, acrid odor), furnace is too hot—cool slightly or move crucible to cooler zone.

**Step 3: Alloying (Optional)**
- To reduce zinc loss, add copper (melting point 1085°C) to lower melt point slightly.
- For every 3 lbs brass, add 0.5 lbs copper to shift ratio toward copper (improved corrosion resistance, stronger).
- Stir and re-skim dross.

**Step 4: Casting (Time: 10-15 minutes)**
- Pour into molds at 950-1000°C.
- Avoid prolonged heating above 1000°C (zinc loss increases).

### Soldering vs Brazing (Joining Metals)

Both techniques join two metal pieces, but using different filler materials and heat.

**Soldering (soft solder, low temperature):**
- Filler: Lead-tin solder (melts at 180-200°C)
- Joint: Filler melts and flows into gap; forms weak mechanical joint
- Strength: Adequate for joints not under stress; good for waterproofing
- Heat source: Soldering iron (~350°C) or hot copper tool
- Process: Apply flux (rosin or acid); heat joint; apply solder; solder flows by capillary action
- Best for: Electrical connections, sheet metal seams, plumbing
- Advantage: Low temperature means less risk to surrounding material; reversible if needed

**Brazing (hard solder, high temperature):**
- Filler: Brass or copper alloy (melts at 700-900°C)
- Joint: Filler flows and metallurgically bonds with base metals; forms strong joint
- Strength: Excellent; joint approaches base metal strength
- Heat source: Furnace, torch, or hot charcoal fire
- Process: Clean surfaces; apply flux (borax); heat to red heat; apply filler rod; flows and solidifies
- Best for: Joining dissimilar metals; high-strength joints; tool handles
- Advantage: Very strong; can join materials that cannot be easily welded

**Choosing between soldering and brazing:**

| Factor | Soldering | Brazing |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| Strength needed | Low (decorative, electrical) | High (structural, mechanical) |
| Joint stress | Minimal | Moderate to high |
| Temperature tolerance | Low (soft solder fails above 150°C) | High (joint remains strong at 300°C+) |
| Equipment needed | Soldering iron (simple) | Furnace or torch (complex) |
| Time to learn | Fast (days) | Moderate (weeks) |
| Cost | Very low | Low to moderate |

**Flux purpose:** Flux (rosin or borax powder) prevents oxidation during heating, allows solder/braze material to flow smoothly, and chemically cleans the joint surface. Always use flux—joints without flux are weak and unreliable.

### Precious Metal Recovery (if available from salvage)

In post-industrial settings, gold, silver, and platinum-group metals may be salvageable from electronics, jewelry, and industrial waste.

**Gold recovery:**
- Found in: Circuit board connectors, old jewelry, dental work
- Recovery method: Chemical dissolution (if nitric acid available) or electrolytic refining
- Melting point: 1064°C (accessible in good furnace)
- Value: High; worth effort even in small quantities
- Hazards: Acid dissolution produces toxic fumes; requires strong ventilation

**Silver recovery:**
- Found in: Old silverware, photographic film (silver halide), mirror backing, electronics
- Recovery method: Chemical dissolution (nitric acid); electrolytic refining; or simple melting if pure
- Melting point: 962°C (accessible)
- Value: Moderate; useful for currency/barter
- Advantage: Silver is easy to melt and cast; small quantities are usable

**Platinum recovery:**
- Found in: Catalytic converters (automotive), lab equipment, high-end jewelry
- Recovery method: Aqua regia dissolution (nitric + hydrochloric acid); extremely hazardous
- Melting point: 1768°C (very difficult to achieve; requires exceptional furnace)
- Value: Extremely high
- **WARNING:** Platinum recovery is dangerous and requires advanced chemistry. Only attempt if you have formal training and proper safety equipment.

**Simple precious metal recovery for survivors:**

Without advanced chemistry, recover precious metals through collection and trade:

1. Gather old electronics, jewelry, dental waste
2. Sort by type (gold items together, silver together)
3. Melt in crucible (if feasible for low-melting items like solder or soft metals)
4. Cast into ingots as crude currency/barter
5. Trade with specialists who have capability to refine further

## Tool Making from Non-Ferrous Metals

Non-ferrous metals are ideal for specific tools where corrosion resistance, electrical properties, or low weight are priorities.

### Copper and Bronze Tool Applications

**Cutting Tools:** Bronze is harder than pure copper and takes a good edge, though not as hard as iron. Bronze tools work for:
- Woodworking (chisels, saw blades) — adequate sharpness
- Bone and soft material working — excellent
- Leather cutting — effective
- NOT suitable for heavy stone work (copper and bronze bend rather than cut cleanly)

**Process for Cast Chisel (8-10 hour total project):**
1. Carve a wooden pattern for the chisel shape (2 inches wide, 6 inches long, 1-inch handle).
2. Create a clay mold around the pattern, allow to dry (2-3 hours in sun or near fire).
3. Heat mold to 200°C to remove moisture and harden it.
4. Melt 2-3 lbs of bronze in crucible (1150°C).
5. Pour into mold cavity carefully.
6. Cool completely (4+ hours).
7. Knock off mold; chisel is rough but functional.
8. Hammer and file chisel edge to desired sharpness over 1-2 hours of work.
9. Harden edge by heating to bright cherry red (700°C) and quenching in water.
10. Anneal slightly (heat to 500°C, cool slowly) to prevent brittleness.

:::warning
**Thermal Shock:** Plunging hot bronze into cold water creates stress. Quench quickly (edge hardens) but allow the rest to cool more slowly. A partial quench (edge only) minimizes cracking risk.
:::

### Wire Drawing

Bronze and copper can be drawn into wire—useful for electrical, binding, and decorative purposes.

**Simple Wire Drawing (Manual Process):**
1. Melt and cast copper into a thin rod (1/4 inch diameter).
2. Anneal the rod (heat to dull red, cool slowly).
3. Hammer the rod to ~3/16 inch diameter and sharp taper at one end.
4. File a hole (or drill, if possible) in a hard steel or stone die—hole diameter slightly smaller than desired wire.
5. Force the tapered rod through the hole by pulling hard with tongs or rope.
6. As the rod narrows and hardens, anneal again.
7. Repeat passes through progressively smaller holes to achieve fine wire.
8. Result: usable wire for binding, electrical connections, or jewelry.

**Yield:** One 1/4-inch-diameter rod roughly 12 inches long yields ~30-40 feet of 1/16-inch wire after multiple drawing passes.

### Casting Decorative and Functional Items

**Lost-Wax Casting Process (Time: 6-8 hours):**
1. Carve or model desired shape in beeswax (or can also use tallow candle if beeswax unavailable).
2. Create a clay mold around the wax, layer by layer (1/2-inch clay shell minimum).
3. Allow mold to air dry 2-3 days or heat near fire to harden thoroughly.
4. Heat mold slowly to 200-300°C (in fireplace, kiln, or careful furnace proximity). Wax melts and drains away, leaving exact cavity.
5. Preheat the empty mold to 400°C to ensure no moisture and improve metal flow.
6. Heat metal to pouring temperature (1150°C for bronze, 1100°C for copper).
7. Pour metal into mold cavity quickly and carefully.
8. Allow to cool 2-3 hours before breaking mold away.
9. Result: detailed bronze or copper casting with excellent surface finish.

:::tip
**Pattern Material:** Any wax works—beeswax, tallow from rendered animal fat, or salvaged paraffin from old candles. Each has slightly different melting characteristics but all work for lost-wax casting.
:::

**Annealing Procedure:**
1. Heat the metal slowly to target temperature (30-45 minutes for thick pieces).
2. Hold at temperature for 5-15 minutes (longer for larger pieces).
3. Cool as specified (slow or fast, depending on metal).
4. Metal is now soft and workable.

### Work Hardening

Repeated hammering hardens metal. This is useful for creating strong edges and maintaining tool hardness without heat.

**Work Hardening Cycle:** Hammer → Metal hardens → Anneal → Hammer again. This cycle repeats throughout a project.

**Typical Project:** Hammering a copper sheet into a vessel takes 5-8 annealing cycles (heat the sheet, cool, hammer, repeat).

### Quenching and Tempering

:::warning
**Bronze Quenching Risk:** Quenching bronze (plunging hot bronze into water) hardens it slightly but can cause cracking if done carelessly. Water quenching is faster than air cooling but increases brittleness risk. For important tools, anneal after quenching to restore ductility.
:::

**Tempering (Color Tempering for Tools):**
After hardening bronze or brass by quenching, heating to moderate temperature reduces brittleness:
- 200°C (pale yellow oxide): Hard, brittle
- 300°C (brown oxide): Good balance—suitable for cutting edges
- 400°C (blue oxide): Softer, more ductile—suitable for springs
- 500°C (dark blue): Soft, flexible

**Visual Tempering Process:**
1. Harden the tool by heating to cherry red (700°C) and quenching in water.
2. Wipe clean with cloth to observe oxide color.
3. Heat the hardened tool slowly (allowing oxide color to progress).
4. When the desired color appears, quench in water.
5. Result: balanced hardness and ductility.

## Quality Testing and Alloy Verification

Ensuring metal purity and alloy composition prevents equipment failure and wasted resources.

### Purity Testing

**Magnet Test (Non-Ferrous Verification):**
- Place the metal near a strong magnet.
- Non-ferrous metals (copper, brass, aluminum, lead) are NOT magnetic.
- If metal is attracted, it contains iron contamination or is ferrous.

**Color Test:**
- Copper: Bright orange-red
- Bronze: Darker red or brown-red
- Brass: Yellow to golden
- Lead: Gray, dull
- Aluminum: Silver-gray, light
- If color doesn't match expected, composition is suspect.

**Density Test (Rough Check):**
- Hold two pieces of equal size.
- Lead is dramatically heavier than copper (lead is 11.3 g/cm³, copper is 8.96 g/cm³).
- Aluminum is obviously lighter (2.7 g/cm³).
- This quick test identifies major contamination.

### Tin Content Verification in Bronze

**Hardness Test (Comparative):**
Pure copper dents easily with a hammer. Bronze (8-12% tin) resists denting. Very hard bronze (15%+ tin) is brittle.
- Carve a small mark with a bronze cutting tool into test samples.
- Bronze tool carves copper easily but bounces off hardened steel → confirms bronze is harder than pure copper.

**Melt Appearance Test:**
- Melt a small sample of the bronze.
- Proper bronze (8-12% tin) appears bright yellow and flows smoothly.
- Pure copper appears more orange and is less fluid.
- Over-tinned bronze (20%+) is grainy and flows poorly.

### Zinc Content in Brass

**Color-Based Assessment:**
- Yellow brass (65-90% Cu, 10-35% Zn): Bright yellow color
- Red brass (80-90% Cu, 10-20% Zn): Reddish-yellow or salmon color
- Higher copper = more red tone
- If color is very yellow and bright, zinc content is likely 25%+

**Oxidation Rate Test:**
- Brass oxidizes faster than pure copper in air.
- Observe how quickly tarnish forms (24-48 hours).
- Heavy tarnish suggests high copper content (lower zinc).

### Lead Contamination Testing

:::danger
**Lead Detection:** Lead is highly toxic. If you suspect lead contamination in copper or bronze, DO NOT WORK WITH IT. Lead contamination occurs when:
- Solder was added to copper (solder contains lead)
- Old lead pipes were melted with copper
- Salvaged plumbing solder was mixed with non-ferrous metals

Salvage copper from automotive radiators or industrial equipment only if you're certain it hasn't contacted lead.
:::

**Simple Test for Lead in Copper:**
1. Melt a small sample of the copper.
2. Pour into a flat mold.
3. Once cool, file the surface.
4. If white particles or layers appear under the surface, lead is present.
5. Discard contaminated metal immediately—do not process further.

## Troubleshooting: Casting Defects and Failures

### Porosity (Holes in Casting)

**Symptom:** Finished casting has visible holes or weak spots.

**Causes:**
- Gas trapped during pouring (air bubbles in mold)
- Moisture in mold (water vaporizing)
- Oxide layer on molten metal folded into casting
- Metal poured too cold (didn't flow completely)

**Solutions:**
1. **Vibrate the mold:** Tap the mold sides gently during pouring to dislodge air bubbles.
2. **Heat molds thoroughly:** Preheat molds to 200-400°C (dry them completely, especially clay).
3. **Skim dross before pouring:** Remove oxide layer with a stick.
4. **Pour hotter:** Ensure metal is fully fluid at pouring temperature (1150-1200°C for bronze).
5. **Use vents:** Drill small holes (1/8 inch) in mold walls to allow air/gas to escape.

### Incomplete Casting (Metal Doesn't Fill Mold)

**Symptom:** Casting is smaller than mold or only partially filled.

**Causes:**
- Metal cooled before reaching all parts of mold
- Mold is too cold
- Metal poured from too low (lacks pressure to flow)
- Mold cavity too complex or narrow

**Solutions:**
1. **Preheat molds:** Heat to 400-500°C before pouring.
2. **Pour from height:** Pour from 12-18 inches above the mold to maintain pressure.
3. **Use hotter metal:** Increase metal temperature by 50-100°C.
4. **Simplify mold design:** Avoid very thin sections or narrow passages.
5. **Use runners and gates:** Create larger channels in the mold to funnel metal to all areas.

:::tip
**Runner System:** In complex molds, create a main channel (runner) that distributes metal to subsidiary channels (gates) leading to each mold cavity. This ensures all areas fill before metal cools.
:::

### Cracking and Brittleness

**Symptom:** Casting cracks during cooling or is brittle and breaks easily.

**Causes:**
- Thermal shock (too-rapid cooling)
- Over-quenched (cooled too fast after heat treatment)
- Alloy composition off (too much tin, making bronze brittle)
- Contamination with harder metals

**Solutions:**
1. **Allow slow cooling:** Don't remove casting from mold immediately; allow 2-4 hours minimum.
2. **Controlled quenching:** Quench only the edge (for tools), not the entire casting.
3. **Anneal after quenching:** Heat to 300-400°C and cool slowly to restore ductility.
4. **Verify alloy ratio:** If recurring issue, test tin or zinc content.
5. **Check for contamination:** Use magnet test and density assessment.

### Alloy Segregation (Uneven Metal)

**Symptom:** Different parts of the casting have different properties (some areas hard, some soft).

**Causes:**
- Insufficient stirring during alloying
- Metal cooled too quickly (tin or zinc settled unevenly)
- Mold was too cool (outer areas cooled differently than interior)

**Solutions:**
1. **Stir thoroughly:** Stir molten alloy for 2-3 minutes before pouring.
2. **Heat molds evenly:** Ensure entire mold is uniformly hot.
3. **Pour faster:** Quick filling reduces time for segregation.
4. **Reheat and recast:** If segregation is severe, remelt and cast again with better technique.

### Oxidation and Dross Formation

**Symptom:** Casting surface is rough, dull, or covered with gray oxide layer; interior areas weak.

**Causes:**
- Oxide layer not skimmed before pouring
- Metal exposed to air too long
- Furnace not hot enough (metal cooled, oxidized)

**Solutions:**
1. **Skim oxide before pouring:** Use a stick or ladle to remove gray-white dross from molten metal.
2. **Work quickly:** Minimize time metal is molten—prepare molds in advance.
3. **Hotter furnace:** Ensure furnace is at target temperature before pouring.
4. **Use flux:** For copper, add a small amount of borax to the melt; it floats oxide to the surface for easier removal.

## Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

### Overheating Copper (Oxygen Embrittlement)

**Mistake:** Heating copper above 1200°C for extended periods.

**Result:** Copper becomes brittle and shatters when worked. The metal absorbs oxygen at high temperature, making it fragile.

**Avoidance:** Keep copper at 1150-1190°C. Once molten and fluid, pour immediately—don't hold at high temperature.

### Adding Tin Too Quickly in Bronze Smelting

**Mistake:** Throwing tin into molten copper all at once.

**Result:** Tin vaporizes instantly (boiling point 2602°C, but tin is lost from the bath at high temperatures before it fully dissolves). Significant tin loss means alloy is under-alloyed (not hard enough). You've wasted material.

**Avoidance:** Add tin in small chunks (1-2 inch pieces), waiting 30 seconds between additions. Stir after each addition. This ensures complete mixing before the next addition.

### Contaminating Lead with Copper or Tin

**Mistake:** Melting lead and copper together in the same crucible.

**Result:** The resulting alloy is unpredictable—neither lead's softness nor copper's workability. Lead-copper alloys are difficult to work and may be brittle.

**Avoidance:** Use separate crucibles for lead. Wash tools and crucibles thoroughly between lead and copper work to avoid mixing.

### Quenching Aluminum After Annealing

**Mistake:** Annealing aluminum (heating to 300-400°C) and then quenching in water.

**Result:** Aluminum becomes WEAKER, not stronger. Rapid cooling causes unfavorable grain structure. Pure aluminum is weakened by quenching; only special aluminum alloys benefit from quenching.

**Avoidance:** Always air-cool aluminum after annealing. Never quench pure aluminum.

### Allowing Brass to Overheat (Zinc Loss)

**Mistake:** Heating brass above 1000°C or holding at high temperature.

**Result:** Zinc vaporizes, leaving copper-rich alloy that is more expensive (zinc cost less than copper). The alloy becomes less resistant to corrosion. Acrid smell indicates zinc loss in progress.

**Avoidance:** Keep brass at 950-1000°C maximum. Pour as soon as molten. If you smell sharp zinc odor, the furnace is too hot—reduce heat immediately.

### Melting Lead Indoors

**Mistake:** Melting lead in a closed space or indoors without ventilation.

**Result:** Lead oxide fumes accumulate; inhalation causes acute poisoning. Even brief exposure can cause neurological effects.

**Avoidance:** ALWAYS melt lead outdoors. Ensure you're upwind of the smoke. Wear a respirator rated for lead particulate. Never melt lead indoors under any circumstances.

:::danger
**NO EXCEPTIONS FOR LEAD:** Lead fumes are not negotiable. Outdoor work with strong ventilation is mandatory.
:::

### Mixing Salvage Metals Without Testing

**Mistake:** Assuming all scrap brass is pure brass, or that all copper is uncontaminated.

**Result:** Casting fails because the alloy composition is wrong. Contamination with lead, iron, or other metals ruins the entire batch.

**Avoidance:** Test salvage metal before melting. Magnet test (ferrous?), weight test (density), and melt a small sample to verify behavior. Scrap from plumbing often contains lead solder—never use it.

### Insufficient Bellows Pressure

**Mistake:** Underpowering the furnace with weak or inconsistent bellows pumping.

**Result:** Furnace can't reach smelting temperature. Ore doesn't smelt, copper doesn't fully melt, alloying fails.

**Avoidance:** Use two-person bellows operation (one person pumps, one person manages the smelt). Maintain rapid, steady pumping (20-30 strokes/minute). Test furnace temperature by observing metal color before proceeding.

### Inadequate Mold Preheating

**Mistake:** Pouring metal into room-temperature molds.

**Result:** Metal chills too fast, incomplete filling, casting is cold-short (weak). Surface layers cool so fast they trap air, creating porosity.

**Avoidance:** Preheat molds to 300-500°C before pouring. Place molds near the furnace while metal is heating. Tap molds to dislodge trapped air before pouring.

## Safety Critical Information

### Lead and Zinc Toxicity

Lead is among the most toxic common metals. Chronic exposure causes irreversible neurological damage, especially in children.

:::danger
**LEAD HAZARDS:**
- Lead dust is readily inhaled and absorbed through the lungs
- Lead oxide fumes (produced above 500°C) are acutely toxic
- Ingestion (touching mouth after lead work) accumulates in bones, causing long-term poisoning
- Pregnant women should not work with lead (fetal damage risk)
- Children exposed to lead suffer permanent IQ loss and behavioral problems
- No safe exposure level exists—minimize to absolute zero
- Lead remains toxic even after cooling

**MANDATORY LEAD SAFETY PROCEDURES:**
1. Work ONLY outdoors (never indoors)
2. Wear heavy-duty gloves, long sleeves, and closed-toe shoes
3. Wear a respirator rated for lead oxide particles (P100 minimum)
4. Wash hands and face thoroughly before eating, drinking, or touching face
5. Change clothes after lead work; wash separately from household laundry
6. Never melt lead above 400°C (stay well below 500°C limit)
7. Have good ventilation (work in open air, away from living spaces)
8. Keep children and pregnant women away from lead work areas
:::

### Zinc Fume Fever

:::danger
**ZINC FUME FEVER (Metal Fume Fever):**
- Occurs 4-12 hours after breathing zinc oxide fumes
- Symptoms: chills, fever, muscle aches, nausea, dry throat (flu-like)
- Typically lasts 24-48 hours (no permanent damage, but extremely unpleasant)
- Some individuals suffer repeated attacks with lower exposure on subsequent occasions

**PREVENTION:**
- Heat zinc/brass only outdoors
- Ensure excellent ventilation (strong breeze, work upwind)
- Wear a respirator (P100 or higher) when heating brass above 700°C
- Limit heating time at high temperature
- If you smell acrid zinc odor, move away immediately and stop work
:::

### Burn Hazards

Molten metal at 1000-1200°C causes severe burns instantly. Even small splashes cause deep tissue damage.

**Burn Prevention:**
- Wear fire-resistant clothing (leather, heavy cotton, NOT synthetics)
- Use long-handled tongs for all hot work—never touch hot metal directly
- Wear heavy work gloves (leather lined with asbestos or ceramic fiber, if available)
- Keep water nearby but DO NOT use on molten metal fires (water causes violent steam explosions)
- For molten metal fire: Use sand or dry chemical extinguisher, not water
- Work area should be clear of trip hazards; burns occur when workers fall into hot materials

### Furnace and Fire Safety

**Furnace Stability:** Ensure furnace walls are solid and stable. A collapsing furnace spills molten metal and causes severe burns.

**Fuel Safety:** Charcoal produces carbon monoxide. Ensure all furnace work is done outdoors where CO can dissipate. Never operate furnace in enclosed spaces.

**Heat Exposure:** Working near a 1200°C furnace for extended periods causes heat exhaustion. Take breaks in shade, drink water frequently, and monitor for dizziness or confusion.

### Respiratory Protection

**Dust from Ore Processing:** Crushing copper or lead ore generates fine dust. Inhaling ore dust can cause poisoning (lead) or lung irritation.

**Flux Fumes:** Rosin flux and borax produce harmless but irritating fumes. Work with ventilation.

**Smelting Fumes:** Copper smelting produces some harmful fumes. Work outdoors always.

:::info-box
**Respirator Selection:** For lead work, use P100-rated respirators (HEPA filters). For general furnace work, N95 or P100 masks are adequate if work is outdoors. Fit-test respirators before use to ensure proper seal.
:::
