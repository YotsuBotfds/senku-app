## Historical Context

Restorative dentistry emerged as a formal discipline in the 18th-19th centuries. Pre-industrial societies used mechanical excavation, herbal preparations, and heat-based treatments. Early permanent restorations relied on lead, gold, and amalgam compounds. The advent of anesthesia (1846) revolutionized dental treatment, shifting from emergency extraction-focused approaches to planned restoration. Field dentistry in military/expedition contexts has evolved sophisticated techniques adaptable to resource-limited settings. Modern survival dentistry borrows from historical methods while incorporating contemporary understanding of infection control and material science.

During wartime and humanitarian crises, dentists adapted procedures for field conditions: simplified instrumentation, portable anesthetics, rapid assessment protocols. Knowledge of these adaptations proves invaluable for off-grid or disaster-scenario dental care. The Hippocratic corpus mentions dental treatment; medieval barber-surgeons performed extraction; 18th-century surgeons developed the philosophies of cavity preparation still taught today. Understanding this evolution contextualizes why certain techniques remain effective despite technological simplification.

<section id="sterile-technique">

## Sterile Technique Prerequisites

Restorative dentistry fails catastrophically without infection control. Infected restorations lead to abscesses, bone loss, systemic infection, and tooth extraction. Prevention is infinitely preferable to treatment.

### Instrument Sterilization Methods

Survival dentistry requires effective sterilization without autoclave (high-pressure steam sterilizer). Multiple methods provide adequate sterilization.

**Boiling Method (Most Practical):**

- Boil instruments in water 20 minutes minimum (kills vegetative bacteria; spore killing requires 30+ minutes)
- Works for: forceps, elevators, scalers, mirrors, hand instruments
- Does NOT work for: burs (heat damages cutting edges), high-speed handpieces
- Process: Place instruments in stainless steel pot, cover with water, bring to rolling boil, maintain boil for minimum time, remove with tongs to sterile cloth for drying
- Store: Keep boiled instruments on clean cloth until use; re-sterilize if touched or dropped

**Pressure Cooker Method:**

- 15 PSI pressure, 15 minutes sterilizes most instruments
- Faster than boiling; effective for spore-forming bacteria
- Same instruments suitable as boiling method
- Allow pressure cooker to cool completely before opening (risk of steam burn otherwise)

**Chemical Sterilization (Formalin/Glutaraldehyde):**

- High-level disinfection for instruments that cannot tolerate heat
- Soak instruments 20-30 minutes in 2% glutaraldehyde or formaldehyde solution
- Effective against all pathogens including spores
- Requires ventilation (toxic fumes)
- Instruments still need physical cleaning before chemical soak (remove blood, debris first)
- Rinse thoroughly with sterile water after chemical soak

**Field-Expedient Sterilization:**

- If no boiling water available: Alcohol (70% isopropyl) or flame sterilization (pass instrument through open flame until visibly hot)
- Alcohol: kills most bacteria and viruses, but NOT spores. Use only for high-level disinfection, not true sterilization
- Flame: effective but risks damaging instrument temper (dulls cutting edges). Use only for sharp instruments that can tolerate extreme temperature change

### Clean Workspace Setup

Restorative dentistry creates multiple infection routes. Workspace design prevents cross-contamination.

**Patient Treatment Area:**

- Designated clean space (table, chair, or tarp on ground in field conditions)
- Patient's head positioned above heart level if possible (reduces bleeding)
- Direct lighting on surgical area (headlamp if electricity unavailable)
- Instruments arranged in logical sequence, within arm's reach
- Sterile drape or cloth covering patient's chest and shoulders (prevents contamination from saliva splash, instruments, or debris)

**Instrument Arrangement:**

- Sterile field: clean cloth or surface with instruments arranged in order of use
- Primary instruments: scaler, excavator, bur holder (or chisel), condensing instruments, finishing tools
- Secondary instruments: suction/drying, mirrors, forceps for tie-down
- Sharps container: designated area for used/contaminated instruments (do not allow contact with clean instruments)

**Waste Disposal:**

- Designated container for contaminated materials (gauze, saliva, blood)
- Sharps container for needles and broken bur tips (prevents needle-stick injuries)
- Do not place contaminated materials on clean surfaces
- Dispose of sharps in sealed, marked container; bury deep (1 meter+) or burn if safe

### Patient Preparation and Draping

**Pre-operative mouth rinse:**

- Patient rinses mouth thoroughly with antiseptic solution (1:1000 dilute chlorhexidine or dilute iodine solution) or salt water
- Reduces oral bacterial load by 30-50%
- Takes 30-60 seconds; gargle and swish vigorously

**Field disinfection of tooth:**

- Apply antiseptic solution to tooth and surrounding gum area with cotton swab
- Allow 30-60 seconds contact time (antimicrobial agents work best with contact time)
- Dry with sterile gauze

**Isolation and drying:**

- Isolate tooth with rubber dam (best method; prevents saliva contamination and retracts tissues)
- Without rubber dam: place gauze/cotton rolls to absorb saliva; have assistant use manual suction or gravity (tilt patient's head to drain saliva to side of mouth)
- Dry tooth surface completely before beginning restoration (wet enamel/dentin prevents proper restoration adherence)

### Infection Control Protocols

**Personnel protection:**

- Wear gloves (latex or nitrile; change between patients or if torn)
- Wear face mask (protects against blood splash and respiratory aerosols)
- Wear eye protection (goggles or face shield prevent fluid splash into eyes)
- Wear gown or apron (protects clothing from blood and debris)

**Hand hygiene:**

- Wash hands thoroughly before and after patient treatment (soap and water minimum 20 seconds)
- Wear sterile gloves during all procedures
- Remove gloves carefully without touching outer surface (avoid self-contamination)

**Patient communication about infection risk:**

- Explain that infection is the primary serious complication
- Symptoms to watch for: fever, swelling, pus discharge, worsening pain after treatment (patient must report these)
- Post-treatment care: avoid chewing on restoration for 24 hours, maintain oral hygiene, report any discomfort

<section id="tooth-anatomy">

## Tooth Anatomy Fundamentals

Mastering restorative dentistry demands complete understanding of tooth structure. Each element serves a functional and mechanical purpose in restoration success.

![Tooth anatomy cross-section showing enamel, dentin, pulp, root canal, periodontal ligament, and bone](../assets/svgs/dentistry-restorative-1.svg)

### Enamel

Hardest substance in human body; crystalline hydroxyapatite mineral structure. Approximately 96% mineral, 4% organic matrix and water. Non-living (no repair capability; damage permanent). Thickness: 1-2mm on crown surface. Functions: protection, aesthetic appearance, acid resistance (within limits). Etching with phosphoric acid disrupts crystal structure, enabling adhesive bonding. Restoration margins on enamel provide better seal than margins on dentin or cementum.

### Dentin

Beneath enamel; comprises bulk of tooth. 70% mineral, 30% organic/water. Contains microscopic tubules extending from pulp to enamel-dentin junction (EDJ). Tubules conduct thermal, osmotic, and sensory stimuli to pulp (explains postoperative sensitivity). Exposed dentin sensitive to temperature, air, sweet substances. Dentin formation continues slowly throughout life (secondary dentin deposition). Darker than enamel; yellow-brown color. More porous than enamel; absorbs stain/bacteria if exposed. Dentin thickness typically 1.5-2mm at cavity floor.

### Pulp Chamber

Central cavity containing pulp tissue: blood vessels, nerves, connective tissue. Vital pulp: living, responds to inflammation, can recover if insult minimal. Necrotic pulp: dead, black/darkened appearance, source of infection if sealed/untreated. Pulp recession in aging teeth (secondary dentin formation) reduces pulp chamber size. Depth from surface varies: <1mm in anterior teeth, up to 1.5mm in molars. Exposure during cavity preparation requires immediate management: temporary seal (protect), monitor for abscess, plan extraction/endodontic treatment.

### Root Canal System

Extends from pulp chamber through root to apical foramen (opening at root tip). Main canal plus lateral branches. Anatomy complex and variable; anterior teeth typically single canal, molars multiple. Apical foramen small (0.3-0.7mm). In extraction, retention of apical fragment (separated root tip) risks chronic infection/periapical pathology. In survival scenarios, extraction completeness paramount; retrieve all root pieces when possible.

### Periodontal Ligament & Supporting Bone

PDL: connective tissue fibers anchoring tooth to alveolar bone. Shock-absorbing, proprioceptive (patient perceives bite force). Bone: houses tooth socket (alveolus). Periodontal disease (gingivitis, periodontitis) destroys PDL and bone, eventually loosening/extracting tooth. Restorations on teeth with poor periodontal support risk failure; assess periodontal status before restoration investment.

<section id="cavity-classification">

## Cavity Classification Systems

G.V. Black's classification (1891) remains standard for describing caries locations and preparation shapes. Understanding classification enables consistent documentation, treatment planning, and communication among providers.

### Black's Classification

-   **Class I:** Pits, fissures, smooth surfaces on molars/premolars. Occlusal involvement; hidden from view. Typically straightforward preparation.
-   **Class II:** Proximal (contact point) involvement on molars/premolars. Cavitation between teeth. Preparation requires access, retentive form, visibility challenges. Restoration must prevent proximal overhang (irritates papilla).
-   **Class III:** Proximal involvement on anterior teeth. Similar to Class II but different tooth type (anterior considerations: aesthetic, smaller pulps, single root). Restoration visibility critical.
-   **Class IV:** Proximal involvement with incisal edge loss (anterior only). Restoration must be aesthetic and withstand mechanical stress of biting edge. Highest failure risk for anterior teeth.
-   **Class V:** Smooth surface at cervical third (gum line) on any tooth. Exposed root surface in periodontal disease; demineralization from acidic saliva/plaque. Shallow preparation; low pulp risk. Challenging retention (divergent tooth shape at cervical).
-   **Class VI:** Cusp/incisal edge involvement (loss/fracture). Restoration must withstand functional forces on masticatory surface.

<section id="cavity-treatment">

## Cavity Treatment Protocol

### Decay Removal

Identify cavity (dark discoloration, cavitation). Local anesthetic if available (2% lidocaine with 1:100,000 epinephrine, infiltrate at base of lesion). If no anesthetic: accept patient discomfort, work quickly. Decay removal: mechanical excavation using dental bur (if handpiece available), or manual with dental chisel/scraper. Remove all caries (undermined enamel, discolored dentin). Cavity should feel hard when fully cleaned (soft dentin = remaining caries). Infected dentin appears darker; brown/gray coloration indicates caries activity.

### Cavity Preparation Anatomy

-   **Outline form:** Define cavity boundaries; walls should diverge toward occlusal (mechanical retention). Angle ~6-8 degrees divergence on facial/lingual walls.
-   **Depth:** Typically 1.5-2mm deep; avoid pulp exposure (pulp chamber ~0.5-1.5mm from surface depending on tooth). Class V shallow (0.5-1mm) due to thin enamel/dentin at cervical.
-   **Walls:** Smooth, slightly divergent (helps filling retention). Avoid internal line angles (stress concentration); round slightly.
-   **Floor:** Concave shape (Class I/II) provides better retention; follows internal tooth anatomy.
-   **Line angles:** No sharp internal angles; slightly rounded to prevent restoration fracture. Line angle stress = common failure mode.
-   **Access:** Adequate access for placement/condensation of filling material. Tooth position, mouth opening, lighting considerations.

### Pulp Exposure Consequences

If drilling/excavation damages pulp (exposure visible as red bleeding spot): emergency treatment required. Options: temporary filling to seal (halt infection progression, allow pulp response), or extraction if infection already present/pulp necrotic. Some vital pulps recover with proper seal. Direct pulp capping with calcium hydroxide reported to promote healing in animal studies; human success rates variable. Document exposure; monitor for abscess formation. Extraction safer in survival scenarios unless endodontic treatment available. Pain intensity after exposure useful diagnostic: severe continuous pain = pulp inflammation/necrosis; moderate transient pain = pulp recovery possible.

## Filling Materials Comparison

![Dental material comparison chart showing properties of amalgam, glass ionomer, and zinc oxide eugenol](../assets/svgs/dentistry-restorative-2.svg)

### Amalgam (Silver Fillings)

Composition: mercury (45-50%) + silver + copper + tin alloy particles. Advantages: durable (20+ year lifespan), affordable, tolerate moisture well, excellent retention in prepared cavity, compressive strength ideal for posterior teeth, easy condensation technique, forgiving of minor moisture contamination. Disadvantages: not tooth-colored (aesthetic limitation anterior teeth), requires more tooth removal for mechanical retention, mercury concerns (generally considered safe but use caution with exposure; avoid skin contact/inhalation during mixing), expansion/contraction slightly with temperature extremes.

Preparation: mix powder alloy with liquid mercury according to ratio (typically 1:1 or 5:8 by weight), condense into cavity with appropriate plugger (serrated, smooth, or carver plugger depending on stage). Allow setting (5-30 minutes depending on formulation; some "fast-set" versions set in <5 minutes). Shine/burnish outer surface with carver to close margins. Complete set requires 24 hours; avoid chewing on filling for 12+ hours. Condensation pressure important: undercarved fills weak/porous; overcarved causes excess mercury release/expansion risk.

### Composite Resin

Plastic + resin material, tooth-colored (aesthetic advantage). Composition: bis-GMA resin matrix + filler particles (glass/ceramic) + initiator system (chemical or light-activated). Advantages: aesthetic (matches tooth color), bonds to enamel/dentin (less tooth removal needed for retention), no mercury, can be sculpted/shaped during placement, reparable (can add composite to failed restoration). Disadvantages: less durable (5-10 years typical), more technique-sensitive, moisture-sensitive during placement (saliva contamination = bond failure), polymerization shrinkage (stress at margins), requires light source (UV/LED; 30-60 second exposure per layer).

Placement procedure: etch enamel with phosphoric acid 37-40% for 15-20 seconds, rinse thoroughly, dry (enamel should appear chalky white); apply adhesive per product instructions (wets etched surface, primer + bonding resin); apply composite in incremental layers (2-3mm max per layer to ensure light penetration), light-cure each layer 30-60 seconds; final layer slightly convex for occlusion; shape/carve to anatomy; finish/polish margins. Difficult without proper equipment in field; light source essential. Contamination during placement dooms restoration.

:::warning
Composite placement is highly moisture-sensitive. Any saliva contamination during bonding = adhesive failure, restoration loss risk within weeks. Isolation CRITICAL: rubber dam ideal; if unavailable, cotton rolls + gauze + active suction + careful technique. If moisture contamination occurs during placement, remove material, re-isolate, dry completely, and reapply adhesive + composite. Do not proceed if adequate isolation impossible.
:::

### Zinc Oxide Eugenol (Intermediate Restorative Material, IRM)

Temporary material: powder (zinc oxide) + liquid (eugenol, aromatic essence of clove). Mix to putty consistency (powder added to liquid incrementally until stiff consistency achieved). Pack into cavity using suitable instrument; carves easily once set (~24 hours). Adequate strength for temporary 6-12 months; adequate for pain relief/infection sealing in emergency. Soluble in saliva (dissolves over time); survival lifespan 3-6 months. Does not bond (mechanical retention only). Irritant if in contact with pulp (eugenol cytotoxic); do not place directly on exposed pulp. In survival: adequate for temporary restoration when permanent materials unavailable. Limitations: not suitable for permanent restoration, stains over time, weak at lateral margins if not fully condensed.

### Glass Ionomer

Powder (alumina silicate glass) + liquid (polyacrylic acid). Mix per product instructions (specific ratios critical); placement window short (~2-3 minutes once mixed; paste becomes unworkable quickly). Sets chemically (no light needed, significant advantage in field/survival scenarios). Releases fluoride (cavity prevention, anti-inflammatory). Bonds to tooth structure via polyalkenoate formation. Adequate strength for temporary restoration, some permanent use in lower-stress areas (Class V, Class III anterior). Advantages: moisture-tolerant setting (unlike composite), fluoride release, chemical set (no light source needed), bonds to dentin.

Disadvantages: less durable than amalgam/composite (5-10 year lifespan at best; often shorter), technique-sensitive (moisture control during mixing/setting critical; delayed cement matrix development if saliva contamination), low flexural strength (fractures easily under sharp stress), poor color stability (may yellow). Placement: condition dentin with polyalkenoate/citric acid solution (improves bond), apply glass ionomer quickly, adapt to cavity walls, burnish surface, protect from moisture for 24 hours (preferably longer). Patient instructions: avoid chewing until completely set, avoid water contact for first hour.

## Temporary Restoration

### Indications for Temporary vs Permanent

**Temporary Indicated:** Survival scenario where permanent restoration delayed/impossible, tooth pain requiring seal (temporary relief pending extraction/definitive treatment), decay arrested but not ready for definitive treatment, pulp exposure requiring monitoring before extraction/endodontic decision, pre-extraction state (protect from further decay while planning extraction).

**Permanent Indicated:** If materials/expertise available, sufficient time for placement/setting, infection absent and controlled, patient cooperation expected (compliance with post-operative instructions), periodontal support adequate, tooth restorable with reasonable longevity expectation.

### Temporary Filling Placement

1.  Prepare cavity (remove decay, shape as described; depth/width appropriate for temporary material chosen)
2.  Isolate tooth thoroughly (cotton rolls, gauze to absorb saliva; critical for most materials except amalgam)
3.  Select material (zinc oxide eugenol most practical in field; glass ionomer if light unavailable but good seal needed)
4.  Mix material per product instructions (amalgam: triturate; zinc oxide eugenol: powder to liquid incrementally; glass ionomer: specific ratios, rapid mixing)
5.  Pack into cavity using condensation instrument; fill completely to level of adjacent tooth, slightly overfull for trimming
6.  Smooth surface with finger/instrument slightly proud to ease removal of excess
7.  Allow setting time per product (typically 5-30 minutes; check instructions; some rapid-set materials set in <3 minutes)
8.  Check bite: patient bites lightly; examine with articulating paper for high spots; adjust with carver if needed
9.  Provide patient instructions: avoid chewing on that tooth for 24 hours, report if sensitivity persists or filling loss occurs, avoid sticky/hard foods

### Temporary Filling Stability

Temporary materials inherently less durable than permanent; expected lifespan weeks to months depending on material and location. Posterior high-stress areas (molar cusps, contact areas) = shorter survival than anterior/smooth surfaces. Patient compliance critical: avoid sticky foods (taffy, caramel), hard chewing (nuts, ice), temperature extremes. Monitor for: filling loss (material fracture/debonding), recurring pain (microleakage, secondary decay, pulpal issues), margin breakdown (material degradation). Plan definitive treatment; temporary is bridge to definitive care, not solution. Document placement material and date for future provider reference.

<section id="extraction-prep">

## Pre-Extraction Preparation

### When Extraction Indicated

Severe decay unrepairable (extensive caries, insufficient tooth structure for retention), abscess present (periapical or periodontal), severe periodontal disease (mobility >Grade 2, bone loss >50%), extraction pre-planned before complications, patient preference in survival scenario (limited resources, extract rather than restore), root fragment retained from prior extraction, tooth fractured below gumline (extraction only option), severe pericoronitis (wisdom tooth partially erupted/impacted with infection).

Alternatives to consider: temporary restoration to delay extraction (if restoration possible, pain relief needed, definitive treatment planned), prophylactic extraction (pre-fracture, prevent future abscess), antibiotic therapy + temporary seal (if infection early, abscess not advanced). In survival scenarios, extraction may be only option if restoration impossible; prioritize infection control + anesthesia/pain management.

### Pre-Extraction Assessment

Clinical examination: tooth mobility (percussion test; Grade 1=<1mm, Grade 2=1-2mm, Grade 3=>2mm), gingival health (erythema, swelling, drainage), root morphology on palpation (anterior single root, posterior often multi-rooted), position/angulation (straight vs tilted), opposing dentition (whether extraction alters occlusion significantly). Patient history: systemic conditions (diabetes, immunosuppression = altered healing, infection risk), medications (anticoagulants = hemorrhage risk, steroids = delayed healing), allergies (anesthetic/antiseptic), smoking (delayed healing).

### Radiography (If Available)

Periapical X-ray shows: root morphology (curvature, length, resorption), bone level (assess periodontal support), presence of periapical pathology (condensing osteitis, radiolucency), root fragments/retained material. CBCT (cone-beam CT) if available shows: 3D root anatomy, inferior alveolar nerve proximity (posterior lower teeth; nerve damage risk if extractions attempted without assessment), bone density (affects extraction force needed), adjacent tooth relationship. In survival: radiography rarely available; proceed on clinical presentation, accept increased complication risk.

### Anesthesia Administration

Infiltration (anterior teeth, buccal aspect): inject at apical level of targeted tooth, deposit local anesthetic (2% lidocaine with 1:100,000 epinephrine typical; slow injection reduces discomfort). Onset 3-5 minutes. Effective for anterior teeth, some premolars.

Nerve block (posterior/difficult cases): target specific nerve (inferior alveolar, mental, buccal, long buccal, greater palatine depending on tooth). Inferior alveolar block (mandibular molars): enter soft palate at pterygomandibular raphe, advance needle to lingula (small bony protuberance on medial mandible), deposit anesthetic. Mandibular block achieved; anesthetizes mandible to midline. Maxillary block: greater palatine (palatal) + infiltration (buccal) + long buccal (distal) for complete anesthesia.

Without anesthesia: accept patient pain, work quickly, consider emergency sedation/pain relief (nitrous oxide, oral anesthetic, patient biting on cloth/leather for pain management). Topical anesthetic (20% benzocaine) reduces injection discomfort. Inhalant anesthesia (ether, chloroform) available in some humanitarian/field settings but requires training, risks. Acupuncture points (LI-4, ST-36) traditionally used; variable effectiveness.

## Extraction Techniques & Instruments

![Dental extraction instrument guide showing elevators, forceps, and proper grip angles for leverage](../assets/svgs/dentistry-restorative-3.svg)

### Extraction Instruments

**Elevators:** Straight elevator (general use, posterior teeth), Cryer elevator (angled tip, intraseptal pressure for Class II/III teeth), Winter elevator (curved handle, complex molars). Elevators work via lever mechanics: tip inserted in periodontal ligament space or against socket rim, fulcrum against adjacent bone/tooth, upward/rotational force applied. Risk: trauma to adjacent teeth/bone, incomplete extraction (apex fracture).

**Forceps:** Universal forceps (fit most teeth with slight modification), specific designs (anterior, posterior, lower). Beaks grasp tooth firmly; handles provide mechanical advantage. Technique: position beaks apical to height of contour, squeeze gently to engage, apply rotational force (posterior teeth especially), apical traction simultaneously. Risk: root fracture (excessive rotational force), alveolar socket fracture (too much apical force), tooth fracture during extraction.

### Extraction Technique Overview

1.  **Initial loosening:** Use elevator/forceps to create movement; let patient feel mobility before major force. Periodontal fibers must rupture; requires persistent gentle pressure, not sudden jerking.
2.  **Rotation:** Posterior teeth especially: gentle rotation 30-45 degrees, pause, rotate opposite direction. Creates expanding motion in socket, disrupts PDL fibers, loosens apex.
3.  **Lateral luxation:** Side-to-side rocking movement; similar mechanism to rotation. Particularly effective for anterior teeth (single root).
4.  **Apical pressure:** Once sufficiently loosened, apply steady apical (upward/outward) traction. Avoid excessive force; if tooth resistant, return to loosening techniques, repeat. Sudden jerking risks root fracture.
5.  **Removal:** Once mobile enough, apply traction to completely remove tooth. Inspect for retained roots/fragments. Irrigate socket with water/saline, remove debris, curettage of socket walls if necessary (remove inflamed granulation tissue).

### Complication Management

**Root fracture:** Apical root fragment retained in socket. Attempt removal with fine elevators/suction. If unsuccessful and fragment small (<3-5mm), small risk if no infection; patient education and follow-up advised. If infection develops or patient wants removal, surgical access may needed. **Alveolar fracture:** Socket wall or surrounding bone fractures. Usually managed conservatively (socket protection, normal healing), risk of prolonged bleeding.

**Hemorrhage:** Arterial/capillary bleeding from socket. Management: direct pressure (gauze pack 20-30 minutes), irrigation with saline, suturing if bleeding persistent, hydrogen peroxide rinse (foams debris/bacteria). Severe hemorrhage (continued bleeding >30 minutes despite pressure): consider blood loss risk, transfusion if available, pressure dressing. Patient instructions: bite on gauze 30 minutes, avoid rinsing/spitting, elevate head, avoid hot drinks, soft diet.

**Nerve injury:** Inferior alveolar nerve (IAN) damage during extraction = numbness of lower lip/chin. Risk higher in deep bone impactions, inexperienced extraction. Prevention: radiographic assessment, careful elevator use avoiding deep socket penetration. Management: none acutely; most return sensation 6-12 weeks. Persistent numbness rare.

## Infection Control Standards

Survival/field dentistry must adhere to infection control principles despite resource limitations. Cross-contamination risks: HIV, hepatitis B/C, tuberculosis, other bloodborne/respiratory pathogens.

### Hand Hygiene

Wash hands vigorously with soap + water before/after each patient. 30+ seconds of scrubbing, pay attention to nails/between fingers. If running water unavailable: waterless hand sanitizer (alcohol-based, 60%+ alcohol), wipes, or allow air drying after exposure. Glove wearing: nitrile gloves preferred (latex allergy consideration); must change gloves between patients, avoid touching face/environment once gloved with patient contact.

### Equipment Sterilization

Instruments (forceps, elevators, dental mirrors, scalers): steam autoclave preferred (121°C, 15+ minutes, high pressure). If autoclave unavailable: boil instruments in water 20-30 minutes, allow cooling, store dry. Surface disinfection: 0.5% sodium hypochlorite (household bleach diluted), 70% isopropyl alcohol, or quaternary ammonium-based disinfectant on treatment surfaces, dental chair. Bloodborne exposure: immediately clean area with disinfectant, follow up with second application.

### Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

Gloves: nitrile, changed between patients (latex allergy risk). Masks: N95 or surgical mask for patient contact (respiratory protection, splatter barrier). Eye protection: safety glasses or face shield (splatter/particle risk). Gown: barrier gown or apron (bloodstain protection). Hand hygiene after PPE removal: contamination risk if gown/gloves removed improperly. Proper removal sequence: gloves first, then gown, then mask, then eye protection; hand hygiene after.

### Sharps Handling

Needles/sharp instruments: never recap by hand; use one-handed scoop technique or discard directly into sharps container. Sharps container: rigid, puncture-proof, marked, filled only 2/3 full. Needle stick injury: wash immediately with soap + water, allow bleeding if minor (helps flush), apply antiseptic, seek testing for bloodborne pathogens if exposed patient status unknown. Document incident for employee health records.

### Waste Disposal

Contaminated sharps: sharps container for eventual proper disposal/incineration. Contaminated soft waste (gauze, gloves): biohazard bag if available, or sealed garbage bag labeled "medical waste." Teeth/extracted material: infectious waste, biohazard bag or cremation. Blood/saliva contaminated items: treated as biohazard.

</section>

<section id="pain-management">

## Pain Management & Anesthesia

### Local Anesthetic Agents

**Lidocaine:** 1-2% concentration, onset 3-5 minutes, duration 30-60 minutes depending on formulation. With epinephrine (1:100,000 or 1:200,000): reduces bleeding, prolongs duration to 60-120 minutes, delays systemic absorption (safer). Toxicity: CNS excitation (tremor, seizure at high doses), cardiovascular depression. Maximum safe dose: ~4-5 mg/kg total, ~7 mg/kg with epinephrine (e.g., 70kg patient = 280-350mg without epinephrine, 500mg with epinephrine).

**Procaine:** 1-2% concentration, older agent, similar duration to lidocaine without epinephrine. Less toxic than lidocaine but shorter action. Used if lidocaine unavailable.

**Bupivacaine:** 0.25-0.5% concentration, longer duration (4-6 hours), slower onset (10-20 minutes). Higher toxicity risk; maximum dose ~2-2.5 mg/kg. Reserved for longer procedures if available.

### Infiltration Technique

Inject directly at site of operation: anterior teeth (buccal infiltration at apex level), premolars (buccal, sometimes lingual), some molars (buccal infiltration). Needle insertion: pierce mucosa at level above apices, inject slowly (reduces pain, allows anesthetic diffusion). Typical volume: 0.5-1mL per tooth. Onset: 3-5 minutes. Adequate for minor procedures (cavity treatment, temporary restoration). Posterior mandibular molars: infiltration may inadequate (dense bone blocks diffusion); nerve block preferred.

### Nerve Block Anesthesia

**Inferior alveolar block:** Mandible complete anesthesia from midline to posterior. Technique: palpate pterygomandibular raphe (soft tissue between maxillary tuberosity + mandibular ramus), insert needle at this landmark, advance posteriorly until bone contact (lingula), deposit anesthetic 0.5-1mL. Complications: inferior alveolar nerve injury (numbness; usually temporary), hematoma (bruising), inadvertent intravascular injection. Onset: 3-10 minutes.

**Maxillary blocks:** Greater palatine block (hard palate anesthesia): insert at greater palatine foramen (posterior hard palate, medial to maxillary tuberosity), advance anteriorly, deposit anesthetic. Long buccal block: anesthetizes buccal gingiva/tissue distal to last molar. Anterior infiltration: standard buccal infiltration for incisors/canines.

### Pain Management Without Anesthesia

If local anesthesia unavailable/contraindicated: accept patient pain, work rapidly, consider supplemental analgesia. Topical anesthesia (20% benzocaine spray, 5% lidocaine ointment): superficial effect, reduces injection discomfort, slight numbing of gingiva. Systemic analgesia: aspirin, ibuprofen, acetaminophen taken pre-procedure (30-60 minutes before) may reduce perception. Distraction techniques: focus on other sensations (cold water spray), breathing exercises, relaxation. Acupuncture: auricular acupuncture (ear points) traditionally used; variable effectiveness, requires training.

### Hemorrhage Management

Bleeding from extraction socket: apply direct pressure (gauze pack) 20-30 minutes, allow clot formation. Reinsertion of gauze if bleeding resumes: repeat pressure. Cold water rinses (avoid excessive rinsing) help. Hydrogen peroxide (3%) applied topically: foams, helps remove debris, may promote hemostasis. Suturing: if persistent bleeding, suture socket margins (absorbable suture if available) or use hemostatic material (gelatin sponge, bone wax if available).

Systemic anticoagulation risk (patient on warfarin/aspirin): increased bleeding, may require modified technique (careful elevation, minimize trauma, suturing if necessary). Tranexamic acid (antifibrinolytic, 500-1000mg orally) may reduce bleeding if available.

</section>

<section id="crowns">

## Crown and Bridge Work

### Temporary Crown Fabrication

After tooth preparation (usually for permanent crown): cover prepared tooth with temporary material to: protect tooth structure, maintain vertical dimension of occlusion, prevent decay of prepared surface, restore function (eating, speech), prevent soft tissue impingement. Field expedient: composite resin or acrylic temporary can be fabricated from alginate impression. Steps: 1) Take alginate/impression material mold of unprepared tooth (tooth shade, anatomy). 2) Prepare tooth as planned (reduction ~0.5-1mm for composite, ~1.5mm for acrylic temporary). 3) Use mold with temporary material to fabricate temporary crown (place composite in mold, cure if available; or mix acrylic powder/liquid, place in mold, allow setting ~10-15 minutes). 4) Fit and adjust (check occlusion, interproximal contacts), cement with temporary cement (zinc oxide eugenol preferred; sets fast, removable at next appointment). 5) Plan for permanent crown fabrication when possible (proper lab processing, superior fit/margins).

### Permanent Crown Placement

Materials: gold alloy (best longevity/fit, cost-prohibitive survival), porcelain fused to metal (PFM, aesthetic + strength), all-ceramic (aesthetic, lower strength). Survival scenario: likely impossible to fabricate professionally. Focus on: preservation of prepared tooth (temporary crown essential), prevention of further decay, extraction if restoration not feasible. If crown available: fit assessment (margins adapted, no gaps visible, interproximal contact appropriate), occlusion check (patient bites lightly, no high spots; articulating paper identifies contact areas), cement with permanent cement (zinc phosphate, resin-modified glass ionomer, or adhesive resin per manufacturer).

### Crown Cementation Technique

Temporary cement (zinc oxide eugenol): powder + liquid mixed to creamy consistency, apply to internal crown walls, seat crown on prepared tooth, apply vertical pressure 5-10 seconds, allow set per product (~5-10 minutes), remove excess cement with explorer/scaler, check occlusion. Temporary cement removable; allows re-cementation if needed (marginal failure, patient displacement).

Permanent cement: zinc phosphate: cool glass plate (reduces setting time), add powder to liquid incrementally (creamy consistency), fill crown interior, seat on tooth, apply pressure, excess bleeds out, wipe away with gauze, allow set 6-7 minutes. Patient instruction: avoid chewing 24+ hours (full set), avoid hot drinks 2+ hours. Resin-modified glass ionomer: chemical set, similar technique to zinc phosphate, fluoride release benefit.

### Re-cementing Loose Crowns

Crown loose but structure intact: clean cement from crown interior (explorer/scaler, ultrasonic cleaner if available) and prepared tooth surface (remove old cement). Dry thoroughly (air syringe, compressed air, or allow air dry 5+ minutes). Re-cement: apply fresh temporary or permanent cement, seat crown, apply pressure, excess bleeds out, wipe. Advise patient to avoid chewing until cement sets (24 hours ideal, minimum 1 hour). If permanent re-cementation: requires careful drying 48+ hours before eating (permanent cement strength develops over days).

</section>

<section id="cement">

## Cement and Adhesive Systems

### Zinc Phosphate Cement (Traditional)

Powder (zinc oxide + magnesium oxide + other metal oxides + glass powder) + liquid (phosphoric acid). Mixing: add powder to liquid in small increments (~0.1g at a time), spatulate on cool glass plate (reduces setting time, thickness/viscosity control). Consistency: creamy for luting (not too thick/thin). Use: temporary and permanent crowns, bridges, orthodontic bands, inlays/onlays. Set time: 6-7 minutes (longer on warm surfaces, shorter on cool). Strength adequate, acceptable for field use. Solubility: slightly soluble in oral fluids over years (margins may fail); longevity 5-10+ years depending on preparation/crown margins.

### Glass Ionomer Cement

Powder (alumina silicate glass) + liquid (polyacrylic acid). Mix per product: specific ratios critical (too much powder = weak, too much liquid = soluble). Sets chemically without light (advantage in field/survival). Fluoride release (0.1-1.0 ppm/day) provides cavity prevention + anti-inflammatory effect. Uses: temporary/permanent cement for crowns, core buildup material, temporary restoration material. Strength: lower than zinc phosphate but adequate for low-stress applications. Bonds to tooth structure via polyalkenoate formation. Moisture sensitivity: protect from saliva during setting (at least 24 hours ideal); use varnish, rubber dam, or careful isolation. If moisture contaminated: material weakens, failure risk increases.

### Resin-Based Adhesive Systems (Etch-and-Rinse, Self-Etch)

**Etch-and-Rinse:** Etching: phosphoric acid 37-40% applied to enamel/dentin 15-30 seconds, rinse thoroughly with water, dry to remove moisture (enamel chalky white appearance). Priming: apply primer per product instructions (wets etched surface, enables resin infiltration). Bonding: apply bonding resin, light-cure 20-40 seconds (enamel bonds strongest, dentin bonds adequate). Creates micromechanical interlocking + chemical adhesion. Advantages: superior retention, predictable bonds. Disadvantages: technique-sensitive, requires light source, moisture contamination fails bond, difficult in field without equipment.

**Self-Etch Systems:** Acidic primer etches + primes simultaneously (no separate etching step). Advantages: faster, potentially less technique-sensitive. Disadvantages: less reliable enamel etching (may require selective enamel etching with phosphoric acid separately), moisture sensitivity still critical. Not practical in most field/survival settings.

</section>

<section id="denture-basics">

## Denture Basics

Complete dentures replace all teeth; partial dentures replace some teeth. In survival/field scenario, fabrication unlikely; focus on maintenance/adjustment of existing dentures or temporary solutions for missing teeth.

### Complete Denture Considerations

Retention depends on: denture base fit (intimate contact with palate/ridge), border extensions (proper length at distal/buccal surfaces), saliva (lubrication, surface tension aids retention). Adjustment: if denture loose (retention poor), relines (tissue surface built up with soft material) improve fit; tissue conditioner (soft material) placed in denture, inserted in mouth, allows tissue rebound, then hardened in clinical laboratory. Bite problems: occlusal adjustments needed if denture high (causes TMJ dysfunction, muscle fatigue); use articulating paper to identify contact areas, grind down selectively.

### Denture Cleaning & Maintenance

Daily: brush denture with soft brush + mild soap/water, soak overnight in denture cleaner solution (sodium hypochlorite-based or enzymes). Avoid hot water (warps acrylic). In field: water rinses alone helpful; avoid harsh abrasion (damages surface). Food debris under denture irritates tissue; ensure proper cleansing. Tissue inspection: red/swollen area = pressure problem (adjustment needed), ulceration (relining needed), candidiasis (fungal infection; treat with antifungal rinse).

### Temporary Tooth Replacement Options

If tooth missing and denture unavailable: removable partial denture fabrication requires laboratory (impractical in survival). Temporary prosthetics: composite tooth built onto adjacent tooth (adhesive-bonded) if space allows, or acrylic resin tooth form attached to framework. Outcome: functional, aesthetic improvement, maintained until permanent denture available. Space maintenance important: prevents adjacent teeth drift, maintains vertical dimension.

</section>

<section id="pediatric">

## Pediatric Dentistry

Children present unique challenges: smaller anatomy, higher anxiety, faster decay progression in primary teeth, special considerations for pulp exposure (primary dentition more prone to exposure due to thinner enamel/dentin).

### Primary vs Permanent Dentition

Primary dentition (ages 6mo-6yrs, all baby teeth): thinner enamel/dentin, larger pulp chambers (closer to surface), faster caries progression. Restoration approach: focus on sealing decay, temporary materials acceptable if extraction impending (primary tooth exfoliation expected). Permanent dentition (age 6+ as permanent teeth erupt): standard restoration protocols apply, enhanced durability needed (longer tooth lifespan). Avoidance of extraction if possible: primary tooth loss may alter eruption patterns of permanent successors.

### Anxiety Management in Pediatric Patients

Tell-show-do technique: explain procedure in child-friendly language, show instruments, demonstrate on finger, then perform. Positive reinforcement: praise cooperation, offer reward after treatment. Parental presence: helpful for anxious children, but remove parent if disruptive. Distraction: music, ceiling mobile/visual stimulation. Gentle handling: avoid sudden movements, loud instruments. Consider nitrous oxide (50% N2O + 50% O2) if available and trained provider (effective anxiety reduction without general anesthesia). Topical anesthetic before injection (benzocaine spray) reduces injection discomfort.

### Primary Tooth Restoration

Decay treatment: removal as described in adults; smaller anatomy requires scaled-down instruments (smaller burs, hand instruments). Filling materials: amalgam acceptable, composite also used (aesthetic for anterior), glass ionomer (fluoride release helps prevent future decay). Pulp exposure: less favorable prognosis in primary teeth; options include: pulpotomy (removal of coronal pulp, leave apical), pulpectomy (complete removal), or extraction if infection present. Primary molar loss: space maintenance device (band + loop) prevents drift; extraction acceptable if >6mo before permanent successor eruption.

</section>

<section id="recognition-assessment">

## Recognition & Assessment Protocol

Proper diagnosis drives treatment decisions. A systematic examination reveals extent, location, and urgency of dental pathology.

### Clinical Examination Sequence

**Visual inspection:** Observe from 2 feet away (overall appearance), then intraoral (systematic tooth-by-tooth review). Note: tooth color (discoloration indicates decay/trauma), tooth position (displacement risk for extraction), gingival appearance (erythema = inflammation, pus = abscess), visible cavitation (size, depth, location). Use dental mirror to view posterior surfaces, maintain indirect vision.

**Tactile examination:** Probe with dental explorer or smooth instrument. Dark discoloration with tactile catch = caries. Probe gently around margins (patient pain indicates pulp involvement). Percussion (tap tooth gently with mirror handle): pain = pulp inflammation or periapical pathology. Palpate gingiva (over apex region) for fluctuance (abscess/pus collection).

**Mobility assessment:** Grasp tooth with thumb + finger, apply lateral force. Grade 1 = <1mm mobility (normal), Grade 2 = 1-2mm (moderate periodontitis), Grade 3 = >2mm or vertical mobility (advanced periodontal disease, extraction candidate). Pathologic mobility = poor restoration prognosis.

:::info-box
**Diagnostic Key:** Pain on percussion + swelling at apex + tooth mobility + radiographic radiolucency = periapical abscess. Requires drainage ± extraction. Proceed to restoration only after infection control.
:::

### Pulp Vitality Testing

**Cold test:** Apply cold (ice, ethyl chloride spray on tooth surface). Vital pulp = sharp pain (patient withdraws). Non-vital = no response. False positives (adjacent tooth stimulated), false negatives (necrotic pulp insensate). Use as supporting evidence, not sole criterion.

**Heat test:** Apply warm instrument to tooth (less reliable, risk of thermal burn). Vital pulp = moderate pain. Non-vital = no response.

**Electric pulp test (if available):** Low-voltage stimulation increasing until patient feels tingling. Vital pulp = response at low voltage. Non-vital = no response at maximum. Technique-dependent, user error common.

**Interpret holistically:** Single test unreliable. Combine clinical signs (discoloration, mobility, percussion pain), radiographic findings (periapical pathology), patient history (trauma, pain character, duration).

:::warning
A tooth may have questionable vitality. In survival scenarios, when in doubt: extract rather than restore over potential infection. Restoration over necrotic, infected pulp spreads abscess, risks sepsis.
:::

<section id="complications-when-stop">

## Complications & When to Stop Treatment

### Immediate Treatment Stoppage Triggers

:::danger
**STOP treatment immediately if:**

- **Anaphylaxis signs:** Urticaria (hives), swollen lips/tongue, respiratory stridor, hypotension, loss of consciousness. → Lay patient supine, establish airway, IV access if possible, epinephrine 0.3-0.5 mg IM, oxygen, transfer to higher care.
- **Cardiac dysrhythmia:** Palpitations, chest pain, syncope during/after local anesthetic injection → Stop injection, monitor, place patient supine, oxygen, CPR if indicated, transfer to hospital immediately.
- **Seizure during local anesthetic:** Tremor → seizure activity → Stop injection, protect airway (position on side), prevent aspiration, seizure precautions (padding), oxygen, IV benzodiazepine if available (diazepam 5-10mg IV/IM).
- **Airway compromise:** Swelling of throat, difficulty breathing, stridor → Elevate head, oxygen, prepare emergency airway (oral airway, cricothyrotomy if trained), antihistamine (diphenhydramine 25-50 mg), epinephrine if anaphylaxis.
- **Hemorrhage uncontrolled:** Bleeding continues >30 minutes despite direct pressure → Elevate head, maintain pressure, apply hemostatic materials (gelatin sponge, bone wax, tranexamic acid topical if available), suture if trained, consider IV fluids/transfusion if available, transfer to hospital.
- **Patient syncope/loss of consciousness:** May be vasovagal (injection fear/pain) or anesthetic toxicity. → Lay patient horizontal (restores cerebral perfusion), oxygen, monitor vitals, establish IV if possible, do not continue procedure.
:::

### Pulp Exposure Complications

**Small exposure (<1mm), vital pulp (bleeding, pain response):** Seal immediately with calcium hydroxide paste (promotes healing) or zinc oxide eugenol (protective). Monitor for abscess (periapical pain, swelling); if abscess develops, extraction/endodontic treatment. Many small vital pulp exposures recover with appropriate seal; success 50-80% with proper technique.

**Large exposure (>2mm), vital pulp:** Risk of pulp necrosis/abscess higher. Options: pulp capping attempt (same as small exposure; lower success rate), extraction (safest in survival scenario). If attempt preservation, ensure infection-free at follow-up (no pain, no periapical changes).

**Necrotic pulp exposure (dark, no bleeding response):** Already infected; extraction strongly indicated (restoration over infected pulp fails, spreads abscess). Temporary seal acceptable only if extraction delayed; monitor closely for abscess.

:::warning
Pulp exposure size, pulp vitality, and infection presence drive management. Small vital exposures may be preserved with excellent technique + follow-up. Large, necrotic, or infected exposures → extraction priority. In field, when unsure: extract rather than risk spreading infection.
:::

### Periodontal Complications Post-Extraction

**Dry socket (alveolar osteitis):** Clot dislodges 2-3 days post-extraction, exposed bone. Causes severe pain (much worse than normal healing pain). Risk factors: smoking, female, oral contraceptives, difficult extraction, infection. Prevention: avoid dislodging clot (no smoking/rinsing/spitting × 48hrs), gentle extraction technique. Treatment: irrigation with saline, packing socket with antiseptic gauze (iodoform gauze or zinc oxide eugenol-impregnated gauze), change dressing every 2-3 days until bone covered (7-10 days typically). Pain control (ibuprofen, topical anesthetic drops if available). Antibiotics only if infection signs (fever, cellulitis).

**Osteitis (bone infection):** Fever, swelling, lymphadenopathy, purulent drainage >3-5 days post-extraction. Risk: deep infection prior to extraction, immunosuppression. Treatment: antibiotics (amoxicillin 500mg 3× daily × 7-10 days, or clindamycin if penicillin-allergic), hot compress/irrigation, ensure adequate drainage (may need incision if pus localized). Referral for possible surgical debridement if available.

**Nerve injury (usually inferior alveolar):** Numbness lower lip/chin from IAN damage. Prevention: radiographic assessment if available, careful extraction avoiding deep socket penetration. Management: none acutely; most sensation returns 6-12 weeks. Persistent numbness (>3 months) rare; refer if available.

<section id="recovery-aftercare">

## Recovery & Aftercare Management

### Immediate Post-Procedure Care (First 24 Hours)

**Restorative (filling, crown) patient instructions:**
- Avoid chewing on restored tooth 24+ hours (material setting completes)
- Avoid extreme temperatures (hot drinks/ice) 24-48 hours (sensitivity risk, material softening risk)
- Take over-the-counter pain reliever if discomfort (ibuprofen preferred for anti-inflammatory effect)
- Do not disturb restoration (no probing with tongue, no picking with food)
- Normal diet when material fully set (timing per material: amalgam, glass ionomer, zinc oxide eugenol 24 hrs; composite light-cured = immediately, but avoid high-stress for 24 hrs)

**Extraction patient instructions:**
- Bite gauze pack continuously 30 minutes to establish clot
- If bleeding continues: replace with fresh gauze, bite another 30 minutes
- Avoid rinsing, spitting, straw drinking (dislodges clot) × 48 hours
- Ice pack to cheek 15 minutes on, 15 minutes off × first 24 hours (reduces swelling)
- Elevate head while sleeping (reduces post-operative swelling)
- Soft diet × 3-7 days (soup, yogurt, applesauce, mashed potatoes, avoid hard/sticky foods)
- Hot drinks/baths first 48 hours may increase bleeding (avoid)
- Smoking/alcohol delay healing (avoid × 48 hours minimum, ideally longer)
- Rinse gently with warm salt water after meals starting day 2 (½ teaspoon salt in 8 oz warm water × 3-4×/day)
- Pain/swelling peak day 2-3 (normal)
- Report uncontrolled bleeding, fever, foul odor, severe pain beyond normal healing trajectory

### Post-Operative Pain Management Timeline

**Expected pain pattern:** Extraction pain peaks day 2-3, decreases by day 7-10. Restorative sensitivity peaks first 24-48 hours, should resolve by day 3-5. Persistent or increasing pain → complication present (infection, high bite, pulp exposure).

**Medication timeline:**
- Days 0-3: ibuprofen 400mg 3-4× daily (strongest anti-inflammatory effect) or aspirin 500mg 3-4× daily if ibuprofen unavailable
- Days 4-7: taper to as-needed (acetaminophen adequate for residual discomfort)
- Beyond day 7: persistent pain → assess for dry socket, infection, other complications

### Long-Term Restoration Care

**Filling/restorative maintenance:** Brush gently around restoration (avoid aggressive scrubbing). Floss carefully at margins (do not traumatize). Avoid sticky foods initially (first week to month depending on material). Report sensitivity or fracture immediately (restoration failure early = easier repair than delayed).

**Crown care:** Floss carefully (threaded floss, avoid excessive pressure at margin). Avoid chewing on hard objects (ice, nuts, hard candy) on crowned tooth. Crown should feel similar to natural tooth; report any discomfort, mobility, or margin gap. Crowned tooth still vulnerable to decay at margin; maintain oral hygiene.

**Regular follow-up:** Schedule follow-up 1-2 weeks post-procedure (verify healing normal, restoration retention intact, occlusion comfortable). Return for any complications (pain, restoration loss, sensitivity increase). Long-term monitoring × several weeks/months for restorations (identify early failure).

:::info-box
**Healing milestones — Extraction socket:**
Day 0-3: clot formation, peak swelling/pain.
Day 4-7: clot organization, granulation tissue formation, swelling decreasing.
Day 7-14: epithelial coverage beginning, pain resolve.
Weeks 2-8: bone remodeling, socket fills, dimensions change.
Months 3-6: bone maturation, complete healing.
Complete osseous healing requires 6+ months; socket appearance stabilizes by 3-4 weeks for denture/implant planning.
:::

</section>

<section id="troubleshooting">

## Troubleshooting Problems

### Restoration Placement Problems

**"Material won't condense properly" (especially amalgam):**
- Check powder-to-mercury ratio (too much liquid = mushy consistency; add powder incrementally)
- Ensure amalgam fresh (old alloy powder = degradation, condensation failure)
- Use proper condenser with adequate pressure (light pressure = loose, weak restoration)
- Work temperature: if cold environment, mixing takes longer; allow brief setting time before condensation
- *Fix:* Start over if condensation clearly inadequate (remove material, dry cavity, remix fresh batch)

**"Composite won't cure/remains sticky":**
- Light source may be inadequate (too dim, wrong wavelength, battery depleted in cordless light)
- Insufficient curing time (minimum 20-40 sec per layer per manufacturer)
- Curing light positioned >5mm from surface (light intensity drops with distance)
- *Fix:* Verify light working (check battery, intensity indicator if available), cure longer at close distance; if material still soft, scrape off, apply fresh composite with longer cure

**"Glass ionomer too thick/difficult to adapt":**
- Mixed too much powder relative to liquid (stiff, unworkable)
- Delayed mixing/application (paste sets quickly, becomes unworkable)
- *Fix:* Remix with proper ratio (follow product instructions exactly), work rapidly, apply immediately; if set occurs before adaptation, start over with fresh mix

**"Filling margins gaps visible, patient feels edges":**
- Incomplete condensation (voids/weak contact at margins)
- Material shrinkage (especially composite polymerization shrinkage)
- Inadequate cavity wall preparation (walls too divergent, poor anatomy)
- *Fix:* Polish/refine margins with bur/finishing instrument (smooth sharp edges), assess for gaps that require replacement; if extensive gaps, consider refacing/replacing restoration

### Extraction Problems

**"Tooth won't loosen despite repeated elevation/rotation":**
- Instrumentation technique incorrect (pressure direction wrong, leverage inadequate)
- Deep bone impaction (common with unerupted/partially erupted teeth)
- Heavy periodontal ligament (patient young, healthy bone support)
- *Fix:* Change elevator angle/fulcrum point; use rotational forces more aggressively; consider small osteotomy (trim bone around tooth with bur/chisel if trained) if available time/resources permit; patience + persistent gentle pressure sometimes succeeds over 5-10 minutes where sudden force fails

**"Continued bleeding from extraction socket after 30 minutes pressure":**
- Arterial bleeding (not simple capillary ooze)
- Anticoagulation/bleeding disorder (patient history)
- Inadequate pressure application (patient not biting firmly on gauze)
- *Fix:* Fresh gauze with sustained firm pressure (30+ minutes), hydrogen peroxide irrigation (helps identify bleeding source), hemostatic agents if available (gelatin sponge, bone wax, tranexamic acid), consider suturing margins or packing socket with iodoform gauze; if uncontrollable, elevate head, monitor blood loss, consider IV access/fluid/transfusion if available, transfer to hospital if resources inadequate

**"Root fracture; apex fragment remains in socket":**
- Excessive rotational force during extraction (technique error)
- Root morphology anatomy (curved roots more prone to fracture)
- *Fix:* Attempt retrieval with fine elevators, suction, or surgical curette under good visualization; if fragment small (<3-5mm) and asymptomatic, document location (radiograph if available), patient education on symptoms of abscess, routine follow-up; if accessible, small surgical incision may retrieve fragment; risk of infection if retained fragment becomes focus, but infection risk modest if truly small + infection-free

:::warning
Root fracture often occurs unnoticed. Inspect extraction site carefully under good light. Radiograph post-extraction (if available) confirms complete removal. Patient education critical: report pain/swelling at extraction site weeks later (may indicate retained fragment infection). Small asymptomatic retained fragments often benign; monitor rather than pursue risky surgical retrieval.
:::

### Post-Operative Problem Resolution

**"Patient reports sensitivity 48+ hours post-restoration":**
- Secondary decay (microleakage) beginning
- High bite (restoration contacts opposing tooth)
- Pulp inflammation (early pulpal response to cavity preparation trauma)
- *Fix:* Check bite with articulating paper (adjust if high spot present), assess cavity margins visually/radiographically for secondary decay (if none, likely pulpal sensitivity = may resolve spontaneously; re-evaluate in 1-2 weeks), if sensitivity persists/increases = restoration failure risk; consider replacement vs. monitoring

**"Patient reports restoration loose/moving":**
- Debonding (adhesive failure) or material failure
- Inadequate retention form (preparation too divergent)
- *Fix:* Remove restoration, assess preparation for caries, retention adequacy; if adequate, clean, dry thoroughly, replace with fresh material; if inadequate, modify preparation (deepen, increase walls), then place restoration; assess filling material choice (amalgam = superior retention in mechanical preparation; composite = adhesive retention + mechanical)

**"Crown margin visible/gapping; patient concerned about aesthetics":**
- Crown placement/fitting problem
- Margin under gingival tissue (normal; visible only if soft tissue recedes)
- Margin poorly adapted during cementation
- *Fix:* If crown intact but margin problem: remove crown, clean margins, re-cement with improved isolation/seating pressure; if crown structure compromised, replacement needed (fabrication required if field setting unavailable); aesthetic concerns in survival may require acceptance of suboptimal results pending permanent restoration

## Field Adaptations & Improvisation

### Improvised Equipment & Materials

**Extraction instruments:** In absence of proper elevators/forceps: use flathead screwdriver (small pry bar for elevation), channel-lock pliers (adapted for forceps-like grasp), or carefully sharpened metal instruments (awl, old dental instruments). Sterilize thoroughly. Technique requires careful, controlled application to avoid excess trauma.

**Anesthesia application:** If syringe/needle unavailable: topical anesthetic (20% benzocaine, 5% lidocaine) applied generously to gingiva allows superficial anesthesia (extraction discomfort reduced but not eliminated). Accept patient pain, work rapidly, provide psychological support (distraction, breathing techniques).

**Filling materials:**
- **Zinc oxide eugenol:** Most practical field material (powder + liquid widely available in humanitarian kits). Adequate temporary restoration, basic permanent filling if no other option.
- **Improvised temporary:** Cotton + zinc oxide eugenol paste (non-ideal, acceptable emergency). Petroleum jelly + talc mixture (not sterile, increased infection risk, use only if no alternatives).
- **Temporary crown:** Impression mold of unprepared tooth, preparation per protocol, reconstruction using composite (if light available) or acrylic powder/liquid (chemical set, no light needed).

**Sterilization without autoclave:** Boiling (20-30 minutes in water, allows cooling) remains standard. Dry instruments completely before storage (moisture promotes rust). Chemical disinfection (0.5% sodium hypochlorite, 70% isopropyl alcohol) acceptable for non-critical items if boiling infeasible. Flame sterilization (holding instrument in flame briefly) possible but uncontrolled, risks material damage, not recommended.

:::tip
Field dentistry success depends on accepting limitations. Perfect technique with improvised equipment often surpasses incomplete treatment with ideal materials. Focus on infection prevention, adequate anesthesia, complete procedure execution. Accept suboptimal aesthetics/durability; survival priority = function, infection control, patient safety.
:::

### Environmental Adaptations

**Visibility:** Headlamp/flashlight essential. Reflective surface (mirror, aluminum foil) behind light source improves illumination. Dental mirrors or improvised mirrors (smooth water surface, polished metal) enable indirect visualization. Bright daylight preferred if possible (outdoors, near window).

**Isolation:** Cotton rolls + gauze for moisture control. Saliva ejector suction: improvised using tubing + syringe (manual negative pressure). Rubber dam (ideal but complex) unlikely available. Accept moisture challenges; ensure isolation as adequate as possible.

**Workspace:** Stable chair/reclined position critical for patient comfort + operator access. Overhead light/headlamp, instrument tray organized, sharps container (rigid, puncture-proof container for needles/sharp items). Waste: biohazard bag if available, or sealed bag labeled medical waste.

**Infection control:** Waterless hand sanitizer (essential if running water unavailable). Gloves (change between patients), masks (simple surgical mask adequate if N95 unavailable), eye protection (simple glasses reduce splatter risk). Disinfection solution (dilute bleach 0.5% sodium hypochlorite, or 70% isopropyl alcohol).

## Restoration Failure Modes

Understanding failure mechanisms enables prevention, early detection, appropriate management.

### Marginal Leakage & Secondary Decay

Definition: microleakage at restoration margin, saliva/bacteria penetrate interface. Causes: inadequate adaptation of material to cavity walls, polymerization shrinkage (composite), material degradation over time, preparation margins on dentin (bonds not as durable as enamel). Prevention: accurate cavity preparation, proper isolation (moisture control), technique-sensitive materials (composite, adhesives) placed carefully, margin location on enamel if possible. Detection: visual (dark line at margin), X-ray (radiolucency beyond margin = secondary decay), patient complaint (sensitivity, pain if decay reaches pulp). Management: assess if decay extensive; if minor, might polish/refine margin; if >1/3 restoration involved, replace entire restoration.

### Restoration Fracture

Composite fracture: stress concentration at restoration margins (line angles especially), polymerization shrinkage stress, inadequate material thickness on cusp (cusps flex during biting). Prevention: avoid sharp internal line angles (round slightly), adequate material thickness (at least 1-1.5mm), avoid high-stress areas (composite not ideal for extensive posterior restorations). Amalgam fracture: rare, usually indicates excessive stress or defect in condensation (voids). Zinc oxide eugenol fracture: common due to material weakness; expected in high-stress areas, especially if thin condensation.

### Restoration Loss (Complete Debonding)

Causes: inadequate retention form (preparation diverges too much, shallow cavitation), material failure (debond due to moisture contamination, inadequate curing), excessive stress concentration, trauma. Prevention: proper cavity preparation (adequate depth, convergent walls, rounded angles), isolation/moisture control during placement, adequate curing (light exposure, setting time). Management: assess preparation for retention; reface/rebuild if acceptable, or extract/excavate if secondary decay too extensive.

### Occlusal Interference (High Bite)

Overfilled restoration causes high contact when jaw closed. Causes: excessive material placement, inadequate contouring before final polish. Consequences: TMJ dysfunction, muscle fatigue, discomfort. Prevention: fill cavity level with adjacent teeth surface, verify no excess material protrudes occlusally. Detection: patient report of discomfort, articulating paper shows high contact on restoration. Management: selective grinding of high spot, verify occlusion normalized, repeat if needed.

### Recurrent Decay (New Cavity)

Decay on different area of same tooth, not at restoration margin (unlike secondary decay). Causes: poor oral hygiene, diet high in fermentable carbohydrates, salivary insufficiency. Prevention: patient education (brushing/flossing, dietary modification), fluoride exposure (toothpaste, rinses, varnish in high-risk patients). Detection: visual, X-ray. Management: standard cavity treatment protocol applies; restore new lesion.

<section id="seasonal">

## Seasonal Considerations in Field Dentistry

Climate, seasonal variation, resource availability affect treatment planning, materials choice, outcomes.

### Cold Weather Impacts

Materials affected: zinc oxide eugenol sets faster in cold (exothermic reaction less noticeable), composite curing may be slower if ambient temperature low (chemical/light-activated polymerization slower). Amalgam: setting time increases in cold. Patient management: avoid cold foods/drinks post-extraction (pain, hemorrhage risk from vasodilation rebound), maintain patient comfort (warm room if possible). Infection risk: cold-induced vasoconstriction reduces healing capacity, immunosuppression risk.

### Hot Weather Impacts

Materials affected: zinc oxide eugenol may set too quickly (limited working time), composite curing faster (light-activated if room temperature elevated), amalgam setting time short. Equipment: instruments warm to touch, patient discomfort; cooled instruments preferable. Sterilization: if autoclave not available, boiling time may increase due to water evaporation (ensure adequate water volume). Infection risk: warm/humid conditions favor bacterial growth, infection complication rates increase. Patient: dehydration risk (reduced salivary flow, altered healing), heat stroke prevention.

### Seasonal Disease Burden

Winter: influenza, respiratory infections increase; patient respiratory status affects anesthesia tolerability. Summer: gastrointestinal infections, parasitic infections more common in tropical regions (affects systemic health, healing). Trauma: seasonal activity variations (skiing winter, water sports summer) change trauma presentation patterns.

### Resource Availability

Rural areas: seasonal road closure may limit patient access, supply delivery. Plan ahead: stockpile materials before seasonal challenges. Tropical regions: humidity damages materials (composite, adhesives moisture-sensitive; store desiccated), fungal infections complicate healing (candidiasis post-extraction common in warm/humid regions). Cold regions: frozen water makes sterilization/irrigation challenging (thaw frozen supplies, maintain heated water supply).

## Community Dental Clinic Setup

In survival/humanitarian scenarios, establishing basic dental clinic enables treatment of population, reduces infection complications, improves quality of life.

### Minimal Equipment Needs

Dental chair/treatment area: stable, allows good visibility/access (surgical spotlight, mirror for indirect visualization). Instrument tray: organized, sterile. Suction: essential for visibility during extraction/procedures; manual syringe suction adequate (tubing + syringe for negative pressure). Handpieces (if available): electric drill, dental bur, or manual handpiece (mechanical rotary tool) for tooth preparation. If handpieces unavailable: manual instruments (chisels, scalers, elevators) accomplish decay removal (slower, more laborious).

### Essential Instruments & Supplies

Extraction: elevators (straight, curved), forceps (anterior, posterior), suction. Restorative: dental mirror, explorer (detect caries), scaler (remove plaque), plugger/condenser (condense filling material), carver (shape restoration). Anesthesia: syringe, needles, local anesthetic (lidocaine), topical anesthetic (benzocaine). Isolation: cotton rolls, gauze, rubber dam (if available, complex setup), saliva ejector/suction. Materials: amalgam (powder + mercury), composite (if light available), zinc oxide eugenol (temporary), glass ionomer, cements. Sterilization: autoclave (if powered), or boiling equipment, disinfectant solutions, PPE (gloves, masks, gowns).

### Infection Control Protocol

Hand hygiene: soap + water before/after each patient. Glove: change between patients. Instrument sterilization: autoclave preferred (121°C, 15+ minutes), boil backup (20-30 minutes). Surface disinfection: 0.5% sodium hypochlorite on treatment area between patients. Sharps container: puncture-proof, biohazard-labeled. Waste: contaminated items in biohazard bag (separate from general refuse). Staff health: vaccination (hepatitis B, influenza), testing for bloodborne pathogens (baseline), education (bloodborne pathogen exposure risks/prevention).

### Patient Education & Prevention

Oral hygiene: brushing (2x/day, 2 minutes), flossing, tongue cleaning. Diet counseling: reduce fermentable carbohydrates (frequency matters more than amount), water after meals, avoid sugary drinks. Fluoride: if available, recommend fluoride toothpaste (1000-1500ppm), fluoride rinses (adults), fluoride varnish application (high-risk patients). Regular checkups: 6-12 monthly (depends on caries risk, available resources). Extraction alternatives: emphasize restoration value (retain natural tooth), discuss long-term consequences of tooth loss (neighboring teeth drift, occlusal changes, functional loss).

<section id="safety">

:::warning
### Critical Restorative Safety

-   Pulp exposure = immediate treatment needed. Seal exposure immediately with temporary material (zinc oxide eugenol preferred, avoid eugenol contact with vital pulp) to halt infection progression. Monitor for abscess; extraction if pulp necrotic or infection present. Vital pulps may recover if sealed early + infection prevented.
-   Infection present = antibiotics prerequisite before restoration. Infected tooth/periapical pathology compromises restoration success; infection spreads if sealed in. Allow infection treatment (drainage, antibiotic therapy) before restoration placement. Abscess drainage: incision at fluctuant area, allow drainage, irrigate, prescribe antibiotics (amoxicillin 500mg 3x/day x 7-10 days typical; adjust for allergy/resistance patterns).
-   Undiagnosed periapical pathology = restoration over infection fails, spreads. Assess for periapical abscess (percussion pain = diagnostic; radiographic change if available). Drain/treat before restoration. Consider extraction if restoration infeasible + infection present.
-   Moisture contamination = bond failure. Isolate tooth adequately; saliva/blood contamination during composite placement = adhesive failure, restoration loss risk. Rubber dam ideal (creates dry field); if unavailable, cotton rolls + gauze + active suction acceptable. Technique: dry tooth + surrounding area vigorously, apply materials, cure/set.
:::

:::warning
### Anesthesia Risks

-   Local anesthetic toxicity: high doses cause CNS excitation (tremor, seizure), cardiac dysrhythmias. Maximum safe dose: ~4-5 mg/kg without epinephrine, ~7 mg/kg with epinephrine. Monitor vital signs if available, educate patient to report tongue/lip numbness (pre-seizure sign). Treatment: stop injection, monitor, seizure management (airway, oxygen, seizure precautions).
-   Needle stick injury: wash immediately with soap + water, allow bleeding, apply antiseptic, seek testing for bloodborne pathogens if exposed patient status unknown. Document incident for employee health/legal records.
-   Allergic reaction: reaction to anesthetic/preservatives (sulphites in epinephrine-containing solutions). History of allergy = use preservative-free solution or non-epinephrine formula. Signs: urticaria, angioedema, anaphylaxis (respiratory distress, hypotension, loss of consciousness). Treatment: antihistamine (diphenhydramine 25-50mg), epinephrine (0.3-0.5mg IM if anaphylaxis), oxygen, IV access if available.
:::

:::danger
### Extraction Contraindications & High-Risk Situations

-   **Uncontrolled hemorrhage history:** Patient with bleeding disorder, anticoagulation therapy, or platelet dysfunction = extraction high hemorrhage risk. Obtain coagulation status if possible. If extraction necessary: fresh-frozen plasma, platelet transfusion, or reversible anticoagulation (hold warfarin × 2-3 days if time permits) per medical provider guidance.
-   **Immunocompromised patient:** HIV/AIDS, chemotherapy, long-term steroids, organ transplant = delayed healing, infection risk increased. Extraction still possible, but antibiotics prophylaxis + close post-operative monitoring essential. Prefer restoration when feasible.
-   **Uncontrolled diabetes:** Hyperglycemia (>250 mg/dL if testable) = delayed wound healing, infection risk elevated. Defer elective extraction until glycemic control improved. Emergency extraction (abscess, severe pain) proceeds with enhanced infection control + close follow-up.
-   **Radiation therapy to jaw:** Prior head/neck radiation = xerostomia (dry mouth) + osteonecrosis risk. Extraction traumatizes devascularized bone. Consult oncology if possible. If extraction necessary: meticulous technique, avoid unnecessary bone trauma, prophylactic antibiotics.
:::

:::tip
### Safe Anesthesia Administration Checklist

Before injecting any local anesthetic:
1. **Confirm patient allergy history** (penicillin-type allergies do NOT necessarily preclude lidocaine; sulphite sensitivity contraindicated epinephrine-containing solutions)
2. **Assess maximum safe dose** for patient weight + choice of anesthetic ± epinephrine
3. **Calculate volume** from concentration (e.g., 2% lidocaine = 20 mg/mL)
4. **Aspirate before injecting** (pull plunger slightly to ensure needle not in blood vessel; redirection if positive aspiration)
5. **Inject slowly** (reduces discomfort, allows tissue diffusion, safer than rapid bolus)
6. **Monitor patient** for adverse reactions (tremor, palpitations, difficulty breathing = STOP, manage emergently)
7. **Document** anesthetic agent, concentration, volume, site, patient response
:::

<section id="mistakes">

:::warning
### Common Restorative Errors

-   **Incomplete decay removal:** Residual caries under restoration = progression, abscess. Cavity should feel hard when probed (soft spot = remaining caries). Ensure all discolored/soft dentin removed, walls divergent. Verify cavity margins at enamel-dentin junction.
-   **Overfilled restoration:** Excessive material causes high bite, TMJ dysfunction, patient discomfort. Adjust occlusion after placement: identify high spot with articulating paper, grind selectively, repeat. Verify patient comfortable on new restoration before discharge.
-   **Marginal gaps:** Microleakage through margins = secondary decay. Proper cavity shape/material adaptation critical. Margins on enamel = better seal. Enamel walls should be 0.5mm from cavity floor minimum; dentin margins more challenging (less durable bond). Polish margins after material sets to optimize seal.
-   **Wrong material selection:** Zinc oxide eugenol not appropriate for high-stress posterior (molar cusps); will fracture/fail quickly. Choose material matching location demands: amalgam/composite (posterior high-stress), composite/glass ionomer (anterior, low-stress), zinc oxide eugenol (temporary only).
-   **Inadequate isolation:** Moisture contamination during placement = restoration failure. Isolate thoroughly: cotton rolls, gauze, suction. Composite especially sensitive (adhesive fails if saliva present during bonding). Rubber dam ideal if available. After isolation, keep dry until curing/setting complete.
-   **Pulp exposure unrecognized:** Probe reveals red bleeding spot = pulp exposure. Do not ignore; address immediately (temporary seal). Assess exposure size (small pinpoint <1mm better prognosis than large). Calcium hydroxide directly on exposed pulp may promote healing; zinc oxide eugenol temporary seal acceptable if calcium hydroxide unavailable.
:::

:::info-box
**Material Failure Progression:**
Materials fail in predictable sequence depending on stress, technique, location. Early warning signs: margin discoloration (microleakage starting), patient sensitivity (enamel/dentin exposure), visible wear at contact areas. Monitor restorations at follow-up. Early intervention (polishing, refacing, replacement) prevents total failure + secondary decay. Replacement easier than managing failed restoration complications (abscess, extraction need).
:::

:::danger
### Critical Procedural Errors to Avoid

-   **Exceeding anesthetic maximum dose:** Patient <1.5 years old receiving adult-dose anesthetic = seizure/cardiac toxicity risk. Always calculate weight-based dose. Error: using predetermined syringe volume (e.g., "one carpule = one dose") without weight consideration. Calculate every patient individually.
-   **Irrigating socket with hydrogen peroxide excessively:** While H2O2 helpful for hemostasis + debris removal, excessive rinsing = clot disruption, dry socket risk. Limit H2O2 to 1-2 minutes. Gentle normal saline rinse preferred subsequently.
-   **Packing infected socket without drainage:** Abscess present = must drain first (incision/evacuation), then pack. Packing over infection = increased pressure, spreading abscess, systemic symptoms (fever, lymphadenopathy). Establish drainage before packing.
-   **Extracting upper anterior tooth with excessive force:** Anterior roots thin, curved; excessive force = root fracture. Use gentle rotation/luxation first, traction only when truly mobile. If resistance high, return to luxation, repeat cycles. Patience prevents apex fracture.
:::
