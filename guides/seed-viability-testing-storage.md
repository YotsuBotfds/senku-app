Seeds are the foundation of food security. Understanding how to test seed viability, store seeds long-term, and save your own seed ensures you can grow food reliably year after year, whether from your own harvest or purchased stock.

For crop selection, isolation, and deciding which parent plants should produce the next generation, route to [Plant Breeding & Seed Saving](plant-breeding-seed-saving.html). This guide stays focused on testing, storage, and viability checks.

<section id="seed-structure">

## Seed Anatomy and Germination Basics

### Seed Parts

A mature seed contains:

- **Embryo:** The plant itself — dormant during storage, awakens when conditions are right
- **Endosperm:** Energy reserves (carbohydrates, proteins, fats) feeding the growing seedling until it's photosynthetically active
- **Testa (seed coat):** Protective outer layer; regulates water uptake, prevents premature germination
- **Hilum:** Scar where seed attached to fruit; water entry point

### Germination Phases

1. **Imbibition:** Seed absorbs water; metabolic activity awakens
2. **Enzyme activation:** Stored enzymes mobilize starches and proteins into usable sugars and amino acids
3. **Radicle emergence:** Root appears first (usually 2–7 days depending on species)
4. **Seedling growth:** Shoot emerges; first leaves unfold; photosynthesis begins

<section id="viability-testing">

## Viability Testing: Germination Tests

Before sowing saved or old seeds, test a sample to determine germination rate.

### Standard Germination Test (Paper Towel Method)

This simple test works for most vegetable seeds.

**Procedure:**

1. **Prepare test bed:** Place damp (not soggy) paper towels on a plate or shallow tray
2. **Sample seeds:** Count exactly 100 seeds; spread evenly on towel
3. **Cover and label:** Place another damp paper towel over seeds; label with variety and date
4. **Optimal conditions:** Keep at species-appropriate temperature:
   - Most vegetable seeds: 68–75°F (20–24°C)
   - Tomatoes, peppers: 75–85°F (24–29°C)
   - Cool-season crops (lettuce, spinach, broccoli): 60–68°F (15–20°C)
5. **Check daily:** Keep towel moist; do NOT flood (oxygen needed)
6. **Count germinated seeds:** When radicle is visible and ≥2 mm long, count it as germinated
7. **Stop test:** When no new germinations occur for 3–5 days (varies by crop)

**Interpretation:**
- **95–100% germination:** Excellent; sow at normal density
- **85–95%:** Good; thin slightly less or oversow slightly
- **70–85%:** Acceptable; oversow to compensate
- **50–70%:** Poor; oversow heavily or don't use
- **<50%:** Reject; plant fresh seed instead

**Timing (approximate, varies by species):**

| Crop | Days to Count |
|---|---|
| Squash, beans, peas | 3–5 |
| Tomatoes, peppers | 7–10 |
| Brassicas (cabbage, kale) | 5–7 |
| Lettuce, spinach | 3–5 |
| Root crops (carrot, parsnip, beet) | 7–14 |

:::tip
Always test questionable seed before investing labor in sowing. A simple germination test takes 10 minutes of setup and daily 30-second checks over a week.
:::

### Tetrazolium Test (Advanced)

For seeds too small or hard to see radicle emergence, or when immediate results are needed:

1. Soak seeds 18–24 hours in water
2. Cut seeds in half lengthwise; expose embryo
3. Immerse in 1% tetrazolium solution (red dye) for 30 minutes
4. Viable embryos stain red; dead embryos remain white
5. Count viable vs dead

(Less practical for home gardeners; more common in seed certification labs.)

## Seed Storage Conditions

Proper storage maintains viability for years or decades.

### The "Big Four" Storage Factors

**Temperature:**
- Cool is better than warm
- Each 18°F (10°C) drop roughly halves aging rate
- Ideal: 32–41°F (0–5°C) — refrigerator or root cellar
- Acceptable: 50–68°F (10–20°C) — cool closet or basement
- Avoid: >75°F (24°C) — accelerates deterioration

**Moisture:**
- Too wet: encourages mold and premature germination
- Too dry: prevents deterioration but seeds need some moisture (0–5% relative humidity is too dry; 40–50% is optimal)
- Ideal: 40–50% relative humidity (RH) in cool space
- Dry climate: seeds naturally at low RH; store as-is
- Humid climate: use desiccants to lower RH

**Light:**
- Darkness is slightly better than light
- Light can stimulate germination in some species or cause seed coat damage
- Keep seeds in opaque containers

**Oxygen:**
- Low oxygen slows aging
- Vacuum-sealed containers or oxygen absorbers extend storage life significantly

### The "Cool Dry Dark" Rule

Store seeds at:
- **Temperature:** 40–50°F (4–10°C) ideally, or 55–65°F (13–18°C) acceptably
- **Humidity:** 30–50% relative humidity
- **Location:** Dark closet, basement, root cellar, or refrigerator/freezer
- **Container:** Paper envelope inside airtight jar with desiccant, or vacuum-sealed bag

### Desiccants

Use desiccants to lower humidity in storage containers.

**Options:**

- **Silica gel packets:** Most common; reusable (dry in oven if saturated). 1–2 packets per quart jar.
- **Powdered milk:** Food-safe alternative; 1–2 tbsp per quart jar. Absorbs moisture; must be replaced annually.
- **Uncooked rice:** Mix with seeds in 1:10 ratio; absorbs moisture slowly. Must be replaced yearly.
- **Calcium chloride:** Highly absorbent; use sparingly. Messy if it liquifies.

**Recharging silica gel:**
- Place used (saturated) packets in 200°F (93°C) oven for 30 minutes
- Cool completely before reusing
- If color indicator shows saturation (blue → pink or other color change), recharge

:::warning
If desiccant liquifies or clumps significantly, remove it and replace. Wet desiccant can rot seeds or cause premature germination.
:::

### Storage Containers

- **Airtight glass jars:** Best; preserve moisture and protect from light
- **Vacuum-sealed bags:** Excellent for long-term; remove air before sealing
- **Paper envelopes in jar:** Economical; seeds in envelope (paper breathes), jar in airtight container with desiccant
- **Avoid:** Cardboard boxes, breathable cloth (not airtight)

### Freezer Storage (Long-Term)

Freezing extends seed life dramatically.

- **Technique:** Place dried seeds (moisture <5%) in airtight jar with desiccant; seal; freeze at 0°F (−18°C) or colder
- **Longevity:** Many seeds viable 10–20+ years when frozen
- **Important:** Do NOT open frozen container in warm room (condensation damages seeds). Remove only needed amount when container reaches room temperature, or retrieve seeds while still cold
- **Best for:** Small quantities of precious seed, heirloom varieties, long-term backup stock

<section id="seed-longevity">

## Seed Longevity by Crop

Longevity varies widely depending on species, storage conditions, and initial viability.

### Long-Lived Seeds (5–10+ years if stored well)

| Crop | Years | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Beans, peas | 3–5 | Legume seeds; relatively hardy |
| Brassicas (cabbage, broccoli, kale) | 4–6 | Store well if dry; cool conditions |
| Cucurbits (squash, cucumber, melon) | 5–10 | Among longest-lived; excellent storage |
| Tomatoes | 4–6 | Acidic seeds; store well in cool, dry |
| Lettuce, spinach | 3–5 | Small seeds; store well |
| Root crops (carrot, parsnip) | 3–5 | Carrot longest (~5 yrs); parsnip shorter (~2 yrs) |

### Medium-Lived Seeds (2–4 years)

- Herbs (parsley, dill, cilantro): 2–3 years
- Alliums (onion, leek, chive): 1–3 years
- Beets: 3–5 years

### Short-Lived Seeds (1–2 years)

- Peppers: 2–3 years if very dry; 1 year if humidity not controlled
- Sweet corn: 1–2 years (dries out quickly)
- Parsnip: 1–2 years (very oil-rich; quickly rancid)
- Okra: 2–3 years

:::info-box
Seeds stored in warm, humid conditions lose viability 5–10 times faster than those stored cool and dry. A one-year-old seed in room temperature might be as viable as five-year-old seed in a freezer.
:::

### Testing Longevity of Your Seeds

Track germination rates annually for seeds you save:
- Year of harvest: test 25 seeds; note germination %
- Year 1, 2, 3 (storage): test 25 seeds same way; track decline
- Plan replacement: when germination drops below 70%, order fresh seed

<section id="dormancy-breaking">

## Dormancy and Germination Requirements

Some seeds have dormancy mechanisms that prevent germination until conditions are right.

### Types of Dormancy

**Physical dormancy (hard seed coat):**
- Examples: Beans, squash, lupine
- Solution: Scarify (scratch) seed coat or soak in hot water
- Techniques:
  - **Scarification:** Rub seed on sandpaper or cut coat slightly (don't damage embryo)
  - **Hot water soak:** Pour boiling water over seeds; let cool 24 hours; sow
  - **Acid scarification:** Soak in sulfuric acid (professionals only; dangerous)

**Chemical dormancy (germination inhibitors in seed coat):**
- Examples: Tomatoes, peppers
- Solution: Leaching — soak in water repeatedly, changing water daily for 3–5 days
- Or: Fermentation (soak 2–3 weeks in water; beneficial microbes break down inhibitors)

**Cold dormancy (stratification required):**
- Examples: Many perennials, some vegetables (carrots, parsnips, spinach)
- Solution: Expose to cold, moist conditions for weeks to months
- Techniques:
  - **Moist chilling:** Layer seeds between moist sand/paper towels in refrigerator for 4–8 weeks
  - **Outdoor stratification:** Sow in fall; natural winter cold does the work; seedlings emerge in spring
  - **Duration:** Varies; 4–12 weeks typical

**Light requirement (photoblastism):**
- Examples: Lettuce, some wildflowers
- Solution: Sow on surface (don't cover); provide light
- Or: Store seed cold first (stratification often reverses light requirement)

### Common Seed Treatments

| Treatment | Seeds | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Soak in water | Beans, squash, sweet corn | 4–8 hours |
| Hot water soak | Hard-coated seeds | 30 min; cool 24 hrs |
| Scarify | Beans, squash, morning glory | Before sowing |
| Cold stratification (moist) | Perennials, carrots, spinach | 4–8 weeks in fridge |
| Leaching | Tomatoes, peppers | 3–5 water changes |
| Light exposure | Lettuce, petunia | Sow on surface |

## Saving Your Own Seed

Seed saving ensures long-term independence and preserves locally adapted genetics.

If you are choosing parent plants, managing selection pressure, or protecting variety purity across generations, use [Plant Breeding & Seed Saving](plant-breeding-seed-saving.html) alongside this guide.

### Isolation Distances (Preventing Cross-Pollination)

Different crops require different distances between varieties to maintain genetic purity.

**Self-pollinating (minimal isolation needed):**
- Tomatoes, beans, peas, lettuce: <3 feet distance
- Rarely cross-pollinate naturally

**Insect-pollinated (require more distance):**

| Crop | Distance |
|---|---|
| Brassicas (cabbage, kale, broccoli) | 800–1000 feet or hand-pollinate |
| Cucurbits (squash, melon, cucumber) | 500–1000 feet |
| Carrot, parsnip (insect-pollinated flowers) | 1000–1500 feet |
| Onion/leek | 800–1000 feet |
| Herbs (cilantro, parsley, dill) | 500–1000 feet |

**Wind-pollinated (maximum distance):**
- Corn: 1000–5000 feet (depending on variety sensitivity)
- Spinach: 1000 feet (wind-pollinated; cross-pollination common)

:::warning
If you don't have adequate isolation distance, hand-pollinate (isolate flowers, manually transfer pollen with brush), or accept unplanned crosses. Hybrid seed often produces inferior offspring; open-pollinated varieties breed true if isolated.
:::

### Harvesting and Drying

1. **Allow full maturity:** Seeds must be fully developed before harvesting
   - Pods dry on plant, turn brown/papery
   - Seeds rattle in pod
   - Flower structures wither
2. **Dry thoroughly:** Spread seeds in single layer on screen or cloth in warm, dry location
   - Duration: 1–3 weeks depending on size and humidity
   - Seeds should snap/shatter if bent (moisture <8%)
3. **Clean:** Remove pod chaff, stem debris, diseased seed
   - Simple: fan or blow gently to separate light chaff
   - More thorough: screen or sieve
4. **Store:** Follow storage guidelines above (cool, dry, dark, airtight)

### Seed Saving by Crop (Examples)

**Tomatoes (self-pollinating):**
1. Allow fruit to fully ripen on plant; collect when soft
2. Ferment seeds (dissolve gel): soak in water 2–3 weeks; stir daily; let settle
3. Rinse thoroughly (multiple water changes)
4. Dry on cloth/screen indoors, 2–3 weeks
5. Store cool and dry

**Squash (insect-pollinated, needs isolation):**
1. Hand-pollinate if isolating, or rely on insects if isolated >500 ft
2. Allow fruit to mature fully on plant; harvest when skin is hard
3. Cure fruit in warm (70–80°F), dry location for 2–4 weeks
4. Extract seeds; clean thoroughly
5. Dry 2–3 weeks; store

**Beans (self-pollinating):**
1. Allow pods to dry completely on plant; turn brown/papery
2. Pick pods; store in cool, dry place for 1–2 weeks
3. Shell out dried beans
4. Store in cool, dry, dark container

## Building a Seed Bank

A home seed bank ensures food security during interruptions (weather, supply chain, economic stress). For vault construction, container options, organized shelving layouts, and community/regional backup strategies, see [Seed Vault & Genetic Preservation](seed-vault.html).

### Organizing Your Bank

**Categories:**
- **Essential:** Staple crops you eat regularly and can grow successfully (tomatoes, beans, squash, lettuce, root crops)
- **Nutritional diversity:** Different plant families (legumes, brassicas, alliums, roots, greens)
- **Backup:** 2–3 years of seed for each variety you plan to grow

**Storage conditions:**
- **Short-term (1–2 years):** Cool closet (55–65°F), in labeled envelopes
- **Medium-term (2–5 years):** Refrigerator or root cellar, airtight jars with desiccant
- **Long-term (5+ years):** Freezer, vacuum-sealed or in airtight jar with desiccant

### Seed Bank Checklist

**Vegetables (basic variety — roughly 20 packets):**
- [ ] Tomato (2–3 varieties: slicing, paste, cherry)
- [ ] Lettuce (2 varieties: head and loose-leaf)
- [ ] Spinach (1)
- [ ] Beans (2–3: snap, dry, pole)
- [ ] Peas (1–2: snap and snow)
- [ ] Squash (2–3: summer and winter types)
- [ ] Cucumber or melon (1)
- [ ] Carrots (1)
- [ ] Beets (1)
- [ ] Onion or garlic (sets, not seed)
- [ ] Brassicas: cabbage or kale (1–2)
- [ ] Herbs: basil, dill, parsley (3)
- [ ] Peppers (1–2 if climate permits)

**Nutritional priorities:**
- Protein: beans, peas, soybeans
- Calories: potatoes, squash, root crops
- Vitamins/minerals: dark leafy greens, brassicas, root crops
- Fats: sunflower, safflower (if climate allows)

### Inventory and Rotation

**Annual review:**
1. List all seeds in storage
2. Test germination of any seed >2 years old
3. Note dates; identify oldest seed first
4. Use oldest seed in season; order replacements

<section id="troubleshooting">

## Troubleshooting Germination Failure

If seeds won't germinate, diagnose the problem:

| Problem | Likely Cause | Solution |
|---|---|---|
| No germination after 2 weeks | Dead seed, poor storage, wrong temperature | Test germination; check temperature |
| Slow germination (>3 weeks) | Too cold, seed coat too hard, dormancy not broken | Warm up; scarify or stratify |
| Seedlings rot before emergence | Soil too wet, fungal infection (damping off) | Use sterile soil; improve drainage; ventilate |
| Few seeds germinate (50% or less) | Old seed, poor storage conditions, low viability | Test fresh seed; discard old stock |
| Seeds germinate but seedlings yellow/weak | Poor light, nutrient deficiency, soil pH wrong | Provide bright light; check soil; add fertilizer if needed |

<section id="conclusion">

## Conclusion

Testing and storing seeds properly ensures reliable harvests and long-term food security. Simple germination tests reveal viability before you invest labor. Cool, dry storage (and freezing for long-term) preserves seed for years. Saving your own seed from successful plants closes the loop, creating resilience and independence. Start with a small seed bank of staple crops; add to it annually as you harvest seed and purchase reliable varieties.
