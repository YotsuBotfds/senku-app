:::danger
Swamps and wetlands are among Earth's most challenging survival environments. Hypothermia, bog entrapment, disease, and insect-borne illness claim more lives than direct trauma. This guide prioritizes systematic hazard management over rapid movement.
:::

<section id="overview">

## Overview: Wetland Terrain Types

Swamps, marshes, and bogs are distinct ecosystems with different survival considerations:

**Swamp (freshwater or brackish):** Standing water (0–2m), trees (cypress, mangrove, tupelo), root tangles, slow current. Mobility reduced to 1–2 km/h. Hypothermia risk if water temp <15°C.

**Marsh:** Shallow water (<1m), grasses dominate, no large trees, open, water visible. Navigation clearer but exposure greater. Insects severe.

**Bog:** Peat substrate (accumulated dead plants), acidic water, sphagnum moss, stunted conifers or deciduous. Extremely spongy and unstable. Sinking hazard high.

**Tidal flats:** Salt marshes near coast, exposed on low tide, flooded on high tide. Navigation window limited.

Common stressors across all types:
- **Disorientation** — featureless terrain, no line of sight
- **Hypothermia** — immersion in cool water, evaporative cooling
- **Disease** — contaminated water, parasites, fungi
- **Insects** — mosquitoes (malaria, dengue), leeches, biting flies
- **Entrapment** — quicksand, bog, root tangles

<section id="navigation">

## Navigation in Featureless Wetlands

### Bearings and Landmarks

Without landmarks, wetland navigation relies on:

1. **Compass bearings** — Set initial heading before entering. Use handheld compass, pre-mark waypoint. Re-check every 500m.
2. **Sun/star position** — If compass lost, use sun (rises E, sets W) or North Star (Polaris in Northern Hemisphere).
3. **Wind direction** — Consistent if not using other methods. Document at entry point.
4. **Water flow** — Rivers exit swamp in drainage direction. Follow current if disoriented.
5. **Topography memory** — Swamps often have subtle ridges or slightly higher ground. Track compass heading + time walked to estimate distance.

### Dead Reckoning

Count footsteps or time walked, multiply by average ground speed:
- **Solid ground:** 4–5 km/h
- **Shallow water (<0.5m):** 2–3 km/h
- **Deep water with trees:** 1–1.5 km/h
- **Dense bog:** 0.5–1 km/h

Example: Walked 45 minutes at estimated 2 km/h = 1.5 km traveled.

### Rope Navigation

In zero-visibility conditions (fog, night):
1. String a rope or long vine across hazardous section.
2. One person holds far end, other follows, hand over hand.
3. Prevents loops and disorientation.

### High-Ground Strategy

Wetlands rarely feature cliffs, but subtle rises (old ridges, natural levees) exist:
- Probe ahead with pole before committing weight.
- Move toward any perceived elevation.
- Travel along edges (dry land adjacent to wetland) when possible.

## Quicksand and Bog Rescue

### Recognizing Quicksand

True quicksand is rare in temperate zones but common in coastal/deserts. Signs:
- **Liquefaction zone:** Water-saturated sand, darker than surrounding area
- **Slow sinking:** You sink at 5–15 cm per step despite normal weight
- **Loss of footing:** Feet find no solid support

### Quicksand Escape Technique

1. **Stop immediately** — Thrashing accelerates sinking.
2. **Lean back** — Distribute weight horizontally. Lie down if necessary.
3. **Move slowly** — Gently "swim" or roll parallel to surface. Takes 30–60 sec per meter.
4. **Compress legs** — Don't bend knees sharply. Extend legs backward under you.
5. **Reach solid ground** — If pulled, rescuer leans back at 45° angle, not straight up.

### Bog (Peat) Entrapment

Bog is less dramatic but more fatal than quicksand. Characteristics:
- **Sphagnum moss** overlays peat (dead plant matter and water).
- **Deceptive firmness** — Surface looks walkable; you sink 1–2m.
- **Suction** — Peat clings; escaping requires enormous force.

**Bog Escape:**
1. **Do not panic** — Sinking is slow (unless you thrash).
2. **Distribute weight** — Lie back, spread legs and arms wide.
3. **Widen escape hole** — Press laterally to enlarge the depression you're sinking into.
4. **Use objects** — Drag logs, branches, or your pack under you as flotation.
5. **Roll toward firmness** — Inch toward higher/drier ground.
6. **Call for help early** — Bystanders can form human chain.

**Rescue assist:**
- Do NOT reach down and pull (dangerous to rescuer, sucks victim deeper).
- Use long pole (3–4m), log, or rope. Spread rescuer's weight wide.

<div class="hazard-box">
**Mud vs. Bog:** Mud (silt + water) has poor suction and allows relatively easy escape. Bog (peat + water) has extreme suction. Always treat solid-looking bog with suspicion.
</div>

<section id="disease-hygiene">

## Waterborne Disease & Hygiene

### Diseases in Wetland Water

| Disease | Agent | Symptoms | Prevention |
|---------|-------|----------|-----------|
| **Malaria** | Plasmodium (mosquito) | Fever, chills, headache (3–30 days onset) | Insect repellent, netting, antimalarials if available |
| **Dengue** | Virus (mosquito) | Fever, rash, joint pain (3–14 days) | Insect repellent, cover skin |
| **Schistosomiasis** | Parasitic worm | Rash, itching, fever, diarrhea (weeks later) | Avoid entering untreated water; if exposed, salt bath helps |
| **Leptospirosis** | Spirochete bacteria | Fever, myalgia, jaundice (2–30 days) | Avoid urine-contaminated water; cover cuts |
| **Giardiasis** | Giardia protozoa | Diarrhea, cramps, bloating (1–3 weeks) | Boil or filter water; avoid beaver/muskrat areas |
| **Cryptosporidium** | Protozoa | Watery diarrhea (2–10 days) | Boil; standard filters may not catch oocysts |

### Hygiene Practices

- **Do not drink untreated water** — Boil 1 min (3 min at >2000m elevation).
- **Soap and clean water** — Wash hands, genitals, and armpits daily if possible.
- **Avoid insect bites** — Long sleeves/pants dusk-dawn; insect repellent (DEET, eucalyptus oil).
- **Wound care** — Keep any cuts clean and covered; fungi thrive in wet environments.
- **Dry shelter** — Sleep elevated above water/damp ground to prevent fungal skin infections.

<section id="insect-protection">

## Insect Protection

Wetlands host mosquitoes, biting flies, and leeches. Bites transmit disease and infection.

### Repellents and Barriers

**Chemical repellents:**
- **DEET (N,N-Diethyl-meta-toluamide):** 20–30% concentration lasts 4–6 hours. Apply to exposed skin, not under clothing.
- **Picaridin:** 10% lasts 8 hours. Similar efficacy to DEET.
- **Plant oils:** Eucalyptus, citronella, lemongrass, peppermint. Reapply every 2–3 hours. Less effective than synthetic but lower toxicity.

**Physical barriers:**
- **Head net:** Lightweight, covers face and neck. Essential in swarms.
- **Long sleeves/pants:** Tuck into socks. White/light colors allow spotting insects.
- **Gloves:** Protects hands and wrists.

**Timing:**
- **Peak biting:** Dawn (5–7 AM) and dusk (6–8 PM). Stay inside shelter during these windows if possible.

### Leech Management

Leeches are aquatic worms that attach and feed on blood. Common in tropical/temperate swamps.

**Prevention:**
- Inspect skin/clothing every 30 min when wading.
- Salt or tobacco on skin discourages attachment.
- Boots and long pants.

**Removal (if attached):**
1. **Do NOT pull** — Mouth parts detach and cause infection.
2. **Scrape off** — Use fingernail or smooth object under body, flick off.
3. **Salt/heat** — Apply salt directly or heat with match/cigarette (leech releases in seconds).
4. **Soap water** — Slippery leeches release after 30 sec exposure.
5. **Wash wound** — Use soap and clean water; monitor for infection.

<section id="water-purification">

## Water Purification in Tannic Swamps

Swamp water is often stained brown/amber by tannins (from decaying vegetation). High tannin content does NOT mean the water is safe—it masks other contaminants.

### Purification Methods

**Boiling:**
- Kills parasites and bacteria reliably.
- Does not remove tannins or chemical contaminants.
- Requires fuel (limited in swamps).
- Minimum 1 minute at sea level.

**Filtration:**
- Use sand/charcoal layers in cloth or container to reduce particulates and tannins.
- Helps clarity but does NOT reliably remove pathogens.
- Follow with boiling for safety.

**Solar disinfection (SODIS):**
1. Fill clear bottle with swamp water.
2. Leave in sunlight 6–8 hours minimum.
3. UV radiation kills bacteria; oxidation by tannins assists.
4. Less effective in cloudy conditions.

**Improvised charcoal filter:**
1. Burn wood until coals remain (not ash).
2. Crush coals into small pieces (1 cm).
3. Layer in cloth: coarse sand → charcoal → fine sand → coarse sand.
4. Pour swamp water through filter 2–3 times.
5. Boil filtered water before drinking.

### Tannin-Specific Considerations

High tannins cause:
- Astringent taste (not dangerous, merely unpleasant).
- Brown staining of lips/teeth (temporary).
- Mild laxative effect in large quantities.

Tannins are NOT pathogens and do NOT preclude drinking if other contaminants removed. The brown color can disguise debris—filter first.

## Shelter on Saturated Ground

Dry land is scarce in swamps. Shelter must account for rising water, insects, and cold.

### Elevated Platform

**Simple pole frame:**
1. Identify two trees 2–3m apart.
2. Lash horizontal poles (logs 10–15 cm diameter) between trees at chest height.
3. Layer smaller branches (2–5 cm) perpendicular, creating deck.
4. Cover with leaves, bark, or cloth for insulation from damp air.
5. Raises sleeper 1.5–2m above ground and insects.

**Time:** 2–3 hours with machete and rope.

### Debris Hut Variant

If no suitable trees:
1. Build frame on driest patch (usually slight rise).
2. Insulate base with dry leaves, grass, or branches to break contact with wet ground.
3. Stack debris around sides, leaving entrance on leeward side.
4. Ventilation opening opposite entrance prevents condensation.

### Insulation Essentials

- **No direct contact with damp ground** — Insulating barrier critical.
- **Leave floor vents** — Stagnant air causes hypothermia.
- **Keep dry materials inside** — Gather and stack dead wood, leaves before dark.

## Food Sources

Wetlands provide abundant calories if you know what to eat.

### Cattails (Typha)

Found in nearly all temperate swamps. Entire plant is edible:

- **Rhizome (root):** Starchy, potato-like. Dig in fall (higher starch content). Boil or roast 20 min. 1 kg yields ~150 kcal.
- **Shoots:** Tender, asparagus-like in spring. Eat raw or boiled.
- **Pollen:** Collect in early summer when male flower head sheds pollen. Protein-rich. Mix into flatbread.
- **Corms (base bulbs):** Harvest in fall, boil 30 min.

**Identification:** Tall grass-like plant (1–3m), distinctive brown cylindrical seed head. No poisonous lookalikes.

### Crayfish and Freshwater Crawfish

Found in swamp waterways. High protein, low effort:

1. **Trapping:** Place baited cage trap in water (bait: fish scraps, carrion). Check daily. Yield: 3–8 crayfish per trap per day.
2. **Hand catching:** In shallow water, flip rocks and grab quickly. Pinch comes from small claws—no danger.
3. **Cooking:** Boil 5 min. Meat is in tail abdomen. Remove hepatopancreas (yellow/green gland) if desired (edible but may contain contaminants).

**Calories:** One crayfish ≈ 5–10 kcal. 20 crayfish = day's meal base.

### Frogs

Abundant in wetlands. Legs are the edible part:

1. **Hunting:** Nighttime with flashlight. Frogs freeze in light. Catch with net or quick hand grab.
2. **Preparation:** Sever head, remove skin, cut off legs at hip joint.
3. **Cooking:** Boil, fry, or roast 10 min.

**Calories:** Two frog legs ≈ 15 kcal. Catch 20–30 frogs for substantial meal.

### Water Chestnuts and Lotus Rhizomes

In Asian/tropical swamps (rare in temperate):

- **Water chestnuts:** Baseball-sized aquatic vegetable. Peel and eat raw or boil.
- **Lotus rhizomes:** Starchy, potato-like. Dig in fall.

### Safety

- **Avoid brackish/salt swamps for food** — Shellfish and fish may contain algal toxins.
- **Test unfamiliar plants** — Swamp plants include toxic species (water hemlock, poison sago, arrow arum). When in doubt, stick to known species.

</section>

## Fire in Wet Conditions

Fuel is damp and abundant. Fire is difficult but essential for warmth and water purification.

### Tinder

Hard to find:

- **Dry dead wood:** Strip outer wet layer to expose dry interior. Split wood reveals driest parts.
- **Bark strips:** Inner bark from birch, cedar, or pine is tinder-like. Scrape white inner layer.
- **Reeds/cattail down:** Gather fluffy seed heads; they burn hot but brief.
- **Cotton or cloth:** Char cloth (cotton boiled in saltpeter) is ideal; carry some.

### Kindling and Fuel

- **Dead branches:** Collect smallest (pencil-thin) and stack in teepee.
- **Softwood:** Pine, spruce, cedar catch quickly.
- **Wet wood:** Split into thin pieces; the inner wood is drier and catches once small flame established.

### Fire-Starting in Damp

1. **Prepare bed** — Raise fire on log or rocks to avoid ground moisture.
2. **Feather splits** — Use knife to shave thin curls on stick (not separating). Creates large surface area for ignition.
3. **Use tinder bundle** — Dry leaves, bark, or cloth compressed tightly.
4. **Protect from wind** — Arrange large logs to windbreak. Wind evaporates precious moisture.
5. **Gradual fuel addition** — Start with thinnest kindling, increase thickness as flames build.

### Maintenance

Once established:
- **Feed constantly** — Wet fuel requires more heat to ignite.
- **Elevate fuel** — Keep wood off damp ground.
- **Use resinous wood** — Pitch-heavy softwoods burn hotter and wetter.
- **Dry wood by fire** — Lay wet branches near flame to dry before burning.

**Time estimate:** 30–60 min to establish sustainable fire in wet conditions.

</section>

<section id="emergency-signals">

## Emergency Signaling in Wetlands

Rescue in swamps is possible but difficult. Make location clear:

- **Smoke signal:** Green branches on established fire create white smoke (day), visible 5+ km.
- **Mirrors:** Any reflective surface flashed skyward during daylight.
- **Three fires:** Traditional distress signal. Arrange in triangle 50m apart, ignite simultaneously.
- **Whistles:** Three short blasts. Sound travels well over water.
- **Bright fabric:** Tie to highest branch; orange or yellow most visible.
