:::danger
**Medical Disclaimer:** Animal bites carry high infection risk and potential rabies exposure. Rabies is nearly 100% fatal once clinical symptoms appear. Post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) is time-critical. When public health resources are available, use them. When unavailable, this guide provides austere-setting protocols for wound management, infection prevention, and rabies risk assessment.
:::

<section id="overview">

## Overview: Animal Bites as Medical Emergencies

Animal bite wounds are deceptively dangerous. The visible injury often underestimates the damage:
- **Puncture wounds** trap bacteria deep in tissue, creating anaerobic environments
- **Crush injuries** from teeth destroy tissue viability and vascular planes
- **Saliva contamination** introduces normal flora that become pathogenic in closed wounds
- **Rabies transmission** occurs through saliva and can develop days or months later

Infection rates: **5–50%** depending on wound type and care. Mortality from untreated bite-wound infection rivals rabies (when infection disseminates to bloodstream).

This guide focuses on **immediate wound assessment and care**, **infection prevention through antibiotics**, **rabies risk stratification**, and **austere-setting post-exposure prophylaxis protocols**.

</section>

<section id="wound-assessment">

## Wound Assessment by Animal Type

### Domestic Dog Bites

**Epidemiology:** Most common bite. ~80% of dog bites are from known animals.

**Wound characteristics:**
- Laceration, crush, or puncture depending on bite depth
- Relatively low infection risk if properly cleaned (5–10%)
- Rabies risk: <1% in vaccinated domestic dogs (check vaccination status if possible)

**Assessment approach:**
- Inspect for depth (does it penetrate subcutaneous tissue?)
- Assess for structural damage (joint spaces, tendons, vessels)
- Hand bites from dogs carry higher infection risk than extremity bites (proximity to vital structures)

### Cat Bites

**Epidemiology:** Puncture wounds predominate (claws + teeth); higher infection rate than dog bites.

**Wound characteristics:**
- Sharp claws create deep punctures with small external openings
- Bacteria (especially Pasteurella multocida) driven deep into tissue
- Infection rate: **30–50%** even with moderate cleaning
- Often present late (2–3 days post-bite) after infection is established

**Assessment approach:**
- Consider cat bites as "infected until proven otherwise"
- Even small punctures over joints/tendons require careful evaluation
- Check for spread of erythema, lymphangitis (red streaking), regional lymphadenopathy

### Wild Carnivore Bites (Fox, Raccoon, Badger, Wolverine)

**Rabies risk: VERY HIGH.** Consider all wild carnivore bites as rabies-exposed until proven otherwise.

**Wound characteristics:**
- Often multiple bite wounds from aggressive animals
- May be heavily contaminated with soil/environmental organisms
- High risk of polymicrobial infection

**Assessment approach:**
- Detailed documentation of incident (when, where, circumstances)
- Trace any visible saliva contamination
- Assume rabies exposure; initiate PEP protocols immediately

### Snake Bites

**Venom vs. non-venom differentiation:**
- Non-venomous bites: Punctures from non-specialized fangs; low systemic toxicity but infection risk present
- Venomous bites: Coagulopathy, hemolysis, tissue necrosis

**Assessment approach:**
- Check for fang marks (non-venomous have small punctures; venomous usually have 2 larger marks ~0.5–1 cm apart)
- Observe for coagulopathy (spontaneous bleeding, oozing that won't stop)
- Watch for progressive edema (venomous spreads rapidly; non-venomous stays localized)
- **Rabies risk from snakes: negligible** (transmission requires saliva in wound; extremely rare)

**Dry bite incidence:** ~20% of snake bites are dry (no venom injected). Observation period 24 hrs can distinguish dry from envenomed.

### Human Bites

**Highest infection risk.** Human oral flora is heavily anaerobic; wounds are easily contaminated but difficult to clean (saliva penetrates poorly-vascularized tissue).

**Wound characteristics:**
- Often bite to hand (fist fight; altercation)
- May be overlooked as "minor" puncture
- Infection rate: **10–30%**

**Assessment approach:**
- Treat all human bites as high-infection-risk
- Hand bites especially dangerous (proximity to joints, tendon sheaths)
- Check for open joint or tooth penetration into synovial space

**Special concern:** Clenched-fist injuries carry extreme risk (hand open when bitten, contamination driven deep, then closed fist traps bacteria)

### Bat Bites

**Rabies risk: EXTREME.** Bats are the leading cause of rabies in developed countries.

**Unique concerns:**
- Bite marks may be microscopic (barely visible)
- Patients often unaware they were bitten (sleeping exposure)
- **Assume any bat contact = rabies exposure until proven otherwise**

**Assessment approach:**
- Even if no visible bite, assume exposure
- Initiate PEP protocols immediately
- Document contact circumstances (where did bat come from? Was it active or lethargic?)

<section id="immediate-care">

## Immediate Wound Care: First 24 Hours

### Irrigation & Debridement

**Critical principle:** Vigorous irrigation and mechanical removal of devitalized tissue reduces infection risk by ~50%.

**Technique:**
1. **Anesthesia:** Local infiltration (lidocaine 1%) or regional block if needed for patient comfort
2. **Irrigation:** Use high-pressure stream (18 mL syringe + 18-gauge needle or splash irrigation from bottle)
   - **Volume:** 500 mL–1 L for deep puncture wounds; adjust based on contamination
   - **Solution:** Normal saline preferred; potable water acceptable if saline unavailable
   - **Temperature:** Warm solution preferred (less tissue irritation)
3. **Mechanical debridement:**
   - Use sterile brush or abrasive gauze to scrub visible contamination
   - For punctures: Irrigate the visible opening thoroughly; do not probe or widen the tract in the field
   - Remove any visible foreign material (dirt, fur, saliva-soaked material)
4. **Edge trimming:** Remove devitalized epidermis/dermis at wound margins (thin trim only; preserve skin)

**Special technique for punctures:**
- Puncture wounds are prone to closure over infected tissue
- Keep the wound open with dressing choice, not by cutting or probing it open
- If a puncture is deep, painful, or needs drainage/debridement, that is clinician-level care

### Primary vs. Delayed Closure Decision

<div class="animal-type">

**PRIMARY CLOSURE (same-day closure):**
- **Indications:** Clean wound, <12 hrs old, no heavy contamination, immune-competent patient
- **Process:** Close in layers (subcutaneous if deep; skin with simple or running stitch)
- **Caveat:** Only if you are confident wound is sterile after irrigation and debridement
- **Dog/cat bites:** Consider delayed closure unless very minor and thoroughly cleaned

**DELAYED PRIMARY CLOSURE (3–5 days):**
- **Indications:** Moderately contaminated, puncture component, cat bite, or human bite
- **Process:** Leave open; pack with moist gauze; observe for infection signs × 24–48 hrs; close when confident infection risk is low
- **Rationale:** Allows infection to declare itself before closure traps bacteria

**OPEN HEALING (no closure):**
- **Indications:** Heavily contaminated, deep puncture, anaerobic risk high
- **Process:** Dress daily; allow granulation; close by secondary intention or delayed primary later
- **Rationale:** Maximizes drainage; prevents abscess formation

</div>

### Tetanus Prophylaxis

Check tetanus vaccination status:
- **Vaccination current (≤10 years):** No booster needed
- **Vaccination lapsed (>10 years) or unknown:** Give tetanus toxoid 0.5 mL IM
- **Never vaccinated:** Tetanus toxoid 0.5 mL IM + tetanus immunoglobulin (TIG) 250 units IM (or high-dose human serum if available)

</section>

<section id="antibiotic-prophylaxis">

## Antibiotic Prophylaxis for Bite Wounds

Prophylactic antibiotics reduce infection risk in bite wounds by **50–70%**. Choice depends on animal type and wound characteristics.

### First-Line Regimens

<div class="antibiotic-regimen">

**Dog/Cat Bites (non-severe):**
- **Amoxicillin-clavulanate:** 875 mg PO twice daily × 5–7 days (covers Pasteurella multocida, Staphylococcus, anaerobes)
- **Alternative (penicillin-allergic, non-anaphylaxis):** Doxycycline 100 mg PO twice daily × 5–7 days + Metronidazole 400 mg TID × 5 days
- **Severe allergy (anaphylaxis):** Fluoroquinolone (Ciprofloxacin 500 mg BID) + Clindamycin 300 mg TID × 5–7 days

**Deep Puncture or Moderate Contamination:**
- **Parenteral:** Ceftriaxone 2 g IV daily (or 1 g BID) + Metronidazole 400–500 mg TID × 24–48 hrs; then switch to PO amoxicillin-clavulanate to complete 7 days

**Heavily Contaminated or Immunocompromised:**
- **Parenteral:** Ceftriaxone 2 g IV BID + Metronidazole 500 mg IV TID × 3–5 days; add Gentamicin 1.7 mg/kg IV daily if concern for gram-negative rods (especially Aeromonas from aquatic animal bites)

**Special organisms to cover:**
- **Pasteurella multocida** (cat/dog bites): Sensitive to amoxicillin-clavulanate, doxycycline, fluoroquinolones
- **Eikenella corrodens** (human bites): Resistant to clindamycin; use amoxicillin-clavulanate or fluoroquinolone
- **Anaerobes** (especially Prevotella): Cover with metronidazole or clindamycin
- **Aeromonas** (aquatic animal bites): Gram-negative rod; sensitive to fluoroquinolones, not to amoxicillin

</div>

### Duration of Prophylaxis

- **Minor dog bite (clean, well-irrigated, <12 hrs):** 5 days PO
- **Moderate dog/cat bite:** 7 days (PO or IV→PO)
- **Deep puncture or hand bite:** 7–10 days
- **Heavily contaminated or immunocompromised:** 10–14 days or longer if signs of infection develop

### Duration vs. Treatment

**Clarification:**
- **Prophylaxis** (5–7 days) is given to a patient with no signs of infection
- **Treatment** (10–14 days or longer) is given if infection has developed (fever, spreading erythema, purulent drainage, lymphangitis)

Monitor patient at 48–72 hrs for signs of infection even on prophylaxis. If infection develops, switch to treatment dosing and extend duration.

</section>

## Rabies Risk Assessment & Post-Exposure Prophylaxis

Rabies is nearly 100% fatal once neurologic symptoms appear. Post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) is nearly 100% effective if given before symptom onset. **Time to initiate PEP is critical: optimal window is within 24 hrs; effective up to 1 year if given before exposure becomes symptomatic.**

### Rabies Incubation & Progression

- **Incubation:** 2 weeks – 1 year (average 1–3 months); faster if bite near face/nerve-rich areas
- **Prodrome (2–10 days):** Fever, paresthesia at bite site, anxiety, irritability, insomnia
- **Acute neurologic (1–2 weeks):** Hallucinations, agitation, hydrophobia (fear of water), hypersalivation, muscle spasms, encephalitis → coma → death
- **Progression:** Once symptoms develop, death is nearly certain within 7–10 days

### Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP) Protocol

<div class="rabies-protocol">

**STEP 1: Immediate Wound Care (as above)**
- Vigorous irrigation with saline or water
- Soap and water scrubbing
- Do NOT close wound if heavily contaminated

**STEP 2: Rabies Immunization Series**

**Vaccine options:**
- **HDCV** (human diploid cell vaccine): 1.0 mL IM in deltoid (arm), 5 doses on days 0, 3, 7, 14, 28
- **PCECV** (purified chick embryo cell vaccine): Same schedule; equivalent to HDCV
- **PVRV** (purified Vero cell vaccine): 1.0 mL IM, same schedule (preferred; fewer side effects)
- **In-dermal regimen:** 0.1 mL × 4 sites on day 0, 1 site on day 7 (WHO approved; uses 25% vaccine but requires careful technique)

**Vaccine administration:**
- Deltoid muscle preferred (not gluteus; poor immunogenicity there)
- Multiple sites if available to spread immune response
- **Do NOT give saline diluent IM without vaccine** (old myth of using saline as substitute; ineffective)

**STEP 3: Rabies Immunoglobulin (RIG)**
- Dose: 20 IU/kg body weight
- Timing: Day 0 (same day as first vaccine dose); essential for effectiveness
- Administration: Infiltrate half of calculated dose around wound; give other half IM in gluteus or anterolateral thigh (not same site as vaccine)
- **Do NOT give RIG after day 7 of vaccine series** (will interfere with antibody production)

**If RIG unavailable:**
- In austere collapse scenario, high-titer human serum (from recovered rabies survivor, if available) may substitute (careful: risk of other bloodborne pathogens)
- Otherwise, vaccine series alone is ~80% effective if given before symptoms

**Alternative if no vaccine available:**
- Milwaukee Protocol (induced coma + antivirals): Not feasible in austere settings
- Supportive care only if already symptomatic (palliative measures; outcome unchanged)

</div>

### When to Initiate PEP

**Decision tree:**
1. **Animal captured/observed:** Can be observed for rabies symptoms × 10 days
   - If animal remains healthy 10 days → PEP can be stopped (if already started)
   - If animal dies or becomes sick → PEP must be completed
2. **Animal unknown/wild:** Assume rabid; start PEP immediately
3. **Animal escaped:** Assume rabid; start PEP immediately
4. **Vaccination status of domestic animal known and current:** May observe dog for 10 days instead of immediate PEP (but PEP takes precedence if any doubt)

### PEP Failure Risk Factors

Factors increasing PEP failure (incomplete protection):
- **Delay >24 hrs** to initiation (still effective up to 1 year, but efficacy decreases)
- **Delay >7 days** to RIG administration (vaccine alone less effective)
- **Immunosuppression** (HIV, cancer treatments; may need extended vaccine series)
- **Incorrect vaccine site** (gluteus instead of deltoid; poor immunogenicity)
- **Inadequate wound care** (unirrigated wounds; bacteria/virus remain)

## Special Wound Considerations

### Hand Bites (Dog, Cat, Human)

**Why hands are dangerous:**
- Rich blood supply but poor vascularization in some compartments (potential for compartment syndrome)
- Multiple tendon sheaths and joint spaces (infection can track proximally along tendons)
- Puncture wounds may penetrate synovial space (monoarthritis if infected)

**Management:**
- **Urgent hand surgery evaluation** if available (to assess joint/tendon/nerve injury)
- **Aggressive antibiotic coverage:** Parenteral antibiotics × 24–48 hrs minimum (penetrate hand tissues better than oral)
- **Careful imaging:** X-ray to exclude fracture or tooth fragment; consider ultrasound for tendon integrity
- **Delayed closure:** Do NOT close hand bite on day 1; wait 3–5 days minimum
- **Monitoring:** Watch for swelling (compartment syndrome risk), inability to move fingers (tendon injury), fever (deep infection)

### Crush Injuries & Punctures

**Crush wounds from animal bites:**
- Tissue damage extends beyond visible injury
- High contamination depth (bacteria driven several cm into tissue)
- Anaerobic environment favors Clostridium perfringens (gas gangrene) and other anaerobes

**Management:**
- **Extended antibiotic coverage:** Add Clindamycin 600 mg IV TID to cover anaerobes; consider 10–14 day course
- **Aggressive debridement:** May need serial debridements (day 3–5) as demarcation becomes clear
- **Observation for gas gangrene:** Fever + severe pain + crepitus (within 24–72 hrs) = surgical emergency; urgent debridement
- **Fasciotomy:** Consider if crush is severe (to prevent compartment syndrome)

### Aquatic Animal Bites (Crocodile, Piranha, etc.)

**Special organisms:**
- Aeromonas hydrophila and other gram-negatives
- Vibrio species if saltwater
- Heavy contamination with environmental organisms

**Management:**
- **Antibiotic selection:** Fluoroquinolone (Ciprofloxacin 500 mg BID) preferred (covers Aeromonas, Vibrio)
- **Duration:** 7–10 days minimum
- **Imaging:** Check for embedded teeth, bone involvement
- **Rabies consideration:** Generally low from reptiles, but check local epidemiology

<section id="infection-monitoring">

## Infection Monitoring Timeline

### Days 1–3: Early Infection Signs

**Monitor for:**
- Increasing pain beyond initial injury pain
- Swelling that worsens despite elevation
- Purulent drainage (pus)
- Red streaking toward lymph nodes (lymphangitis)
- Fever (>38.5°C / 101.3°F)
- Regional lymph node enlargement (inguinal for lower extremity, axillary for upper extremity)

**If signs present:**
- Start parenteral antibiotics immediately (upgrade from PO to IV)
- Consider surgical re-exploration if significant swelling or purulence
- Culture drainage if available (guides antibiotic adjustment)

### Days 4–7: Established Infection Risk

**Continue monitoring for:**
- Spreading cellulitis (erythema extending >5 cm from bite site)
- Fluctuance or abscess formation
- Systemic toxicity (fever, tachycardia, hypotension)
- Signs of spread (proximal swelling, new tender areas)

**If abscess suspected:**
- Ultrasound or physical exam for localized collection
- Incision and drainage (I&D) required; antibiotics alone insufficient

### Weeks 2–4: Late Complications

**Potential delayed issues:**
- Tenosynovitis (hand bites): Swelling along tendon sheath, pain with finger extension, fever
- Osteomyelitis (if bone involved): Chronic drainage, exposed bone, persistent fever
- Joint infection: Swelling, pain with motion, fever

**If suspected:**
- Radiographs to assess bone
- Continued antibiotics (may require 4–6 weeks for osteomyelitis)
- Possible surgical debridement or drainage

</section>

## Amputation vs. Salvage Criteria

Some bites damage tissue so severely that amputation is more humane and practical than attempted salvage.

### Indicators Favoring Amputation

- **Devascularized extremity:** Pale, cold, no capillary refill despite cleaning/elevation
- **Severe crush:** >50% of digit or limb is macerated (irretrievable damage)
- **Contamination:** Extensive soil/fecal contamination with unable vascular compromise (gangrene inevitable)
- **Infection with systemic toxicity:** Spreading cellulitis/sepsis; limb tissue clearly dying
- **Patient factors:** Immunocompromised (if salvage risks life from infection)
- **Functional loss:** Severe nerve injury rendering limb useless

### Indicators Favoring Salvage Attempt

- **Marginal vascular status but capillary refill present:** May salvage with aggressive debridement and drainage
- **Crush with intact blood supply:** Can be debrided; function may recover
- **Patient wishes:** If young, motivated, and infection risk manageable
- **Cultural/functional importance:** Finger or hand salvage even if partial function

### Amputation Technique (Austere Setting)

If amputation is chosen:
1. **Anesthesia:** Regional block (brachial/axillary for upper extremity; spinal for lower extremity) preferred
2. **Soft tissue amputation:** Cut soft tissue (skin, muscle, fascia) to length that allows skin flaps to close over bone
3. **Bone amputation:** Use bone cutter or saw (not knife; crushing damages bone edges); cut cleanly perpendicular to long axis
4. **Vessel ligation:** Ligate major arteries and veins to prevent hemorrhage
5. **Nerve division:** Cut nerves cleanly (retracts into tissue; less painful than crushed nerve)
6. **Close:** Skin flaps closed over stump with simple sutures; ensure no bone/muscle exposed
7. **Antibiotics:** 10–14 days post-operative coverage

<section id="summary">

## Quick Reference: Bite Wound Care Algorithm

1. **Determine animal type and vaccination status** (if known)
2. **Assess rabies risk** (initiate PEP immediately if high-risk animal or unknown)
3. **Irrigate and debride** vigorously (500 mL–1 L saline; remove devitalized tissue)
4. **Consider closure:** Delayed closure preferred for cat/human bites; primary only if very minor and clean
5. **Start antibiotics:** Amoxicillin-clavulanate PO × 7 days (or parenteral if deep/contaminated)
6. **Give tetanus** if lapsed
7. **Monitor** days 1–3 for infection signs; escalate care if fever, spreading erythema, purulent drainage
8. **Rabies observation:** If animal available, observe 10 days for symptoms before declaring PEP complete

</section>
