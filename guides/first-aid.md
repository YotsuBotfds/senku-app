**Critical Triage Warning:** If the person has any sudden red-flag symptom below, stop reading and call emergency services or begin the fastest available evacuation now. Do not wait for a full diagnosis or for symptoms to improve.

**Symptom-first emergency rule:** If the question sounds like "their face is drooping," "one arm went weak," or "their words suddenly sound wrong," treat it as stroke now. If it sounds like "there is heavy chest pressure," "pain is going into the jaw or arm," or "they are sweaty, short of breath, and something feels very wrong," treat it as a heart emergency now. If it sounds like "too many pain pills and will not stay awake," "mixed pills and alcohol, hard to wake," "naloxone helped but sleepy again," or "accidental double-dose medicine and now confused," treat it as an overdose emergency now.

**Electrical scene fast track:** If someone was shocked and cannot let go, got shocked and collapsed, or is down near a live wire, do not touch the person first. Call emergency services if available, isolate power from a dry safe location, and keep everyone back. Only after the person is clear of the electrical source should you check breathing/pulse, start CPR, and use an AED if available. Do not give food or fluids by mouth after collapse or serious electrical injury. Route scene control to [Electrical Safety & Hazard Prevention](../electrical-safety-hazard-prevention.html) before routine shock positioning.

:::danger
**Collapse + chest pain + stroke signs fast track:** If someone suddenly collapses with chest pain or pressure plus one-sided weakness, slurred speech, or barely responding, call emergency services or begin the fastest evacuation now. If unresponsive or not breathing, start CPR immediately. Do not give food, water, or pills by mouth.
:::

### Red-flag fast track - use this if ANY of these are present

- Not breathing, only gasping, or no pulse: start [CPR](#cpr) immediately once the scene is safe. If this is an electrical shock or live-wire scene, isolate power before touching the person.
- Electrical shock, cannot-let-go contact, collapsed after shock, or live-wire scene: do not touch the person until power is isolated; call emergency services, keep people back, and go to [Electrical Safety & Hazard Prevention](../electrical-safety-hazard-prevention.html) first. Once safe, start [CPR](#cpr) and use an AED if breathing is absent or abnormal.
- Severe uncontrolled bleeding, spurting blood, or amputation: go to [Hemorrhage Control](#bleeding) now.
- Sudden face droop, one-sided weakness or numbness, confusion, trouble speaking or understanding, sudden vision change, or cannot repeat a sentence: treat as stroke and go to [Stroke recognition](#stroke-recognition--act-within-minutes).
- Heavy chest pressure, chest tightness, or chest pain; pain into the jaw, arm, shoulder, or back; cold sweat, shortness of breath, nausea, or "something feels very wrong": treat as heart attack and go to [Acute Coronary Syndrome & Cardiac Emergencies](../acute-coronary-cardiac-emergencies.html).
- Severe trouble breathing, throat swelling, stridor, or blue lips: treat as an airway emergency; if the person becomes unresponsive, go to [CPR](#cpr).
- Too sleepy to stay awake after pills or alcohol, slow breathing, pinpoint pupils, confusion after a double dose, or sleepy again after naloxone, including complaint-first patterns like "too many pain pills and will not stay awake," "mixed pills and alcohol, hard to wake," "naloxone helped but sleepy again," or "accidental double-dose medicine and now confused": treat as an overdose emergency, keep the airway open, use naloxone if available, and go to [Poisoning and Overdose](#poisoning-and-overdose) now.
- Hard or rigid belly, involuntary guarding, right-lower-quadrant pain with fever or pain when walking upright, one clearly tender abdominal spot with fever, repeated vomiting with swelling and inability to pass stool or gas, or complaint-first patterns like "right lower belly pain and will not walk upright," "vomiting and the belly is getting hard," or "belly pain with fever and one spot very tender": treat as an abdominal emergency and go to [Acute Abdominal Emergencies & Surgical Decision-Making](../acute-abdominal-emergencies.html).
- Active seizure or convulsions in an adult: go to [Adult Seizure & Status Epilepticus Field Protocol](../adult-seizure-status-epilepticus-field-protocol.html).
- Severe burn, open fracture, amputation, or major collapse after trauma: stay here for stabilization and evacuation steps.

**If none of the above apply** and the person is alert and breathing comfortably, see [Common Ailments: Recognition & Basic Home Care](../common-ailments-recognition-care.html) instead.

### Symptom-first emergency routing

- **Unresponsive, not breathing, or no pulse:** start [CPR](#cpr) immediately once the scene is safe. If this follows electrical shock or a live-wire contact, isolate power before touching the person.
- **Severe bleeding or amputation:** go to [Hemorrhage Control](#bleeding) immediately.
- **Stroke signs** - sudden face droop, one-sided weakness or numbness, trouble speaking, words coming out wrong, sudden confusion, or sudden vision change: call emergency services or begin the fastest evacuation now, note the last-known-normal time, and go to [Stroke recognition](#stroke-recognition--act-within-minutes) first; if chest pressure or pain is also present, default to emergency services or the heart emergency guide first, and treat this as a stroke/cardiac overlap rather than an airway or needle-decompression problem unless there are actual airway signs (stridor, throat swelling, blue lips, inability to speak) or actual chest-trauma signs (penetrating or blunt chest injury, sudden unilateral absent breath sounds, JVD, tracheal deviation). Do not give food, water, or pills by mouth.
- **Heart-attack signs** - heavy chest pressure or tightness, pain into the jaw/arm/back, cold sweat, nausea, sudden shortness of breath, or "something feels very wrong": call emergency services or begin the fastest evacuation now, stop exertion, keep the person resting, and go directly to [Acute Coronary Syndrome & Cardiac Emergencies](../acute-coronary-cardiac-emergencies.html) while you monitor breathing.
- **Too sleepy to stay awake after pills, mixed pills and alcohol, slow breathing, pinpoint pupils, confusion after a double dose, or sleepy again after naloxone, including "too many pain pills and will not stay awake," "mixed pills and alcohol, hard to wake," "naloxone helped but sleepy again," or "accidental double-dose medicine and now confused":** go directly to [Poisoning and Overdose](#poisoning-and-overdose); treat this as an overdose emergency first, not a routine side effect or ordinary sleepiness, keep the airway open, use naloxone if available, and prepare for [CPR](#cpr) if breathing becomes absent or abnormal.
- **Hard or rigid belly, guarding, right-lower-quadrant pain with fever, pain that worsens with walking, coughing, or bumps, one clearly localized tender spot with fever, or repeated vomiting with distention and inability to pass stool or gas:** go directly to [Acute Abdominal Emergencies & Surgical Decision-Making](../acute-abdominal-emergencies.html); this includes complaint-first patterns like "right lower belly pain and will not walk upright," "cannot pass stool or gas and keep vomiting," or "stomach flu or surgical abdomen." Treat this as a possible surgical abdomen or bowel obstruction first, not ordinary constipation, reflux, or stomach flu.
- **Seizure or convulsion in an adult:** go directly to [Adult Seizure & Status Epilepticus Field Protocol](../adult-seizure-status-epilepticus-field-protocol.html).
- **Severe allergic reaction or throat swelling:** keep the airway open, give epinephrine if available and trained, and prepare for [CPR](#cpr) if the person stops breathing.
- **Electrical shock, exposed live wire, or someone cannot let go:** do not touch the person until power is off; de-energize if safe, then go to [Electrical Safety & Hazard Prevention](../electrical-safety-hazard-prevention.html). Once clear, return here for [CPR](#cpr), AED use if available, burns, or shock care.
- **Not an emergency:** if the person is alert, breathing comfortably, and has no red-flag symptoms, route to [Common Ailments: Recognition & Basic Home Care](../common-ailments-recognition-care.html).

### Stroke recognition - act within minutes

Sudden onset is the key word. If any of these appeared abruptly, or a bystander says the person was normal a few minutes ago, treat as stroke until proven otherwise:

- Face: one side droops or feels numb; the person cannot smile evenly; their eyelid sags on one side
- Arm: one arm drifts downward or goes weak when they try to raise both; they may say "my arm feels heavy" or "I can't grip anything"
- Speech: words are slurred, wrong words come out, or they cannot repeat a simple sentence ("The sky is blue")
- Other sudden warning signs: one-sided numbness, sudden vision loss or double vision, sudden severe imbalance, or abrupt confusion with trouble following simple commands
- Time: note the exact time symptoms started or the last-known-normal time; treatment windows are measured from onset

**If even one FAST sign is present, or you are unsure:** call emergency services or begin the fastest available evacuation immediately. Do not wait to see if symptoms improve on their own. Keep the person lying on their side if unconscious, monitor breathing, and prepare for [CPR](#cpr) if they become unresponsive. Do not give food, water, or any pills by mouth - swallowing may be impaired.

### Heart attack recognition - act within minutes

Severe chest pain is the classic signal, but not every heart attack looks the same. Many people describe pressure, heaviness, tightness, burning, or "something feels very wrong" instead:

- Chest: heavy pressure, squeezing, or crushing pain in the center or left side of the chest, often described as "an elephant sitting on my chest," lasting more than a few minutes or going away and coming back
- Radiation: pain, tingling, or numbness spreading to the left arm, both arms, jaw, neck, shoulders, or back
- Breathing: sudden shortness of breath with or without chest pain
- Skin: cold sweat, pale or gray color, clammy feeling
- Other signs: sudden nausea, vomiting, light-headedness, unusual weakness, or an overwhelming sense of dread, especially in women, older adults, and people with diabetes

**If chest discomfort plus any one of the above is present, or the person says "something feels very wrong":** call emergency services or begin the fastest available evacuation immediately. Stop activity, keep the person calm, and let them sit or lie in whatever position is most comfortable for breathing. Monitor breathing and pulse; prepare for [CPR](#cpr) if they become unresponsive. Do not let them walk around or "push through it." If stroke signs, confusion, vomiting, or reduced alertness are also present, do not give anything by mouth. Review [shock management](#shock) for positioning and supportive care.
## Overview

First aid encompasses the immediate, life-preserving interventions provided in the first minutes to hours after injury or sudden illness. In survival scenarios, professional emergency services may be unavailable for hours or days, making these skills essential for preventing unnecessary death. First aid priorities are simple: maintain airway patency, ensure adequate breathing, restore and maintain circulation, and prevent deterioration until professional care is reached or the patient stabilizes. Early intervention in the "golden hour" (first 60 minutes after trauma or onset of acute illness) dramatically improves survival odds. This guide covers recognition, assessment, and field management of life-threatening emergencies using principles that work with or without equipment.

## Recognition & Assessment of Life-Threatening Conditions

Rapid, systematic assessment is the foundation of effective first aid. Use the ABCDE approach:

- **A - Airway** Ã¢â‚¬â€ Is the airway patent? Can the patient speak or cry?
- **B - Breathing** Ã¢â‚¬â€ Is the patient breathing? Are breath sounds present bilaterally?
- **C - Circulation** Ã¢â‚¬â€ Is there a pulse? Is there severe bleeding?
- **D - Disability** Ã¢â‚¬â€ What is the patient's mental status? Can they respond to commands?
- **E - Exposure** Ã¢â‚¬â€ Full assessment of all injuries; assess for hypothermia, hidden wounds.

Take only 60-90 seconds for primary assessment. Treat immediately identified threats (airway obstruction, apnea, cardiac arrest, severe hemorrhage) before moving to secondary assessment. Document what you find; if multiple casualties, re-triage periodically as conditions change.

:::danger
**Life-Threatening Signs Requiring Immediate Intervention:**
- No pulse or absent breathing
- Severe airway obstruction (stridor, inability to speak)
- Severe hemorrhage (arterial spurting, limb amputation)
- Tension pneumothorax (extreme distress, unilateral absent breath sounds, JVD, tracheal deviation)
- Severe anaphylaxis (stridor, respiratory distress, systemic edema)
:::

## First Aid Essentials

### Wound Assessment and Cleaning

**Initial Wound Assessment:**

1.  Stop the bleeding (see bleeding control section)
2.  Examine wound for debris, foreign objects, or contamination
3.  Assess wound depth: superficial (epidermis only), partial thickness (dermis involved), or full thickness (extends to muscle/bone)
4.  Note wound edges: clean cut (lower infection risk) vs. jagged/crushed (higher risk)
5.  Check for signs of infection: pus, redness, warmth, foul odor, red streaking toward heart
6.  Determine if professional medical care is needed based on depth, contamination, and location

**Wound Cleaning Procedure:**

1.  Wash hands thoroughly with soap and clean water
2.  Use clean gloves if available
3.  Irrigate wound with clean water (boiled and cooled if off-grid) using a syringe or clean cloth
4.  For contaminated wounds, irrigate with high pressure (20cc syringe with 18-gauge needle creates appropriate pressure)
5.  Remove visible debris with clean tweezers (sterilized in boiling water if possible)
6.  If wound appears infected or has dead tissue (necrotic), gently scrub with soft brush or cloth to remove bacteria
7.  Rinse thoroughly until water runs clear
8.  Pat dry with clean cloth (don't rub)
9.  Apply antiseptic (iodine, chlorhexidine, hydrogen peroxide, or alcohol if nothing else available)

### Dressing Changes and Improvised Bandages

Once the wound is clean, the dressing is there to keep dirt out and stop rubbing. It does not need to stay on forever, and it should not stay wet.

- **Change daily** for most clean wounds, or sooner if the dressing is wet, dirty, loose, or smells bad
- **Change 2-3 times daily** if the wound is draining heavily or the area is getting macerated
- **Reuse cloth only if** it can be washed with soap and clean water, then boiled or heat-dried before the next use
- **Do not reuse** cloth that held pus, stool, vomit, or moldy material, or cloth that still smells bad after washing
- **If out of gauze,** use clean cotton cloth, a washed T-shirt, bandanna, or pillowcase strip folded into layers
- **Avoid** tissues, paper towels, and fuzzy fabric that sheds fibers into the wound
- **If the dressing sticks,** wet it with clean water or saline and let it loosen before pulling

For chronic drainage or wounds that must be packed, see [Chronic Wound Care](../wound-care-chronic.html) and [Wound Hygiene, Infection Prevention & Field Sanitation](../wound-hygiene-infection-prevention.html).

## Hemorrhage Control

### Bleeding Control - The Cascade Method

**Step 1: Direct Pressure (Most Effective)**

1.  Apply firm, direct pressure with clean cloth or gauze
2.  Maintain pressure for 10-15 minutes without checking the wound
3.  If blood soaks through, apply additional cloth without removing first layer
4.  Once bleeding stops, leave cloth in place and secure with bandage
5.  Elevate the injured area above the heart if possible

### Wound Classification

- **Abrasion:** Superficial scrape, minimal bleeding, high infection risk
- **Laceration:** Clean cut from sharp object, predictable bleeding, varies by location/depth
- **Puncture:** Deep, narrow wound from pointed object; risk of deep infection, hidden blood loss
- **Avulsion:** Tissue torn away; significant bleeding, tissue viability depends on mechanism
- **Crush Injury:** Traumatic tissue damage from blunt force; severe pain, rhabdomyolysis risk

### Direct Pressure Technique

Apply firm, continuous pressure with clean cloth directly on wound for minimum 10 minutes. Do NOT repeatedly lift cloth to check. If blood soaks through, add layers on top (don't remove). Elevation helps: position limb above heart level. If extremity, consider distal pulse and limb viability. Pressure should stop bleeding in 10-15 minutes for most wounds.

**Step 2: If Direct Pressure Fails**

- **Limb bleeding:** Move promptly to a tourniquet rather than spending time on pressure-point maneuvers
- **Groin/armpit wounds:** Pack the wound if possible and maintain firm direct pressure
- **Neck/face wounds:** Do not use carotid pressure points; apply firm direct pressure and evacuate urgently

### Tourniquet Application

**Step 3: Tourniquet Application (Last Resort for Limb Bleeding)**

For severe limb hemorrhage uncontrolled by pressure (amputation, massive laceration):

1.  Place tourniquet 2-3 inches above the wound (between wound and heart)
2.  If wound is on hand/foot, place just above ankle or wrist
3.  Tighten until bleeding completely stops and pulse below tourniquet is no longer palpable
4.  Mark tourniquet with time applied (write directly on the tourniquet or on tape attached to it Ã¢â‚¬â€ critical for surgical handoff)
5.  **Do NOT loosen or remove the tourniquet in the field.** Once applied, leave it in place until the patient reaches surgical care
6.  Monitor the patient for signs of shock and prepare for evacuation to surgical care

:::danger
**Do NOT loosen tourniquets in the field.** Previous guidance to "loosen every 15-20 minutes" is outdated and dangerous. Loosening a tourniquet releases pooled blood containing potassium, lactic acid, and myoglobin from ischemic tissue, which can cause fatal cardiac arrhythmia (reperfusion injury) and renewed hemorrhage. Only a surgeon in a controlled setting with monitoring should remove a tourniquet.
:::

### Wound Packing

For deep wounds with uncontrolled bleeding: pack wound firmly with hemostatic gauze (Combat Gauze, Celox, or plain if unavailable) saturated with direct pressure. Maintain pressure for full recommended time (up to 3 minutes for hemostatic gauze). Cover with compression dressing. Do not remove packing prematurely; let it stay in place for 24-48 hours.

### Hemorrhage Staging

### Burn Percentage (Rule of 9s)

Head: 9%, each arm: 9%, chest: 18%, abdomen: 18%, each leg: 18%, genitals: 1%. Burns >20% require fluid resuscitation (severe dehydration risk). Burns >30% mortality significant even with professional care. >50% burns have poor prognosis in field settings.

1.  Stop burning: extinguish flames, remove from heat source
2.  Cool wound: immerse in cool water for 10-20 minutes (reduces depth), avoid ice (risks frostbite)
3.  Assess: determine depth percentage, estimate % of body surface area
4.  Cover: use clean, dry cloth; prevents contamination and reduces heat loss

## Shock Management

:::danger
**Electrical shock is not routine shock management.** If someone was shocked, cannot let go, collapsed after shock, or is near a live wire, do not start by laying them down, elevating their legs, or giving fluids. First isolate power from a dry safe location, call emergency services if available, and keep bystanders back. Once the scene is safe, check responsiveness, breathing, and pulse; start [CPR](#cpr) and use an AED if available before routine shock positioning.
:::

### Shock Recognition

Shock is inadequate tissue perfusion. Early signs: anxiety, restlessness, tachycardia (heart rate >100), rapid shallow breathing, pale clammy skin, delayed capillary refill (>2 seconds). Late signs: unconsciousness, barely palpable pulse, severe bradycardia, cold extremities. Act immediately; shock is life-threatening.

### Types of Shock

- **Hypovolemic:** Blood/fluid loss. Treatment: control bleeding, fluid replacement, elevate legs.
- **Cardiogenic:** Heart pump failure. Treatment: elevate head, minimize activity, medications if available.
- **Septic:** Systemic infection. Treatment: antibiotics, fluid replacement, identify infection source.
- **Anaphylactic:** Severe allergic reaction. Treatment: epinephrine IM, airway support, antihistamines.
- **Neurogenic:** Spinal cord injury. Treatment: immobilize spine, fluid support cautiously.

### Shock Management Protocol

1.  Maintain airway; position patient on back with legs elevated 8-12 inches (cardiac exception: head elevated if pulmonary edema suspected)
2.  Control all bleeding; apply pressure and tourniquet as needed
3.  Prevent further heat loss; insulate from ground, cover with blankets
4.  Maintain calm reassuring presence; anxiety worsens shock
5.  Prepare for evacuation; shock is medical emergency
6.  Monitor closely; record vital signs every 15 minutes if possible
7.  If accessible: start IV lines, give fluids for hypovolemic shock (if internal bleeding suspected, limit fluids)

:::note
**Signs of Shock:**

- Pale, cool, clammy skin
- Rapid weak pulse (>100 bpm)
- Rapid, shallow breathing
- Anxiety, confusion, or altered mental status
- Nausea or vomiting
- Decreased urine output
:::

**Shock Treatment Protocol:**

1.  Lay person flat
2.  Elevate legs 12 inches above heart level (unless head/spinal injury suspected)
3.  Keep person warm with blankets but avoid overheating
4.  Control any bleeding with direct pressure
5.  Provide fluids if conscious and can swallow (sips of water)
6.  Monitor vital signs: pulse, breathing, consciousness
7.  Do not give food or drink if surgery may be needed
8.  Seek professional medical help immediately
9.  Monitor for deterioration; be prepared to give CPR

:::danger
**Shock Decompensation:** If patient deteriorates to Class IV shock (altered mental status, barely palpable pulse, skin cold/clammy), time is critical. Prepare for CPR. If no IV access and oral fluids impossible (unconscious or vomiting), patient is at imminent risk of death. In field settings, keep patient warm, elevate legs, and focus on evacuation.
:::

:::tip
**Field Fluid Resuscitation:** If IV fluids unavailable and patient conscious, provide small frequent sips of oral rehydration solution (ORS) made with 1 liter clean water + 6 level teaspoons sugar + 1/2 level teaspoon salt. Avoid large volumes that trigger vomiting. Monitor urine output (if possible); dark urine indicates dehydration.
:::

## CPR and Rescue Breathing

### Adult CPR Sequence

For unresponsive, non-breathing adult:

1. Call for help
2. Place supine on firm surface
3. Open airway (head tilt-chin lift)
4. Give 30 chest compressions at 100-120 bpm (press hard, push fast, allow full recoil)
5. Give 2 rescue breaths (watch chest rise)
6. Continue 30:2 ratio until AED available, patient revives, or clearly deceased

### Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation - Comprehensive Procedures

**Adult CPR:**

1.  Check responsiveness: tap shoulders, shout "Are you okay?"
2.  If unresponsive and not breathing normally, begin CPR immediately
3.  Position person on firm, flat surface
4.  Place heel of hand on center of chest between nipples
5.  Place other hand on top, lock fingers together
6.  Keep arms straight, lean forward
7.  Push hard and fast on chest: compress at least 2 inches deep at rate of 100-120 compressions per minute
8.  Allow chest to recoil completely between compressions
9.  Continue without interruption until: emergency help arrives, patient begins breathing, you are exhausted
10.  **With rescue breaths:** After 30 compressions, give 2 breaths (30:2 ratio)

**CPR - Child (1-8 years):**

1.  Use same hand placement as adult
2.  May use single hand if child is very small
3.  Compress at least 2 inches or 1/3 of chest depth
4.  Use rescue breaths: after 30 compressions, give 2 gentle breaths covering mouth and nose
5.  Continue until help arrives or signs of life return

**CPR - Infant (under 1 year):**

1.  Check responsiveness and breathing
2.  Place 2 fingers (index and middle) on breastbone just below nipple line
3.  Compress 1.5 inches at rate of 100-120 per minute
4.  After 30 compressions, give 2 gentle breaths (infant-mouth-to-mouth-nose)
5.  Continue until emergency help arrives

### Compression Technique

Position: heel of one hand on lower half of breastbone, other hand on top, fingers interlaced. Depress 2-2.4 inches at least 100 bpm but no more than 120 bpm. Allow full chest recoil between compressions. Minimize interruptions. Rotate compressor every 2 minutes if multiple rescuers to prevent fatigue.

### Rescue Breathing

After 30 compressions: pinch nose, create seal with mouth, blow for 1 second until chest visibly rises. Deliver 2 breaths. If breaths don't cause chest rise, reposition airway and try again. Continue CPR immediately after breaths. If unable to provide rescue breaths, hands-only CPR (continuous compressions) nearly as effective, especially in adults.

### Recovery Position

For unconscious, breathing patient: place on side with head tilted back to maintain airway. Left arm under head, right arm across chest. Right knee bent. This position allows drainage, prevents aspiration. Check airway frequently. Monitor breathing and pulse continuously.

:::danger
**When to Stop CPR:** CPR may be discontinued if: (1) ROSC achieved (return of spontaneous circulationÃ¢â‚¬â€pulse returns); (2) advanced life support assumed; (3) rescuer exhaustion/danger to self; (4) obvious death (rigor mortis, decomposition, decapitation); (5) do-not-resuscitate order present. In wilderness settings with no access to medical care, CPR may continue for 30-60 minutes if patient is normothermic and hypothermia is ruled out.
:::

:::warning
**CPR Complications:** Rib fractures occur in ~25% of CPR recipients; this is normal and not a reason to stop. Never pause compressions >10 seconds. In cold-water drowning, patient is "not dead until warm and dead"Ã¢â‚¬â€continue resuscitation until body temperature >30Ã‚Â°C or lifeless for >3 hours.
:::

## Choking and Airway Management

### Choking Response

**Quick branch before you touch the person:**

- If an adult or older child is still coughing and talking: encourage continued coughing, keep them upright, and watch closely. Do **not** start abdominal thrusts yet and do **not** do blind finger sweeps.
- If an adult or older child cannot speak, cannot cough, cannot breathe, or is turning blue: call for emergency help and start abdominal thrusts immediately.
- If the choking person is pregnant or too large for abdominal thrusts: use chest thrusts instead of abdominal thrusts.
- If an infant is gagging or crying strongly: the airway is still moving air. Stop feeding, keep the infant upright, and watch closely. If the infant cannot cry, cannot cough, or cannot breathe, switch immediately to 5 back blows and 5 chest thrusts.
- If a choking person collapses or becomes unresponsive: lower them to the floor, call for emergency help/AED, start CPR, and only remove an object if you can clearly see it.

**Conscious Adult - Heimlich Maneuver:**

1.  Ask "Are you choking?" - if they cannot cough or speak, act immediately.
2.  Stand behind the person.
3.  Place a fist just above the navel and below the ribcage.
4.  Grasp the fist with the other hand.
5.  Perform quick, hard, upward thrusts into the abdomen.
6.  Repeat thrusts until the object is dislodged or the person becomes unconscious.
7.  If the person becomes unconscious, lower them safely and begin CPR.

### Choking Adult (Conscious)

If the person is coughing and talking, encourage continued coughing and monitor closely. If the person cannot speak, cannot cough, or cannot breathe, perform abdominal thrusts. Stand behind, wrap your arms around the abdomen just above the umbilicus, make a fist with one hand, grasp it with the other, and press firmly upward with quick thrusts until the object is expelled or the person loses consciousness. If unconscious, lower the person safely and begin CPR.

### Foreign Body Airway Obstruction (FBAO)

Signs: unable to speak, weak cough, no breathing sounds, panic, cyanosis. If partial obstruction (still coughing/talking): encourage continued coughing, monitor, and prepare to act if it worsens. If complete obstruction: immediate intervention is needed - perform abdominal thrusts for most adults, chest thrusts for pregnancy or very large body habitus, and back blows plus chest thrusts for infants.

### Infant Choking

**Infant Choking (under 1 year):**

1.  If the infant cannot cry or cough, give 5 back blows: hold the infant face-down on your forearm, support the chin, and deliver sharp blows between the shoulder blades.
2.  Then give 5 chest thrusts: turn the infant face-up, place 2 fingers on the lower breastbone, and deliver thrusts.
3.  Repeat the cycle until the object is expelled or the infant becomes unresponsive.
4.  Do NOT perform abdominal thrusts on infants (risk of organ damage).
5.  If the infant is gagging or crying loudly, stop feeding and watch closely rather than treating it as complete choking.

For infant (<1 year) with FBAO: 1) Give 5 back blows: support chin/jaw with hand, deliver sharp blows between shoulder blades. 2) Give 5 chest thrusts: place 2 fingers on breastbone just below nipple line, press downward 1-1.5 inches, 5 times. 3) Open the mouth only to remove a clearly visible object. 4) Repeat the cycle until the object is dislodged or the infant becomes unconscious.


### Airway Positioning

Head tilt-chin lift: Place hand on forehead, tilt head back gently. Place fingers under chin, lift gently. Opens airway by removing tongue obstruction. For trauma: avoid neck extension; use jaw thrust instead (fingers under angle of jaw, lift forward without neck extension).

### Oral Airway Insertion

For unconscious patient without gag reflex: measure oral airway (corner of mouth to earlobe). Insert upside down, rotate 180 degrees as sliding into mouth, or insert directly right-side up. Airway keeps tongue forward, allows breathing around. Monitor closely; removes airway if patient becomes conscious/gags.

## Fracture and Splinting

### Fracture Signs

Pain (especially with movement), swelling (usually within minutes), deformity (bone angle abnormal), discoloration (bruising appears), loss of function, crepitus (grinding sensation). Open fractures have break in skin; high infection risk. Compartment syndrome (swelling so severe as to cut off circulation) is surgical emergency.

### RICE Protocol (Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation)

1.  **Rest:** Stop activity immediately, keep limb immobile
2.  **Ice:** Apply cold (wrapped ice pack or cold water) for 15-20 minutes, repeat every 2-3 hours for first 48 hours
3.  **Compression:** Wrap with elastic bandage to reduce swelling
4.  **Elevation:** Keep injured limb above heart level
5.  Continue for 48 hours or until swelling plateaus

:::info-box
**Fracture vs. Sprain:** Sprains involve ligament damage (joint injury), fractures involve bone breaks. Both cause pain, swelling, and limited mobility. When in doubt, treat as fracture and immobilize until professional evaluation.
:::

### Splinting Principles

- **Immobilize:** Prevent movement of fracture site; reduces pain and prevents neurovascular damage
- **Include joints:** Splint joint above and below fracture site
- **Pad well:** Prevents pressure ulcers; use cloth, foam, or rolled newspaper
- **Check distal circulation:** After splinting, verify pulses, color, temperature, capillary refill distal to splint
- **Monitor for swelling:** Loosen splint if swelling worsens or toes/fingers become cold/blue
- **Elevate when possible:** Reduces swelling, improves comfort

### Field Expedient Splinting

Without commercial splints: improvise with rolled newspapers, cardboard, boards, or pillows. Secure with cloth strips, torn fabric, or vines. Sling and swathe for upper limb using cloth strips. For leg: double-splint (board on each side) secured with cloth strips every 4-6 inches. For spine: rigid splint or firm surface (backboard). Ensure splint doesn't cut off circulation.

### Open Fracture Management

Priority: control bleeding (arterial bleeding immediate threat), clean wound, stabilize fracture before transport. Cover wound with clean dressing; do not probe. Administer antibiotics if available (reduces infection risk significantly). Tetanus prophylaxis if available. Open fractures almost always require professional care; stabilize and evacuate.

## Wound Management

### Wound Cleaning

For minor wounds: wash with soap and clean water, removing visible dirt and debris. For field expedient: use boiled cooled water (minimum) or saline solution. Avoid harsh antiseptics on large wounds (kills healthy tissue). For contaminated wounds: aggressive cleaning more important than infection prevention medication.

### Infection Prevention

- Remove all foreign material (dirt, fiber, etc.)
- Irrigate copiously with clean water (2+ liters for heavily contaminated)
- Consider high-pressure irrigation if available and wound not heavily contaminated initially
- Avoid tourniquet effect from tight bandaging; allow some drainage initially
- Elevate when possible to reduce swelling and promote drainage
- Monitor for signs of infection (increasing pain, red streaking, purulent drainage, fever)

### Closure Decisions

Primary closure (immediate suturing): appropriate for clean wounds <6-8 hours old. Delay closure: contaminated/heavily bleeding wounds or after 8 hours. May close after infection risk passes (24-48 hours). For human puncture wounds, never close the wound in the field; allow drainage and monitor closely for infection. Wounds on highly vascular areas (face, scalp) tolerate delayed closure better than less vascular areas.

### Human Deep Puncture Wounds

For a human deep puncture wound: control major bleeding first, irrigate thoroughly, do not probe or widen the tract, never seal or suture it in the field, and monitor closely for retained foreign body or deep infection.

### Dressing Changes

For wounds with continuous drainage: change dressing daily or when soaked through. For healing wounds: change dressing every 2-3 days if keeping closed, or every day if wet. Clean gently, assess for signs of infection, apply fresh antiseptic/antibiotic ointment, cover with clean dressing. Watch for malodor, excessive drainage, discoloration indicating infection.

:::warning
**Infection Recognition Timeline:** Early infection (24-48 hours): redness, warmth, increasing pain, minor drainage. Progressive infection (3-7 days): spreading erythema, purulent drainage (yellow/green), red streaking toward heart, fever. Systemic infection (>7 days): fever, chills, malaise, possible septic shock. Start antibiotics immediately if available upon recognizing infection progression.
:::

:::info-box
**Antibiotic Dosing for Common Wound Infections:** Amoxicillin 500mg three times daily Ãƒâ€” 7 days; Cephalexin 500mg four times daily Ãƒâ€” 7 days; Clindamycin 300-450mg four times daily Ãƒâ€” 7 days (covers MRSA). Dosing assumes normal renal function. If infection suspected with fever/systemic signs, increase vigilance and begin evacuating patient.

### Advanced Wound Cleaning

**Irrigation Levels by Contamination:**

1.  **Clean wounds:** Gentle irrigation with clean water, 500-1000 mL
2.  **Contaminated wounds:** Forceful irrigation with 1-2 liters clean water using 20cc syringe and needle
3.  **Heavily contaminated/dirty wounds:** High-volume irrigation (3+ liters), consider mild soap solution followed by clean water rinse

**Debridement (Removing Dead/Damaged Tissue):**

1.  Clean hands and use sterile gloves if available
2.  Identify dead tissue: gray/black color, loss of sensation when touched
3.  Use sterile scissors or scalpel (boiled metal if necessary) to carefully trim away necrotic tissue
4.  Trim only obviously dead tissue - err on side of caution
5.  Irrigation after debridement to remove tissue fragments
6.  Monitor for infection in following days

### Suturing and Wound Closure Techniques

:::note
**When to Suture vs. Leave Open:**

**Suturing Materials and Techniques:**

1.  **Materials:** Use non-absorbable suture (silk, nylon) for skin closure, 2-0 to 4-0 gauge for most wounds
2.  **Needle selection:** Curved needle for skin (easier to use than straight)
3.  **Preparation:** Sterilize needle and thread in boiling water (10 minutes) if not pre-sterilized
4.  **Hand position:** Hold needle in middle with needle driver, don't touch needle tip
5.  **Stitching technique:** Pierce skin 2-3mm from wound edge, cross wound, exit 2-3mm from opposite edge
6.  **Stitch spacing:** Place stitches 5-10mm apart depending on wound location and tension
7.  **Knot tying:** Tie with square knots (right-left-right for 4-knot security)
8.  **Stitch removal timing:** Face = 3-5 days; scalp/arm = 7 days; leg/body = 10 days; joint areas = 14 days

:::warning
**Suturing Complications:** If wound becomes increasingly red, swollen, warm, or produces pus after closure, the sutures may need removal to allow drainage. This indicates infection.
:::

### Butterfly Closures and Steri-Strips

**When to Use:**

- Small clean cuts less than 1/4 inch deep
- Wounds where minimal scarring is desired
- Areas of high mobility (joints) - provides flexibility
- Patients who prefer not to have stitches

**Application:**

1.  Clean and dry wound thoroughly
2.  Cut steri-strip or create butterfly from medical tape
3.  Pull wound edges together
4.  Apply strips perpendicular to wound (across, not along)
5.  Leave in place 3-5 days, replace as needed
6.  Gradually allow strips to peel off naturally

### Wound Packing

**For Deep Wounds or Cavities:**

1.  Clean wound thoroughly with irrigation
2.  If left open to heal, pack with sterile gauze to prevent premature surface closure
3.  Use iodoform-impregnated gauze if available (antimicrobial), or plain sterile gauze
4.  Pack loosely - not tight - to allow drainage and gas exchange
5.  Change packing daily or every other day
6.  Watch for signs of infection: increased drainage, odor, fever
7.  Gradually stop packing as wound fills from bottom up

### Infection Signs and Monitoring

:::note
**Early Infection Signs (Act Immediately):**

- Increasing redness around wound edges
- Warmth and swelling
- Pus drainage (yellow/green/bloody)
- Foul or unusual odor
- Pain that increases after initial 24-48 hours
- Red streaking extending from wound toward heart (lymphangitis)
:::

**Managing Wound Infection:**

1.  If sutures present, remove them to allow drainage
2.  Increase irrigation and cleaning frequency to 2-3 times daily
3.  Apply warm compresses to encourage drainage
4.  If abscess forms (collection of pus), may need drainage procedure
5.  Start antibiotics if available: oral amoxicillin-clavulanate 500/125 mg three times daily, or doxycycline 100 mg twice daily
6.  Monitor for spreading infection/cellulitis (red streaking, fever, chills)
7.  Seek professional care if infection worsens or doesn't improve in 48-72 hours

### Wound Assessment Cascade: Step-by-Step Triage Flow

A systematic approach to wound evaluation ensures nothing critical is missed and resources are directed appropriately. The wound assessment cascade progresses through sequential decisions, each step determining the urgency and intensity of care required.

**Step 1: Initial Assessment (Immediate, <1 minute)**

Assess the patient's overall condition before examining the wound in detail.

- **Airway/Breathing/Circulation (ABC):** Is patient breathing? Pulse present? Are they alert and responsive?
  - If no pulse/breathing: begin CPR, this supersedes wound care
  - If severe respiratory distress: address airway before wound management
- **Level of consciousness:** Alert, confused, or unresponsive?
- **Skin perfusion:** Is skin color normal, pale, or bluish? (indicates shock if pale)
- **Bleeding severity:** Is there uncontrolled bleeding? (life-threatening, address immediately)

  - If no or controlled Ã¢â€ â€™ continue to Step 3

- Bright red arterial spurts (pulsatile bleeding)
- Continuous heavy flow that doesn't slow with direct pressure
- Soaked through multiple bandages within seconds
- Patient showing signs of shock (confusion, pale skin, weak pulse)

If any present: STOP and apply direct pressure or tourniquet before wound classification

Management:
- Apply firm direct pressure with clean cloth for 10-15 minutes without lifting to check
- If bleeding continues: apply tourniquet above wound (2 inches proximal, tighten until distal pulse is absent and bleeding stops completelyÃ¢â‚¬â€complete arterial occlusion is required)
- Elevate wound above heart if possible
- Once bleeding controlled, proceed to Step 3

**Step 3: Wound Classification (5-10 minutes)**

**Is the wound type one of these?**

a) **Puncture wound (penetrating injury)**
   - Small entry hole, deep penetration possible
   - Examples: nail in foot, knife wound, impaled object
   - **Concern:** Internal organ damage, compartment syndrome, infection risk
   - **Action:** Remove object only if can be safely extracted (minimal manipulation); assess for deep structural damage; refer to professional care if possible
   - Proceed to Step 4 (Contamination Assessment)

b) **Laceration (cut with clean edges)**
   - Clean separation of tissue
   - Examples: cut from glass, knife, sharp object
   - **Concern:** Bleeding, nerve/tendon damage, infection
   - **Action:** Irrigate, assess for deep structure involvement, close if edges align well
   - Proceed to Step 4

c) **Abrasion (scraped/road rash)**
   - Superficial tissue damage with embedded debris
   - Examples: falls, scrapes on concrete
   - **Concern:** Infection from embedded particles, pain, scarring
   - **Action:** Extensive cleaning/brushing, assess for foreign body, treat as contaminated
   - Proceed to Step 4

d) **Crush injury (bruising, tissue damage without necessarily breaking skin)**
   - Blunt force trauma, tissue damage internal
   - Examples: finger crushed by door, impact injury
   - **Concern:** Hidden deep damage, compartment syndrome, rhabdomyolysis
   - **Action:** Assess for compartment syndrome (pain, swelling, numbness beyond proportion to visible injury); elevate; may need specialist
   - Proceed to Step 4 (if skin broken) or to Step 5 (if intact skin)

e) **Burn (thermal injury)**
   - Classified by depth: superficial (red), partial thickness (blistering), full thickness (charred)
   - **Action:** See separate Burn Treatment section in this guide
   - Proceed to specialized burn assessment

**Step 4: Contamination Assessment (5-10 minutes)**

Determine how dirty/contaminated the wound is.

Is the wound **clean** (from sharp clean object, minimal delay in care)?
- Yes Ã¢â€ â€™ **Minor contamination risk.** Standard cleaning and suturing acceptable.
- No Ã¢â€ â€™ Is it **contaminated** (dirt, animal bite, delayed care >6-12 hours)?
  - Yes Ã¢â€ â€™ **Significant infection risk.** Extended antibiotics likely needed; suturing may be deferred to allow drainage.
  - No Ã¢â€ â€™ Is it **dirty/infected** (visible debris, animal/human bite, soil/rust exposure)?
    - Yes Ã¢â€ â€™ **High infection risk.** Do not suture; aggressive cleaning and antibiotics essential; may need surgical debridement.

**Tetanus status check:**
- Wound is contaminated/dirty AND patient's last tetanus vaccine >5 years ago?
  - Yes Ã¢â€ â€™ Give tetanus booster if available, or provide prophylaxis course
  - No Ã¢â€ â€™ Continue to Step 5

**Step 5: Treatment Decision (Based on Wound Size, Depth, Contamination)**

**Decision tree:**

a) **Small (<1 cm), clean laceration, minimal contamination Ã¢â€ â€™ CLOSE (suture/butterfly/steri-strip)**
   - Irrigate with clean water, remove debris
   - Close with sutures or strips if edges approximate well
   - Bandage, monitor for infection
   - Outcome: faster healing, minimal scar

b) **Medium (1-5 cm), clean edges, low contamination Ã¢â€ â€™ CLOSE WITH PRECAUTION**
   - Irrigate extensively
   - Close wound but monitor closely for infection
   - Plan suture removal in 7-10 days
   - Outcome: good cosmetic result if infection avoided

c) **Large (>5 cm), or deep, or significant contamination Ã¢â€ â€™ CLEAN EXTENSIVELY, DEFER CLOSURE**
   - Do NOT suture immediately
   - Irrigate repeatedly over hours/days (2-3 times daily if possible)
   - Allow wound to drain and heal from depth outward
   - After 48-72 hours without infection signs: may close secondarily
   - Outcome: lower infection risk, but slower healing and worse scar

d) **Human contaminated puncture wound Ã¢â€ â€™ IRRIGATE, ANTIBIOTICS, OBSERVE**
   - Difficult to clean internally
   - Do not probe, widen, or suture the tract in the field
   - Systemic antibiotics essential
   - Monitor for deep infection/abscess formation
   - May need imaging (ultrasound/x-ray) to rule out foreign body
   - Outcome: observe for delayed infection

e) **Animal or human bite Ã¢â€ â€™ LEAVE OPEN, AGGRESSIVE ANTIBIOTICS**
   - High risk of infection (polymicrobial, including anaerobes)
   - Do NOT close; let drain
   - Tetanus booster essential
   - Antibiotics: amoxicillin-clavulanate or doxycycline + metronidazole
   - Outcome: prevent serious infection (cellulitis, abscess, rabies in animal bites)

**Step 6: Follow-Up Schedule (Ongoing)**

:::info-box
### Standard Follow-Up Timings

- **24 hours:** Check for early infection signs (increasing pain, redness, warmth)
- **48 hours:** Assess for abscess formation; increase monitoring if contaminated wound
- **5-7 days:** Remove sutures if healing well and no infection (if wound is on face/scalp; longer on extremities)
- **10-14 days:** Final suture removal; assess for persistent redness or drainage
- **2-4 weeks:** Assess for delayed infection (late abscess, chronic drainage)
- **If any sign of infection:** See Step 2 infection management above
:::

**Red flags requiring immediate escalation to professional care:**

:::danger
### Wound Assessment Red Flags

- Uncontrollable bleeding
- Severe pain disproportionate to wound size
- Wound edges that cannot be approximated (gap >1 cm)
- Signs of neurovascular compromise (numbness, absent pulse, color change)
- Signs of deep structure injury (exposed bone, tendon, or muscle)
- Spreading cellulitis (red streaking toward heart, fever)
- Compartment syndrome signs (severe pain, swelling, inability to move distal parts)
- Wound on face, hand, or joint (higher infection/scarring risk; professional closure often better)
- Immunocompromised patient (diabetic, on steroids, HIV positive, etc.)
:::

### Bandaging Techniques

**Proper Bandaging Method:**

1.  **Layer 1 - Contact layer:** Sterile non-adhesive dressing directly on wound
2.  **Layer 2 - Absorption layer:** Sterile gauze pad(s) to absorb drainage
3.  **Layer 3 - Compression layer:** Elastic bandage wrap or gauze roller to hold in place
4.  **Tension:** Tight enough to control bleeding/swelling but not cut off circulation (you should fit one finger under the bandage)
5.  **Coverage:** Extend bandage 2 inches beyond wound edges
6.  **Secure:** Use medical tape, clips, or tie ends
7.  **Changing:** Change daily or when soaked through, unless healing well then every 2-3 days

:::info-box
**Signs of Poor Bandaging:** Numbness, tingling, increased pain, color change of limb beyond bandage (pale or blue), or inability to move fingers/toes indicates the bandage is too tight - loosen immediately.
:::

## Making Herbal Antiseptics & Pain Relief

### Natural Antiseptics from Plants

:::note
**Raw Honey Poultice**

- Properties: Raw honey kills bacteria through hydrogen peroxide production and osmotic effect
- Application: Apply directly to clean wound, cover with cloth. Change dressing daily or when honey becomes diluted.
- Effectiveness: One of the best natural antiseptics. Studies show it's effective against MRSA and other resistant bacteria.
- Caution: Do not use on infants under 1 year (botulism risk). Process honey from reputable sources.
:::

:::note
**Garlic Preparation**

- Active compound: Allicin, produced when garlic cells are crushed
- Fresh preparation: Crush 3-4 garlic cloves, add to 1 cup boiled water (cooled), soak wound 2-3 times daily
- Poultice: Crush garlic, mix with honey or lard, apply to wound. Pungent smell is normal.
- Effectiveness: Broad-spectrum antibacterial activity. Works against common wound pathogens.
- Caution: Can irritate sensitive skin; dilute if irritation occurs
:::

:::note
**Thyme Tea Wash**

- Preparation: Steep 2 tablespoons dried thyme in 1 cup boiling water for 15-20 minutes, strain and cool
- Use: Wash wound 3-4 times daily, or soak cloth and apply as compress
- Properties: Thymol is a powerful antimicrobial. Also reduces inflammation and promotes healing.
- Advantage: Gentle, can be used on sensitive skin without irritation
:::

:::note
**Plantain Poultice**

- Preparation: Pick young plantain leaves, chew or crush to release juices, apply directly to wound
- Properties: Promotes blood clotting, reduces inflammation, antimicrobial compounds
- Traditional use: Called "nature's bandaid" - draws out infection, promotes healing
- Best for: Infected wounds, splinter removal, bee stings
:::

:::note
**Charcoal & Clay Paste**

- Preparation: Crush activated charcoal (from burned wood), mix with bentonite clay and water to create paste
- Use: Apply thick layer to infected wound, cover with cloth, change daily
- Properties: Absorbs toxins and bacteria. Draws out infection. Charcoal is antimicrobial.
- Caution: Stains skin/cloth dark color; temporary effect
:::

### Herbal Pain Relief

:::note
**Willow Bark Decoction (Natural Aspirin)**

- Active ingredient: Salicin, converted to salicylic acid in body (same compound as aspirin)
- Preparation: Harvest inner bark from willow branches (white, young growth). Dry and chop finely.
- Decoction: Boil 1 tablespoon dried bark in 1 cup water for 5-10 minutes, strain, drink warm
- Dosage: 1 cup 2-3 times daily for pain and inflammation. Effects take 15-30 minutes.
- Caution: Do not use if allergic to aspirin. Not suitable for pregnancy. Can cause stomach upset; take with food.
:::

:::note
**Turmeric & Ginger Paste (Anti-inflammatory)**

- Ingredients: 1 tablespoon turmeric powder + 1 tablespoon fresh ginger paste + honey to bind
- Internal use: Mix with warm milk/water, drink 1-2 times daily
- External use: Apply directly to inflamed areas, cover with cloth
- Properties: Curcumin in turmeric is potent anti-inflammatory. Ginger adds pain relief.
- Timeline: Effects build over 3-5 days with regular use. Most effective for chronic pain.
:::

:::note
**Arnica Compress (Bruise & Muscle Pain)**

- Preparation: Steep dried arnica flowers (1 tablespoon per cup) in boiling water 10-15 minutes
- Use: Soak cloth in cooled liquid, apply as compress to bruised/sore areas
- Properties: Reduces bruising, inflammation, speeds healing of muscle injuries
- Caution: Never ingest arnica (toxic). Use only topically. Do not apply to broken skin.
:::

## Orthopedic Emergencies

### Splinting Techniques for Different Bones

**General Splinting Principles:**

1.  Immobilize the joint above and below the fracture
2.  Remove tight clothing/jewelry before swelling occurs
3.  Check circulation: can feel pulse, normal color/temperature distal to injury
4.  Check sensation: person can feel distal to injury
5.  Use padding to protect bony prominences
6.  Apply ice after splinting to reduce swelling (20 min on, 20 min off)
7.  Elevate when possible
8.  Do NOT move the fracture during splinting

**Arm Fracture Splinting (Upper Arm/Humerus):**

1.  Use sling to immobilize arm across chest
2.  Can add padding and wrap arm to body for additional support
3.  Keep elbow at 90 degree angle
4.  Check circulation in hand regularly

**Forearm/Wrist Fracture Splinting:**

1.  Align forearm in neutral position (palm down)
2.  Splint from just below elbow to beyond fingertips
3.  Use padded board, roll of cloth, or cardboard
4.  Immobilize with sling and wrap around body
5.  Keep wrist elevated

**Hand Fracture Splinting:**

1.  For individual finger: tape to adjacent finger (buddy tape), or use small splint
2.  Keep hand elevated in functional position (as if holding golf ball)
3.  Apply ice, elevate

**Leg Fracture Splinting (Femur/Thigh):**

1.  This is a major fracture with severe bleeding risk
2.  Apply traction splint if possible (realigns bone, reduces bleeding and pain)
3.  Position person on back, measure from groin to 4 inches past heel
4.  Wrap traction splint around leg, create anchor at ankle using cloth loops
5.  Apply gentle longitudinal traction to realign limb
6.  Without traction splint: immobilize with padded boards on both sides of leg, secure at multiple points
7.  Monitor circulation: check foot color, temperature, pulse at ankle

**Knee Fracture/Dislocation:**

1.  Immobilize knee in position found - do NOT try to straighten
2.  Use pillow or padding under and around knee
3.  Wrap with elastic bandage for support
4.  Elevate leg
5.  Apply ice

**Ankle Fracture Splinting:**

1.  Remove shoe carefully
2.  Apply ice before swelling becomes severe
3.  Use pillow splint: wrap pillow around ankle and foot, secure with cloth
4.  Or use rigid splint: padded board on both sides of ankle
5.  Elevate foot above heart
6.  Do NOT try to straighten ankle if deformed

### Reduction of Simple Dislocations

:::warning
**Critical:** Only attempt reduction if: (1) you have some training, (2) professional help is not available, (3) there is no associated fracture, (4) you have adequate pain relief. Get X-ray confirmation when possible after reduction.
:::

**Shoulder Dislocation (Anterior - Most Common):**

1.  **Check first:** X-ray if possible to rule out fracture
2.  **Anesthesia:** Give strong pain relief before attempting reduction
3.  **Technique:** Patient lying on back, bend elbow to 90 degrees, gently rotate arm outward (external rotation) until shoulder pops back into socket
4.  **Alternative:** Some methods use gentle traction on arm while internally rotating
5.  **Success:** You will feel/hear the shoulder slip back into place
6.  **After:** Immobilize in sling for 2-4 weeks, ice, pain relief
7.  **Complications:** Stop immediately if severe pain, signs of nerve/blood vessel damage

**Finger Dislocation (Most Common Hand Dislocation):**

1.  **Anesthesia:** Give pain relief - even local if available
2.  **Technique:** Apply gentle longitudinal traction to finger while straightening it
3.  **Direction:** Usually finger is bent backward - apply traction and gently flex forward
4.  **Success:** Feel/see the joint slide back into place
5.  **After:** Buddy tape to adjacent finger, ice, elevation
6.  **Check:** Make sure finger looks straight and aligned

### Sling Construction

**Improvised Arm Sling:**

1.  Take cloth strip (scarf, bandage, or torn sheet) about 40-50 inches long
2.  Place one end at shoulder, bring diagonally across chest
3.  Support arm at 90 degree elbow angle
4.  Bring cloth under elbow and around to opposite shoulder
5.  Tie at shoulder, ensuring arm is fully supported
6.  Adjust so no pressure on neck vessels or nerves
7.  Arm should be at heart level or above

### When NOT to Move a Patient

:::warning
**Conditions Requiring Spinal Immobilization:**

- Trauma to head, neck, or back
- Loss of consciousness or altered mental status
- Severe pain in neck or back
- Inability to move arms or legs
- Tingling or numbness in extremities
- Any mechanism of injury that could cause spinal damage

**Action:** Keep person still, support head and neck, seek immediate professional help. Moving a person with spinal injury can cause paralysis.
:::

## Environmental Emergencies

### Hypothermia (Core Temperature Below 95Ã‚Â°F/35Ã‚Â°C)

**Hypothermia Stages and Treatment:**

1.  **Mild (90-95Ã‚Â°F):** Shivering, confusion, slurred speech. Warm gradually (blankets, warm drinks), avoid rapid rewarming
2.  **Moderate (82-90Ã‚Â°F):** Shivering stops, drowsiness, poor coordination, possible irregular heartbeat. Handle gently (rough movement can cause cardiac arrhythmia). Insulate from ground, apply heat packs to groin, armpits, neck
3.  **Severe (68-82Ã‚Â°F):** Unconscious, barely perceptible pulse/breathing, possible rigid muscles. Do NOT attempt to rewarm in field - could cause afterdrop (further cooling when peripheral blood returns to cold core). Insulate, monitor vitals, get to professional help with care
4.  **Profound (<68Ã‚Â°F):** Appears dead (no pulse, no breathing, rigid). Do NOT give up - with proper rewarming, people can recover from severe hypothermia. Transport carefully, gentle rewarming only in hospital with ECMO capability if available

**Rewarming Procedure (Mild to Moderate):**

1.  Move to shelter, remove wet clothing
2.  Apply passive external rewarming: blankets, insulation from ground
3.  Active external rewarming: heat packs, warm (not hot) water bottles placed at core (groin, armpits, neck)
4.  For mild: warm drinks if alert and can swallow
5.  Avoid hot drinks, alcohol, exercise (these cause rapid heat loss from core)
6.  Monitor core temperature if possible
7.  Continue warming for several hours
8.  Watch for afterdrop: further drop in core temperature after rewarming starts

:::warning
**Hypothermia Saying:** "It's not over until it's over" - severely hypothermic patients can recover with no brain damage after prolonged rewarming. Continue resuscitation attempts.
:::

### Heat Stroke and Heat Exhaustion

:::note
**Heat Exhaustion (Core Temp 98.6-104Ã‚Â°F):**

- **Symptoms:** Heavy sweating, weakness, dizziness, nausea, fast pulse, cool/clammy skin, muscle cramps
- **Treatment:** Move to cool area, drink water, cool body with wet cloths, elevate legs
- **Recovery:** Usually improves within 30 minutes with cooling and fluids
:::

:::note
**Heat Stroke (Core Temp >104Ã‚Â°F) - MEDICAL EMERGENCY:**

- **Symptoms:** No sweating, hot/dry skin, severe weakness, confusion, possible seizures, altered consciousness
- **Difference from heat exhaustion:** Lack of sweating and altered mental status are key - body's cooling system has failed
- **Treatment:** Immerse in cold water if possible (most effective), or apply ice packs to neck, armpits, groin (major blood vessels)
- **Fluids:** Only if alert and can swallow
- **Urgency:** Medical emergency - seek help immediately, death risk is high
:::

### Drowning Response and Recovery

**Drowning Rescue and Resuscitation:**

1.  **Safety first:** Don't become another drowning victim - extend object or reach from shore if possible rather than entering water
2.  **Remove from water:** Get person out as quickly as possible
3.  **Airway:** If not breathing, begin rescue breaths immediately (water in lungs doesn't prevent breathing rescue)
4.  **CPR:** Start chest compressions if no pulse
5.  **Hypothermia:** Drowning in cold water may cause hypothermia - follow hypothermia protocol for rewarming
6.  **Recovery position:** If breathing but unconscious, position on side to allow water drainage
7.  **Seek medical help:** Even if person seems to recover, secondary drowning (pulmonary edema from water in lungs) can occur hours later - get professional evaluation

### Snakebite Protocols

**Snakebite First Aid:**

1.  **Prevention:** Most important - wear boots, watch where hands/feet go, leave snake alone
2.  **Immediately after bite:** Remove jewelry, immobilize limb, place below heart level
3.  **Venom extraction:** Do NOT cut, suck, or use tourniquet (these cause more harm)
4.  **Cleaning:** Gently wash bite with soap and water
5.  **Immobilization:** Splint the limb - venom spreads with movement
6.  **Monitoring:** Mark the swelling with pen and time - swelling should not progress beyond marked area in 15 minutes if dry bite (20% of bites are dry)
7.  **Pain management:** Opioids if available for pain
8.  **Antivenom:** If available and patient is progressing (worsening swelling, systemic symptoms), antivenom is most effective within first few hours
9.  **Observation:** Monitor for systemic symptoms: bleeding, neurologic symptoms, shock
10.  **Seek professional help:** Medical care should be sought - even dry bites need monitoring

### Insect Sting Reactions

**Local Sting Reaction:**

1.  Remove stinger if visible (scrape off, don't pinch)
2.  Clean area with soap and water
3.  Ice for pain and swelling
4.  Hydrocortisone cream for itching
5.  Antihistamine if itching severe
6.  Avoid scratching to prevent infection

**Anaphylactic Reaction to Sting (Medical Emergency):**

1.  **Signs:** Difficulty breathing, swelling of mouth/throat, hives, dizziness, abdominal pain
2.  **Use epinephrine immediately:** EpiPen 0.3-0.5mg IM into outer thigh
3.  **Position:** Lie flat with legs elevated (unless having respiratory difficulty)
4.  **Follow-up medications:** After epinephrine, give antihistamine (diphenhydramine 50mg) and corticosteroid if available
5.  **Repeat epinephrine:** If symptoms don't improve in 5-15 minutes, give second dose
6.  **Seek emergency help:** Anaphylaxis can progress, medical care is essential

### Altitude Sickness

:::note
**Acute Mountain Sickness (AMS) - Up to 8,000 feet:**

- **Symptoms:** Headache, nausea, fatigue, dizziness, shortness of breath
- **Prevention:** Ascend gradually, stay hydrated, avoid alcohol
- **Treatment:** Descend if symptoms severe, rest, oxygen if available, acetaminophen for headache
- **Resolution:** Usually improves within 24 hours with acclimatization
:::

:::note
**High Altitude Cerebral Edema (HACE) - Serious:**

- **Symptoms:** Severe headache unresponsive to pain relief, confusion, ataxia (poor coordination), altered mental status
- **Treatment:** DESCEND IMMEDIATELY - this can be fatal
- **Oxygen:** If available, give supplemental oxygen
- **Dexamethasone:** 4mg every 6 hours if available (corticosteroid to reduce brain swelling)
- **Seek emergency care:** Medical evacuation needed
:::

### Lightning Strike

**Lightning Strike Treatment:**

1.  **Safety:** Don't approach if lightning storm still active - wait until all clear
2.  **Check responsiveness:** Person may appear dead but can recover
3.  **CPR:** If no pulse/breathing, begin CPR immediately and continue for extended period
4.  **Entry/exit wounds:** Check for burn marks where current entered and exited
5.  **Spinal precautions:** Potential for spinal injury from lightning or fall - immobilize spine
6.  **Cardiac monitoring:** Arrhythmias may occur - monitor heart rate and rhythm if possible
7.  **Fluid resuscitation:** May need significant fluids (more than usual burn)
8.  **Seek medical help:** All lightning strikes need professional evaluation

## Poisoning and Overdose

### Poison Assessment

Determine: substance (if possible), route of exposure (ingestion, inhalation, skin, injection), time of exposure, amount, patient condition. Symptoms vary by toxin: altered mental status, seizures, respiratory depression, arrhythmias, GI upset, skin/eye irritation.

### General Poisoning Management

- **Activate airway protection:** Position for drainage, be prepared to suction
- **Decontamination:** Remove contaminated clothing, wash skin thoroughly with water (avoid harsh scrubbing)
- **Induced vomiting:** Generally NOT recommended (risks aspiration, further tissue damage); only for specific substances with professional guidance
- **Activated charcoal:** If available and ingestion <1-2 hours: 50-100g mixed with water (only if airway protected). Binds many toxins, prevents absorption
- **Supportive care:** Fluids, monitor airway, manage seizures with benzodiazepines if available
- **Antidotes:** Specific to toxin (naloxone for opioids, methylene blue for methemoglobinemia, etc.)

### Overdose fast route

Use this route immediately. Treat these patterns as overdose or toxidrome emergencies first, not as routine medication side effects or something to "sleep off."

Use this route immediately if the person:

- took too many pain pills or unknown pills and will not stay awake
- mixed pills and alcohol and is hard to wake
- has slow breathing or pinpoint pupils after pills
- is confused or dangerously sleepy after a double dose
- wakes up after naloxone and then gets sleepy again
- may be overdose or toxidrome rather than a routine side effect, especially if breathing or alertness is getting worse

### What matters first

1. Check breathing right now.
2. If breathing is slow, absent, or abnormal, start [CPR](#cpr) and use naloxone if available.
3. If breathing but hard to wake or confused, place on the side, keep the airway open, and do not give more pills, alcohol, food, or drink by mouth.
4. Treat repeat sleepiness after naloxone as repeat sedation from ongoing overdose until proven otherwise.
5. Call emergency services or begin the fastest available evacuation now. Do not leave the person alone.

## Complications & When to Stop Care

### Life-Threatening Complications

Complications during first aid require immediate recognition and response. The most dangerous are:

:::danger
**Tension Pneumothorax (Life-Threat):** Collapsed lung with continued air entry creates pressure that collapses the other lung. Signs: severe dyspnea, unilateral absent breath sounds, JVD (jugular venous distension), tracheal deviation toward opposite side, shock-like presentation. Field treatment: needle decompression (insert 14-16 gauge needle at midclavicular line, 2nd intercostal space). If this occurs post-trauma, suspect penetrating injury to chest.
:::

:::danger
**Hemorrhagic Shock Progression to Cardiac Arrest:** Despite interventions, Class IV hemorrhage may progress to cardiac arrest. Perform CPR if patient arrests. If no ROSC after 20 minutes of resuscitation in setting where evacuation is impossible and patient is normothermic, consider terminating resuscitation.
:::

:::danger
**Airway Compromise from Angioedema:** In anaphylaxis or severe facial trauma, swelling of airway can occlude it completely within minutes. Signs: difficulty swallowing, drooling, stridor (on inspiration) or stertor (on expiration), tripod positioning. Prepare for emergency airway if trained; otherwise position for drainage, have suction ready, consider epinephrine IM immediately if anaphylaxis suspected.
:::

### Secondary Complications

- **Rhabdomyolysis (crush injury):** Myoglobin release causes dark urine. Risk of acute kidney injury. Aggressive fluid resuscitation (goal urine output 200-300 mL/hour) can mitigate. If dark cola-colored urine develops, increase fluids and prepare for evacuation.
- **Fat embolism (after long-bone fracture):** Delayed (24-48 hours): confusion, petechial rash on chest/axillae, shortness of breath. Supportive care only in field; prevent by early immobilization.
- **Tetanus risk (after puncture/crush wounds):** If booster status unknown or >5 years since last dose, tetanus risk high. If available, give tetanus toxoid IM (0.5 mL). If previously never vaccinated, give 3-dose series over 6 months if possible.

### When to Terminate Resuscitation

In resource-limited settings, resuscitation is terminated when:

1. ROSC achieved (return of spontaneous circulation)
2. Obvious death (rigor mortis, dependent lividity, decapitation, decomposition, immersion in ice >6 hours)
3. Resuscitation ongoing for >30-60 minutes with no signs of life (normothermic patient)
4. Patient hypothermic but unrecovered after passive external rewarming and >3 hours no vital signs (or until core temperature >30Ã‚Â°C)
5. Rescuer safety threatened or rescuer exhaustion prevents effective CPR
6. Signed DNR (do-not-resuscitate) order present

:::info-box
**Cold Water Exception:** Hypothermic patients have survived complete cardiac arrest >3 hours if in extremely cold water. Motto: "No one is dead until they are warm and dead." Continue gentle resuscitation (avoid rough movement that can trigger VF in hypothermic heart) and slow rewarming.
:::

## Recovery & Aftercare

### Post-Trauma Monitoring (0-72 hours)

Immediate post-trauma period is high-risk. Monitor for delayed deterioration:

**Hours 0-6:**
- Vitals every 15-30 minutes (heart rate, blood pressure, respiratory rate, mental status)
- Assess for hidden bleeding (abdominal distension, femoral area swelling, chest asymmetry)
- Pain control: ibuprofen 600-800 mg every 6 hours (if no GI bleed suspected) or acetaminophen 650-1000 mg every 4-6 hours
- Prevent aspiration: NPO (nothing by mouth) if altered mental status or potential abdominal injury
- Tetanus status: update if needed

**Hours 6-24:**
- Vitals every 1-2 hours
- Monitor wound for oozing, drainage character change
- Start dressing changes if indicated (wet or contaminated)
- Elevate injured areas
- Reassess neurovascular status in fractured limbs every 2-4 hours

**Hours 24-72:**
- Vitals every 4-6 hours (if stable)
- Wound care daily, watch for infection
- Pain reassessment; consider reducing opioids if available
- Early movement of uninjured joints to prevent stiffness
- Monitor for fever (indicates infection or other complication)

### Aftercare Timeline by Injury Type

Infection risk is highest days 2-10 post-injury. Daily assessment protocol:

- **Appearance:** Redness extending >2 cm from wound edges, yellow/green/foul drainage, black discoloration (necrosis), increasing warmth
- **Systemic signs:** Temperature >38.5Ã‚Â°C (101Ã‚Â°F), chills, malaise, headache, lymphadenopathy (swollen nodes in draining direction)

## Troubleshooting: What If Your Intervention Isn't Working?

### CPR Not Producing Chest Rise with Rescue Breaths

**Problem:** Breaths don't cause visible chest rise; you're giving breaths but airway still obstructed.

**Diagnosis:** Airway repositioning hasn't worked, or foreign body still present.

**Action:**
1. Reposition airway aggressively: head-tilt-chin-lift or jaw-thrust
2. If jaw-thrust not yet tried, try it
3. Look in mouth; if object visible and can grasp safely, remove it
4. Consider oral airway insertion if available and patient unconscious
5. If still unsuccessful after 2-3 attempts at repositioning: continue hands-only CPR (compressions alone), as this is nearly as effective in adults

### Tourniquet Applied but Bleeding Continues

**Problem:** Tourniquet in place for 2+ minutes but blood still oozing around it or wound continues to bleed.

**Diagnosis:** Tourniquet placement incorrect (too distal, not tight enough, or placed loosely over clothing)

**Action:**
1. Tighten significantly more (should be tight enough that patient would complain if conscious)
2. If still bleeding, apply second tourniquet above (proximal to) the first
3. Check that tourniquet is on skin/bone, not just soft tissue
4. If it's limb amputation above tourniquet, consider packing proximal wound with gauze
5. Document time and move to next priority

### Severe Burn Patient Hypothermic Despite Being Near Fire/Heat

**Problem:** Large burn patient is shivering, confused, core temperature dropping despite environmental heat.

**Diagnosis:** Massive fluid/heat loss through burned skin causing severe hypothermia risk.

**Action:**
1. Cover burn wounds with clean, dry blankets (both insulate and prevent evaporative cooling)
2. Remove wet clothing
3. Provide warm fluids (if conscious and swallowing; avoid hot liquids that increase fluid loss)
4. Seek passive rewarming (shelter, keep dry, close skin-to-skin if available)
5. If IV fluids available, warm them before administration
6. Prepare for evacuation; severe burns combined with hypothermia is high mortality

### Patient with Crush Injury Refuses Evacuation

**Problem:** Patient trapped briefly, now alert and refusing evacuation. Crush injury to leg.

**Diagnosis:** Possible crush syndrome; patient may deteriorate rapidly once tourniquet or compression released.

**Action:**
1. Explain that rhabdomyolysis (muscle breakdown from crush) may cause sudden shock/kidney failure within hours
2. Start IV fluids immediately if available (aggressive hydration critical)
3. Urine may turn dark brown/cola-colored; this is sign of severe myoglobinuria
4. Insist on evacuation even if patient protests; crush injuries are deceptive
5. If evacuation delayed >4 hours, maintain aggressive fluid intake, monitor urine color hourly

## Field Adaptations: Making Do Without Ideal Equipment

### CPR Without CPR Barrier

If no pocket mask or face shield: hands-only CPR (compressions without breaths) is preferred in adults. If trained in rescue breathing and willing: tilt head, pinch nose, create seal with mouth, deliver breath. Risk of disease transmission exists but lower than no resuscitation.

### Bleeding Control Without Hemostatic Gauze

Hemostatic agents accelerate clotting. Without them: clean gauze, any cloth, even leaves or moss can work. Key is sustained direct pressure for 10-15 minutes without lifting. If tourniquet needed, cloth tourniquets work as well as commercial; wrap tightly 2-3 inches above wound.

### Pain Relief Without Medications

- Willow bark tea (contains salicin, precursor to aspirin): steep inner bark 5-10 minutes
- Ginger tea: anti-inflammatory
- Elevation and ice (if available) to reduce swelling
- Immobilization of injured part reduces pain significantly
- Guided breathing: slow, deep breathing activates parasympathetic nervous system

### Fluid Resuscitation in Dehydrated Patient with No IV Access

- Oral rehydration solution (1 L boiled water + 6 teaspoons sugar + Ã‚Â½ teaspoon salt)
- Small frequent sips (every 5 minutes, 30 mL per sip) better tolerated than large volumes
- If patient vomiting: wait 10 minutes, try again more slowly
- Target: 2-3 L over 24 hours if conscious and tolerating oral (equivalent to modest IV resuscitation)

### Improvised Splinting Materials

- Cardboard: lightweight, can be shaped, secure with cloth strips
- Rolled newspaper: surprisingly stiff when wrapped, compress with tape
- Branches/bark: natural availability, may need padding with cloth
- Pillows: soft, good for upper extremity
- Sling: use cloth strip around neck, support upper arm
- Elevate on rolled clothing to reduce swelling

:::tip
**Universal Principle:** Any immobilization is better than none. Swelling and pain worsen if fracture moves. Even crude splinting prevents converting simple fracture to compound (open) fracture.
:::

### Critical Safety Protocols

- Scene safety is paramount: do not become victim yourself. Assess hazards before entry.
- Airway emergency (inability to maintain patent airway): prepare for emergency airway procedures (cricothyrotomy if trained)
- Severe hemorrhage with shock: apply tourniquet immediately if limb injury, even if pressure hasn't failed yet
- Poisoning with altered consciousness: assume full stomach; protect airway, prepare for vomiting
- Anaphylaxis: epinephrine IM (0.3-0.5mg IM in outer thigh, repeat every 5-15 min as needed) before antihistamines

## Common First Aid Errors

1. **Tourniquet hesitation:** Applying too late results in permanent disability or loss of limb; apply early for severe limb bleeding. Consequence: tissue death, amputation. *Fix: If arterial bleeding, don't wait for pressure to failÃ¢â‚¬â€apply tourniquet promptly.*

2. **Ineffective CPR:** Shallow compressions (pressing <2 inches or <100 bpm) fail to generate adequate perfusion. Consequence: brain death despite continued CPR. *Fix: Press hard (2-2.4 inches), push fast (100-120 bpm), allow full recoil between compressions.*

3. **Airway neglect:** Unprotected airway in altered consciousness = aspiration of vomit/blood. Consequence: asphyxiation, aspiration pneumonia. *Fix: Position on side immediately, monitor airway, prepare for suctioning.*

4. **Infection spread:** Reusing contaminated dressings or touching wounds with unwashed hands spreads bacteria. Consequence: wound infection, sepsis. *Fix: Use clean/sterile materials for each dressing change, wear gloves, wash hands between patients.*

5. **Shock underestimation:** Waiting for shock to appear before intervening wastes critical time. Consequence: progression to Class IV shock, death. *Fix: Anticipate shock; begin interventions (elevation, bleeding control, fluids) immediately upon injury.*

6. **Over-tightening tourniquets:** Excessive tightness can crush remaining soft tissue. Consequence: tissue damage beyond the original injury. *Fix: Tighten until bleeding stops completely, then stopÃ¢â‚¬â€tight is good, crushing is not.*

7. **Ignoring subtle signs:** Mild tachycardia, anxiety, mild confusion early in shock are often dismissed. Consequence: shock progression missed. *Fix: Take vital signs systematically; recognize early compensation as sign of blood loss.*

:::danger
**Critical Mistake: Removing Tourniquet in Field:** Tourniquet removal by non-surgeon can cause sudden reperfusion shock (fluid released from dead muscle tissue causes hypotension) and renewed hemorrhage. Leave the tourniquet in place until professional surgical care is available; do not remove it in the field just to inspect the wound or adjust clothing.
:::
