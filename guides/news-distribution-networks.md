## Overview

Information is a public good. Well-informed communities make better decisions, respond faster to emergencies, and resist manipulation. Without reliable information channels, rumors multiply, panic spreads, and bad decisions result. Modern communities rely on internet; in low-connectivity settings, low-tech systems (bulletin boards, town criers, printed newsletters, radio broadcasts) maintain shared knowledge.

Effective information systems balance accessibility (everyone can receive messages), reliability (information is accurate), speed (news reaches people quickly), and resistance to misinformation.

</section>

<section id="bulletin-boards">

## Bulletin Boards: Public Notice System

A **bulletin board** is a physical posting surface where community notices are displayed. It serves as the primary information source in communities without electronic media.

### Design and Placement

**Location:** Central, high-traffic area (town square, market, main entrance to meeting hall). Visible to 70%+ of daily foot traffic.

**Physical design:**

| **Component** | **Specification** | **Purpose** |
|---|---|---|
| Frame | Wood, 4×4 posts, 6–8 ft height | Visibility; weatherproofing |
| Surface | Cork, felt, or slate | Accepts pins/nails without damage |
| Roof | Sloped or peaked; overhang 2 ft | Protects postings from rain |
| Lighting | Lantern (if evening reading expected) | Allows nighttime reading |
| Size | 4×8 feet typical | Holds ~50 notices simultaneously |

**Organization:** Divide surface into sections:

```
[Emergency alerts at top, red background]
[Announcements: meetings, events, 2nd row]
[Regulations and laws: 3rd row]
[Community news and achievements: 4th row]
[Lost/found, personal items: lower section]
```

### Notice Standards

All notices follow a standard format for consistency:

```
[Title, large print, all caps]
WHO: Organization issuing notice
WHAT: What event/announcement
WHEN: Date, time, or deadline
WHERE: Location or contact
WHY: Brief explanation (optional)
CONTACT: Name, method to reach organizer

DATED: [Date posted]
EXPIRES: [Date to remove]
```

**Example:**

```
WATER MAIN REPAIR

WHO: Town Works Department
WHAT: Main street water line replacement
WHEN: March 15–17, 8 AM–5 PM daily
WHERE: Main Street from 2nd to 4th Avenue
WHY: Pipes 30+ years old; leaking; replacement prevents water loss

CONTACT: John Smith, Works Director, Main Hall second floor

DATED: March 1, 2026
EXPIRES: March 20, 2026
```

### Maintenance

- **Daily:** Remove expired notices; ensure readable remaining notices.
- **Weekly:** Clean surface; check for damage.
- **Monthly:** Repaint or re-cover if worn.

**Notice lifetime:** Typically 1–4 weeks (depends on urgency). Emergency notices (disease, evacuation) stay up until threat passes. Routine announcements removed after event/deadline.

<section id="town-crier">

## Town Crier and Oral Proclamation

A **town crier** is a person who announces information verbally in public spaces. Fast for urgent messages; reaches people who don't regularly check bulletin boards.

### Crier Training and Protocol

**Responsibility:** Crier is trained in:

- **Clear pronunciation:** Speaks slowly, loudly, repeating each announcement 3× (people hear at distance with noise).
- **Accuracy:** Memorizes exact wording (misstatement causes panic or confusion).
- **Judgment:** Knows which information is urgent enough for proclamation vs. routine bulletin board posting.

**Announcement format:**

```
[Ring bell or use hand drum for attention]

"Hear ye! Hear ye! Hear ye!"  [Pause; let crowd gather]

[Message, slow and clear]
"Water shortage expected. Ration water to essential uses only. Limit to 1 gallon per person per day starting tomorrow morning."

[Repeat message]
"Water shortage expected. Ration water to essential uses only. Limit to 1 gallon per person per day starting tomorrow morning."

[Repeat once more]
"Water shortage expected. Ration water to essential uses only. Limit to 1 gallon per person per day starting tomorrow morning."

"Directions available at town hall. God save the community!"
```

### Crier Routes and Schedule

**Route:** Fixed path through town visiting high-traffic areas (market, town square, church steps, harbor, main road).

**Timing:** Early morning (before work), midday (lunch break), and evening (before dusk) to maximize audience.

**Frequency:** Daily for recurring announcements; every 4 hours for urgent news (emergency, public health threat).

**Crier assignment:** In larger towns, 2–3 criers cover different neighborhoods simultaneously.

<section id="printed-newsletters">

## Printed Newsletter Production

A **newsletter** is a periodic printed publication (weekly or monthly) summarizing community news, announcements, and important information.

### Design and Content

**Frequency:** Weekly (easier to produce; captures time-sensitive news) or monthly (less resource-intensive; good for established routine).

**Format:** One or two sheets (4–8 pages when folded); fits in pocket.

**Content sections:**

| **Section** | **Content** | **Space** |
|---|---|---|
| Masthead | Newsletter name, date, issue number | 10% |
| Emergency alerts | Urgent news (disease, disaster, evacuation) | 5–15% |
| Community news | Births, deaths, weddings, achievements | 15% |
| Announcements | Upcoming meetings, events, deadlines | 20% |
| Regulations/laws | New rules, enforcement updates | 10% |
| Market/trading | Goods for sale, trade notices | 10% |
| Personal items | Lost/found, requests for help | 10% |
| Editorial | Community leader's message, commentary | 10% |

**Sample 4-page layout:**

```
Page 1: Masthead + emergency alerts + featured announcement
Page 2: Community news + upcoming events
Page 3: Announcements + regulations
Page 4: Classifieds (personal items, trading) + editorial
```

### Production Process

**Timeline (weekly newsletter, published Thursday):**

| **Day** | **Task** | **Duration** |
|---|---|---|
| Monday | Collect announcements (deadline Monday 5 PM) | Ongoing |
| Tuesday | Write articles, organize layout | 3–4 hours |
| Wednesday | Printing and folding | 4–6 hours |
| Thursday | Distribution begins | - |

**Staffing:**

- **Editor:** Gathers and writes content (4 hours/week).
- **Layout/design:** Arranges text and images (2 hours/week).
- **Printer/operator:** Operates printing press (4–6 hours/week).
- **Distribution:** Delivers copies to homes, market, public places (2–3 hours/week).
- **Total:** ~15–20 person-hours per week for 500-copy print run.

**Printing technology:**

- **Letterpress (traditional):** Lead or wood type arranged by hand; slow (100 copies/hour) but robust.
- **Offset lithography:** Photographic process; faster (500+ copies/hour); requires darkroom/chemicals.
- **Hectograph (gelatin):** Carbon-based copying; 20–50 copies per stencil; low-cost but poor quality.

**Distribution:** Hand-deliver to homes (door-to-door, 1–2 hours per neighborhood), post at bulletin boards, place at market and public buildings.

## Information Verification and Rumor Control

Rumors spread quickly and cause panic. Communities need systems to verify information and counter false claims.

### Verification Protocols

**Primary source requirement:** Information is accepted only if from direct witness, official record, or credible messenger from official source.

```
Claim: "Disease spreading in the valley; people are dying"
Verification:
- Direct witness: Person who saw sick individuals or deaths (credible if knows the valley)
- Official record: Doctor's log or town registry showing death certificates
- Credible messenger: Doctor or health official with responsibility for the area

Without one of these, the claim is rumor and should not be spread.
```

**Cross-check:** If information is urgent, verify with at least 2 independent sources.

```
"Water shortage announced. Check at town hall."

Source 1: Town crier said so.
Source 2: Check with works director or official notice at town hall.

If both confirm, the information is reliable and can be announced.
If sources conflict, investigate further before announcing.
```

### Rumor Management

**Rumor identification:**

- **Vague claims:** "Something bad is happening" (no specifics).
- **Attribution to unnamed sources:** "I heard from someone who heard..." (chain of unnamed sources).
- **Emotional language:** "Disaster!" "Terrible news!" (heightens alarm without facts).
- **Spread pattern:** Claim changes as it spreads; each retelling adds embellishment.

**Counter-rumor process:**

1. **Identify:** Track which rumor is spreading (what is it claiming?).
2. **Investigate:** Find out what actually happened (talk to official sources).
3. **Respond:** Publish official information promptly (wait delays response).

```
Rumor: "They're poisoning the water supply to control us!"
Fact: Water treatment uses chlorine to kill disease-causing bacteria.
Response (published in newsletter and announced by crier): "Water chlorination prevents cholera and dysentery. Chlorine level is safe for drinking and cooking. No population control intent. Ask any doctor."

Publication delay: If response takes 1 week, rumor spreads unchecked; respond within 24 hours if possible.
```

**Official denial format:**

```
"The following is FALSE: [State the rumor clearly]

The TRUTH is: [State the facts with evidence]

For verification: [Direct people to official source or person]"
```

### Trusted Messengers

Certain people are trusted to deliver accurate information:

- **Health official:** On health matters.
- **Government official:** On regulations, law enforcement, public works.
- **Religious leader:** On moral/spiritual matters (sometimes can overcome institutional distrust).
- **Community elder:** On local tradition and precedent.

If these people make statements, community accepts them more readily than stranger or anonymous source.

:::warning
Misinformation can cause more harm than the original problem. A false claim that medicine is poisoned may cause people to reject treatment and die from treatable disease. Rumor control is as essential as primary information. Invest in accurate, timely communication.
:::

## Community Radio Broadcasting

A **radio station** (low-power transmitter) can reach 5–50 km depending on power and terrain. Radio is faster than printed media and reaches people continuously.

### Radio System Setup

**Components:**

- **Transmitter:** 1–10 watts (community broadcast range: 5–15 km); 25–100 watts (15–50 km range).
- **Antenna:** Dipole or monopole; mounted 20+ feet high for best range.
- **Receiver (at home):** AM/FM radio receiver (common, inexpensive).
- **Broadcast material:** News, music, educational content; live or pre-recorded.

**Power consumption:** 10–100 watts of electrical power for transmitter; requires daily solar charge or generator fuel.

**Frequency allocation (if governance possible):** Local communities can choose frequencies if not regulated by government:

- **AM band (530–1700 kHz):** Longer range; better ground wave propagation; historical use.
- **FM band (88–108 MHz):** Shorter range but cleaner audio; modern standard.

### Programming Format

**Daily schedule (hypothetical 10-hour broadcast):**

| **Time** | **Content** | **Duration** |
|---|---|---|
| 6:00 AM | News summary, weather, announcements | 15 min |
| 6:15 AM | Music (local or recorded) | 45 min |
| 7:00 AM | Market prices and trading notices | 15 min |
| 7:15 AM | Music | 45 min |
| 8:00 AM | Educational program (health, agriculture) | 30 min |
| 8:30 AM | Music | 30 min |
| 9:00 AM | Break (7-hour silence; reduce power) | - |
| 4:00 PM | News update | 15 min |
| 4:15 PM | Music | 45 min |
| 5:00 PM | Community messages (personal notices, lost/found) | 30 min |
| 5:30 PM | Music | 30 min |
| 6:00 PM | Evening news, announcements, sign-off | 30 min |

**Total broadcast:** 6 hours/day; production time 8–10 hours (planning, recording pre-recorded segments, live hosting).

### Content Guidelines

**Announce all key information:**

```
"Road closure on Highway 5 from mile marker 12 to mile marker 20. Expected duration: 3 days. Detour via route 8 and Highway 2. Repeat: Highway 5..."

Repeat key facts 3× (listeners tune in at different times; repetition ensures reaching them).
```

**Separate fact from opinion:**

```
Fact: "Flood waters rose 2 feet overnight; 30 families evacuated."
Opinion: "We must prepare better for future floods by building a dam."

Make it clear which is which.
```

**Avoid spreading panic:**

```
Instead of: "People are dying from the new disease!" (creates panic)
Say: "New respiratory illness reported in 3 cases. Stay informed by listening to health updates. If you develop symptoms, report to the clinic." (factual, measured)
```

## Press Freedom and Independence

Press freedom--the right to publish information without government censorship--is essential for informed communities. However, responsibility must accompany freedom.

### Rights and Responsibilities

**Right to publish** (journalists can report on government, leaders, public events without fear of punishment).

**Responsibility to accuracy** (journalists must fact-check; deliberate falsehoods undermine credibility).

**Balance:** A free press that prints lies is worse than a controlled press, because it erodes trust and makes people unable to discern truth.

### Editorial Independence

**Independence principle:** News editors make editorial decisions (what to publish, what to hold) based on public interest, not government directives or personal favor.

```
Example: Government official announces "New regulation on trade."
Editor decision: Publish it (public needs to know).

Example: Local merchant wants advertisement of goods.
Editor decision: Separate section (classifieds) for commercial notices; editorial section reserved for news/announcements, not paid promotions.

Example: Rumor circulating that official embezzled funds.
Editor decision: Investigate (interview official, audit records, check sources). If credible, publish. If unverified, do not publish (would damage official's reputation unfairly).
```

### Code of Ethics

Journalists follow standard practices:

1. **Verify before publishing:** Do not print rumors without checking.
2. **Identify sources:** Say where information comes from (unnamed if necessary for safety, but identify the type of source: "official document," "witness account," "government statement").
3. **Correct errors:** If you publish something false, issue correction promptly and prominently.
4. **Separate news from opinion:** Use different sections for reporting (factual, neutral) and commentary (author's viewpoint).
5. **Respect privacy:** Don't publish names of minor children in crime reports, medical details of private citizens without consent.
6. **No conflicts of interest:** Journalists don't report on companies they own or people they have financial ties to.

## Information Priority and Classification

In emergencies or crises, some information is more urgent or sensitive than others.

**Priority levels:**

| **Level** | **Example** | **Distribution** | **Timing** |
|---|---|---|---|
| Critical | Active threat (fire, flood, enemy attack), evacuation order | All available channels (criers, sirens, radio, runners) | Immediate (minutes) |
| Urgent | Disease outbreak, resource shortage, safety guidance | Radio, bulletin boards, crier | Within hours |
| Routine | Announcements, meetings, trading notices | Newsletter, bulletin boards, optional radio | Within days |
| Background | Community history, education, cultural information | Newsletter, archive | No urgency |

**Access control (sensitive information):**

Some information should not be broadly distributed to avoid harm:

- **Military/defense information:** Enemy positions, troop movements, weapons (distribute to authorized personnel only; breaching security compromises defense).
- **Medical information:** Patient records, infectious disease cases (protect privacy; but public health threat overrides privacy).
- **Criminal investigation:** Suspect identity before arrest; witness names (protect ongoing investigation and witness safety).

**Public interest exception:** Normally private information is released if public safety requires it.

```
Example: Suspected typhoid case
Normal practice: Patient identity is private.
Outbreak situation: Names and locations of all cases are released so community can avoid the source and clean water supplies.
```

## Integration Example: Information System for 5,000-Person Town

**Town structure:** Central town + 4 neighborhoods; 500 families; economy based on agriculture + trading.

**Information system:**

**Bulletin boards (5 total):**

1. Town center (main, large, managed by town clerk).
2. Market (for trading, business notices).
3. North neighborhood (local announcements, lost/found).
4. East neighborhood (same).
5. Clinic (health notices only).

**Town crier (1 person):**

- Employed part-time by town (5 hours/day).
- Route: Market 6 AM, Town square 7 AM, Market again 11 AM, Town square 4 PM.
- Announcements issued by town clerk or official representatives only.

**Newsletter (weekly):**

- Produced by town clerk + volunteer writer.
- 2 pages, 500 copies printed on hectograph (allows quick production).
- Distributed door-to-door + markets + public buildings (reaches 70–80% of households).

**Radio station (low-power, 5 watts):**

- Broadcast range: 8 km (sufficient for town + surrounding farms).
- Schedule: 6–9 AM, 12–1 PM, 4–6 PM daily.
- Content: News summary, market prices, announcements, music, educational content.
- Power: Solar panels (2 kW peak) with battery backup; runs 3 hours/day.

**System integration:**

```
Information flows:
Town council decision → Town clerk → Bulletin boards (immediate) + Crier (next day) + Newsletter (next weekly issue) + Radio (next broadcast)

Emergency information:
"Fire at mill!" → Crier (immediate, door-to-door) → Radio (immediate broadcast) → Bulletin board (follow-up) → Newsletter (next issue)

Community response:
All households aware of major events within 24 hours (combined system redundancy).
```

**Cost estimate:**

| **Item** | **Cost** | **Notes** |
|---|---|---|
| Bulletin board (5 × $500) | $2,500 | Wood, nails, shelter |
| Town crier (salary, 5 h/day) | $5,000/year | Part-time position |
| Newsletter (production + printing) | $2,000/year | Paper, ink, labor |
| Radio transmitter + antenna | $3,000 | One-time; simple setup |
| Radio maintenance (power, replacement) | $1,000/year | Solar panels, batteries |
| **Total annual** | **~$8,000/year** | Per 5,000 people = $1.60 per capita |
