## 1. Overview: Why Thermal Injuries Kill

The human body operates within an extremely narrow core temperature range — approximately 97.0-99.5°F (36.1-37.5°C). Deviation of just a few degrees in either direction triggers cascading physiological failure. Thermal injuries account for more wilderness deaths than animal attacks, falls, and drowning combined.

**Two categories of thermal injury:**

1. **Hyperthermia (overheating):** Heat exhaustion progressing to heat stroke — the body's cooling system overwhelmed or failing
2. **Hypothermia (overcooling):** Progressive loss of core temperature — the body's heat generation unable to match heat loss

Both conditions share a dangerous characteristic: **cognitive impairment is an early symptom**. Victims often cannot recognize their own deterioration, making bystander recognition critical.

### The Thermoregulatory System

The body manages temperature through four mechanisms:

- **Radiation:** Infrared heat emission from skin (accounts for ~40% of heat loss at rest)
- **Conduction:** Direct heat transfer through contact with surfaces (significant when lying on cold ground)
- **Convection:** Heat carried away by moving air or water (wind chill, immersion)
- **Evaporation:** Sweating and respiratory moisture (primary cooling mechanism in heat)

Understanding these mechanisms is essential because treatment for thermal injuries targets specific pathways. Cooling a heat stroke victim uses conduction (ice packs) and evaporation (misting). Treating hypothermia blocks convective and conductive loss (shelter from wind, insulation from ground).

<section id="heat-exhaustion">

## 2. Heat Exhaustion: Recognition & Treatment

Heat exhaustion is the body's warning that its cooling system is overwhelmed. It is reversible with prompt treatment but progresses to heat stroke if ignored.

### Causes

- Prolonged exertion in hot environments (>85°F / 29°C)
- Inadequate fluid intake — dehydration reduces sweat production
- High humidity — prevents sweat evaporation, blocking the primary cooling pathway
- Lack of acclimatization — unacclimatized individuals lose 2-3x more sodium in sweat
- Medications that impair sweating (anticholinergics, antihistamines, beta-blockers)

### Symptoms

-   Heavy sweating with weakness
-   Dizziness, nausea, headache
-   Cool, clammy skin
-   Rapid weak pulse (100+ bpm)
-   Normal to slightly elevated core temperature (up to 103°F / 39.4°C)
-   Muscle cramps (from sodium depletion)
-   Dark concentrated urine (dehydration marker)

:::tip
**The sweating test:** In heat exhaustion, the patient is still sweating — their cooling system is working but overwhelmed. When sweating stops, the condition has likely progressed to heat stroke.
:::

### First Aid

1.  **Move to cool location:** Shade or any cooler area — even 5°F reduction helps
2.  **Drink fluids:** Cool water or oral rehydration solution (salt + sugar in water)
3.  **Cool body:** Remove excess clothing, apply cool wet cloths to skin, fan the patient
4.  **Rest:** Lie down with legs elevated 8-12 inches (improves venous return)
5.  **Recovery:** Usually responds to rest and fluids within 30-60 minutes

### When Heat Exhaustion Becomes Dangerous

Seek emergency care if:
- Symptoms do not improve within 30 minutes of treatment
- Patient cannot keep fluids down (vomiting)
- Mental status changes (confusion, irritability, combativeness)
- Core temperature exceeds 103°F (39.4°C)
- Patient has pre-existing heart or kidney conditions

<section id="heat-stroke">

## 3. Heat Stroke: Life-Threatening Emergency

Heat stroke represents complete failure of the thermoregulatory system. Core temperature rises uncontrollably, causing organ damage and death if not reversed within 30-60 minutes. Mortality ranges from 10-80% depending on treatment delay.

### Two Types

**Exertional heat stroke:** Occurs in physically active individuals (laborers, hikers, soldiers) — typically younger, healthy people pushed beyond their cooling capacity.

**Classic (non-exertional) heat stroke:** Occurs during prolonged heat exposure without adequate cooling — typically affects elderly, chronically ill, or those without access to shade/water.

### Burn Degree Classification Diagram

![Burn degree diagram showing skin layers affected: first degree superficial, second degree partial thickness, third degree full thickness, and fourth degree damage to underlying tissue](../assets/svgs/thermal-injuries-1.svg)

### Symptoms

-   High core temperature (104°F+ / 40°C+), person feels very hot to touch
-   Altered mental state (confusion, aggression, hallucinations, seizures)
-   Little or no sweating (paradoxical — cooling failure), though exertional heat stroke patients may still be sweating
-   Strong, fast pulse (120+ bpm)
-   Red, hot, dry skin (classic type) or red, hot, moist skin (exertional type)
-   Possible unconsciousness or seizures
-   Rapid, shallow breathing

:::danger
**Time is critical.** Every minute above 104°F causes cumulative organ damage. Brain, kidneys, and liver are most vulnerable. Begin cooling immediately — do not wait for emergency services to arrive.
:::

### Emergency Response Protocol

1.  **Cool rapidly — this is the single most important intervention**
    - **Cold water immersion** is the gold standard: submerge in any available cold water (stream, pond, tub, barrel)
    - If immersion unavailable: pour cold water over entire body continuously
    - Apply ice or cold packs to neck, armpits, and groin (major blood vessels closest to skin)
    - Fan vigorously while misting with water (evaporative cooling)

2.  **Remove from heat source:** Move to shade, remove clothing to maximize skin exposure

3.  **Position:** Lay on side (recovery position) if unconscious — protects airway if vomiting occurs

4.  **Monitor temperature:** Continue aggressive cooling until core temperature drops below 102°F (38.9°C), then stop to prevent overcorrection

5.  **Fluid management:**
    - If conscious and alert: small sips of cool water only
    - **Do NOT force fluids** on a confused or unconscious person — aspiration risk
    - IV fluids are ideal but require medical training and supplies

6.  **Seek definitive care:** Heat stroke causes rhabdomyolysis (muscle breakdown), kidney failure, liver damage, and coagulopathy that require hospital treatment even after successful field cooling

### Cooling Rate Targets

- **Goal:** Reduce core temperature by 0.1-0.2°C (0.2-0.35°F) per minute
- **Cold water immersion** achieves 0.2°C/min (fastest)
- **Ice packs to major vessels** achieves 0.05-0.1°C/min
- **Evaporative cooling** (fan + misting) achieves 0.05°C/min
- Faster is better — every minute matters

</section>

<section id="heat-prevention">

## 4. Heat Injury Prevention

Prevention is vastly more effective than treatment. Heat acclimatization, hydration planning, and work-rest cycling prevent nearly all heat casualties.

### Hydration Strategy

**Rate of sweat loss:** 0.5-2.0 liters per hour depending on conditions and exertion level

:::info-box
**Fluid Replacement Formula:** Required Fluid = Sweat Loss (liters/hour) x Activity Duration (hours) + 10-20% margin

Practical guideline: Drink 5-10 ounces water every 15-20 minutes during exertion in heat. Sip — do not gulp.
:::

**Example:** Hiking in 95°F desert for 6 hours:

```
Sweat loss: 1.5 liters/hour x 6 hours = 9 liters
Replacement: 9 liters + 2 liters buffer = 11 liters total needed
Drinking schedule: ~500 mL every 15 minutes
```

### Electrolyte Replacement

**Electrolyte replacement formula:**

```
Salt needed (grams) = Sweat Loss (liters) x 0.5-1.0 g/liter
For 9 liters: 4.5-9 grams salt (~1-2 teaspoons)
```

Dissolve salt + 4-6 teaspoons sugar per liter water (sugar aids intestinal absorption via sodium-glucose co-transport).

:::warning
**Hyponatremia danger:** Without electrolyte replacement, excessive water intake causes dangerously low blood sodium. Symptoms: weakness, confusion, seizures — mimics heat exhaustion but worsens with plain water. **Always add salt to replacement fluids** during prolonged heat exposure.
:::

### Acclimatization

Full heat acclimatization requires 10-14 days of graduated exposure:

- **Days 1-3:** 50% of normal workload in heat
- **Days 4-7:** 75% of normal workload
- **Days 8-14:** Full workload with monitoring
- **Result:** Sweat rate increases 50-100%, sodium losses decrease, cardiovascular efficiency improves
- **Loss:** Acclimatization decays within 1-2 weeks without continued exposure

### Additional Prevention Measures

-   **Light, loose clothing:** Allows sweat evaporation — the primary cooling mechanism
-   **Head covering:** Wide-brimmed hat reduces direct solar radiation to head and neck
-   **Shade:** Reduce direct sun exposure during peak hours (10 AM - 4 PM)
-   **Buddy system:** Partners monitor each other for early signs of heat illness
-   **Pre-hydrate:** Drink 500 mL water 30 minutes before exertion in heat
-   **Total daily need:** 15-20 liters water possible in extreme heat — plan accordingly

<section id="hypothermia-overview">

## 5. Hypothermia: Recognition & Stages

Core body temperature dropping below 95°F (35°C) causes hypothermia. It is most dangerous in cold, wet, windy conditions, but occurs even at moderate temperatures (40-50°F) when wet or exhausted. Many victims are unaware of the danger until confusion sets in — the brain is one of the first organs affected.

:::warning
**Paradoxical undressing:** In late stages of hypothermia, victims may remove clothing believing they are overheating. This is a sign of severe hypothermia requiring immediate emergency care. The phenomenon occurs because blood vessels near the skin surface suddenly dilate, creating a temporary sensation of warmth.
:::

### Hypothermia Stages & Core Temperature Progression

<table class="temp-table"><thead><tr><th scope="row">Core Temp</th><th scope="row">Stage</th><th scope="row">Symptoms</th><th scope="row">Prognosis</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>90-95°F (32-35°C)</td><td>Mild</td><td>Shivering, confusion, slurred speech, poor coordination, apathy</td><td>Good with rewarming</td></tr><tr><td>82-90°F (28-32°C)</td><td>Moderate</td><td>Shivering ceases, muscle rigidity, drowsiness, paradoxical undressing, slowed heart rate</td><td>Good with careful rewarming</td></tr><tr><td>68-82°F (20-28°C)</td><td>Severe</td><td>Unconscious, minimal vital signs, dilated pupils, very slow or irregular heartbeat</td><td>Requires hospital care — possible recovery with ECMO</td></tr><tr><td>Below 68°F (20°C)</td><td>Profound</td><td>No detectable vital signs, cardiac arrhythmias, appears dead</td><td>Usually fatal unless extracorporeal rewarming available</td></tr></tbody></table>

:::info-box
**Critical rule:** "No one is dead until they are warm and dead." Hypothermic patients have survived without heartbeat for extended periods. Do not stop resuscitation until core temperature has been raised to at least 90°F (32°C) or until definitive medical care pronounces death.
:::

### How Hypothermia Progresses

1. **Early (95-97°F):** Shivering begins. Judgment impaired — victim may deny being cold. Fine motor control degraded (cannot strike a match, zip a zipper).

2. **Mild (90-95°F):** Violent shivering. Confusion worsens. Speech becomes slurred. Victim stumbles, drops items. May become withdrawn or combative.

3. **Moderate (82-90°F):** Shivering stops — this is an ominous sign meaning the body has lost the ability to generate heat through muscle contraction. Muscles become rigid. Drowsiness sets in. Heart rate and breathing slow.

4. **Severe (<82°F):** Unconsciousness. Pulse may be undetectable. Pupils dilate. Cardiac arrhythmias develop. The heart becomes extremely irritable — any rough handling can trigger ventricular fibrillation.

<section id="hypothermia-treatment">

## 6. Hypothermia Field Treatment

Treatment varies dramatically by severity. Aggressive rewarming that is appropriate for mild hypothermia can be fatal in moderate-to-severe cases.

### Mild Hypothermia (Conscious, Shivering)

1.  Move to shelter — get out of wind, rain, and cold ground contact
2.  Remove wet clothing — replace with dry layers
3.  Wrap in warm blankets, sleeping bag, or any available insulation
4.  Insulate from ground (foam pad, branches, dry leaves — anything between victim and cold earth)
5.  Provide warm sweet drinks (NOT alcohol — alcohol dilates blood vessels and increases heat loss)
6.  Apply gentle heat sources: warm water bottles wrapped in cloth placed against trunk (not extremities)
7.  Encourage gentle movement if patient is able (generates metabolic heat)
8.  Monitor for deterioration — if shivering stops without warming, condition is worsening

### Moderate/Severe Hypothermia (Unconscious or Not Shivering)

:::danger
**Handle with extreme gentleness.** The hypothermic heart is electrically unstable. Rough movement, jostling, or even rolling the patient too quickly can trigger ventricular fibrillation — a fatal heart rhythm. Move the patient horizontally, not vertically. Avoid unnecessary physical manipulation.
:::

1.  **Handle gently:** Rough movement triggers fatal cardiac arrhythmias
2.  **Call for emergency evacuation:** Requires hospital rewarming (extracorporeal rewarming in severe cases)
3.  **Prevent further heat loss:** Insulate from ground, shelter from wind, cover with every available layer
4.  **Begin CPR if no pulse detected** after checking for 60 seconds (hypothermia slows pulse dramatically — do not assume death)
5.  **Do NOT attempt rapid rewarming:** Dangerous "afterdrop" occurs when cold blood from extremities returns to core, further dropping core temperature
6.  **Passive rewarming only:** Blankets, move to warm environment, warm humidified air if available
7.  **Warm IV fluids** if medical supplies available (104°F / 40°C normal saline)
8.  **Do NOT give hot drinks** to unconscious patients (aspiration risk)

### The Afterdrop Phenomenon

When a hypothermic patient begins to rewarm, blood vessels in cold extremities dilate, sending cold peripheral blood back to the core. This can cause core temperature to drop an additional 2-5°F after rewarming begins. This is why:

- **Rewarm the core first** (trunk, armpits, groin) — not the extremities
- **Do not immerse in hot water** (causes massive peripheral vasodilation)
- **Do not rub or massage extremities** (drives cold blood toward core)
- **Monitor closely** for 30-60 minutes after initiating rewarming

## 7. Hypothermia Prevention

Prevention is far more effective than treatment. Once hypothermia progresses beyond the mild stage, field treatment has limited effectiveness without medical facilities.

### The COLD Principle

- **C — Cover:** Keep covered. Wear a hat (head accounts for significant heat loss due to high blood flow)
- **O — Overexertion avoid:** Sweating wets clothing, which then cools you. Regulate pace to minimize sweating
- **L — Layers:** Multiple thin layers trap insulating air and allow adjustment. Wool and synthetics retain insulation when wet; cotton does not ("cotton kills")
- **D — Dry:** Stay dry. Wet clothing loses up to 90% of its insulating value. Change wet socks and inner layers immediately.

### Layer System

1. **Base layer:** Wicking material (wool, synthetic) pulls moisture from skin
2. **Insulating layer(s):** Fleece, wool, down — traps warm air
3. **Shell layer:** Windproof and waterproof outer layer — blocks convective and evaporative loss
4. **Vapor barrier (extreme cold):** In sub-zero conditions, a vapor barrier liner between base and insulating layers prevents moisture from reaching insulation

### High-Risk Situations

- **Wet + windy conditions at moderate temperatures (40-55°F):** This combination kills more people than extreme cold because victims underestimate the danger
- **Immersion in cold water:** Water conducts heat 25x faster than air. Survival time in 50°F water: 1-3 hours. In 32°F water: 15-45 minutes
- **Exhaustion:** Depleted glycogen stores eliminate the ability to shiver — the body's primary heat-generation response
- **Alcohol consumption:** Dilates blood vessels (increases heat loss), impairs judgment, and masks symptoms
- **Elderly and very young:** Reduced thermoregulation capacity — onset faster, recognition harder
- **Trauma and blood loss:** Shock impairs thermoregulation — all trauma patients should be assumed hypothermic

### Nutrition for Cold Environments

- **Increase caloric intake** by 25-50% in cold conditions — the body burns significantly more calories maintaining temperature
- **Carbohydrates** fuel immediate shivering response (quick energy)
- **Fats** provide sustained metabolic heat (long-duration cold exposure)
- **Hot meals and drinks** provide direct thermal energy plus morale benefit
- **Eat before sleeping** — metabolic heat from digestion helps maintain temperature through the night

## 8. Special Populations & Considerations

### Children

- Higher surface-area-to-mass ratio means faster heat loss AND faster overheating
- Less effective thermoregulation — sweating response less developed in young children
- Cannot reliably report symptoms — rely on behavioral observation (irritability, lethargy, flushed skin)
- Treat at lower thresholds — do not wait for classic symptoms

### Elderly

- Reduced sweating capacity and diminished thirst sensation increase heat illness risk
- Medications (diuretics, beta-blockers, anticholinergics) impair thermoregulation
- Chronic conditions (heart disease, diabetes) reduce tolerance for temperature extremes
- May develop hypothermia indoors at temperatures that seem moderate (65-68°F)

### Chronic Medical Conditions

- **Diabetes:** Impaired peripheral circulation increases hypothermia risk; neuropathy masks cold injury
- **Cardiovascular disease:** Reduced cardiac output limits both heat generation and heat dissipation
- **Thyroid disorders:** Hypothyroidism dramatically increases cold sensitivity; hyperthyroidism increases heat intolerance
- **Prior thermal injury:** Previous heat stroke or frostbite permanently impairs thermoregulation in affected areas

<section id="field-assessment">

## 9. Field Assessment Without Equipment

In survival situations, thermometers and medical equipment may not be available. Use these field assessment techniques:

### Estimating Core Temperature

- **Shivering present:** Core temp likely 90-95°F (mild hypothermia) — treatable in field
- **Shivering absent + conscious:** Core temp likely 82-90°F (moderate) — needs medical evacuation
- **Unconscious:** Core temp likely below 82°F (severe) — critical emergency

### Heat Illness Assessment

- **Sweating + coherent speech:** Likely heat exhaustion — field treatable
- **No sweating + confusion/combativeness:** Likely heat stroke — extreme emergency
- **Skin feel:** Hot, dry skin = heat stroke. Cool, clammy skin = heat exhaustion. Hot, moist skin = possible exertional heat stroke.

### The "Umbles" Test for Hypothermia

Watch for the progressive "umbles" — signs of deteriorating coordination and cognition:
1. **Fumbles** — dropping objects, can't manipulate zippers or buttons
2. **Stumbles** — unsteady gait, tripping
3. **Mumbles** — slurred or incoherent speech
4. **Grumbles** — irrational complaints, combativeness, refusal of help
5. **Tumbles** — falls, inability to stand

If a person shows two or more "umbles," treat for hypothermia immediately — do not wait for confirmation.

## 10. Emergency Quick Reference

:::info-box
**Heat Exhaustion First Aid:** Cool + Fluids + Rest = recovery in 30-60 minutes

**Heat Stroke Emergency:** Cool immediately and aggressively (cold water immersion best). Ice to neck/armpits/groin. Core temp >104°F is organ-damage territory. Every minute counts.

**Hypothermia — Gentle Handling:** Handle gently — rough movement triggers fatal arrhythmias. Insulate, shelter, passive rewarm. CPR if no pulse after 60-second check. "Not dead until warm and dead."
:::

### Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS) Recipe

For heat illness fluid replacement:

```
1 liter clean water
6 level teaspoons sugar (30g)
½ level teaspoon salt (2.5g)
```

Sip slowly — 200-400 mL per hour. Adjust salt upward in heavy sweating conditions.

### Emergency Warming Methods (Field Expedient)

When no medical supplies are available:

1. **Body-to-body warming:** Skin-to-skin contact in sleeping bag — most effective field method for mild-to-moderate hypothermia
2. **Heated rocks:** Warm (NOT hot) rocks placed in cloth, positioned against trunk
3. **Hot water bottles:** Any sealed container filled with warm water, wrapped in cloth
4. **Warm sweet liquids:** For conscious patients only — provides both thermal and caloric energy
5. **Fire + reflector:** Position patient between fire and reflective surface (rock wall, emergency blanket) to maximize radiant heat capture

### Common Mistakes That Kill

-   **Overheating individuals:** Aggressive rewarming in moderate hypothermia (core temp 82-90°F) triggers fatal cardiac arrhythmias. Rewarm slowly and gently.
-   **Ignoring early heat stroke signs:** Confusion and aggression mistaken for other causes — delayed treatment dramatically reduces survival. Cool aggressively at first sign.
-   **Inadequate hydration planning:** Dehydration and electrolyte loss compound heat stress. Drink consistently (every 15-20 minutes) with salt replacement.

-   **Inattention to acclimatization:** Cold adaptation takes 10-14 days; premature intense activity in cold causes hypothermia. Start gently.
-   **Giving alcohol to hypothermic patients:** Alcohol causes vasodilation, increasing heat loss. The warming sensation is an illusion as core temperature drops faster.
-   **Rubbing frostbitten skin:** Causes tissue damage. Rewarm gently in warm (not hot) water only if refreezing can be prevented.
