**Medical Disclaimer:** Poisoning is a medical emergency with diverse presentations and outcomes. Toxidrome recognition is based on clinical signs; definitive diagnosis requires toxicology testing. These protocols cover field management when laboratory confirmation is unavailable. Supportive care and specific antidotes are the pillars of poisoning management.

<section id="overview">

## Toxidromes: Syndrome Recognition without Labs

A **toxidrome** is a constellation of clinical signs that points to a class of poisoning. In austere settings without toxicology testing, toxidrome recognition allows you to predict the likely poison and administer targeted treatment.

If the immediate question is "a child swallowed something unknown" rather than "which toxidrome is this," start with <a href="../unknown-ingestion-child-poisoning-triage.html">Unknown Ingestion & Child Accidental Poisoning Triage</a>. If the poisoning presents mainly as ongoing convulsions, cross-check <a href="../adult-seizure-status-epilepticus-field-protocol.html">Adult Seizure & Status Epilepticus Field Protocol</a>.

## Pinpoint or Dilated Pupils With Confusion - Antidote Triage

Use this guide early when poisoning presents with pupil changes plus breathing or mental-status changes.

Quick lay signals:
- "medicine reaction or panic, diarrhea, fever, cannot stop moving"
- "changing meds, restless, overheated, twitching all over"
- "new antidepressant combo, shaking, sweaty, legs keep jerking"
- "too much serotonin medicine, rigid muscles, very hot"
- "antidepressants plus cough medicine, agitated, sweaty, trembling"
- "flu or dangerous medication reaction, clonus, fever, confusion"
- "pinpoint pupils and slow breathing"
- "dilated pupils, dry mouth, confusion"
- "sweating, twitching, vomiting after chemical exposure"
- "new antidepressant combo and now shaking, sweaty, legs jerking"
- "antidepressants plus cough medicine, agitated, trembling, fever"
- "serotonin medicine overdose, rigid muscles, very hot, confused"
- "dangerous medication reaction with clonus, fever, confusion"
- "medicine reaction or panic with diarrhea, fever, cannot stop moving"
- "too many pain pills and will not stay awake"
- "mixed pills and alcohol, hard to wake"
- "naloxone helped but sleepy again"
- "accidental double-dose medicine and now confused"

If the immediate problem is hard to wake, slow breathing, or repeat sleepiness after naloxone, treat that as an overdose or airway emergency first with [First Aid & Emergency Response](./first-aid.md), then use this guide to sort the toxidrome pattern.

If the immediate problem follows a new serotonergic medicine, antidepressant combination, tramadol, linezolid, MAOI, dextromethorphan cough/cold medicine, serotonin-medicine overdose, changing meds, or a dangerous medication reaction and includes shaking, sweating, diarrhea, clonus, rigidity, fever, confusion, overheating, twitching, severe restlessness, leg jerking, or "cannot stop moving," treat it as possible serotonin syndrome first. Do not route these prompts to panic, flu, menopause, routine GI illness, dehydration-first diarrhea care, anticholinergic-first routing, or generic anxiety before toxicology escalation. If there is no medication trigger, medication mix, overdose, or serotonergic source phrase, keep ordinary flu, GI illness, heat illness, and anxiety/restlessness in their usual guides unless general emergency red flags apply.

Medication-triggered serotonin patterns that should be indexable as first-choice routing: new antidepressant combo plus shaking, sweating, or jerking; medicine reaction or panic plus diarrhea, fever, or cannot stop moving; too much serotonin medicine plus rigidity or feeling hot; antidepressants plus cough medicine; clonus, fever, or confusion with medication concern; changing meds plus restless, overheated, or twitching. These are emergency toxicology patterns first. Ordinary flu, GI illness, heat symptoms, panic, or menopause without a medication trigger should not be pulled into this serotonin route solely by overlapping symptoms.

### Why Toxidrome Recognition Matters

1. **Rapid diagnosis:** Clinical signs develop before lab results (which you may not have access to)
2. **Targeted treatment:** Each toxidrome has specific antidotes
3. **Predictable management:** Certain toxidromes require certain interventions (airway protection, specific antidotes)
4. **Prognosis:** Some toxidromes are immediately life-threatening; others evolve slowly

### The Major Toxidromes

- **Anticholinergic** (atropine overdose, antihistamines, tricyclic antidepressants)
- **Cholinergic** (organophosphate pesticides, nerve agents, physostigmine overdose)
- **Opioid** (heroin, morphine, codeine, some pharmaceuticals)
- **Sympathomimetic** (cocaine, amphetamines, decongestants, caffeine overdose)
- **Serotonergic / serotonin syndrome** (SSRI/SNRI/MAOI combinations, tramadol, linezolid, dextromethorphan cough medicine, serotonergic overdose)
- **Sedative/Hypnotic** (benzodiazepines, barbiturates, GHB)
- **Hallucinogenic** (LSD, psilocybin, mescaline)

<section id="anticholinergic">

## Anticholinergic Toxidrome

**Mechanism:** Blocks acetylcholine at muscarinic receptors; loses parasympathetic function

**Sources:**
- Antihistamines (diphenhydramine, chlorpheniramine)
- Tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline, nortriptyline)
- Atropine overdose
- Some plants (deadly nightshade [Atropa belladonna], jimsonweed [Datura])
- Anticholinergic medications (benztropine)

<div class="toxidrome-card">

### Anticholinergic Signs ("Hot as a Hare, Dry as a Bone, Red as a Beet, Blind as a Bat, Mad as a Hatter")

**Mnemonic translation:**
- **"Hot as a hare":** Fever (no sweating; loss of heat dissipation)
- **"Dry as a bone":** Dry mouth, dry skin; no sweating (hallmark sign)
- **"Red as a beet":** Flushed, hot skin; tachycardia
- **"Blind as a bat":** Dilated pupils (mydriasis); blurred vision; photophobia
- **"Mad as a hatter":** Confusion, agitation, hallucinations, delirium

**Physical exam findings:**
- **Pupils:** Dilated (>5 mm), non-reactive to light
- **Skin:** Hot, dry, flushed; no sweating despite fever
- **Heart:** Tachycardia (HR >100)
- **Mouth:** Dry mucous membranes
- **GI:** Decreased bowel sounds; constipation; urinary retention
- **Mental status:** Confusion, agitation, hallucinations, delirium
- **Vital signs:** Fever, tachycardia, hypertension (may have)

</div>

### Anticholinergic Management

1. **Supportive care:** IV fluids, cooling measures for hyperthermia
2. **Airway protection:** If altered mental status, consider intubation
3. **Benzodiazepines:** For agitation/seizures (diazepam 5-10 mg IV; repeat q5-15 min as needed)
4. **Physostigmine (if available):** **SPECIFIC ANTIDOTE**
   - Dose: 1-2 mg IV push slowly over 1 min
   - Onset: 5-15 minutes
   - Effect reverses anticholinergic signs
   - **Risk:** Can cause cholinergic crisis if overdosed
   - **Caution:** Do NOT use if tricyclic antidepressant poisoning (risk of arrhythmias); use supportive care instead
5. **Monitoring:** Watch for seizures, arrhythmias, hyperthermia

</section>

<section id="cholinergic">

## Cholinergic Toxidrome

**Mechanism:** Excess acetylcholine; blocks breakdown of ACh (via organophosphates inhibiting acetylcholinesterase)

**Sources:**
- Organophosphate pesticides (malathion, parathion, sarin)
- Carbamate pesticides (carbaryl, aldicarb)
- Nerve agents (sarin, VX)
- Physostigmine overdose

<div class="toxidrome-card">

### Cholinergic Crisis Signs ("DUMBELS" Mnemonic)

**DUMBELS:**
- **D**iarrhea / Defecation
- **U**rination
- **M**iosis (pinpoint pupils)
- **B**ronchospasm, Bronchorrhea (excessive mucus)
- **E**mesis (vomiting)
- **L**acrimation (tearing)
- **S**alivation, Seizures

**Additional signs:**
- **Pupils:** Pinpoint (<1 mm), reactive (unlike anticholinergic)
- **Respiratory:** Wheezing, crackles from pulmonary edema; may have stridor
- **Skeletal muscle:** Fasciculations (visible twitching); muscle weakness; paralysis
- **Heart:** Bradycardia (slow heart rate), variable BP
- **GI:** Diarrhea, cramping, vomiting
- **Neurologic:** Anxiety, confusion, seizures (often late)
- **Overall:** "Wet" patient (excessive secretions: drooling, tearing, sweating, diarrhea)

</div>

### Cholinergic Crisis Management

**This is a MEDICAL EMERGENCY.** Respiratory failure from bronchospasm + muscle weakness = death within minutes.

1. **Personal protection:** Organophosphates are dermally absorbable. **Rescuer must wear gloves/protective equipment.** Do NOT touch contaminated skin.

2. **Airway:** Be ready to intubate; cholinergic patients aspirate easily (excessive secretions)

3. **Atropine (FIRST-LINE ANTIDOTE):**
   - **Dose:** 2-5 mg IV push; repeat every 5-10 min until signs of atropinization (dry mouth, dilated pupils)
   - **Goal:** Titrate to dry airway + clear lungs (no wheezing)
   - **Can give high doses** (up to 50 mg total if needed)
   - **Mechanism:** Blocks muscarinic effects; does NOT affect nicotinic (muscle weakness)

4. **Pralidoxime (2-PAM) - if available:**
   - **Dose:** 600-1200 mg IV bolus (or 600 mg IM if no IV access)
   - **Mechanism:** Reactivates acetylcholinesterase enzyme; removes phosphate group from enzyme
   - **Window:** Must be given within hours of exposure (preferably <24 hrs; older exposures less responsive)
   - **Use with atropine:** 2-PAM does NOT replace atropine; must use BOTH

5. **Seizure control:** Benzodiazepines (diazepam 5-10 mg IV; repeat q5-15 min)

6. **Supportive care:**
   - Suctioning of airway (excessive secretions)
   - Intubation if respiratory failure
   - IV fluids

7. **Decontamination:**
   - Remove contaminated clothing
   - Wash skin with soap and water (or saline if available)

### Organophosphate vs. Carbamate Poisoning

| Feature | Organophosphate | Carbamate |
|---|---|---|
| **Onset** | Minutes to hours | Minutes (faster) |
| **Duration** | Hours to days (slow metabolism) | Minutes to hours (rapid metabolism) |
| **Enzyme reactivation** | Irreversible (phosphorylation) | Reversible (carbamylation) |
| **2-PAM efficacy** | Highly effective (early) | Less effective |
| **Management approach** | Aggressive atropine + 2-PAM | Supportive; may not need 2-PAM |

</section>

<section id="opioid">

## Opioid Toxidrome

**Mechanism:** Mu receptor agonism; respiratory depression, analgesia, sedation

**Sources:**
- Heroin, morphine, codeine, fentanyl
- Pharmaceutical opioids (oxycodone, hydrocodone)
- Tramadol, methadone
- Some plants (opium poppy)

<div class="toxidrome-card">

### Opioid Toxidrome Signs

**Classic triad:**
1. **Respiratory depression** (slow, shallow breathing; <8 breaths/min is critical)
2. **Altered mental status** (drowsiness, coma)
3. **Miosis** (pinpoint pupils)

**Additional signs:**
- **Skin:** Needle marks if IV use; track marks
- **Mental:** Coma, unresponsiveness, confusion
- **Respiratory:** Severe respiratory depression, apnea; pulmonary edema (pink frothy sputum)
- **Temperature:** Hypothermia (losing heat; lack of movement)
- **GI:** Constipation
- **Vital signs:** Bradycardia, hypotension, bradypnea

**Hallmark:** The respiratory depression distinguishes opioid from other toxidromes.

</div>

### Opioid Management: Naloxone (LIFE-SAVING ANTIDOTE)

**Naloxone (Narcan):**
- **Mechanism:** Competitive mu receptor antagonist; reverses opioid effects rapidly
- **Onset:** 1-3 minutes (IV); 2-5 minutes (IM)
- **Duration:** 30-90 minutes (shorter than most opioids; patient may re-sedate)

<div class="antidote-regimen">

**Naloxone Dosing:**

| Route | Dose | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **IV (preferred)** | 0.4-2 mg IV push | Fastest; can repeat q2-3 min if no response |
| **IM** | 0.4-2 mg IM | If no IV access; slightly slower onset |
| **Intranasal (if spray available)** | 4 mg per nostril | Public health access; convenient |
| **Repeat dosing** | Re-dose q2-3 min if still unresponsive | May need multiple doses for potent opioids (fentanyl) |

**Management protocol:**
1. Establish airway; prepare for intubation
2. Give naloxone IV/IM
3. Reassess after 2-3 minutes:
   - **Good response:** Patient breathing spontaneously, opening eyes, alert
   - **Poor response:** Respiratory depression persists; repeat naloxone
4. **Important:** Naloxone duration is SHORT (30-90 min). Opioids last longer. Patient will re-sedate. Options:
   - **Naloxone drip:** 0.4-0.8 mg IV dissolved in 100 mL saline, infused at rate to maintain wakefulness
   - **Observation + repeat dosing:** Give naloxone, observe closely, re-dose if re-sedation occurs
5. **Caution:** Naloxone can precipitate acute withdrawal (agitation, sweating, anxiety); prepare patient

</div>

### Opioid Overdose Complications

**Pulmonary edema:**
- Opioids can cause "dry" drowning (fluid in lungs without heart failure)
- Signs: Pink/frothy sputum, wheezing, crackles
- Management: Oxygen, PEEP if intubated, supportive care
- Prognosis: Improves as opioid wears off + naloxone reverses effect

</section>

<section id="sympathomimetic">

## Sympathomimetic Toxidrome

**Mechanism:** Excess norepinephrine/dopamine; sympathetic overdrive

**Sources:**
- Cocaine (blocks reuptake of catecholamines)
- Amphetamines (MDMA/Ecstasy, methamphetamine)
- Pseudoephedrine, phenylephrine (decongestants)
- Caffeine overdose
- Theophylline toxicity
- Some stimulant plants (khat, ephedra)

<div class="toxidrome-card">

### Sympathomimetic Crisis Signs

**Cardiovascular:**
- Tachycardia (HR >120 common; can exceed 200)
- Hypertension (may be severe; systolic >180)
- Arrhythmias (premature contractions, supraventricular tachycardia)
- Chest pain (MI or coronary vasospasm)

**CNS:**
- Agitation, anxiety, paranoia
- Hallucinations, psychosis
- Tremor, hyperreflexia
- Seizures
- Hyperthermia (thermogenesis + reduced sweating early)
- Headache

**Pupils:** Dilated (mydriasis)

**Skin:** Diaphoresis (sweating), flushed

**GI:** Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain

**Severe presentation:**
- Uncontrolled hypertension -> stroke, cardiac ischemia
- Severe hyperthermia -> rhabdomyolysis, acute kidney injury
- Arrhythmias -> sudden cardiac death
- Seizures -> status epilepticus

</div>

### Sympathomimetic Management

**No single specific antidote;** treatment is supportive + symptom control.

1. **Benzodiazepines (FIRST-LINE for agitation/seizures):**
   - **Diazepam:** 5-10 mg IV; repeat q5-15 min as needed
   - **Lorazepam:** 2-4 mg IV; repeat q5-15 min
   - **Goal:** Control agitation, prevent seizures, reduce sympathetic overdrive
   - Can give HIGH doses (up to 50 mg or more if needed)

2. **Antihypertensive agents (if hypertensive crisis):**
   - **Nitroprusside (if available):** 0.5-10 mcg/kg/min IV (immediate onset; careful titration)
   - **Labetalol:** 10-20 mg IV; can repeat q10-20 min (combined alpha/beta blockade)
   - **Esmolol:** 500 mcg/kg IV bolus; then 50-300 mcg/kg/min infusion (beta-blocker; fast-acting)
   - **Nifedipine:** 10-20 mg sublingual or chewed (calcium channel blocker; slower)
   - **Avoid:** Pure beta-blockers without alpha blockade (can cause unopposed alpha = paradoxical hypertension)

3. **Arrhythmia management:**
   - Many cocaine-induced arrhythmias resolve with benzodiazepines + time
   - Avoid antiarrhythmic drugs that increase catecholamine sensitivity (flecainide, procainamide)
   - If sustained VT: Amiodarone (but ineffective if not addressing underlying sympathomimetic excess)

4. **Hyperthermia control:**
   - Active cooling: Ice packs, cold water immersion, fans
   - Aggressive fluid resuscitation (goal: urine output 2-3 mL/kg/hr to prevent rhabdomyolysis)
   - Monitor for rhabdomyolysis: Dark urine, elevated CPK, acute kidney injury
   - Alkalinize urine with sodium bicarbonate (if available) to prevent myoglobin precipitation in kidneys

5. **Seizure control:** Benzodiazepines (see above)

6. **Monitoring:** Watch for cardiac ischemia (chest pain), stroke (focal neurologic deficit), kidney failure

### Cocaine-Specific Considerations

**Crack cocaine vs. powder cocaine:**
- Crack: Smoked; rapid onset; short duration (5-30 min)
- Powder: Snorted/injected; slower onset; longer duration (30 min-2 hrs)

**Cocaine-induced MI:**
- Can occur even in young, healthy individuals
- Coronary vasospasm is the mechanism (not atherosclerosis)
- Management: Benzodiazepines, nitrates (if available), avoid beta-blockers (unopposed alpha-vasospasm)

## Serotonin Syndrome Routing Pattern

Use this as a toxicology routing pattern when the complaint starts with a medication change, medication interaction, or overdose rather than a primary infection, panic attack, menopause symptom, or routine stomach illness.

**High-yield source phrases:** new antidepressant combo, mixed antidepressants, SSRI/SNRI/MAOI, tramadol, linezolid, dextromethorphan cough medicine, cough/cold medicine plus antidepressant, serotonin medicine, took too much serotonin medicine, changing meds, or dangerous medication reaction.

**Complaint-first symptom cluster:** shaking, sweating, diarrhea, clonus, hyperreflexia, leg jerking, twitching, rigid muscles, fever, confusion, overheating, agitation, severe restlessness, trembling, or "cannot stop moving."

**Exact first-choice aliases:** "started a new antidepressant combo and now I am shaking sweaty and my legs keep jerking," "medicine reaction or panic because I have diarrhea fever and cannot stop moving," "took too much serotonin medicine and now my muscles are rigid and I feel very hot," "agitated sweaty and trembling after mixing antidepressants and cough medicine," "flu or dangerous medication reaction because I have clonus fever and confusion," and "after changing meds I am restless overheated and twitching all over."

**Routing rule:** When a serotonergic or medication-reaction source phrase and this symptom cluster appear together, escalate as possible serotonin syndrome and cross-check [Toxicology and Poisoning Response](../toxicology-poisoning-response.html). Do not answer first as panic, flu, menopause, ordinary GI illness, dehydration-first diarrhea care, anticholinergic-first toxicity, or generic anxiety. Symptom-only flu, diarrhea/vomiting, overheating, or anxiety without a medication trigger should not route here solely from those symptoms.

**Lay first-action wording:** Say "do not take another dose of the suspected serotonergic medicine/source while contacting Poison Control, EMS, or a clinician" rather than "stop all serotonergic medications," "cease any potential source," "stop taking the combo immediately," or "stop all medicines." Cooling and urgent escalation come first. Benzodiazepines, cyproheptadine, or other antidote/sedation decisions require trained medical direction.

</section>

## Plant & Mushroom Poisoning

### Dangerous Mushrooms

<div class="mushroom-dangerous">

**Amanita phalloides (Death Cap) - THE MOST LETHAL**

**Appearance:**
- Cap: White, greenish, or tan; 5-15 cm diameter
- Ring (skirt) below cap: Present
- Cup-like structure at base (volva): Present
- Gills: White, free (not attached to stem)
- Geographic range: Worldwide (especially temperate regions)

**Toxin:** Amatoxins (alpha-amanitin); destroy liver + kidneys

**Timeline:**
- **Lag phase (0-24 hrs):** Few to no symptoms ("deceptive silence")
- **Gastrointestinal phase (24-72 hrs):** Severe vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain
- **Apparent recovery phase (3-5 days):** Patient seems to improve; appears to be getting better
- **Hepatorenal failure phase (5-7 days):** Jaundice, elevated liver enzymes, kidney failure, encephalopathy, death

**Management:**
- Activated charcoal (50 g PO) if available AND within 2 hrs of ingestion
- **Supportive care only:** IV fluids, monitoring liver/kidney function (if labs available)
- Silymarin (milk thistle) - controversial; may have some benefit
- **No antidote exists.** Focus on supportive care and liver protection
- **Prognosis:** 50% mortality even in hospitals with advanced care; higher in austere settings

**Do NOT be fooled by symptom-free period (lag phase). Assume any mushroom poisoning = dangerous until proven otherwise.**

**Amanita muscaria (Fly Agaric)**

**Appearance:**
- Cap: Red with white spots; distinctive
- Ring below cap: Present
- Cup at base: Present

**Toxin:** Ibotenic acid, muscimol (psychoactive)

**Symptoms:** Agitation, confusion, hallucinations, tremor, seizures, delirium (NOT liver failure like A. phalloides)

**Management:** Benzodiazepines for agitation/seizures; supportive care; prognosis good

</div>

### Other Deadly Mushroom Types

| Mushroom | Toxin | Onset | Symptoms | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Galerina marginata** | Amatoxins (like Death Cap) | 6-24 hrs | GI symptoms, then liver failure | Lethal |
| **Cortinarius rubellus** (Webcap) | Orellanine (kidney toxin) | Days to weeks | Kidney failure | Often fatal |
| **Lepiota brunneoincarnata** (False Parasol) | Amatoxins | 6-24 hrs | GI, then liver failure | Lethal |
| **Psilocybe spp.** (Magic Mushrooms) | Psilocybin (hallucinogen) | 30 min-2 hrs | Hallucinations, euphoria, anxiety | Rarely fatal |

### Toxic Plants

<div class="plant-poisoning">

**Ricinus communis (Castor Bean)**
- **Toxin:** Ricin (protein; interferes with protein synthesis)
- **Route:** Ingestion (beans); chewing releases toxin
- **Symptoms:** Severe vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain within hours; followed by shock, kidney/liver failure
- **Management:** Supportive care, IV fluids, monitoring
- **Prognosis:** Varies; can be lethal

**Strychnine tree (Strychnos nux-vomica)**
- **Toxin:** Strychnine (antagonizes GABA)
- **Symptoms:** Muscle rigidity, opisthotonus (arched back), "risus sardonicus" (fixed grin), seizures
- **Management:** Benzodiazepines (high doses), supportive care, mechanical ventilation if needed
- **Prognosis:** Moderate if treated; high mortality without care

**Oleander, Foxglove (Digitalis plants)**
- **Toxin:** Cardiac glycosides
- **Symptoms:** Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, bradycardia, arrhythmias
- **Management:** Supportive care, activated charcoal, IV calcium (if available; antagonizes digitalis), monitoring for arrhythmias
- **Prognosis:** Good if supportive care given

**Belladonna (Atropa belladonna), Jimsonweed (Datura)**
- **Toxin:** Atropine-like alkaloids
- **Symptoms:** Anticholinergic toxidrome (see above): dry mouth, dilated pupils, hyperthermia, delirium
- **Management:** Supportive care, benzodiazepines for agitation, cooling, airway protection
- **Prognosis:** Good; mortality low

**Poison Hemlock (Conium maculatum)**
- **Toxin:** Alkaloids (gamma-coniceine)
- **Symptoms:** Tremor, excessive salivation, bradycardia, respiratory depression, weakness
- **Management:** Supportive care, respiratory support if needed
- **Prognosis:** Variable

</div>

### General Approach to Unknown Plant/Mushroom Poisoning

1. **Identify the toxin if possible:**
   - Obtain specimen (photograph, bring physical sample if safe)
   - Consult local herbals or poison control (if phone access)
   - If mushroom: Look for: cap color, gills, ring, cup at base, bruising color

2. **Decontamination:**
   - **Skin exposure:** Wash thoroughly with soap/water
   - **Ingestion:** Activated charcoal 50 g PO (if within 1-2 hrs; some toxins not well absorbed by charcoal)

3. **Supportive care:**
   - IV fluids (most plant poisonings cause vomiting/diarrhea)
   - Monitor vital signs
   - Airway protection if altered mental status
   - Seizure precautions

4. **Specific interventions:**
   - Anticholinergic toxin (belladonna, jimsonweed, foxglove): Benzodiazepines, airway protection, cooling
   - Cholinergic toxin (hemlock): Atropine if needed, respiratory support
   - Unknown toxin: Observe closely; most resolve with supportive care over 24-72 hrs

5. **Red flags suggesting mushroom = Amanita phalloides:**
   - Lag phase of symptom-free period (0-24 hrs)
   - Severe GI symptoms followed by apparent improvement
   - Liver failure developing (jaundice, elevated enzymes)
   - **Assumption:** Any mushroom poisoning in lag phase = potential Death Cap; treat with extreme caution

</section>

<section id="decontamination-general">

## General Decontamination & Supportive Care

### Decontamination from Environmental Toxins (Pesticides, Chemical Agents)

1. **Remove contaminated clothing** (pesticides/chemicals coat skin)
2. **Wash skin thoroughly** with soap + water (or saline if available)
3. **Hair:** Wash thoroughly (toxins may cling)
4. **Eyes/mouth:** Rinse with water if exposure
5. **Respiratory:** Move to fresh air; oxygen if available

### Activated Charcoal (For Ingested Toxins)

**Indications:** Recent ingestion (<1-2 hrs) of toxin; not yet absorbed

**Dose:** 50 g PO as slurry in water

**Mechanism:** Adsorbs toxin in gut; prevents absorption

**Exceptions (DO NOT give charcoal):**
- Hydrocarbons (gasoline, petroleum)
- Alcohols (methanol, ethylene glycol)
- Heavy metals (lead, arsenic, iron)
- Acids/bases (caustic ingestions)
- Already vomiting (risk of aspiration)

**Note:** Activated charcoal is non-specific and works best within 1-2 hrs. In austere settings with delayed presentation, benefit is limited.

### IV Fluids & Supportive Care

- **Fluids:** Most toxins cause GI symptoms (vomiting, diarrhea). Isotonic crystalloid IV fluids maintain perfusion
- **Electrolytes:** Many toxins affect kidney function; monitor urine output (goal 0.5-1 mL/kg/hr)
- **Airway:** If altered mental status, prepare for intubation
- **Monitoring:** Temperature, vital signs, mental status, urine output

## Common Antidotes Summary Table

| Poison | Antidote | Dose | Route |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Anticholinergic** | Physostigmine | 1-2 mg | IV over 1 min |
| **Cholinergic** | Atropine | 2-5 mg; repeat q5-10 min | IV |
| **Cholinergic** | Pralidoxime (2-PAM) | 600-1200 mg | IV or IM |
| **Opioid** | Naloxone | 0.4-2 mg; repeat q2-3 min | IV, IM, or intranasal |
| **Cyanide** | Hydroxocobalamin (if available) | 5-10 g | IV |
| **Cyanide** | Sodium thiosulfate (if available) | 12.5 g | IV |
| **Methanol/Ethylene glycol** | Fomepizole (if available) | 15 mg/kg loading | IV |
| **Acetaminophen** | N-acetylcysteine | 150 mg/kg loading | IV or PO |

| **Warfarin/Bleeding** | Vitamin K1 | 10 mg | IV or PO |
| **Organophosphate** | Atropine + Pralidoxime | See above | IV |

## Austere Toxicology Kit

**Essential equipment:**
- **Gloves/protective equipment** (pesticide/nerve agent exposure risk)
- **IV fluids:** Normal saline, Ringer's Lactate (5+ liters)
- **Activated charcoal** (50 g)
- **Airway equipment:** Intubation supplies, oxygen

**Medications:**
- **Naloxone (Narcan):** Multiple 0.4-2 mg vials
- **Atropine:** 1 mg/mL x 10 mL vials (for cholinergic crisis)
- **Pralidoxime (2-PAM) (if available):** 600 mg vials
- **Benzodiazepines:** Diazepam 5 mg/mL, Lorazepam 2 mg/mL (for seizures, agitation)
- **Antihypertensives:** Labetalol 5 mg/mL, Nifedipine (for sympathomimetic hypertension)
- **Physostigmine (if available):** 1 mg/mL (for anticholinergic)

**Reference materials:**
- Local poison control number (if phone available)
- Plant/mushroom identification guides (physical book or laminated photos)
- Toxidrome recognition flowchart
