<section id="why-contracts-matter">

## 1. Establish Contract and Agreement Systems Immediately

Contract systems are invisible in functioning economies because they work. When they collapse, the absence becomes catastrophic.

### Take Action: Prevent Disputes Before They Start

Without contract systems, these problems escalate rapidly:
- [ ] Broken promises → Trade collapses
- [ ] Wage theft → Labor becomes unavailable
- [ ] Non-delivery of paid goods → Theft becomes common
- [ ] Unclear expectations → Conflicts erupt
- [ ] Disputes escalate → Violence without resolution mechanism

**What you must do:** Establish written contracts for all trades, labor, and agreements above minimal value (suggest: >5 labor units). Make contracts standard practice now.

### What Contract Systems Enable

Communities that implement contract systems immediately:
- [ ] **Enable trade:** Buyers know goods will be delivered; sellers know they'll be paid
- [ ] **Resolve disputes fairly:** Neutral third party resolves conflicts both sides trust
- [ ] **Enforce accountability:** Promises have consequences for violation
- [ ] **Reduce violence:** Conflicts resolved through process, not fighting
- [ ] **Protect vulnerable people:** Written agreements prevent exploitation

</section>

<section id="contract-elements">

## 2. Include These Eight Elements in Every Contract

Every contract must include all eight parts. Missing any element creates dispute risk.

<div class="contract-element">

**1. Parties (Identify everyone involved)**
- [ ] Full names of all people/organizations
- [ ] Title or role (identify which John if multiple exist)
- [ ] Signature or mark (prevents "that wasn't me" claims later)

**2. Terms (Be absolutely specific)**
- [ ] Exact description of what will be provided
- [ ] Quality standard (what does "acceptable" look like?)
- [ ] Quantity or amount (exact numbers, not estimates)
- [ ] Timeline (exact dates, not "soon" or "eventually")
- [ ] Exceptions or special conditions

**3. Consideration (What each party gets)**
- [ ] Party A provides: [specific item/service/value]
- [ ] Party B provides: [specific item/service/value]
- [ ] Mutual benefit (both get something; otherwise it's a gift)
- [ ] Roughly equal value (prevents exploitation claims)

**4. Duration (How long does this last?)**
- [ ] Start date
- [ ] End date
- [ ] Renewal terms (annual? permanent? one-time?)
- [ ] Early exit clause (what if someone wants out?)

**5. Dispute Resolution (What if problems arise?)**
- [ ] Who decides if there's a violation? (third party? leader?)
- [ ] What process? (direct conversation → mediation → arbitration → community council)
- [ ] What remedies? (redo work, refund money, compensation?)

**6. Consequences for Breach (Violations have consequences)**
- [ ] Specific penalties for each type of violation
- [ ] Examples: Refund, redo work, pay penalty, return property
- [ ] Escalation (what if they don't comply with first consequence?)

**7. Witnesses (Make it real)**
- [ ] At least 2 witnesses (neutral parties present when agreeing)
- [ ] Witnesses sign/mark to confirm they saw agreement
- [ ] Prevents later denial that agreement happened

**8. Signatures and Date (Make it official)**
- [ ] Date agreement is made
- [ ] Both parties sign or mark
- [ ] Witnesses sign or mark
- [ ] Community authority signs if official (council member or mayor)

</div>

### Create Your Standard Contract Template

**Every community must create a standard template.** This ensures consistency and prevents missing elements.

**Action steps:**
- [ ] Copy this template below and post at your community meeting place
- [ ] Designate one person as "contract administrator" (usually your record-keeper)
- [ ] Everyone wanting contracts must use the template
- [ ] Administrator reviews all contracts before signing to ensure completeness
- [ ] Store all signed contracts in safe archive (see Archival Records guide)


<section id="contract-types">

## 3. Common Contract Types and Examples

Different situations need different contracts:

<div class="contract-table">

| Contract Type | Parties | Subject | Duration | Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Trade** | Seller/Buyer | Goods exchange | One-time (at completion) | 10 bushels grain for 5 pounds salt |
| **Labor/Wage** | Employer/Worker | Labor exchange | Weeks, months, or indefinite | 5 days/week at harvest; paid in grain |
| **Loan** | Lender/Borrower | Money/goods borrowed | Months or years | Borrow 10 labor units; repay 11 at harvest |
| **Apprenticeship** | Master/Apprentice | Skills training | Years | 3-year blacksmithing; housing + food + wages |
| **Lease/Rent** | Owner/Tenant | Use of property | Months or years | Land for growing; half harvest to owner |
| **Marriage/Partnership** | Two partners | Domestic partnership | Lifetime or until dissolved | Commitment to support; property rights; children |
| **Service** | Provider/Client | Service provision | Variable | Healer treats patient; paid in goods |
| **Construction** | Builder/Owner | Build structure | Months/years | Build house; payment upon completion |
| **Community Bylaw** | Community/Members | Rules for all | Ongoing | Rules for trade post; consequences for violations |

</div>

### Detailed Examples

<div class="contract-example">

**Example 1: Simple Trade Contract**

```

Date: 4th month, 15th day

PARTIES:
Maria (grain farmer), Signature: ________
Thomas (salt trader), Signature: ________

TERMS:
Maria will provide: 10 bushels of wheat grain
- Quality: Good, dry, no mold
- Delivered: 4th month, 20th day
- Location: Trade post

Thomas will provide: 5 pounds of salt
- Quality: Pure, dry, no rocks
- Delivered: 4th month, 20th day
- Location: Trade post

DURATION: One-time trade on specified date

If salt is contaminated: Thomas must replace by 4th month, 25th day
If either party doesn't show: Other party can trade with someone else; no penalty

WITNESSES:
John (trade post manager): __________ DATE: __________
Sarah (community member): __________ DATE: __________

SIGNED:
Maria: __________ DATE: __________
Thomas: __________ DATE: __________
Council Approval: __________ DATE: __________
```

<section id="written-vs-verbal">

## 4. Written vs. Verbal Agreements

Not all agreements can be written (some people can't write; paper is scarce). Communities must establish which agreements require writing and which can be verbal.

### Written Agreements Required For

<div class="contract-element">

**High-value or long-term agreements:**
- Apprenticeship (years of commitment)
- Large trade (many labor units of value)
- Loans or debt (repayment over time)
- Property agreements (land, buildings)
- Marriage or partnership (lifetime commitment)
- Community bylaws or major rules

**Why writing:** High value means high stakes for disputes. Written record prevents false claims later.

</div>

### Verbal Agreements Acceptable For

<div class="contract-element">

**Low-value or immediate agreements:**
- Small trades (quickly completed)
- One-time labor (day work)
- Simple borrowing (repay within week)
- Routine services

**Requirement:** Always have witnesses (even if not written)
- 2+ neutral people see agreement and hear terms
- If dispute later, witnesses can confirm what was agreed

**Fallback:** If verbal agreement is disputed, parties can go to community leader/council to resolve (witnesses provide evidence)

</div>

### Witness Requirement (Even for Verbal)

<div class="enforcement-box">

**Why witnesses matter:**
- Prevents one person claiming "you said that" when other party denies
- Provides evidence if dispute arises
- Discourages lying (people are less likely to lie in front of others)

**Number of witnesses:** 2 minimum (1 witness can be accused of bias; 2 witnesses are harder to dispute)

**Witness quality:**
- Should be neutral (not friend of either party; not benefiting from agreement)
- Should have good memory and character
- Should be willing to testify if needed

**Witness responsibility:**
- Hear terms of agreement clearly
- Ask clarifying questions if confused
- If asked later, confirm what was agreed
- Sign/mark if written agreement

</div>

</section>

<section id="dispute-resolution">

## 5. Dispute Resolution and Enforcement

Even good contracts are violated sometimes. Communities need clear process for resolving disputes.

### Dispute Resolution Process (Step-by-Step)

<div class="dispute-protocol">

**STEP 1: Direct Communication (Informal)**
- One party approaches other party
- Explains problem: "You promised X; you delivered Y. That's not what we agreed."
- Other party responds
- If agreement reached, they shake hands and move on
- No third party needed; no formal record

**STEP 2: Mediation (Facilitated)**
- If direct communication fails, ask neutral third party (community leader, mediator) to facilitate
- Mediator brings parties together
- Each side explains their perspective
- Mediator helps find solution both sides can accept
- If agreement reached, possibly written confirmation

**STEP 3: Arbitration (Formal)**
- If mediation fails, matter goes to arbitrator (community leader, elder, or council)
- Both parties present evidence (contracts, witnesses, goods)
- Arbitrator hears both sides
- Arbitrator makes decision
- Decision is binding (both parties must comply)

**STEP 4: Enforcement (If Decision Not Followed)**
- If arbitrator's decision is ignored, matter goes to community
- Consequences: Fines, forced labor, loss of trade privileges, public shame, expulsion

If the dispute is really about shared-resource access, who may set the rule, or whether someone can be trusted again, hand off to the commons, governance, or reputation guide before escalating the contract remedy.

</div>

### Evidence in Disputes

<div class="enforcement-box">

**Strong evidence (most reliable):**
- Written contract with both signatures
- Witnesses who saw the agreement
- Physical items (the good; the receipt; the ledger record)
- Multiple witnesses agreeing on facts

**Medium evidence:**
- Ledger entries documenting the transaction
- Word of respected community member
- Pattern of behavior (if person has violated contracts before)

**Weak evidence:**
- One person's word vs. other's
- Circumstantial evidence
- Hearsay (someone heard from someone)

**Arbitrator's decision:**
- Based on strongest evidence
- If evidence is mixed: Decide who is more credible
- If still unclear: Decision may favor whoever has less power (protect vulnerable)

</div>

### Common Dispute Types and Resolution

<div class="contract-table">

| Dispute | Claim | Evidence | Typical Resolution |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Non-delivery** | "You said you'd give me grain; you didn't" | Contract, witnesses, ledger | Force delivery or refund |
| **Quality dispute** | "Grain you gave was moldy; not good quality" | Physical inspection of grain, standard comparison | Replacement or compensation |
| **Non-payment** | "I worked; you didn't pay" | Labor log, witnesses, contract | Payment owed, possibly with penalty |
| **Wage theft** | "You said higher wage; paid less" | Original contract, ledger showing payment | Backpay the difference |
| **Lost property** | "You said you'd store my goods; they're gone" | Receipt/contract, witness, inventory | Replacement or compensation |
| **Injury during work** | "Got hurt on your property; you're responsible" | Injury evidence, witnesses, contract terms | Compensation or medical care |

</div>

</section>

<section id="enforcement-mechanisms">

## 6. Enforcement Mechanisms and Consequences

An agreement is only valuable if it's enforced. Communities need clear consequences for violation.

### Graduated Consequences

<div class="enforcement-box">

**First Violation (Minor):**
- Verbal warning from community leader
- Public acknowledgment ("I violated agreement; I'm sorry")
- Reputation damage (others know you don't honor agreements)

**Second Violation (Moderate):**
- Forced restitution (redo work, repay money, replace goods)
- Fine (pay community for violating agreement)
- Loss of trading privileges (can't trade at market for 1 month)
- Public shame (name posted; story told)

**Third Violation (Serious):**
- Extended loss of privileges (banned from trade for 6 months)
- Labor obligation (work off debt through assigned labor)
- Possible physical punishment (if culture allows; limited and specific)

**Repeated Violation (Severe):**
- Expulsion from community (person is exiled; cannot live there)
- Permanent loss of trading privileges
- Seizure of property to repay victims
- Possible injury or death if extremely serious (varies by culture/crime)

**Mitigating Factors:**
- Was violation intentional or accidental?
- Can person make restitution?
- Is person genuinely sorry?
- First-time or repeat offender?
- Severity of violation?

</div>

### Self-Help Remedies (Before Going to Authority)

<div class="enforcement-box">

If someone violates agreement, what can the non-violating party do immediately?

**Withholding of payment:**
- "You didn't deliver good grain; I'm withholding the salt until you fix this."
- Risk: Other party might escalate; use only if confident

**Seizure of collateral:**
- If agreement included collateral, can take it without waiting for arbitrator decision
- But: Must return it if arbitrator rules other party was right

**Public declaration:**
- Announce breach publicly (market, community gathering)
- Makes other party lose reputation
- Risk: Other party can sue for libel if untrue

**Refusal to work with offender in future:**
- Personally withdraw from trade with them
- Others may follow suit (social pressure)
- Risk: Doesn't force compliance; just damages relationship

**Formal complaint:**
- Report to community leader
- Request formal mediation/arbitration
- Most reliable; prevents escalation

</div>

</section>

<section id="community-bylaws">

## 7. Community Bylaws and Standing Agreements

Beyond individual contracts, communities need overall rules (bylaws) that apply to everyone.

### What Bylaws Cover

<div class="contract-element">

**Trade post rules:**
- Hours of operation
- Banned items
- Dispute resolution process
- Fees (% taken by community)

**Work and labor:**
- Minimum wage (if applicable)
- Work safety standards
- Children labor restrictions (age limits)
- Slavery prohibitions (rules about indentured service)

**Property:**
- How property is bought/sold
- How property is inherited
- How disputes over property are resolved

**Justice:**
- Crimes and punishments
- Rights of accused
- Rights of victims
- Appeals process

**Community:**
- Who can live here
- Who can trade here
- Exile process
- Rule-making process (how to change rules)

</div>

### Creating and Enforcing Bylaws

<div class="contract-element">

**Process:**
1. Community council drafts proposed bylaws
2. Community members discuss and suggest changes
3. Vote or consensus on final version
4. Written (if possible) and publicly posted
5. Council enforces through punishment for violations
6. Regular review (every 1–2 years) to update

**Enforcement:**
- Same as individual contracts (warnings, fines, loss of privileges, expulsion)
- Council responsible for enforcement
- Disputes over interpretation of bylaws go to council

**Fairness:**
- Bylaws must apply to everyone equally
- No person exempt (including leaders)
- Appeals allowed (if person claims unfair application)

</div>

</section>

<section id="special-agreements">

## 8. Special Agreements: Marriage, Property, Covenants

Some agreements are especially important and require special care.

### Marriage/Partnership Agreements

<div class="contract-example">

```
MARRIAGE AGREEMENT

Date: [date]

PARTIES:
[Person 1] and [Person 2]

COMMITMENT:
Both parties commit to a lifelong (or specified duration) partnership.

PROPERTY:
- Property owned before marriage: Remains with original owner
- Property acquired during marriage: Jointly owned (unless otherwise specified)
- If partnership ends: Each keeps their own; jointly owned divided equally

CHILDREN:
- Custody: Both parents are responsible until child reaches adulthood
- If one parent dies: Other parent has custody
- If both die: [Designated guardian]

SUPPORT:
- Both commit to financial/emotional support of each other
- If one party becomes unable to work: Other supports them
- Standard of living: Roughly equal (not unequal amounts of food, shelter, etc.)

DISSOLUTION:
- Either party may end partnership with [duration] notice
- Upon dissolution: Property division as above
- Children: Joint custody or agreed arrangement

WITNESSES:
[Community leader, family members, etc.]

SIGNATURES:
[Person 1]: __________ DATE: __________
[Person 2]: __________ DATE: __________
Witnesses: __________ DATE: __________
```

</div>

### Property Agreements (Land, Buildings)

Property agreements prevent disputes over "who owns what" and what people can do with property.

<div class="contract-element">

**Key elements:**
- Current owner and new owner
- Description of property (location, size, boundaries)
- Purchase price (or if inheritance, state that)
- Rights included (can they build on it? Farm it? Mine it?)
- Restrictions (environmental, cultural, safety)
- Warranty (seller claims no previous owner claims it)
- Dispute resolution (if later someone claims they own it, how resolve?)

</div>

### Community Covenants (Binding Multiple People)

<div class="contract-example">

```
COMMUNITY COVENANT

We, the undersigned, commit to:

1. Fair trade (no fraud, no theft)
2. Honest dealing (tell truth; keep agreements)
3. Support of vulnerable (community helps elderly, injured, children)
4. Conflict resolution (disputes resolved through mediation, not violence)
5. Shared resources (in emergency, help each other)
6. Respect of property (don't steal or trespass)
7. [Other commitments specific to community]

We understand:
- Violation of covenant results in consequences (fines, loss of privileges)
- Community council enforces covenant
- Disputes over covenant interpretation go to council
- This covenant can be amended with community vote

SIGNATURES (everyone in community signs):
[List names and signatures]
```

</div>

<section id="record-keeping">

## 9. Record-Keeping and Archival of Contracts

Contracts only work if they're preserved and accessible.

### Contract Storage

<div class="enforcement-box">

**Central repository:**
- One location where all community contracts are stored
- Climate-controlled if possible (protect from moisture, insects)
- Organized by type (trade, labor, apprenticeship, etc.)
- Indexed (list of what's stored; can find specific contract)

**Backup copies:**
- Important contracts copied; backup stored in second location
- Prevents loss if fire/flood/damage to main storage
- Community leader and council each have copy

**Access:**
- Parties to contract can request their copy
- Council can review for disputes
- Old contracts preserved (show history; evidence of pattern)

</div>

### Contract Registry

<div class="contract-element">

Create a ledger (contract registry) listing:
- Date contract made
- Parties involved
- Subject (trade, labor, apprenticeship, etc.)
- Contract ID/location (so it can be found)
- Status (active, expired, disputed, resolved)

**Why register:** Allows quick lookup ("What contracts does John have?")

</div>
