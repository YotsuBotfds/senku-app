:::danger
**Medical Disclaimer:** A first-time adult seizure, repeated seizures, or a seizure lasting more than 5 minutes is a medical emergency. The main job in the field is to protect the patient, support airway and breathing, look for reversible causes, and recognize status epilepticus early.
:::

<div class="red-flag">

### Seizure quick-triage — do this now

1. **Start a timer.** Note the exact time the seizure started.
2. **Clear the space.** Move hard or sharp objects away. Protect the head with something soft.
3. **Do not restrain.** Do not put anything in the mouth. Loosen tight clothing at the neck.
4. **Watch the clock.** If the seizure reaches **5 minutes** or seizures repeat without full recovery between them, this is **status epilepticus** — begin urgent emergency escalation. Medication decisions belong only to trained, equipped responders following an approved protocol.
5. **After the jerking stops:** roll onto side, keep airway clear, check breathing and color, check glucose if possible.

If the patient is pregnant, has a head injury, or this is their first seizure, escalate evacuation regardless of duration.

</div>

<section id="overview">

## Overview

This guide answers adult queries such as:

- "Someone is having a seizure."
- "An adult is convulsing, shaking, jerking, or fitting."
- "The person's body is stiffening or their eyes rolled back."
- "Someone is having uncontrollable movements or twitching."
- "A seizure won't stop — is it status epilepticus?"
- "What do I do right now for a seizure?"

There is seizure content elsewhere in pediatric, toxicology, and withdrawal guides. This guide exists so adult seizure retrieval does not depend on those other contexts.

</section>

<section id="first-minute">

## What To Do In The First Minute

1. **Start timing immediately.**
2. **Protect from injury.** Move furniture, tools, fire, or sharp objects away.
3. **Do not restrain the limbs.**
4. **Do not put anything in the mouth.**
5. **Loosen tight clothing around the neck.**
6. **If possible, place something soft under the head.**

If the patient is near water, stairs, traffic, or machinery, scene safety takes priority.

</section>

<section id="during-the-seizure">

## During the Seizure

### What matters

- Duration
- Breathing
- Color
- Visible trauma
- Any clue to cause: pills, alcohol withdrawal, fever, head injury, diabetes, pregnancy

### What not to do

- Do not force the jaw open.
- Do not try to make the patient drink.
- Do not hold them down unless absolutely necessary to prevent immediate lethal injury.

Brief blue discoloration can occur during a generalized tonic-clonic seizure. Persistent apnea after the convulsion ends is more dangerous and requires action.

</section>

<section id="status-epilepticus">

## When To Call It Status Epilepticus

Treat as **status epilepticus** if:

- a seizure lasts **more than 5 minutes**, or
- repeated seizures occur **without return to normal awareness between them**

This threshold matters because prolonged seizures are harder to stop, injure the brain, and create airway failure, acidosis, and trauma.

Once the seizure crosses that threshold, this is no longer a "wait and see" problem. It is an urgent airway-and-evacuation problem.

</section>

<section id="after-the-convulsion">

## Immediately After The Convulsion

### Recovery position

Once the jerking stops and it is safe to move them:

- roll the patient onto their side
- keep the airway clear
- watch the chest rise
- be ready to suction or wipe away vomit if equipment exists

### Postictal state

Many adults are confused, sleepy, combative, or incontinent for minutes to hours after a seizure. That can be normal postictal behavior, but you still reassess repeatedly.

</section>

<section id="reversible-causes">

## Reversible Causes To Check Early

### Glucose / hypoglycemia

If a glucometer exists, check glucose as soon as it is safe.

- If glucose is low and the patient is awake enough to swallow, give oral sugar.
- If the patient is not awake enough to swallow, do **not** give anything by mouth.

### Opioid clue

If the patient has slow breathing and pinpoint pupils after the seizure or collapse, consider opioid exposure and give naloxone if available.

### Withdrawal / toxicology clue

Think about alcohol withdrawal, benzodiazepine withdrawal, stimulant overdose, pesticide exposure, or toxic ingestion if there is a fitting history or scene evidence.

</section>

<section id="medication-boundaries">

## Medication Boundaries

This guide is not a benzodiazepine dosing protocol. Medication treatment for ongoing seizure activity belongs only to trained responders who have the correct equipment, airway support capability, and a protocol-approved seizure pathway.

Medication may be considered only within that trained/equipped/protocol-approved setting when:

- seizure lasts more than 5 minutes
- repeated seizures occur without recovery
- obvious convulsions continue

Seizure medicines can worsen respiratory depression, especially in mixed overdose or when airway support is limited.

If you do **not** already have protocol-approved seizure medication, airway gear, and training, do **not** improvise IV drugs or stack random sedatives from scavenged stock.

If breathing worsens after medication, airway support becomes the next priority.

Do **not** keep stacking sedatives blindly without airway monitoring capability. Do **not** improvise IV medications or repurpose sedatives outside a seizure protocol. Focus on timing, injury prevention, breathing support, and evacuation.

</section>

<section id="red-flags">

## Red Flags Requiring Urgent Escalation

- first seizure in an adult
- seizure longer than 5 minutes
- repeated seizures
- persistent failure to wake
- head injury
- fever or stiff neck
- pregnancy or possible eclampsia
- low oxygen, severe breathing trouble, or cyanosis after the event
- focal weakness after the seizure
- toxic exposure or suspected overdose

</section>

<section id="postictal-care">

## Postictal Care

- Keep the patient on their side until they can protect their airway.
- Recheck breathing, pulse, and mental status often.
- Look for tongue bite, head trauma, shoulder dislocation, or other injury from the fall.
- Do not allow the patient to walk off immediately after a hard generalized seizure.
- Document start time, stop time, number of seizures, and drugs given.

If the patient returns fully to normal after a brief known seizure disorder event, the next decision is still about cause, injury, and risk of recurrence.

</section>

<section id="summary">

## Summary

1. Time the seizure.
2. Protect from injury, but do not restrain or place anything in the mouth.
3. Turn onto the side once the convulsion stops.
4. Check glucose and breathing as soon as feasible.
5. Treat seizure beyond 5 minutes as status epilepticus.
6. Keep medication decisions inside trained, equipped, protocol-approved care with airway-support limits.
7. Evacuate urgently for any first-time adult seizure, repeated seizure, or seizure beyond 5 minutes.
