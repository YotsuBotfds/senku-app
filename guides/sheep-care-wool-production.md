Sheep are one of the most efficient livestock for small-scale farming, producing meat, wool, milk, and hides. They require moderate space, handle rough pasture, and provide a renewable fiber source. This guide covers everything from selecting breeds to processing wool.

Daily husbandry is a quick loop: check appetite, water, posture, manure, breathing, and fleece condition; refresh bedding and minerals; and isolate anything off-feed, limping, coughing, or scouring.

## Breed Selection and Purpose

Sheep breeds vary dramatically in wool quality, meat production, hardiness, and milk yield. Choose based on your climate, market, and goals.

### Wool Production (Fiber Focus)

- **Rambouillet** â€” Fine wool + decent meat; cold-hardy

### Dual-Purpose (Meat + Wool)

- **Corriedale, Columbia, Polypay** â€” Good wool and meat balance

- Lambs: born in spring, sold or kept for breeding by fall
- Replacement ewes: keep best young ewes; cull older ones (productive lifespan 4â€“6 years)

## Fencing and Shelter

### Fencing Requirements

Sheep are strong and clever escapers despite seeming docile.

**Wire fencing (most common):**
- **Woven wire:** 48â€“52 inches high; contains lambs and adult sheep
- **Welded wire:** 4â€“6 inch spacing, no smaller (sheep's head gets caught)
- **Electric fencing:** Works well; sheep learn quickly to respect it; lower cost than woven

**Installation:**
- Posts 10â€“15 feet apart (woven), 15â€“20 feet (electric)
- Extend fencing 6 inches below ground (prevents digging by predators)
- Bury or extend outward 12 inches at base (deters tunneling by coyotes)
- Sagging fencing is an escape route; keep it tight

**Gates:**
- Minimum 4 feet wide
- Latch securely (sheep open simple latches)
- Self-closing (hydraulic or spring) prevents escapes from human forgetfulness

:::warning
Predator-proof fencing is critical. Woven wire excludes foxes, coyotes, and dogs. Roof the enclosure or use guardian animals (dogs, llamas, donkeys) for serious predator areas.
:::

### Shelter

Sheep are hardy and don't require elaborate housing, but protection from extreme weather is essential.

- **Three-sided shelter (run-in shed):** Provides wind and rain protection without full enclosure
- **Size:** Minimum 15â€“20 sq ft per sheep (bigger is better)
- **Orientation:** Open side faces south (winter sun); blocks north wind
- **Bedding:** Straw or shavings, removed weekly (ammonia accumulation is unhealthy)
- **Ventilation:** Important â€” moisture and ammonia buildup cause respiratory disease
- **Predator protection:** Close at night or surround with fencing and guard animals

**Winter housing:**
- In harsh climates, full-roofed barn with adequate ventilation needed
- Separate pregnant ewes (pre-lambing) into smaller pens for monitoring

<section id="daily-husbandry">

## Daily Husbandry

A short daily walk-through catches most problems early:

- Check appetite, water, posture, manure, breathing, and fleece condition
- Refresh clean bedding, loose minerals, and feed areas
- Watch for limping, coughing, wool loss, scours, or any ewe/lamb that is separated from the flock
- Isolate any sheep that looks off so you can monitor it closely

## Feeding and Nutrition

Sheep are ruminants and efficient grazers; they're designed to eat pasture.

### Pasture Management

- **Rotation grazing:** Move flock to fresh pasture every 3â€“7 days (prevents parasite buildup and soil degradation)
- **Stocking rate:** 4â€“8 sheep per acre depending on pasture quality and climate
- **Legumes:** Clover, alfalfa are high-protein and improve soil; include in pasture mix
- **Supplemental browse:** Sheep thrive on bushes, brambles, leaves; let them clean up overgrown areas

### Hay (Winter Feeding)

- **Quality:** Leafy, green hay (not brown, dust-filled)
- **Quantity:** Roughly 4â€“5 pounds hay per sheep per day in winter
- **Storage:** Waterproof, well-ventilated location; mold is dangerous
- **Legume hay:** Alfalfa or clover hay has higher protein; reduces grain supplementation

### Grain Supplementation

Most sheep do well on pasture + hay alone if pasture is good quality. Supplemental grain is needed:

- **During late pregnancy:** 0.5â€“1 lb per ewe per day (last 6 weeks before lambing)
- **During lactation:** 0.5â€“1.5 lbs per ewe per day (depending on milk production demands)
- **Growing lambs:** 1â€“2 lbs per day until market weight
- **Wool growth:** Protein-rich grain (oats, barley, legume meal blend) supports fleece quality

**Grain mix (example):**
- Oats or barley: 60%
- Legume meal (alfalfa, clover meal): 20%
- Molasses: 10% (palatability)
- Minerals/salt: 10%

### Water

- Constant access to clean, fresh water
- Winter: heated waterer prevents freezing
- Daily volume: 1â€“1.5 gallons per adult sheep (doubles in summer or with lactation)

### Minerals and Vitamins

Most pasture provides adequate minerals, but supplementation may be needed:

- **Loose mineral mix:** Sheep often ignore mineral blocks; loose mix placed in feeder is better consumed
- **Salt:** 0.5% of grain mix or free-choice loose salt/mineral
- **Copper:** Critical for wool quality and health; deficiency causes fiber discoloration. Some pastures are copper-deficient; consult a veterinarian.
- **Selenium:** Deficiency causes "white muscle disease" in lambs. Regional deficiency requires supplementation.

:::info-box
Work with a veterinarian or extension agent to test your pasture and water for mineral content. Mineral requirements vary by region; blanket supplementation is waste, but deficiency is dangerous.
:::

## Shearing and Wool Handling Basics

### Shearing Frequency and Timing

- **Annual shearing:** Once per year in spring (before warm weather, after lambing)
- **Timing:** When lambs are old enough to thermoregulate without fleece (4-6 weeks old)
- **Professional shearing:** Hire a contractor ($3-8 per sheep depending on region); saves time and sheep are handled safely

### Shearing Flow

1. **1-2 weeks before:** Dag soiled wool, check body condition, and schedule a dry shearing day.
2. **Shearing day:** Work with a dry fleece, shear close to the skin in one clean pass when possible, and avoid second cuts.
3. **Right after:** Move sheep to a sheltered, dry pen, then skirt, sort, roll, label, and store the fleece.

### Preparing for Shearing

- **Dagging:** Remove soiled wool from hindquarters and legs 1-2 weeks before shearing
- **Weathering:** Keep sheep sheltered for 2 days before shearing (dry fleece is easier to shear and keeps clean)
- **Parasite control:** Shear before internal parasite treatment so skin-applied products work directly on the skin.

### Fleece Handling

After shearing:

1. **Skirting:** Remove VM (vegetable matter - hay, seeds, twigs) and soiled/discolored edges while fleece is still pliable
2. **Sorting:** Grade fleece by color, staple length, and quality; keep colors separated
3. **Rolling:** Roll fleece staple-inward (natural oils outward), then bag in a breathable (burlap or mesh) bag labeled by sheep, date, and breed
4. **Storage:** Cool, dry, dark place; humidity control prevents mold growth. Do not store greasy wool in sealed plastic.

### Wool Yields

- **Average:** 8-20 lbs clean equivalent per sheep per year (varies by breed)
- **Greasy weight:** What comes off the sheep (includes lanolin and dirt; 20-30 lbs)
- **Clean weight:** After washing and scouring (40-50% of greasy weight)
- **Staple length:** Longer staples (3+ inches) command premium prices

:::tip
Market wool based on micron (fiber diameter) and staple length. Merino wool is fine (14-24 micron), while carpet wools are coarse (24+ micron). Spinners prefer fine, long staple; coarse wool is suited to carpets and insulation.
:::

## Lambing and Reproduction

### Breeding Cycle

- **Ewe heat cycle:** 17 days (visible signs: tail flagging, restlessness, swollen vulva)
- **Breeding season:** Fall (naturally short day) in most breeds; ewes cycle Septemberâ€“November
- **Gestation:** 145â€“150 days (roughly 5 months)
- **Lambing season:** Spring (Marchâ€“May in northern hemisphere)
- **Fertility:** Rams can breed year-round; ewes are seasonal breeders

### Pre-Lambing Preparation

**6 weeks before lambing:**
- Increase nutrition (see Feeding section)
- Vaccinate for clostridial diseases (CDT vaccine) â€” protects lambs passively through colostrum
- Trim feet
- Dagg wool from hindquarters for cleanliness during birth

**Lambing facilities:**
- Separate pens (jugs) for each ewe-lamb pair; allows bonding and monitoring
- Heat lamp for lambs in cold weather (keep shed >50Â°F)
- Bedding changed daily (cleanliness reduces disease)

### Lambing Management

**Normal labor and birth:**
- Restlessness, tail twitching, straining for 30 minutesâ€“2 hours before birth
- Amniotic sac appears first (water bag)
- Lamb born nose-first, followed by front legs, then back
- Ewe usually stands immediately and turns to lick lamb

**Complications (seek veterinary help if available):**
- No progress after 2 hours of straining
- Abnormal presentation (leg first, breech, head back)
- Ewe in severe distress

**Post-birth care:**
- Remove amniotic membrane from lamb's face; ensure breathing
- Allow ewe and lamb to bond (lick and nuzzle)
- Lamb nurses within 1â€“2 hours
- If lamb weak or ewe won't nurse, bottle-feed colostrum (first milk) immediately
- Umbilical cord disinfection (iodine dip) prevents navel ill

### Bottle Feeding (if necessary)

- **Colostrum:** First 24 hours essential (provides immunity antibodies)
- **Lamb milk replacer:** From 24 hours onward (cow's milk is inadequate; too low fat and lactose)
- **Feeding schedule:** 4x daily first week, then 3x daily, weaning by 6â€“8 weeks
- **Volume:** 10â€“20% of body weight per day in divided feedings

:::warning
Lambs rejected by ewes are often weak or sickly. Before investing in bottle-feeding, assess whether the lamb is likely to thrive. Culling weak lambs is humane and economically necessary on farms.
:::

## Common Diseases and Health Management

### Parasites (Internal)

**Gastrointestinal nematodes** (roundworms) are the most common and economically significant parasite.

- **Signs:** Diarrhea, weight loss, pale mucous membranes (anemia), bottle-jaw (swelling under jaw)
- **Risk:** Young lambs and ewes in early lactation are most susceptible
- **Prevention:** Rotation grazing, stocking rate management, minimize fecal contamination
- **Treatment:** Deworming with anthelmintic (sheep have growing resistance; rotate drug classes)
  - Benzimidazoles (fenbendazole, albendazole)
  - Avermectins (ivermectin, doramectin)
  - Levamisole
- **Timing:** Spring (before lambing) and summer/fall (high-risk periods)
- **Routine control:** Use fecal testing or targeted visual checks when risk is high, and treat selectively when possible to slow resistance.
- **After treatment:** Move treated sheep to cleaner pasture or a dry lot if you can, so they are not immediately reinfected.

### Diseases (Infectious)
**Clostridial diseases (enterotoxemia, blackleg):**
- Caused by *Clostridium perfringens* and *Clostridium chauvoei*
- Vaccination (CDT vaccine) is highly effective
- Acute deaths, often in best-conditioned lambs

**Pneumonia (respiratory infection):**
- Common in young lambs, especially with poor ventilation
- Caused by bacteria (*Pasteurella*, *Mannheimia*) or viruses
- Prevention: good housing, avoid drafts, minimize stress

**Foot rot:**
- Bacterial infection of foot tissue (see Hoof Care section)

**Mastitis (udder infection):**
- Especially in lactating ewes
- Signs: Hard, hot udder; ewe reluctant to nurse; lamb starving
- Prevention: sanitation, good milking practices, teat dips
- Treatment: antibiotics if available; cull if chronic

**Sore mouth (orf):**
- Viral infection causing scabs on lips and udder
- Highly contagious to humans (wear gloves)
- Usually self-limiting; treatment is supportive

:::warning
If multiple lambs or ewes die suddenly, suspect clostridial disease. Vaccination of the flock can prevent further losses. Always vaccinate breeding flocks.
:::

### Parasites (External)

- **Lice:** Visible movement in wool; cause itching and wool loss. Treat with pour-on or dip.
- **Mites:** Less common than lice; cause scabby skin and hair loss.
- **Blowfly strike:** Fly larvae infest soiled wool (daggy areas). Prevention: dagging, tail docking (short tails), insect repellents.

## Hoof Care and Foot Health

Healthy hooves are critical for grazing animals.

### Anatomy

Sheep hooves are cloven (two toes) and grow continuously. Without wear from hard ground or trimming, hooves overgrow and curl.

### Trimming

- **Frequency:** Every 4â€“8 weeks, depending on pasture wear
- **Tools:** Hoof knife or pruners
- **Technique:** Trim dead sole/wall material; maintain horizontal surface and slight point at toe. Do not cut too short (bleeding = infection risk).
- **Angle:** View from side â€” foot should have a 50-degree angle from fetlock to ground
- **Frequency:** Less often on hard ground (natural wear), more on soft pasture

### Foot Rot

**Cause:** *Dichelobacter nodosus* bacteria and environmental moisture

**Signs:** Lameness, red-line at hoof-skin junction, foul smell, possible pus

**Treatment:**
- Trim foot, removing all diseased tissue (may be painful)
- Disinfect with antibiotic or foot bath (copper sulfate, zinc sulfate solutions, or commercial foot trims)
- Isolate affected sheep
- Repeat treatment in 1â€“2 weeks
- In severe cases, systemic antibiotics

**Prevention:**
- Avoid prolonged wet conditions (standing in mud)
- Dry shelters, well-drained pasture
- Regular hoof trimming
- Culling chronically affected sheep

<section id="predator-protection">

## Predator Protection

Sheep are vulnerable prey. Protecting them is essential unless predator risk is very low.

### Guardian Animals

**Livestock guardian dogs (LGDs):**
- Bred to live with flock 24/7
- Breeds: Great Pyrenees, Anatolian Shepherd, Komondor
- Cost: Significant upfront, but very effective
- Training: Imprinted on sheep from puppyhood; bond to flock, not owner

**Llamas or alpacas:**
- Natural predator vigilance; will chase coyotes and small dogs
- Less effective against large dogs or packs
- Low cost; multi-purpose

**Donkeys:**
- Territorial and aggressive toward canids
- Some breeds gentler; others aggressive toward sheep
- Good combination with LGD

### Fencing and Confinement

- Predator-proof fencing (see Fencing section)
- Nighttime confinement in secure barn or pen (where possible)
- Minimize vulnerable times (kidding, very young lambs)

### Trapping and Removal

If predator losses continue despite precautions:
- Contact wildlife agency for removal (may trap and relocate or euthanize)
- Shooting (if legally permitted)
- Avoid poisoning (indiscriminate, risks non-target species)

:::info-box
The combination of guardian animals + fencing + vigilant management is most effective. One approach alone rarely provides complete protection against determined predators.
:::
