<section id="overview">

## Overview

Indoor mold is usually a moisture problem first and a cleaning problem second. Mold grows where water, condensation, or persistent humidity lets it feed on wood, drywall, paper, cloth, dust, or food. If you remove the moisture and dry the area quickly, most small household problems stay small. If you scrub the visible mold but leave the leak or humidity source alone, the mold will usually come back.

This guide is written for common household cases:

- Black mold on walls or around windows
- Moldy clothes, bedding, and stored linens
- Mold in a root cellar, basement, pantry, or storage room
- Humid cabin problems, condensation, and stale air
- Deciding what can be cleaned and what should be discarded
- Protecting people during cleanup

:::tip
If you remember one sentence, make it this: dry the source first, clean the surface second, and throw away anything that cannot be fully cleaned and dried.
:::

<section id="spotting-mold">

## Spotting Mold and the Usual Causes

Mold can look fuzzy, powdery, slimy, dusty, or blotchy. It may be white, gray, green, brown, orange, or black. Color alone does not tell you how dangerous it is. A black patch on a wall is not automatically a special "black mold" species; it is often just a damp growth on paint, drywall, caulk, or dust.

### Common signs

- Musty, earthy, or stale smell
- Dark spotting on corners, windows, closets, or exterior walls
- Condensation on windows, pipes, or cold surfaces
- Peeling paint, swollen baseboards, soft drywall, or warped wood
- Clothes, books, or boxes that smell damp after storage
- Repeated mildew in the same room after cleaning

### Common causes

- Roof, plumbing, or window leaks
- High indoor humidity from cooking, bathing, or drying clothes indoors
- Poor ventilation in bathrooms, kitchens, closets, crawlspaces, and cabins
- Cold walls and uninsulated surfaces that collect condensation
- Wet basement floors, damp soil, or a leaking foundation
- Stored goods packed too tightly with no airflow
- Rotting produce, damp bedding, or dirty laundry left closed up

### Fast identification questions

Ask these in order:

1. Is there visible water, condensation, or a leak?
2. Does the problem appear after cooking, showering, or sleeping with windows shut?
3. Is the material porous, such as drywall, carpet padding, mattress foam, paper, or particleboard?
4. Does the smell come back after you clean?

If the answer is yes to the first, second, or fourth question, solve the moisture problem first.
If you are trying to decide whether the space is ready for occupancy after cleanup, use `building-inspection-habitability-checklist.md` alongside this guide.

<section id="first-response">

## First Response When You Find Mold

1. Open windows or doors if outdoor air is drier and safer than indoor air.
2. Stop the water source if you can do so safely.
3. Remove people who are sensitive to mold from the area.
4. Wear gloves, eye protection, and a respirator rated at least N95 if you will disturb the growth.
5. Bag loose contaminated items before carrying them through the house.
6. Clean the area only after it is no longer wet from leaks or flooding.
7. Dry the space completely with airflow, heat, dehumidification, or sun as appropriate.

Do not paint over moldy surfaces, and do not caulk over an active problem. That hides the symptom while trapping the cause.

:::warning
Do not mix bleach with ammonia, vinegar, or other cleaners. Mixed cleaners can make dangerous fumes.
:::

<section id="moisture-control">

## Moisture Control: The Main Fix

The key to mold control is moisture control. A space that stays dry stays hard for mold to colonize.

### Humidity targets

- Aim for indoor humidity around 30-50% when practical
- Try to stay below 60% relative humidity
- Watch for condensation on windows, cold walls, and pipes as a warning sign

### Reduce moisture at the source

- Fix roof leaks, flashing failures, and plumbing drips promptly
- Clean gutters and downspouts so water moves away from the house
- Slope soil away from the foundation
- Vent bathrooms, dryers, kitchens, and laundry rooms to the outside
- Use exhaust fans during and after showering or cooking
- Dry wet shoes, coats, towels, and mops outside the living space
- Do not keep damp firewood, cardboard, or boxes indoors
- Keep furniture and storage bins a little away from exterior walls

### Reduce condensation

- Insulate cold surfaces that stay damp
- Add airflow around windows, behind furniture, and in closets
- Warm the room enough to keep surfaces above the dew point
- Use a dehumidifier where electricity and conditions allow
- Avoid sealing in moisture with heavy plastic unless the assembly is designed for it

### Humid cabin problems

Cabins often get mold from a mix of tight construction, wood heat, wet boots, and poor airflow. The usual fixes are:

- Air out the cabin daily when weather permits
- Keep a little heat on during damp weather so surfaces dry faster
- Vent the bathroom, kitchen, and sleeping loft
- Store firewood, wet clothes, and muddy gear outside or in a separate shed
- Check around windows, roof edges, and north-facing walls for condensation
- Inspect under beds, behind cupboards, and in closets where air gets trapped

<section id="black-mold-walls">

## Black Mold on Walls, Windows, and Bathrooms

Black spots on walls are common around windows, shower walls, baseboards, and poorly insulated corners. The color may be dark because of dust, paint, or the species involved, but the cleanup logic is the same: find the moisture source, clean the surface, and dry it fully.

If someone asks "black mold on wall," this is the section to start with.

### What to look for

- Dark streaks around window frames from condensation
- Mold on bathroom grout, ceiling corners, or behind the toilet
- Growth on drywall near a leak, especially if the wall feels soft or swollen
- Mould returning in the same spot after repeated wiping

### What to do

1. Check for a leak or cold-surface condensation.
2. If the wall is painted or tiled and still firm, scrub with detergent and water.
3. Rinse as needed and dry thoroughly.
4. Improve ventilation so the wall dries faster after showers or cooking.
5. If drywall is swollen, crumbly, or keeps growing mold, remove and replace the affected section.

### When wall material should usually be discarded

- Drywall that has softened or swollen
- Insulation that got wet and stayed wet
- Ceiling tiles, fiberboard, or acoustic panels
- Wallpaper or wall covering with mold behind it

Hard wall surfaces like glazed tile, glass, metal, sealed countertops, and painted wood are usually cleanable if they are still sound.

<section id="clean-vs-discard">

## What Can Be Cleaned Versus Discarded

Use material type, not sentiment, to decide.

| Material | Usually Cleanable? | What to Do |
|---|---:|---|
| Glass, glazed tile, sealed metal, hard plastic | Yes | Wash with detergent and water, scrub, rinse, and dry completely |
| Solid wood furniture | Sometimes | Clean surface mold, dry thoroughly, and monitor for softening or odor |
| Painted drywall | Sometimes, if the mold is very limited | Clean only if the surface is firm and the moisture source is fixed |
| Drywall, insulation, ceiling tile, carpet padding | Usually no | Replace if moldy or water-damaged for more than a short period |
| Mattresses and pillows | Usually no | Discard if mold reached the filling or there is persistent odor |
| Upholstered furniture | Usually no | Discard if mold is in cushions, fabric, or foam |
| Books, paper, cardboard, photographs | Usually no | Separate dry salvage from moldy items; discard badly affected material |
| Clothes, towels, bedding | Often yes | Wash promptly and dry fully if fabric is still structurally sound |
| Leather, suede, wool, and delicate textiles | Sometimes | Clean carefully if lightly affected; discard if deeply colonized or rotting |
| Food and produce | Usually no | Discard moldy food unless the item is a known exception and the guide for that food says otherwise |

### A simple rule

If mold has grown into the inside of the item, or the item cannot be fully dried, it is usually cheaper and safer to discard it.

### A few special cases

- A small amount of mold on a hard, nonporous surface is often cleanable
- A musty smell in a mattress, pillow, or carpet pad usually means mold is deeper than the surface
- If the item is porous and expensive, salvage may be possible with a specialist, but do not count on it

<section id="cleaning">

## Cleaning Molded Surfaces Safely

### Personal protection

Before you start:

- Wear gloves
- Wear eye protection
- Wear a respirator or at least an N95
- Wear long sleeves and clothes you can wash immediately
- Keep children, pets, and sensitive people away from the area

### Basic cleanup method for hard surfaces

1. Ventilate the room.
2. Wet the surface lightly if dry dust would be disturbed.
3. Wash with detergent and water.
4. Scrub until visible growth is removed.
5. Rinse if needed.
6. Dry completely.

For small household jobs, detergent and water are usually the main tools. Strong chemicals are not required for routine cleanup.

### Do not rely on bleach alone

Bleach can lighten stains on some surfaces, but it is not a good all-purpose answer for mold. It is especially poor on porous materials because it does not reach deep growth well, and the moisture in the cleaner can leave the material wet longer. On hard surfaces, cleaning and drying matter more than the brand of chemical.

### Cleaning order

1. Remove loose debris
2. Clean the dirtiest visible areas first
3. Work from cleaner zones toward dirtier zones
4. Bag cloths, wipes, or disposable wipes when finished
5. Wash hands and clothes after the job

:::tip
If you can dry the surface fast, you are reducing the chance that the mold problem returns.
:::

## Moldy Clothes, Bedding, and Stored Linens

Cloth is one of the most common household mold problems because fabric traps moisture and dust. The risk is highest in closets, laundry rooms, damp basements, and beds that sit against cold walls.

### Moldy clothes

- Wash washable clothing as soon as you find it
- Use the hottest water the fabric can tolerate
- Add detergent and dry completely
- Sun and moving air help remove remaining odor and moisture
- If the item still smells musty after thorough washing and drying, repeat or discard it

For full hand-wash and drying steps, see `laundry-clothes-washing.md`.

### Bedding and mattresses

Bedding should not stay damp overnight. Wash sheets, blankets, mattress pads, and pillow covers promptly if they smell musty or have visible spots. Dry them fully before putting them back on the bed.

Discard the bedding if:

- Mold reached the fill material inside a pillow, comforter, or mattress
- The mattress is wet through and cannot dry quickly
- The fabric is rotten, stained throughout, or still smells strongly after cleaning

### How to prevent mold in bedding

- Keep the bed away from exterior walls if possible
- Let air move under and around the bed
- Wash bedding regularly
- Do not store wet blankets, sleeping bags, or sheets in a closed tote
- Use breathable storage for dry bedding, not damp plastic bags
- Dry the bedroom air if windows or walls get wet from condensation

### Stored goods made of cloth or paper

- Keep linen, blankets, and seasonal clothing dry before storage
- Store items off the floor on shelves or pallets
- Leave some space between boxes for airflow
- Do not pack damp clothes or quilts for later
- Check stored goods seasonally for odor, spotting, or insect damage

<section id="root-cellar">

## Mold in a Root Cellar or Pantry

Root cellars are supposed to be cool and humid, but they still need airflow and inspection. The goal is not to make them bone dry. The goal is to keep produce from rotting and to stop mold from spreading from one item to the next.

### Common root cellar problems

- Condensation on walls, lids, or metal shelves
- Rotten apples, potatoes, squash, or onions left too long
- Damp cardboard boxes or sacks
- Poor airflow in corners or under stacked crates
- Soil moisture entering through the floor or foundation

### Better root cellar practices

- Use ventilated shelving and breathable crates
- Keep produce dry on the outside before storage
- Separate damaged or overripe items immediately
- Remove any item that smells rotten or feels slimy
- Wash and dry shelves, bins, and floor surfaces after a spoilage event
- Keep cardboard, paper sacks, and loose fabric from staying damp against walls

### What to discard from the root cellar

- Moldy food that is soft, wet, or rotten through
- Paper cartons, cardboard, or labels that have gotten wet and fuzzy
- Produce with visible mold spreading into the flesh
- Bins or sacks that keep smelling musty after cleaning

### What can usually be saved

- Clean, dry shelving
- Hard containers that wash clean
- Sound root vegetables with only small localized spoilage removed promptly

:::warning
In a root cellar, one rotten item can seed the rest of the storage. Check often and remove bad produce quickly.
:::

<section id="prevention">

## Prevention for the Long Term

Prevention is mostly maintenance.

### Weekly and monthly habits

- Look for leaks, drips, and stains
- Check window condensation and bathroom fans
- Inspect closets, under beds, behind furniture, and around exterior walls
- Rotate stored clothes, bedding, and pantry goods
- Smell for mustiness before mold becomes visible
- Keep floor drains, gutters, and downspouts clear

### Storage habits

- Store dry goods in clean, dry containers
- Keep boxes off concrete floors if the floor sweats
- Leave space around stored items for air movement
- Use breathable covers for furniture and bedding when needed
- Do not store wet boots, tarps, or towels in the same closed space as clean goods

### Building habits

- Insulate cold surfaces that condense
- Seal leaks quickly
- Use exhaust fans where moisture is produced
- Keep crawlspaces and basements dry if possible
- Repair water damage within a day or two rather than letting it sit

</section>

<section id="when-to-stop">

## When to Stop and Get Help

Stop DIY cleanup and call for help if:

- The moldy area is large or keeps spreading
- The wall cavity, attic, or HVAC system is involved
- Sewage, floodwater, or contaminated water is involved
- The material is structurally damaged
- Someone in the home has severe asthma, immune suppression, or repeated symptoms around the mold
- You cannot find or stop the moisture source

Professional mold cleanup is not about making the home "sterile." It is about removing damaged material, stopping water intrusion, and restoring dry conditions.
