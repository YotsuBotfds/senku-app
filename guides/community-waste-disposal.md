## Introduction

In settlements and camps, unmanaged waste attracts disease vectors (rats, flies, mosquitoes), contaminates water sources, and rapidly degrades living conditions. Community-scale waste management requires organization, infrastructure, and sustained effort. This guide covers designing waste collection systems, sorting and composting facilities, handling hazardous materials, and community engagement strategies to prevent waste-related disease outbreaks and improve sanitation conditions.

## Understanding Waste Streams

### Waste Categories

**Organic waste (40–60% of total):**
- Food scraps (vegetable peelings, bones, spoiled food)
- Garden waste (leaves, branches, grass clippings, weeds)
- Paper and cardboard (if uncoated or minimally treated)
- Biological waste from food processing or livestock

**Inorganic waste (30–50%):**
- Plastics (bags, bottles, containers, films)
- Glass (bottles, jars, broken items)
- Metal (cans, wire, aluminum foil, tools)
- Ceramics and stone

**Hazardous waste (1–5%):**
- Medical waste (bandages, used syringes, contaminated dressings)
- Pesticide containers and chemical residues
- Batteries and electronic waste
- Fuel and oil containers

**Bulky waste:**
- Discarded furniture, tools, wood scraps
- Building materials and rubble
- Textiles and clothing beyond repair

### Waste Generation Rates

Typical waste generation per person per day:

| Setting | Rate | Notes |
|---------|------|-------|
| Affluent urban area | 1.5–2.5 kg/person/day | High plastics and packaging |
| Moderate-income settlement | 0.8–1.2 kg/person/day | Mix of organic and inorganic |
| Low-income camp | 0.3–0.6 kg/person/day | Primarily organic and minimal packaging |
| Rural or subsistence village | 0.2–0.4 kg/person/day | High self-consumption; minimal waste |

For planning, use the rates for your setting and multiply by the number of residents. A camp of 5,000 people at 0.6 kg/person/day generates 3 metric tons of waste daily.

## Collection System Design

### Route and Frequency

**Collection frequency:**
- **Daily:** In warm, wet climates where decomposition is rapid and flies breed quickly
- **Every 2–3 days:** In moderate climates or during cooler seasons
- **Weekly:** In cold climates or during dry season (slower decomposition and fewer disease vectors)
- **On-demand:** In very low-density settlements, collection whenever piles accumulate

**Collection routes:**
1. Divide the settlement into zones (8–12 households per collector in dense areas; 30–50 in sparse areas)
2. Mark collection points (central dumps or collection bins) within walking distance (~100 m) of households
3. Schedule routes so each zone is collected once per day or cycle
4. Assign collection teams (2–3 people per route) and rotate duties weekly to spread responsibility

### Collection Infrastructure

**Bins and containers:**
- Large drums (200 liters), woven baskets, wooden boxes, or concrete bins
- Containers should have lids or covers to prevent rain, flies, and rodents
- Position at accessible locations (central areas, crossroads) but away from water sources and living areas
- Ensure containers can be emptied by hand or with simple tools (avoid complex mechanical systems that may break)

**Transportation:**
- **Hand-carts:** Low-tech, suitable for 20–50 kg loads, powered by humans
- **Animal carts:** Ox, donkey, or horse-drawn carts for larger loads; require animal fodder but are reliable
- **Bicycles with trailers:** For lighter loads and narrow paths
- **Vehicle transport:** Only viable if fuel and maintenance are sustainable; avoid if not self-sufficient

**Tipping site or transfer station:**
- Located 200–500 m downwind and downslope from settlement
- Surrounded by a berm or fence to contain waste and prevent scattering
- Access controlled; limited to authorized personnel
- Separate areas for organic waste, inorganic materials, and hazardous waste

### Community Engagement in Collection

**Household waste separation:**
- Provide simple instructions (verbal or pictorial) for households to separate waste at source
- Distribute collection containers or assign collection days
- Establish rules: no liquid waste, medical waste, or hazardous materials in the general stream

**Community involvement:**
- Train 2–5 community waste managers per 100 households to oversee collection
- Rotate collection duties among households or organize a volunteer schedule
- Hold monthly community meetings to discuss waste management challenges
- Celebrate high-participation areas or teams with community recognition

**Incentives:**
- Use recovered materials (aluminum, metals, plastic) to generate income, shared among collection volunteers
- Prioritize waste collection areas that achieve high participation with improved amenities (water points, communal areas)
- In camps, allocate food or cash-for-work payments to waste collection teams

## Sorting and Processing Facilities

### Separation Station Design

A facility where waste is sorted into processing streams.

**Layout (for a community of 2,000–5,000):**
1. **Receiving area:** Where collected waste is dumped
2. **Sorting tables or platforms:** Elevated work surfaces (chest height) where sorters manually separate waste
3. **Organic compost area:** Dedicated space for food scraps and garden waste
4. **Recyclables storage:** Covered area for sorted metals, plastics, glass, and paper
5. **Hazardous waste containment:** Locked area for medical, chemical, and other hazardous materials
6. **Finished compost area:** Where aged compost is stored before use or distribution

**Staffing:**
- 1 sorter per 100–200 kg of daily waste processed
- For 3 tons/day (typical for 5,000 people), target 15–30 sorters
- Organize into teams: food waste, plastics, metals, glass, paper, and hazardous materials
- Provide gloves, masks, and basic medical supplies

**Safety:**
- Train all sorters on hazard recognition (broken glass, sharp metal, contaminated items)
- Require gloves, masks, and footwear at all times
- Set up a first aid station; maintain supplies for minor injuries
- Screen sorters for vaccination status (tetanus, hepatitis B, polio) if possible

### Composting at Scale

Large-scale composting serves multiple purposes: reduces organic waste volume (by 60–80%), produces soil amendment, and eliminates odor-attracting materials that harbor flies and rats.

**Windrow composting (most practical for communities):**
1. Pile organic waste in long, low ridges (windrows)
2. Dimensions: 2 m wide, 1.5 m high, length as needed for daily or weekly input
3. Layer food waste, leaves, and grass clippings in alternating 20 cm layers
4. Cover the pile with a 10 cm layer of soil or finished compost to reduce flies and odor
5. Every 2–4 weeks, turn the pile with a shovel or small machinery (if available)
6. Finished compost (dark, crumbly, no recognizable food) ready in 3–6 months (warmer climates) or 6–12 months (cooler climates)

**Factors affecting compost quality:**
- **Carbon-to-nitrogen ratio:** Mix 50% organic waste (food and garden waste combined) with 50% bulking agent (dry leaves, straw, cardboard shreddings, sawdust from partner workshops)
- **Moisture:** 50–65%; if pile is too wet, add bulking material; if too dry, dampen with water or greywater
- **Aeration:** Turning is essential; piles that are not turned become anaerobic, smell awful, and attract flies

**Finished compost use:**
- Distribute free or low-cost to community members for home gardens
- Use on public green spaces, tree planting projects, or reforestation efforts
- Store for 3–6 months before use on food crops to ensure safety
- In camps, prioritize distribution to most vulnerable groups (families with children, elderly)

**Compost facility staffing:**
- 1 supervisor per facility, overseeing operations and safety
- 3–5 workers for pile maintenance, turning, and distribution
- In large camps, organize compost work as cash-for-work activity, benefiting unemployed residents

:::tip
**Quick-Rotting Strategy:** If composting space is limited, bury fresh organic waste in shallow pits (30 cm deep) in the forest or distant area, covering with soil. This accelerates decomposition away from the settlement and eliminates odor. After 6 months, the decomposed material can be returned for use. This "pit composting" method works well in rural or dispersed settlements.
:::

## Burn Pits and Controlled Burning

In some contexts, burning is necessary or unavoidable. However, burning waste creates air pollution, releases carbon, and produces hazardous ash. Minimize burning and do so carefully.

### When Burning Is Acceptable

- Medical waste (bandages, contaminated dressings, syringes) in designated pits with adequate ventilation
- Plastic-heavy waste with no recyclable value (in regions without recycling markets)
- Woody or bulky waste with no other use
- Building debris and cardboard (uncoated, no paint)

### When Burning Is Prohibited

- Plastic that can be recycled or reused (bags, bottles, containers)
- Materials that release toxic fumes: PVC, treated wood, electronics, batteries, foam
- Wet or fresh organic waste (creates smoke and incomplete combustion)
- In high-wind conditions or near homes (smoke hazard)

### Safe Burning Procedure

1. **Pit construction:**
   - Dig a pit 1–2 m deep and 1 m diameter in a remote location (downwind and downslope from settlement)
   - Line with stones to concentrate heat and contain ash
   - Clear vegetation 5 m around the pit

2. **Material preparation:**
   - Allow waste to dry thoroughly (wet material produces smoke)
   - Break bulky items into smaller pieces
   - Keep hazardous materials (plastics, chemicals) separate

3. **Burning:**
   - Light the fire with dry wood or cardboard
   - Add material gradually; overloading creates smoke
   - Allow flames to burn down before adding more material
   - Never leave a burning pit unattended
   - Have water or sand ready for emergencies

4. **Ash management:**
   - Allow ash to cool completely (24 hours minimum)
   - Medical ash should be placed in sealed containers and disposed of separately
   - Non-hazardous ash can be mixed with compost (small quantities) or used as soil amendment
   - Avoid spreading ash from burnt plastics or treated wood on gardens

:::danger
**Health Risks of Burning:** Burning plastic and treated materials releases dioxins, furans, and heavy metals. Inhalation can cause respiratory disease, cancer, and developmental problems in children. Avoid burning in settlements; establish burn pits at least 500 m away from homes and burn only necessary materials under strict conditions.
:::

## Recycling and Repurposing Workshops

Establishing repair and repurposing workshops extends material life, generates income, and reduces waste.

### Types of Workshops

**Metal working:**
- Collect scrap metal (aluminum, iron, copper) and sort by type
- Sell to scrap dealers or partner with metalworkers who create tools, cookware, or decorative items
- Training: basic metalworking skills (heating, shaping, joining) for 5–10 community members

**Plastic repurposing:**
- Collect plastic bottles, bags, and containers
- Wash and dry; sort by color
- Create items: plant containers, rope (from melted plastic straps), woven mats (from bags), or building material (compressed plastic blocks)
- Training: basic techniques require little equipment; teach to 3–5 workshop staff

**Textile and clothing:**
- Sort usable clothing for distribution to vulnerable groups
- Repair damaged items (sewing workshops) for re-use
- Shred or compost unusable textiles
- Training: sewing, pattern adjustment, mending; teach to 5–10 volunteers

**Paper and cardboard:**
- Collect uncontaminated paper and cardboard
- Shred for animal bedding, mulch, or pulping
- Partner with soap makers (cardboard can be composted with other organic waste)
- Training: paper shredding and classification; basic equipment

**Glass and ceramics:**
- Collect unbroken glass and ceramics for reuse as drinking vessels or decorative items
- Broken glass: crush and use in concrete, drainage, or artistic mosaics
- Establish glass collection carefully (sharp hazard); distribute to trained users only
- Training: glass crushing safety; mosaic or concrete work techniques

### Establishing Workshops

**Planning:**
1. Assess available materials (conduct a waste audit to see what is abundant and valuable)
2. Identify community members with existing skills (metalworkers, seamstresses, craftspeople)
3. Start small: 1–2 workshops with high material demand (metal and plastic most common)
4. Grow gradually as market demand and staff capability increase

**Infrastructure:**
- Simple shelter (roof, open sides) to protect workers and materials from rain
- Basic tools specific to each workshop (hammers, anvils for metal; sewing needles and thread for textiles; etc.)
- Storage for raw materials and finished products
- Safety equipment (gloves, masks for plastic fumes, first aid supplies)

**Revenue model:**
- Sell finished products to community members or external markets
- Pay workers a portion of revenue (often 50–70% to workers, 30–50% to maintain materials and equipment)
- Reinvest some revenue in training and equipment upgrades

**Community benefit:**
- Provides employment and income (especially valuable in camps and displaced settlements)
- Reduces waste volume reaching landfills or burn pits
- Supplies affordable household items and tools
- Builds community skills and social cohesion

:::info-box
**Workshop Sustainability:** Successful recycling workshops depend on stable demand for finished products and reliable material supply. Before starting, survey the community to understand what items are needed and what waste streams are available. Avoid starting workshops for items already abundant or easily obtained, as finished products will not sell.
:::

## Medical and Hazardous Waste Management

Human waste is one source of infection, but medical waste—used bandages, syringes, and contaminated dressings—poses acute infection risk if mishandled.

### Medical Waste Segregation

**Collection:**
- Train health workers and patients to place medical waste immediately in dedicated, marked containers
- Use containers distinct from general waste (color-coded if possible: red, yellow, or black for medical waste)
- Line containers with plastic bags for safe handling and transport

**Categories:**
- **Sharps (syringes, needles, blades):** Rigid puncture-proof containers to prevent needle stick injuries
- **Contaminated dressings and swabs:** Plastic bags or containers; handle with gloves
- **Pathological waste (tissues, organs, blood):** Immediate incineration or burial in designated pit
- **Liquid waste (blood, body fluids):** Disinfection (bleach treatment) before disposal

### Safe Disposal Methods

**Incineration (if facility available):**
- Use high-temperature burn (800–1,200°C) to destroy pathogens
- Requires dedicated medical incinerator or industrial facility (not a simple burn pit)
- Ash is safer than unburned waste but still requires careful handling

**Safe burial (when incineration unavailable):**
1. Dig a pit 1.5–2 m deep and 1 m wide, away from water sources
2. Line with plastic sheeting or clay
3. Identify the pit clearly (painted rocks, warning signs) to prevent accidental disturbance
4. Cover daily waste layer with 30 cm of soil
5. Seal and mark the pit after it is full; do not use the site for building or agriculture for at least 2 years

**Sharp segregation and management:**
- Use rigid puncture-proof containers; never reuse sharps containers
- Seal and label containers when full
- Incinerate sealed containers or bury in a secure pit
- Train all staff on needle stick protocols: immediate cleaning, first aid, and post-exposure treatment if available

**Disinfection (for non-sharps, before burial):**
- Soak contaminated materials in 0.5% bleach solution (1 part bleach to 100 parts water) for 30 minutes
- Rinse with water and allow to dry
- Place in sealed containers for burial
- Note: Disinfection is not a substitute for incineration; use only when incineration is impossible

:::warning
**Health Worker Training:** All staff handling medical waste must be trained on safe practices, including universal precautions (gloves, masks, hand hygiene), sharps safety, and vaccination status (tetanus, hepatitis B, polio if available). Needle stick injuries or exposure to blood can transmit bloodborne pathogens; establish protocols for immediate washing and post-exposure follow-up.
:::

## Seasonal Considerations

### Rainy Season

- **Challenge:** Organic waste decomposes rapidly, attracting flies and mosquitoes; waste piles may become waterlogged and smelly
- **Solution:**
  - Increase composting frequency (turn piles every 10–14 days instead of monthly)
  - Cover compost piles with tarps or roofs to prevent excess water infiltration
  - Increase collection frequency (every 1–2 days instead of 3–4) to minimize accumulation
  - Ensure drainage around collection and compost sites; dig trenches to divert water

### Dry Season

- **Challenge:** Organic waste decomposes slowly; wind can scatter light materials
- **Solution:**
  - Reduce collection frequency (weekly may suffice if decomposition is slow)
  - Water compost piles lightly to maintain 50–60% moisture
  - Use heavier mulch (leaves, soil) to cover piles and prevent wind scattering
  - Increase burn pit use if necessary (though burning is generally discouraged)

### Cold Season (Temperate Regions)

- **Challenge:** Freezing temperatures slow decomposition; material may accumulate
- **Solution:**
  - Stockpile dry organic waste in fall; begin composting in spring
  - Increase compost pile size to retain heat
  - Cover piles with insulating material (straw, leaves) to reduce heat loss
  - Plan storage areas for inorganic recyclables that cannot be processed during winter

## Community Buy-In and Behavior Change

Successful waste management depends on sustained community participation. Technical infrastructure alone is insufficient if residents do not cooperate.

### Education and Awareness

- **Health messaging:** Explain the link between waste management and disease (flies, rodents, water contamination)
- **Visual teaching:** Use pictures or demonstrations to show disease transmission; appeal to desire to protect children
- **Community meetings:** Hold regular forums (monthly or quarterly) to discuss waste challenges and solutions
- **School education:** Include waste management in school curriculum; train children as advocates in their families

### Community Organization

- **Waste management committee:** Establish a group of 5–10 residents responsible for planning and overseeing operations
- **Roles and responsibilities:** Clearly define who collects waste, manages facilities, sorts materials, and handles hazardous waste
- **Accountability:** Post responsibility assignments publicly; rotate duties regularly to prevent burnout

### Incentives and Recognition

- **Financial:** Cash payments for waste collection or recycling work; profits from sold recyclables
- **Non-financial:** Public recognition, community events celebrating waste reduction, preferential access to compost or recycled materials
- **Tangible benefits:** Distribute finished compost free to households that participate in waste separation
- **Symbolic:** Create badges or markers for high-participation households; celebrate achievements

### Troubleshooting Resistance

| Challenge | Root Cause | Solution |
|-----------|-----------|----------|
| Low participation in waste collection | Perceived unfairness; burden on poorest families | Rotate duties; provide cash-for-work; ensure collection points are accessible |
| Mixing of waste streams | Lack of understanding; no clear separation system | Simplified pictorial instructions; frequent reminders; community education |
| Collection workers absent or unmotivated | Low pay; dangerous working conditions; lack of respect | Increase wages; provide safety gear and medical support; public recognition |
| Compost not used by community | Poor quality or perception of waste product; cultural resistance | Demonstrate compost benefits through demonstration gardens; distribute to respected community members first |
| Recycling workshop fails | No market for products; poor material quality | Market research before launch; improve sorting and quality control; seek external buyers (schools, organizations) |

## Measuring Success

Track these indicators to assess program effectiveness:

- **Waste reduction:** Measure total waste generated over time (target: 20–30% reduction through recycling and composting)
- **Participation rate:** % of households regularly separating waste (target: >80%)
- **Compost production:** Tons of compost produced and distributed (track quantity and community uptake)
- **Health outcomes:** Monitor disease incidence (diarrhea, dysentery) in participating communities; expect 20–30% reduction with improved waste management
- **Community employment:** Number of jobs created through collection, recycling, and composting (document jobs and income levels)
- **Cost recovery:** If collecting fees for waste services, track revenue and operating costs; aim for financial sustainability

## Conclusion

Community waste management is a complex undertaking requiring infrastructure, trained personnel, community engagement, and sustained effort. By implementing a comprehensive system—collection, sorting, composting, recycling, and safe disposal of hazardous materials—communities can improve sanitation, reduce disease, create employment, and build toward environmental sustainability. Success depends equally on technical choices and community ownership; involve residents from the start, ensure transparency, and celebrate progress together.
