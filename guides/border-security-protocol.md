## Overview: Gatekeeping & Security

A settlement's borders are its first line of defense. Controlling who enters and exits prevents:
- **Infiltration by enemies:** Spies, scouts, saboteurs
- **Disease vectors:** Plague, epidemic, contaminated food or water
- **Theft and crime:** Unvetted strangers may commit theft or violence
- **Economic leakage:** Smuggling prevents taxation and fair trade
- **Strategic surprise:** Knowing who approaches gives warning time

Yet complete isolation is impossible and economically disastrous. Communities must trade, accept legitimate refugees, and maintain diplomatic relations. Border security balances openness and safety.

This guide covers:
1. **Checkpoint design** (physical infrastructure)
2. **Identification systems** (verifying identity)
3. **Quarantine procedures** (disease prevention)
4. **Integration of refugees** (including outsiders safely)
5. **Caravan inspection** (trade security)
6. **First contact protocols** (diplomatic safety)

<section id="checkpoint-design">

## Checkpoint Design & Placement

Physical checkpoint design either facilitates security or creates problems.

### Location Selection

- **Strategic chokepoint:** Place checkpoints where ALL foot/cart traffic must pass (roads, bridges, gates)
- **Multiple approaches:** If checkpoints are easy to bypass, people bypass them. Barriers should force convergence
- **Avoid entrapment:** Don't design checkpoints where crowds back up uncontrollably (stampede risk)
- **Defensible position:** Checkpoint guards should have elevated/protected positions

### Physical Layout

**Components:**

1. **Outer barrier:** Fence, wall, or ditch forcing traffic to funnel to gate
2. **Queue area:** Room for 20-50 people to wait without congestion
3. **Inspection station:** Small structure (4×6 ft) where guards check people/cargo
4. **Search area:** Room to unpack and inspect carts/packs
5. **Guard positions:** Elevated perches or positions where guards see approaching crowds
6. **Secondary holding:** Enclosed area for quarantine or detainees (if needed)

**Example Layout:**

```
[Main Gate/Opening] (8-12 ft wide)
      ↓
[Queue Area] (50 ft long, 20 ft wide)
  ↓ ↓ ↓ (3 parallel queues to reduce wait time)
[Inspection Stations] (3 stations)
  ↓ ↓ ↓
[Search Area] (carts pulled aside for detailed inspection)
  ↓
[Allowed Through] → Into Settlement
[Held for Review] → Secondary Holding/Quarantine
```

### Defensive Features

- **Wall/barrier 12+ feet tall:** Prevents easy jumping or climbing
- **Guard towers:** Elevated positions for arrow/ranged defense
- **Gates with locks:** Can close in seconds if threat appears
- **Fall-back position:** Secondary gate or interior wall as second defense if outer is breached

### Capacity Management

Gate size determines throughput:
- A 12-foot-wide gate can process ~100 people per hour (5-10 minutes each for basic check)
- 8-foot gate: ~50-60 people per hour
- Multiple gates reduce wait time but require more guards

Plan gate width based on expected daily traffic, not peak traffic. A narrow gate creates natural bottleneck preventing sudden invasion.

## Identification Verification Systems

How do you verify someone is who they claim to be?

### ID Document Systems

**Paper Passes:**
- **Format:** Signed document (by settlement leader, elder, or recognized authority) certifying person's identity
- **Content:** Name, approximate age/appearance, settlement of origin, purpose of travel, expiration date
- **Verification:** Guard knows the seal/signature of issuing authority
- **Problem:** Easy to forge; requires literacy

**Tokens/Seals:**
- **Physical object:** Carved wooden token, stamped metal disc, or leather band with identifying mark
- **Verification:** Unique mark (personal carving, leader's seal) matches known authority
- **Problem:** Can be stolen; doesn't prove bearer is legitimate owner

**Tattoos or marks:**
- **Permanent ID:** Facial or hand marking indicating membership in trade guild, family, or community
- **Verification:** Guard recognizes mark and what it means
- **Problem:** Ethically problematic; permanent; can be faked with scars/branding
- **Historical use:** Medieval guilds used tattoos; some cultures still use marks for membership

### Oral Verification

When documents aren't available (post-collapse):

**Vouching System:**
- Stranger arrives with someone from the settlement who knows them
- Voucher asserts: "This is honest, I know them, I'll be responsible if they commit crime"
- Voucher signs/marks a record
- If the stranger commits crime, the voucher faces penalty
- Creates incentive for honest vouching

**Password/Phrase System:**
- Traders traveling between settlements exchange a password known only to settlement leadership
- "What is the word for this season?" → expected response: "Harvest" (or other agreed phrase)
- Changes monthly to prevent compromise
- Problem: Easy to forget; vulnerable to torture/beating

### Contextual Verification

Even without formal ID:

- **Knowledge of settlement:** Ask detailed questions about local geography, people, events
- **Language/accent:** Does their speech match claimed origin?
- **Clothing/gear:** Does their outfit match claimed culture/trade?
- **Social connections:** Do they know people they claim to know? Can someone verify?

A spy who infiltrates convincingly would have invested enormous effort to pass these checks. Most threats aren't that sophisticated.

:::info-box
**Historical Example:** Medieval travelers carried letters of introduction from recognized authorities. A letter from a bishop in Rome was accepted across Catholic Europe. Trading cities issued merchant licenses. These created portable identity.
:::

## Quarantine & Disease Prevention

Disease spreads through population contact. Before allowing entry during epidemic risk:

### Quarantine Procedures

1. **Isolation Area:** Separate newly arrived people from settlement for a set period (typically 2 weeks for plague-like illnesses)
2. **Observation:** Watch for fever, lesions, vomiting, or death
3. **Testing:** If available, test for infection (blood test, exposure check)
4. **Cleansing:** Require washing, provide clean clothes, isolate worn clothing
5. **Release:** After 2 weeks with no symptoms, allow entry

### Duration by Disease

| Disease | Quarantine Duration | Detection Method |
|---------|-------------------|------------------|
| Plague/Bubonic | 14-21 days | Fever, swollen nodes (buboes), death |
| Small Pox | 21 days | Fever, pustules on body |
| Cholera | 5-7 days | Severe diarrhea, dehydration |
| Influenza/Cold | 3-7 days | Cough, fever, runny nose |
| Malaria | 14 days | Intermittent fever, chills |

### Quarantine Facilities

- **Isolated buildings:** 100+ feet from settlement (upwind if possible)
- **Beds:** Crude but functional; bedding can be burned if needed
- **Water source:** Separate from main supply
- **Food supply:** Provide adequate nutrition but quarantine remains effective
- **Guard:** Ensure quarantined people don't escape
- **Staff:** Minimal, dedicated staff (not rotating through settlement)

### Death Protocol

If someone dies in quarantine:
- Isolate the body immediately
- Cremate if possible (prevents plague spread from corpse)
- Or bury deeply, far from settlement
- Burn bedding and clothing
- Deep clean the quarantine area before reuse

:::warning
**Risk Assessment:** Stringent quarantine prevents epidemics but creates friction with traders/refugees. Balance disease risk against economic/humanitarian cost. A settlement with plague kills its economy anyway.
:::

## Refugee Integration & Vetting

Refugees are vulnerable people fleeing danger. They also carry security/integration risks.

### Initial Assessment

When a refugee group arrives:

1. **Threat Assessment:**
   - Are they armed? (Militia, soldiers, bandits?)
   - Do they match reports of nearby threats?
   - Are they fleeing a real danger, or are they scouts/infiltrators?

2. **Capacity Assessment:**
   - How many people? Can we feed them?
   - Do they have skills we need? (Crafts, medicine, farming)
   - Will integration strain resources or benefit community?

3. **Integration Plan:**
   - Temporary shelter or permanent acceptance?
   - Quarantine period?
   - Job/role assignment?

### Hosting Framework

**Short-term (1-4 weeks):**
- Emergency shelter (community hall, barn)
- Basic food and water
- Medical care if needed
- Minimal vetting (basic questions, health assessment)
- Goal: Keep refugee group stable while assessing long-term options

**Medium-term (1-3 months):**
- Permanent housing assignment
- Job assignment (farming, crafts, defense)
- Integration into settlement routines
- Ongoing vetting (character, skills, trustworthiness)
- Goal: Determine if refugees will stay and contribute

**Long-term (3+ months):**
- Full community membership if vetted
- Assigned work/role
- Rights and responsibilities like locals
- Occasional loyalty testing (can they be trusted in conflict?)

### Vetting Process

**Background Checking:**
- Why did they flee? (Verify the danger they claim to be fleeing)
- Where are they from? (Verify settlement exists, matches description)
- Do they have family/connections in claimed origin? (Vouch for them?)
- Who are allies/enemies in home settlement?

**Skill Assessment:**
- What work can they do? (Farming, blacksmithing, medicine, crafts)
- Have them demonstrate skills
- Assign work that tests both ability and honesty

**Character Assessment:**
- Observe how they treat settlement people
- Do they respect property/laws?
- Do they follow instructions?
- Can they be trusted with sensitive work?

### Loyalty Testing

For refugees who might be spies or infiltrators:

- **Information separation:** Don't tell them sensitive military/trade secrets initially
- **Observation:** Watch for suspicious behavior (gathering intelligence, communicating with outsiders)
- **Trust building:** Integrate gradually; increase privileges as trust grows
- **Verification:** If they claim relatives in another settlement, verify the connection independently

### Expulsion Protocol

If a refugee proves untrustworthy or dangerous:
- Expel them immediately with minimal possessions
- Provide 1-2 days' food and water for travel
- Make clear: return and face execution
- Inform neighboring settlements of the expulsion (so they don't accept the person back as a spy)

## Caravan & Trade Inspection

Merchant caravans present security opportunities and risks.

### Pre-Clearance

Before arrival, establish what the caravan brings:

- **Declaration:** Merchants submit list of goods before arrival
- **Duty assessment:** Calculate taxes/tariffs based on goods
- **Dangerous goods:** Identify weapons, alcohol, medicine (special handling)

### Arrival Inspection

When caravan enters checkpoint:

1. **Cart inventory:** Match actual cargo to declaration
2. **Weapons check:** Identify any weapons beyond what's declared (spies, assassins might be hiding weapons)
3. **Passenger count:** Count people; if more people than declared, investigate
4. **Health check:** Any signs of illness in merchant group?
5. **Contraband search:** Look for prohibited items (narcotics, weapons being smuggled)

### Search Procedures

**Visual inspection:**
- Open carts and examine contents
- Feel inside grain sacks (weapons hidden inside)
- Check under carts for attachments
- Examine wheels/axles for hidden compartments

**People search:**
- Frisk merchants for concealed weapons
- Check for forged passes (feel paper quality, examine ink/signatures)
- Question merchants about their cargo and origin

**Time-consuming searches:**
- For high-risk caravans, conduct detailed inspection
- Unpack entire cart if suspected smuggling
- Question each merchant individually about cargo details
- Verify merchant signatures against known authorities

### Contraband Definitions

Define what's prohibited:

- **Weapons:** Swords, bows, blackpowder-blasting, siege equipment
- **Narcotics:** Opium, strong alcohol, psychoactive plants
- **Poison:** Anything marked as poison or identified as toxic
- **Forged documents:** Fake passes, false permits
- **Stolen goods:** Goods reported stolen from settlement
- **Political materials:** Writings advocating rebellion/overthrow (optional, depending on governance)

### Tariff Collection

Successful trade requires fair taxation:

- **Clear tariff rates:** Published rates for common goods (grain, metal, cloth, etc.)
- **Scale weighing:** Weigh goods on certified settlement scales
- **Payment methods:** Accept payment in coin, barter (goods), or labor credit
- **Written receipt:** Document what was paid to prevent disputes

:::info-box
**Economic Balance:** Too-high tariffs drive merchants to smuggle or use other routes. Too-low tariffs fail to fund government. Optimal rate is ~5-10% of goods value, collected openly and fairly.
:::

## Gate Watch & Standing Procedures

Effective border security requires consistent personnel and procedures.

### Guard Rotation

- **2-4 guards per gate** at all times
- **12-hour shifts:** 6 AM to 6 PM and 6 PM to 6 AM
- **Rotation every 3 days:** Prevent fatigue and complacency
- **Backup rotation:** Ready to relieve if incident occurs

### Daily Procedures

**Morning shift (6 AM - 6 PM):**
- 6 AM: Relieve night shift, check gate locks/barriers
- 6-9 AM: Peak morning traffic; all guards active
- 9 AM-6 PM: Normal traffic; 2-3 guards active, 1 on patrol/rest
- 6 PM: Log day's traffic, brief night shift

**Night shift (6 PM - 6 AM):**
- 6 PM: Relieve day shift, lock gate if security threat is high
- 6 PM-10 PM: Evening traffic; all guards active
- 10 PM-6 AM: Minimal traffic; 1 guard alert, others rest in rotation
- 6 AM: Unlock gate if closed, brief day shift

### Logging & Records

Maintain written records:
- **Traffic log:** Numbers of people/carts entering/exiting
- **Incident log:** Any suspicious activity, conflicts, warnings
- **Quarantine log:** Names of quarantined people, dates, status
- **Expulsion log:** Names of expelled people, reason, date

Review logs weekly for patterns (increase in traffic from certain direction, repeat suspicious visitors, etc.).

### Alert Procedures

If guards observe threat:

1. **Sound alarm:** Bell, horn, or distinctive sound
2. **Close gate:** Lock main gate immediately
3. **Alert settlement:** Send runner to leadership
4. **Assess threat:** Determine if armed response needed
5. **Negotiate or defend:** Based on threat assessment

## First Contact & Diplomatic Protocol

When unknown groups approach, establish safe first contact procedures.

### Initial Approach

1. **Identify intent:** Ask why they're here (trade, refuge, diplomacy, hostile?)
2. **Request leader:** Ask to speak with group's official representative
3. **Maintain distance:** Keep 30+ feet away initially (bow/arrow range reduction)
4. **Show respect:** Low-key stance, avoid appearance of hostility

### Information Gathering

Ask:
- Where are you from?
- How many people and armed with what?
- What is your purpose here?
- Do you have messages/letters from recognized groups?
- How long will you stay?

### Threat Assessment

Red flags:
- **Large armed group:** Potential raiders
- **Refusal to identify:** Suspicious
- **Contradictory stories:** Different group members give different accounts
- **Scoutlike behavior:** Asking specific questions about settlement defenses, population, resources

Green flags:
- **Clear purpose and credentials:** "We're traders from [settlement], carrying [goods], seeking trade with your community"
- **Willing communication:** Answer questions openly
- **Family/children in group:** Suggests peaceful intent (though not definitive)
- **Known vouchers:** Group includes people settlement knows

### Diplomatic Reception

If group appears peaceful:

1. **Guest quarters:** Provide shelter separate from settlement initially
2. **Leader meeting:** Your settlement leader meets with their leader (formal negotiation)
3. **Trade negotiation:** If traders, agree on goods and prices
4. **Integration timeline:** If refugees, assess how long they'll stay
5. **Departure preparation:** Arrange onward travel

### Hostile Contact

If group appears hostile:

1. **Full alert:** Ring alarm, mobilize defense
2. **Gate closure:** Lock gate; do not allow entry
3. **Defensive positions:** Guards take arrow/weapon positions
4. **Negotiation attempt:** Offer them passage if they continue onward (avoid violence if possible)
5. **Defense if attacked:** Only use force if group attacks first

:::warning
**Cultural Sensitivity:** Behavior that seems hostile (large armed group, limited communication, strange customs) may be normal for that culture. Don't overreact to misunderstandings. Attempt negotiation before violence.
:::

## Border Coordination with Neighbors

A single settlement's borders are only as secure as the surrounding region's.

### Mutual Intelligence

Establish communication with neighboring settlements:

- **Regular patrols:** Share information about travelers, suspicious groups, refugees
- **Early warning system:** If a raider band is heading south, northern settlements warn southern ones
- **Trader networks:** Merchants report news and unusual activity
- **Formal meetings:** Quarterly gatherings of settlement leaders to share intelligence

### Border Agreements

Formalize relationships:

- **Mutual defense:** If one settlement is attacked, neighbors provide aid
- **Refugee policy:** Agreed standards for accepting/rejecting refugees
- **Trade routes:** Establish safe corridors; agree not to tax/intercept each other's merchants
- **Communication:** Establish runners/messengers between settlements

### Joint Border Patrols

For major trade routes:

- **Rotating patrols:** Settlements take turns patrolling shared border areas
- **Mounted scouts:** Small groups on horses (if available) cover larger areas
- **Regular reports:** Patrols report findings to all participating settlements

- [ ] Locate checkpoint at natural chokepoint (road, bridge, gate)
- [ ] Design funnel layout forcing single-file passage
- [ ] Build inspection station with search area
- [ ] Establish holding area for quarantine
- [ ] Create guard positions with visibility of approaches

**ID System:**
- [ ] Choose ID method (written pass, token, vocal verification)
- [ ] Establish issuing authority and verification method
- [ ] Create backup procedures if ID is lost/damaged
- [ ] Train all guards on verification procedures

**Quarantine Procedures:**
- [ ] Establish separate quarantine area (100+ feet from settlement)
- [ ] Set quarantine duration by disease type
- [ ] Create protocol for illness/death in quarantine
- [ ] Maintain quarantine supply (food, water, bedding)

**Refugee Integration:**
- [ ] Establish vetting timeline (short/medium/long-term)
- [ ] Create background verification process
