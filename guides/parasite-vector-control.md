If the complaint is mosquito bites, ticks on skin or clothing, lice/fleas, or reducing disease-vector risk around sleeping areas, water, or travel, this is the right guide. If the main problem is bugs already in the house, pantry/storage pests, or cleanup after an infestation, use [pest-control](./pest-control.md) instead.

## Overview: Vector-Borne and Parasitic Disease Control

<section id="overview">

Use this guide when the complaint is mosquito bites, tick exposure, lice/flea risk, or other disease-vector concerns rather than cleanup of a household infestation. Parasitic and vector-borne diseases cause significant morbidity and mortality in low-connectivity environments where medical resources are scarce, and illnesses like malaria, dengue, Lyme disease, schistosomiasis, and intestinal parasites thrive where sanitation is compromised and disease vectors are not controlled.

This guide provides practical, low-tech methods to:
- **Reduce vector populations** through habitat modification, biological control, and personal protection
- **Interrupt parasite life cycles** via environmental and behavioral measures
- **Recognize disease symptoms** and implement supportive care protocols
- **Establish community surveillance** to track disease incidence and enable early response
- **Produce botanical insecticides** from locally available plants

The key principle: most vector-borne and parasitic diseases require specific conditions to complete their life cycles. Disrupting even one stage can prevent transmission and break the disease cycle.

</section>

## Mosquito Biology and Management

<section id="mosquito-management">

### Mosquito Life Cycle and Vulnerability Points

All mosquitoes undergo complete metamorphosis: egg → larva → pupa → adult. Each stage has distinct habitat requirements and vulnerabilities.

**Egg stage (1-3 days):**
- *Aedes and Anopheles:* Laid individually on water surface or damp soil; hatch when submerged
- *Culex:* Laid in rafts of 100-300 eggs; float on water surface
- **Vulnerability:** Eggs can desiccate; prevent re-wetting of breeding sites

**Larval stage (7-10 days):**
- Aquatic; develop in standing water
- Feed on algae, bacteria, organic detritus
- Must surface to breathe (spiracle on abdomen)
- **Vulnerability:** Require standing water; easily disrupted by water draining, oiling, or toxicants

**Pupal stage (1-4 days):**
- Non-feeding; found in water
- Breathe through trumpets on thorax
- **Vulnerability:** Less vulnerable to toxicants (don't feed); but water draining kills them

**Adult stage (2-30+ days):**
- Terrestrial; seek hosts for blood meal
- Females require blood protein to produce eggs
- Males feed only on plant nectar (not disease vectors)
- **Vulnerability:** Vulnerable to bed nets, screening, repellents, and environmental management

### Breeding Site Elimination (Habitat Reduction)

The most sustainable control: eliminate breeding habitat.

**Standing water sources to eliminate:**
- Puddles and low-lying areas: Fill with soil or sand, or create drainage channels
- Flower pots, buckets, barrels, rain gutters: Store inverted or covered; empty after rain
- Tree holes: Fill with sand or concrete; keep covered
- Swampy areas: Drain or dike to maintain flowing water (mosquitoes breed in stagnant only)
- Unused wells: Cover with netting; maintain water level (mosquitoes breed in standing water, not flowing)
- Discarded containers: Remove all vessels that collect water
- Clogged drains: Clear ditches and gutters

**Inspection schedule:** Weekly during wet season; after every rainfall.

**Community-scale habitat management:**
- Grade land for drainage (slight slope away from dwellings)
- Maintain vegetation margins (mowing reduces resting habitat)
- Clear water channels to maintain flow
- Stock ponds with fish (see biological control below)

:::warning
**Inspect water containers used for drinking and cooking.** Mosquito larvae are tiny (1-2 mm); visible eggs appear as dark specks or rafts. Filter drinking water through cloth or screen before use.
:::

### Larvicides: Bti and Oil Films

**Bti (Bacillus thuringiensis israelensis):**

Soil bacterium that kills mosquito larvae; safe for humans and aquatic life.

- **Product:** Dried spores sold as powder or granules (e.g., VectoBac, Bti briquettes)
- **Application rate:** Follow package; typically 1-2 pints per 100 sq ft of water surface
- **Effectiveness:** Kills larvae within 24-48 hours; lasts 7-14 days (degrades by UV)
- **Cost:** Moderate; available in agriculture supply stores or online
- **How it works:** Larvae ingest spores; spores germinate in gut and produce toxins
- **Reapplication:** Every 1-2 weeks during mosquito season (especially after rainfall adds fresh water)

**Application method:**
1. Drain and refill water containers weekly (disrupts larval development)
2. If containers cannot be drained (livestock water, fountains), apply Bti granules per package instructions
3. Sprinkle directly on water surface
4. Monitor for larvae; reapply after 2 weeks or after new water added

**Oil films:**

Thin oil layer on water surface suffocates larvae and pupae (blocks spiracles).

- **Oil source:** Mineral oil, vegetable oil, or kerosene (vegetable oil preferred for food/livestock water)
- **Application:** Light spray or drip to cover 80-90% of water surface
- **Amount:** ~1 tbsp per 10 sq ft of water surface
- **Effectiveness:** Kills larvae/pupae within 24 hours; must cover entire surface
- **Duration:** Lasts 2-4 weeks; breaks up with rain/wind; reapply as needed
- **Safety:** Oil in animal drinking water is acceptable (vegetable oil); avoid kerosene for livestock

**Application protocol:**
1. Evening application (reduces evaporation overnight)
2. Use oil-soaked cloth strips or hand-operated sprayer to apply thin, even coat
3. Inspect weekly; reapply if coverage drops below 80%
4. For large ponds: Apply along edges and high-traffic feeding areas first

### Bed Nets and Screening

**Bed nets (untreated):**

Physical barrier between sleeping person and mosquitoes; especially effective against *Anopheles* (night feeders).

- **Construction:** Lightweight fabric (cotton or nylon) in rectangular envelope suspended above bed
- **Mesh size:** 156 holes per inch (1.2 mm mesh); small enough to block mosquitoes
- **Deployment:** Tuck net edges under mattress; ensure no gaps or holes
- **Maintenance:** Inspect weekly; repair holes with needle and thread
- **Lifespan:** 2-3 years with care

**Untreated net effectiveness:** 50-60% reduction in mosquito contacts (better than nothing; permethrin-treated nets ~70% effective but require periodic re-treatment).

**Building screening for windows and doors:**

- **Material:** Aluminum or fiberglass screening (1.2 mm mesh, 18-20 gauge)
- **Frame:** Wood or plastic extrusion; hinged to allow opening/closing
- **Installation:** Seal gaps around frame with caulk or foam tape; ensure overlap at door
- **Maintenance:** Repair holes promptly; clean monthly to prevent blockage
- **Effectiveness:** 85-90% reduction in indoor mosquito entry

**Permethrin treatment (optional enhancement):**

Synthetic pyrethroid insecticide; kills/repels insects on contact.

- **Application to nets:** 0.5 g permethrin per kg of fabric (pre-treated nets available; OR treat at home)
- **Home treatment method:**
  1. Soak net in permethrin solution (0.5% concentration)
  2. Rinse lightly; hang to dry (2-4 hours)
  3. Effectiveness lasts 6-12 months (gradually decreases)
- **Application to screening:** Spray frame and netting lightly (dry within 30 min)
- **Safety:** Permethrin is toxic to aquatic life; avoid runoff to water sources; keep children away during treatment

### Biological Control: Predatory Fish and Insects

**Gambusia fish (*Gambusia affinis*):**

Mosquito fish; eat mosquito larvae voraciously.

- **Feeding rate:** One fish consumes 100+ mosquito larvae per day
- **Size at maturity:** 1-3 inches; adapt to various water temperatures
- **Stocking:** 1-2 fish per 100 sq ft of water surface; require 6-12 inches depth minimum
- **Habitat:** Tolerate poor water quality; thrive in ponds, ditches, slow-moving streams
- **Lifespan:** 3-5 years
- **Source:** Aquaculture suppliers; may be available locally in warm regions
- **Deployment:**
  1. Obtain fingerlings (juvenile fish) or adults
  2. Acclimate to pond water temperature (1-2 hours in bucket floated on water surface)
  3. Release into target water body
  4. Monitor population; restock if fish disappear (predation by wading birds, drought)

**Dragonfly nymphs:**

Aquatic predators; eat mosquito larvae and pupae.

- **Habitat:** Ponds, ditches, slow-moving streams with vegetation
- **Enhancement:** Plant emergent vegetation (cattails, water lilies) to provide habitat
- **Effectiveness:** Moderate (less efficient than Gambusia, but establish naturally)
- **Advantage:** Indicator of ecosystem health; signal functioning pond

**Copepods (*Mesocyclops* spp.):**

Tiny crustaceans; eat mosquito larvae and *Copepoda* parasites (Guinea worm vectors).

- **Application:** Add to water storage containers or cisterns
- **Stocking rate:** ~10 copepods per liter of water
- **Effectiveness:** Very high (>70% larval predation); persist in stored water
- **Source:** Research institutions, public health agencies; can be cultured locally
- **Culture method (basic):**
  1. Obtain copepod starter culture
  2. Maintain in clean water with algae food (green water or algae powder)
  3. Transfer small populations to drinking water containers
  4. Copepods survive 6-8 weeks in stored water; refresh as needed

### Monitoring Mosquito Populations

**Larval surveys (weekly):**

1. Select 10 potential breeding sites (water containers, puddles, ditches)
2. Use white cup or net; dip 5 times in each site, collecting water sample
3. Count larvae and pupae visible; note species if identifiable
4. Record date, location, count
5. If >5 larvae found, increase control efforts (additional Bti, oil, or drainage)

**Adult surveys (index trap):**

1. Hang white or light-colored cloth in sheltered area (porch, shed) away from light
2. At dusk (peak feeding time), count mosquitoes that land on cloth
3. Record species if identifiable (red-brown = *Aedes*; spotted = *Anopheles*)
4. Compare counts week-to-week; spikes indicate breeding success, declining counts indicate control success

**Action threshold:**
- Larval surveys: >10% of sites positive → escalate control
- Adult surveys: >50 landings per night → escalate control

## Tick Biology and Management

<section id="tick-management">

### Tick Life Cycle and Disease Transmission

Hard ticks (*Ixodes*, *Dermacentor*, *Amblyomma*) require three hosts, taking 2-3 years to complete life cycle.

**Life stages:**
1. **Eggs (spring):** 500-3000 eggs laid in soil; hatch in 3-4 weeks
2. **Larvae (summer):** Seek small hosts (rodents, birds); feed 3-10 days; drop off to molt
3. **Nymphs (fall/spring):** Seek larger hosts (deer, raccoons); feed 3-5 days; drop off to molt
4. **Adults (fall/winter):** Seek large hosts (deer, humans); feed 5-7 days

**Disease transmission:**
- Pathogens acquired by tick during feeding on infected host
- Pathogens maintained through molting (transstadial transmission)
- Risk peaks during spring-summer (nymph peak feeding)
- Transmission occurs only after tick has fed for 24-48 hours (allows pathogen entry into host tissues)

**Diseases of concern:**
- **Lyme disease** (*Borrelia burgdorferi*) — spirochete; causes fever, rash, joint pain, neurological effects
- **Rocky Mountain spotted fever** (*Rickettsia rickettsii*) — severe fever, rash; high mortality if untreated
- **Babesiosis** (*Babesia* parasites) — malaria-like illness; hemolysis
- **Anaplasmosis** (*Anaplasma* — fever, malaise, thrombocytopenia

### Habitat Modification

**Reduce tick habitat:**
- **Mow grass to 2-3 inches:** Ticks cannot establish in short grass; weekly mowing critical
- **Remove leaf litter:** Rake and dispose of fallen leaves where ticks overwinter
- **Create buffer zones:** 10-15 foot mowed strip between lawn and forest edge
- **Remove brush piles:** Decomposing wood harbors tick hosts (rodents)
- **Stack firewood 5+ feet from dwelling:** Reduces rodent harborage near humans

**Manage tick hosts (wildlife):**
- **Deer removal/exclusion:** Primary tick host; hunting or deer exclusion fencing reduces tick populations
- **Rodent control:** Reduce by removing food sources (keep garbage secure); do not poison (poisoned rodents poison predators)
- **Landscaping:** Remove acorns, nuts that attract deer and rodents

### Personal Protective Measures

**Clothing:**
- Light-colored long sleeves and long pants (easier to spot ticks)
- Tuck pants into socks (prevents ticks from crawling up legs)
- Wear boots rather than sandals

**Permethrin treatment of clothing:**
- Spray clothing, gear, boots with 0.5% permethrin solution
- Allow to dry (2-4 hours)
- Effectiveness lasts through ~6 washings
- Apply before entering tick habitat; reapply before season starts
- Do NOT apply to skin; permethrin is safe on fabric but toxic if ingested

**Tick checks (essential):**
- Inspect body daily (especially hairline, armpits, groin, behind ears)
- Use mirror to check back and scalp
- Immediately upon returning from tick habitat

### Tick Removal

**Timing:** Remove immediately; reduced transmission risk if removed within 24 hours (early transmission ~0%; risk increases after 24-48 hours feeding).

**Correct removal method:**

:::warning
**DO NOT:**
- Squeeze tick (releases pathogen into host)
- Apply heat or petroleum jelly (causes tick to regurgitate pathogens)
- Twist tick off (leaves mouthparts in skin; risk of infection)
:::

**Proper technique:**
1. Use fine-tipped tweezers or tick hook
2. Grasp tick as close to skin as possible (near mouthparts)
3. Pull steadily and slowly perpendicular to skin (~10 seconds)
4. Place removed tick in sealed container with a damp paper towel (preserve for identification if illness develops)
5. Clean skin with soap and water or antiseptic
6. Do NOT crush tick with bare hands; wear gloves or use tweezers

**After removal:**
- Save tick in sealed container labeled with date and location
- Monitor health for 2-4 weeks for symptoms
- If fever, rash, or joint pain develops, seek medical care and provide tick to healthcare provider

### Tick-Borne Illness Supportive Care

**Early Lyme disease (erythema migrans rash within 3 weeks):**
- Fever, fatigue, muscle aches, headache
- Antibiotic treatment (if available): Doxycycline 100 mg 2x daily for 14-21 days
- Supportive: Rest, fluids, antipyretics (acetaminophen/ibuprofen)

**Rocky Mountain spotted fever (within 5-10 days):**
- High fever, severe headache, petechial rash (begins on wrists/ankles, spreads centrally)
- MEDICAL EMERGENCY if untreated; mortality >20% untreated
- Antibiotic treatment (if available): Doxycycline 100 mg 2x daily for 7 days
- Supportive: Aggressive fever management (cool compresses, fluids, antipyretics)

## Intestinal Parasite Life Cycles and Interruption

<section id="intestinal-parasites">

### Roundworm (*Ascaris lumbricoides*)

**Life cycle:**

<ol class="lifecycle-steps">
<li>Eggs in human feces reach soil</li>
<li>Eggs embryonate in soil (warm, moist); become infective within 2-4 weeks</li>
<li>Humans ingest embryonated eggs (contaminated food/water, contaminated hands)</li>
<li>Eggs hatch in small intestine; larvae penetrate intestinal wall, enter bloodstream</li>
<li>Larvae migrate to lungs; ascend trachea; are swallowed; return to intestine</li>
<li>Adult worms develop in small intestine; produce 200,000+ eggs daily in feces</li>
<li>Worms live 12-18 months; exit body in feces</li>
</ol>

**Interruption strategies:**

- **Sanitation:** Human feces must not contaminate soil/water; use latrines 50+ feet from water sources and downhill
- **Hand hygiene:** Wash hands with soap and water before eating and after defecating
- **Water treatment:** Boil (5 minutes) or filter drinking water (eggs are 60-90 μm; filter to <10 μm)
- **Food hygiene:** Peel or cook fresh vegetables (grown in contaminated soil)
- **Deworming:** Anthelmintic (mebendazole 100 mg 2x daily for 3 days, if available)

**Symptoms:** Abdominal pain, malnutrition, intestinal obstruction (large worm burdens).

### Hookworm (*Necator americanus, Ancylostoma duodenale*)

**Life cycle:**

<ol class="lifecycle-steps">
<li>Eggs in feces reach warm soil</li>
<li>Eggs hatch within 1-2 days; larvae develop to infective stage in 5-10 days</li>
<li>Humans contact infective larvae (bare skin on contaminated soil)</li>
<li>Larvae penetrate skin (itch/rash); enter bloodstream</li>
<li>Larvae migrate through lungs; ascend trachea; are swallowed</li>
<li>Attach to small intestine; feed on blood; produce eggs within 2-3 weeks</li>
<li>Chronic infection: Heavy worm burden causes anemia, protein loss, stunted growth</li>
</ol>

**Interruption strategies:**

- **Sanitation:** Pit latrines; human feces must not contact soil
- **Footwear:** Wear boots/shoes to prevent skin contact with contaminated soil
- **Hand hygiene:** Wash hands after contact with soil
- **Deworming:** Mebendazole 100 mg 2x daily for 3 days (if available)

**Symptoms:** Severe anemia (due to chronic blood loss), protein malnutrition, exercise intolerance, delayed growth in children.

### Tapeworm (*Taenia solium* — pork; *Taenia saginata* — beef)

**Life cycle:**

<ol class="lifecycle-steps">
<li>Eggs/proglottid segments in human feces reach soil/pasture</li>
<li>Livestock (pigs, cattle) ingest eggs while grazing</li>
<li>Eggs hatch; larvae penetrate intestine; lodge in muscle tissue as cysts</li>
<li>Humans consume undercooked meat (rare/raw) containing cysts</li>
<li>Cysts develop into adult tapeworm in human small intestine</li>
<li>Tapeworm produces proglottids (segments); releases 100,000+ eggs in feces monthly</li>
<li>Infection persists years if untreated</li>
</ol>

**Interruption strategies:**

- **Food safety:** Cook meat to 70°C (158°F) internal temperature; avoid raw/undercooked meat
- **Sanitation:** Prevent human feces from contaminating livestock pastures
- **Deworming:** Praziquantel 5-10 mg/kg single dose OR niclosamide 2 g single dose (if available)

**Symptoms:** Anemia, malnutrition, abdominal pain, diarrhea; visible proglottid segments in stool.

**Prevention of neurocysticercosis** (*Taenia solium* only): Ingestion of human feces containing *T. solium* eggs → cysts lodge in brain → seizures, edema. Prevention: strict sanitation; deworming infected individuals.

### Pinworm (*Enterobius vermicularis*)

**Life cycle:**

<ol class="lifecycle-steps">
<li>Adult worms live in colon; females migrate to anus at night to deposit eggs</li>
<li>Eggs become infective within 4-6 hours on skin/fomites</li>
<li>Humans ingest eggs (contaminated hands, bedding, fomites)</li>
<li>Larvae develop to adults in cecum within 2-4 weeks</li>
<li>Intense itching at anus causes scratching → eggs under fingernails → fecal-oral spread</li>
</ol>

**Interruption strategies:**

- **Hand hygiene:** Wash hands immediately after defecating and before eating; keep fingernails short
- **Bedding:** Wash bedding in hot water daily during infection
- **Deworming:** Mebendazole 100 mg single dose, repeat after 2 weeks (if available)
- **Community treatment:** All household members should be treated simultaneously

**Symptoms:** Intense itching around anus (especially at night); sleep disruption; rarely, secondary infections from scratching.

### Waterborne Parasites

**Giardia (beaver fever):**
- Source: Contaminated water (springs, streams)
- Cysts are acid-resistant; survive stomach acid
- Symptoms: Severe diarrhea, bloating, malabsorption
- Prevention: Boil (1 minute) or filter (1 μm) drinking water
- Treatment: Metronidazole 250 mg 3x daily for 5-7 days (if available)

**Cryptosporidium:**
- Source: Contaminated water (resistant to chlorine)
- Symptoms: Chronic watery diarrhea; severe in immunocompromised
- Prevention: Boil or filter (<1 μm) drinking water
- Treatment: Supportive (oral rehydration); antiparasitic drugs minimally effective

**Guinea worm (*Dracunculus medinensis*):**
- Source: Drinking water containing infected copepods
- Life cycle: Infected person enters water → copepods consumed → larvae develop in tissue over 12 months → emerge through blister
- Prevention: Filter (100 μm mesh) drinking water; use copepods (see mosquito section) in water storage
- Treatment: Supportive (pain management); worm extraction by rolling on stick (traditional method)

## Biological Control Agents

<section id="biological-control">

### Parasitoid Wasps

Small wasps that parasitize pest insects; lay eggs inside host, developing larvae consume host from inside.

**Common parasitoids:**
- **Braconid wasps:** Parasitize caterpillars, aphids, beetles
- **Ichneumon wasps:** Parasitize butterfly/moth pupae
- **Trichogrammatid wasps:** Parasitize insect eggs

**Enhancement:**
- Plant flowering plants near crops (wasps feed on nectar/pollen)
- Avoid broad-spectrum insecticides (kill wasps)
- Purchase cultured parasitoid pupae from suppliers; release into field

**Effectiveness:** 30-70% parasitism of target pest under good conditions.

### Entomopathogenic Fungi

Fungi that infect insect pests; spores germinate on insect cuticle, grow into body, kill host.

**Common species:**
- *Beauveria bassiana:* Infects aphids, beetles, flies
- *Metarhizium anisopliae:* Infects grasshoppers, crickets, beetles
- *Isaria fumosorosea:* Infects whiteflies, thrips

**Application:**
- Mix spores with water (suspension); spray on crops
- Effective in high-humidity conditions (spores require moisture to germinate)
- Purchased as powder or liquid formulation

**Effectiveness:** 50-80% mortality within 7-14 days (slower than chemical insecticides but sustainable).

### Predatory Insects

**Ladybug beetles (*Coccinella* spp.):**
- Consume 50-60 aphids/day per beetle
- Larvae are voracious predators (100+ aphids before pupation)
- Enhancement: Plant pollen/nectar flowers (dill, fennel, yarrow)
- Purchase and release: 100-1000 beetles per acre; effective when temperatures >60°F

**Lacewing (*Chrysopidae*):**
- Larvae consume 100+ aphids, scales, mites before pupation
- Adults feed on nectar/pollen
- Enhancement: Plant flowering plants in field margins
- Cultured larvae available for release

**Ground beetles (*Carabidae*):**
- Nocturnal predators of slug, snails, soil-dwelling insects
- Enhance by providing debris for shelter (mulch, leaf litter)

</section>

## Integrated Vector Management Strategy

<section id="ivm">

**IVM combines multiple, complementary control layers:**

1. **Environmental:** Reduce breeding habitat (drainage, removal of standing water, vegetation management)
2. **Biological:** Release predatory fish, parasitoid wasps, entomopathogenic fungi
3. **Chemical (if needed):** Apply Bti, botanical insecticides, permethrin to clothing/nets
4. **Personal protection:** Bed nets, screening, protective clothing, repellents, tick checks
5. **Community:** Surveillance, reporting, coordination of control efforts, quarantine/isolation of ill persons

**Implementation sequence:**

- **Phase 1 (Weeks 1-2):** Habitat elimination (drain standing water, mow grass, remove leaf litter)
- **Phase 2 (Weeks 2-4):** Deploy biological controls (Gambusia, Bti, parasitoid wasps)
- **Phase 3 (Ongoing):** Maintain habitat reduction; monitor populations; scale up chemical controls if needed
- **Phase 4 (Community):** Coordinate neighborhood efforts; share resources; report outbreaks

**Expected timeline to impact:**
- Mosquito reduction: 3-6 weeks (depends on breeding site recovery)
- Tick reduction: 4-8 weeks (longer due to life cycle)
- Parasite reduction: 4-12 weeks (depends on sanitation implementation)

</section>

## Community Surveillance and Monitoring

<section id="surveillance">

### Disease Incidence Tracking

**Passive surveillance:** Healthcare worker or volunteer records suspected cases.

**Case definition example (malaria):**
- Fever (>37.5°C) + headache/chills/sweats within past 2 weeks

**Data collection:**
- Name, age, address, symptoms, onset date, location (if traveled)
- Test result (if available: blood smear, RDT)
- Treatment given, outcome

**Reporting:**
- Weekly summary to district health official
- Identify clusters (multiple cases in same area = outbreak)
- Alert if cluster detected (triggers response)

### Vector Surveillance

**Larval surveillance:** Dip nets, white cups; inspect standing water (see Mosquito section)

**Adult surveillance:** Light traps or CDC bottle traps (attract resting mosquitoes, trap for count)

**Tick surveillance:** Walk through vegetation with white cloth dragged; ticks adhere to cloth; count by species

**Reporting format:**
- Date, location, species (if identifiable), count
- Trend (increasing, stable, decreasing)
- Action triggered (escalate control if populations spike)

</section>

## Botanical Insecticide Production

<section id="botanical-insecticides">

### Pyrethrum (Chrysanthemum-Derived)

**Source:** Flowers of *Chrysanthemum cinerariifolium* or *Tanacetum parthenium* (feverfew)

**Active compounds:** Pyrethrins (natural insecticides)

**Extraction method:**
1. Dry flower heads (remove stems)
2. Pound dried flowers into powder
3. Mix powder with water (1 part powder : 5 parts water)
4. Let sit 24 hours (pyrethrins dissolve)
5. Strain; apply as spray
6. Reapply after rain or every 7-10 days

**Effectiveness:** 60-80% knockdown of flying insects (kills on contact); short persistence (2-3 days)

**Safety:** Low mammalian toxicity; breaks down quickly in sunlight

### Neem Oil (*Azadirachta indica*)

**Source:** Seeds of neem tree; cold-pressed or solvent-extracted

**Active compound:** Azadirachtin (growth regulator; disrupts insect hormones)

**Preparation:**
- Purchase neem oil (10-15% azadirachtin concentration)
- Dilute: 1-2% solution (1-2 tbsp neem oil per gallon water)
- Spray on plant foliage, focus on undersides of leaves

**Effectiveness:** 70-85% reduction in feeding; slower acting than pyrethrum (3-5 days); persistent (7-10 days)

**Application timing:** Early morning or late evening (reduces beneficial insect exposure); avoid flowering times

**Safety:** Mild skin irritant; avoid inhaling spray mist

### Citronella

**Source:** Leaves and stems of *Cymbopogon nardus* (citronella grass)

**Preparation:**
1. Harvest leaves; dry
2. Steep in water (1 part dried leaves : 5 parts water) for 24 hours
3. Strain; bottle

**Effectiveness:** Repellent (not insecticide); prevents mosquito feeding for 30-120 minutes

**Application:** Topical on skin (rub oil on arms, legs, neck); avoid eyes

**Production of citronella oil:**
- Distill fresh leaves (requires still or improvisational steam distillation)
- Labor-intensive; yields ~0.5% oil by weight

### Garlic and Hot Pepper Extract

**Garlic (*Allium sativum*):**
- Contains sulfur compounds (repellent)
- Blend 1 bulb + 1 liter water; strain
- Spray on plants; repels aphids, beetles, mites

**Hot pepper (*Capsicum*):**
- Contains capsaicin (irritant to insects)
- Blend 10-20 peppers + 1 liter water; strain
- Spray on plants; irritates feeding insects
- Can also be mixed with soap to improve adhesion (few drops liquid soap per liter)

**Effectiveness:** Moderate repellent; requires frequent reapplication (3-5 days)

</section>

## Conclusion

Vector-borne and parasitic diseases thrive when sanitation is poor, medical care is inaccessible, and vector populations are uncontrolled. Low-tech interventions—habitat elimination, biological control, personal protection, and community coordination—can substantially reduce disease burden and sustain health in off-grid communities. The key is persistence: parasites and vectors bounce back if control lapses, requiring year-round commitment to habitat management and surveillance.
