## Overview

The wheelwright and cartwright are complementary craftspeople. The wheelwright builds wheels; the cartwright builds the wagon body and assembles the whole machine. A medieval or pre-industrial cart or wagon is a marvel of engineered wood—the wheel especially is a precise, load-bearing structure that must resist failure under enormous stress. A single broken wheel can strand a merchant's goods or a farmer's harvest.

The wheel is the foundation of land transportation. A wooden wheel consists of several components working in concert: the hub (the center), spokes (radial members), the felloe (rim pieces), and the iron tire (binding hoop). Each component must be precisely fitted and proportioned, and the whole wheel must be perfectly round and balanced.

This guide covers the wheelwright's craft: hub construction, spoke fitting, felloe shaping, tire shrinking, and the axle systems that support everything.

<section id="hub-construction">

## Hub Boring and Mortising

The hub is the heart of the wheel. It accepts the axle on its inner diameter and holds the spokes on its outer surface.

### Hub Material and Dimensions

**Wood:** Elm is traditional and ideal—its interlocking grain resists splitting when spoke tenons are driven in. Oak and ash are acceptable alternatives.

**Dimensions (for a cart wheel ~4–5 feet in diameter):**
- Diameter: 5–6 inches
- Length (axle bore): 4–5 inches
- Usually composed of two or three stacked pieces glued together, then bored and mortised as one unit.

**Why stack the hub?** A large, single piece of elm may not be available. Stacking allows smaller pieces to be joined into a large, monolithic hub that resists splitting during spoke insertion.

### Axle Hole Boring

The axle hole is the central bore that carries the wheel on the axle and transfers all load.

**Method:**

1. Mark the center of the hub carefully (measure diagonal corners; where they intersect is center).
2. Use an auger or bow drill to bore the axle hole from both ends, meeting in the middle.
3. Hole diameter should be slightly larger than the axle diameter (typically 1/4 inch larger). This allows the wheel to rotate freely while the axle rotates with it.
4. Smooth the hole with a reamer or sandstone to ensure even contact along the entire bore.
5. **Square vs. round hole:** Traditional carts often use a tapered or square hole with a corresponding tapered axle. This prevents axle slip during forward motion. Modern wheels use round holes and rely on wedges or clips to prevent slip.

:::info-box
A loose axle hole causes the wheel to wobble and wears rapidly. A too-tight hole generates friction and heat, which can ignite wood on long journeys. The axle hole must be precisely sized.
:::

### Spoke Mortises

Mortises are the rectangular holes cut in the hub's outer surface where spoke tenons (male ends) are inserted.

**Spoke arrangement:**

- Standard cart wheels have 8, 10, or 12 spokes radiating from the hub.
- Spokes are evenly spaced: for 8 spokes, each is 45° apart; for 10, each is 36° apart.
- Mark spoke positions on the hub's circumference using a trammel or dividing compass.

**Mortise dimensions:**

- Width: about 1 inch
- Depth (into the hub): 2–3 inches
- Length: slightly longer than the spoke tenon to allow for seasonal wood movement

**Cutting mortises:**

1. Drill a line of holes along each mortise outline (use a 3/4-inch auger).
2. Use a chisel to clean out and square the mortise walls.
3. Ensure mortise bottoms are flat and perpendicular to the hub's radial direction.
4. Mortises on opposite sides of the hub (e.g., spoke 1 and spoke 5) should align perfectly so a measurement across the hub from spoke face to spoke face is consistent—this ensures the wheel is balanced.

### Hub Strength and Reinforcement

**Seasoning:** Hub wood should be partially seasoned (semi-dry) to minimize checking (radial splitting) as it dries further. Fully green (freshly cut) hubs will split as they dry; kiln-dried hubs are brittle and prone to brittle failure.

**Reinforcement bands:** Some cartwrights drove a thin iron band around the hub's outer surface to prevent radial splitting. This band is seated in a shallow groove and helps contain cracks if they form.

## Spoke Shaping and Tenon Joinery

### Spoke Selection and Dimensions

Spokes are individual pieces of wood, typically ash or oak, that radiate from hub to felloe (rim).

**Dimensions (for a 4.5-foot wheel):**
- Length: 20–24 inches (hub center to felloe inner surface)
- Cross-section: roughly square in the hub (about 1.25 × 1.25 inches) tapering to oval near the felloe (about 1 inch × 0.75 inches)
- Grain should run lengthwise along the spoke for strength

**Spoke tapers:**
- Radially (hub to rim): the spoke is thicker near the hub where stress is greatest, thinning toward the felloe
- Tangentially (width): some wheels have spokes that are thicker along their outer edges (where rim loads push inward) and thinner along the inner edges

### Tenon Cutting

The spoke's hub end is shaped into a tenon—a rectangular protrusion that fits into the hub mortise.

**Method:**

1. Mark the tenon dimensions on the spoke's hub end:
   - Width: slightly less than mortise width (about 1 inch), allowing 1/16-inch gaps on each side for seasonal wood movement
   - Length: about 3 inches to fit fully into the hub mortise

2. Use a handsaw to cut the four faces of the tenon:
   - Saw from the shoulder (where tenon meets shaft) toward the tip
   - Cut carefully so tenon sides are parallel and perpendicular to the spoke axis

3. Chisel and plane to refine tenon fit:
   - Test fit the tenon in the hub mortise repeatedly
   - Tenon should slide in smoothly with slight friction; avoid forcing

4. The hub end of the spoke may be fitted with a slight shoulder or step. This allows the spoke to be driven firmly into place and not shift if the hub relaxes slightly.

### Spoke Curvature

Spokes are not straight—they are slightly curved to accommodate the wheel's rotation and loading pattern.

**Why curve?** When the wheel is under load, spokes on the loaded side (bottom) compress and those on the unloaded side (top) are in tension. A slight curve (usually an inward bow) improves load distribution and reduces fatigue failures.

**Curvature amount:**
- Typical: 1/4 to 1/2 inch inward curve (concave when viewed from the side) along a 20-inch spoke length
- Achieved by selecting wood with a natural curve and/or shaping with a draw knife

<section id="felloe-fitting">

## Felloe Fitting and Shaping

The felloe (or felly) is the rim of the wheel, composed of curved wooden segments that arc from spoke to spoke.

### Felloe Composition

For an 8-spoke wheel, there are typically 8 felloe segments, each spanning the arc between two adjacent spokes. For 10-spoke wheels, 10 segments, and so on.

**Dimensions:**
- Outer diameter (with spokes): 4.5–5.5 feet depending on wheel size
- Felloe width (arc direction): width = wheel circumference ÷ number of spokes. For an 8-spoke wheel, 48-inch circumference ÷ 8 = 6 inches per felloe segment
- Felloe thickness: 1.5–2.5 inches (determines wheel strength and rim contact with iron tire)

### Shaping Felloes

**Curved surface:** Each felloe is a segment of a circle matching the wheel's outer radius.

1. Use a template (a wooden arc) cut to match the wheel's outer curve.
2. Shape each felloe's outer surface with a draw knife and plane to match the template profile.
3. Test fit repeatedly—a poorly curved felloe creates a wobbling rim.

**Inner surface (spoke seating):** The inner surface must match the angle where the spoke meets the rim.

1. At each end of a felloe segment, two spokes arrive at a calculated angle (360° ÷ number of spokes).
2. Cut a mortise or notch at each end of the felloe to accept the spoke's end (the spoke tenon expands from hub mortise to rim).
3. Alternatively, the spoke end is tapered into the felloe, creating a flush joint.

**End joints:** Adjacent felloe segments are joined at their ends (where two felloes meet between two spokes).

1. These joints are typically butt joints reinforced with dowels (wooden pegs).
2. Align segments carefully so the rim remains circular and doesn't develop a flat or wavy profile.

### Felloe-to-Spoke Connection

Two methods are common:

**Mortise and tenon:** The spoke extends through the felloe rim. A rectangular mortise is cut into the felloe at each spoke position, and the spoke's end tenon is fitted into it. This is strong but complex.

**Spoke tenon into felloe:** The spoke end is simply shaped into the felloe surface with a tapered joint, then reinforced by the iron tire shrinking over the rim.

<section id="iron-tire">

## Iron Tire Shrinking and Fitting

The iron tire is a metal band that wraps around the wheel's circumference, tightening as it cools after being heated and fitted.

### Tire Fabrication

The tire is typically a flat steel strap, 1.5–2 inches wide and 1/8 to 1/4 inch thick, bent into a circle to match the wheel's outer diameter.

**Method (smith's work):**

1. Measure the wheel's outer circumference precisely.
2. The tire is cut about 1/4 inch shorter than the circumference so it contracts to fit tightly as it cools.
3. Heat the tire in a blacksmith's forge to cherry-red (about 600–700°F).
4. Using a bending fork or tire bender (a lever tool), gradually bend the tire into a circle.
5. The tire is fitted over the wheel while still hot and the gap is welded shut or overlapped and riveted.

:::warning
Hot metal tires reach temperatures that cause severe burns instantly. Wear leather gloves and apron. Keep water and sand nearby for emergencies. Never touch the tire directly; always use tongs or a lever tool.
:::

### Shrinking the Tire Onto the Wheel

This is the critical operation that tightens everything.

**Process:**

1. After the tire is welded or riveted into a circle and while it's still hot (or after reheating), the wheel is positioned vertically on the ground.
2. The tire is lifted and threaded over the spokes from above (or the wheel is lifted up through the tire if it's already on the ground).
3. As the tire cools, it contracts. The contraction is slight (about 1/4 inch on a 5-foot wheel) but tremendously strong.
4. **Cooling:** The tire should cool slowly in air. **Never plunge a hot tire-fitted wheel into water**—the thermal shock can crack wood and snap spokes.
5. The shrinking action compresses all felloe joints, tightens spoke mortises, and seats spokes firmly in the hub.

**Tire shrinkage effect:**
- The tire pulls the felloe segments and spokes tightly into a unified, rigid structure.
- Spoke mortises in the hub are compressed by the radial force.
- Felloe end joints are crushed together.
- The rim becomes effectively a single unit.

### Tire Repair and Retireing

Over time, tires may loosen, crack, or wear thin.

**Loose tire:** A tire loosens if wood shrinks (very dry conditions) or if the wheel is overloaded and flexes excessively, stretching the tire.
- **Solution:** Remove the tire by heating it and lifting it off, or by driving a wedge under it to break the weld. Cut out a section 2–4 inches long, re-weld the ends together with an overlap of 2–3 inches (this creates a double-strength joint), and shrink the tire back on.

**Tire replacement:** If the tire becomes too thin or cracks longitudinally, it must be removed and replaced with a new one. This is a blacksmith's job.

## Axle Construction and Alignment

The axle supports the wheel and transmits force from the cart body to the ground. An axle can rotate (modern design) or the cart body rotates around a fixed axle (traditional design). Both types require precision.

### Axle Materials and Dimensions

**Material:** Iron or steel. Historically, wrought iron; modern carts may use cast iron or modern steel.

**Diameter:**
- Main shaft: 1.5–2.5 inches depending on cart weight and load
- Tapered section (wheel seat): slightly larger, typically 2–3 inches

**Length:**
- Must accommodate two wheels with spacing equal to the axle bore distance (typically 4–5 inches), plus the bearing surfaces outside the wheels, plus clearance (usually 1/2 to 1 inch overhang on each side)

### Axle Shapes

**Tapered axle:** The axle is thicker where it meets the wheel's axle hole and thinner at the outer ends. This shape allows the wheel to be driven onto the axle and wedged in place (as the taper is compressed, it locks the wheel).

**Parallel axle with cotters:** The axle is cylindrical, and after the wheel is seated, a wedge-shaped pin (cotter pin) is driven through a hole drilled at the axle's end to prevent the wheel from slipping outward.

**Bent/cranked axle:** Some carts have an axle that is bent upward at the ends (forming a U shape) so that the outer wheels sit higher than the inner wheels. This is useful for narrow tracks where the inner wheel would strike obstacles.

### Alignment and Toe-In

Wheel alignment affects how smoothly a cart rolls and how quickly tires wear.

**Camber:** The angle of the wheel relative to vertical. A slight outward tilt (camber) at the hub side is traditional—typically 1–2 inches of tilt over a 4-foot wheel diameter. This improves load distribution and allows the wheel to run on the outer edge of the axle hole.

**Toe-in:** The angle of the wheel's direction relative to the cart's centerline. Front wheels are sometimes set to toe inward slightly (toes pointing toward each other) for directional stability.

**Measurement:** To check alignment, place the cart on level ground and measure the distance from a fixed reference (e.g., a frame member) to the hub of each wheel at both front and rear. The measurements should match within 1/4 inch.

:::tip
A carpenter's square or plumb bob can verify that wheels are vertical. A wheel that leans will cause uneven tire wear and harder steering.
:::

## Grease Formulations and Axle Lubrication

Even the best-fitted axle generates friction. Lubrication is essential for smooth operation, load capacity, and longevity.

### Traditional Greases

**Tallow-based grease:**
- **Recipe:** Render animal fat (tallow) until clear. Cool slightly, then work in finely powdered chalk, clay, or wood ash (about 1 part powder to 4 parts tallow) while still warm.
- **Properties:** Holds well in the axle hole, moderately water-resistant, hardens as it cools. Becomes stiff in very cold weather.
- **Best for:** Ox carts and slow-moving wagons.

**Hoof/horn oil:**
- **Source:** Rendered hoof and horn shavings from cattle processing.
- **Properties:** Lighter than tallow-based greases, penetrates wood better, resists water reasonably well.
- **Application:** Mix 3 parts hoof oil to 1 part lime powder or clay for a spreadable consistency.

**Vegetable oil-based grease:**
- **Recipe:** Heat linseed oil or walnut oil with beeswax (about 4:1 ratio) until they blend, then cool. Add powdered chalk or dried herb powder for thickening.
- **Properties:** Long-lasting, water-resistant if beeswax content is high, becomes hard when cool.
- **Best for:** Fine carts where squeaking is undesirable.

**Beeswax and hog fat:**
- **Recipe:** Melt beeswax and rendered hog fat in a 1:2 ratio, then cool in a shallow pan.
- **Properties:** Very water-resistant, slightly elastic, resists hardening in cold.
- **Application:** Difficult to apply cold; usually warmed before use.

### Axle Lubrication Procedure

1. **Initial packing:** Before fitting the wheel onto the axle, pack grease into the axle hole of the hub. Fill the hole about 3/4 full.
2. **Fit the wheel:** Drive the wheel onto the axle. Some grease will be forced out; wipe away excess.
3. **Wheel axle cup:** Traditional wheels have a shallow cup (wooden or metal) around the base of the hub that collects and redistributes grease as the wheel rotates.
4. **Repacking:** After several days of use, inspect the axle hole. If grease appears depleted, carefully tap the wheel off the axle, remove old grease, pack new grease, and re-fit the wheel.
5. **Seasonal maintenance:** Before winter or before heavy use, repack all axles. In spring, check for gumming (old grease oxidized and hardened). Remove and replace with fresh grease.

### Wear and Replacement

**Axle hole wear:** Over time, the axle hole enlarges due to friction and wood abrasion. The wheel becomes looser.
- **Solution:** Drive wooden shims (thin wedges) between the wheel and axle to take up the gap. Alternatively, wrap the axle with linen cloth or rawhide to increase its effective diameter.

**Axle shaft wear:** A heavily used axle may wear grooves or develop corrosion.
- **Solution:** Smooth the axle with file or sandstone, or have a blacksmith turn down the axle diameter on a lathe and fit a new wheel with a smaller axle hole.

## Wheel Balancing and Quality Control

A balanced wheel rotates smoothly without vibration. An unbalanced wheel causes the cart to bounce, stresses joints, and wears tires rapidly.

### Balancing Methods

**By weight adjustment:**
- Weigh each spoke by suspending the wheel horizontally on a balanced beam or by rocking it gently on the ground.
- Identify the heaviest spoke. File or rasp the lighter spokes slightly to match.
- The goal is for all spokes to be nearly identical weight.

**By hub symmetry:**
- Ensure the hub mortises are cut with absolute symmetry. Unequal mortise depths or poor spoke fitting creates asymmetry.
- Measure mortise depths with a probe.

**Test for wobble:**
- Set the completed wheel on a spindle or axle in a vertical position.
- Spin it gently by hand.
- Watch for radial wobble (the rim moving closer and farther from a fixed point).
- If wobble is apparent, check felloe roundness and spoke curvature.

### Roundness and Run-Out

**True the wheel** by checking its outer profile against a fixed gauge:

1. Mount the wheel on its axle in a stand.
2. Position a pointer or gauge arm so its tip just clears the rim.
3. Spin the wheel slowly.
4. Any gap variation greater than 1/4 inch indicates the wheel is out of round.
5. Adjust by filing or planing the rim, or by loosening and re-fitting the iron tire.
