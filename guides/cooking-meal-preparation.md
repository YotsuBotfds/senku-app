Cooking well is mostly about four things: clean heat, enough water, enough time, and not wasting fuel. If you can boil, simmer, fry, and roast, you can make almost any simple meal from shelf-stable staples, leftovers, forage, or salvaged ingredients.

This guide covers *what to cook and how to cook it*: methods, recipes, doneness, one-pot meals, and cooking-order tactics. It is not about the fire itself.

**First step:**
- If you want to cook dinner, boil rice, fry eggs, or plan a meal, start here.

## Minimal Tools and Improvised Cookware

### Basic tools

- One pot with a lid
- One pan or skillet
- One spoon or spatula
- Knife and cutting board
- Bowl or bucket for mixing
- Strainer, cloth, or clean lid for draining
- Tongs, stick, or folded cloth for handling hot cookware

### Useful improvised cookware

- Heavy steel pot, enamel pot, cast iron pan, kettle, or Dutch oven
- Clean food-grade metal can or bucket for boiling water or soup
- Clay pot or clay lid if fired properly and not cracked
- Flat stone for griddle-style cooking
- Sheet metal or salvaged baking tin for roasting and baking

### Avoid for cooking

- Painted, lined, or unknown-coated containers
- Galvanized metal for acidic food or long simmering
- Plastic containers near heat
- Anything with lead solder, flaking rust, or a strange chemical smell

### Simple kitchen setup

For fire layout, coal management, and pot-position heat control, see `daily-cooking-fire-management.md`.

If you only have a pan:

1. Use the pan for frying, warming, and quick sauces.
2. Add a small splash of water to steam vegetables or finish tough food.
3. Cover with a lid or another pan to trap heat.

## Core Cooking Methods

| Method | What It Looks Like | Best For | Key Rule |
|---|---|---|---|
| Boil | Large, rolling bubbles | Pasta, blanching, tough starts for beans | Use enough water to keep food moving |
| Simmer | Small, gentle bubbles | Rice, beans, soup, stew | Keep heat low and steady |
| Fry | Food sizzles in fat | Eggs, flatbread, potatoes, meat pieces | Do not crowd the pan |
| Roast | Dry heat surrounds food | Roots, squash, meat, onions | Turn food for even browning |
| Bake | Enclosed dry heat | Bread, flatbread, casseroles | Preheat the heat source first |

### Boiling

Boiling is useful when you need fast heat transfer or when the food needs to become fully hydrated.

Steps:

1. Add food and enough water to cover it.
2. Bring to a full boil.
3. Reduce only if the recipe asks for simmering.
4. Stir occasionally so food does not stick.

Use boiling for:

- Initial bean cooking
- Cooking potatoes and root vegetables
- Pasta and dumplings
- Safe reheating of soups and stews

### Simmering

Simmering is the workhorse method for everyday cooking. If the pot is boiling hard, you are usually wasting fuel.

Steps:

1. Bring the pot up to a boil if the food needs it.
2. Reduce heat until you see just a few bubbles rising at the surface.
3. Keep the lid on.
4. Stir only often enough to prevent sticking.

Use simmering for:

- Rice
- Beans after the first boil
- Soup
- Stew
- Tough meat that needs time to tenderize

### Frying

Frying is for flavor, browning, and quick cooking.

Steps:

1. Heat the pan first, then add fat.
2. Add food in a single layer.
3. Leave space so moisture can escape.
4. Turn food when one side browns.

Use frying for:

- Eggs
- Onions, garlic, and aromatics
- Thin meat strips
- Potatoes and quick vegetable sides
- Flatbread

### Roasting and Baking Without Modern Appliances

You do not need a modern oven to roast or bake. You need enclosed heat.

Options:

- Dutch oven with coals on the lid and under the pot
- Covered pot set near coals
- Reflector oven made from sheet metal or stone
- Brick, clay, or earth oven
- Covered pan over a fire with coals arranged around it

Steps:

1. Preheat the cooking chamber or pot.
2. Put the food in a covered vessel.
3. Keep the heat steady, not wild.
4. Rotate or shift the vessel if one side cooks faster.

Use roasting and baking for:

- Bread and flatbread
- Potatoes, onions, and squash
- Bone-in meat pieces
- Casseroles and baked grain dishes

## Rice, Beans, Meat, Soup, and Flatbread

### Rice

Rice is one of the easiest staple foods to prepare and one of the most useful for one-pot meals.

Basic rice method:

1. Rinse rice until the water looks less cloudy.
2. Add 1 part rice to 2 parts water for plain white rice.
3. Bring to a boil.
4. Cover and reduce to a low simmer.
5. Cook until the water is absorbed and the grains are tender.
6. Turn off the heat and rest covered for 10 minutes.

Helpful notes:

- Brown rice usually needs more water and more time than white rice.
- If the pot is running dry before the rice is tender, add a small splash of hot water and keep simmering.
- If the rice is mushy, you used too much water or too much heat.

### Beans, Lentils, and Peas

Beans are cheap, filling, and excellent in one-pot meals, but they need enough water and enough time.

Basic bean method:

1. Sort out stones, shriveled beans, and debris.
2. Rinse well.
3. Soak overnight if you can.
4. Drain and add fresh water.
5. Bring to a boil, then simmer until tender.
6. Add acidic ingredients like tomato, vinegar, or lemon after the beans are mostly soft.

Helpful notes:

- Older beans take longer than fresh beans.
- Lentils and split peas cook faster than whole beans.
- Kidney beans and some other red beans should be boiled hard at first, then simmered until fully soft.
- Beans are done when they mash easily between finger and thumb or taste soft all the way through, not chalky in the center.

### Meat and Fish

The safest everyday rule is simple: cook meat until the center is no longer raw-looking.

Basic meat method:

1. Cut meat into smaller pieces if you want it to cook faster.
2. Brown the outside first for flavor.
3. Add water or broth if you want a stew or braise.
4. Simmer tougher cuts until they become tender.

Safe doneness heuristics:

- Poultry should be fully cooked with no pink near the bone and juices running clear.
- Ground meat should be cooked all the way through with no raw center.
- Fish should flake easily and look opaque, not glassy.
- Tough stewing meat should be tender enough to chew without effort.

If you have a thermometer, use it. If you do not, cut the thickest piece open and check the center.

### Soup and Stew

Soup is the easiest way to turn scraps into a real meal.

Basic soup formula:

1. Start with water, stock, or broth.
2. Add onions, garlic, or other aromatics if available.
3. Add the longest-cooking ingredients first: beans, roots, grains, or meat.
4. Add quick-cooking vegetables later.
5. Salt gradually and taste near the end.

Basic stew formula:

1. Brown meat or onions first if you have fat.
2. Add water plus any tough vegetables.
3. Simmer until everything is tender.
4. Thicken with rice, flour, mashed beans, potatoes, or bread crumbs.

### Flatbread and Simple Bread

Flatbread is the fastest bread you can make without a modern oven.

Basic flatbread:

1. Mix flour or meal with a pinch of salt.
2. Add just enough water to form a soft dough.
3. Knead briefly.
4. Rest 10 to 20 minutes if you have time.
5. Roll or flatten thin.
6. Cook on a hot dry pan, griddle, or stone.

Useful variations:

- Add a little oil or fat for a softer bread.
- Add chopped herbs, onion, or garlic for flavor.
- Cook thicker dough under a lid if you want a more bread-like result.

## Fuel-Efficient Cooking

The easiest way to save fuel is to stop using full heat when full heat is no longer needed. For fire-side fuel economy (wood choice, coal banking, smoke reduction), see `daily-cooking-fire-management.md`.

Fuel-saving cooking habits:

- Soak beans before cooking
- Cut food into smaller pieces
- Keep lids on pots
- Use just enough water, not extra
- Cook several foods in one pot
- Bring food to a boil, then drop to a simmer
- Use residual heat after turning the fire down

### Best order for one-pot cooking

1. Start with the food that takes the longest: beans, roots, or tough meat.
2. Add medium-cooking items: rice, squash, potatoes, or grains.
3. Add fast-cooking items last: greens, herbs, or tender vegetables.
4. Finish with salt, vinegar, or fresh seasoning.

### Batch cooking

If fuel is scarce, cook a large pot once and turn it into multiple meals:

- Eat part of it hot
- Save part as a thick stew
- Reheat the next meal with a little water added back
- Turn leftovers into fried rice, hash, or soup

## Simple Meal Templates

### Rice and Beans

Ingredients:

- Rice
- Beans or lentils
- Water or broth
- Fat
- Salt
- Onion, garlic, or spices if available

Method:

1. Cook the beans first or use already-cooked beans.
2. Add rice and the right amount of water.
3. Simmer covered until both are tender.
4. Stir in fat and seasoning at the end.

Why it works:

- Cheap
- Filling
- Stores well
- Gives a better protein balance than either food alone

### Soup With Grain

Ingredients:

- Water or stock
- Grain such as rice, barley, or cracked grain
- Vegetables
- Small amount of meat or beans

Method:

1. Simmer the longest-cooking ingredients first.
2. Add quick vegetables near the end.
3. Taste and season before serving.

Why it works:

- Very forgiving
- Good for leftovers
- Easy to stretch

### Frying Pan Hash

Ingredients:

- Cooked potatoes, rice, or bread
- Any cooked meat or beans
- Onion or leftover vegetables
- Fat

Method:

1. Heat fat in the pan.
2. Add the starchy base first.
3. Add the rest of the leftovers.
4. Stir until heated through and lightly browned.

Why it works:

- Uses scraps
- Fast
- Good for breakfast or late-day meals

### Campfire Stew

Ingredients:

- Water
- Meat or beans
- Roots, potatoes, or squash
- Salt and seasonings

Method:

1. Put the longest-cooking ingredient in first.
2. Keep the pot near coals, not in tall flame.
3. Cover the pot.
4. Simmer until tender.

Why it works:

- One pot
- Minimal attention
- Easy to scale up for a group

### Flatbread and Eggs

Ingredients:

- Flour or meal
- Water
- Salt
- Eggs
- Fat for the pan

Method:

1. Mix and cook the flatbread first.
2. Fry or scramble the eggs in the same pan.
3. Eat together, or wrap the eggs in the bread.

Why it works:

- Very fast
- Good when you only have a pan
- Easy to season with any herbs or scraps

## Doneness and Safety Heuristics

If you do not have exact equipment, use a mix of sight, touch, smell, and texture.

### Rice

- No hard white center
- Water fully absorbed or drained
- Grains separate instead of crunching

### Beans

- Soft all the way through
- No chalky or gritty center
- Easy to mash between fingers

### Meat

- Poultry: fully opaque, no pink near the bone, juices clear
- Ground meat: no raw center
- Fish: opaque and flakes easily
- Stew meat: tender, not rubbery

### Soup and stew

- Steady simmer before serving
- Ingredients taste cooked through, not raw
- Pot smells clean, not sour or rotten

### When to be cautious

- Do not eat undercooked beans
- Do not trust a browned outside alone on meat
- Reheat leftovers thoroughly
- If a food smells off, looks slimy, or has gas buildup where it should not, discard it

## Quick Troubleshooting

- Rice burned on bottom: heat was too high or water was too low.
- Rice mushy: too much water or too much stirring.
- Beans still hard: too little time, old beans, or acid added too early.
- Meat dry: cooked too fast or with too much direct heat.
- Flatbread tough: dough was too dry or cooked too long.
- Soup bland: add salt, acid, or fat at the end.
- Soup too thin: simmer uncovered, or add mashed beans, rice, or flour.

## Everyday Rule of Thumb

If you remember nothing else:

1. Keep the lid on.
2. Start high, then turn down to simmer.
3. Cook the longest ingredient first.
4. Use one pot whenever you can.
5. Check doneness by cutting, tasting, and testing texture.

That is enough to make dependable meals from simple ingredients, whether you are cooking in a kitchen, at a campsite, or over a fire. For fire control, see `daily-cooking-fire-management.md`. For group meal service, see `community-kitchen-mess-hall-operations.md`.
