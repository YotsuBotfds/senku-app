## Overview

:::info-box
**Start here if:** the question is symptom-first or day-to-day, such as how to clean a wound, whether a cut is getting infected, what a bad smell or pus means, whether to leave it open, or how often to change a dressing on a newer wound.

**Use the sibling guide instead** if the wound has stayed open for weeks, is a bedsore or pressure sore, is a diabetic foot ulcer, or needs chronic follow-up/offloading/debridement planning. See <a href="../wound-care-chronic.html">Chronic Wound Care</a>.
:::

Infection kills more wilderness survival patients than the original injury. A minor cut can progress to life-threatening sepsis in 7–10 days without hygiene and early intervention. This guide covers wound assessment, cleaning with limited resources, signs of infection at each stage, recognition of sepsis, and the difficult decision of when to leave a wound open versus close it. This guide is the essential follow-up to <a href="../trauma-hemorrhage-control.html">Trauma Hemorrhage Control</a> and <a href="../surgery-field.html">Field Surgery & Emergency Procedures</a>. For systemic sepsis recognition and treatment, see <a href="../sepsis-recognition-antibiotic-protocols.html">Sepsis Recognition, Escalation & Empiric Antibiotic Protocols</a>.

:::danger
Sepsis (systemic infection) can be fatal within 24–48 hours of symptom onset. Death from infection in wilderness settings happens rapidly once the cascade begins. Prevention is far more effective than treatment.
:::

## Timeline of Infection Progression

Understanding when infection typically emerges helps you intervene before systemic spread.

### Hour 0–6: Immediate Post-Injury

**What is happening:**
- Bacteria colonize wound surface
- Immune response begins (inflammation increases blood flow, white blood cells arrive)
- Wound starts to swell and redden slightly

**What you should do:**
- Clean wound thoroughly with boiled water, soap, or saltwater
- Remove debris, foreign material, dead tissue
- Assess need for closure vs. leaving open
- Apply hemostatic agents or dressing
- **Avoid unnecessary tourniquets** (they increase infection risk)

**Warning signs at this stage:** Increasing pain beyond the trauma itself may indicate compartment syndrome (see later section).

### Hour 6–24: Early Inflammatory Phase

**What is happening:**
- Swelling increases visibly
- Redness expands from wound edges
- Warmth develops around wound
- Patient may have low-grade fever (99–101°F / 37–38°C)
- Pain may decrease slightly as swelling buffers nerve endings

**What you should do:**
- Assess wound for debris or foreign body by gentle probing
- Change dressing if soaked or visibly dirty
- Continue elevation to reduce swelling
- Provide fluids and rest
- Monitor temperature (if you can measure it)
- **Leave wound open** unless it was cleaned and is in a sterile environment

**Red flags at this stage:**
- Rapid expanding redness (spreading beyond 1 inch per hour)
- Severe pain increasing rather than stabilizing
- Fever above 102°F (39°C)
- Pus with foul odor (indicates bacterial proliferation)

### Day 2–3: Critical Infection Decision Point

**What is happening:**
- If bacteria load is low and immune system is strong: wound begins to granulate (pink tissue forms), swelling peaks then starts to decline
- If bacteria load is high or immune system is compromised: infection accelerates, spreading cellulitis (spreading red, warm area), pus formation, worsening systemic signs
- Fever often rises to 102–103°F (39–39.5°C)
- Wound pain may increase sharply if abscess (pocket of pus) forms

**What you should do:**
- **Assess spreading cellulitis.** Mark the edge of the red area with a pen or line. Check every 2 hours to see if redness is spreading beyond the line.
- **Check for fluctuance.** Gently press around the wound for soft, pus-filled pockets (abscess). If present, drain (see "Abscess Management" section).
- **Monitor fever trend.** If fever is stable or declining, you are likely winning. If fever is rising, sepsis may be developing.
- **Increase local wound care.** Clean 2–3 times daily with boiled water, salt solution, or honey.
- **Consider herbal support** if available: willow bark, echinacea, or garlic for immune support.

**Danger signs at day 2–3:**
- Spreading cellulitis (redness expanding > 1 inch in 6 hours)
- Fever rising despite local care
- Increased systemic symptoms: chills, muscle aches, nausea
- Rapid swelling of lymph nodes (glands) above or below wound

### Day 4–7: Infection vs. Healing

**Good trajectory (infection controlled):**
- Redness stops expanding
- Swelling peaks and gradually decreases
- Wound begins to show granulation (pink tissue, shiny appearance)
- Fever declines
- Pain decreases
- Drainage (if any) decreases in volume and becomes clear/pale yellow

**Bad trajectory (infection spreading):**
- Redness continues to expand or develops striping (red lines toward lymph nodes)
- Fever continues or increases
- Lymph node swelling worsens (palpable lumps in groin, armpit, neck)
- Systemic symptoms worsen: confusion, lethargy, rapid heartbeat
- Wound may become darker (blue-black), indicating tissue death

:::warning
Red streaking moving up the limb from the wound toward the heart represents lymphangitis (infection spreading via lymph vessels). This is a sign of impending sepsis and requires immediate escalation of treatment or amputation decision.
:::

### Day 7+: Systemic Sepsis Risk

By day 7, if local infection has not been controlled, systemic sepsis becomes likely.

**Signs of sepsis at day 7+:**
- High fever (103°F+ / 39.5°C+) or paradoxical low temperature (< 95°F / 35°C)
- Rapid heartbeat (> 120 bpm at rest)
- Rapid breathing (> 20 breaths/min)
- Confusion or altered mental status
- Severe nausea, vomiting, diarrhea
- Wound appears darker (purple, black, or greenish)
- Fluid balance failure: unable to drink enough, dehydration despite access to water

**At this point:**
- **Arrange evacuation urgently.** Sepsis without advanced medical care is fatal within days.
- **Consider amputation** if infection is localized to a limb and has not yet reached systemic circulation.
- **Increase fluid intake.** Dehydration from fever and diarrhea accelerates septic shock.
- **Stop preservation-focused wound care.** If amputation is being considered, do not spend time optimizing a dying limb.

## Early Wound Cleaning

The first hours after injury are the most critical for preventing infection. Thorough cleaning dramatically reduces bacterial colonization.

### Step-by-Step Wound Cleaning

1. **Prepare cleaning solution.**
   - **Boiled water:** Safest option. Boil water for 1 minute, allow to cool to warm (not scalding).
   - **Saltwater:** Mix 1 tablespoon salt per liter of boiled water. This is mildly antimicrobial and promotes osmotic drainage.
   - **Soap water:** Use any mild soap (bar or liquid). Soap kills bacteria without irritating tissue.
   - **If nothing is available:** Use collected rainwater or clean stream water (though risk of parasitic contamination exists).

2. **Remove gross debris.** Use clean hands or cloth to gently wipe away visible dirt, plant material, and blood clots.

3. **Irrigate the wound.** Pour or pour/splash cleaning solution over the wound to flush out smaller particles. If you have a syringe or pump, higher-pressure irrigation (spraying water through a small hole) is more effective at removing debris.

4. **Probe for embedded material.** Using a clean finger or small stick, gently probe the wound to check for embedded glass, metal, wood splinters, or other foreign bodies. Remove anything you can access. Leave objects too deep to reach safely (risk of causing more damage).

5. **Flush again.** After removing visible debris, irrigate once more with clean solution.

6. **Pat dry gently** with clean cloth. Do not scrub the wound itself (damages forming clot).

7. **Assess wound depth and character.**
   - **Shallow (< 1/4 inch):** Abrasion or minor laceration. Low infection risk.
   - **Moderate (1/4 to 1/2 inch):** Laceration. Moderate infection risk. Consider closing if edges are clean and can be held together.
   - **Deep (> 1/2 inch or penetrating):** High infection risk. Leave it open to allow drainage.

### Wound Closure Decision

**Close the wound (pull edges together) if:**
- Wound is clean (no visible contamination remaining)
- Edges are smooth and you can hold them closely together without tension
- Wound is on face, hand, or other area where function is important
- You have means to close it (sutures, tape, or cloth strips)
- Wound is < 6 hours old (risk of bacterial sealing in)
- Patient is not showing signs of infection risk (diabetes, immunosuppression, etc.)

**Leave wound open if:**
- Wound is heavily contaminated (fell in dirt, cut by rusty object)
- Wound is > 6–8 hours old (bacteria likely already established)
- Wound is puncture-type or has jagged edges (hard to clean thoroughly)
- Edges cannot be held together without tension
- You suspect foreign material remains inside
- Patient is at high infection risk

:::info-box
**Dirty Wound Definition:** Any wound exposed to soil, animal contact, standing water, or contaminated objects (rusty metal, teeth, feces). Dirty wounds have 10,000+ times more bacterial colonization than clean wounds.
:::

## Dressing Changes and Bandage Reuse

The day-to-day question is usually not whether a wound needs a dressing at all. It is how often to change it, what to do when supplies are limited, and when a cloth covering has crossed the line from "usable" to "too contaminated to trust."

If the wound has become a weeks-long nonhealing ulcer, bedsore, or diabetic foot wound, hand off to <a href="../wound-care-chronic.html">Chronic Wound Care</a> for chronic staging, pressure relief, and longer-term dressing strategy.

### How Often To Change a Dressing

- **Clean, low-drainage wounds:** change once a day or every 1-2 days if the dressing stays dry and clean
- **Wounds with drainage, sweat, or dirt exposure:** change daily, or sooner if the dressing gets wet
- **Wounds with infection or heavy fluid:** change 2-3 times daily
- **Any dressing that is soaked, dirty, smelly, loose, or painful to remove:** replace it right away

### Reuse Boundaries for Cloth Dressings

- **Reusable if:** the cloth can be washed well with soap and clean water, then boiled or dried fully before reuse
- **Do not reuse if:** it is soaked with pus, stool, vomit, or something you cannot wash out cleanly
- **Do not reuse if:** the cloth keeps shedding fibers into the wound, has an odor after washing, or has any mold or rot
- **If a cloth has touched an infected wound, keep it in a separate dirty pile until it is washed**

### If You Are Out of Gauze

Use a clean cotton cloth, bandanna, T-shirt, pillowcase strip, or similar lint-light fabric folded into layers. Secure it so it does not slip, but do not wrap so tightly that it cuts off circulation.

- Best choice: clean, washed cotton that does not shed much lint
- Better than nothing: a boiled cloth strip or clean shirt cut into pads
- Avoid: tissues, paper towels, fluffy towels, or fabric that falls apart and leaves fibers in the wound

### Removing a Dressing Safely

- If the dressing sticks, moisten it with clean water or saline and let it loosen before pulling
- Do not rip off crusted material if it is firmly attached and the wound is still open
- Look for spreading redness, increasing pain, odor, or thicker drainage each time you change it
- Wash hands before and after each change

## Improvised Antiseptics and Antimicrobial Agents

When sterile dressings or commercial antiseptics are unavailable, natural materials can reduce bacterial load.

### Honey

**Antimicrobial properties:**
- Hydrogen peroxide produced by enzyme glucose oxidase
- Osmotic effect (draws fluid from wound, preventing bacterial growth)
- Low pH inhibits bacterial growth
- Contains propolis (antibacterial from bee resin)

**Use:**
- Apply raw or processed honey directly to clean wound
- Cover with cloth dressing to prevent contamination
- Change dressing every 12–24 hours
- Apply fresh honey each time

**Effectiveness:** Honey is effective against most wound bacteria, including antibiotic-resistant strains. Historical use supports its efficacy.

**Caution:** Do not use honey on infants under 12 months (botulism risk). Do not apply to wounds that will be closed in a hospital (honey contamination impedes suturing).

### Salt Solution

**Antimicrobial properties:**
- Osmotic effect draws fluid from bacterial cells, killing them
- Mild pH change inhibits some bacteria
- Reduces tissue swelling

**Use:**
- Mix 1 tablespoon salt per cup of boiled water
- Soak wound by submerging or packing cloth soaked in solution onto wound
- Soak 10–15 minutes, 2–3 times daily
- Can be used as dressing by keeping cloth moist

**Caution:** Excessive salt causes pain and tissue damage. Rinse with fresh water after 15 minutes.

### Charcoal

**Properties:**
- Absorbs bacteria and toxins
- Provides structural support for dressing
- Historically used for infected wounds

**Preparation:**
- Burn wood completely in a fire to create charcoal
- Crush charred wood into fine powder
- Mix with water to create paste (charcoal : water, 1:2 ratio)

**Use:**
- Apply thin layer to wound surface
- Cover with cloth dressing
- Change daily

**Caution:** Charcoal alone is not sufficient for serious infection. Use as adjunct to other methods.

### Boiled Cloth Dressings

**Preparation:**
- Tear clean cloth into strips or pads
- Boil in water for 10 minutes to sterilize
- Allow to cool
- Store in sealed cloth to prevent recontamination

**Use:**
- Change dressing 1–2 times daily (more frequently if soaked with drainage)
- Each time, inspect wound for signs of infection
- Replace with fresh boiled cloth

**Effectiveness:** Boiled cloth prevents bacterial contamination from the air and provides clean contact with wound surface. This alone prevents many infections.

### Willow Bark Infusion

**Antimicrobial properties:**
- Salicin (similar to aspirin) reduces inflammation
- Mild antimicrobial compounds
- Reduces fever

**Preparation:**
- Strip inner bark from willow branches
- Dry bark or use fresh
- Simmer in water for 10–15 minutes
- Cool before use

**Use:**
- Soak dressing in infusion
- Apply to wound 1–2 times daily
- Can also drink infusion for systemic anti-inflammatory effect

### Plantain (Plantago major/minor)

**Antimicrobial properties:**
- Contains polysaccharides that promote wound healing
- Mild astringent and antimicrobial
- Historically used for trauma and infection

**Preparation:**
- Harvest fresh leaves
- Crush to release juices
- Use immediately or dry for later use (rehydrate with boiled water)

**Use:**
- Apply crushed leaves directly to wound
- Bind with cloth
- Change 1–2 times daily

**Effectiveness:** Plantain promotes clotting and may reduce infection risk. Not as potent as honey but widely available.

### Garlic

**Antimicrobial properties:**
- Allicin (active compound) kills broad spectrum of bacteria
- Potent but irritating to tissue

**Use:**
- Crush fresh garlic cloves to release allicin
- Mix with honey or fat to create paste
- Apply thin layer to wound periphery (not inside)
- Avoid direct contact with intact wound tissue (causes pain and irritation)

**Caution:** Do not use inside deep wounds (tissue damage). Use only on surface areas.

## Abscess Management

An abscess is a pocket of pus-filled fluid. On day 2–4, if you feel a soft, fluctuant area next to the main wound, an abscess is likely forming.

### Recognition

- Fluctuance: Press gently on the swollen area. If it feels soft and "squishy," fluid is likely present.
- Heat and redness localized to one area
- Patient reports relief when area is pressed (fluid shifting reduces local pressure)
- Possible point of maximum tenderness

### Drainage Technique

1. **Prepare the area.** Clean skin surface with soap and water or alcohol if available.

2. **Create incision.** Using the sharpest blade available, make a small incision (1/4 to 1/2 inch) at the point of maximum fluctuance. Hold the blade perpendicular to skin.

3. **Allow drainage.** Pus will drain. Let it flow naturally; do not squeeze excessively.

4. **Collect drainage.** Use cloth to collect draining pus (infection control).

5. **Explore cavity gently.** Using a small probe (stick, bone) or your finger, feel inside the cavity to check for foreign objects or membranes. Remove any obvious material.

6. **Do NOT pack the cavity.** Leave open to continue draining. Packing traps bacteria inside.

7. **Clean and dress.** Irrigate with saltwater. Apply honey or cloth dressing.

8. **Monitor for re-accumulation.** Check daily. If abscess re-forms, drain again.

:::warning
An abscess that re-forms despite repeated drainage indicates inadequate source control or ongoing bacterial introduction. Consider whether wound care is clean enough or if debridement (removal of dead tissue) is needed.
:::

## Secondary Wound Care: Days 2–7

Once initial infection risk has been assessed, ongoing wound care focuses on preventing secondary contamination and promoting healing.

If this has moved beyond a newer wound and is now a persistent open sore lasting weeks, switch to <a href="../wound-care-chronic.html">Chronic Wound Care</a> for chronic ulcer pathways, TIME-based wound-bed preparation, and pressure/offloading management.

### Daily Wound Care Routine

**Morning and evening (or 1–2 times daily):**

1. **Inspect the wound.** Look for:
   - Expanding redness
   - Increased swelling
   - Change in color (deeper red, purple, blue-black)
   - Pus or unusual drainage (foul-smelling, greenish, or increasing in volume)
   - Stripe-like redness extending toward nearest lymph node

2. **Change dressing.** Remove old dressing (do not disturb any adherent clot). Inspect dressing for signs (increased drainage, discoloration). Discard safely.

3. **Gentle cleaning.** If dressing is soaked or visibly dirty, rinse wound gently with boiled water or saltwater. Do not probe or disturb healing tissue.

4. **Apply fresh dressing.** Use honey, salt paste, boiled cloth, or combination. Ensure dressing covers entire wound and extends 1 inch beyond edges.

5. **Secure dressing.** Use cloth strips, cord, or tape to keep dressing in place without restricting circulation.

6. **Monitor temperature.** If you can measure temperature, record it (even mentally). Fever trending up suggests worsening infection.

### Elevation and Immobilization

- Keep injured limb elevated above heart level when resting to reduce swelling
- Immobilize the limb to prevent reopening the wound (splint or sling)
- Movement and muscle contraction increase swelling and bleeding, slowing healing

### Fluid and Nutrition

- Infection accelerates metabolism. Patient needs extra fluids (dehydration from fever) and calories (tissue healing).
- Provide water, broth, high-protein food if available
- If available, herbal tea with willow bark or echinacea supports immune function

## Recognizing and Managing Tetanus Risk

Tetanus (lockjaw) is caused by *Clostridium tetani*, a spore-forming bacterium found in soil. Spores enter through wound contamination.

### Tetanus Risk Factors

**Very high risk (assume tetanus risk; treat as such):**
- Wound contaminated with soil, animal feces, or manure
- Puncture wound (especially from dirty object)
- Crush injury or wound with devitalized tissue
- Burns
- Frostbite

**Moderate risk:**
- Clean laceration from potentially contaminated source
- Age > 50 years (immunity may have waned)

**Low risk:**
- Clean laceration from clean object
- Minor abrasion

### Tetanus Prevention without Vaccine

If you have no tetanus vaccine or booster in the past 10 years:

1. **Thorough wound cleaning.** Remove all debris and devitalized tissue.

2. **Keep wound open to prevent anaerobic (oxygen-poor) environment.** Tetanus thrives in oxygen-free conditions.

3. **Honey application.** Honey creates an antimicrobial environment hostile to spores.

4. **Antibiotic if available.** If you have antibiotics (doxycycline, penicillin, tetracycline), use prophylactically.

5. **Monitor for tetanus symptoms** (see recognition section). Early treatment with supportive care (airway management) improves survival.

### Tetanus Symptom Recognition

**Early signs (3–21 days post-injury):**
- Muscle stiffness, especially in jaw and neck
- Difficulty swallowing
- Muscle twitching
- Irritability

**Late signs (indicates systemic tetanus):**
- Trismus: jaw locked (cannot open mouth)
- Risus sardonicus: facial muscles frozen in grimace
- Opisthotonus: severe arching of back
- Autonomic instability: rapid heartbeat, blood pressure fluctuations, fever

**Treatment in wilderness:**
- Supportive care (airway management, hydration, wound care)
- Wound care to remove remaining spores
- If any antibiotics available, use immediately
- Evacuation is critical (tetanus requires ICU care in hospital)

## Systemic Infection and Sepsis Escalation

Once local infection has progressed to systemic signs, interventions become more aggressive.

### Sepsis Recognition (Day 4+)

**Systemic signs indicating sepsis:**
- Fever > 103°F (39.5°C) or temperature < 95°F (35°C) — hypothermia from septic shock
- Rapid heartbeat (> 120 bpm)
- Rapid breathing (> 20 breaths/min, may feel like hyperventilation)
- Confusion, difficulty concentrating, altered mental status
- Severe nausea, vomiting, diarrhea
- Dehydration despite access to water

### Escalated Wound Management

If sepsis is developing:

1. **Aggressive debridement:** If possible, remove all visibly dead or discolored tissue. This removes bacterial food source.

2. **Frequent wound cleaning:** Change dressing every 6 hours (vs. 12–24 hours for non-infected wounds).

3. **Systemic antimicrobials:** If you have any antibiotics, use them immediately (don't save for later). Common antibiotics useful for wound infection:
   - Doxycycline (100 mg twice daily)
   - Tetracycline (250–500 mg 4 times daily)
   - Penicillin V (250–500 mg 4 times daily)
   - Any broad-spectrum antibiotic if available

4. **Herbal antimicrobial escalation:** Garlic, echinacea, willow bark, all used simultaneously.

5. **Aggressive fluid replacement:** Force fluids (water, broth, weak tea) to maintain hydration and support kidney function. Sepsis causes fluid loss via fever, vomiting, diarrhea.

### Amputation Decision at Sepsis Stage

If wound infection has reached systemic sepsis and the infection is localized to a limb:

**Consider amputation if:**
- Infection began in limb (arm, leg) and has not yet reached torso or bloodstream
- Limb shows unmistakable gangrene (dark tissue) or spreading necrosis
- Patient has clear sepsis symptoms and limb is source
- Perform amputation before the patient becomes too weak from septic shock

**Do not amputate if:**
- Sepsis is already severe (patient hypotensive, confused); amputation trauma will be fatal
- Infection is already bloodborne (fever preceded by distributed symptoms)
- Patient is unconscious or unable to consent

**Timeline:** Perform amputation within 24–48 hours of clear sepsis diagnosis. After 48 hours, septic shock is usually irreversible without hospital care.

### If Evacuation Is Possible

- Accelerate evacuation timing at first sign of systemic infection
- Do not delay for perfect wound closure or finishing local treatment
- Transport patient rapidly to facility with IV antibiotics, fluids, and surgical capability

## Summary: Infection Prevention Checklist

**Hour 0–6 (Immediate):**
- ☐ Clean wound thoroughly
- ☐ Remove visible debris and contamination
- ☐ Apply antimicrobial (honey, salt, boiled cloth)
- ☐ Cover with clean dressing
- ☐ Assess for closure vs. open management

**Day 1–2:**
- ☐ Change dressing 1–2 times daily
- ☐ Assess for expanding redness (mark edge)
- ☐ Monitor temperature if possible
- ☐ Elevate limb, immobilize
- ☐ Provide fluids and rest

**Day 3–4 (Critical decision point):**
- ☐ Check for cellulitis expansion or striping
- ☐ Assess for abscess (fluctuance) and drain if present
- ☐ Monitor fever trend
- ☐ Escalate dressing frequency if signs worsen
- ☐ Consider evacuation if infection accelerating

**Day 5–7:**
- ☐ Assess for sepsis (high fever, altered mental status, rapid heart/breathing)
- ☐ If sepsis present: escalate antimicrobials (herbal + any antibiotics)
- ☐ Evaluate amputation candidacy if infection localized to limb
- ☐ Prioritize evacuation if any sepsis sign

**Ongoing (all phases):**
- ☐ Keep wound clean and dressing fresh
- ☐ Maintain hydration and nutrition
- ☐ Reduce patient anxiety and stress
- ☐ Prepare for evacuation at first serious sign of systemic infection

If the wound remains open for weeks despite cleaning and infection control, route next to <a href="../wound-care-chronic.html">Chronic Wound Care</a> for pressure-ulcer, diabetic-foot, venous-ulcer, and chronic dressing-management guidance.
