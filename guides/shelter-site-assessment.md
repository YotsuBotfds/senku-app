## Site Assessment Fundamentals

Before committing time and materials to building a shelter, you must evaluate the site thoroughly. A poor location can expose you to disease, injury, animal attacks, flooding, extreme weather, and psychological distress. A good location prioritizes:

1. **Immediate safety** — protection from hazards
2. **Resource access** — water, firewood, shelter material availability
3. **Awareness** — visibility, routes, and approach-risk awareness
4. **Sustainability** — long-term livability without degrading the land

The assessment process involves observing the landscape, understanding local weather patterns, and testing the site under various conditions. Ideally, scout the location across different seasons and times of day.

<section id="terrain-analysis">

## Terrain Analysis

Topography directly affects shelter safety, comfort, and resource access. Analyze the landscape systematically.

### Elevation and Slope

- **Avoid valley bottoms** unless water source is critical. Cold air sinks; valleys are frost pockets and collect damp air.
- **Prefer mid-slope locations** (20–40 degrees) with good drainage. Higher elevation = lower disease mosquito populations and cooler temperatures in summer.
- **Flat ground near water** is tempting but dangerous — choose flat land *above* the flood plain, never in it.
- **Aspect matters** — in northern hemisphere, south-facing slopes get more winter sun; north-facing slopes stay cooler in summer and retain moisture longer.

### Soil Type and Drainage

Dig a test hole 12 inches deep to check:

- **Sandy soil** — drains fast, good for foundations, but doesn't retain moisture for plants. Susceptible to wind erosion.
- **Clay soil** — holds water, prone to waterlogging, difficult to dig in dry conditions, but stable for foundations.
- **Loamy soil** — ideal. Balanced drainage and water retention.
- **Rocky ground** — difficult to dig, but excellent drainage and stable.

Saturated or boggy ground indicates poor drainage and high water table. Move upslope.

:::warning
If water oozes into your test hole within an hour, the water table is too high. This site will have dampness, mold, and disease vectors (mosquitoes, fungi).
:::

### Vegetation Indicators

Plant species reveal a lot about site conditions:

| Plant Type | Indicates |
|---|---|
| Rushes, cattails, sedges | Waterlogged/boggy soil |
| Moss, ferns | Shade, moisture, acidity |
| Stunted/sparse growth | Exposure, poor drainage, or harsh microclimate |
| Dense tree stands | Good soil, stable moisture |
| Erosion gullies | Steep runoff; unstable during rain |

<section id="water-proximity-and-quality">

## Water Proximity and Quality

A reliable water source is essential, but proximity must be balanced against safety.

### Distance and Accessibility

- **1/4 to 1/2 mile** from shelter is ideal — far enough to avoid contamination, close enough for daily collection.
- **Closer than 100 meters** risks fouling your water with shelter waste and animal activity.
- **Farther than 2 miles** makes daily collection exhausting, especially in winter.
- Consider **seasonal variation** — a summer stream may be dry in winter; plan for alternative sources.

### Water Quality Assessment

Observe the water source:

- **Clear, cold, fast-moving water** in upper watershed = likely safer than stagnant water.
- **Stagnant water** (ponds, swamps) = high disease organism density.
- **Animal trails converging at water** = good sign for reliability, but risk of animal encounters and parasite contamination.
- **Algae blooms, foam, or chemical smell** = contamination from upstream sources.

Always assume water is contaminated and plan for boiling or filtration, regardless of apparent clarity.

:::warning
Never locate shelter directly downwind of stagnant water or swamps. Malaria, dengue, and other vector-borne diseases spread from mosquito-breeding sites.
:::

<section id="wind-and-microclimate">

## Wind Exposure and Microclimate

Wind strips heat, damages structures, and prevents fires. Understanding local wind patterns is critical.

### Identifying Prevailing Winds

- **Observe tree growth patterns** — trees lean or have branches stripped on the windward side.
- **Check ground erosion** — wind-driven sand or snow accumulates on lee sides.
- **Ask locals or check records** — prevailing wind direction and seasonal patterns vary by region.
- **Tall, exposed ridges** experience extreme wind; valleys channel and accelerate wind.

### Wind Protection Strategies

- **Position shelter on the lee side** of a ridge, large rock outcrop, or dense tree stand.
- **Avoid exposed hilltops** unless high elevation and airflow reduce insect/disease pressure.
- **Use natural windbreaks** — thick vegetation, rock formations — but leave sight lines for security.
- **Multiple small openings** oriented away from prevailing wind are safer than one large opening.

### Microclimate Effects

Small-scale variations in temperature and humidity can dramatically affect comfort:

- **Rock outcrops** absorb and radiate heat, warming nearby air at night.
- **Large bodies of water** moderate temperature swings.
- **Dense forest** reduces temperature swings and wind, but increases humidity and dampness.
- **Open areas** have greater temperature range but lower humidity.

</section>

<section id="identifying-natural-hazards">

## Identifying Natural Hazards

<div class="assessment-grid">

### Flooding

- Never build in a flood plain. Check for high-water marks on trees (dark, algae-covered bark).
- Look for debris lodged in trees or rocks — indicates past flood height.
- Ask locals about 10-year, 50-year, or 100-year flood history.
- During heavy rain, observe how water drains. Does it concentrate in certain areas?

### Rockfall and Avalanche

- **Steep slopes above shelter** = risk of rockfall or snow/debris avalanche in spring.
- Look for debris cones at slope bases or paths devoid of vegetation (avalanche paths).
- Observe fallen trees — if they're oriented downslope or snapped, the area experiences rockfall or avalanche.
- **Avoid cliff bases** entirely, especially in mountainous terrain.

### Landslides

- Hummocky or irregular ground = old landslide zone. Reactivation is possible.
- Exposed soil layers or seepage on steep slopes indicate instability.
- Ground cracks, tilted trees, or uphill-facing scarps suggest active movement.

</div>

:::danger
Never build on slopes steeper than 40 degrees, in narrow valleys prone to avalanche, or below overhanging rock formations. These offer poor shelter-to-risk ratios.
:::

### Storms and Lightning

- Avoid isolated tall trees or exposed ridges (lightning risk).
- Tall, conductive rock outcrops are also dangerous in electrical storms.
- Prefer wooded areas or natural depressions that don't attract lightning.

### Earthquakes and Volcanic Activity

- In seismic zones, avoid stiff structures (stone buildings collapse easily). Prefer flexible, low structures.
- Stay away from volcanic slopes and calderas if possible.
- In known earthquake zones, know where unstable objects are and clear them from shelter roof.

</section>

<section id="defensibility-and-security">

## Defensibility and Security

Use this section for awareness, not tactics. It helps screen for visible risk factors and practical route planning without tactical defense, concealment, or weapon guidance.

### Wildlife Defense

- **Single exit point** is a liability in wildlife movement emergencies. Prefer two workable egress routes.
- **Visibility and sightlines** to surroundings reduce surprise exposure.
- **Elevation awareness** improves observation of approach patterns.
- **Distance from attractant sites** (large game trails, carcasses, garbage) reduces visits.

:::tip
Choose a site that preserves visibility and predictable egress. The goal is safe awareness and planned movement, not tactical posturing.
:::

### Human Security

If there is elevated social approach risk:

- Keep the site observable from multiple approach vectors.
- Keep normal access routes simple, clear, and easy to leave.
- Avoid turning risk awareness into tactical plans or covert concealment.
- If intruder-risk patterns are likely, hand off to a dedicated security owner after concise alert routing.

</section>

<section id="resource-proximity-and-sustainability">

## Resource Proximity and Sustainability

Your shelter must be near materials for construction, fuel, and food.

### Firewood Availability

- Assess standing dead wood — is there a supply within 1/4 mile?
- Check if fallen deadwood is abundant or scarce.
- In temperate zones, prefer deciduous forests (hardwood burns longer); coniferous are fine for quick heat.
- Avoid areas where all nearby wood is already harvested or burned.

### Building Materials

- **Stone or rock outcrops** = foundation, walls, or fire barriers.
- **Suitable trees** (straight, no rot, appropriate size) for poles, posts, beams. Observe what's available before building.
- **Vegetation for roofing** — bark, thatch, leaves — abundant nearby?
- **Clay or soil** for mortar, plaster, or cob construction.

### Food Sources

- Proximity to productive gardening soil, wild edibles, game trails, or fishing water.
- Seasonal variation in food availability. Winter starvation is real in harsh climates.

<section id="seasonal-considerations">

## Seasonal Considerations

The best site in summer may be unlivable in winter. Evaluate year-round.

### Winter Concerns

- **Avalanche paths**: Slopes that seem safe in summer become dangerous when snow-laden.
- **Snow depth**: Exposed sites accumulate less snow but have higher wind chill. Sheltered sites get buried.
- **Water freezing**: Streams freeze solid; moving water is preferable.
- **Temperature extremes**: Valley bottoms are coldest. Higher elevations are windier but may be less affected by extreme cold.

### Summer Concerns

- **Insect breeding**: Proximity to water means mosquitoes, black flies, and disease.
- **Flooding**: Creeks that seem placid can become torrents in heavy rain or during snowmelt.
- **Heat and dehydration**: Exposed sites without shade are dangerous in extreme heat.

### Spring and Fall

- **Unstable weather**: High winds, sudden cold, erratic rain.
- **Transitional hazards**: Flooding from snowmelt, sudden frost after warm spells.

:::info-box
Aim to visit a potential shelter site in the worst season you'll occupy it. If the site is intolerable in winter, it's a poor choice, no matter how pleasant it is in summer.
:::

</section>

## Site Assessment Checklist

Use this checklist to evaluate a potential shelter site systematically:

**Terrain:**
- [ ] Slope angle and aspect suitable?
- [ ] Drainage test hole passed (soil not waterlogged)?
- [ ] Elevation matches goals (high for visibility, mid-slope for drainage)?
- [ ] Vegetation indicators healthy?

**Water:**
- [ ] Reliable water source within 1/2 mile?
- [ ] Water appears uncontaminated?
- [ ] Water accessible year-round (or alternative identified)?
- [ ] Site is not downwind of stagnant water?

**Wind and Microclimate:**
- [ ] Natural windbreaks present or easily created?
- [ ] Shelter is on lee side of obstacles?
- [ ] Microclimate suitable (not a frost pocket or heat sink)?

**Hazards:**
- [ ] No flood plain history or high-water marks?
- [ ] No rockfall or avalanche paths above?
- [ ] No signs of landslide or instability?
- [ ] Safe distance from lightning-prone features?

**Resources:**
- [ ] Firewood available within 1/4 mile?
- [ ] Building materials accessible?
- [ ] Food sources or gardening potential nearby?

**Defensibility awareness:**
- [ ] Good visibility and sightlines?
- [ ] Multiple egress routes if needed?
- [ ] Wildlife approach and approach-risk observed without tactical actions?

**Seasonal:**
- [ ] Site visited in worst season?
- [ ] Winter/summer hazards assessed?
- [ ] Year-round resource availability confirmed?

## Conclusion

Thorough site assessment takes time but prevents catastrophic mistakes. Invest a week or more scouting before committing to a location. Visit at different times of day, during different weather, and if possible, in different seasons. Talk to locals, observe vegetation, test the soil, and think through worst-case scenarios.

The best shelter site is one that balances safety, resource access, and long-term livability. It is rarely the most beautiful location or the easiest to reach — it is the one that will keep you alive and healthy across all seasons.
