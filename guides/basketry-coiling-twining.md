## Overview

Basketry is one of humanity's oldest crafts—baskets appear in the archaeological record earlier than pottery. A basket is a simple technology: flexible plant materials interlaced or coiled to create a lightweight, durable container. Unlike clay (which requires fire) or metal (which requires smelting), baskets can be made with only a knife and the plant materials at hand.

There are two primary basketry techniques: coiling (where a long, flexible foundation rope is spiraled and stitched together) and twining (where two or more strands are twisted around rigid vertical warp strands). A third technique, plaiting (weaving), creates strong, regular patterns.

This guide covers all three methods, material preparation, and finishing techniques.
If you still need to turn raw fiber into cordage, strips, or other ready-to-use supports first, use `basketry-cordage` and come back here for the construction steps.

## Coiled Basketry: Foundation and Stitching

Coiling is the simplest basketry technique and produces strong, attractive baskets.

### Foundation Materials

The foundation is the core rope that is coiled and stitched together to form the basket's walls and bottom.

**Material choices:**

1. **Willow rods or branches:** Cut branches 1/4 inch diameter, soaked until pliable.
2. **Rushes:** Long, hollow plant stems, bundled together to create a rope-like core.
3. **Cordage:** Plant fiber rope or twine; if you need to make it from raw fibers first, see `basketry-cordage`.
4. **Straw or dried grass:** Bundled tightly and wrapped with finer plant material to hold the bundle shape.
5. **Pine needles:** Bundled together; produces fine, tight coils suitable for small baskets.

**Foundation rope size:**

- Small baskets (4–8 inches diameter): 1/4 to 3/8 inch diameter foundation.
- Large baskets (12+ inches diameter): 1/2 to 3/4 inch diameter foundation.

### Stitching Materials (Weft)

The stitching material wraps around the coil, binding it to the previous coil and holding the foundation rope's spiral shape.

**Materials:**

1. **Flat strips:** Woven from plant material:
   - Willow strips (split fresh willow rods and dried).
   - Reed strips (split from dried reeds).
   - Ash splint (split white ash wood into thin, flexible strips).

2. **Cordage:** Plant fiber yarn or twine.

3. **Sinew:** Dried animal tendon, traditional material for basket stitching.

**Width and thickness:**

- Fine stitching: 1/8 to 1/4 inch wide strips (produces tight, neat baskets).
- Coarse stitching: 3/8 to 1/2 inch wide strips (faster production, more rustic appearance).

### Coiling Process

1. **Start the center:** Begin with a small coil of the foundation material, coiling it into a flat spiral (about 2–3 inches diameter).

2. **First stitches:** Thread the stitching material through the center coil, looping it around both the new foundation rope (forming the next coil) and the previous coil to join them. Each stitch should be tight and regular.

3. **Continue coiling:** As you stitch, continuously bring a new coil of foundation material next to the previous one, stitching to join them as you go.

4. **Stitch patterns:**
   - **Figure-8 stitch:** The most common. The stitch goes over the foundation rope and through the previous coil in a pattern that locks the coils together firmly.
   - **Lazy squaw stitch:** A simpler stitch used for baskets where structure is less critical. The stitching material is simply looped around the foundation rope and the previous coil.

5. **Building height:** As you complete each spiral round, begin the next round on top of the previous one (rather than continuing outward). This builds up the side walls of the basket.

6. **Shaping:** To create a curved sides (typical for most baskets), gradually decrease how far out each coil extends. The spiral becomes tighter near the rim.

### Finishing the Rim

When the basket reaches the desired height, finish the rim to prevent unraveling and add structural rigidity.

1. **Add a border:** The last coil may be reinforced with a rigid material (a willow rod or splint) inserted into the final coils.

2. **Bind the edge:** Stitch tightly around the rim, integrating the border material into the stitching pattern.

3. **Tuck or finish the stitching material:** The last stitching material should be tucked under previous stitches and secured (not left as a loose end).

## Twined Basketry: Warp and Weft

Twining creates a different structure where weft strands (plural) are twisted around stationary warp strands, creating an interlocking pattern.

### Warp and Weft Concept

**Warp:** Rigid vertical elements that form the basket's frame. Typically:
- Willow rods (1/4 inch diameter)
- Bamboo splits
- Reed splits
- Wooden dowels

**Weft:** Flexible strands twisted around the warp. Typically:
- Willow strips
- Reed strips
- Ash splint
- Plant fiber cordage

### Preparing the Warp Frame

1. **Gather warps:** Cut rigid warp strands to length (example: 18 inches for a 6-inch-tall basket plus handle allowance).

2. **Bundle arrangement:** Arrange warps radiating from a central point (like spokes on a wheel). This creates a flat bottom.
   - For a circular basket: 8–16 warps arranged radially.
   - For an oval or rectangular basket: arrange warps in parallel rows.

3. **Secure the base:** Either:
   - Bind the warps tightly at the center with cord, or
   - Insert warps through slots in a wooden base board.

4. **Spread the warps:** Carefully spread the warp ends outward, preparing for twining.

### Twining Process

1. **Begin twining at the base:** Starting near the center with two weft strands twisted together.

2. **Twist the weft:** Take two weft strands, separate them, and cross them over and under the warp strands, twisting as you go.
   - Weft strand 1 goes over warp 1, under warp 2.
   - Weft strand 2 (twisted with strand 1 in the space between warps) goes over warp 2, under warp 3.
   - Continue this over-under pattern, and the weft strands naturally twist together between warps.

3. **Build upward:** Continue the twining pattern in progressive rows, moving upward to form the basket's side walls.

4. **Add new weft:** As a weft strand ends (runs out), overlap the end of a new weft strand and continue twining.

5. **Shaping:** To create a basket shape:
   - Gradually angle the warp strands outward and upward as you twine.
   - Use angled stitches to guide the shaping.

### Multi-Strand Twining Variations

**Three-strand twining:**

Using three weft strands instead of two creates a thicker, more visible pattern and stronger fabric. The three strands are twisted around the warp in a sequence, creating a braided appearance.

**Four-strand twining (decorative):**

Used for decorative bands on baskets. Creates a complex interlocking pattern.

## Plaited and Woven Patterns

Plaiting (weaving) is another technique where strands are interlaced in a regular pattern, creating a tight, strong fabric.

### Simple Plaiting

**Two-strand plaiting (over-under weave):**

1. Arrange vertical strands (warps) in parallel rows (e.g., 8 vertical strands).
2. Starting at the bottom, weave a horizontal strand (weft) through the vertical strands, going over one and under the next, alternating.
3. Advance to the next row and weave the next weft, but offset the over-under pattern so it creates a checkerboard effect.
4. Continue until the basket is complete.

**Result:** A flat, tightly woven surface with strength in both directions.

### Complex Plaiting Patterns

**Three-color patterns:**

Using three colors of weft strands creates complex geometric designs. This is decorative and was historically used to distinguish baskets or indicate ownership.

**Herringbone pattern:**

Weft strands are angled (alternating left and right), creating a distinctive V-shaped pattern.

**Diagonals:**

Weft strands are woven at angles, creating diagonal lines across the basket.

## Material Preparation and Harvesting

The quality of your materials directly affects the finished basket.

:::note
This section is the basket-relevant harvest and prep summary. For full cordage processing, retting, stripping, splitting, and storage workflows, use `basketry-cordage`.
:::

### Willow

**Harvesting:**

1. Cut willow branches in late winter or early spring (when sap is rising but leaves haven't fully emerged).
2. Select straight branches 1/4 to 3/4 inch diameter.
3. Cut close to the base so the tree will regrow.

**Preparation:**

1. **Fresh use:** Fresh willow is pliable and can be used immediately.
2. **Soaking:** Soak for 12–24 hours to soften dried willow before using.
3. **Stripping:** Strip bark from soaked willow by holding the branch and pulling it through a strap or against a sharp blade. The bark separates easily when soaked. The white wood beneath is revealed.
4. **Drying:** Dry stripped or whole branches in a dry place for several weeks before storing long-term.

### Reeds and Rushes

**Harvesting:**

1. Cut reeds or rushes in late summer after growth slows.
2. Dry completely (2–4 weeks) in a dry, shaded location.

**Preparation:**

1. **Splitting:** Dried reeds can be split into thinner strips using a sharp knife or a specialized reed splitter.
2. **Soaking:** Before use, soak dried material for 1–2 hours to make it pliable.

### Ash Splint

**Harvesting:**

1. Select white ash trees (Fraxinus americana) with straight, unbranched trunks.
2. Cut a tree or large branch 3–4 inches diameter.

**Preparation (traditional method):**

1. **Soak:** Soak the log in water for several weeks (ideally through summer).
2. **Pound:** Strike the log with a hammer to loosen the growth rings.
3. **Split:** Remove rings by hand or lever, peeling them from the log.
4. **Scrape:** Remove the inner bark and outer heartwood from the white sapwood rings.
5. **Dry:** Dry the thin rings, which naturally curl into tube shapes.
6. **Flatten and split further:** Once dry, soak again briefly and flatten under weight, then split into strips (1/4 to 1/2 inch wide) using a knife.

### Pine Needles

**Harvesting:**

1. Collect fallen pine needles (ideally recently fallen, still flexible).
2. Or gather fresh needles from tree pruning.

**Preparation:**

1. **Dry slightly:** If too fresh, needles are sticky and hard to handle. Dry in sun for a few hours.
2. **Bundle:** Gather into bundles and bind loosely.
3. **Use:** Pine needles are used as the foundation in coiled baskets, bundled tightly and stitched with finer material.

## Form Shaping and Construction

Baskets take many forms depending on their purpose.

### Basic Shapes

**Shallow bowl (open top, flat bottom):**

- Coiled basketry is ideal; the foundation spirals flat and then rises slightly.
- Used for serving food or display.

**Deep basket (open top, rounded bottom):**

- Twined basketry suits this; warps angle inward as height increases.
- Traditional for storage and carrying.

**Closed or small-mouth basket:**

- The sides angle inward as you approach the rim, reducing the opening.
- Traditional for storing grain or dried goods (closing reduces access by pests).

**Oval or rectangular baskets:**

- Can be made by twining or plaiting.
- The warp arrangement is linear (parallel rows) rather than radial.

### Controlling Shape During Construction

**For coiled baskets:**

1. **Flat bottom:** Coil tightly at the center without increasing the spiral diameter.
2. **Outward curve:** Gradually increase how far each coil extends outward from the center.
3. **Vertical walls:** Once the bottom is complete, bring coils vertically upward without lateral extension.
4. **Inward curve (closing):** Gradually angle coils inward to narrow the opening at the rim.

**For twined baskets:**

1. **Spread warps:** Angle warp strands to control curvature (outward angle = expanded sides; inward angle = closing shape).
2. **Weft pattern:** Dense twining (rows close together) creates rigidity; sparse twining allows more flexibility.

## Handles, Rims, and Structural Reinforcement

Functional baskets need strong handles and well-defined rims.

### Handle Construction

**Single handle (for small baskets):**

1. Select two strong warp ends on opposite sides of the basket (or add new warps if needed).
2. Allow them to extend above the rim (typically 6–12 inches above for a comfortable grip).
3. Bind the handle together using the same stitching material as the basket, wrapping it around both strands repeatedly as you move from base to tip.
4. Curve the handle gently into an arch shape as you bind.

**Double handles (for larger baskets):**

1. Add or select four warps (two on each side).
2. Bind pairs together from opposite sides, creating two separate arched handles.

**Wrapped handle:**

1. Once the basic handle structure is in place, wrap it with finer material (thin strips or cordage) in decorative patterns.
2. Wrapping both reinforces and decorates.

### Rim Finishing

**Rolled rim (for coiled baskets):**

1. After the final coil is stitched, bend it back on itself to create a doubling effect.
2. Stitch the bent coil to the previous coil, securing it firmly.
3. This creates a reinforced, comfortable rim.

**Bound rim (for twined baskets):**

1. After the final twining row, the warp ends extend above the rim.
2. Bend the warp ends inward and downward, interlacing them with the side walls.
3. Bind them with cordage or fine plant material, creating a decorative and structural border.

**Splint rim (rigid reinforcement):**

1. For baskets that will bear weight or stress, add a rigid splint (flat strip of wood) inserted into the final coils or rows.
2. Bind it tightly with stitching material.
3. This prevents the rim from deforming.

## Waterproofing and Interior Sealing

Some baskets are used to hold liquids or wet goods, requiring waterproofing.

### Pitch Waterproofing

**Traditional method using pitch (resin):**

Pitch is a sticky, waterproof substance derived from tree resin or coal tar.

1. **Source pitch:** Collect resin from trees (pine pitch from cuts or cracks), or obtain pitch from a traditional supplier or by rendering. See Food Preservation guide for detailed pitch rendering.

2. **Heat the pitch:** Warm pitch over gentle heat (it should be fluid but not smoking) in a dedicated pot (don't use cookware).

3. **Apply:** Once the basket is complete and dry, carefully brush melted pitch onto the interior surfaces, paying special attention to seams and the bottom.

4. **Coat evenly:** One to three coats provide increasing waterproofing. Each coat should be thin (excess pitch becomes sticky and collects debris).

5. **Dry:** Allow each coat to cool and set (several hours) before applying the next.

**Properties:**

- Very effective waterproofing.
- Slightly stiff feeling (pitch-coated baskets are less flexible).
- Darkens the interior (pitch is dark brown or black).
- Can become sticky in very hot weather.

### Natural Oil Waterproofing

**Mild waterproofing using linseed oil or walnut oil:**

1. Apply food-safe oil (linseed or walnut) to the interior with a brush.
2. Multiple thin coats provide gradual waterproofing.
3. Less waterproof than pitch but maintains the basket's flexibility.
4. Suitable for baskets holding dry goods where minor moisture is acceptable.

### Cloth Lining

**For baskets holding delicate items or food:**

1. Line the interior with linen or cotton cloth.
2. Glue or sew the cloth to the interior basket walls.
3. Provides waterproofing and protection from splinters.

:::info-box
Pitch-waterproofed baskets were historically used for food storage, water carrying, and cooking (heated stones were placed in baskets with water to boil it). The pitch prevents leakage while the basket's flexibility allows the base to flex under load.
:::
