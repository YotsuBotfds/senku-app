<section id="loom-construction">

## Loom Construction

A loom is framework holding parallel threads (warp) under tension, allowing perpendicular threads (weft) to be woven through. Simple designs can be constructed from wood and rope.

### Backstrap Loom (Most Portable):

Simplest loom design: two wooden bars, one attached to fixed structure, other to weaver's waist via strap. Space between bars creates weaving width (typically 30-60cm). Suitable for narrow fabrics (belts, trim) or larger cloths in sections. Minimal materials needed; portable for traveling weavers.

### Frame Loom (Most Practical):

1.  **Build frame:** Assemble sturdy wooden frame (4x1 meter typical) with cross-supports every 30cm
2.  **Warp bars:** Two horizontal bars at top and bottom of frame, 3cm diameter hardwood
3.  **Create tension:** Drill holes through frame uprights; insert wedges to adjust warp tension
4.  **Warp path:** Wrap cordage around front/back of warp bars, creating 40-80 warp threads at even spacing (1-2cm apart)
5.  **Shed sticks:** Insert smooth hardwood stick perpendicular to warp (separates threads for easy shuttle passage)

### Upright Loom (Vertical, Large-Scale):

Tall wooden frame (1.5-2m height) with horizontal warp bars top and bottom. Used for wide fabrics and tapestries. More complex setup but produces full-size cloth (2-3 meters wide possible). Typical in medieval and traditional textile production.

</section>

<section id="warping">

## Warping & Setup

### Warp Preparation:

1.  **Calculate length:** Cloth length + 20cm extra for tension adjustment + 10cm for waste
2.  **Measure yarn:** Use measuring device (pegs or frame) to ensure uniform warp length
3.  **Spacing:** Mark warp bar at 1-2cm intervals (guides thread placement)
4.  **Threading:** Insert warps from side to side, keeping even tension throughout
5.  **Tension:** Properly tensioned warp should have slight resistance when pressed (not slack, not overly tight)

### Heddle Creation (Weaver's Shortcut):

Create heddle bar: wooden rod with loops of cordage attached (one loop per warp thread). Heddle bar lifts alternate warps, creating automatic shed (separation) when raised. Eliminates manual thread lifting for every pass.

### Shed Stick:

Insert flat stick perpendicular to warps. Creates natural division: threads above stick alternate with threads below when stick is rotated.

</section>

<section id="plain-weave">

## Plain Weave Basics

Simplest and most fundamental weave: weft passes over and under alternating warp threads. All other weaves variations of this principle.

### Weaving Process:

1.  **Create shed:** Raise heddle (or lift alternate warps manually), creating opening for shuttle
2.  **Pass shuttle:** Pass weft through shed from left to right
3.  **Beat:** Use sword (flat stick) to press weft against previously woven fabric
4.  **Reverse shed:** Lower heddle (or lift different warps), creating opposite shed
5.  **Return pass:** Pass shuttle right to left through new shed
6.  **Repeat:** Continue for desired cloth length (60-100 passes per minute typical rate)

### Tension & Consistency:

Uniform tension produces even cloth. Loose tension creates loose fabric; tight tension creates dense fabric. Tension maintained by beating (tightening weft) and keeping warp stretch consistent. Experienced weavers produce cloth with minimal tension variation.

### Finishing Weft:

Change color/yarn thickness intentionally for visual interest. Stripes easily created by changing weft color every 5-10 passes. Pattern emerges from color changes and weaving variations.

</section>

<section id="advanced-weaves">

## Advanced Weave Patterns

### Twill Weave:

Weft passes over 2+ warp threads instead of 1. Creates diagonal line pattern. Common variation: 2-over-2-under produces 45-degree diagonals. Twill is stronger than plain weave (superior fabric for clothing and wear). More complex threading but produces superior cloth.

### Satin Weave:

Float (passes over multiple warps without interlacing). Example: weft over 4 warps, under 1. Creates smooth, lustrous surface. Requires 4-8 harnesses (manual control possible but complex). Satin weaves are delicate (loose floats catch and snag) but produce beautiful cloth.

### Tapestry Weave:

Weft is thicker than warp; weft completely covers warp in final fabric. Used for pictorial designs (pattern created by changing weft colors). Labor-intensive but produces image-bearing cloth. Decorative rather than functional.

### Basket Weave:

Multiple threads woven as one unit (2-2 basket: pairs of warp and weft). Creates checkered appearance. Balanced weave produces square fabric units.

</section>

## Natural Dyes

Survival context emphasizes natural dyes from available plants. Traditional dyes produce colors varied by mordant (chemical fixative) and dye plant used.

### Common Natural Dyes:

### Dyeing Process (Cold Dye):

1.  **Prepare dye bath:** Simmer dye plant material 1-2 hours; strain to collect liquid
2.  **Mordant yarn:** Soak yarn in mordant solution (alum, tannin, or iron) for 30 minutes to 1 hour
3.  **Dye:** Add pre-mordanted yarn to dye bath; simmer gently 30-60 minutes
4.  **Cool:** Allow yarn to cool in dye bath (improves color absorption)
5.  **Rinse:** Rinse in cool water until water runs clear
6.  **Dry:** Hang to dry; color develops fully after drying

### Color Variation Techniques:

-   **Different mordants:** Same dye + alum produces yellow, alum + tannin produces brown, alum + iron produces greenish
-   **Temperature:** Hotter dye bath produces darker color; cooler bath lighter shade
-   **Dye concentration:** More plant material produces deeper color
-   **Layering:** Dye same yarn in two different dyes sequentially for new colors (yellow + blue = green)

<section id="finishing">

## Finishing Techniques

### Washing:

First washing removes dust and excess dye. Use cool water with mild soap (soap made from guides above). Gentle agitation only—avoid rough handling. Dry flat or hanging to maintain shape.

### Fulling (Felting):

Hot water + agitation causes fibers to entangle and lock together. Creates denser, warmer fabric. Suitable for wool and animal fibers; not effective on plant fibers. Process: alternate hot water soaking and gentle wringing, 5-10 cycles until desired density achieved.

### Calendering (Smoothing):

Press fabric between heavy boards or with hot iron (traditional: heated stones). Creates smooth, glossy surface. Temporary effect on plant fibers (fades with washing). Useful for special occasions or fine cloth.

### Napping:

Brush fabric surface to raise fibers. Creates soft, fuzzy surface. Done after fulling. Use coarse brush or carded brush and stroke in one direction (up the nap). Provides insulation and visual interest.

</section>

<section id="fabric-types">

## Fabric Types & Uses

### Linen (Flax Fiber):

Crisp, cool to touch, durable, strong when wet (improves with washing). Ideal for: hot climates, clothing, table linens. Plain weave most common. Medium difficulty to weave; requires consistent tension.

### Wool (Sheep Fiber):

Warm, felts easily, resilient, absorbs moisture while maintaining warmth. Ideal for: cold climates, blankets, outer clothing. Twill and plain weaves common. Relatively easy to weave; fiber properties forgiving of tension variation.

### Cotton (Seed Fiber):

Soft, absorbent, durable, reasonably easy to grow in warm climates. Ideal for: warm climates, underwear, general clothing. Requires ginning (separation from seed) before spinning. Plain and twill weaves common.

### Nettle/Bast Fibers:

Strong, fine, historically used for fine linen. Labor-intensive to process (retting required). Results in exceptional durability and sheen. Requires skilled spinning and weaving.

</section>

<section id="common-mistakes">

## Common Mistakes & Solutions

### Mistake 1: Uneven Warp Tension

**Error:** Some warp threads slack, others tight. Results: cloth is uneven width, selvedges wavy, tension difficult to manage.

**Solution:** Check tension before weaving begins. Adjust individual warps as needed. Maintain consistent beat (pressure when closing shed) to preserve tension during weaving.

### Mistake 2: Skipping Warps or Threading Errors

**Error:** Missing warp threads during warping or threading shuttles incorrectly. Results in holes or distortion in finished cloth.

**Solution:** Double-check warp count before beginning. Verify shuttle path each pass. For large projects, mark loom every 10cm for thread count verification.

### Mistake 3: Improper Dye Mordant Preparation

**Error:** Insufficient mordant or wrong chemical causes dye to wash out quickly or colors to remain dull.

**Solution:** Measure mordant precisely. Allow adequate soaking time (1+ hours). Use recommended mordants for each dye plant.

### Mistake 4: Over-Beating

**Error:** Pressing weft too tightly, creating dense cloth that becomes stiff and brittle.

**Solution:** Beat with gentle, consistent pressure. Feel fabric as it develops—proper beat produces cloth with balanced density and drape.

</section>

<section id="production-speed">

## Production Efficiency & Speed

Weaving speed increases dramatically with practice. Practical rates to expect:

| Experience Level | Plain Weave Speed | Notes |
|------------------|------------------|-------|
| Complete beginner (first week) | 10-20 passes/minute | Slow, deliberate, frequent corrections |
| Beginning proficiency (month 1-2) | 30-50 passes/minute | Gaining rhythm; fewer errors |
| Intermediate (month 3-6) | 60-100 passes/minute | Automatic hand motions; fast rhythm |
| Skilled (year 1+) | 120-150 passes/minute | Professional pace; minimal waste |

**Output estimate:** A skilled weaver produces ~1-2 meters of plain weave cloth per day of 8-hour work.

:::tip
Economy of motion matters. Every wasted gesture slows production. Position loom at waist height, seat yourself comfortably, arrange materials within arm's reach. Practice "flowing" motions rather than sudden jerks. After months, weaving becomes meditative and efficient.
:::

### Calculating Cloth Yield

How much finished cloth from given fiber supply?

- **Warp waste:** 15-20% lost to setup/tension compensation
- **Weaving waste:** 2-5% for breaks/errors
- **Processing waste:** Fulling, rinsing, drying reduce by 10-15%

Example: 10 kg flax fiber → 8.5 kg after carding/spinning → 6.8 kg warp setup (20% waste) → 6.46 kg after weaving → 5.5 kg finished cloth after processing.

Estimate conservatively: 1 kg raw fiber ≈ 0.55 kg finished fabric.

<section id="specialty-weaves">

## Specialized Weaving Techniques

### Tapestry Weaving (Pictorial)

Create pictures in cloth using technique: weft covers warp entirely, image made by changing weft color.

**Technique:**
1. Set up warp vertically (upright loom best)
2. Use very thick weft, often thicker than warp
3. Change colors to create patterns: red weft over warps 1-5, blue weft over warps 6-12
4. Build image by layering colors, shading by varying color density
5. Each row of weft multiple passes to cover warp completely

**Challenges:** Labor-intensive (small image might take weeks). Requires artistic ability to plan design. High-value product (tapestries historically expensive trade goods).

### Damask Weave

Combines twill and satin in complex patterns. Requires threading loom with multiple harnesses (8+ typical). White damask: woven on plain cotton, pattern creates areas of reflection difference (satin weave areas reflect light, plain areas don't), creating visual pattern despite single color.

Pattern emerges from weave structure only—requires complex threading setup. Suitable for advanced weavers with 4+ harnesses available.

### Jacquard & Brocade Techniques

Traditional jacquard uses mechanical loom with punch-card system (predecessor to computer punch cards). For low-tech alternative:

**Hand-manipulated Brocade:** Inlay extra colored yarns during weaving to create pattern. Requires: main plain weave, plus secondary colored threads inlaid at specific rows. Labor-intensive but creates ornate effect.

### Warp-Ikat Tie-Dyeing

Create patterns by tying warp threads before weaving:

1. After warping, tie off sections of warp with thread (tight wrapping)
2. Dye entire warp (dye cannot penetrate tied sections)
3. Untie after dyeing—creates pattern of dyed/undyed stripes
4. Weave normally—pattern visible in finished cloth

Result: striped or complex patterns determined by tie pattern. Variable but beautiful.

</section>

<section id="yarn-preparation">

## Yarn Preparation & Sizing

### Carding & Combing

Before spinning, raw fiber must be prepared:

**Carding:** Card fibers (like hair brushes in pairs) to align fibers randomly. Creates uniform texture, removes tangles and debris. Hand cards (~15×20 cm with wire teeth) sufficient for small quantity. Work-intensive but necessary for smooth yarn.

**Combing:** Fine fibers (flax, wool blend) benefit from combing with fine-tooth comb after carding. Produces very smooth yarn, removes short fibers, improves uniformity.

### Spinning Techniques

**Spindle spinning:** Drop spindle (weighted stick) rotates, twisting fibers. Hand feeds fiber. Portable, requires no equipment. Slow (~50 meters per hour for beginner).

**Spinning wheel:** Foot-powered rotation creates twist faster. Wheel circumference determines gear ratio. Typical wheel: 0.5-1 kg fiber per hour output. More efficient than spindle but requires equipment.

### Yarn Sizing (Thickness Standardization)

Thickness consistency matters for even weaving. Establish standard:

1. **Measure final yarn:** Weigh 10 meters of your standard yarn
2. **Create gauge:** Write down: "Standard weft yarn = X grams per 10 meters"
3. **Consistency check:** Periodically measure new yarn—if heavier or lighter than standard, adjust spinning/materials

Standardized yarn allows weavers to estimate project requirements and produces even cloth.

### Sizing & Starch Preparation

**Warp sizing** (applying starch/glue to warp threads) helps prevent breakage during weaving:

1. Prepare sizing paste: mix starch (or gelatin, egg white) with water to thin glue consistency
2. Apply to warp: dip warped threads in sizing, let dry partially
3. Benefits: threads less likely to break, easier to maintain even tension
4. Drawback: sizing must be washed out after weaving (adds processing step)

Traditional approach: light sizing on flax/linen (strong fiber, needs less starch); heavier sizing on wool (weaker fibers benefit more).

</section>

<section id="loom-building-detail">

## Advanced Loom Construction

### Heddle Frame Construction (for faster weaving)

Manual shed creation (lifting individual warps) is slow. Build heddle frame:

1. Create rectangular frame from hardwood: 60×30 cm typical, 4 cm deep
2. Drill holes along length at 2 cm spacing (for 60 cm width, ~30 holes)
3. Insert dowels or cords through holes (one per hole)
4. Tie small loop of string to each dowel (one loop per warp thread)
5. Thread one warp thread through each loop
6. Lifting frame lifts alternating warps automatically

**Result:** One person can operate loom easily—lift heddle, pass weft, lower heddle, reverse shed without manual manipulation.

### Loom Sizing Calculation

For planned cloth: determine required warp length and width.

**Width:** Warp threads = desired finished width / thread spacing (typically 2 cm).
Example: 1 meter finished width ÷ 2 cm spacing = 50 warp threads.

**Length:** Finished cloth length × (1 + 0.20) for setup waste + 0.10 for tension compensation.
Example: 2 meter cloth = 2 × 1.3 = 2.6 meters warp length.

Build loom with 20% oversize (larger than required) to allow for adjustment.

### Tension Adjustment Mechanism

Proper tension critical for even weaving:

**Method 1 - Wedges:** Warp bar held by wedges; drive wedges in/out to adjust tension. Simple, effective, but crude.

**Method 2 - Screw system:** Warp bar threaded onto screws at both ends. Rotating screws in/out adjusts tension precisely. More complex but superior control.

**Method 3 - Weight system:** Warp bar connected to weights hanging from frame. Increasing weight increases tension. Elegant but requires significant weight (20-40 kg typical).

Choose based on available materials and precision required.

</section>

<section id="safety-tips">

## Safety & Physical Considerations

### Repetitive Strain

Weaving involves repetitive motions. Prevent injury:

- **Breaks:** Every hour of weaving, take 10-minute break. Stretch arms, shoulders, neck
- **Posture:** Sit upright; loom height should be comfortable (waist-level ideal). Poor posture causes pain after hours
- **Technique:** Efficient motion reduces strain. Jerky movements tire muscles faster
- **Rotation:** Vary tasks—don't weave 8 hours daily. Mix with spinning, dyeing, finishing other days

### Chemical Safety (Dyes & Mordants)

Many traditional mordants are toxic:

- **Alum:** Generally safe; skin irritant if concentrated
- **Iron (ferrous sulfate):** Toxic if ingested; skin irritant; wear gloves
- **Tannin:** Irritant; adequate ventilation needed
- **Mercury compounds (historic):** Highly toxic—never use

**Safe practice:**
- Wear gloves when handling mordant/dye baths
- Adequate ventilation (outdoors preferred)
- Keep children away from dye baths and heating
- Never taste or touch dye bath without gloves
- Dispose of dye baths away from drinking water sources

### Fire Safety (Heating Dye Baths)

Open flame under dye pots is hazard:

- Use stable tripod/fire pit setup
- Never leave heating dye bath unattended
- Keep water bucket nearby for emergency
- Ensure pot is stable and won't tip
- Allow cooling before moving pots

<section id="production-economics">

## Weaving Production Economics

### Time Investment per Garment:

- **Simple shirt (plain weave, 2 meters cloth):** 8-12 hours weaving + 2-3 hours spinning yarn + 1-2 hours finishing = 11-17 hours total
- **Complex fabric (twill, dyed, 3 meters):** 15-20 hours weaving + 5-8 hours spinning + 2-3 hours finishing = 22-31 hours total
- **Tapestry (pictorial, 1 meter square):** 40-80 hours weaving (pictorial work is slow)

### Material Costs (Survival Context):

Raw fiber: negligible if wild harvested. Processing costs: time (primary cost). Equipment wear: minimal for simple looms.

### Trading Value:

- **Simple cloth (1 meter plain weave):** Equivalent to 2-5 units currency (moderate value)
- **Dyed cloth (1 meter, colored yarn):** 5-10 units (labor-intensive)
- **Specialized fabric (twill, heavy):** 10-20 units (superior durability justifies premium)

### Annual Production (Single Weaver):

Working 6 hours daily, 300 days yearly:
- 1800 hours annual capacity
- Producing simple cloth: 100-200 meters/year (equivalent to 50-100 garments)
- Producing specialized cloth: 50-80 meters/year

Production limited more by yarn availability than weaving time (spinning fiber is bottleneck).

:::tip
**Efficiency Strategy:** Weaving and spinning are naturally paired. While woven cloth dries/finishes, spinner creates next batch of yarn. Continuous workflow maximizes both occupations.
:::

## Advanced Dyeing Techniques

### Resist Dyeing (Creating Patterns):

Bind/tie woven cloth before dyeing to create patterns:

1. **Tie-dye:** Tie off sections tightly with string; dye; untie to reveal undyed areas
2. **Batik:** Cover sections with wax; dye; remove wax (requires heat source and wax supply)
3. **Ikat:** Tie off individual yarn sections before weaving; pattern emerges in finished cloth

Each technique produces unique patterns; learning curve moderate (first attempts rough, improvement rapid).

### Multiple Dye Baths (Complex Colors):

Sequential dyeing achieves colors unavailable from single dye:

1. Dye yellow (weld + alum)
2. Overdye with indigo → green
3. Optional: dip in iron bath → olive or brown-green

Color control challenging; experimentation necessary to achieve desired hues.

### Mordanting Variations:

Different mordant sequences produce color variations from same dye plant:

| Dye Plant | Sequence 1 | Sequence 2 | Sequence 3 | Result |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Madder** | Alum | Alum + tannin | Alum + iron | Orange, rust, dark brown |
| **Weld** | Alum | Alum + iron | — | Yellow, greenish, olive |
| **Woad** | Ferrous sulfate | — | — | Blue (consistent) |

Maintain detailed notes of mordant sequence and resulting colors for reproducibility.

## Finished Fabric Care & Maintenance

### Wool Fabric (Animal Fiber):

- **Washing:** Cool water, mild soap, gentle agitation only
- **Drying:** Lay flat; never wring (distorts shape)
- **Storage:** Fold with moth-repellent herbs (lavender, cedar); check periodically for damage

### Linen Fabric (Plant Fiber):

- **Washing:** Hot water acceptable; becomes softer with repeated washing
- **Drying:** Hang or lay flat; slight sun exposure acceptable (unlike wool)
- **Storage:** Fold loosely; air circulation prevents mustiness

### Cloth Repair:

- **Small holes:** Patch using matching cloth; overlay and hand-stitch
- **Torn seams:** Use ladder stitch (invisible mending) for best appearance
- **Worn edges:** Bind with contrasting yarn (functional and decorative)

Well-maintained cloth lasts decades; storage quality determines longevity.

:::warning
**Storage Hazards:** Moisture causes mold; heat accelerates fiber deterioration; insects (moths, carpet beetles) damage wool and cellulose fibers. Preventive storage essential for valuable textiles.
:::

<section id="trade-goods">

## Textiles as Trade Goods

### Marketable Fabric Types:

- **Plain cloth:** Baseline value; stable demand; easy production
- **Patterned cloth:** Premium value (resist-dyed, woven patterns); higher skill required
- **Dyed yarn:** Partially-finished product; easier to transport than cloth; valued by other weavers

### Quality Markers:

Buyers (especially in survival contexts) assess:
- **Cloth density:** Dense cloth appears quality (less transparent to light)
- **Evenness:** Consistent weaving throughout (visible tension control)
- **Finish:** Clean selvages, even edges (signs of care)
- **Fiber quality:** Smooth, lustrous fiber (well-processed, well-spun)

Reputation important: consistent quality = premium pricing and repeat customers.

### Packaging & Presentation:

- Roll cloth tightly (prevents wrinkling)
- Wrap in clean cloth or paper (protects against dust)
- Display sample showing weave pattern and color
- Tag with fiber type, weave, and care instructions (if trading at distance)
