<section id="identification">

## Plant Identification

Correct identification is critical. Wrong plant identification can be fatal — for detailed information on managing plant poisoning emergencies, see <a href="../toxicology.html">Toxicology & Poison Management</a>. Use multiple identification features: leaf shape, stem characteristics, flowers, root structure, smell, and habitat.

### Identification Features to Document

-   Leaf arrangement (opposite, alternate, whorled)
-   Leaf shape (oval, lanceolate, serrated, smooth)
-   Stem characteristics (smooth, hairy, woody, herbaceous)
-   Flowers (color, number of petals, arrangement)
-   Fruit or seed characteristics
-   Root type (taproot, fibrous, rhizome)
-   Habitat (wet, dry, shaded, sunny)
-   Season when found
-   Height and growth pattern

### Identification Safety Rule

If identification uncertain in ANY way, do not use. Many toxic plants resemble edible ones. Example: Poison hemlock appears similar to wild carrot and parsnip. When in doubt, go without rather than risk poisoning.

### Dichotomous Key Use

Field guides with dichotomous keys provide reliable identification through sequential yes/no questions about plant features. Cross-reference multiple keys to confirm identification. Never rely on single source for identification.

<section id="preparation">

## Preparation Methods

Different preparation methods extract active compounds efficiently. Selection depends on herb type and desired effect.

### Tea Infusion

Steeping method for leaves and flowers. Use 1 teaspoon dried herb per cup of boiling water. Steep 5-10 minutes. Strain through cloth. Drink warm or cooled. Tastes better than other methods. Best for: chamomile, peppermint, willow bark, plantain. Infusions stay fresh 24 hours in cool conditions.

### Decoction

Boiling method for roots, bark, and hard woody parts. Place 1 teaspoon dried herb in pot with 1 cup water. Bring to boil, simmer 10-20 minutes. Strain. More potent than infusions, extracts compounds not soluble in hot water alone. Best for: ginger, willow bark, hawthorn. Stronger taste than infusions.

### Tincture Production

Alcohol extraction for concentrated preparation. Fill jar with fresh herb (or 1/3 jar with dried herb), cover completely with alcohol (vodka, moonshine, any 40%+ alcohol). Seal jar, store dark location. Shake daily. After 2-4 weeks, strain through cloth. Tinctures last months to years. Dosage: 20-30 drops in water, 2-3 times daily. Most concentrated preparation method.

### Poultice Application

Direct application to skin for wounds, inflammation. Mash fresh herb into paste using mortar and pestle (or between stones). Apply directly to affected area. Cover with cloth. Change every 2-4 hours. Poultices draw inflammation, kill bacteria, promote healing. Best for: plantain, comfrey, calendula.

### Salve/Ointment Creation

Herb-infused oil mixed with wax for skin application. Warm oil (coconut, animal fat, or rendered plant oil), add crushed herb, simmer gently 1-2 hours. Strain through cloth. Mix with melted beeswax or plant wax at 3:1 oil to wax ratio. Cool in containers. Salves protect wounds, reduce infection risk, moisturize skin.

### Compress

Cloth soaked in herbal tea, applied to affected area. Useful for sprains, inflammation, wounds. Prepare strong infusion or decoction, soak cloth, apply while warm. Reheating allows longer use. Best for: inflammation, sprains, some wounds.

<section id="common">

## Common Medicinal Plants

### Plantain (Plantago major/minor)

One of most useful survival herbs. Leaves contain mucilage and antimicrobial compounds. Raw leaves can be chewed or made into poultice for cuts, stings, and minor wounds. Use: mash fresh leaves on wound, apply as poultice 2-4 times daily. Infusion helps with respiratory issues. Found nearly worldwide in disturbed areas. Identification: broad oval leaves in basal rosette, parallel veins, small flowers on tall spike.

### Willow Bark

Natural aspirin source. Contains salicin which converts to salicylic acid in body. Reduces fever, pain, inflammation. Prepare decoction: simmer 1 teaspoon bark 10-15 minutes. Drink 2-3 cups daily. Taste bitter. Safe for adults but NOT for children under 12 or aspirin-allergic individuals. Harvest bark in spring for best potency.

### Ginger Root

Warming herb for digestion, nausea, inflammation. Use fresh rhizomes when available or dried. Tea: grate thumb-size piece fresh ginger, steep in hot water 5-10 minutes. Reduces nausea, aids digestion. Can be eaten raw in small amounts. Warming effect useful in cold conditions.

### Chamomile

Calming herb for anxiety, insomnia, digestive upset. Dried flowers steep 5-10 minutes. Mild flavor, easy to prepare. Safe for most people. Helps induce sleep when 30-60 minutes before bed. Also helps with mild digestive issues.

### Peppermint

Digestive aid, pain relief. Fresh or dried leaves steep 5-10 minutes. Cooling effect useful in heat, warming in cold (stimulates circulation). Helps with headaches, stomach cramps, nausea. Safe for most but not large amounts during pregnancy.

### Echinacea (Purple Coneflower)

Immune support herb. Root and aerial parts used. Tincture or decoction. Drink at first sign of infection. Most effective when used early in illness. Root stronger than leaves. Use for: minor infections, common cold, wound healing.

### St. John's Wort

Mood support herb. Yellow flowers and leaves used. Requires 2-4 weeks regular use for effectiveness. Tincture or infusion. WARNING: significantly interacts with many medications. Do not use if on pharmaceuticals without medical approval.

### Yarrow

Wound healer, fever reducer, anti-inflammatory. Feathery foliage, flat flower clusters (white, pink, yellow). Poultice or infusion. Leaves can be chewed for bleeding wounds (promotes clotting). Bitter tea helps reduce fever by promoting sweating.

### Comfrey

Bone and wound healing. Root used primarily. Decoction or poultice applied to sprains, fractures, wounds. Promotes rapid healing but contains compounds that can accumulate in liver with extended use. Use externally primarily, limit internal use to 2-3 weeks maximum.

### Garlic

Antimicrobial, antibacterial. Fresh cloves most powerful. Can be eaten raw or cooked. Helps prevent infections, boost immunity. Pungent taste acceptable when raw. Cooked is milder. Use liberally for food as well as medicine.

<section id="dosing">

## Dosing and Administration

### Standard Tea Dosage

1-3 cups daily for acute conditions (colds, flu). 1 cup daily for maintenance. Spread dosage throughout day. Most herbs have narrow margin between effective and excessive dose - when uncertain, use lower doses.

### Tincture Dosage

20-40 drops in water, 2-3 times daily. Some bitter herbs can be taken straight on tongue, though less pleasant. Alcohol concentration preserves tinctures but not suitable for young children.

### Decoction Dosage

1/4 to 1/2 cup, 2-4 times daily. For strong decoctions, smaller doses. Start with smaller amounts to assess tolerance. More potent than infusions - don't assume same dosage as teas.

### Poultice Frequency

Change poultice 2-4 times daily for acute wounds. Less frequently (once daily) for chronic conditions. Cover with cloth to prevent bacterial contamination.

### Duration of Use

Acute illness: use 5-7 days then reassess. If improving, continue shorter course. If not improving, try different herb or seek additional help. Some herbs for maintenance can be used indefinitely (ginger, garlic). Others should be cycled (echinacea - 2 weeks on, 1 week off; St. John's wort - requires continuous use to be effective).

### Individual Variation

Children use 1/4 to 1/2 adult dose depending on age. Elderly may respond to smaller doses. Pregnant women avoid most herbs - consult sources before use. Body weight affects dosing - larger individuals may need slight increases.

<section id="safety">

## Safety Considerations

Herbal medicine can be powerful but requires respect and caution.

:::warning
**WARNING:** Do not use herbs as substitute for emergency medical care. Serious infections, severe wounds, or life-threatening conditions require professional medical treatment. Use herbs for support, not primary treatment of serious illness.
:::

### Toxicity Concerns

Some plants contain toxic compounds even if related species are safe. Digitalis (foxglove) contains cardiac glycosides - even small amounts can cause heart problems. Hemlock species contain neurotoxins - lethal in small amounts. Extreme care with identification prevents tragedies.

### Contamination Risks

Herbs harvested near roads accumulate heavy metals from vehicle exhaust. Herbicide or pesticide residues on non-organic herbs. Never harvest from areas treated with chemicals. Mold or bacterial growth in stored herbs causes illness. Keep herbs dry and properly stored.

### Overdose Prevention

Start with smaller doses to assess tolerance. If no adverse reaction after 2-3 uses, can increase to standard dose. Some people have sensitivities to specific herbs. Monitor for: nausea, dizziness, rash, headache, stomach upset. Stop immediately if adverse effects occur.

### Preparation Cleanliness

Use clean equipment. Sterilize containers before tincture production (boiling or alcohol rinse). Use clean water for infusions/decoctions. Improper preparation can introduce bacteria or mold.

### When to Avoid Herbal Treatment

-   Severe wounds requiring stitches or professional care
-   Signs of serious infection (fever above 39°C, spreading redness, pus)
-   Severe pain without identifiable cause
-   Suspected broken bones or internal injuries
-   Allergic reactions or anaphylaxis
-   Unconsciousness or altered mental state

## Drug Interactions

Herbal medicines interact with pharmaceuticals, sometimes dangerously. For comprehensive pharmaceutical drug interaction tables, see <a href="../medications.html">Essential Medications</a>. If person on medication, verify herb safety before use.

### High-Risk Interactions

St. John's Wort interferes with birth control effectiveness, blood thinners, antidepressants, and many other medications. Gingko interferes with blood thinners. Licorice interacts with blood pressure medications. Ginger may thin blood slightly (avoid with blood thinners). Turmeric may increase bleeding risk.

### Interaction Severity

### Safe Herb and Drug Combinations

Plantain generally safe with medications (topical use). Chamomile minimal interaction risk. Peppermint minimal risk. Garlic slight blood thinning effect (monitor with anticoagulants). When uncertain, use herbs known as food (garlic, ginger) rather than medicinal-only herbs.

### Documentation

If treating someone on medications, document any herbs used. If person receives outside medical care, inform providers of herbal treatments used. Some reactions may be attributed to pharmaceutical interactions rather than herb toxicity.

## Seasonal Harvesting

Plant potency varies by season and plant part. Harvest at peak potency for best results.

### Harvesting Schedule

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Plant Part</th><th scope="row">Best Season</th><th scope="row">Timing</th><th scope="row">Notes</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Aerial (leaves/flowers)</td><td>Spring-Summer</td><td>Just before flowering</td><td>Peak potency before flowering</td></tr><tr><td>Flowers</td><td>Summer</td><td>When fully open</td><td>Dry immediately after collection</td></tr><tr><td>Roots/Rhizomes</td><td>Fall/Spring</td><td>Fall after foliage dies</td><td>Skip flowering season</td></tr><tr><td>Bark</td><td>Spring</td><td>Sap flowing</td><td>Most potent in spring</td></tr><tr><td>Seeds</td><td>Late Summer</td><td>Just before falling</td><td>Dry completely before storage</td></tr></tbody></table>

### Harvesting Rules

-   Never harvest entire plant - leave roots to regenerate
-   Don't take more than 1/3 of plant population in area
-   Harvest sustainably - return to same location annually
-   Use sharp tools to cleanly cut (crushing damages plant)
-   Morning harvesting (after dew dries) best for potency
-   Avoid plants near roads, contamination sources

### Drying and Storage

Dry herbs in shade with good air circulation. Direct sun degrades active compounds. Hang upside down or lay on cloth. Completely dry (brittle, not bendable) before storage. Store in cool, dark, dry location in sealed containers. Properly dried herbs last 6-12 months. Discard if mold, discoloration, or off-smell develops.

### Storage Duration

Leaves/flowers: 6-12 months (less potent after 6). Roots/bark: 1-2 years. Dried teas decline in potency after 6 months. Tinctures last 2-5 years. Fresh herbs use immediately or process same day.

<section id="condition-treatment">

## Treating Common Survival Conditions

### Fever and Chills

Fevers indicate immune system fighting infection. If fever below 39°C and patient alert, supportive care is preferred over aggressive treatment. Encourage fluids (rehydration essential), rest, and light clothing (heavy coverings increase core temperature further).

Willow bark and yarrow promote sweating which reduces fever. Steep 1-2 teaspoons willow bark in hot water 10-15 minutes, drink 3-4 times daily. Fever reduction occurs within 30 minutes. Maintain hydration - sweating causes significant fluid loss.

**High fever management (above 39°C):** Cool externally with damp cloths on forehead, neck, armpits, groin (major blood vessels near surface - cooling these areas reduces core temperature). Continue herbal fever reducers. Seek advanced medical care if fever exceeds 40°C or persists beyond 3 days.

### Infection Prevention and Treatment

Infection prevention is superior to treatment. Clean all wounds immediately with boiled water, apply poultice of plantain or comfrey. Change poultices 2-3 times daily. Monitor for spreading redness (streaking up arm or leg indicates bloodstream infection - serious).

Early infection treatment: increase plantain poultices to every 2 hours, add echinacea tincture (20-30 drops 3-4 times daily), and if a wound is pus-filled or worsening, seek drainage-capable medical care rather than trying to open it yourself. For a detailed wound care protocol using herbal antimicrobials (honey, garlic, berberine), see <a href="../herbal-antibiotics.html#wound-care-protocol">Herbal Antibiotics — Wound Care Protocol</a>. For infection prevention through aseptic technique, see <a href="../infection-control.html">Infection Control</a>. If infection signs persist beyond 3 days despite aggressive herbal treatment, seek advanced medical care.

:::danger
**DANGER:** Untreated infections spread to blood and cause sepsis (life-threatening). Rapid progression to septic shock possible within 24-48 hours. Warning signs: fever above 39°C, spreading redness, lymph node swelling (under arm for arm infection, groin for leg infection), chills, altered mental state. Seek immediate medical care.
:::

### Digestive Issues

Diarrhea in survival situations causes rapid dehydration and mineral loss. Most common causes: contaminated water (despite treatment), sudden dietary changes, stress, parasites.

Mild diarrhea (3-5 times daily): drink electrolyte solution (1 liter water + 6 level teaspoons sugar + 1/2 level teaspoon salt - or use honey if available), increase peppermint or ginger tea. Avoid dairy and high-fat foods.

Severe diarrhea (10+ times daily, contains blood, accompanied by fever): rehydration becomes critical. Electrolyte replacement (salt and sugar in water) prevents dehydration-related death. Continue herbal support but prioritize fluid intake. Seek medical care if bloody diarrhea or severe cramping.

### Pain Management

Willow bark and meadowsweet provide pain relief through salicin (converts to salicylic acid internally). Most effective for muscle pain, headaches, and arthralgia. Less effective for severe trauma pain (broken bones).

Decoction preparation: simmer 1 teaspoon willow bark 10-15 minutes, drink 3 times daily. Tastes bitter - can add honey or ginger to improve palatability. Effect takes 30-45 minutes. Maximum relief at 2-3 hours post-dosing.

For severe pain (trauma, severe wounds), focus on immobilization and rest rather than relying on herbal pain relief. Splinting and elevation reduce pain effectively. Herbal teas provide psychological benefit and minor analgesia.

<section id="herb-cultivation">

## Growing Medicinal Herbs

In extended survival scenarios, cultivating common medicinal herbs ensures long-term supply. Most medicinal herbs grow easily from seed or transplant.

### Easy-to-Grow Survival Herbs

**Chamomile:** Direct sow seeds in spring, needs light to germinate (don't cover completely), germinates 7-10 days. Grows in poor soil, minimal water, full sun. Harvest flowers throughout summer.

**Peppermint:** Nearly indestructible perennial. Grows from tiny plant or root cutting. Spreads aggressively (contain in designated area). Perennial - harvests year after year once established.

**Ginger:** Requires warm climate (above 15°C). Grow from ginger rhizome piece. Takes 8-10 months to produce harvestable rhizomes. Needs consistent moisture and partial shade.

**Echinacea:** Grow from seed or transplant. Takes 2 years from seed to mature plant. Extremely hardy perennial. Drought tolerant once established. Flowers mid-summer through fall.

**Garlic:** Plant individual cloves in fall, harvest in summer. Grows in almost any condition. Minimal care required. Multiply over years - save cloves for replanting.

### Seed Collection and Storage

Collect seed heads when mature (brown, papery, seeds fall from plant). Hang upside down in paper bag to catch falling seeds. Store in cool, dry, sealed container. Most herb seeds remain viable 2-3 years with proper storage.

Willow seeds have short viability (weeks only) - use fresh. Most other herb seeds store well for extended periods if kept dry.

<section id="toxic-lookalikes">

## Dangerous Plant Lookalikes

Misidentification of plants with toxic lookalikes causes death. Learning to distinguish similar-appearing plants is critical skill.

### High-Risk Toxic Plants

**Hemlock (Conium maculatum)** - resembles wild carrot and parsnip. Difference: hemlock has hairless, blotchy purple-red spotted stems; wild carrot has hairy stems without blotches. Hemlock smell: musty, unpleasant (unlike pleasant carrot smell). **All parts deadly** - even small leaf amounts cause neurological damage and death.

**Foxglove (Digitalis purpurea)** - resembles comfrey. Difference: foxglove flowers are tubular with speckled interior; comfrey flowers are bell-shaped plain. **All parts extremely toxic** - affects heart rhythm dangerously. Tiny leaf amount can cause fatal heart arrhythmia.

**Poison parsnip (Heracleum mantegazzianum)** - resembles wild parsnip. Both are large plants with white umbrella-shaped flower clusters. Difference: poison parsnip has much larger leaves (30cm+), plant reaches 2+ meters tall; wild parsnip is smaller (1-1.5 meters) with smaller leaves. Poison parsnip sap causes severe blistering when exposed to sun.

### Identification Safety Rules

**Rule 1:** Use multiple identification sources (minimum 2 field guides showing same plant). Cross-reference all characteristics.

**Rule 2:** When uncertain, do not use. Missing one beneficial herb is safer than ingesting toxic plant.

**Rule 3:** For plants with dangerous lookalikes, collect complete plant (root, leaves, flowers, stem) for confirmation. Never use partial plant description for identification.

:::warning
**WARNING:** Plant identification errors cause tragic deaths. Poison hemlock deaths are documented from single leaf ingestion. When identification uncertain in ANY WAY, do not use plant.
:::

<section id="herbal-quality">

## Herbal Quality and Potency

Medicinal herb potency varies dramatically by growing conditions, harvest time, storage method, and preparation. Understanding quality factors ensures effective treatment.

### Potency Factors

**Growing conditions:** Herbs grown in optimal soil with adequate sunlight produce higher active compound concentrations than stressed plants. Herbs grown in poor soil may contain 30-50% less active compounds.

**Harvest timing:** Most herbs have peak potency at specific growth stages. Leaves highest potency just before flowering. Flowers at peak when fully open but before petal drop. Roots highest after foliage dies in fall. Early or late harvesting reduces potency significantly.

**Drying method:** Sun-dried herbs lose 30-40% potency compared to shade-dried (sunlight degrades active compounds). Shade drying with good air circulation optimal. Heat drying (over 40°C) damages heat-sensitive compounds. Room temperature drying best preserves potency.

**Storage conditions:** Dried herbs stored in sealed containers in cool, dark location maintain potency 6-12 months. Room temperature storage loses 20% potency per month. Humid conditions encourage mold and decay. Frozen storage (if possible) extends potency to 2+ years.

### Testing Herb Potency

Taste test indicates potency for some herbs: strong peppermint flavor indicates fresh, potent peppermint leaf. Weak, faded taste indicates degraded potency. Ginger should taste hot/spicy (pungent) - weak taste suggests age or poor drying.

Visual inspection: dried herbs should retain color (fading indicates light damage or age). Smell should be recognizable - weak smell suggests degraded potency.

### Batch Documentation

Document herbal preparations for future reference and learning. Record: date prepared, materials used (species, quantity, source), preparation method and duration, results (effectiveness, potency, issues), storage method and duration.

Over time, documentation reveals patterns: which preparation methods most effective, which plant sources highest quality, which drying techniques preserve potency best. Survival situations benefit from detailed knowledge accumulation.

**Batch marking:** Label containers with preparation date and herb type. Simple system: write on cloth tag with charcoal, attach to storage container. Facilitates tracking storage duration and rotation (use oldest batches first).

</section>

<section id="teaching-herbal-knowledge">

## Teaching Herbal Medicine to Others

In survival groups, knowledge sharing prevents loss if key person injured/dies. Systematic teaching ensures continuity.

### Documentation Methods

**Written guides:** If paper available, document key information: plant identification features (sketches, dried samples), preparation methods with ratios, dosages, safety concerns, harvesting season.

**Apprenticeship:** Direct teaching through hands-on experience. Show identification in field, assist with preparation, observe effects. Learning through doing more effective than reading.

**Collection preservation:** Maintain dried plant reference samples. When teaching identification, show reference sample alongside field plant. Students connect field appearance to dried form.

### Skill Transfer

**Basic competency level:** Teach 5-7 most common/useful herbs first (plantain, willow bark, ginger, chamomile, yarrow). Ensure multiple people can identify and prepare each.

**Advanced competency:** Once basic herbs mastered, introduce preparation variations, drug interactions, advanced plant identification, cultivation methods.

**Safety emphasis:** Repeatedly emphasize identification risks. Show toxic lookalikes. Drill students on "when in doubt, leave it out" principle.

### Succession Planning

Identify younger group members as herbal medicine apprentices. Ensure knowledge transfer before someone dies/leaves. Herbal knowledge survives generational transitions.

</section>

<section id="post-survival-integration">

## Transitioning from Herbal to Modern Medicine

Survival situations eventually end. Transitioning from herbal to pharmaceutical medicine requires awareness of interactions and gradual changes.

### Herbal-Pharmaceutical Interactions

Some herbs interact dangerously with modern pharmaceuticals. If transitioning to medical care: inform providers of all herbal medicines used during survival period. Some reactions may result from herbal-drug interactions, not drug failure.

**Critical transitions:**
- **St. John's Wort → Antidepressants:** Gradual discontinuation of St. John's Wort over 2-3 weeks (abrupt stopping causes depression relapse)
- **Willow Bark → Aspirin:** Both contain salicylates (double dosing risk). Don't take aspirin within 24 hours of willow bark
- **Garlic → Blood Thinners:** Garlic and warfarin/other anticoagulants increase bleeding risk. Coordinate with provider
- **Ginger → Blood Thinners:** Similar interaction to garlic (additive effect possible)

### Medical Provider Communication

Document herb use during survival: which herbs, dosage, duration, effects observed. Medical providers need this information for complete picture. Unexpected interactions or side effects may relate to herbal treatment rather than new medications.

### Laboratory Testing

After transition to medical setting, blood work may show lingering herbal effects (elevated liver enzymes if herbal preparations used, altered clotting time if blood-thinning herbs used). Testing informs safe pharmaceutical transitions.

</section>

<section id="extraction-science">

## Extraction Science & Potency Optimization

Different herbal compounds extract preferentially in different solvents. Understanding extraction chemistry maximizes medicinal effect.

### Water-Based Extraction

**Infusions (hot water, short duration):** Water-soluble compounds (sugars, tannins, some alkaloids, vitamins). Best for: leaves, flowers, delicate plant parts that degrade with heat.

Temperature: 100°C (boiling water). Duration: 5-15 minutes. Compounds extracted: volatile oils (lost to steam), water-soluble alkaloids, tannins, sugars.

**Decoctions (hot water, prolonged):** Harder to extract compounds (woody tissues, roots, bark). Extended heating breaks down plant cell walls, releasing more compounds.

Temperature: 100°C (simmering). Duration: 10-30 minutes. Compounds extracted: all water-soluble compounds plus some heat-stable oils.

**Cold infusions:** No heating; relies on time to dissolve compounds. Useful for heat-sensitive compounds. Duration: 2-24 hours (longer extraction time compensates for lower efficiency).

### Alcohol-Based Extraction

**Mechanism:** Ethanol (alcohol) dissolves lipophilic (fat-soluble) compounds not soluble in water. Examples: resins, essential oils, some alkaloids.

**Optimal alcohol concentration:** 40-50% ethanol best for most herbs (balances hydrophilic and lipophilic extraction). Higher concentrations (95%+) preferentially extract lipids; lower concentrations (20-30%) preferentially extract water-soluble compounds.

**Duration:** 2-4 weeks minimum. Extended time (up to 3 months) improves extraction. Tinctures last years due to alcohol preservation.

### Oil-Based Extraction

**Infused herbal oils:** Heat-extract into carrier oil (coconut, olive, animal fat). Useful for: lipophilic compounds, herbal salves/ointments, skin absorption.

Temperature: 60-80°C (never boiling—damages compounds). Duration: 1-2 hours minimum, up to 24 hours.

**Maceration:** Cold soak herbs in oil for 2-4 weeks. Slower but preserves more volatile compounds. Useful for: aromatic herbs, luxury preparations.

### Glycerin Extraction

**Glycerin (vegetable glycerin):** Sweet, viscous liquid. Extracts some compounds water and alcohol extract, but weaker. Slower extraction than alcohol.

Advantage: Glycerin is pleasant-tasting, easier for children. Disadvantage: weaker extraction (lower concentration of active compounds), shorter shelf life (6-12 months vs. 2-3 years for alcohol tinctures).

## Standardizing Herbal Dosages

Inconsistency is herbal medicine's weakness. Fresh plants vary in potency; dried plants vary based on storage. Creating consistent preparations improves reliability.

### Concentration-Based Dosing

**Principle:** Dilute concentrated extract to standard strength, then dose by volume.

**Example:** If tincture is made at 1:5 ratio (1 part herb to 5 parts alcohol), then 30 drops (approximately 1mL) contains specific amount of herb material. Scale dosing proportionally.

**Advantage:** Consistent dosing despite variation in plant potency.

**Disadvantage:** Requires precise measurements initially.

### Weight-Based Dosing

**Standard dose:** 1-3 grams dried herb per 150mL hot water (standard tea). Measure dried herb on scale if available, or estimate (roughly 1 teaspoon = 1-2 grams of dried herb).

**Consistency:** Weigh each batch of dried herb if scale available. Discard if older than 1-2 years (potency degrades significantly).

### Titration Method

Start with small doses and gradually increase until effect observed. Useful when potency unknown.

**Procedure:**
1. Take small dose (half standard dose)
2. Monitor for effect for 1-2 hours
3. If no effect, take another small dose
4. Repeat until effect observed
5. Future doses: take dose slightly less than amount that produced effect

**Advantage:** Accounts for unknown potency, individual variation.

**Disadvantage:** Time-consuming, requires multiple tests.

</section>

## Monitoring Herbal Quality & Degradation

Herbal quality declines with time, temperature, humidity, and light exposure. Monitoring and proper storage prevents waste and maintains efficacy.

### Visual Degradation Signs

**Fading color:** Loss of vibrant green, yellow, or color tone. Indicates oxidation and compound breakdown. Potency reduced 30-50%.

**Mold or visible fungi:** Growing fuzzy white/black fuzz on herbs. Indicates moisture contamination. DO NOT USE (mold toxins potentially more dangerous than original disease).

**Insect damage:** Holes, webbing, or insect presence. Indicates pest infestation. Effectiveness unknown; not recommended for consumption.

**Condensation in container:** Indicates moisture infiltration. Separates herbs into a different container with desiccant packets. Use within 1-2 weeks (accelerated degradation).

### Smell and Taste Testing

**Smell:** Strong, characteristic aroma indicates freshness. Faded or no smell indicates oxidation and potency loss. Musty or sour smell indicates mold (discard).

**Taste (tiny sample only):** Bitter herbs should taste notably bitter; bland taste indicates potency loss. Spicy herbs (ginger, cayenne) should maintain heat; faded spice indicates degradation.

### Potency Testing

If resources available: **Comparison test** - brew similar amount of suspect herb and known-fresh herb side-by-side. If suspect herb produces noticeably weaker flavor/color/effect, potency reduced.

### Storage Optimization

**Conditions:** Cool (15-20°C), dark (opaque containers), dry (desiccant packets), sealed (airtight). These conditions extend potency to: leaves/flowers 6-12 months, roots/bark 1-2 years, dried tinctures 2-3 years.

**Prevention:** Transfer to airtight container immediately upon receipt. Store away from light, heat sources, and humidity. Check monthly for deterioration.

</section>
