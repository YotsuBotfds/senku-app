## Overview

A temporary emergency shelter built for immediate survival differs fundamentally from a long-term inhabited camp. This guide covers the progression of shelter improvements across seasons, from initial basic protection to a hardened, habitable structure capable of sustaining a group through an annual cycle.

Most post-collapse groups transition through phases: **Emergency (days 1-14) → Seasonal (weeks 2-16) → Semi-Permanent (months 4-12) → Permanent (year 2+)**. Each phase involves specific upgrades and adaptations.

:::info-box
The most critical timeframe is the first year. A shelter that fails to adapt to winter, spring flooding, or summer heat will force abandonment, consuming resources and group morale. Plan systematically.
:::

## Phase 1: Emergency Shelter (Days 1-14)

### Minimum Viable Shelter

- **Purpose:** Protect from elements, deter casual intrusion
- **Time to build:** 2-4 hours for experienced team
- **Materials:** Salvaged tarps, branches, natural materials
- **Expected lifespan:** 2-4 weeks before requiring upgrade
- **Weather-first priority:** Build roof and wind-break before walls; orient entrance away from prevailing wind and storm direction; check drainage before placing floor

**Basic configuration:**
- Single-layer roof (tarp or woven branches) with 15-20% overhang
- Wind breaks on two sides (facing prevailing wind)
- Elevated floor (10-20 cm above ground, prevents water pooling and ground cold transfer)
- Single entrance facing away from wind

### Initial Site Selection Criteria

- Elevated terrain (drainage, flood mitigation)
- Near water source but not in flood zone
- Protected from prevailing wind (dense trees or terrain)
- Concealed from established trails (avoid visibility from distance)
- Space for future expansion (at least 20 m × 20 m level area)

## Phase 2: Seasonal Shelter (Weeks 2-16)

### Month 1-2: Late Spring/Early Summer Setup

**Objectives:** Establish primary structure, create work/storage areas, improve comfort

**Shelter improvements:**
- Upgrade to double-layer roof (air gap for insulation and moisture management)
- Construct frame from saplings or salvaged wood (A-frame or lean-to)
- Add walls on 3 sides (leave one side partially open for ventilation)
- Insulate floor with straw, leaves, or salvaged materials (10-15 cm depth)
- Create internal sleeping platform elevated 30-50 cm above ground

**Camp infrastructure:**
- Establish water source (cistern or gravity-fed from nearby stream)
- Create latrine 20+ meters downwind and downhill from living area
- Establish fire area with wind protection and water supply nearby
- Designate storage area for tools, food, and materials
- Create work area (with tool-hanging systems visible for organization)

**Time investment:** 3-5 full days for primary shelter and basic infrastructure

### Month 2-3: Mid-Summer Improvements

**Focus:** Ventilation, cooling, water management, security

**Shelter updates:**
- Adjust roof angle or add ridge vent (allows hot air to escape)
- Create shaded overhang (0.5-1 m roof extension on south-facing side reduces interior heat)
- Install window/door screening (salvaged cloth or woven materials) for ventilation with insect control
- Dig shallow drainage trenches around shelter perimeter (prevents water pooling)
- Improve interior air circulation (door positioned to create cross-ventilation)

**Weather resilience:**
- Inspect for leaks during rain; patch as needed
- Reinforce roof with additional guy-lines (preparation for wind/storms)
- Create water collection gutters (harvest rainwater for storage)

### Month 3-4: Early Fall Preparations

**Focus:** Winterization begins, storage preparation, security hardening

**Critical shelter upgrades:**
- Upgrade roof to thick thatch or multi-layer materials (R-value equivalent to ~10-15)
- Insulate walls with straw, leaf litter, or salvaged materials (fill cavity between frame and outer layer)
- Seal all gaps >1 cm with clay, straw mixture, or salvaged caulking
- Create interior wall insulation or hung blankets (air gap between walls and interior creates thermal buffer)
- Install proper door closure system (frame + latch, not just a flap)

**Storage construction:**
- Build covered wood shed (separate from living shelter; prevents fire hazard and dehumidifies food storage)
- Create sealed grain/food storage containers (protects from rodents and moisture)
- Establish tool storage with weather protection

:::warning
Do not delay insulation upgrades until fall. Wet insulation loses effectiveness; early installation allows material to dry and settle before heating begins.
:::

### Month 4-5: Late Fall Transition to Winter

**Focus:** Heating systems, interior comfort, final winterization

**Heating implementation:**
- Install chimney or fire structure if not already present
  - Small indoor fire (if well-ventilated): Heats interior to 15-25°C even in freezing weather
  - Central fire pit outside shelter: Radiates heat into shelter, reduces smoke inside
  - Rocket mass heater: Improvised high-efficiency heating with salvaged materials
- Create thermal mass inside shelter (mud-filled barrels or large rocks) to store heat from fire, release at night
- Establish fuel storage (covered, dry location for 6+ weeks of firewood)

**Interior comfort:**
- Install sleeping platform with thick insulation underneath (cold radiates up from ground)
- Hang interior blankets/tarps to create smaller insulated sleeping chamber within larger shelter
- Create ventilation path that prevents direct wind contact with sleepers (heat loss)

## Phase 3: Semi-Permanent Winter (Months 6-10)

### Winter (Months 6-8): Months November-January

**Shelter performance requirements:**
- Maintain interior temperature >10°C without excessive fuel consumption
- Prevent moisture condensation (condensation leads to mold, rot, and hypothermia risk)
- Provide protection from snow load (roof slope or reinforcement)
- Maintain access during heavy snow (shovel area or wind-sheltered entrance)

**Winter upgrades:**
- Verify roof slope is adequate to shed snow (>30 degrees recommended)
- Add interior temperature monitoring (simple thermometer to track nighttime drops)
- Adjust ventilation for heating season (increase heat retention, controlled venting only)
- Create insulated vestibule at entrance (reduces heat loss from door opening)

**Ventilation balance:** Critical challenge in winter is preventing CO buildup while maintaining warmth
- If using interior fire: Ensure chimney or vent draws properly (test with paper held near opening—should move toward vent)
- If using sealed shelter: Establish controlled ventilation (crack door slightly during cooking/heating to prevent CO buildup)
- Monitor for signs of CO poisoning in group: Headache, dizziness, nausea (evacuate and ventilate immediately)

**Fuel consumption:**
- Typical shelter requires 50-100 kg firewood per day in deep winter (varies by climate, fuel type, shelter insulation)
- Plan for 4-6 months fuel storage (1-2 metric tons for group of 8-10 people)

### Spring (Months 9-10): March-April

**Focus:** Damage assessment, moisture control, storage reorganization

**Post-winter inspection:**
- Check roof for leaks (interior water staining indicates failure areas)
- Inspect walls for mold or moisture damage (indicates ventilation failure)
- Check foundation/floor for rot or structural damage (wood submerged in water or chronically wet)
- Assess tool integrity (rust, breakage, wood deterioration)

**Repairs and upgrades:**
- Patch roof leaks with salvaged materials or improved sealant
- Replace rotted materials promptly (structural integrity risk)
- Dry interior thoroughly (open shelter to air flow, remove excess moisture-absorbent materials temporarily)
- Reorganize storage for summer (remove heavy winter insulation temporarily if building hot storage)

## Phase 4: Permanent Shelter (Months 11-12)

### Late Spring/Summer Reinforcement (May-August Year 2)

By month 11-12, the shelter has proven capability through a full cycle. Now implement permanent improvements:

**Structural hardening:**
- Replace salvaged materials with more durable construction
- Strengthen roof with additional framing or reinforcement
- Install permanent flooring (if using elevated platform) with treated wood or salvaged materials
- Create permanent walls if previous design was temporary

**Security upgrades:**
- Add lockable door (prevents unauthorized entry)
- Create secure storage for valuable tools/supplies
- Position shelter to minimize visibility from distance (plant screening or terrain use)
- Install defensive barriers if threat assessment indicates (fence, ditch, controlled access points)

**Infrastructure improvements:**
- Develop permanent water system (gravity-fed, sealed cistern, or well)
- Improve latrine hygiene (permanent structure, not just hole)
- Establish permanent tool storage and work areas
- Create weather-protected food storage with temperature/humidity control

:::info-box
Permanent improvements should align with group security posture and available resources. A completely hidden, defensible shelter is resource-intensive but may be necessary depending on collapse context. Balance effort with actual threat assessment.
:::

```

4      | Pre-fall        | Storage, tool organization
5      | Fall transition | Insulation, heating prep
6-7    | Deep winter     | Heating, CO monitoring, fuel
```

## Insulation & Heat Management

### Effective Insulation Materials (Low-Tech)

| Material | R-value (per inch) | Availability | Durability | Notes |
|----------|-------------------|--------------|-----------|-------|
| Straw/hay | 1.0-1.5 | High | Poor (needs replacement) | Compacts over time, molding risk |
| Leaves | 0.5-1.0 | High | Poor (needs replacement) | Effective when dry; heavy when wet |
| Sawdust/woodchips | 1.2-1.5 | Medium | Fair | Compacts; attracts insects |
| Shredded cloth/paper | 1.5-2.0 | Medium | Fair | Good thermal value, mold-resistant |
| Moss/peat | 1.0-1.5 | High (climatic) | Fair | Acidic, preserves organic materials |
| Sand | 0.0-0.1 | High | Excellent | Not insulating; useful for ventilation |

**Insulation target for winter shelter:** 6-10 inches total equivalent R-value (combination of multiple materials)

### Ventilation in Winter (Preventing Moisture)

**Problem:** As interior warms from heating, warm air holds moisture from breathing, cooking, and activities. When moisture hits cold exterior surface, condensation forms, leading to mold and rot.

**Solution—managed ventilation:**
1. Establish air path: inlet (lower) and outlet (upper) to create natural convection
2. Inlet: 1-2 small holes at floor level opposite heating source
3. Outlet: 1-2 small holes near roof peak
4. Damper control: Open outlet slightly during heating, close when not needed
5. Interior thermal break: Hang blanket or insulation between occupied space and cold exterior walls

**Alternative—exterior ventilation:**
- Some post-collapse shelters accept higher heat loss in exchange for eliminating moisture problems
- Heat interior to 15-20°C (survivable, not comfortable)
- Allow air circulation (cracked door, gaps in walls)
- Trade fuel efficiency for interior drying

## Cooling Strategies (Summer)

### Passive Cooling

**Orientation and overhang:**
- Position shelter to maximize shade during hottest hours (south and west-facing shade in northern hemisphere)
- Extended roof (1.5+ m overhang) reduces interior solar gain

**Ventilation:**
- Create cross-ventilation with openings on opposite sides
- Position vents to capture prevailing breezes
- Close openings during day heat; open in early morning and evening

**Thermal mass:**
- Interior water storage or heavy materials absorb day heat, release at night
- Buried or semi-buried shelter naturally cools (lower temperatures 1-2 m below surface)

### Active Cooling (if resources available)

- **Evaporative cooling:** Hang damp cloth over intake air; evaporation cools incoming air
- **Water feature cooling:** Circulate water through shelter exterior (if water is available)
- **Radiant cooling:** Shade cloth over shelter exterior, separate from roof structure

## Drainage & Moisture Prevention

### Perimeter Drainage

**During construction:**
- Dig shallow trench (10-20 cm deep) in circle around shelter, 0.5-1 m out
- Slope toward downhill side
- Line with gravel if available (prevents erosion)
- Route drainage to natural drainage area (stream, low point)

**Maintenance:**
- Clear trenches after heavy rain
- Repair erosion or blockages
- Monitor interior floor—if dampness appears, check/improve drainage

### Foundation Moisture Barrier

- Elevate floor 30-50 cm above ground (prevents water seeping up from ground)
- Use salvaged plastic, clay, or other materials under floor to reduce capillary moisture
- Maintain air gap between floor and ground (ventilated subfloor if possible)

## Group Dynamics in Shared Shelters

As shelters become semi-permanent and house multiple people, social considerations become critical:

**Privacy and comfort:**
- Designate sleeping zones (separate or screened areas for couples/families)
- Create quiet zones for rest, work zones for tool use and storage
- Establish "off-hours" ventilation rules (e.g., no heavy work fire during sleep)

**Conflict prevention:**
- Clear storage assignments (who owns what, where it is stored)
- Designated water/fuel responsibility (reduces dispute over rationing)
- Established heating/cooling protocols (when to open/close vents, fuel rationing)

**Sanitation protocols:**
- Daily cleaning of common areas (prevents disease)
- Latrine usage schedule (prevents congestion)
- Waste disposal system (kitchen scraps, ash, other refuse)

## Common Seasonal Shelter Mistakes and Recovery

| Mistake | Consequence | Recovery |
|---------|------------|----------|
| Building flat roof in rain zone | Pooling, leaks, collapse | Re-pitch to 30°+ minimum; add center ridge pole |
| Skipping floor insulation | Ground cold drains body heat | Add 10-15 cm dry leaves or straw; elevate floor on branches |
| Sealing shelter without ventilation | CO buildup, condensation, mold | Open vent at top and bottom; never seal fully with fire inside |
| Using wet insulation materials | Loss of R-value, mold, rot | Remove, dry, and re-pack; prefer dry leaves/straw over damp |
| Delaying winterization until cold | Wet materials freeze; no time to dry | Start insulation in late summer; allow 2-4 weeks drying time |
| Placing shelter in frost pocket | Night temperatures far below forecast | Relocate upslope; check for cold-air drainage paths |
| Ignoring roof leaks after first rain | Rot, structural weakening | Patch immediately; add extra thatch or tarp overlay |
| No drainage trench around shelter | Floor flooding after heavy rain | Dig 10-20 cm perimeter trench sloped downhill |

:::tip
If a shelter has already failed in weather (collapsed roof, flooded floor), the fastest recovery is usually to salvage usable materials and rebuild with correct slope and drainage rather than patching the failed structure. A correctly built small shelter outperforms a patched large one.
:::
