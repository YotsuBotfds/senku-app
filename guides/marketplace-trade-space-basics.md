## What A Good Market Does

A good market is easy to find, easy to walk through, easy to understand, and hard to cheat. If people cannot tell where to go, where to wait, what a stall costs, or who is in charge, the market quickly becomes noisy and unfair.

The goal is not to create a fancy commercial center. The goal is to create a space where people can trade without confusion, crowding, hidden fees, or constant disputes.

Three rules make a market work:

- People can see the layout at a glance.
- Prices, fees, and weights are posted or explained the same way every time.
- There is a clear way to settle disputes before they spread.

## Choose The Site

Pick a place that is:

- Flat or gently sloped
- Dry enough to stay usable after rain
- Close to the main path or road, but not blocking traffic
- Large enough for stalls, walking space, loading, and a small dispute area
- Easy to supervise from one or two standing points
- Far enough from latrines, waste piles, animal pens, and smoke sources

Avoid sites that are:

- Muddy after rain
- Hard to reach with carts or hand trucks
- Hidden from view
- Too close to homes if noise is a problem
- Too close to food storage if pests or theft are a concern

If possible, keep a little room for growth. A market that fits only today's crowd will fail when trade expands.

If the market is part of a larger settlement plan, use [`settlement-layout-growth-planning`](./settlement-layout-growth-planning.md) to confirm road access, drainage, public-space zoning, and future expansion before you finalize the market footprint.

## Lay Out The Space

The simplest market layout and stall arrangement is a clear entry, a central walking lane, stalls on both sides, and a separate loading or storage edge.

This section assumes the settlement-scale location is already chosen. If you still need to decide where the market belongs in the town or village plan, hand that question to [`settlement-layout-growth-planning`](./settlement-layout-growth-planning.md) first.

For physical market-day layout prompts, keep the answer inside the market footprint: where stalls sit, how carts pass, which corners stay open, where foot traffic turns, where the loading edge begins, and where notices are visible before people enter the stall lanes.

### Basic Zones

- **Entry and notice zone**: first thing people see; post rules, fees, and times here
- **Main stall zone**: where selling and trading happen
- **Weights and measures point**: one visible place for checking goods
- **Loading and handoff zone**: for carts, bundles, crates, and inventory transfer
- **Quiet dispute corner**: for resolving problems without blocking traffic
- **Waste and cleanup zone**: away from food and narrow walkways

### Simple Flow

Try to make movement feel obvious:

1. Enter.
2. Read notices.
3. Walk the stalls.
4. Check weights and quality.
5. Pay or trade.
6. Leave by the same route or a separate exit.

If the market is busy, use one-way walking loops or marked lanes so people are not constantly colliding.

Keep corners open. A blocked corner turns into a knot of shoppers, carts, and vendors because people cannot see who is turning, stopping, or loading.

### Stalls And Spacing

Do not place stalls so close together that people cannot stop and look without blocking the lane.

Good spacing should allow:

- Two adults to pass without shoulder contact
- A buyer to step aside while inspecting goods
- A cart or hand truck to move through the loading zone
- A person to leave a stall without pushing into the next one

Put heavier goods near the loading edge and lighter goods deeper inside the market. Put fragile goods, food, and high-value items where they are visible and protected.

If carts must pass through the same footprint as shoppers, give carts a clear loading edge or timed lane. Do not make carts cross the tightest stall corners or the main stopping area unless there is no other route.

## Use Clear Notices

The market should have one obvious notice board or announcement point near the entry. People should not have to ask three different people where the rules are.

Inside the market footprint, place notices where they shape movement: at the entry, beside the stall assignment point, near weights and measures, and before any one-way lane or loading edge. Avoid putting the only notice board deep inside a crowded stall row.

Post:

- Open and close times
- Stall fees
- Market rules
- Standard weights and measures
- Prohibited goods or restricted goods
- Emergency closures or weather changes
- Contact for disputes or lost items

Keep notices short, plain, and repeated in the same place each market day. If many people are low-literacy, use symbols, color blocks, or pictures as well as text.

For notice-board design and public announcements, see [`community-bulletin-notice-systems`](./community-bulletin-notice-systems.md).

## Standardize Weights And Measures

A market becomes unfair fast if every seller uses a different cup, basket, or hand scale.

Set one community standard for common measures:

- Weight
- Volume
- Length
- Bundle size
- Bucket size

Use the same reference tools every market day. Keep reference weights, measuring cups, and a visible balance scale in a known place. If the market trades grain, salt, cloth, or metal, standard measures matter even more.

### Practical Rules

- Post the standard unit names where everyone can see them.
- Do not allow private scales that nobody can verify.
- Check one seller's measure against the reference if a complaint arises.
- Recalibrate or replace damaged reference tools immediately.

For the measurement system itself, see [`weights-measures-standards`](./weights-measures-standards.md) and [`trade-standards-exchange`](./trade-standards-exchange.md).

## Keep Crowd Flow Calm

Crowd flow is a safety issue, not just a comfort issue. Bad flow causes arguments, theft, spills, tripping, and panic.

### Crowd Flow Rules

- Keep the main lane wide enough for two-way movement if possible
- Do not allow stalls to spill into the lane
- Put waiting lines to the side, not in the middle
- Keep corners and lane turns clear enough that carts, shoppers, and stall workers can see each other
- Keep loading carts on the loading edge, not weaving through the busiest foot-traffic lane
- Reserve one clear path for carts, stretchers, or emergency access
- Keep children, animals, and loud bargaining away from the tightest spots
- Keep clinic intake or triage lines outside the market flow if a clinic shares the same frontage

### What Causes Chaos

- No posted prices
- Too many people at one stall
- A single narrow entrance with no exit logic
- Sellers shouting over each other
- Buyers stopping in the lane to haggle
- People using the loading area as a casual meeting point

If a crowd starts to bunch up, move the problem rather than the people. Open space near the choke point usually works better than repeated shouting.

## Hand Off Goods Cleanly

Storage handoff is where markets often become messy. Goods are counted in one place, sold in another, and stored in a third place. If the transfer rules are unclear, losses and accusations follow.

Use a simple handoff process:

- Count incoming goods at the loading zone
- Tag them by owner, stall, or lot
- Record what left storage and what returned unsold
- Keep bulk storage separate from open market display
- Return unsold goods at the same time each day if possible

If your community uses a warehouse or shared storehouse, do not let market goods drift in and out without a record. The market should connect to storage, not replace it.

For storage systems and rotation, see [`warehousing-inventory`](./warehousing-inventory.md).

## Make Fairness Visible

Fairness in a market is not only about being honest. It is also about being able to prove that the system is honest.

### Fairness Basics

- Same stall class should pay the same fee.
- Same measure should mean the same amount.
- Same rule should apply to everyone on the same day.
- Complaints should go to one named steward, not to whoever is loudest.
- Posted prices or posted fee rules should be public before the market opens.

### Common Fairness Problems

- Charging a different fee depending on who is asking
- Using a hidden or unmarked scale
- Giving favored sellers better space without a rule
- Changing fees after people have already set up
- Letting one group block the best entrance or shade every week

If a rule cannot be explained in one sentence, it is probably too vague for a market.

## Set Fee Boundaries

Market fees can be useful. They help pay for cleanup, layout, shade, notice boards, and supervision. But fees become abusive when nobody knows who can collect them or why.

### Fee Boundaries That Prevent Abuse

- Only one authorized collector, or a small named team
- Fees posted before the market opens
- A fixed collection point and collection time
- Receipts or tokens when practical
- A clear difference between a market fee and a tax
- No "extra" payments for good spots unless the rule is public

### What Fees May Cover

- Cleanup
- Board maintenance
- Security or supervision
- Table, tent, or stall upkeep
- Shared scales and measuring tools
- Waste disposal

### What Fees Should Not Become

- An excuse for bribes
- A private toll on access
- A way to favor friends
- A surprise charge after trade has started

For broader tax and revenue rules, see [`taxation-revenue-systems`](./taxation-revenue-systems.md).

## Keep The Market From Becoming Chaotic

Markets stay orderly when the rules are simple and visible.

### Useful Market Roles

- **Market steward**: keeps the layout and rules consistent
- **Notice keeper**: posts schedules, prices, and announcements
- **Weights checker**: verifies measures when needed
- **Fee collector**: handles market fees only
- **Cleanup lead**: closes the market and resets the space

### Daily Open Checklist

- Notices posted
- Stalls assigned
- Weights checked
- Loading zone clear
- Emergency access open
- Fee collection point set
- Dispute process known

### Daily Close Checklist

- Unsold goods recorded
- Fees counted and logged
- Waste removed
- Lost items checked
- Damage noted
- Next market day posted

## Common Mistakes

- Mixing market fees with personal bargaining
- Letting notices change during the market without announcement
- Using different units at different stalls
- Putting the loading zone through the main crowd lane
- Letting the quiet dispute process become public shouting
- Letting the market footprint consume the only clean road, drainage line, or future expansion space
- Treating the market as a storage room instead of a trade space

## Short Setup Rule

If you remember only one thing, remember this:

**A good market is a place where people can tell where to go, what things cost, how much they weigh, who may charge fees, and how to leave without a fight.**
