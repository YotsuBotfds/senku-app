Many parasites are zoonotic (transmissible to humans). Practice good hygiene and wear gloves when handling animals or fecal samples. Overuse of antiparasitics drives drug resistance.

## Overview

:::info-box
**Routing guide:** Use this guide for fecal counts, FAMACHA scoring, dewormer selection, pasture spelling, and parasite-specific monitoring. If animals are losing condition because of poor housing, crowding, or feed and water layout, pair this with [Animal Husbandry and Veterinary](animal-husbandry.html). If handling stress, sorting, or herd movement is worsening exposure or treatment success, pair it with [Animal Behavior and Ethology](animal-behavior-ethology.html). For fever, pneumonia, wounds, or other non-parasite illness, switch to [Veterinary](veterinary.html).
:::

Parasites are among the most economically damaging health issues in survival livestock systems. A single untreated Haemonchus infection can kill a young animal within 4-6 weeks through anemia and blood loss. Across temperate regions, 70-90% of grazing animals carry detectable parasite loads; in tropical zones, year-round transmission maintains parasite pressure at 95%+. This guide provides practical diagnosis, treatment, and prevention strategies for herds operating without immediate veterinary access.

The fundamental principle: **interrupt the parasite lifecycle through management rather than relying solely on drugs.** Rotation grazing, sanitation, and selective treatment can reduce parasite burden by 50-80% without pharmaceutical inputs. Understanding parasite biology — how long they survive on pasture (typically 3-12 weeks for roundworms), when they're most infectious (larvae peak at 5-10 days in warm conditions), and which animals are most vulnerable (young 3-12 months, pregnant, immunocompromised) — enables targeted interventions.

:::info-box
**Key metrics:** Target 70-95% egg reduction 2 weeks post-treatment. EPG <100 = controlled. >500 = severe. Most untreated herds peak at 1,000-5,000 EPG during high transmission season.
:::

<section id="internal">

## Internal Parasites (Helminths)

### Gastrointestinal Nematodes (Roundworms)

**Common species:** Cooperia, Haemonchus, Oesophagostomum (livestock); Toxocara, Ancylostoma (dogs/cats). Transmission: fecal-oral route. Symptoms: diarrhea, anemia, weight loss, stunted growth, poor feed conversion, pale mucous membranes, lethargy. Haemonchus (blood-feeder) causes severe anemia. Severe infections: edema of jaw (bottle jaw), potential death. Diagnosis: fecal float/McMaster count. Treatment: anthelmintics at 5-15mg/kg depending on drug/species.

### Tapeworms (Cestodes)

Transmission: intermediate hosts (fleas for pets; arthropods for livestock). Symptoms: weight loss, malabsorption, segments visible in stool (rice-grain appearance). More severe in young animals. Diagnosis: visual identification of segments or fecal exam. Treatment: praziquantel 20-30mg/kg or albendazole 10-15mg/kg.

### Flukes (Trematodes)

Transmission: through water (contaminated sources). Common: liver flukes (Fasciola), lung flukes. Symptoms: anemia, weight loss, poor production, potentially fatal. Diagnosis: fecal exam. Treatment: triclabendazole 10mg/kg. Prevention: drain boggy areas, fence from water sources. Survival strategy: improve drainage, accept higher risk if water contaminated.


<section id="external">

## External Parasites

### Ectoparasites (Lice, Mites, Fleas)

**Lice:** Transmission: direct contact, fomites. Symptoms: itching, hair loss, scabbing, weight loss, anemia in heavy burdens. **Mites:** Cause mange/scabies. Highly contagious. Sarcoptes (severe); Psoroptes/Chorioptes (less severe). Symptoms: severe itching, hair loss, skin thickening. **Fleas:** Irritation, anemia, tapeworm transmission, disease vectors. Diagnosis: visual identification or scraping. Treatments: dips, dusts, herbal options (sulfur, neem oil).

### Tick Infestation

Heavy burden causes anemia, weight loss, stress, potential paralysis. Removal: grasp near mouthparts, pull straight out. Do NOT crush. Do NOT use alcohol/petroleum. Disinfect site. Control: pasture management, rotating grazing, seasonal dipping. Herbal options: sulfur 5%, neem oil 2%, tobacco 1:10 (use cautiously).

### Fly Infestation

**Botflies:** Skin lesions from larval migration. **Screwworms:** Flesh-eating in open wounds (high mortality). **Other flies:** Blood-feeders causing stress, reduced production. Treatment: eliminate breeding sites, proper manure management (>60°C for 3+ weeks kills larvae). Ivermectin 0.2mg/kg covers some parasites. Prevention: maintain sanitation.

</section>

<section id="protozoa">

## Protozoan Parasites

### Coccidiosis

Transmission: fecal-oral. Common in young animals 3-12 weeks post-weaning. Symptoms: bloody diarrhea, anemia, dehydration, weight loss, potential death. Diagnosis: fecal exam (oocysts). Treatment: coccidiostats or toltrazuril 20mg/kg, supportive care. Prevention: sanitation, age separation, rotation grazing, stress management.

### Giardiasis

Transmission: water/food contamination. Symptoms: chronic watery diarrhea, weight loss, malabsorption in young animals. Common <6 months age. Diagnosis: fecal antigen or microscopy. Treatment: metronidazole 15-20mg/kg TID x 7 days or albendazole 15mg/kg. Prevention: clean water, sanitation. Boil water if giardia suspected.

### Cryptosporidiosis

Transmission: oocysts in water/feces. Highly contagious, resistant to disinfectants. Symptoms: severe watery diarrhea, dehydration, weakness. Severe in young/immunocompromised. Diagnosis: fecal exam or antigen kits. Treatment: supportive care (fluids). Prevention: sanitation (bleach 5%), isolation, clean water. Focus on fluid replacement.

</section>

<section id="historical">

## Historical Context and Theoretical Principles

### Evolution of Parasitology Practice

Modern parasitology emerged with microscopy development. Pre-modern herders managed parasites through observation and empirical management. Clinical signs (diarrhea, anemia, weight loss) guide diagnosis; treatment follows seasonal patterns with drug rotation and sanitation. Historical records: 20-40% mortality without management vs 5-10% with basic management.

### Biological Principles

Life cycle interruption through sanitation, rotation, or separation reduces burden. Resistance develops through repeated same-drug dosing (5-10 years); rotate classes to mitigate. Pre-patent period: 2-4 weeks for nematodes, 4-6 weeks for tapeworms.

### Survival-Era Implications

Return to older methods: herbal dips, pasture rotation, selective grazing. Focus on management rather than eradication. Expected: 50% higher parasite loads than modern management, but maintained viability.

<section id="climate-soil">

## Climate & Environmental Conditions for Parasite Transmission

### Temperature and Moisture Effects on Development

Parasite larvae develop fastest at 15-25°C (59-77°F). Below 5°C, development halts; above 35°C, many larvae die. Moisture is critical: wet conditions (rain, dew) allow larvae to migrate from feces to herbage; dry conditions desiccate larvae in 2-5 days. Cold/dry climates therefore naturally suppress parasites, while warm/wet climates enable year-round transmission.

**Practical implications:** Temperate climates (cool winters, dry summers) have 4-6 month high-risk season (spring/early summer). Tropical climates have 12-month transmission. High-altitude/cold regions may see only 6-8 weeks of peak transmission (assume June-July only).

:::tip
**Spring flush rule:** In temperate zones, parasite transmission peaks 4-6 weeks after spring growth (typically May-June in Northern Hemisphere). Timing varies by 2-4 weeks depending on local spring temperature. Plan treatment and rotation before this window.
:::

### Regional Parasite Prevalence Patterns

**Wet tropical regions:** Liver flukes endemic; coccidiosis severe; year-round treatment needed. **Temperate:** Primarily GI nematodes, seasonal coccidiosis. **Arid:** Low parasite load baseline; disease driven by water source contamination or overstocking. **Coastal areas:** High snail populations → fluke risk. **High altitude:** Short transmission season; winter housing concentrates risk.

:::warning
**Water sources:** Streams, ponds, and wet pastures are parasite reservoirs. Flukes, giardia, and crypto cannot be eliminated from water by herding alone — fencing or alternative water sources is essential in endemic areas.
:::

<section id="lifecycles">

## Parasite Life Cycles: Direct vs Indirect

### Direct Life Cycle Example: Roundworms (Haemonchus)

![Roundworm direct life cycle diagram with host and environmental stages](../assets/svgs/veterinary-parasitology-1.svg)

**Key Point:** Direct life cycle means no intermediate host required. Infected animal sheds eggs → environmental development (5+ days on pasture) → ingestion by new host. Pasture rotation breaks cycle: move animals before larvae mature (3-4 week rotation) or spell pasture for 8+ weeks (larvae die without host).

### Indirect Life Cycle Example: Liver Fluke (Fasciola)

![Liver fluke indirect life cycle with intermediate host snail](../assets/svgs/veterinary-parasitology-2.svg)

**Key Point:** Indirect life cycle requires intermediate host (snail for flukes). Complex transmission means control focuses on: 1) preventing water contamination, 2) eliminating snail habitat (drainage), 3) fencing animals away from water sources. Disease risk seasonal (highest during wet seasons when snails active).

### Pre-Patent Period and Treatment Windows

**Pre-patent period** is the interval between infection and egg shedding (when fecal tests become positive). For GI nematodes: 2-4 weeks. For tapeworms: 4-6 weeks. For flukes: 6-12 weeks. **Practical implication:** Animals infected 2 weeks ago may test negative on fecal exam but will become positive (and infectious) shortly. Treat all exposed animals even if tests are negative; repeat fecal 2-3 weeks later to catch emerging parasites.

:::tip
**Treatment timing strategy:** If outbreak suspected (diarrhea in multiple animals), treat all young animals immediately and repeat 14-21 days later. This covers both existing and recently-acquired infections before pre-patent period ends.
:::

</section>

<section id="diagnosis">

## Diagnosis Methods

### Fecal Examination Techniques

**Direct smear:** Fresh feces + saline on slide, quick but less sensitive. **Flotation:** Feces + solution (salt or sugar); parasites float to surface. Most practical method. Salt solution best for nematodes. **Sedimentation:** Heavy parasites sediment. **McMaster count:** Quantifies eggs per gram (EPG) for burden assessment. Mix feces with McMaster solution (SG 1.20), load chamber, count eggs, calculate EPG.

### Visual Inspection Methods

Feces: observe for tapeworm segments (rice-grain) or worms if heavy burden. Ectoparasites: lice (fast-moving), mites/ticks (examine under 10x). Skin scrapings: mite identification (10 scrapings + mineral oil, examine 10-40x). Flea combing: collect on white paper, identify by movement and blood content.

### Clinical Signs Assessment

Suggestive symptoms: diarrhea, weight loss, anemia, reduced production, poor coat quality, failure to thrive. History: contaminated pasture, new animals, seasonal patterns (spring nematodes, summer protozoa). Treat empirically if signs present.

### Dosing Considerations and Accuracy

Weight-based dosing critical: under-dosing = failure + resistance. Over-dosing = toxicity (especially levamisole, narrow margin). Accurate weighing essential; estimate if needed (visual ±20-40%). Fatting animals: recalculate as weight increases. Young animals: may need higher mg/kg. Pregnant: verify drug safety (levamisole contraindicated, morantel safe). Liver/kidney disease: may need adjustment.

### Treatment Timing, Frequency, and Monitoring

Treat before grazing season (spring), mid-season if high burden. Repeat 2-3 weeks later (emerging larvae). Monitor fecal exam 2 weeks post-treatment. Failure to respond suggests resistance or incorrect diagnosis. Rotate drug classes annually. Document drug, date, parasite type, efficacy.

<section id="herbal">

## Herbal Anthelmintics and Traditional Treatments

### Herbal Dewormers with Evidence

**Garlic:** 10-20g fresh or 2-5mL oil daily. ~40-60% efficacy. Very low cost. **Pumpkin seeds:** 100-300g daily. ~30-50% efficacy vs tapeworms. Low cost. **Wormwood:** 5-10g daily, max 2-3 weeks (liver toxic). ~50-70% efficacy. **Black walnut:** 1-2g powder daily. Anthelmintic, esp. fleas/lice. **Diatomaceous earth:** 2-3% of diet. 20-40% efficacy, mechanical action. Use food-grade only.

### Herbal Anthelmintics Efficacy Summary

Herbal options ~30-60% as effective as commercial drugs. Best as preventive tool with management + sanitation. Rotate herbal types to minimize resistance. Expected: 20-30% burden reduction vs 60-80% with commercial drugs.

</section>

<section id="deworming">

## Chemical Dewormer Salvage and Dosing Precision

### Salvaging Expired or Compromised Dewormers

Assess condition: powder lumping, liquid separation indicate degradation. Check temperature history. Efficacy timeline: 70-80% effective up to 5 years if stored properly. Use at 1.5x dose if <5 years expired, or only if no alternative. Prioritize high-risk animals with best stock. Repeat treatment 2 weeks later if using older drug.

### Precision Dosing Without Commercial Scales

Weight estimation: body weight tapes, palpate ribs (normal/over/underweight), measure height + length. Estimate ±10-20%. Example: 50kg animal, levamisole 7mg/kg = 350mg. 10% solution (100mg/mL) = 3.5mL. Double-check: weight × dose ÷ concentration = volume. Round conservatively for narrow-margin drugs (levamisole), round up for broad-safety drugs.

### Storage and Preservation of Anthelmintics

Store cool (<15°C), dark, dry. Keep powders sealed (add desiccant). Keep injectables in original packaging. Label: drug, dose, expiration, storage date. Use oldest first. Shelf life: powders 5-7 years, liquids 3-5 years, injectables 2-4 years. Don't freeze.

</section>

<section id="resistance">

## Resistance Management and Drug Rotation Strategies

### Understanding Anthelmintic Resistance

Develops from repeated same-drug dosing over years. Emerges after 5-10 years at high frequency. Early signs: decreased efficacy, need for higher doses. Contributing: under-dosing, unnecessary treatment, continuous use. Susceptible population takes years to re-establish.

### Drug Rotation and Combination Strategies

**Rotation:** Rotate classes annually (not individual drugs). Year 1: benzimidazole, year 2: levamisole, year 3: ivermectin, year 4: return to benzimidazole. **Combination:** Use two classes simultaneously if available (broader coverage, less resistance). **Targeted:** Treat only high-risk animals (young, anemic, pregnant). Extends useful life from 5-10 years to 10-20 years.

### Monitoring and Documentation

Track drug, date, animals, parasite type. Observe post-treatment efficacy (count reduction, clinical improvement). High fecal count 2 weeks post-treatment = suspect resistance (switch class). Share data with other herders. If resistance in one herd, likely spreading regionally.

</section>

<section id="prevention">

## Prevention Strategies

### Pasture Management Systems

-   **Rotation grazing:** Move before larvae mature (3-4 weeks min). Don't return until 4-8 weeks. Optimal: 7+ paddocks (2-3 weeks graze, 4-6 weeks rest)
-   **Alternate species:** Different species have different parasites. Cattle → sheep rotation. Poultry post-grazing pecks larvae
-   **Spelling:** >8-12 weeks for complete kill; 4-6 weeks for 50-80%. Use for young stock
-   **Clean pastures:** Young animals on uncontaminated pasture (8+ weeks rested)
-   **Avoid wet areas:** Drain where possible, fence from marshes during wet season

### Sanitation Protocols

**Separate water from feces:** Move troughs away from high-traffic areas. **Clean feeders/waterers:** monthly, scrub or sun-dry. **Isolate treated animals:** 2-3 weeks (prevent shedding). **Compost manure:** >60°C for 2-3 weeks. **Bedding:** change weekly if possible, compost used bedding.

### Nutrition and Immunity Support

Good nutrition supports parasite resistance: protein (20-25% young, 10-15% adult), minerals (copper, selenium, zinc), vitamins (A, D, E). Young animals: best-quality forage, supplemental protein. Minimize stress (transport, weather changes). Colostrum provides passive immunity in newborns.

### Selective Treatment Approach

Not all need treatment. Treat high-risk: young (3-12 mo), pregnant/lactating, immunocompromised, anemic. Monitor non-treated animals. Reduces drug use, slows resistance. FAMACHA system: score eye color (red = good, pale = treat).

</section>

<section id="procedures">

## Detailed Procedures

### Fecal Flotation Step-by-Step

1.  Collect fresh feces (5-10g) in labeled container
2.  Prepare solution: salt (3 T + 1 cup water) or sugar (1:1). Use salt for nematodes, sugar for protozoa
3.  Mix feces + solution (3-4x volume). Let sit 2-3 hours or centrifuge 5-10 min
4.  Collect surface layer with loop, transfer to slide
5.  Add coverslip, press lightly
6.  Examine 10x (scan for parasites), then 40x (identify). Count if quantifying
7.  Record: presence/absence, abundance estimate, unusual findings, EPG if counted

### Herbal Dip Preparation (Sulfur Solution)

1.  Obtain agricultural sulfur (verify freshness)
2.  Mix: 5% sulfur (5g + 95mL water, or 2 T + 2 cups). Let settle 30 min
3.  Apply: submerge or spray/pour. Work neck to tail. 1-2 min, 2-3x weekly
4.  Duration: 3-4 weeks (multiple life stages)
5.  Safety: Avoid eyes. Rinse after dip if possible
6.  Disposal: Dilute 10x before disposal

</section>

<section id="fecal-counting">

## Fecal Egg Counting Techniques and Interpretation

### McMaster Counting Method

**Equipment:** McMaster chamber, microscope (40x min), fecal sample, flotation solution (SG 1.20). **Procedure:** 1) Mix 1g feces + 29mL solution (1:29). 2) Load chambers. 3) Settle 2-3 min. 4) Count eggs at 10x. 5) Calculate: (eggs / 0.6mL) × 50 = EPG. **Interpretation:** <100 = light, 100-500 = moderate, >500 = heavy. Trending important (70%+ reduction post-treatment goal).

### Simplified Counting (Without McMaster Chamber)

Use standard slide, mark grid (10-20 squares). Prepare flotation. Let 30 min. Count parasites in 5-10 random squares (10x). Estimate: 50+ eggs = heavy, 10-50 = moderate, <10 = light. Advantage: no special equipment. Disadvantage: less precise.

### Interpretation of Parasite Load

Light (<100 EPG): no clinical signs. Moderate (100-500): slight diarrhea, reduced gain. Heavy (>500): clear disease. Young animals <6 months at highest risk.

<section id="pasture">

## Pasture Management Systems for Parasite Control

### Paddock Rotation

![Pasture rotation paddock layout with rest periods and larval die-off timeline](../assets/svgs/veterinary-parasitology-4.svg)

**Optimal:** 7+ paddocks (2-3 weeks graze, 4+ weeks rest). **Acceptable:** 3-4 paddocks (2-3 weeks graze, 4-6 weeks rest). Young animals on clean paddocks; older on grazed paddocks.

### Pasture Spelling Strategy

**Complete:** 12+ weeks (99% larvae kill by 8-10 weeks). **Mixed grazing:** Cattle + sheep/goats, poultry post-grazing. **Hay:** Peak season (spring/summer) feed hay instead of grazing.

### Drainage and Environmental Management

Improve drainage in wet areas (eliminates snail habitat, reduces nematode survival). Techniques: 1) Install tile/dig ditches. 2) Avoid trough in low areas. 3) Fence swamps/water. 4) Create sacrifice areas. Drainage reduces disease pressure, improves forage.

</section>

<section id="species-specific">

## Species-Specific Considerations

### Ruminants (Cattle, Sheep, Goats)

**Primary:** GI nematodes. Haemonchus (blood-feeder): anemia. Coccidiosis: young 3-12 weeks post-weaning. Flukes: wet areas. Lice: housed animals. Dosing: weight × dose/kg. **Young:** Treat monthly during grazing season or use hay + selective pasture. **Pregnant:** Treat before breeding, repeat if clinical signs.

### Poultry

**Internal:** Roundworms, tapeworms, cecal worms. **Coccidiosis:** 90%+ mortality if untreated (chicks 3-8 weeks). Stress triggers disease. **External:** Lice, mites. **Treatment:** Add to water/feed (flock). **Sanitation:** Change litter frequently, compost >60°C. Move pens regularly, avoid crowding.

### Dogs and Cats

**Roundworms:** Puppies/kittens (maternal transmission). **Hookworms:** Anemia, skin penetration. **Tapeworms:** Common (flea ingestion). **Heartworms:** Mosquito-transmitted, endemic areas need prevention. **Giardia:** Common young animals, water contamination. **Fleas/ticks:** Year-round prevention. Dosing: weight-based, young need higher mg/kg, seniors reduce if liver/kidney disease.

</section>

<section id="yield-impact">

## Parasite Impact on Production and Economic Thresholds

### Production Loss by Parasite Burden

Light infection (<100 EPG): <5% production loss (often undetectable). Moderate (100-500 EPG): 10-20% loss (reduced weight gain 0.2-0.5 kg/day, reduced milk 5-10%, wool loss 5%). Heavy (>500 EPG): 30-50% loss (weight loss, anemia, reduced reproduction).

**Quantified impact:** Sheep: 0.5kg/week weight loss per 500 EPG. Goats: 0.4kg/week. Cattle: 2-3kg/week. **Timeline:** Clinical signs appear 3-4 weeks into heavy infection.

:::info-box
**Economic threshold:** Treat if expected treatment cost < lost production value. Example: Sheep flock of 50 animals at 500 EPG over 8 weeks = 200kg lost gain. At $2/kg = $400 loss. Dewormer cost $100. Clear treatment ROI. Below 100 EPG, treatment usually costs more than benefit.
:::

### Reproduction and Young Stock Performance

Heavy parasitism reduces conception rates by 20-30%, extends lactation recovery by 2-4 weeks, and causes 30-50% higher mortality in young animals. Parasite-free dams nursing parasite-exposed young show 15-25% better survival. Strategic treatment of breeding animals (pregnant females 2 weeks pre-breeding, weaned young on clean pasture) dramatically improves herd viability.

### Expected Performance Under Different Management Levels

**Good management (rotation, treatment, sanitation):** 5-10% parasite-related mortality. **Moderate (rotation only, no treatment):** 15-25% mortality. **Poor (continuous grazing, no management):** 40-60% mortality in untreated young stock. Survival herds aiming for 10-15% baseline loss should prioritize rotation + selective treatment over accepting 40% loss.

</section>

<section id="biosecurity">

## Biosecurity for New Animals and Quarantine

### Pre-Purchase and Introduction Protocols

**Pre-purchase:** Observe signs (diarrhea, anemia, coat). Request history; assume high load if unknown. **Quarantine:** 2-4 weeks isolated. 1) Fecal exam. 2) Treat if parasites. 3) Re-test 2 weeks. 4) House separately. 5) Feed separately. 6) Isolate manure. 7) Cull if severe.

### Mixed Herd Introduction

**Timing:** After quarantine + treatment. **Gradual:** Mix with older animals first (established immunity). **Monitor:** 1-2 weeks, isolate if illness. **Pasture:** Rested paddock initially, normal rotation after 1-2 weeks.

</section>

<section id="zoonotic">

## Zoonotic Parasites and Human Risk

### Parasites Transmissible to Humans

**Toxoplasma:** Cat feces. Risk pregnant women. Prevent: gloves, wash hands. **Giardia/Crypto:** Water-borne. Risk immunocompromised/children. Prevent: protect water, boil/filter. **Nematodes:** Soil/feces. Prevent: gloves, sanitize, cook vegetables. **Echinococcus:** Dog feces. Risk undercooked meat. Prevent: cook meat, prevent dog access to raw meat. **Strongyloides:** Soil. Prevent: wear shoes.

### Hygiene Practices to Reduce Human Risk

**Handling:** Wear gloves, wash hands (5+ min) with soap/hot water, don't touch face before washing, disinfect surfaces (bleach 1:10). **PPE:** Gloves, animal-specific boots, wash station. **Food:** Cook thoroughly (165°F, 15 min), wash/cook vegetables from manured soil. **Children:** Supervise, teach hand washing. Higher risk in children/pregnant/immunocompromised.

</section>

<section id="common-mistakes">

## Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

### Underdosing Due to Weight Estimation Errors

The most frequent cause of treatment failure. Herders often underestimate weight (animals look smaller than actual weight). A 60kg animal estimated at 50kg receives 17% under-dose, reducing efficacy by 40-60%. Solution: 1) Use weight tape (±5%). 2) Compare to known reference weights. 3) Err slightly on heavy side for broad-safety drugs. 4) Weigh animals pre-treatment if possible on portable scale.

:::warning
**Critical:** Under-dose by >20% is worse than not treating at all — it selects for resistance while failing to cure the infection. Always verify weight twice; when in doubt, consult weight tape or ask more experienced herders.
:::

### Treating Without Diagnosis

Giving dewormers to animals without confirmed parasites wastes expensive drugs and accelerates resistance. A normal-appearing animal with good weight gain may have light parasite load (<50 EPG) requiring no treatment. Rule: **Do one fecal exam per season per management group.** Treat only if moderate (100-500 EPG) or heavy (>500 EPG), or if clinical signs present.

### Neglecting Pasture Rotation After Treatment

Treated animals shed fewer eggs briefly (2-4 weeks), but resistance parasites reinfect within 3-6 weeks if kept on contaminated pasture. Without rotation, herds cycle between treatment and re-infection. Solution: Move to clean pasture immediately post-treatment. Wait 4-8 weeks before returning to original pasture.

:::tip
**Effective rotation:** Treat young animals 2 weeks before expected move to clean pasture. This synchronizes treatment with environmental strategy for maximum impact.
:::

### Incorrect Drug Choice or Route

Oral drugs not given by mouth (poured on feed, inconsistent absorption). Injectable drugs given orally (inactive). Praziquantel given to non-tapeworm infections (ineffective). Always verify drug, species, and route before administration.

### Poor Record Keeping

Without documentation, herders cannot track which drugs were used, when resistance appeared, or which animals improved. Keep simple log: **Date | Animal ID | Drug | Dose (mg/kg) | Route | Parasite type | Follow-up fecal date | Result.** Refer back when failures occur; share across community to detect regional resistance trends.

</section>

### High Parasite Load Post-Treatment

**Causes:** Under-dosing (most common), resistance, improper route, drug degradation, inadequate interval, re-infection. **Solutions:** 1) Re-weigh, recalculate dose. 2) Review method. 3) Check storage. 4) Switch class if resistance suspected. 5) Repeat in 2-3 weeks. 6) Improve management (rotation, sanitation).

### Repeated Infection Despite Treatment

**Causes:** Poor pasture management, contaminated water, poor sanitation, high stocking. **Solutions:** 1) Pasture rotation (4+ weeks). 2) Test/treat water. 3) Separate feeders, change water. 4) Reduce stocking. 5) Increase treatment frequency. 6) Accept higher baseline if limited options.

### Adverse Drug Reaction

**Signs:** Tremors, weakness, paralysis. **Causes:** Over-dosing, sensitivity, liver/kidney disease, narrow margin drug. **Response:** Stop, support care, rest. **Treatment:** Usually self-limiting (24-48 hours). **Prevention:** Check dose twice, use alternative class, reduce dose for sensitive animals, document.

### Anemia Unresponsive to Deworming

**Causes:** Heavy parasite burden, concurrent disease, malnutrition, iron deficiency. **Solutions:** 1) Iron supplements (oxide 1-2g or ferrous sulfate). 2) Repeat treatment in 2 weeks. 3) Improve diet (legumes, leafy plants). 4) Monitor conjunctiva weekly. 5) If no improvement in 2-3 weeks, investigate other causes. 6) Blood transfusion if severe.

### Vaccine Non-Responsiveness

**Cause:** Heavy parasite burden suppresses immunity. **Solution:** Treat parasites first, then vaccinate 2 weeks later.

</section>

<section id="seasonal">

## Seasonal Parasite Pressure Patterns

### Temperate Climate Seasonal Cycles

**Spring:** Peak nematodes. Deworm, move to clean pastures. **Summer:** High nematodes, peak coccidiosis. Monitor monthly, improve sanitation. **Fall:** Declining transmission. Treat if burden high, lice treatment before housing. **Winter:** Low external, high internal in housed animals. Clean litter weekly, isolate affected.

### Geographic and Climate Variations

**Wet climates:** Extended season, high coccidiosis risk, fluke risk. **Dry:** Short season, fast parasite kill. **Tropical:** Year-round transmission. **Cold/high altitude:** Short season, winter housing concentrates risk. Plan around local climate.

</section>

<section id="climate-adapt">

## Climate-Specific Parasite Management Adaptations

### Temperate (Cool/Wet) Regions — 4-6 Month Season

**Challenge:** Spring/early summer nematode peak followed by summer coccidiosis in wet years. **Strategy:** 1) Drench all young animals pre-spring (March-April). 2) Move to clean/hay-fed pasture April-May. 3) Summer: monthly fecal exams, treat if >100 EPG. 4) Fall treatment before housing (lice control). 5) Winter: monitor housed animals, change litter weekly. **Rotation:** 4-6 paddocks sufficient; spell 4-6 weeks minimum. **Dosing:** Standard. **Drug choice:** Benzimidazole (Feb-April), rotate to levamisole (June-July), ivermectin (Sept).

:::tip
**Spring prep checklist:** Treat in late winter (February-March, 2-3 weeks before turnout), allowing time for efficacy before larval season peaks.
:::

### Tropical/Subtropical (Year-Round Transmission)

**Challenge:** Continuous infection, no seasonal relief. **Strategy:** 1) Treat all young <12 months monthly (every 4 weeks). 2) Treat adults quarterly. 3) Pasture rotation essential but insufficient — must combine with treatment. 4) Water source control critical (snails, crypto). 5) Intensive sanitation (change feeders weekly, manure >60°C). **Rotation:** 10+ paddocks minimum, 2 week graze, 6-8 week spell. **Drug choice:** Rotate classes every 3 months to prevent resistance (benzimidazole/levamisole/ivermectin/combination).

### Arid/Dry Climates (Short Season, Fast Die-Off)

**Challenge:** Low baseline but rapid outbreak if contamination. **Strategy:** 1) Minimal treatment needed; fecal-based only. 2) Focus on water source protection. 3) Rotation less critical but still valuable. 4) Sanitation (compost manure) highly effective. 5) Monitor young animals closely during rare wet seasons (parasite surge). **Dosing:** Standard when needed. **Frequency:** As-needed rather than scheduled.

:::warning
**Arid region trap:** Low parasite baseline can mask ongoing low-level contamination. A single wet month or unseasonal rain can trigger outbreak. Keep anthelmintics on hand even in low-risk areas.
:::

### Cold/High-Altitude Regions (8-12 Week Season)

**Challenge:** Compressed season, intense winter housing concentrates parasites. **Strategy:** 1) Time treatment for 2-3 weeks pre-peak (estimate local spring warmup date). 2) Move to clean pasture immediately post-treatment. 3) Housing: change litter 1-2x weekly, compost on-site. 4) Reduce stocking density indoors. **Rotation:** 3-4 paddocks acceptable (long rest periods kill parasites naturally). **Drug choice:** Single effective drug sufficient if rotation strategy solid.

<section id="surveillance">

## Community Veterinary Parasite Surveillance

### Setting Up Herd Monitoring Program

**Objective:** Track parasite prevalence/resistance patterns. **Participation:** 5-10 herds, monthly/quarterly sampling. **Data:** Herd ID, age/species, management type, parasite types, egg counts. **Analysis:** Trend by season, compare between herds, track resistance. **Feedback:** Share results, recommendations.

### Resistance Monitoring and Community Response

**Early signs:** Increased EPG post-treatment, higher doses needed, increased frequency. **Confirm:** Fecal egg reduction test (FECRT): EPG pre/post, calculate % (should be 95%+). **Response:** Alert community, switch classes, intensify management, share strategies, plan rotation.

</section>
