**Laceration and Eye Injury Hazard**
Flint knapping produces razor-sharp flakes and fragments that cause deep lacerations and puncture wounds. Stone chips travel at high velocity and can cause permanent eye damage. Always wear safety glasses or goggles and heavy leather gloves. Work on a leather lap pad to catch fragments. Never knap toward your body or other people. Obsidian flakes can be sharper than surgical scalpels — handle all debitage as if it were a blade. Dispose of waste flakes safely; they remain hazardous indefinitely. Seek hands-on instruction from an experienced knapper before attempting advanced techniques.

<section id="overview">

## Overview

Flint knapping—the creation of sharp stone tools through controlled flaking—is a fundamental skill in low-technology survival. A sharp stone blade (created through knapping) can be used for cutting, scraping, hunting, and processing materials for shelter and clothing. This guide covers the geology and selection of flint-like stone (flint, obsidian, chert, jasper), percussion flaking with hard and soft hammers, pressure flaking for refinement, biface (two-sided blade) reduction sequences, blade production from prepared cores, hafting (attachment) methods using sinew and natural pitch adhesives, safety, and troubleshooting.

Flint knapping requires patience and practice—a skilled knapper can produce a usable cutting tool in 30 seconds, but novices may require 10–20 minutes of practice.

</section>

<section id="stone-selection">

## Stone Selection and Properties

Not all stone can be knapped. The ideal materials are fine-grained, homogeneous, and brittle in a way that produces predictable fractures.

### Ideal Stone Types

**Flint (cryptocrystalline quartz):**
- Appearance: opaque, usually dark gray, black, or brown; smooth when broken
- Properties: extremely hard, creates razor-sharp edges, takes a high polish
- Origin: found as nodules in chalk deposits; common in Northern Europe
- Workability: excellent; predictable fracture patterns

**Obsidian (volcanic glass):**
- Appearance: shiny black or translucent green/red; glass-like luster
- Properties: sharper than steel; extremely brittle
- Origin: found near volcanic areas (Pacific Northwest, Mediterranean, East Africa, Central America)
- Workability: excellent; sharp edges are easy to produce but edges are fragile

**Chert (cryptocrystalline quartz):**
- Appearance: similar to flint; ranges from black to red, yellow, or translucent
- Properties: slightly less hard than flint, but still sharp and durable
- Origin: widespread; found in sedimentary deposits, often in riverbeds
- Workability: good; slightly more unpredictable than flint but readily available

**Jasper (cryptocrystalline quartz):**
- Appearance: opaque, red, yellow, brown, or multicolored
- Properties: harder than flint, takes an edge but not as sharp
- Origin: found worldwide in many geological settings
- Workability: acceptable; more resistant to fracture than ideal knapping stones

**Basalt (volcanic rock):**
- Appearance: dark gray or black; crystalline structure visible
- Properties: less brittle than flint; produces dull edges
- Origin: widespread; found in lava flows
- Workability: poor; useful for percussion (hammering) tools rather than cutting edges

### Stone to Avoid

- **Quartz (non-cryptocrystalline):** Forms granular chunks; doesn't produce sharp edges
- **Granite:** Coarse-grained; fractures unpredictably
- **Schist or shale:** Layers that split unevenly; can't control flake direction
- **Wet stone:** Absorbed water reduces the stone's ability to fracture predictably
- **Weathered stone:** Outer surface oxidation creates different fracture patterns than fresh stone

### Testing Stone Quality

Before committing time to knapping:
1. Strike the stone sharply with a hammer on its edge
2. Observe the resulting fracture: does it break into sharp flakes, or does it crumble?
3. Sharp, clean fractures = good knapping stone
4. Crumbling or shattering into many small pieces = poor stone

## Flaking Techniques: Percussion and Pressure

### Percussion Flaking (Primary Reduction)

Percussion flaking uses hammer strikes to remove large flakes and shape the stone roughly.

**Hard hammer (percussion hammer):**
- Material: stone (basalt, granite) or bone; dense and heavy
- Mass: 300–800 grams (0.7–1.75 lbs)
- Shape: roughly spherical or hammerhead; smooth working surface
- Technique: strike the stone's edge or platform at a ~30–45° angle, aiming to remove a flake 2–5 cm long
- Force: solid, confident strikes (hesitant tapping usually bounces off rather than flaking)
- Result: large flakes removed quickly; creates a usable, but rough-edged blade

**Soft hammer (bone or antler hammer):**
- Material: bone, antler, or hardwood
- Mass: 150–300 grams (lighter than hard hammer)
- Shape: rounded or chisel-shaped end for striking
- Technique: similar to hard hammer, but with slightly less force
- Advantage: more control; produces smaller, more refined flakes; less likely to over-fracture
- Result: more precise control of flake size and direction

**Softer hammer progression:**
Some knappers use a series of progressive soft hammers:
1. Hard hammer: initial shaping (removes 2–5 cm flakes)
2. Medium antler hammer: refinement (removes 1–2 cm flakes)
3. Soft bone or wooden hammer: finishing (removes 5–10 mm flakes for edge refinement)

### Pressure Flaking (Refinement)

Pressure flaking uses levered force (not hammer impact) to remove small, precise flakes.

**Tool construction:**
- Shaft: bone or wooden stick, ~30 cm long, ~6 mm diameter
- Point: sharpened or split to create a small contact point
- Handle: can be held in hand or used with a lever action

**Technique:**
1. Position the tool point against the edge of the stone at a ~45° angle
2. Push or lever firmly (not strike) to apply gradual pressure
3. The stone will flake; the size of the flake depends on the applied force and angle
4. Remove chips 5–10 mm in size (smaller than percussion flaking)
5. Repeat along the edge to refine the blade

**Control and precision:**
- Pressure flaking gives far more control than percussion
- Can target specific locations on the edge
- Produces finer, more consistent flakes
- Takes longer but yields higher-quality tools

## Biface Reduction Sequence

A biface is a tool worked on both sides, creating a sharp edge all around. The biface reduction sequence is a standard progression for creating blades, hand axes, and points.

### Blank Preparation (Platform Creation)

Before removing flakes, create a platform (a striking surface):

1. **Select a core:** A chunk of stone with at least one relatively flat face
2. **Strike the first flake:** Use a hard hammer to remove a large flake from one edge, creating a roughly flat working surface
3. **Check the platform:** The surface should be at ~30–40° angle to the direction you want to flake

### Biface Reduction Stages

**Stage 1: Rough shaping (hard hammer, 5–10 minutes)**
- Strike the edge alternately from both sides (flip the stone over after each strike)
- Remove large flakes (2–5 cm) to shape the stone into a rough lens shape
- Focus on creating an overall form rather than edge sharpness
- The edge will be irregular and wavy

**Stage 2: Refinement (soft hammer, 5–10 minutes)**
- Switch to a soft hammer or antler
- Continue alternating sides
- Smaller flakes (1–2 cm) are removed
- Begin regularizing the edge; it becomes sharper but still rough

**Stage 3: Edge finishing (pressure flaking or soft hammer, 5–20 minutes)**
- Use pressure flaking for maximum control
- Remove very small flakes (5–10 mm) from the edge
- Goal: create a straight, sharp edge; the edge should cut skin easily if tested carefully
- Continue until satisfied with edge geometry

**Final result:** A biface blade with two sharp edges and a relatively sharp point (if the biface is pointed in shape).

<section id="blade-production">

## Blade Production from Prepared Cores

Blade production (creating thin, long, razor-sharp blades from a specially prepared core) is an advanced technique.

### Blade Core Preparation

A blade core is shaped to produce predictable, parallel-sided blades.

1. **Core shape:** cylindrical, 8–12 cm tall, with a flat top (striking platform)
2. **Platform angle:** the top is ground or flaked to a ~75° angle (steep) to the core body
3. **Ridge preparation:** using pressure flaking, create a visible ridge down the center of the core (helps guide subsequent blade removal)
4. **Platform lip:** a small lip (1–2 mm) at the platform edge helps initiate blade flakes

### Blade Removal Sequence

1. **Hard hammer strike:** Strike the platform edge firmly, angling slightly downward; a blade flake (thin, long, ~2 cm wide × 8 cm long) should separate from the core
2. **Blade character:** ideal blades are parallel-sided, thin (3–5 mm), and sharp on both lateral edges
3. **Repeat:** rotate the core and strike again, producing a second blade
4. **Successive blades:** cores can produce 20–40 blades before becoming exhausted

**Blade advantages:**
- Extremely sharp (two sharp edges per blade)
- Efficient: produce many blades from a single core
- Versatile: can be hafted as arrow or spear points, used as cutting blades, or fractured into smaller cutting implements

**Blade disadvantages:**
- More fragile than biface tools
- Requires more practice to master
- Core preparation is time-consuming

<section id="hafting">

## Hafting: Attaching Blades to Handles

A blade is significantly more useful when attached to a handle (hafted). Hafting also protects the user's hand from the blade's edge.

### Hafting Methods and Materials

**Sinew (animal tendon) wrapping:**
- Material: sinew from deer, elk, or other large animals; dry and pound to separate the fibers
- Preparation: Soak sinew briefly to soften it; it becomes very strong when wet and even stronger when it dries
- Method:
  1. Place the blade against a wooden or bone handle
  2. Wrap sinew tightly around the blade and handle (winding the fibers around)
  3. Make 8–12 wraps to hold the blade securely
  4. Allow to dry (sinew shrinks and becomes very tight as it dries)
- Advantage: strong, renewable (can use sinew repeatedly if bound loosely)
- Disadvantage: can come loose if moisture causes swelling; requires replacement eventually

**Pitch and hide adhesive:**
- Material: tree pitch or tar (see below for preparation)
- Method:
  1. Heat the pitch until it's viscous (like honey)
  2. Apply to the blade tang (the portion that will be inserted into or against the handle)
  3. Press the blade into the handle
  4. Wrap sinew around it while the pitch is still warm for extra reinforcement
  5. Allow to cool and harden completely
- Advantage: very strong; permanent bond
- Disadvantage: difficult to redo; requires heat to disassemble

**Pitch adhesive preparation (from tree resin):**
1. Collect raw pitch (resin that seeps from pine, spruce, or fir trees)
2. Heat slowly in a stone or ceramic container (direct heat will burn it)
3. Add powdered charcoal (10% by volume) to make it less sticky and easier to work with
4. Heat until it flows like honey (thin enough to apply but thick enough to hold shape)
5. Store in a closed container; can be reheated when needed

**Bone or horn shaft construction:**
- Ideal hafting material: bone or horn is straight-grained and strong
- Shape: drill a hole through the bone (using a stick and sand to gradually enlarge the hole) to fit the blade tang
- Insert the blade: use sinew wrapping and pitch to secure

### Simple Hand Axe (Blade + Wooden Handle)

1. Select a flint or obsidian biface blade with a sharp edge
2. Wrap sinew around the edge where it will be held (protects hand)
3. Lash to a sturdy wooden handle (wood piece ~30 cm, gripped at one end) using sinew wrapping and pitch adhesive
4. Test the attachment: the blade should not move relative to the handle

## Safety and Health Considerations

### Eye and Hand Protection

- **Eye protection:** Stone flakes can fly; wear eye protection or keep your face back from where flakes will travel
- **Gloves:** Wear thick leather gloves to protect hands (flint produces razor-sharp fragments)
- **Sharp edge awareness:** Flaked stone is extremely sharp; handle carefully and cut away from your body

### Health Hazards

- **Silicosis:** Stone dust from flaking contains silica; inhaling large amounts can cause respiratory disease
- **Prevention:** Work outdoors or with ventilation; wear a dust mask if working indoors
- **Cut wounds:** Stone is sharper than steel; cuts can be deep and bleed profusely. Keep a first aid kit nearby.

### Disposal and Cleanup

- **Sharp fragments:** Sweep up all stone chips; many are microscopic and very sharp
- **Wear shoes:** Never work barefoot on a flint knapping area
- **Water source:** Have water available to rinse stone dust from eyes and hands

<section id="troubleshooting">

## Troubleshooting and Common Problems

**Problem: Stone shatters into many small pieces (no clean flake)**
- Cause: Stone is low quality (too brittle or granular); or wrong angle of strike
- Fix: Try a different stone; check the strike angle (should be 30–45°, not perpendicular)

**Problem: Flakes are too small or chip off unexpectedly**
- Cause: Hammer is too light; or platform angle is too steep
- Fix: Use a harder, heavier hammer; adjust the platform angle (aim for ~40° rather than 75°)

**Problem: Flakes curve sideways instead of going straight**
- Cause: Ridge direction is off; or the strike is angled incorrectly
- Fix: Create a more prominent ridge on the core; aim the hammer strike more parallel to the ridge

**Problem: Edge is dull after flaking**
- Cause: Wrong stone type (too coarse); or edge geometry is wrong
- Fix: Try flint or obsidian instead of other stone; ensure the edge is thin (pressure flake away the thick bits)

**Problem: Blade fractures when hafted**
- Cause: Too much force applied during hafting; or blade is too thin
- Fix: Use gentle pressure when hafting; try a slightly thicker blade

<section id="conclusion">

## Conclusion

Stone tool production is a fundamental skill for anyone in a low-technology environment. With practice and the right stone source, a skilled knapper can produce sharp cutting tools from rocks and stones. The progression from rough hammering to fine pressure flaking develops control and intuition. Start with easily accessible stone (chert, jasper) and practice making simple bifaces before advancing to the more demanding blade core preparation and hafting. The combination of a sharp stone blade, a well-crafted handle, and proper hafting creates a versatile tool that can serve cutting, hunting, and processing tasks for many years.
