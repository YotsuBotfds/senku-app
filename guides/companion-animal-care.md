## Overview

<section id="overview">

Dogs and cats are easiest to keep healthy when the basics are stable: water, food, shelter, sanitation, and daily observation. In off-grid or supply-limited settings, the goal is not luxury care. The goal is to keep the animal alive, comfortable, clean enough to stay healthy, and separated from food stores so it does not become a sanitation or rodent problem.

This guide focuses on routine care for household dogs and cats. It is written for practical use when commercial pet food, power, or veterinary access are limited.

Use this guide for stable pets and everyday care questions: feeding, water, shelter, sanitation, diet transitions, mild behavior changes, and first-pass symptom triage. If the animal is actively sick, injured, collapsed, having trouble breathing, or needs medication, surgery, or birthing help, hand off to [Veterinary Medicine](veterinary.html).

Core priorities:
- Water before almost everything else
- Species-appropriate food in consistent portions
- Dry shelter with wind and heat protection
- Clean waste handling
- Early recognition of vomiting, diarrhea, lethargy, dehydration, and breathing trouble
- Strong separation between pet feeding areas and human food storage

</section>

## Daily Care at a Glance

<section id="daily-care-at-a-glance">

| Task | Dogs | Cats |
|---|---|---|
| Water | Fresh water always available | Fresh water always available, ideally in more than one place |
| Food | 1 to 2 meals per day; adjust by size and activity | Smaller meals, often 2 to 4 feedings per day |
| Shelter | Dry bedding, wind block, shade, insulated floor | Dry bed or hiding box, quiet location, draft protection |
| Waste | Pick up stool daily; keep a toilet area away from food | Clean litter box daily; replace litter regularly |
| Check | Appetite, stool, energy, limping, coughing | Appetite, vomiting, litter habits, hiding, coat condition |

If you can only do three things well, do these:
1. Keep water clean and reachable.
2. Feed on a predictable schedule.
3. Keep waste away from food, water, and sleeping areas.

## How Do I Feed My Dog Without Dog Food?

<section id="feed-dog-without-dog-food">

Dogs are flexible eaters. A healthy adult dog can do well on a mixed human-food ration if the ration is balanced and the dog is not being overfed. The most useful off-grid rule is simple: a dog needs enough protein, fat, carbohydrates, and calcium to stay in condition.

### Good off-grid dog foods

- Cooked meat, poultry, or fish with bones removed
- Eggs
- Plain rice, oats, potatoes, or pasta
- Pumpkin, carrots, green beans, and similar vegetables
- Small amounts of plain yogurt or kefir if the dog tolerates dairy
- Organ meat in moderation, especially liver and heart

### Practical rationing

- Most adult dogs eat about 2 to 3 percent of body weight per day in total food.
- Active, cold-weather, nursing, or working dogs may need more.
- Overweight, sedentary, or elderly dogs may need less.
- Feed in measured portions instead of leaving food out all day if scavenging is a problem.

### Important balancing points

- Do not feed only lean meat for long periods. It lacks enough calcium and can cause nutritional imbalance.
- Do not rely on only bread, rice, or scraps with little protein.
- If you are using home-prepared food for more than a short emergency, add a calcium source and keep the diet varied.
- Change diets gradually over 5 to 7 days so the stomach and bowel adjust.

### Foods to avoid

- Onions, garlic, chives, and leeks
- Chocolate, coffee, tea, and other caffeine sources
- Grapes and raisins
- Xylitol or anything labeled sugar-free with xylitol
- Alcohol
- Moldy food or spoiled meat
- Cooked bones, especially poultry bones
- Very salty scraps in large amounts

### Simple field meal pattern

For a medium dog, a practical meal can be:
- One part cooked meat or fish
- One part starch such as rice, oats, or potato
- One part cooked vegetables
- A small calcium source if the food is home-prepared

If the dog is vomiting, has diarrhea, or refuses food for more than a day, stop trying to "push through" with scraps and watch the animal closely for dehydration or obstruction signs.

</section>

## How Do I Feed a Cat Without Cat Food?

<section id="feed-cat-without-cat-food">

Cats are not small dogs. They are obligate carnivores and need meat-based food more than dogs do. A cat can survive a short emergency on improvised food, but a long-term cat ration must stay meat-heavy and should not become mostly grain or vegetables.

### Good off-grid cat foods

- Cooked chicken, turkey, rabbit, beef, or fish
- Heart and other meat tissues
- Small amounts of liver, not as the main food
- Eggs
- Canned fish in water, used as a backup rather than a full-time diet

### Cat-specific needs

- Cats need taurine and other nutrients found mainly in animal tissue.
- Cats should not be fed a dog diet for long periods.
- Cats should not fast for long. A cat that stops eating can become seriously ill quickly.
- Kittens, pregnant cats, nursing cats, and overweight cats are at higher risk if they are not eating.

### Practical rationing

- Feed smaller meals more often than you would feed a dog.
- Wet food or moist meat is better than dry, dusty scraps when water is limited.
- Add a little clean water to the food if the cat will eat it.

### Foods to avoid

- Onion, garlic, and chive family foods
- Chocolate and caffeine
- Raw dough
- Alcohol
- Spoiled meat or fish
- Dog food as the only diet for long periods

### Cat food rule of thumb

If you are making a temporary emergency ration, make it mostly meat and treat it as a bridge back to proper cat food. If a cat is not eating well, do not wait for several days. Cats can go downhill fast.

</section>

## Hydration

<section id="hydration">

Clean water matters more than almost any other daily task. A pet that is mildly underfed can often recover. A pet that is severely dehydrated can crash fast.

### Good hydration habits

- Keep water bowls clean and filled.
- Put water in more than one place if the animal has access to a large area.
- Use a heavy bowl that cannot be tipped easily.
- In cold weather, check that water has not frozen.
- In hot weather, offer extra bowls in shaded areas.

### Signs of dehydration

- Dry gums
- Sunken eyes
- Weakness or sluggish movement
- Thick saliva
- Skin that returns slowly when gently pinched
- Dark urine or very little urine

### When hydration becomes urgent

- The animal will not drink
- Vomiting prevents water from staying down
- Diarrhea is heavy or repeated
- The animal is weak, collapsed, or unsteady
- The gums are tacky or pale

For a sick cat, not drinking for even part of a day can matter. For a dog, dehydration plus vomiting or diarrhea is a serious concern even if the dog still seems alert.

</section>

## Shelter and Temperature

<section id="shelter-temperature">

Dogs and cats do not need fancy housing, but they do need dry, protected shelter. Wet bedding, wind exposure, and overheating are common preventable problems.

### Shelter basics

- Keep bedding dry.
- Block wind and direct rain.
- Raise bedding off cold ground if possible.
- Give cats a quiet box, crate, or room where they can hide.
- Give dogs a bed or mat that stays out of standing water and mud.

### Cold weather

- Use dry straw, blankets, or washable bedding.
- Keep shelter small enough to trap body heat but large enough to move in.
- Check paws, ears, and tail tips for cold injury.
- Feed a little more if the animal is burning extra calories to stay warm.

### Hot weather

- Shade and airflow matter more than a closed kennel.
- Never leave a pet in a parked vehicle or sealed shed.
- Watch for panting, drooling, weakness, and confusion.
- Cats often hide when hot or stressed, so their distress can be easy to miss.

### Indoor safety

If the animal lives indoors, keep the sleeping area away from ovens, heaters, fuel fumes, and food prep surfaces. A healthy pet still becomes a problem if it sheds on food, sleeps in storage, or knocks over supplies.

</section>

## Waste and Sanitation

<section id="waste-sanitation">

Waste control is one of the fastest ways to stop a pet from becoming a health problem.

### Dogs

- Pick up stool every day.
- Keep a regular toilet area away from vegetable beds, wells, and food storage.
- Clean urine-soaked bedding quickly so odor does not attract pests.
- Wash the dog after heavy mud, feces, or carcass contact.

### Cats

- Use one litter box per cat plus one extra if possible.
- Scoop daily.
- Keep litter away from the kitchen, pantry, and sleeping food stores.
- Replace or refresh litter before the odor gets strong enough to attract flies or rodents.

### Off-grid litter options

- Commercial litter if available
- Dry sand
- Wood pellets
- Dry soil used only for the box, not from food-growing areas

Do not use waste from the box or dog toilet area as fertilizer on edible crops. Keep it out of wells, cisterns, and wash water.

</section>

## Is My Cat Vomiting a Danger Sign?

<section id="cat-vomiting-danger-sign">

One vomit does not always mean an emergency. Repeated vomiting, vomiting with weakness, or vomiting with refusal to eat is much more serious.

### Usually less urgent

- One isolated hairball
- One episode after eating too fast
- A cat that vomited once but is still alert, drinking, and behaving normally

### Danger signs

- Repeated vomiting in a short period
- Vomiting plus lethargy or hiding
- Not eating for 24 hours or more
- Blood in vomit
- A swollen or painful belly
- Trouble breathing
- Diarrhea with vomiting
- Vomiting after possible poison exposure
- A kitten, senior cat, pregnant cat, or overweight cat that stops eating

### Why this matters

A cat that keeps vomiting can dehydrate quickly, and a cat that stops eating can develop a serious liver problem. If the cat is vomiting more than once, cannot keep water down, or seems weak, treat it as a real warning sign rather than a minor upset stomach.

</section>

## Warning Signs That Need Attention

<section id="warning-signs">

Use the table below as a quick triage guide.

| Sign | What it can mean | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Not eating | Pain, nausea, fever, stress, dental trouble, obstruction | Watch closely for 12 to 24 hours; sooner if cat, kitten, or already sick |
| Not drinking | Dehydration, fever, mouth pain, kidney or stomach trouble | Offer water in multiple places; urgent if weak or vomiting |
| Repeated vomiting | Toxin, obstruction, infection, kidney or liver issue | Treat as urgent |
| Diarrhea with weakness | Infection, diet shock, parasites, dehydration | Isolate and monitor hydration |
| Bloated belly | Gas, obstruction, internal problem | Emergency |
| Coughing or labored breathing | Heat stress, heart/lung problem, choking | Emergency |
| Pale gums | Poor circulation, blood loss, shock | Emergency |
| Collapse or wobbling | Heat stroke, poisoning, severe illness | Emergency |
| Eye discharge, thick nasal discharge | Infection or irritation | Monitor closely; seek care if worsening |
| Sudden pain, yelping, hiding, aggression | Injury or internal pain | Rest and assess; seek care if severe |

### Dog-specific urgent signs

- Distended painful abdomen
- Repeated unproductive retching
- Sudden weakness after exercise or heat
- Swollen face after a bite or sting

### Cat-specific urgent signs

- Not eating
- Repeated vomiting
- Straining in the litter box without urine output
- Rapid breathing
- Sudden hiding with stiffness or pain

</section>

## Keeping Pets from Becoming a Health or Food-Storage Problem

<section id="food-storage-problem">

The easiest way to prevent trouble is to separate pet routines from human food routines.

### Food storage rules

- Store pet food in sealed containers.
- Do not leave open bags on the floor.
- Keep pet food in a dry place away from mice, insects, and moisture.
- Clean spills immediately.
- Feed animals in one designated place.
- Remove uneaten wet food before it spoils.

### Rodent control

Pets can attract rodents if food is left out. Rodents then contaminate human food and spread disease.

- Use tight bins with lids.
- Keep bowls off pantry shelves and counters.
- Do not let cats or dogs eat from the same area where grain, flour, or dried food is stored.
- Keep crumbs and scraps cleaned up so pests do not build a food trail.

### Human food safety

- Do not let dogs lick pots, cutting boards, or counters used for raw meat.
- Keep cat litter and dog waste far from food prep areas.
- Wash hands after handling bowls, waste, bedding, or raw meat.
- If an animal has diarrhea, vomiting, or flea contamination, keep it away from food storage until it is clean and stable.

### Behavior habits that help

- Feed on a schedule so animals do not beg constantly.
- Train dogs not to counter-surf or raid trash.
- Feed cats enough that they do not hunt human food but not so much that they lose interest in normal eating patterns.
- Use closed trash cans and secure compost.

</section>

## Off-Grid Routine

<section id="off-grid-routine">

This routine works when power, stores, or vet access are limited.

### Morning

- Check water first.
- Look at appetite and energy.
- Check stool, litter, or urine.
- Look for limping, coughing, or discharge.

### Evening

- Feed measured portions.
- Clean bowls.
- Remove waste.
- Check bedding for wet spots, fleas, ticks, or dirt.

### Weekly

- Brush coat and remove mats.
- Trim nails if needed.
- Check body condition.
- Refresh bedding.
- Inspect shelter for leaks, drafts, pests, or sharp edges.

### If supplies are tight

1. Keep the animal drinking.
2. Feed species-appropriate food.
3. Protect the shelter.
4. Remove waste.
5. Watch for early warning signs.

</section>

## Short Rules to Remember

<section id="short-rules">

- Dogs can live on practical mixed food; cats need a meat-heavy diet.
- Water is non-negotiable.
- Cats should not go long without eating.
- Vomiting plus weakness, dehydration, or no appetite is a real warning sign.
- Waste control protects both the household and the food supply.
- A pet that is not separated from pantry, bins, and waste areas eventually becomes a pest or contamination problem.

</section>

## When to Seek Help

<section id="when-to-seek-help">

Seek veterinary help if you can when:

- The animal cannot keep water down
- Vomiting or diarrhea continues or gets worse
- The animal collapses, cannot stand, or seems confused
- Breathing becomes hard or noisy
- The belly is swollen and painful
- The animal has not eaten for a full day, especially if it is a cat
- You suspect poisoning, obstruction, major injury, or heat stroke

When in doubt, isolate the animal, offer clean water, keep it warm or cool as needed, and watch closely for change. Fast observation is often what prevents a small problem from becoming a crisis.

If the animal is not stable, is getting worse, or has signs that fit the vet-triage list above, stop treating it as a routine care issue and use [Veterinary Medicine](veterinary.html).

</section>
