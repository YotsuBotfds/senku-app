## Overview

Cattle convert grass into high-quality protein and manure for soil building. Beef cattle require minimal management—primarily pasture rotation, supplemental hay in winter, water, and disease monitoring. A single cow-calf pair can sustain itself on 1–2 acres of good pasture and produce 500–800 lbs of meat at butchering (18–24 months of age). This guide covers breed selection, pasture management, feed calculations, common disease recognition, calving assistance, and integration with butchering systems.

## Breed Selection: Beef vs. Dairy vs. Dual-Purpose

Cattle breeds are specialized for beef, dairy (milk), or dual-purpose (meat and milk). For meat production, beef breeds are most efficient.

### Beef Breeds

| **Breed** | **Frame Size** | **Growth Rate** | **Feed Efficiency** | **Temperament** | **Notes** |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|---------------------|-----------------|-----------|
| **Angus** | Medium (1200–1800 lbs) | Fast (2–2.5 lbs/day) | Excellent | Calm, docile | Most popular in US; black color; polled (no horns) |
| **Hereford** | Medium-large (1400–2000 lbs) | Fast | Excellent | Calm | Red body, white face; polled/horned varieties; hardy |
| **Charolais** | Large (1500–2200 lbs) | Very fast | Excellent | Calm, gentle | Cream-colored; fast growth; feed-efficient |
| **Simmental** | Large (1600–2400 lbs) | Very fast | Good | Calm | Dual-colored or solid; good feed conversion; large frame |
| **Brahman** | Large (1600–2400 lbs) | Moderate | Fair | Can be flighty | Heat/parasite tolerance; useful for hot climates |
| **Red Angus** | Medium (1100–1600 lbs) | Moderate | Good | Docile | Similar to Black Angus; red color; polled |

### Dual-Purpose Breeds (Meat + Milk)

Dual-purpose breeds produce both milk (for family use or sale) and meat.

| **Breed** | **Milk/Year** | **Meat Potential** | **Temperament** | **Notes** |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|-----------------|-----------|
| **Shorthorn** | 3000–5000 lbs | Moderate | Docile | Rare; heritage breed; good family milk and meat |
| **Dexter** | 2000–4000 lbs (small cow) | Moderate (small) | Calm, manageable | Very small (600–900 lbs); ideal for small farms |
| **Belted Galloway** | 2000–3000 lbs | Moderate | Calm | Distinctive black with white stripe; cold-hardy |

### Calf Source

- **Feeder cattle** (3–6 months old, 150–300 lbs): Buy at livestock auctions; raise from calf stage to butchering weight (18–24 months, 1000–1200 lbs). Cheapest option; requires bottle-feeding early calves.
- **Cow-calf pair** (bred cow + nursing calf): Bred cows produce 1 calf per year; calf nursing reduces dairy stress. More expensive initially but self-sustaining.
- **Beef herd** (multiple cows + one bull): Pasture-based breeding system; bulls can be rented.

:::info-box
For a family beef operation, purchase 2–4 feeder calves (6 months old, 250–350 lbs each) in spring; raise on pasture + supplemental hay; butcher the following fall/winter at 18–20 months old. Total cost: ~$400–800 per calf; total meat: ~500–600 lbs per calf.
:::

## Housing and Fencing

Cattle are hardy and thrive outdoors in most climates with minimal shelter. Primary needs: shade in summer, windbreak in winter, water, and secure fencing.

### Shelter

**Pasture shelter** (minimal):
- **Run-in shed**: 3-sided structure, open south-facing, ~200 sq ft for 4 cattle. Provides shade and wind protection.
- **Windbreak**: Trees or hedgerow on north side of pasture.
- **Water access**: Year-round; in winter, provide unfrozen water via heated trough or frequent refill.

**Winter housing** (only in extreme cold):
- **Space**: 40–50 square feet per cow.
- **Floor**: Well-bedded (straw, sawdust); provide drainage.
- **Ventilation**: Prevent ammonia buildup; open roof vents.

### Fencing

Cattle require 4-foot minimum fencing; 5 feet is better for larger breeds.

**Fence types:**
- **Woven wire (field fence)**: 4–5 feet, 2–4 inch spacing. Durable; ~$1–2/ft installed.
- **Welded cattle panels**: 5 feet, 2-inch spacing. Easy to move; portable; ~$2–3/ft.
- **Electric fence**: Single or double strand; portable. Good for rotational grazing. Requires power source.
- **Avoid**: Barbed wire alone (snagging injury); narrow spacing can trap heads.

**Perimeter check**: Walk fence weekly for gaps, rust, or damage. Repair immediately.

**Gates**: 4 feet wide minimum; heavy-duty hinges and latches.

<section id="pasture-management">

## Pasture Management and Rotational Grazing

Cattle gain weight faster on good pasture than grain alone. Rotational grazing prevents overgrazing, improves soil, and manages parasites.

### Pasture Quality

**Good pasture composition:**
- 60–70% grasses (timothy, orchard grass, tall fescue, bluegrass).
- 20–30% legumes (clover, alfalfa, sainfoin) for nitrogen fixation and protein.
- <5% weeds.

**Quality assessment:**
- Lush, green growth = abundant nutrition.
- Brown, sparse, short grass = overgrazed or poor soil.
- Bare patches = overgrazing; remove cattle, allow 4+ weeks recovery.

### Rotational Grazing System (4-Paddock Example)

Divide pasture into 4 sections. Move cattle every 7–14 days.

**Example: 40 acres, 4 paddocks (10 acres each), 20 cattle:**

| **Week** | **Cattle Location** | **Other Paddocks** |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| 1 | Paddock A (grazing) | B, C, D resting |
| 2 | Paddock B (grazing) | A, C, D resting |
| 3 | Paddock C (grazing) | A, B, D resting |
| 4 | Paddock D (grazing) | A, B, C resting |
| 5 | Return to Paddock A | (4 weeks recovery) |

**Benefits:**
- Pasture recovers between grazings; longer growing season.
- Uniform manure distribution; improved soil structure.
- Parasite control; eggs drop on dung; cattle move away before reinfection occurs.
- Higher productivity: 80–90% utilization vs. 40–50% continuous grazing.

### Stocking Rate

**Pasture-based cattle production:**
- **Excellent pasture** (rich, well-watered): 1–2 cattle per acre.
- **Good pasture** (typical managed): 0.5–1 cattle per acre.
- **Marginal pasture** (dry, sparse): 0.25–0.5 cattle per acre.

Rotate to allow adequate recovery (3–4 week rest period between grazings). Monitor pasture height; if grass falls below 4 inches, stop grazing to allow recovery.

<section id="feeding-calculations">

## Feeding Requirements and Calculations

Cattle eat 2–3% of body weight daily in forage (grass, hay).

### Pasture Season (Spring–Fall)

**Typical:**
- 1000 lb steer grazes 2–3% body weight = 20–30 lbs fresh grass daily.
- Pasture provides adequate nutrition if quality is good.

**Supplementation:**
- If pasture is sparse or poor quality, add grain or hay.
- Grain supplementation: 0.5–1 lb per 100 lbs body weight per day (optional; increases growth rate).

### Hay Season (Winter, ~120–180 Days)

**Daily hay requirement:**
- **1000 lb cattle**: 2–3% body weight = 20–30 lbs hay per day.
- **Annual requirement** (180 days): 30 lbs/day × 180 days = 5400 lbs = ~3 tons per cow.

**Hay quality matters:**
- **Good hay** (legume-rich, leafy): 12–15% crude protein; 60–70% digestible energy. Cattle maintain/gain weight.
- **Poor hay** (mature grass, weathered): 6–8% protein; <50% digestible energy. Cattle lose weight; supplement with grain.

**Hay storage:**
- 1 large round bale (1200–1500 lbs) covers ~2 weeks for one cow.
- 3 tons per year = ~2 large bales per month, or ~20–24 bales per year per cow.
- Store under cover; rotate old hay first.

### Grain Supplementation (Winter)

**If hay is of poor quality**, add grain:
- **1000 lb cattle**: 0.5–1.5 lbs grain per 100 lbs body weight = 5–15 lbs grain per day.
- **Cost**: Grain costs more than hay but adds weight faster.

**Grain types:**
- **Oats**: 11% protein; safe; palatable.
- **Barley**: 11% protein; good energy.
- **Corn**: High energy but low protein; mix with legumes or other grains.
- **Commercial cattle mix**: 14–16% protein; balanced for beef cattle.

:::warning
Sudden grain changes cause bloat and acidosis (rumen disorder). Introduce grain gradually over 10–14 days, starting with 2–3 lbs/day and increasing slowly.
:::

### Water Requirement

**Daily water intake:**
- **Pasture (summer)**: 8–12 gallons per day per animal.
- **Winter (on dry hay)**: 10–15 gallons per day per animal.

Provide fresh water at all times. Winter: Prevent freezing with heated trough or frequent refill. In pasture, locate water to encourage even grazing distribution.

<section id="common-diseases">

## Common Diseases and Health Monitoring

### Blackleg

Bacterial infection (Clostridium) causing sudden death. Vaccinate calves at 4–6 months; booster at 1 year.

**Signs:** Lameness, fever, death (often sudden with no other signs). Rare in vaccinated cattle.

### Bovine Respiratory Disease (BRD)

Viral/bacterial pneumonia affecting young cattle after stress (shipping, weaning, cold weather).

**Signs:** Nasal discharge, cough, fever, depression, reduced feed intake.

**Prevention:** Minimize stress; provide good ventilation; adequate nutrition; vaccination.

**Treatment:** Antibiotic (consult veterinarian); provide shelter and extra feed.

### Pinkeye

Infectious keratoconjunctivitis (bacterial eye infection) causing eye redness, tearing, and temporary blindness.

**Signs:** Pink/red eye; discharge; squinting; photophobia (light sensitivity).

**Prevention:** Fly control (irritate eyes, spreading infection); adequate ventilation.

**Treatment:** Antibiotic eye ointment; shade/shelter (light sensitivity); recovery usually within 2 weeks.

### Lice and Parasites

External (lice, mange mites) and internal (worms, flukes) parasites reduce growth and health.

**Signs:** Poor growth, rough hair coat, anemia (pale mucous membranes), diarrhea, scratching.

**Prevention:** Rotational grazing (parasite control); regular deworming (1–4 times/year depending on climate and grazing system).

**Treatment:** Antiparasitic (pour-on, injectable, or feed additive; consult veterinarian for recommendations).

### Bloat

Gas accumulation in rumen from rapid legume consumption.

**Signs:** Left-side abdominal distension, difficulty breathing, drooling, distress.

**Prevention:** Introduce legume pasture gradually; don't graze fresh legumes when damp (frost or dew increases foaming).

**Emergency treatment:** Pass a tube into the rumen (dowel rod or stomach tube) to release gas; position animal downslope.

:::danger
Severe bloat is fatal within hours. Treat as emergency.
:::

### Foot Rot

Bacterial infection of hooves causing lameness.

**Signs:** Lameness, swelling above hoof, discharge between claws, odor.

**Prevention:** Maintain dry bedding; avoid muddy pastures; trim hooves regularly.

**Treatment:** Isolate affected animal; trim away diseased tissue; soak hoof in copper sulfate or iodine solution; antibiotic if systemic infection.

<section id="calving-assistance">

## Calving and Assistance

Calving (birth) is typically straightforward but occasionally requires assistance.

### Signs of Imminent Calving

- Udder swelling and filling ("bagging up").
- Relaxation of ligaments around tailhead (palpate, they feel soft).
- Mucus discharge (vaginal plug).
- Restlessness; isolating behavior.
- Visible straining.

### Normal Calving

1. **Early labor**: Pacing, restlessness, tail raised. Lasts 2–12 hours.
2. **Active labor**: Visible straining. First calf appears within 30 minutes of strong pushing.
3. **Expulsion**: Front legs and nose appear first; calf slides out within 15–30 minutes.
4. **Recovery**: Cow rests; calf stands and nurses within 1 hour.

### Assisted Calving (Dystocia)

**When to assist:**
- Straining >30 minutes with no progress.
- Visible malpresentation (only back legs, no front legs; sideways; buttocks first).

**Assistance steps:**
1. Wash hands and forearms; use lubricant.
2. Reach into vagina; feel for calf's position.
3. **Normal presentation**: Two front hooves and nose visible. Pull gently on hooves during contractions.
4. **Malpresentation**: Requires repositioning (requires skill) or veterinary help.
5. Stop if excessive force is needed (risk of uterine rupture or calf death).

**Call veterinarian if:**
- Straining >1 hour with no progress.
- Excessive bleeding or fluid loss.
- Calf is very large relative to cow (genetic issue; may need cesarean section).

:::warning
Difficult calving (dystocia) can kill both cow and calf if not managed properly. Do not attempt aggressive pulling. Call for professional help if progress is slow or situation is unclear.
:::

### After Calving

1. Allow cow to lick and dry calf (bonding).
2. Ensure calf nurses within 2 hours; colostrum (first milk) is critical for immunity.
3. Check for placenta passage (should occur within 8 hours). Retained placenta requires veterinary treatment.
4. Monitor for milk fever (calcium deficiency, rare but serious): signs are weakness, paralysis, lethargy.

</section>

<section id="emergency-herd-management">

## Emergency Herd Management During Drought and Flood

Extreme weather events—prolonged drought and unexpected flooding—test herd survival. Advance planning prevents catastrophic loss.

### Drought Management

**Immediate responses when water/forage become scarce:**

#### Water Rationing Protocols

When water sources dry up or become contaminated:

- **Prioritize:** Lactating cows (produce milk daily) and young calves (grow daily) over dry cows.
- **Minimum water need:** 1000 lb cattle require 8 gallons/day minimum (emergency ration, not sustainable long-term); normal need 10–15 gallons/day.
- **Rationing schedule:** During acute shortage, provide water every 12 hours rather than continuous access. Cattle consume less when stressed by limited availability.
- **Alternative sources:** Pond water (algae-covered is safe; strain vegetation), snowmelt (collect and store in drums), or dig shallow wells if ground water exists.
- **Transport:** In extreme cases, haul water in barrels using cart/animals. One person can deliver 50 gallons to herd daily if source within 1 mile.

:::tip
Cattle excrete up to 50% less urine when water-stressed, reducing manure volume but degrading soil quality. This is temporary; normal function resumes when water abundant again.
:::

#### Emergency Forage Sources (Feed Triage)

When pasture dries completely or becomes depleted:

| Emergency Feed | Calories/lb | Protein % | Notes | Preparation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Crop residues (corn stalks, grain straw)** | 800–1000 | 4–6% | Low nutrition; bulky; must supplement with protein | Chop if possible; soak in water to increase palatability |
| **Hay reserves** | 1200–1400 | 8–12% | Emergency supply depleted quickly; ration at 50% normal | Stretch supply; mix poor-quality with crop residue |
| **Dried range grasses (drought-stressed)** | 600–800 | 2–3% | Extremely low; cattle lose weight rapidly | Collect and dry; supplement with any available legumes |
| **Browse species (tree leaves/branches)** | 1000–1400 | 6–14% | Palatable; cattle eat willingly; variable nutrition | Lop branches; strip leaves; remove toxic species |
| **Dried aquatic plants (duckweed, water hyacinth)** | 900–1100 | 8–12% | High protein; excellent for supplementation if available | Sun-dry; store in sealed containers; may cause bloat if fresh |
| **Milling byproducts (bran, husks, oats)** | 1300–1500 | 8–12% | If trade/barter available; stretches scarce hay | Mix with roughage to prevent bloat |

:::warning
Extreme drought feeding (survival rations only): cattle lose weight rapidly. Pregnant cows on emergency rations may abort. Accept weight loss and plan for slow recovery post-drought; priority is survival, not gain.
:::

#### Cattle Evacuation (Flood Risk)

When flooding threatens:

1. **Identify high ground:** Pre-identify pastures, fields, or neighbor's land at elevation before flooding occurs.
2. **Move timing:** Do not wait until water arrives. Move cattle 2–3 days before predicted flood crest (they move slowly; don't rush).
3. **Loading procedure:**
   - Use portable panels to funnel cattle into trailer or truck
   - Load calmly; panicked cattle are hard to handle
   - Transport in groups; separate ages only if necessary
   - Ensure trailer ventilation (prevent heat stress)

4. **Temporary shelter on high ground:**
   - Electric fence defines temporary grazing area (200–300 square feet per cow minimum)
   - Provide water source (truck, trough, or well on high ground)
   - Shelter: Even temporary 3-sided structure protects from sun and wind
   - Feed: Hay if pasture unavailable (assume 1 week supply per 20 cattle)

5. **Return after water recedes:**
   - Check for water contamination (livestock require fresh water; stagnant or chemical-polluted water causes disease)
   - Pasture covered in silt is unusable for 2–4 weeks (rot-prone plants); graze alternate land if possible
   - Inspect fencing for damage and repair immediately

### Winter Feeding Emergency

When heavy snow prevents pasture access and hay supplies run short:

- **Caloric need increases:** Cold cattle require 20–30% more calories to maintain body heat
- **Rationing strategy:** Feed 1.2–1.5× normal amount for 1–2 weeks of emergency
- **Emergency feed priority:** Legume hay (alfalfa) > grass hay > straw; grain supplementation if available (5–10 lbs/day)
- **Salt and minerals:** Cattle under stress require extra minerals (calcium, magnesium); provide salt block and mineral supplement

:::info-box
After extreme stress (drought, flood, disease outbreak), cattle immune systems are compromised. Monitor carefully for secondary infections (pneumonia, foot rot). Consult veterinarian if illness appears.
:::

<section id="alternative-feed-sources">

## Alternative Feed Sources and Unconventional Forages

In survival/off-grid scenarios, traditional forage sources may fail. Creative feed substitution prevents starvation losses.

### Crop Residues

After grain/vegetable harvest, residues become feed:

#### Corn Stover (Stalks, Cobs, Husks)

- **Yield:** ~1.5 tons dry matter per acre of corn
- **Calories:** ~800 kcal/lb
- **Protein:** 4–6% (marginal; supplement with legume)
- **Preparation:** Harvest stalks after corn removed. Chop (optional); store dry. Cattle prefer chopped material (easier to eat).
- **Feeding:** 10–15 lbs/day per 1000 lb cow; nutritionally incomplete alone (pair with legume hay or grain)

#### Grain Straw (Wheat, Oats, Barley)

- **Yield:** ~0.5–0.8 tons per acre of grain crop
- **Quality:** Fair to poor (lower protein than hay; digestibility 40–50%)
- **Feeding:** 15–20 lbs/day; feeds cattle, but weight loss expected. Straw is filler, not nutrition.
- **Advantage:** Stores indefinitely if kept dry; excellent winter bedding material

#### Legume Crop Residues

After dry bean or pea harvest, remaining plant material (vines, leaves, pods) is valuable:

- **Yield:** ~500 lbs dry matter/acre
- **Protein:** 8–12% (superior to grass residue)
- **Preparation:** Dry on field 1–2 weeks; gather and store in hay form
- **Feeding:** 8–10 lbs/day; excellent protein supplement for grain-fed cattle

### Browse Species: Tree Leaves and Branches

In regions with woody vegetation, lopping branches provides emergency feed:

| Species | Palatability | Protein | Notes | Caution |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Oak (leaves, acorns)** | Good | 6–8% | Protein-rich; acorns calorie-dense after leaching | High tannins; limit to 30% diet (can cause constipation) |
| **Willow (branches, leaves)** | Excellent | 8–10% | Highly preferred by cattle; tender shoots ideal | None; safe and nutritious |
| **Birch (leaves, bark)** | Good | 6–8% | Aromatic; cattle eat readily; bark edible when thin | Bark stripping damages trees; sustainable harvest requires rotation |
| **Ash (leaves, seeds)** | Good | 7–9% | Seed pods nutritious; young leaves preferred | None; excellent drought-hardy species |
| **Cottonwood (leaves, twigs)** | Fair | 5–7% | Preferred in arid regions; very drought-hardy | Provide salt (mineral balance) if cottonwood exclusive diet |
| **Fruit tree prunings (apple, cherry)** | Good | 4–6% | Young leaves preferred; calorie-sparse | Remove cherry/plum leaves in spring (cyanide content) |

**Harvesting browse:**

1. Lop branches 1–2 inches diameter using hand saw or machete
2. Feed fresh or allow to wilt 1–2 days (reduces palatability slightly but easier transport)
3. Strip leaves by hand (labor-intensive; cattle eat leaves preferentially) or feed whole twigs
4. Sustainable practice: harvest from multiple trees; allow 2–3 year recovery per tree

:::tip
In cold climates, dried browse branches harvested in late summer can be stored and fed throughout winter. Stack branches under cover; cattle will strip leaves and bark when pasture unavailable.
:::

### Silage from Non-Conventional Plants

When grass fails to produce adequate hay, alternative plant silage provides emergency feed:

#### Banana Stem Silage (Tropical/Subtropical)

- **Yield:** 3–4 tons fresh matter per plant × 100 plants/acre = massive tonnage
- **Preparation:** After harvest, chop banana pseudostem (hollow stem) and leaves into 2–3 inch pieces; layer with legume residue (to raise protein) in silo or pit; seal and ferment 3–4 weeks
- **Nutrition:** 500–700 kcal/lb fresh; 3–5% protein (low alone; mix with 20% legume)
- **Cattle preference:** Moderate palatability; higher digestibility than dried straw

#### Palm Frond and Fruit Residue Silage (Tropical)

- **Yield:** Available continuously from mature palm plantations
- **Preparation:** Fronds + leaf sheaths chopped fine; mixed with small amount of grain/molasses to encourage fermentation; pit-silage 4–6 weeks
- **Nutrition:** 400–600 kcal/lb; protein 2–4% (use as filler; supplement legume)
- **Benefit:** Year-round availability in palm-growing regions

#### Vegetable Crop Residues (Leafy Greens, Brassicas)

After harvest of cabbage, kale, spinach, or lettuce:

- **Yield:** 1–2 tons fresh residue per acre of vegetables
- **Preparation:** Chop and ensile with legume or grain bran (moisture content high; add dry material); ferment 2–3 weeks
- **Nutrition:** 300–400 kcal/lb fresh; 4–6% protein
- **Caution:** Some vegetables (large quantities of kale, cabbage) contain goitrogens (suppress iodine); limit to 20% of diet

### Dried Seaweed and Marine Algae (Coastal Regions)

- **Yield:** Seaweed harvested from shore or farmed kelp beds; 2–5 tons dry matter/acre
- **Nutrition:** 600–800 kcal/lb; protein 8–12%; extremely high in minerals (iodine, potassium, magnesium)
- **Preparation:** Sun-dry 1–2 weeks until brittle; grind or chop; mix 5–10% into grain/hay mix
- **Benefit:** Iodine supplementation prevents goiter in coastal communities
- **Caution:** High sodium; provide fresh water, limit to 5–10% of diet

:::warning
Avoid moldy or decomposed browse/silage. Any feed with musty smell or visible mold may contain aflatoxins or other mycotoxins. Discard rather than risk cattle illness.
:::

### Supplementation Strategy During Feed Shortage

When primary feed (hay/pasture) is limited but not absent:

1. **Maintain hay baseline:** Provide 50% of normal hay intake (10–15 lbs/day per 1000 lb cow)
2. **Fill with alternatives:** Crop residue, browse, or silage = remaining 50% of caloric need
3. **Monitor body condition:** Score cattle weekly (see next section); if scores decline, add grain or increase legume supplement
4. **Maintain minerals:** Provide salt block and mineral mix year-round; stressed cattle require 30% more minerals

<section id="body-condition-scoring">

## Body Condition Scoring System: Monitoring Herd Nutrition

Body Condition Score (BCS) is a hands-on assessment of cattle fat and muscle. It's the single best indicator of whether feed intake is adequate. Regular scoring prevents malnutrition-related disease and reproductive failure.

### The 1–9 Scale

| **Score** | **Visual Description** | **Physical Feel (hand on rib/hip)** | **Management Decision** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1 (Emaciated)** | Skeleton visible; no fat or muscle | Ribs, spine, hip bones protrude sharply; no fat layer | Emergency: increase feed immediately or call veterinarian. Likely disease (parasites, infection). |
| **2 (Very Thin)** | Ribs visible; sunken flanks | Ribs felt easily under thin skin; hip/shoulder bones prominent | At-risk: increase hay, add grain/legume. Monitor closely. Reproductive failure likely. |
| **3 (Thin)** | Outline of ribs visible; waist visible behind ribs | Ribs feel with firm pressure; hip bones easily found | Marginal: adequate for young stock, but breeding cows losing condition. Increase feed. |
| **4 (Below Avg)** | Slight rib visibility; definition present but rounded | Ribs feel with slight pressure; not prominent | Monitor: acceptable for growing cattle. Breeding cows should be score 5+. |
| **5 (Moderate/Ideal)** | Smooth outline; ribs not visible but felt with gentle pressure | Ribs feel easily but not prominent; back level; hip/shoulder bones smooth, not sharp | **Target score for breeding cows and bulls.** Maintains fertility, milk production, and health. |
| **6 (Above Avg)** | Fully rounded; smooth outline; ribs not visible or felt | Ribs feel with very firm pressure; hip bones smooth and rounded | Acceptable for growing cattle; breeding cows may become too fat. |
| **7 (Fat)** | Very rounded; excessive smoothness | Ribs feel with difficulty; hip bones rounded and padded with fat | Overfed: reduce grain, limit grain supplement. Fat reduces fertility, increases health problems. |
| **8 (Very Fat)** | Heavy deposits; no shape definition | Ribs barely felt through fat pad; body squared-off | Problem: overfed. Reduce feed; fat damages heart, joints, reproduction. |
| **9 (Obese)** | Hanging fat deposits; distorted shape | Ribs not felt; massive fat pad on back/sides; excessive flab | Critical: thin immediately. Obesity causes calving difficulty, reproductive disease, metabolic disease. |

### Scoring Frequency and Action Plan

**Breeding cows:**
- Score every 2 weeks during growing season (spring–fall)
- Score monthly during winter (feeding adjustments less frequent)
- **Target:** Maintain score 5–6 year-round
- **Action if score drops:** Add 1–2 lbs grain daily or 5 lbs legume hay daily

**Bulls:**
- Score monthly year-round
- **Target:** Score 5–6; bulls use energy for breeding season (summer)
- **Action if score drops:** Increase feed 1–2 weeks before breeding begins

**Growing cattle (calves/yearlings):**
- Score every 3 weeks during grazing season
- **Target:** Score 4–5 (growing, not yet breeding)
- **Action if score drops below 4:** Switch to better-quality hay or add grain

### Scoring Technique (Hands-On)

1. **Position cattle:** Restrained in chute or held against fence. Well-lit area essential.
2. **Palpate ribs:** Place palm on side of cattle; feel for individual rib spacing
3. **Palpate spine:** Run hand along backbone from shoulder to hip
4. **Palpate hips:** Feel hip bone and pin bone prominence
5. **Assess overall:** Fat deposits visible or felt? Muscle definition? Overall outline?

:::info-box
**Quick Visual Shortcut:** If you can see individual ribs from 10 feet away = score 1–3. If ribs smooth but definable by touch = score 4–5 (ideal). If no rib definition and heavy rounding = score 6–9.
:::

### Body Condition and Reproduction

**Breeding success depends directly on body condition:**

- **Score <4:** Cows fail to cycle (no heat, no pregnancy). Calves born weak, poor nursing. This is the #1 cause of reproductive failure on low-input farms.
- **Score 5 (ideal):** Cows cycle regularly, conceive quickly, carry calves to term, produce strong calves with good nursing vigor.
- **Score >7:** Obesity causes difficult calving (dystocia), retained placenta, and reduced milk production. Fat accumulates in reproductive tract, physically blocking conception.

**Herd fertility improves simply by maintaining body condition during winter** — often the bottleneck for beef operations in cold climates.

### Body Condition and Health

Beyond reproduction, BCS predicts disease risk:

| **Score** | **Health Risk** | **Intervention** |
|---|---|---|
| <3 | Parasite susceptibility; respiratory disease; mastitis (dairy); immune suppression | Deworm; improve feed; vaccinate if disease present |
| 4–5 | Minimal risk | Routine care only |
| >7 | Metabolic disease (acidosis, ketosis); joint/leg problems; heat stress intolerance | Reduce feed; limit grain; increase exercise |

</section>

<section id="alternative-feed-sources-expanded">

## Alternative Feed Sources: Browse, Residues, and Unconventional Forage
<div style="margin-top: 0.5rem;"></div>

When pasture fails, browse (tree leaves and branches) and crop residues prevent starvation losses. This section expands on emergency feeding strategies beyond the emergency management section.

### Identifying Palatable Browse Species by Region

Cattle consume browse readily when trained early (calves browsing = adults browsing). Not all woody species are equally valuable.

**High-value browse:**

| **Species** | **Palatability** | **Nutritive Value** | **Availability/Season** | **Cautions** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Willow** | Excellent | 8–10% protein, highly digestible | Year-round (branches); fresh shoots in spring | Slight laxative effect (limit to 40% diet) |
| **Poplar/Aspen** | Excellent | 6–8% protein; bark edible in winter emergency | Winter (bark stripping when forage scarce) | Bark stripping kills young trees; selective harvest only |
| **Oak (leaves, acorns)** | Good–excellent | 6–8% protein; acorns 5% fat (high energy after leaching) | Fall (acorns) and spring (leaves) | High tannins; limit to 30% of diet (causes constipation if excessive) |
| **Ash (leaves, seed pods)** | Good | 7–9% protein; seed pods highly nutritious | Spring–fall (leaves and pods); persistent into winter | None; safe and reliable |
| **Cottonwood** | Fair–good | 5–7% protein; very drought-hardy | Especially in arid climates; winter branches | Lower nutrition than willow but survives in deserts |
| **Fruit tree prunings** | Good | 4–6% protein; minimal nutrition but acceptable filler | Seasonally (pruning waste in winter/spring) | Remove cherry, plum, apricot leaves in spring (mild cyanide); ripe fruit ok |

### Browse Harvesting Techniques

**Sustainable harvest prevents depletion:**

1. **Select trees:** Don't repeatedly strip same tree. Rotate harvest across multiple individuals.
2. **Branch size:** Cut branches 1–3 inches diameter using hand saw or machete. Avoid girdling (stripping complete bark ring) on trunk.
3. **Timing:** Late summer/fall harvest can be dried and stored for winter feeding. Green browse used immediately.
4. **Preparation:** Lop branches; allow brief wilting (1–2 days) to improve palatability and ease transport.
5. **Feeding:** Offer fresh to cattle. Dried branches (stored under cover) soften in cattle rumen and are consumed.

**Conservative estimate:** One mature willow tree yields ~20–30 lbs usable browse per year. A 10-tree woodlot supplies supplemental feed for 2–3 cattle during 6-month shortage.

:::tip
In regions with mature orchards, prune trees annually (fruit quality improves with pruning). Offer prunings to cattle immediately—apple, pear, and cherry branches are palatable and extend winter feed reserves.
:::

### Crop Residue Feeding Protocols

After grain/vegetable harvest, residues become cattle feed. Timing and moisture control determine value.

**Corn stover (stalks, husks, cobs):**
- Harvest after corn removed from field (fall)
- Yield: ~1.5 tons dry matter/acre
- Nutrition: 800 kcal/lb, 4–6% protein (inadequate alone)
- Feeding: 10–15 lbs/day per 1000 lb cow; pair with legume hay or grain for complete nutrition
- Advantage: Indefinite storage when dry; excellent winter roughage

**Grain straw (wheat, oats, barley):**
- Lower quality than corn stover (40–50% digestibility)
- Use as filler/roughage when primary forage scarce
- 15–20 lbs/day; expect slow weight loss
- Stores indefinitely; double as bedding material

**Legume crop residues (pea/bean vines after seed harvest):**
- Yield: ~500 lbs dry matter/acre
- Nutrition: 8–12% protein (significantly higher than grain straw)
- Excellent supplementation for grain-fed cattle
- 8–10 lbs/day improves nitrogen balance

**Vegetable residues (cabbage leaves, carrot tops, brassica waste):**
- Limited availability on most farms
- High water content (20–30% dry matter)
- Feed fresh or ensile with grain bran
- Acceptable but not reliable as primary source

:::warning
Avoid moldy, wet, or decomposed residues. Aflatoxin and other mycotoxins cause liver disease. Discard suspect material rather than risk herd illness.
:::

## Working Facilities for Handling and Butchering

### Handling Facilities

**Essential for:**
- Vaccination and health checks.
- Loading for transport/sale.
- Butchering on-farm.

**Components:**
- **Chute**: 24–30 inches wide, 4–5 feet tall. Narrows cattle into single file for restraint.
- **Headgate**: Manual or hydraulic device clamping the head (allows vaccination, dehorning, etc.).
- **Loading ramp**: Angled 25–30°; cattle walk up onto truck.
- **Working area**: Holding pen (~100 sq ft per cattle) to sort/separate animals.

**Cost**: Basic system $1500–5000; larger systems more.

**Alternative**: Use portable panels to funnel cattle into small pen; process individually using halters/ropes.

### Slaughter Facilities

Small-scale/on-farm slaughter is legal in most jurisdictions but regulated. Check local laws.

**Requirement**: Usually must use USDA-inspected facility OR obtain exemption for direct-to-consumer sales (varies by state).

**Home slaughter process** (if legal):
1. Cattle is sedated or stunned (captive bolt or rifle; must be instant and humane).
2. Exsanguination (bleeding out).
3. Hide removal.
4. Evisceration (removal of organs).
5. Chilling (within 2 hours to <45°F).
6. Butchering and freezing.

**Processing time**: ~2 hours per animal (experienced person).

**Meat yield**: 1000 lb live cow produces ~600 lbs carcass, ~450–500 lbs retail cuts (75–85% of carcass weight).

**Offal (organs) disposal**: Most organs (liver, kidney, heart) are edible; others require burial or composting.

## Integration with Land and Soil Building

### Manure Management

Cattle produce ~50 lbs manure per day per animal. This is valuable for soil building.

**Pasture-based system:**
- Cattle deposit manure directly on pasture (distributed during rotational grazing).
- Manure N-P-K: ~2–1–1 (lower than fresh manure due to leaching losses).
- Soil improves over years as organic matter accumulates.

**Winter confinement manure:**
- Collect and compost (if confined indoors).
- Or apply fresh to non-grazed fields (use for hay fields, then graze/cut hay next season).
- 1 ton fresh manure = ~50 lbs nitrogen (as organic matter; slow-release).

### Feed Crop Integration

**On-farm feed production reduces costs:**
- Hay fields: Grow legume-grass hay; feed to cattle; repeat annually.
- Crop residue: Grain straw, corn stalks (low nutritional value but supplements hay).
- Pasture legumes: Self-renewing nitrogen through grazing.

### Grazing Impact on Soil

Well-managed rotational grazing:
- Increases soil organic matter.
- Improves infiltration and water retention.
- Reduces erosion (plant coverage maintained).
- Improves soil structure (manure + root systems).

**Over 5–10 years**: Degraded pasture improves dramatically with proper management.

## Summary: Beef Cattle Production

- **Breed selection**: Angus, Hereford, Charolais for beef; Shorthorn/Dexter for dual-purpose.
- **Housing**: Minimal; run-in shed + windbreak + water sufficient for most climates.
- **Fencing**: 4–5 feet tall; woven wire or welded panels.
- **Pasture management**: Rotational grazing in 4+ paddocks; 0.5–1 cattle per acre on good pasture.
- **Feeding**: Pasture spring–fall; 3 tons hay per cow per winter; optional grain supplementation.
- **Water**: 8–12 gallons per day; unfrozen year-round.
- **Health monitoring**: Disease recognition; vaccination for blackleg; parasite management.
- **Calving**: Usually automatic; assist only if dystocia suspected; veterinary help if needed.
- **Butchering**: Legal requirements vary; ~500–600 lbs meat per 1000 lb steer.

A small beef herd (4–6 head) requires ~5–10 acres of good pasture, minimal housing, and 5–10 hours/week management. Returns: ~2000–3000 lbs meat annually, plus manure for soil building.
