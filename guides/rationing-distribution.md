## Overview

Rationing is the systematic, mandatory distribution of limited resources to prevent hoarding, starvation, and inequality. It is not a normal economic system—it is a crisis response. This guide covers when to implement rationing, caloric requirements for different population groups, ration card design and anti-fraud mechanisms, priority categories, water and medical rationing, psychological and ethical dimensions, black market prevention, and how to transition back to free distribution when scarcity ends.

Historical examples (WWII Britain, Leningrad siege, Vietnam famine) show that well-designed rationing can keep entire populations alive through extreme shortage. Poor rationing (corruption, favoritism, inadequate calories) leads to starvation despite available food.

<section id="when-to-ration">

## When to Implement Rationing

Rationing is expensive in administration and morale. Implement only when necessary.

### Trigger Criteria

**Implement Rationing When:**

1. **Supply Falls Below 30 Days**: If your community's food supply is less than one month, implement rationing immediately. Without it, speculators and hoarders will buy up available food, creating artificial scarcity and price inflation.

2. **Prices Rising Rapidly**: When food prices double in a single month, ration before a panic buying stampede empties stores.

3. **Critical Resources Become Scarce**: Medical supplies (antibiotics, bandages), fuel (oil, firewood), or water drops below 2 weeks' supply.

4. **Visible Starvation**: If any community member is visibly malnourished (sunken cheeks, prominent ribs, lethargy), rationing is overdue.

**Do NOT Ration If:**
- Supply exceeds 6 months at current consumption.
- Prices are stable.
- No visible malnutrition.
- Population is willing to self-limit through community cooperation.

Rationing creates resentment and administrative burden. Use only when scarcity is acute and self-regulation has failed.

:::warning
**Announce Rationing Early**: If supply projections show scarcity in 3 months, announce rationing now. This allows people to adjust mentally and prevents panic. Do not announce rationing the day it begins.
:::

## Caloric Requirements by Population Category

The human body requires a baseline number of calories per day. This varies by age, sex, activity level, and health status. Rationing must account for this variation.

### Baseline Caloric Needs (Kcal/Day)

| **Category** | **Age/Description** | **Light Activity** | **Moderate Activity** | **Heavy Labor** | **Notes** |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Sedentary Adult** | 18–50, no work | 1,600–1,800 | N/A | N/A | Elderly, children <13, disabled |
| **Light Activity** | 18–50, light work | 1,800–2,000 | 2,200–2,400 | N/A | Office work, light farming |
| **Moderate Activity** | 18–50, regular labor | N/A | 2,200–2,600 | 2,800–3,200 | Farming, construction, nursing |
| **Heavy Labor** | 18–50, heavy labor | N/A | N/A | 3,200–3,600+ | Mining, blacksmithing, chopping wood |
| **Child (4–12)** | 4–12 years | 1,200–1,600 | 1,600–2,000 | N/A | Varies by height/weight |
| **Adolescent (13–18)** | 13–18 years | 1,800–2,200 | 2,200–2,600 | 2,400–3,200 | Higher than adults due to growth |
| **Pregnant Woman** | Any age | +300 kcal/day | +300 kcal/day | +500 kcal/day | Add to baseline category |
| **Nursing Mother** | Any age | +500 kcal/day | +500 kcal/day | +700 kcal/day | Add to baseline category |
| **Infant (0–1)** | 0–12 months | 400–500 | N/A | N/A | Breastmilk (mother eats more) |
| **Toddler (1–3)** | 1–3 years | 1,000–1,200 | N/A | N/A | Transitioning to solids |

### Minimum Survival Rationing

If supply is extremely limited (e.g., 800 kcal/person/day), prioritize as follows:

| **Priority** | **Category** | **Allocation (kcal/day)** |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Infants (0–1), nursing mothers | 2,000–2,500 (mother) |
| 2 | Children (1–12) | 1,200–1,400 |
| 3 | Pregnant women, sick/injured | 1,800–2,000 |
| 4 | Sedentary/light work adults | 1,000–1,200 |
| 5 | Essential workers (medical, defense) | 1,600–2,000 |
| 6 | Heavy labor (if continued) | 2,000–2,500 |
| 7 | Elderly/disabled (non-essential) | 800–1,000 |

**Reality Check**: Allocations below 1,200 kcal/day cause long-term organ damage, weakness, and death. At 800 kcal/day, life expectancy is weeks to months. Allocation below 500 kcal/day is lethal in days. If you are at these levels, the situation is terminal—focus on triage (see Medical Rationing section).

:::info-box
**The Starvation Curve**: During the Leningrad siege (1941–1944), rations ranged from 300–800 kcal/day depending on category. Death rates were catastrophic—over 1 million died. Survival required supplementation (catching rats, eating leather, cannibalizing corpses). Plan to maintain 1,200 kcal/day minimum, or accept mass death.
:::

## Ration Card Systems

A ration card is a physical document entitling the holder to a specific quantity of food per period (day, week, month). It prevents hoarding and ensures equitable distribution.

### Card Design

**Physical Specifications:**
- **Material**: Thick cardboard or cloth (durable, cannot be easily forged).
- **Size**: 3×5 inches (fits in pocket, not bulky).
- **Color**: Different colors for different categories (red = child, blue = adult, green = heavy laborer, yellow = essential worker). Colors are instantly recognizable and prevent fraud.
- **Serial Number**: Unique number, hand-written or printed. Enables authorities to track if a card is lost, stolen, or duplicated.
- **Holder Name**: Full name, age, address, and signature. A card cannot be transferred to another person.
- **Authority Seal**: Official stamp/seal of the rationing authority (town council, military, etc.).

### Ration Allocation Periods

**Weekly Ration Vouchers** (Most Common)

Each ration card contains 4 weekly sections (tear-off tabs or perforated lines). Each week, the card holder receives one week's ration. On a set day (e.g., Monday), all food distribution points open. Card holders present their card for the current week, present their grain ration stub (proving they took last week's ration), and receive their allocation.

**Example Weekly Ration Card (Adult, Light Labor):**

```
RATION CARD - ARCATA COMMUNITY - 2026
Serial: AC-2026-004521
Name: Sarah Chen
Age: 35
Address: Mill Street, House 3
Signature: SC [official stamp]

WEEK 1 (Feb 1–7): Grain (7 lbs) | Legumes (1.5 lbs) | Oil (0.3 lbs) | Salt (0.1 lbs)
WEEK 2 (Feb 8–14): Grain (7 lbs) | Legumes (1.5 lbs) | Oil (0.3 lbs) | Salt (0.1 lbs)
WEEK 3 (Feb 15–21): Grain (7 lbs) | Legumes (1.5 lbs) | Oil (0.3 lbs) | Salt (0.1 lbs)
WEEK 4 (Feb 22–28): Grain (7 lbs) | Legumes (1.5 lbs) | Oil (0.3 lbs) | Salt (0.1 lbs)

[Signature line for distribution clerk each week]
```

### Distribution Logistics

**Distribution Points:**
- Food warehouses (central, secure storage).
- Community halls (for accessibility).
- Neighborhood depots (for large communities).

**Distribution Staff:**
- 1 supervisor (oversees the depot).
- 2–3 weighers (measure rations on scales, verify weights).
- 2–3 checkers (verify ration cards, mark cards as distributed).
- 1–2 guards (prevent queue-jumping, theft).

**Distribution Hours:**
- Set specific hours (e.g., Monday 8 AM–6 PM). This spreads crowds and prevents chaos.
- Allow flexibility for workers on night shifts (special distribution time, or proxy collection).

**Queue Management:**
- Use numbered tickets (each person takes a number upon arrival; order is called numerically). This prevents disputes over who is "next."
- Enforce single-file lines (no queue-jumping, no proxy collection except for elderly/disabled).
- Set time limits (30 minutes per household maximum). This keeps lines moving.

:::warning
**Verification and Anti-Fraud**: Before handing over rations, the checker must:
1. Verify the card is authentic (check seal, serial number, signature).
2. Verify the card-holder's identity (ask their address, age, or other personal details).
3. Check that the current week has not already been collected (mark the week "COLLECTED" on the card).
4. Verify that the person matches the photo/description on the card (if available).

A single card-checker error can result in duplicate rations issued and theft.
:::

## Priority Categories and Exemptions

Not all people can labor equally. Some are more vulnerable, and some perform essential functions. Rationing systems must account for this.

### Standard Priority Tiers

**Tier 1: Essential Military/Medical**
- Medical doctors, nurses, surgeons.
- Military commanders, combat soldiers.
- Allocation: 2,200–2,800 kcal/day (higher than general population).
- Justification: These people are keeping others alive or defending the community.

**Tier 2: Children and Dependents**
- Children (1–12 years): 1,200–1,400 kcal/day.
- Adolescents (13–18 years): 1,600–2,000 kcal/day.
- Disabled persons unable to work: 1,000–1,200 kcal/day.
- Allocation is higher than might be justified by pure need because children represent the future, and their survival is essential for long-term community continuity.

**Tier 3: Pregnant and Nursing Women**
- Pregnant: Add 300 kcal/day to baseline.
- Nursing: Add 500–700 kcal/day to baseline.
- Justification: These women are creating new life; inadequate calories lead to miscarriage, low birth weight, and infant death.

**Tier 4: Essential Workers (Non-Medical)**
- Farmers, millers, water-pump operators, power plant staff, engineers.
- Allocation: 2,000–2,600 kcal/day (above general population).
- Justification: These people maintain infrastructure that keeps everyone alive. If they starve, the system collapses.

**Tier 5: General Population (Healthy Adults)**
- Sedentary/light work: 1,200–1,600 kcal/day.
- Moderate work: 1,600–2,000 kcal/day.
- Allocation is lower than workers because general population demands are not immediately critical to survival.

**Tier 6: Elderly and Very Low-Ability Individuals**
- Elderly (65+), chronically ill, severely disabled.
- Allocation: 1,000–1,200 kcal/day (minimum to prevent rapid decline).
- Justification: Utilitarian grim logic—these groups contribute little to community production and survival. In extreme scarcity, they may receive lower allocation.

### Exemptions and Hardship Waivers

Even within tiers, hardship occurs. Establish an exemptions committee to handle cases:

- **Medical Exemptions**: Individuals with specific medical conditions (hyperthyroidism, recovery from surgery, or severe injury) receive supplemental rations.
- **Hardship Exemptions**: Individuals caring for multiple dependents (e.g., a woman caring for 5 children under age 12) receive supplemental rations.
- **Appeal Process**: Any person denied rations (or allocated to a lower tier) can appeal to the committee. The committee reviews the case and can grant waivers.

:::tip
**Fairness Perception**: Even if rationing is mathematically fair, it will be perceived as unfair if the process is opaque. Make exemptions public (post the names and reasons on a town board). This transparency builds trust and reduces resentment.
:::

## Water Rationing Protocols

Water is essential for drinking, cooking, hygiene, and sanitation. Without it, disease spreads rapidly. Yet in drought, water becomes scarce.

### Minimum Water Requirements

| **Category** | **Drinking/Cooking (liters/day)** | **Hygiene (liters/day)** | **Total (liters/day)** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Survival Minimum** | 0.5 | 0.5 | 1 liter |
| **Basic Health** | 1–1.5 | 2–3 | 3–4.5 liters |
| **Comfort** | 2–3 | 10–15 | 12–20 liters |
| **Industrial/Agricultural** | + 5–50 (varies) | + 50–500 (varies) | Unlimited in productive times |

**Rationing Allocation** (assuming municipal water supply):

- **Drinking/Cooking**: 1.5 liters/person/day (non-negotiable; less causes dehydration and death).
- **Hygiene/Sanitation**: 2–3 liters/person/day (basic washing, toilet flushing; less causes disease).
- **Laundry/Cleaning**: 1–2 liters/household/day (rationed; low-priority).
- **Industrial/Agricultural**: Only for essential activities (hospitals, food production).

### Water Distribution

**Systems:**
- **Centralized Taps**: Community-wide standpipes or wells where people gather daily to collect water. Requires secure location and guards to prevent queue chaos.
- **House-to-House Delivery**: Water teams use carts or buckets to deliver rations to homes. More equitable but labor-intensive.
- **Rotating Access**: Divide the town into zones; each zone gets water for 2–4 hours per day. This spreads demand and prevents resource depletion.

**Verification:**
- Bucket/container standardization: Each household receives one standard bucket (e.g., 5-liter plastic bucket). Daily water ration fills this bucket exactly once.
- Container sealing: After collection, containers are sealed with a mark or wax seal to prevent hoarding or dilution. Opened containers indicate theft.
- Inspection: Water checkers spot-check containers during distribution to detect fraud.

:::warning
**Hygiene and Disease**: If water is rationed below 2 liters/person/day, hygiene fails. Cholera, typhoid, dysentery, and other waterborne diseases spread. Allocate minimum 1.5 liters for drinking and cooking, and 2 liters for basic hygiene, even if it stresses the water supply. Disease outbreaks will kill more people than starvation.
:::

</section>

<section id="medical-supply-rationing">

## Medical Supply Rationing and Triage Ethics

In a post-collapse environment, medical supplies (antibiotics, bandages, anesthetics, vaccines) are finite and irreplaceable. Rationing is ethically fraught.

### Triage Categories

**Category Red (Immediate, High Survival Chance)**
- Treatable injuries (cuts, broken bones that can be set and stabilized).
- Treatable infections (bacterial infections susceptible to available antibiotics).
- Pregnant complications that can be resolved (breech presentation, preeclampsia).
- Allocation: Full medical resources (antibiotics, anesthetics, bandages).

**Category Yellow (Delayed, Moderate Survival Chance)**
- Injuries that are stable but require attention (deep lacerations, multiple fractures).
- Chronic illnesses (diabetes, asthma) requiring ongoing medication.
- Allocation: Limited antibiotics; palliative care; delayed surgery.

**Category Green (Minor, No Threat to Life)**
- Minor wounds, minor infections, minor illness.
- Allocation: Bandages, basic care; no antibiotics unless infection spreads.

**Category Black (Expectant, Low/No Survival Chance)**
- Severe burns covering >50% of body.
- Massive head trauma with no reflexes.
- Abdominal injuries with significant bleeding (untreatable without blood transfusion, which is unavailable).
- Allocation: Comfort care only (pain management, warmth, company; no life-extending treatments).

### Antibiotic Rationing

Antibiotics are precious. Establish rules:

- **Severe Infection Only**: Antibiotics are reserved for life-threatening infections (sepsis, pneumonia, severe wounds). Minor infections (small cut infection) are treated with boiling water, honey, or herbal remedies.
- **Dosage Discipline**: Antibiotics must be taken for the full course (e.g., 7–10 days), even if symptoms disappear. Partial courses create antibiotic resistance and wastage.
- **Priority Order**: Pregnant women, children, medical staff, and essential workers receive antibiotics before general population.
- **Inventory Tracking**: Keep a detailed log of every antibiotic dose dispensed, to whom, when, and why. Spot-check for fraud and hoarding.

### Triage Committee Decision-Making

- A committee of doctors (if available) or senior medical practitioners reviews cases for Red/Yellow/Black classification.
- Committee decisions are final but can be appealed to a higher authority.
- Decisions are kept confidential but aggregate statistics (number of Red/Yellow/Black cases per week) are published to maintain transparency.

:::warning
**Ethical Burden**: Triage involves deciding who receives life-saving resources and who does not. This is psychologically traumatic. Rotate triage committee members monthly to prevent burnout. Provide counseling for staff involved in Category Black decisions.
:::

</section>

<section id="black-market-prevention">

## Black Market Prevention

As soon as rationing begins, a black market emerges—people trading rations illegally for higher prices, or hoarding to resell.

### Causes

- **Insufficient Calories**: If rations are below 1,500 kcal/day, people are desperate and will trade rations for luxury items or hoard to prevent starvation.
- **Favoritism Perception**: If people believe rations are distributed unfairly, they lose faith in the system and turn to black market trade.
- **Inequality**: If officials or wealthy people receive larger rations while poor people starve, resentment drives black market activity.

### Prevention Strategies

**1. Adequate Rations**

Keep rations at or above 1,500 kcal/day per person. This removes the desperation motive. People will not risk their ration card for a few extra calories if their baseline is adequate.

**2. Transparent Allocation**

Publish ration levels publicly. Show the math: "Available food: 100 tons. Population: 10,000. Ration per person: 1,500 kcal/day. This leaves 5 tons in reserve." Transparency builds confidence.

**3. Anti-Hoarding Searches**

Periodically (weekly or monthly), authorities conduct surprise searches of homes, looking for excess food hoards. Hoards above a small legal reserve (e.g., 1 week's extra rations) are confiscated. This is invasive but effective.

**4. Informant Networks**

Encourage citizens to report black market activity. Offer small rewards (extra ration tokens, small luxuries) for tips. This creates a culture of enforcement.

**5. Harsh Penalties**

Publicly punish black market participants. Examples:
- First offense: Loss of ration for one week (severe hardship).
- Second offense: Loss of ration for one month + public shaming (name posted on town board).
- Third offense: Exile from the community or enslavement (work off debt).

The severity must be high enough to deter attempts.

**6. Ration Card Non-Transferability**

Explicitly state that ration cards cannot be transferred to another person. Enforce this by requiring photo ID (if available) or by asking personal questions (address, family member names) to verify the card-holder.

:::tip
**The Leningrad Model**: During the siege, ration cards were tied to a person's workplace. Cards could only be redeemed at that workplace, preventing transfer. Additionally, cards were frequently changed (new card every 1–2 weeks), and the new card was tied to a specific ration distribution point, further reducing transferability. This nearly eliminated black markets, though it did not eliminate all starvation.
:::

</section>

<section id="psychological-aspects">

## Psychological Aspects of Rationing

Rationing is psychologically traumatic. People fear starvation, resent scarcity, and distrust authorities. Managing morale is as important as managing food supply.

### Fear and Anxiety

- **Regular Announcements**: Publish food supply status and expected duration of rationing. "We have 90 days of food remaining. At this rate, rationing will end on 20 May." Knowing when rationing will end reduces panic.
- **Visible Reserves**: If possible, allow citizens to see the food stores (granary, warehouse). Psychological reassurance that food exists reduces hoarding behavior.
- **Emergency Protocols**: Announce worst-case plans. "If supply drops below 30 days, we will reduce rations to 1,200 kcal/day and delay non-essential activities."

### Fairness Perception

- **Equal Suffering**: Officials and administrators must eat the same rations as everyone else. If officials receive double rations while workers starve, the system collapses. This is non-negotiable.
- **Transparent Rationing Committee**: Include representatives from all major community groups (workers, elderly, youth, disabled). Decisions are made publicly and voted on, not by decree.
- **Public Accountability**: Committee members are subject to recall (removal) if communities lose confidence. This prevents entrenched corruption.

### Morale and Dignity

- **Celebrate Small Wins**: If harvest is better than expected, or supply is stable, announce it publicly. Host a small celebration (an extra ration of a luxury like honey or spices). This builds hope.
- **Recognition of Hard Work**: Acknowledge essential workers publicly. Announce their names, their role, their sacrifice. This builds solidarity.
- **Avoid Public Shaming**: Do not publicly humiliate people for black market activity or cheating. Shame is socially destructive and increases resentment. Punish privately if possible.

<section id="black-market-enforcement">

## Black Market Prevention Strategies

As soon as rationing begins, an underground economy emerges—people trading rations at inflated prices, hoarding, or stealing. Preventing black markets requires enforcement, incentives, and fairness.

### Enforcement Without Police

**Community Reporting System**

Establish a mechanism for citizens to anonymously report black market activity:

- **Sealed suggestion box** at town hall where people can post tips
- **Reward system**: "Report confirmed black market activity and receive 1 week extra rations"
- **Confidentiality**: Reporters are never identified (prevents revenge)
- **Follow-up**: Tips are investigated within 48 hours

**Example Tip:**
"John Smith is selling his family's ration cards for 2 tokens each to people outside his immediate family. This is illegal. He keeps the tokens for himself instead of trading for other food."

**Investigation Process:**
1. Investigator interviews John Smith directly (accusation must be confronted)
2. Asks to see his ration cards (should have exactly 6 cards for 6 family members)
3. If cards are missing or he admits selling: evidence of violation
4. Case referred to council for hearing and judgment

**Penalties for Black Market Participation**

| Violation | Penalty |
|---|---|
| **Selling rations for profit** | Loss of rations for 1 week (severe hardship) |
| **Hoarding above legal reserve** (>1 week extra) | Confiscation of excess + community service (20 days) |
| **Trading ration cards** to unauthorized person | Loss of rations for 2 weeks + permanent marking (name posted) |
| **Repeat offense** | Loss of rations for 1 month + public shaming + possible exile |

### Fairness Perception Management

Black markets thrive when people believe rationing is unfair. Prevent this:

**Transparent Allocation**

Publish exactly how many rations each household receives and why:

"Weekly Rations for 35-Person Household - February 2026:

| Name | Family Size | Activity | Caloric Need | Ration |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Smith Family | 6 people | 2 heavy labor | 1,800 cal/person | 10,800 cal total |
| Chen Family | 4 people | 1 moderate labor | 1,600 cal/person | 6,400 cal total |
| Johnson Family | 5 people | 2 light labor | 1,400 cal/person | 7,000 cal total |

Total Allocated: 24,200 calories
Per-Person Average: 1,650 calories
[Calculations shown; anyone can verify math]"

This transparency shows people the system is fair, reducing resentment and black market appeal.

**Officials Eat Same Rations**

This is non-negotiable. If council members and officials receive double or triple rations while citizens starve, the system collapses.

**Process**: Officials publicly collect their rations at the same distribution points as everyone else (or rations are delivered openly to their homes).

**Effect**: When people see leaders sharing hardship, support for rationing increases and black market appeal decreases.

**Equal Suffering Creates Legitimacy:**
- "We are all hungry together" → acceptance
- "I am starving while officials feast" → rebellion

**Public Posting of Ration Distribution**

Weekly, post how rations were distributed:

"Week of 15-21 February 2026 - Distribution Report

Total Households: 35
Total Rations Distributed: 24,200 calories (correct: all 35 families received rations)
Collected Rations: 0 (no one refused or failed to collect—compliance 100%)
Status: NORMAL

Next Distribution: 22-28 February 2026"

This creates transparency and allows anyone to spot if families are being skipped or receiving less than entitled.

### Incentive-Based Compliance

**Work-Credit System**

Offer extra rations to people who perform additional community work:

- **Base ration**: Everyone receives fair share (1,500 calories/day)
- **Work credits**: Additional 3+ hours/week of community work earns 200 extra calories/day
- **Tracking**: Work hours logged openly; anyone can verify "John Smith worked 5 hours on road repairs, deserves 200 extra calories this week"

**Effect**:
- Incentivizes work and community engagement
- Feels earned, not like favoritism
- Motivates people to contribute

### Spoilage Accounting

**Documented Spoilage Loss**

Food naturally spoils. Account for this honestly:

**Spoilage Documentation:**

| Date | Item | Amount | Reason | Witnesses | Disposal |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2026-02-18 | Grain | 5 lbs | Mold detected (moisture damage) | Chen, Wong | Composted |
| 2026-02-20 | Oil | 0.5 lbs | Container leaked | Lee, Smith | Discarded |

**Why This Matters:**
- Prevents accusations that lost food was stolen
- Allows accurate tracking of supply depletion
- Shows spoilage is real, reducing resentment

**Spoilage Factors by Food Type:**

| Food | Monthly Loss | Storage Method |
|---|---|---|
| Grain (wheat, rice) | 0.5-2% | Cool, dry, sealed containers |
| Legumes (beans, lentils) | 1-3% | Cool, dry, sealed containers |
| Oil | 2-5% | Cool, dark, sealed containers |
| Root vegetables | 3-8% | Cool cellar, moderate humidity |
| Dried meat | 1-3% | Cool, sealed, with salt |

**Reserve Calculation:**

If you have 1,000 lbs grain and expect 2% monthly spoilage:
- Month 1: 1,000 lbs - 20 lbs spoilage = 980 lbs remaining
- Month 2: 980 lbs - 19.6 lbs spoilage = 960.4 lbs remaining
- Plan accordingly; build spoilage into supply calculations

## Nutritional Adequacy Validation for Ration Plans

Even if calorie goals are met, malnutrition can occur if ratios of nutrients are wrong. This section validates that ration plans meet minimum nutritional requirements.

### Micronutrient Targets

**Minimum Daily Requirements** (adjust upward for children, pregnant/nursing women, heavy laborers):

| Nutrient | Daily Target | Deficiency Risk After |
|---|---|---|
| **Vitamin C** | 75-90 mg | 8-12 weeks (scurvy) |
| **Vitamin D** | 600-800 IU | 12-16 weeks (bone pain, muscle weakness) |
| **Vitamin A** | 700-900 mcg | 16+ weeks (night blindness, infections) |
| **Iron** | 8-18 mg | 4-8 weeks (anemia, fatigue) |

| **Calcium** | 1,000-1,300 mg | 12+ weeks (bone loss, weakness) |

**Weekly Health Checks:**

| Sign | Possible Deficiency | Action |

4. **Escalate if needed** (distribute supplementary items to at-risk group)

## Transitioning Back to Free Distribution

When scarcity ends (harvest succeeds, supply lines reopen, production recovers), transition away from rationing gradually.

### Timeline

**Week 1–2: Relaxation**
- Announce that rationing will end in 4 weeks.
- Increase rations by 10–20% to signal improvement.

**Week 3–4: Gradual Derationing**
- Allow purchase of additional food at market price (with ration card for reduced price, or cash for regular price).
- Ration card becomes optional (not required for survival).
- Prices are regulated but free trade is permitted.

**Week 5+: Complete Derationing**
- Ration cards are no longer valid.
- All food is sold at market price.
- Government food stores can be sold off or kept as emergency reserve.

### Pitfall: Hoarding Rebound

When rationing ends, people often hoard excessively, causing a secondary shortage. Prevent this by:

- **Gradual Price Reduction**: Lower prices slowly over several weeks. Rapid price drops encourage panic buying.
- **Supply Announcements**: Repeatedly announce that supply is abundant and prices will remain stable. Reduce fear.
- **Availability Visibility**: Keep stores well-stocked and open extended hours. Show people that food is accessible, reducing urgency to hoard.

:::info-box
**Historical Example**: Post-WWII Britain maintained bread rationing for 2 years after the war ended—longer than during wartime. Why? Partially to control hoarding and prevent inflation, and partially because supply recovery was slow. The public resented it, but it prevented the economic chaos that occurred in other nations.
:::
