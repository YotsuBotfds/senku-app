:::danger
**Burn Hazard:** Forging involves heating metal to 1000–1300°F (540–700°C). Direct contact with hot metal causes severe burns in seconds. Always wear leather work gloves, apron, and eye protection. Never reach across the forge or touch metal without testing temperature first. Keep water or sand bucket nearby for emergency cooling, but do not immerse hot metal in water (steam explosion risk—cool slowly in air or sand).

**Eye Protection Critical:** Flying scale (oxidized metal flakes) and spark splatter from hammer impacts cause serious eye injury. Always wear safety glasses or face shield with clear lens when forging.

**Noise:** Hammer impacts can exceed 100 dB, causing hearing damage with prolonged exposure. Use hearing protection if forging regularly.
:::

<section id="overview">

## Overview

Fasteners—nails, rivets, bolts, screws—are essential to construction and machine assembly. Industrial societies rely on millions of mass-produced fasteners; without them, building and repair become exponentially harder. Hand-forged fasteners require skill and time but are achievable at any scale from one blacksmith serving a community to small-scale batch production. This guide covers the principal fastener types, forging techniques, heat treatment, and production methods to enable communities to manufacture nails, rivets, bolts, and screws without industrial machinery.

</section>

<section id="cut-nails">

## Cut Nails (Square/Flat Nails)

The simplest and most efficient hand-forged nail is the cut nail—a wedge of steel sheared from bar stock. Cut nails were the dominant fastener type until the 1880s and remain viable for heavy construction.

### Design Principles

**Cut nail geometry:**
- **Shaft:** Flat, slightly tapered (wider at head, narrower at point)
- **Head:** Flat, roughly square (1–2 mm larger than shaft width)
- **Point:** Four-sided wedge (created by shaping the end of the shaft)
- **Typical sizes:** 1–4 inches long (25–100 mm), 3–8 mm wide

**Why square-tapered design works:**
- Wedge shape splits wood fibers apart as nail is driven (provides holding power through friction)
- Flat shaft resists rotation (unlike round nails)
- Tapered shape allows hammer-driving without excessive force

### Tools Required

1. **Hardy and anvil:** Sturdy anvil (50–150 lbs) with hardy hole (square opening for holding tools)
2. **Hot shears:** Heavy-duty shears mounted in the hardy hole, for cutting hot bar stock
3. **Nail header (swage):** Hardened steel die with shallow indentation (nail-head-shaped) mounted in hardy hole
4. **Hammer:** 2–4 lb cross-peen hammer for working at forge
5. **Punch or drift:** Small diameter rod for piercing or shaping
6. **Forge:** Charcoal or coal fire, sufficient heat to reach yellow-red (1000°F / 540°C) metal temperature

### Procedure for Forging Cut Nails

#### Step 1: Prepare Bar Stock

- Use mild steel bar (3/8" × 1/2" cross-section, or adjust for desired nail width)
- Cut into segments slightly longer than final nail length
- Mark positions where nails will be sheared (1–2 cm apart along the bar)

#### Step 2: Heat Bar and Position in Hardy Shears

1. Place bar in fire until glowing yellow-red (1000–1050°F)
2. Lift bar with tongs, position at first shear cut mark
3. Insert bar into hot shears in the hardy hole, with cut line just at the shears
4. Shears must be heated slightly so bar does not cool rapidly when clamped

#### Step 3: Shear the Blank

1. Strike the top of the shear handles with hammer to close shears and cut through bar
2. One cut produces a single nail blank (stub of metal, typically 3 mm × 8 mm × length)
3. Remove blank with tongs. It will be hot

#### Step 4: Taper the Shaft

1. Heat the blank to yellow heat again
2. Place blank on anvil, narrow edge up
3. Using hammer and anvil, draw out the blank—strike to spread and thin the metal
4. Rotate blank between strikes, creating a roughly pyramidal taper
5. Continue until the head end is ~8–10 mm wide and the point end is ~3–4 mm
6. The shaft should be slightly thinner in the middle than at the head (improves holding power)

#### Step 5: Shape the Head

1. Re-heat to yellow heat
2. Place blank vertically in nail header (the indentation in the hardy-mounted die)
3. The blank sits in the shallow indentation, with the head portion above the die
4. Strike the head portion sharply with hammer
5. The head will mushroom out and flatten
6. One or two strikes usually suffice
7. If head is asymmetrical, file or re-heat and re-form

#### Step 6: Point the Nail

1. Re-heat to yellow heat
2. Place nail on anvil with point end overhanging the edge
3. Strike the two sides of the point end to create a wedge shape
4. Strike first one side, then the opposite side, driving the tapered end downward
5. Result: A pyramidal point with four facets
6. File or grind point slightly if needed for sharpness

#### Step 7: Finishing

- Cool slowly in air (immersing in water causes cracking)
- Light filing of the head to smooth mushroomed edges (optional)
- Test: point should be reasonably sharp and not cracked

**Production rate:** Skilled blacksmith can produce 50–100 nails per hour (depending on size and quality demanded).

### Nail Sizes and Specifications

Establish standard sizes for your production. Example:

```
Size       Length    Head Width    Purpose
1-inch     25 mm     4 mm          Light framing, shingles
2-inch     50 mm     6 mm          Standard framing (2x lumber)
3-inch     75 mm     7 mm          Thicker boards, rafters
4-inch     100 mm    8 mm          Heavy framing, beams
```

For each size, maintain tools (headers, shears settings) to ensure consistency.

## Wrought Iron Nails

Historically, nails were hand-forged entirely by hammering—no shears involved. This method is more time-consuming but produces excellent results and uses less scrap.

### Procedure for Wrought Nails

1. **Select wire or bar:** Round or square mild steel wire, 2–3 mm diameter, cut into lengths ~50 mm longer than final nail
2. **Heat and point one end:** Heat to yellow heat, place on anvil, hammer the end into a wedge/point (similar to above)
3. **Taper the shaft:** Rotate wire and hammer evenly to create gradual taper from middle to point
4. **Form the head:** Re-heat, place on nail header, strike to form head
5. **Finish:** Cool, file if needed

**Advantage:** Less material waste than cut nails (only excess length is waste, not the sheared pieces).
**Disadvantage:** More time per nail, requires thinner starting stock (round wire typically).

### Clinch Nails (Colonial Nail Type)

These are wrought nails designed with a specially shaped point that could be bent back (clinched) over the back of a board to lock in place.

1. After pointing and tapering, **do not form the head yet**
2. Create a small barbed or forked point that can catch in wood fibers
3. Bend the point slightly (10–15° angle) off the center axis
4. Then form the head
5. When driven through a board, the bent point grabs and prevents withdrawal
6. Excess length is bent (clinched) back over the board surface, locking the nail

## Rivet Fabrication and Installation

Rivets permanently join metal pieces by forming a mechanical lock. Unlike nails, rivets are not removable.

### Rivet Geometry

**Standard rivet:**
- **Shaft:** 3–6 mm diameter, variable length (depends on thickness of material being joined)
- **Head:** Roughly hemispherical or domed (formed from flat blank by peening)
- **Types:** Flat-head, roundhead (most common), countersunk (for smooth surface)

### Fabrication of Rivets

#### From Wire

1. **Cut blanks:** Round steel wire (3–6 mm diameter), cut into segments roughly 1.5× the shaft length
2. **Form the head:** Place blank in rivet header (a die with hemispherical indentation mounted in hardy hole)
3. **Strike:** Use hammer to form a hemispherical head on the blank
4. **Result:** One blank rivet with pre-formed head and a shaft

#### From Bar (Alternative)

1. Cut from mild steel bar using hot shears
2. Taper one end slightly on anvil
3. Form the head using header
4. Result: Similar to wire-based rivet

### Rivet Installation

#### Step 1: Prepare Materials

- Drill or punch holes in metal pieces to be joined
- Hole diameter should match rivet shaft diameter closely (~0.5 mm larger)
- Holes should be aligned perfectly

#### Step 2: Insert Rivet

1. Place rivet through hole, with pre-formed head on one side
2. Rest pre-formed head on an anvil or bucking block (heavy steel tool held against the back side)

#### Step 3: Peen the Rivet (Hot Installation)

1. Heat rivet to bright red (1100–1200°F)
2. Strike the exposed shaft end with a peening hammer (hammer with rounded face)
3. Each strike expands the hot metal, filling the hole and forming a head
4. Strike multiple times (5–10 strikes depending on rivet size), working in a circular pattern
5. The shaft end will mushroom out, forming a locking head
6. Continue until a solid head is formed (shaft no longer visible)

#### Step 4: Cool and Finish

- Allow rivet to cool slowly in air
- Once cool, the rivet is locked (it cools and contracts, creating tension that locks the joint)
- File any protruding metal smooth if desired

**Installation notes:**
- Hot installation is standard for structural rivets (provides best strength)
- Cold installation is possible but requires much more force (hand striking or mechanical riveter)
- Rivets are not removable once installed—if a joint needs to be disassembled, the rivet must be drilled or ground out

### Joint Strength

Rivet strength depends on:
- **Shaft diameter:** Larger diameter = stronger joint
- **Material:** Steel > iron; annealed (soft) rivet material yields better results than brittle stock
- **Installation quality:** Properly peened joint with no voids is strongest
- **Overlap length:** Material pieces should overlap at least 3–4 times the rivet diameter for adequate strength

## Bolt and Nut Fabrication

Bolts (threaded fasteners with nuts) are more complex than nails or rivets but more versatile (removable, adjustable).

### Simple Bolt from Rod

#### Step 1: Prepare Bolt Blank

1. Cut mild steel rod (5–10 mm diameter) to length (typically 50–150 mm)
2. One end will be the head, the other the threaded shaft

#### Step 2: Form the Head

1. Heat one end to yellow heat
2. Place in bolt header (a die with head-shaped indentation)
3. Strike to form head (typically hexagonal or round)
4. Head should be ~1.5× the shaft diameter

#### Step 3: Create the Shank

1. Hammer or draw out the shaft portion below the head to the desired diameter
2. This portion will remain unthreaded (smooth)

#### Step 4: Thread the Bolt

See section on hand-tapping threads, below.

### Hand-Cut Threads (Taps and Dies)

Creating threads by hand requires tools (taps and dies) that are themselves specialized to forge.

#### Tap (for internal threads in holes)

A tap is a hardened steel rod with grooves cut to form the thread shape. One end is twisted into a hole, cutting an internal thread.

**Simple tap fabrication:**

1. **Forge a steel rod:** Mild steel, hardened by quenching, roughly 5–8 mm diameter, 50–80 mm long
2. **Cut thread grooves:** Using a file and chisel, hand-carve helical grooves (or use a lathe if available)
3. **Hardening:** Heat to critical temperature (red heat), quench in oil. The hardened tap is brittle but cuts reliably
4. **Hand usage:** Align with hole, twist clockwise to cut threads, reverse to remove

**Simplified alternative:** Use a file to create a non-helical thread (straight grooves along the rod). Less effective but workable.

#### Die (for external threads on rods)

A die is a hardened steel ring with internal thread grooves. A rod is twisted through the die, forming external threads.

**Simple die fabrication:**

1. **Forge a steel tube:** Drill or bore a hole in the center of a steel rod, creating a ring
2. **Cut internal grooves:** File or carve internal grooves matching the desired thread pitch
3. **Harden the die:** Heat to critical temperature, quench in oil
4. **Hand usage:** Align die with rod end, twist die around rod to cut threads

#### Thread Pitch

Common pitch (distance between threads):
- Fine threads: 0.5–1 mm per turn (used for precision work)
- Coarse threads: 1–2 mm per turn (standard for construction fasteners)

For simplicity, use coarse 1.5–2 mm pitch—easier to hand-cut and adequate for structural fasteners.

### Nuts

Nuts are relatively simple—hardened steel blocks with internal threads.

**Fabrication:**

1. **Forge a steel cube:** ~12 × 12 × 10 mm for a standard nut, adjusted for bolt size
2. **Center a hole:** Drill or punch a hole in the center
3. **Cut internal thread:** Use tap tool to create threads inside hole
4. **Harden:** Heat and quench

Production rate: 5–10 nuts per hour (slower than nails due to threading complexity).

## Hand-Cut Screws

Screws are more complex than bolts but useful for fine fastening and removal without tools (unlike rivets).

### Screw Design

- **Shaft:** Tapered rod, threaded along most of the length
- **Head:** Flat or rounded, with a slot or cross-slot for screwdriver
- **Point:** Tapered to sharp point (self-drilling into soft materials)

### Fabrication

#### Step 1: Forge Blank

1. Cut mild steel rod to length (30–50 mm for common wood screws)
2. Forge or taper one end to a point
3. Forge the head area (optional—can be forged as part of the screw or shaped later)

#### Step 2: Cut Threads

Using a die (as described above), twist the die around the blank to cut threads.
- Leave a pointed tip at the end (unthreaded)
- Leave a smooth shaft below the head (for the slot to be cut)

#### Step 3: Create Head Slot

1. Once threads are complete, the head region is typically left smooth (unthreaded)
2. File a slot across the top of the head—a straight channel for a flat-blade screwdriver
3. Slot depth should be ~1/3 the head thickness

#### Step 4: Harden (Optional)

- Quench in oil to harden the entire screw (improves durability)
- Or leave as-forged (softer but less likely to shatter if overtightened)

**Production rate:** 2–5 screws per hour (threading and slotting are time-consuming).

</section>

## Heat Treatment for Hardness and Strength

Fastener hardness affects durability and holding power. Proper heat treatment is essential for long-lived fasteners.

### Steel Properties and Heat Treatment

**Annealed (soft) steel:** Workable, easy to forge, but soft and less wear-resistant
**Hardened steel:** Hard, brittle, resists deformation but can shatter if overloaded

### Hardening Process

1. **Heat to critical temperature:** Bright red or orange heat (1050–1150°F / 565–620°C), depending on steel composition. Heat until magnetic properties change (steel loses magnetism near critical temperature)
2. **Quench rapidly:** Plunge hot steel into oil (not water—water is too fast, causes cracking). Oil cools more slowly, reducing shock
3. **Temper (reduce brittleness):** Reheat to a lower temperature (300–500°F) for a few minutes, then cool slowly. This removes excess brittleness while retaining hardness
4. **Check color:** Steel changes color as it tempers: pale straw (~300°F), brown (~350°F), blue (~500°F). Blue is typical for fasteners

### Hardening Different Fastener Types

- **Nails:** Often left as-forged (mild steel is soft enough). If extra hardness is desired, harden and temper to straw or brown color
- **Rivets:** Soften slightly (anneal before installation) so they peen easily. After installation, they harden slightly naturally
- **Bolts and screws:** Harden and temper to brown or light blue for durability
- **Dies and taps:** Harden to full hardness (blue or purple) since they must resist wear from cutting

### Practical Simplification

For basic fasteners, a simpler approach:
1. Forge at yellow-red heat
2. Cool slowly in air (no quenching needed)
3. Result: Steel is normalized (neither too soft nor too brittle)

This produces acceptable fasteners without requiring precise temperature control.

## Specialized Fasteners

### Horseshoe Nails

Used historically for attaching horseshoes to hooves. Design is distinctive: curved shaft (to follow the curve of the hoof), clinch point, tapered.

**Fabrication:** Similar to wrought nails, but forged slightly curved rather than straight.

### Roofing Nails

Short, wide-head nails (head diameter 10–15 mm) used to hold roofing shingles. The wide head prevents tearing through shingle material.

**Fabrication:** Use extra-large nail header to form the oversized head.

### Boat Nails (Clinch or Burr Nails)

Marine fasteners with special heads designed to lock and resist corrosion. Often forged from copper or bronze (non-corrosive) rather than steel.

**Material considerations:** Copper and bronze forge at lower temperatures than steel, are softer, and require less force but more refinement.

### U-Bolts (Clamps)

U-shaped bolts used for clamping pipes, cables, or structural members. Fabricated by bending steel rod into a U, then threading the ends.

**Fabrication:**
1. Forge rod of desired diameter
2. Bend into U-shape while hot (bend over rounded surface of anvil)
3. Cut to length
4. Thread the two ends using dies
5. Forge or attach nuts

## Production Methods and Rates

### One-Blacksmith Workshop

A single blacksmith with basic tools (anvil, forge, hammer, shears, headers) can produce:
- **Nails:** 50–150 per hour (depending on size)
- **Rivets:** 20–40 per hour
- **Bolts:** 5–15 per hour (slower due to threading)
- **Screws:** 2–5 per hour (slowest—threading and slotting)

Annual production (working 200 days, 8 hours per day):
- ~80,000 nails, OR
- ~30,000 rivets, OR
- ~10,000 bolts, OR
- ~3,000 screws (or mixed production)

### Small-Scale Batch Production

Two blacksmiths working together can increase efficiency through specialization:
- **Blacksmith 1:** Heats and cuts blanks; forms heads
- **Blacksmith 2:** Shapes shafts and points; finishes

Coordination and pre-planning of tool positions increase production rates by 30–50%.

### Water-Powered Hammer

If water power is available, a tilt-hammer or reciprocating hammer can automate the striking phase, freeing the blacksmith to position and guide metal. Production rates increase 2–3×.

## Quality Control and Testing

Fasteners must meet basic strength and durability standards.

### Visual Inspection

- **Head:** Should be smooth, well-formed, not cracked
- **Shaft:** Should be straight, not twisted
- **Point (nail):** Should be sharp, edges aligned, not bent
- **Threads (bolt/screw):** Should be visible, evenly spaced, not stripped

### Mechanical Testing

- **Tensile test:** Pull fastener until it breaks. Fastener should not break before withstanding ~10,000 PSI (69 MPa) for mild steel nails. Stronger fasteners withstand 20,000+ PSI
- **Bending test:** Bend nail or bolt until it breaks. Mild steel should bend significantly before breaking; brittle fasteners snap cleanly
- **Thread strength:** Screw into hardwood; hand-pull to see if threads strip. Should withstand several pulls without damage

### Documentation

Maintain records of production:
- Date and blacksmith
- Type and size of fastener
- Number produced
- Any defects noted
- Performance in use (feedback from users)

Over time, this data improves quality and production efficiency.

## Summary

Hand-forged fasteners are essential when industrial supply chains fail. Cut nails, the simplest type, are sheared from bar stock and shaped on an anvil—50–100 per hour per blacksmith. Wrought nails are entirely hand-forged from wire or bar, with fewer waste but more labor. Rivets permanently join metal pieces through peening (hammering the shaft end to form a locked head). Bolts and screws are more complex, requiring thread-cutting with taps and dies. All fastener types benefit from proper heat treatment—hardening and tempering—to balance strength and durability. Specialized fasteners (horseshoe nails, roofing nails, U-bolts, boat nails) serve specific purposes. Annual production of a single blacksmith reaches 80,000 nails, 30,000 rivets, 10,000 bolts, or 3,000 screws, depending on focus. Quality control through visual inspection, mechanical testing, and documentation ensures reliable fasteners. Proper eye protection and burn prevention are critical safety measures when forging.
