## Overview: Zoology and Practical Animal Husbandry

<section id="overview">

Zoology is the study of animals; animal husbandry is the practical management of domesticated animals. In survival contexts, knowledge of animal anatomy, physiology, breeding, nutrition, and disease management is essential for livestock production, food security, and sustainable animal use.

This guide covers animal classification, anatomy and physiology, livestock requirements, breeding and genetics, health management, and practical husbandry techniques.

Key principles:
- **Animals are systems:** Anatomy (structure) and physiology (function) are interdependent
- **Nutrition is foundational:** Balanced diet is prerequisite for health and productivity
- **Breeding aims for improvement:** Selective breeding for productivity, hardiness, and health
- **Health prevention is key:** Good conditions prevent most diseases
- **Species-specific care matters:** Different animals have different requirements

</section>

## Animal Classification and Diversity

<section id="classification">

### Major Animal Groups Relevant to Survival

**Mammals:**
- Warm-blooded; fur or hair; mammary glands produce milk
- Complex brains; parental care; high metabolic demands
- Livestock: cattle, sheep, goats, pigs, horses, chickens (birds, not mammals)

**Birds:**
- Warm-blooded; feathers; egg-laying
- Efficient respiratory system; vision-dominant
- Livestock: chickens, ducks, geese, turkeys

**Reptiles:**
- Cold-blooded; scales; some are livestock in certain cultures (alligators, snakes)

**Fish:**
- Cold-blooded; gills; live in water
- Most efficient at converting feed to meat; rapid growth in aquaculture

**Arthropods (insects):**
- Exoskeleton; jointed legs
- Some are livestock for silk, honey, or food (crickets, mealworms)

### Ruminants vs. Non-Ruminants

**Ruminants (cattle, sheep, goats, deer):**
- Four-chambered stomach
- First chamber (rumen) ferments forage with microbial help
- Can digest plant fiber (cellulose) that non-ruminants cannot
- Efficient conversion of plant material to meat/milk
- Require time to chew cud (regurgitated food)
- Vulnerable to bloat if diet changes abruptly

**Non-ruminants (pigs, horses, chickens, humans):**
- Single stomach
- Cannot digest cellulose efficiently
- Require more grain/concentrated feed than ruminants
- Faster food passage through digestive system
- Less selective in diet

**Practical implication:** Ruminants can thrive on pasture and low-quality forage. Non-ruminants require grain or high-quality forage. In survival scenarios, ruminants are easier to sustain.

</section>

## Livestock Anatomy and Physiology

<section id="anatomy-physiology">

### Skeletal and Muscular Systems

**Skeletal system:**
- Provides structure, protection, and movement
- Bone marrow produces blood cells
- Varies by species (cattle have larger frames; chickens have hollow bones for flight)

**Muscular system:**
- Skeletal muscles enable movement
- Smooth muscles control internal organs
- Cardiac muscle sustains heartbeat
- Muscle tissue is primary meat product

**Practical assessment:**
- Body condition scoring: Visual assessment of fat and muscle; indicates nutritional status
- Lameness (abnormal gait): Indicates pain or structural problem; requires investigation

### Digestive System

**Ruminant digestion:**
1. Ingestion and chewing
2. Swallowing to rumen (fermentation chamber)
3. Regurgitation of cud for additional chewing
4. Further fermentation and microbial breakdown of fiber
5. Movement to omasum, abomasum, and intestines
6. Absorption of nutrients and water
7. Excretion of feces

**Diet implications:**
- Ruminants thrive on forage (grasses, legumes, hay)
- Abrupt diet changes kill rumen microbes; causes bloat and acidosis
- Change diet gradually over 10–14 days

**Non-ruminant digestion:**
1. Ingestion and chewing
2. Swallowing to stomach
3. Gastric digestion (stomach acid + enzymes)
4. Movement to small intestine (primary site of nutrient absorption)
5. Movement to large intestine (water absorption, fecal formation)
6. Excretion of feces

**Diet implications:**
- Non-ruminants cannot digest large amounts of fiber
- Require easily digestible carbohydrates and proteins
- Grains and legumes are good feed sources

### Reproductive System

**Female reproductive system:**
- Estrous cycle (reproductive cycle) ranges 17–21 days (cattle) to 3–4 days (chickens lay eggs daily)
- Estrus (heat) is period when female is sexually receptive and fertile
- Pregnancy duration: 283 days (cattle), 115 days (sheep), 114 days (pigs)

**Male reproductive system:**
- Continuous production of sperm (unlike females' cyclical estrus)
- Testosterone drives aggressive behavior
- Castration (removal of testes) reduces aggression; improves meat quality

**Practical breeding:**
- Breed females when in estrus (visual signs: swelling, discharge, standing to be mounted)
- One male can breed multiple females
- Gestation is species-dependent; plan breeding for optimal birthing season

:::info-box
**Estrus detection:** Females in heat show specific behaviors: restlessness, increased vocalization, swelling of genital region, discharge, standing to be mounted. Detecting estrus is critical for efficient breeding.
:::

</section>

## Livestock Nutrition and Requirements

<section id="nutrition">

### Nutrient Requirements

All livestock require:

**Energy (carbohydrates and fats):**
- Measured in calories or MJ (megajoules)
- Maintenance requirement: Sustain body at rest; increases with size and environmental stress
- Production requirement: Support growth, milk production, or reproduction
- Cold or stress increases energy requirement

**Protein (amino acids):**
- Essential for growth, milk production, and muscle maintenance
- Quality varies: Complete proteins contain all essential amino acids
- Sources: Legumes, grains, oil meals (soybean meal), grass

**Vitamins and minerals:**
- **Calcium and phosphorus:** Bone and milk production
- **Vitamin A:** Vision, immune function; abundant in fresh grass
- **Iron:** Blood production
- **Trace minerals:** Copper, zinc, selenium, iodine in small amounts

**Water:**
- Essential; clean water must be available at all times
- Requirement increases with environmental temperature and milk production
- Dehydration rapidly reduces productivity and can be fatal

### Feeding Systems

**Pasture-based:**
- Animals graze on living vegetation
- Minimal external feed required (if pasture is adequate)
- Seasonal variation; summer abundance, winter scarcity
- Suitable for ruminants; supplemental grain may be needed in winter

**Hay-based:**
- Dried grass or legume; stored for winter feeding
- Lower nutritional quality than fresh pasture; may require supplementation
- Critical for winter feeding in cold climates
- Hay quality varies by harvest timing (early-cut hay is higher quality)

**Grain-based:**
- Concentrated feed; high energy and protein (when legumes are combined)
- More expensive than forage
- Necessary for non-ruminants and high-production animals (dairy cows)
- Risk of overfeeding grain causes digestive problems

**Mixed systems (common in survival scenarios):**
- Pasture as base for ruminants
- Hay in winter
- Limited grain supplement for high-production animals
- Kitchen scraps and garden waste as supplemental feed

:::tip
**Feed budgeting:** A 500 kg cow requires ~12 kg hay or forage equivalent per day. Plan winter feed storage: 5–6 tons hay per cow for 6-month winter (assumes no pasture).
:::

</section>

## Livestock Health and Disease Management

<section id="health">

:::warning
**Occupational Zoonotic Diseases in Livestock Handling:** Direct contact with livestock carries risk of disease transmission to humans. Brucellosis (causes chronic fever and arthralgia from unpasteurized dairy), leptospirosis (from contaminated urine—fatal if untreated), and Q fever (highly infectious, respiratory illness) are occupational hazards. Prevention: Wear protective equipment when handling livestock or raw milk (gloves, eye protection, respiratory mask when needed). Practice rigorous hygiene (handwashing before eating/touching face). Ensure milk is pasteurized before human consumption. Veterinary testing for livestock disease status reduces risk. If you develop unexplained fever, joint pain, or respiratory symptoms after livestock contact, seek medical evaluation and inform provider of exposure.
:::

### Disease Prevention (Best Strategy)

**Sanitation:**
- Clean water: Provide uncontaminated water; prevent contamination by feces or pathogens
- Clean housing: Remove manure regularly; allow for bedding replacement
- Quarantine new animals: Isolate for observation before introducing to herd
- Vaccination: If available, vaccinate against major diseases

**Nutrition:**
- Balanced diet prevents deficiency diseases (rickets from calcium deficiency, anemia from iron deficiency)
- Adequate protein supports immune function
- Clean, fresh feed prevents mold and toxin ingestion

**Stress reduction:**
- Minimize handling stress: Calm animals are healthier
- Provide shelter: Protection from extreme weather
- Maintain social groups: Animals are stressed when isolated or mixed with unfamiliar groups

### Common Livestock Diseases and Management

**Parasites (internal and external):**
- Worms (internal): Reduce weight gain, weaken animals
- Lice, mites, ticks (external): Cause skin irritation, disease transmission
- Management: Sanitary conditions, pasture rotation, natural antiparasitic herbs (garlic, diatomaceous earth)

**Infection and inflammation:**
- Mastitis (breast infection in dairy animals): Cleanliness, proper milking technique, early antibiotic treatment
- Hoof rot: Trim hooves regularly; avoid wet, muddy conditions
- Wounds: Cleanse and disinfect; apply salve to prevent infection

**Nutritional deficiency:**
- Calcium deficiency: Milk fever in dairy cows; prevent with proper diet
- Iron deficiency: Anemia; particularly in rapidly growing young animals
- Vitamin A deficiency: Night blindness; provide green forage or liver

**Bloat (in ruminants):**
- Gas accumulation in rumen; can be fatal if untreated
- Cause: Sudden diet change to legumes or grain
- Prevention: Gradual diet transitions; allow adaptation period
- Treatment: Walk animal, massage rumen, relieve pressure (severe cases)

### Castration and Reproductive Management

**Castration benefits:**
- Reduces aggression and fighting
- Improves meat quality (less "ram/boar taint")
- Prevents unwanted breeding
- Easier to manage

**Castration methods:**
- **Surgical:** Cut and ligate spermatic cord (requires skill to avoid infection)
- **Banding:** Apply rubber band or clamp to cut off blood supply (less stressful)
- **Chemical:** Immunize against reproductive hormones (emerging method)

**Timing:**
- Young animals recover faster
- Too late castration may not fully reduce aggression
- Typical: Few weeks to few months old

## Practical Husbandry Techniques

<section id="husbandry">

### Housing and Shelter Requirements

**Protection from elements:**
- Shelter from wind, rain, and extreme heat
- Ventilation to prevent ammonia buildup and respiratory disease
- Space allowance: Varies by species (cattle: 1–2 m² per animal)

**Bedding:**
- Straw, wood shavings, or other dry material
- Absorbs urine; provides comfort
- Regular replacement prevents ammonia and ammonia-related disease

**Water access:**
- Always available
- Decontaminated; protected from fecal contamination
- Adequate trough space (multiple troughs prevent fighting)

### Breeding and Record-Keeping

**Records track:**
- Date of birth and parentage
- Estrous cycle dates (for mating timing)
- Health history and treatments
- Production (milk yield, weight gain, egg production)

**Decision-making based on records:**
- Identify best producers; breed from them
- Identify problem animals; cull if unproductive
- Track disease patterns; adjust management
- Plan breeding for optimal timing (spring births for seasonal animals)

### Seasonal Management

**Spring:**
- Lambing/calving/piglet season; intensive management required
- Increased nutrition for pregnant and nursing females
- Pasture available; move to pasture gradually

**Summer:**
- Rapid growth on lush pasture
- Heat stress; provide shade and water
- Prepare for winter (cut and dry hay)

**Fall:**
- Harvest and breeding season
- Prepare shelter and feed storage
- Vaccinate and treat parasites before winter housing

**Winter:**
- Confined housing; hay feeding
- Monitor body condition; adjust feed as needed
- Minimal reproduction; focus on maintenance

</section>

## Summary

<section id="summary">

Successful animal husbandry requires knowledge of animal biology and disciplined management.

Key principles:
1. **Anatomy and physiology are interrelated:** Structure determines function; disease affects both
2. **Nutrition is foundational:** Balanced diet supports all productive functions
3. **Ruminants are efficient:** Convert forage to meat/milk better than non-ruminants
4. **Breeding improves productivity:** Selective breeding increases yield and hardiness over generations
5. **Disease prevention is key:** Clean conditions prevent most health problems
6. **Records enable optimization:** Tracking production guides breeding and culling decisions
7. **Stress reduction matters:** Calm animals are healthier and more productive
8. **Species-specific care is essential:** Different animals have different requirements

With knowledge of animal biology and commitment to good husbandry, livestock provides reliable food security and sustainable agriculture in survival scenarios.
