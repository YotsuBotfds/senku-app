:::info-box
**Step 1 — What should you salvage first from a building? Quick priority check:**

- Metal roofing and flashing (copper, lead, aluminum) — highest reuse value, grab first.
- Solid dimensional lumber (old-growth pine, oak, chestnut) — irreplaceable structural material.
- Bricks and stone (especially pre-1950 lime-mortar) — easy to clean, lasts centuries.
- Window glass and frames, doors, hardware (brass/bronze) — high trade value, compact to carry.
- Insulation, asphalt shingles, particle board — low priority; salvage only if time and capacity allow.

Before removing any materials, confirm the building is safe to enter: [Structural Safety for Building Entry](../structural-safety-building-entry.html). For time-sensitive materials (food, medicine, fuel) that decay faster than building materials, see [Time-Decay Priority Framework](../salvage-time-decay-priorities.html).
:::

## Lumber Recovery

Salvaged lumber is among the most valuable building materials because of volume, usability, and the immense time required to mill dimensional lumber from trees. A single older residence can contain 40,000-80,000 board feet of usable wood.

### Denailing Techniques

The largest task in lumber recovery is removing fasteners. Proper technique prevents wood damage and improves both safety and material quality.

#### Hand Pulling Methods

-   **Pry bar technique:** Place a thin pry bar (2-3 inches wide) directly under the nail head. Lever gently rather than violently—sudden movement can split the wood. Work from multiple angles if needed. Repeat along the nail length rather than pulling straight up.
-   **Nail puller use:** Specialized nail pullers provide 3-5x mechanical advantage. Position the claw under the nail head and rock backward slowly. Effective for driven nails but requires good positioning.
-   **Wedge and knock method:** Drive a thin wooden wedge beside a stuck nail to lift it partially, then pull with a pry bar. Time-consuming but minimizes wood damage on valuable lumber.
-   **Drilling through:** For stubborn fasteners, drill through the wood on one side to release pressure, allowing easier removal from the other side. Only for non-through-fasteners where exit damage is acceptable.

:::info-box
Denailing is tedious, repetitive work that requires focus. A single moment of inattention can split a board or cause hand injury from a slipping tool. Take breaks; fatigue causes mistakes.
:::

:::tip
Patience pays off in denailing. Rushing causes wood splitting and damaged boards. For valuable lumber, spend extra time with careful hand techniques rather than power tools that may cause collateral damage.
:::

#### Power Tool Methods

-   **Reciprocating saw:** Cut nails or bolts rather than extracting them when removal would damage wood. Only practical for demolition-phase recovery, not for finished lumber.
-   **Grinder:** Grind nail heads flush or cut fastener shanks. Creates sparks and dust—wear eye protection and respirator. Effective for bolt cutting and heavily corroded fasteners.
-   **Impact driver reverse:** Modern impact drivers can sometimes unscrew driven fasteners by reversing direction with impact force. Limited success but worth attempting on lightly driven nails.

:::warning
Power tools create significant risk of hand injury and wood damage. Hand methods are slower but produce superior results for valuable lumber. Reserve power tools for demolition-phase work where speed is more important than material preservation.
:::

#### Chemical Methods

-   **Penetrating oil:** Spray fasteners with penetrating oil and wait 24-48 hours before pulling. Reduces friction significantly, especially on corroded or rusted fasteners.
-   **Rust converter:** For heavily corroded iron nails, apply rust converter to chemically neutralize corrosion, making extraction easier.

### Lumber Grading and Selection

Not all recovered lumber is equal. Develop a grading system to categorize material for appropriate reuse.

:::warning
Grade lumber immediately after recovery while condition is fresh. As wood sits in storage, rot and insect damage may increase. Accurate grading when material arrives prevents future confusion and misallocation.
:::

#### Grade A: Premium Structural and Finish

-   Straight grain, no visible warping or cupping
-   No knots larger than 1 inch diameter
-   No splits, checks, or cracks deeper than surface
-   Original dimension intact (not compressed or damage-reduced)
-   Species: old-growth hardwoods preferred (oak, chestnut, walnut)
-   Use for: Structural beams, flooring, finish carpentry, visible cladding

#### Grade B: Standard Structural

-   Minor warping acceptable (less than 1/4 inch over 8 feet)
-   Knots acceptable up to 2 inches diameter
-   Light surface checking acceptable
-   Dimension acceptable if loss is less than 1/4 inch
-   Use for: Framing, joists, rafters, non-visible structural members

#### Grade C: Utility and Component

-   Significant warping or cupping acceptable
-   Multiple knots, larger knots acceptable
-   Surface cracks and checks acceptable
-   Dimension loss acceptable up to 1/2 inch
-   Use for: Blocking, bracing, temporary structures, firewood processing, split-outs for components

#### Grade D: Salvage and Recycles

-   Severe damage, warping, or rot in small areas
-   Suitable for cutting around damaged sections
-   Possible remill for smaller pieces
-   Use for: Component salvage, firewood, mulch, compost material

### Detecting Rot and Insect Damage

Structural integrity depends on detecting degradation before material fails.

:::danger
Using rotted lumber in structural applications can cause collapse. A visually intact board may be hollow internally, held together only by surface layers. Probing is non-negotiable before using salvaged lumber in load-bearing applications.
:::

#### Visual Inspection

-   **Dry rot:** Sunken areas, surface cracks in cubic pattern, wood powder accumulation, discoloration (brown or gray)
-   **Wet rot:** Soft wood, moss growth, discoloration (black or very dark), wood fibers separate easily
-   **Termite damage:** Hollow chambers under surface, mud tubes on exterior, wood powder and frass (termite droppings)
-   **Carpenter ant damage:** Smooth tunnels and galleries, sawdust piles, lack of mud tubes (unlike termites)
-   **Woodworm damage:** Small (1-3mm) exit holes, fine wood powder, possible active infestation indicated by fresh holes with clean edges

#### Probing Technique

-   Use an awl or pointed tool to probe suspect areas
-   Healthy wood resists penetration; rotted wood allows easy puncture
-   Probe edges of suspect areas to determine extent of damage
-   Test at multiple points—localized damage doesn't necessarily reject the board

#### Moisture Testing

-   Use a moisture meter if available (below 20% is safe for long-term storage)
-   Visual assessment: wood should be dry, not damp or musty-smelling
-   Weight assessment: sound wood is heavier than rotted wood of equivalent size

### Stacking, Drying, and Storage

Proper storage prevents additional degradation and prepares material for use.

<!-- SVG-TODO: Lumber stacking and drying diagram showing proper gaps, spacers, and cover configurations -->

#### Stacking Methodology

-   Stack horizontally on level ground, elevated on supports (concrete blocks or treated lumber)
-   Never stack directly on ground—standing water causes rot
-   Leave 1-2 inch gaps between pieces for air circulation
-   Use spacers (stickers) of 1-2 inches between layers, aligned vertically to distribute weight
-   Organize by grade, dimension, and length for easy access
-   Largest and heaviest pieces at bottom
-   Cross-stack (alternate direction) if storing mixed dimensions for stability

:::tip
Grade lumber placement: Place Grade A (premium) in accessible top locations, Grade B below it, Grade C/D at bottom. This prevents accidentally using high-grade lumber for low-value applications and makes inventory management easier.
:::

#### Storage Protection

-   Cover stacks loosely with shade cloth or roofing material to prevent rain while allowing airflow
-   Do not use plastic sheeting—traps moisture and causes rot
-   Remove vegetation and debris from ground around stacks—prevents pest harborage
-   Inspect regularly for mold, insect activity, or new water damage
-   Restack if pieces settle or gaps close

### Dimensional Lumber vs. Engineered Wood

Salvaged wood includes both traditional dimensional lumber and engineered products. Each has different recovery and reuse considerations.

#### Solid Dimensional Lumber

-   Salvage priority: Very high
-   Reusable as-is for most structural applications
-   Can be milled or hand-dressed for finish use
-   Indefinite lifespan if dry-stored
-   Preferred: Old-growth wood with tighter grain and superior strength

#### Engineered Wood Products

-   **Plywood:** Glue deteriorates with moisture and age. Re-gluing or component separation may be necessary. Limited reusability in structural applications.
-   **Particle board and MDF:** Loses integrity when exposed to moisture. Not reliable for structural reuse. May contain formaldehyde—handle carefully.
-   **Laminated beams:** Glue failure is common. Suitable for component salvage but not reliable for original structural use.
-   **Oriented strand board (OSB):** Similar issues to particle board. Moisture sensitivity is high. Limited structural reuse.

### Treating Salvaged Wood

Some salvaged wood benefits from treatment to extend lifespan or prepare for specific uses.

#### Insect Prevention

-   Heat treatment: Exposure to 130°F+ for several hours kills most wood-boring insects and larvae
-   Neem oil treatment: Natural insecticide, limited effectiveness but non-toxic
-   Boron compounds: Sodium borate application kills active insects and prevents future infestation. Apply to cut ends and any gaps.
-   Freezing: Exposure to 0°F or below for 2+ weeks kills most insects. Effective for stored lumber in winter regions.

#### Rot Prevention

-   Surface drying: Most important factor—keep wood below 20% moisture
-   Boron treatment: Also prevents fungal growth and rot. Apply to cut ends.
-   Oil coating: Linseed oil or similar provides water resistance and fungal inhibition
-   Lime sulfur: Traditional fungicide, effective but caustic—apply carefully

#### Weather Protection

-   Exterior use: Apply linseed oil, natural sealers, or outdoor stains to protect from UV and water
-   Interior use: Oil finishes for aesthetic purposes, minimal treatment needed if dry
-   Ground contact: Boron treatment plus oil or traditional wood preservation methods necessary

## Brick & Masonry Reclamation

Brick and stone from demolished structures represent irreplaceable materials—most modern brick cannot match the color, texture, or quality of older production. A single older residence can contain 10,000-50,000 usable bricks.

### Breaking Mortar Joints

The primary challenge in brick recovery is separating bricks without damage. Mortar must be broken, not the brick.

:::info-box
Old lime mortar (pre-1950) is much softer and easier to remove than modern cement mortar. Check mortar type before deciding on recovery method. Lime mortar may respond to water soaking; cement mortar requires more aggressive techniques.
:::

#### Hand Methods

-   **Chisel and hammer:** Place a cold chisel into mortar joints. Strike sharply but not violently. Work along the joint rather than trying to split at one point. Most effective and least damaging method.
-   **Pointing tool:** A specialized tool with angled point designed specifically for joint work. Rock rather than strike for controlled joint separation.
-   **Pry bar insertion:** Insert a thin pry bar into opened joints and rock gently to separate bricks
-   **Impact chisel:** For larger operations, power chisels speed work significantly. Requires skill to avoid striking brick faces.

#### Power Methods

-   **Angle grinder with masonry blade:** Grind away mortar lines before attempting separation. Creates significant dust and noise—wear full protective equipment. Not recommended in occupied areas.
-   **Masonry saw:** Cuts through mortar and brick together, separating bricks without hand labor. Best for large-scale recovery operations.

#### Technique Optimization

-   Identify mortar type (lime, cement, or mixed)—older lime mortar is softer and easier to remove
-   Work horizontally (along bed joints) preferentially—gravity assists separation
-   Remove head joints (vertical) after bed joints are open
-   Work from top to bottom to avoid material falling on completed work
-   Chip away excess mortar after separation, not during separation

### Cleaning Recovered Bricks

Removing residual mortar improves appearance and reusability without reducing structural value.

#### Hand Cleaning

-   **Wire brush:** Most common method. Vigorously brush brick faces and edges to remove loose mortar. Dust reduction is important.
-   **Brick trowel or scraper:** Scrape away thick mortar deposits. Work perpendicular to brick surface to avoid gouging.
-   **Hammer striking:** Careful strikes on mortar deposits can fracture thick mortar without damaging brick

#### Water Methods

-   Pressure wash: 1500-2500 PSI can remove mortar. Risk of damaging soft or deteriorated bricks—test first.
-   Soaking: Soak bricks in water for 24-48 hours softens mortar, making hand cleaning easier
-   Acid cleaning: Muriatic acid (hydrochloric acid) dissolves mortar remnants. Hazardous—requires PPE and ventilation. Neutralize with baking soda after.

### Assessing Structural Bricks vs. Facade Bricks

Not all bricks are equal. Load-bearing applications require structurally sound material.

#### Structural Grade Bricks

-   Solid or mostly solid (no large voids or delamination)
-   Uniform color—indicates consistent firing and composition
-   Sharp, defined edges and corners
-   Heavy weight relative to size—indicates dense, well-fired brick
-   Rings clearly when struck lightly with hammer—indicates proper firing
-   Original dimensions intact—no compression, warping, or damage

#### Facade/Non-Structural Bricks

-   May have visible voids or delamination
-   Color variation acceptable (often intentional for aesthetic effect)
-   Edge damage acceptable if not extensive
-   Lighter weight acceptable for veneer use
-   Use: Cladding, non-load-bearing walls, decorative applications

### Concrete Block Salvage

Concrete blocks are reusable for structural, infill, and foundation purposes.

-   Separate by removing mortar using same techniques as brick
-   Assess structural integrity—should not crumble when struck
-   Check for major cracks or spalling
-   Minor surface damage is acceptable
-   Reuse for foundations, retaining walls, non-bearing walls
-   Can be crushed for aggregate if damaged

### Stone Recovery

Stone is the most durable masonry material and highly valuable for recovery.

#### Granite and Granite-Type Stone

-   Extremely durable—typically shows minimal damage
-   Remove mortar using techniques above
-   Salvage priority: Very high
-   Reuse: Structural, foundation, cladding, decorative applications

#### Limestone and Marble

-   More brittle than granite—handle carefully during recovery
-   Subject to chemical weathering—check for surface pitting or dissolution
-   Acid sensitive—do not use muriatic acid for cleaning
-   Reuse: Cladding, decorative, high-end masonry

#### Slate and Flagstone

-   Thin pieces prone to cracking—handle carefully
-   Natural layering allows splitting if damage occurs
-   Reuse: Roofing, flooring, cladding, decorative applications

## Roofing Materials

Roofing materials vary dramatically in reusability and value. Early recovery in demolition projects yields the best material.

<!-- SVG-TODO: Roofing material types and removal techniques comparison diagram -->

### Metal Roofing (Highest Value)

Metal roofing is among the most valuable salvage materials—standing seam panels, corrugated, and metal shingles all have excellent reuse potential.

#### Removal Technique

-   Remove fasteners carefully—usually screws that can be reused if removed properly
-   For seamed roofing, break the seams using a seamer tool or hammer and chisel
-   Panel removal should begin from a roof edge to avoid damage
-   Store flat or loosely rolled to prevent creasing

#### Assessment and Reuse

-   **Surface condition:** Light rust or patina acceptable; deep pitting indicates thin remaining material
-   **Structural integrity:** No major dents, creases, or permanent deformation
-   **Fastening compatibility:** Older panels may not align with modern fastener spacing
-   **Lifespan:** 40-60 years remaining depending on original type and condition
-   **Reuse priority:** Very high—metal roofing is expensive and long-lasting

### Asphalt Shingles (Limited Reuse)

Asphalt shingles are difficult to remove without damage and degrade rapidly. Limited reuse potential.

#### Removal

-   Shingles are typically nailed through overlapping upper shingles—must remove multiple courses to access fasteners
-   Heated shingles are more pliable and less likely to tear—remove during warm weather if possible
-   Careful prying with flat bar can separate shingles
-   Expect 30-50% loss to tearing or cracking during removal

#### Assessment

-   Color fading indicates UV exposure and aging
-   Texture loss (missing granules) indicates approaching end of life
-   Soft or tacky shingles suggest degradation and poor reuse prospects
-   Brittle or cracked shingles are unsuitable for reuse
-   If removed carefully and undamaged, shingles may have 5-10 years remaining lifespan

#### Reuse Strategy

-   Use salvaged shingles only for secondary structures or temporary roofing
-   Combine salvaged shingles of similar color to minimize visual mismatch on patch repairs
-   Never rely on asphalt shingles for primary weather protection in long-term structures

### Slate and Tile Roofing (High Value)

Slate and ceramic tile are durable, reusable, and valuable. Careful removal preserves material.

#### Slate Removal

-   Slate is held by nails that must be broken from underneath rather than pulled from above
-   Reach underneath roof structure and break nails with hammer or pry bar
-   Or remove from interior if attic space allows
-   Handle slate carefully—it fractures along natural planes if struck improperly
-   Properly removed slate can be reused multiple times

#### Tile Removal

-   Tile is often mortared in place—must be carefully pried without breaking
-   Chisel away mortar before prying
-   Handle tiles carefully to avoid edge damage
-   Spanish or barrel tiles are valuable; assess color matching for restoration work

#### Assessment and Reuse

-   Check for cracks, breaks, or chips—damage reduces value but doesn't prevent reuse
-   Slight variations in color are normal and add character
-   Lifespan: 50-200+ years depending on material type
-   Reuse priority: Very high—rare and expensive new material

### Flashing, Gutters, and Downspouts

Metal roofing accessories are valuable and easy to salvage.

-   **Lead flashing:** Extremely valuable; remove carefully and store securely
-   **Copper flashing:** Very valuable; assess for patina (desirable) or thin areas (degradation)
-   **Aluminum gutters:** Useful; remove by unbolting or cutting fasteners
-   **Downspouts:** Salvage for reuse in drainage systems or metal recycle
-   **Assessment:** Remove from roof attachment points, clean, and straighten as needed

## Window Glass Salvage

Quality windows are valuable for their glass, frames, and hardware. Careful removal maximizes usable material.

:::warning
Broken glass is sharp and creates serious cut hazards. Wear heavy gloves and eye protection when working with salvaged glass. Sharp edges can penetrate thin leather—leather gloves should be heavy-duty or doubled.
:::

### Safe Glass Removal Techniques

Working with glass requires careful technique to avoid cuts and breakage.

#### Wooden Frame Windows

-   **Interior approach:** Remove frame interior trim (muntins and sashes) to access glass from inside
-   **Glazing compound removal:** Carefully scrape hardened glazing compound around glass edges using a putty knife
-   **Glazing points:** Remove metal points holding glass using needle-nose pliers or scraper
-   **Gentle removal:** Once compound and points are removed, carefully lift glass from one corner, supporting fully as it comes free
-   **Frame preservation:** Clean away old glazing compound and points carefully to preserve frame for reuse

#### Modern Insulated Glass Units

-   Modern double/triple-pane units are sealed—individual glass cannot be separated without breaking seals
-   Remove entire unit as assembly
-   Hardware may be reusable even if glass is broken
-   Value is in complete units or metal frames if glass is damaged

#### Metal Frame Windows

-   Glass is typically caulked or sealed with sealant rather than glazing compound
-   Cut sealant with utility knife or heat gun to soften for removal
-   Remove stops or molding if present
-   Lift glass carefully from frame
-   Clean adhesive residue carefully to preserve frame

### Scoring and Cutting Glass

If salvaged glass needs sizing or damage removal, proper cutting technique prevents further breakage.

#### Score-and-Break Method

-   Secure glass on work surface with one edge slightly overhanging
-   Use glass scorer (carbide wheel tool) to score along desired line—apply light, consistent pressure
-   Do not press hard; score only, do not cut through
-   Tap underneath the scored line gently with hammer
-   Glass should break along the scored line
-   Practice on scrap glass first

#### Wet Saw Cutting

-   Glass wet saws use water cooling and diamond blades
-   Slower than dry cutting but prevents chipping
-   Requires equipment investment but yields cleaner edges
-   Wear eye protection against water spray and glass particles

### Frame Preservation

Window frames have significant reuse value independent of glass condition.

#### Wooden Frames

-   Clean away glazing compound and paint carefully
-   Assess for rot—probe questionable areas with awl
-   Minor rot in non-structural areas may be acceptable
-   Refinish or paint for weather protection if restored to use
-   Reuse as is if historically appropriate

#### Metal Frames

-   Remove adhesive and sealant residue
-   Assess for corrosion—surface rust is acceptable; deep pitting indicates material loss
-   Polish or clean as appropriate for reuse type
-   Test for leaks if planning to reuse in weather-exposed application

### Insulated Glass Units (IGU) Reuse

-   Intact units from buildings with climate control may be fully functional
-   Check seals visually—fogging between panes indicates seal failure
-   Intact units reusable for replacement windows or new construction
-   Failed units (fogged) have value for glass recovery or frame salvage only
-   Size matching is critical for reuse—measure carefully before planning

### Safety Glass Types

Some glass types have special properties and constraints.

-   **Tempered glass:** Cannot be cut or resized—shatters if scored. Reuse only as complete units.
-   **Laminated glass:** Has plastic interlayer for safety. Can be cut but may separate. Reuse if intact.
-   **Regular annealed glass:** Can be scored and cut. Most flexible for reuse.

## Insulation Types & Reuse

Insulation materials vary in reuse potential, handling requirements, and health considerations. Careful recovery preserves usable material.

<!-- SVG-TODO: Insulation material types and identification guide -->

### Fiberglass Insulation

The most common insulation type. Reusable if carefully removed without compression or contamination.

#### Removal and Handling

-   Wear respiratory protection—fiberglass fibers are respiratory irritant
-   Wear gloves and long sleeves to prevent skin irritation
-   Remove carefully to avoid compression—compressed fiberglass loses R-value
-   Roll loosely or fold carefully to minimize damage
-   Store in sealed containers to prevent fiber release in storage areas

#### Condition Assessment

-   **Dry fiberglass:** Maintains 80-90% of original R-value if uncompressed
-   **Damp fiberglass:** Loses insulating value; allow drying before reuse or discard
-   **Contaminated fiberglass:** Discard if contaminated with asbestos, mold, or chemical residue
-   **Compressed fiberglass:** Reuse at reduced R-value for non-critical applications or shred for infill

#### Reuse Applications

-   Attic insulation replacement—thickness adjustment for original R-value
-   Wall cavity infill where settling has reduced density
-   Non-critical insulation applications
-   Avoid for applications requiring specific R-value rating due to degradation risk

### Rigid Foam Insulation

Expanded polystyrene (EPS), extruded polystyrene (XPS), and polyurethane boards have excellent reuse potential.

#### Recovery Techniques

-   Remove carefully to avoid breaking into pieces—intact boards are much more valuable
-   Remove fasteners first, then gently pry away from substrate
-   Hot wire cutting if boards must be sized
-   Stack flat in dry location for storage

#### Condition Assessment

-   Check for water damage—foam will degrade if waterlogged
-   Look for compression or crushing that indicates load damage
-   Assess for UV degradation (yellowing, brittleness)—sun-exposed foam degrades
-   Minor surface damage acceptable if interior is intact
-   Reusable lifespan: 20-50 years depending on storage conditions and degradation

#### Reuse Applications

-   Insulation layer in new construction—intact boards
-   Shredded into loose fill for cavity insulation
-   Base material for other insulation products if broken
-   Non-critical insulation where exact R-value is not required

### Mineral Wool Insulation

Rock wool and slag wool insulation are durable and reusable.

#### Recovery and Handling

-   Wear respiratory protection—mineral fibers can be irritating
-   Remove carefully to avoid breakage into small fragments
-   Handle with care—mineral wool is heavier and denser than fiberglass
-   Moisture damage is less critical than with fiberglass—mineral wool can be dried and reused

#### Reuse Potential

-   High reuse value—does not degrade significantly with age if kept dry
-   Fire resistance maintained indefinitely
-   Sound absorption maintained
-   Reuse for thermal, fire, or acoustic insulation

### Natural Insulation Materials

Historic buildings may contain cork, cellulose, or hemp insulation.

-   **Cork:** Extremely durable; assess for water damage or deterioration
-   **Cellulose:** Treated with fire retardant; assess for moisture and pest damage
-   **Hemp or wool:** Natural products; check for mold, pest damage, or deterioration
-   Reuse or salvage as reclaimed specialty product—market demand exists for authentic materials

### Asbestos Identification and Avoidance (Critical)

Asbestos was common in insulation, pipe wrap, floor tiles, and roofing materials until the 1970s. Do not disturb suspected asbestos.

:::danger
Asbestos exposure is irreversible and dose-accumulative. Mesothelioma can develop 20-50 years after initial exposure. Never disturb or remove suspected asbestos-containing materials. This is not a value judgment—it's a survival decision.
:::

#### Visual Identification (Not Definitive)

-   Loose, fluffy insulation that appears corroded or deteriorated
-   Gray or white pipe wrap material
-   Sprayed-on ceiling or wall insulation
-   Floor tiles or roofing materials from pre-1980s buildings

#### Safe Handling Protocol

-   Do NOT disturb or remove suspected asbestos materials
-   Do NOT cut, drill, sand, or handle asbestos products
-   Hire licensed asbestos abatement professionals for removal—required by law
-   Do not salvage or reuse any suspected asbestos-containing materials
-   Exposure risk is minimal if materials are left undisturbed

#### Professional Testing

-   If uncertain about material, hire certified asbestos inspector for testing
-   Lab analysis is inexpensive insurance against exposure
-   Do not salvage questionable material until testing confirms asbestos absence

## Doors, Fixtures & Hardware

Architectural hardware, fixtures, and doors have significant reuse and aesthetic value, especially older high-quality pieces.

### Door Hardware

Hinges, locks, handles, and decorative hardware are valuable salvage items.

:::tip
Old brass and bronze hardware often has higher quality and durability than modern alternatives. Even ornate "decorative" hardware is functional and valuable for restoration projects or trade goods.
:::

#### Hinges

-   **Cast brass or bronze:** Highest value; clean and reuse as-is or refinish
-   **Steel hinges:** Reusable; assess for rust and functionality
-   Remove by unbolting—screws often seized but generally removable with patience and penetrating oil
-   Test for full range of motion before declaring functional
-   Old hinges often stronger and more durable than modern hardware

#### Locks and Handles

-   High-quality locks from older buildings often superior to modern locks
-   Skeleton keys are sought by collectors and restoration specialists
-   Remove carefully—many antique locks are fragile
-   Clean and lubricate, but avoid modern lubricants that may damage mechanism
-   Test function thoroughly before installation

#### Decorative Hardware

-   Brass plates, door knockers, mail slots, and kick plates are valuable
-   Clean and assess for damage or missing components
-   Patina is often desirable—minimize aggressive cleaning
-   Can command premium prices in antique and restoration markets

### Plumbing Fixtures

Quality plumbing fixtures have reuse and aesthetic value.

#### Removal Technique

-   Turn off water supply before beginning work
-   Drain fixture and connected lines
-   Disconnect water supply lines (may require wrench work if connections are corroded)
-   Disconnect waste lines if present
-   Remove mounting bolts or fasteners
-   Clean away old caulking or sealant carefully

#### Fixture Types and Reuse

-   **Sinks:** Cast iron, porcelain-coated steel, or vitreous china—assess condition and refinish if needed
-   **Faucets:** Brass faucets are highest value; test for leaks and functionality
-   **Tubs and showers:** Cast iron tubs are very reusable; test for damage and integrity
-   **Toilets:** Older china toilets are often more durable; assess for cracks

### Electrical Fixtures

Light fixtures, switch plates, and outlet covers have both functional and aesthetic value.

-   **Light fixtures:** Brass, copper, and ceramic fixtures are reusable; assess for damage to interior wiring
-   **Switch plates and outlet covers:** Porcelain or bakelite covers are collectible; remove carefully to avoid breakage
-   **Restoration:** Original fixtures can be rewired for modern electrical codes
-   **Lighting elements:** Period-appropriate bulbs and shades may still be available or reproducible

### Cabinet Hardware

Historic cabinet hardware—knobs, pulls, hinges—is valuable for restoration work.

-   Remove carefully by unbolting from cabinet interior
-   Assess for matching sets—handles and hinges of coordinated design command higher prices
-   Clean gently to preserve patina unless restoration is intended
-   Catalog with measurements for matching future discovery or sale

### Appliance Components

Vintage appliances often contain salvageable hardware and components.

-   **Stoves and ranges:** Grates, thermostats, timers, and hardware are reusable
-   **Refrigerators:** Seals, hinges, shelving, and hardware salvageable
-   **Washers and dryers:** Motors, pulleys, and mechanical components often reusable or salvageable for parts
-   **Assessment:** Verify functional status and compatibility with models where components might be reinstalled

## Quality Assessment

Developing a comprehensive grading system ensures consistent evaluation and appropriate material allocation.

### Universal Grading System

#### Grade A: Premium/Structural

-   Minimal visible damage or defects
-   Original dimensions intact
-   No structural compromise
-   Fully functional and ready for reuse
-   Use: Primary structural, finish work, visible applications
-   Price: Premium—authentic old or rare material

#### Grade B: Good/Standard

-   Minor damage or wear acceptable
-   Fully functional
-   Suitable for structural and most visible applications
-   May need minor cleaning or touch-up
-   Use: General structural, most construction applications
-   Price: Standard—good value for reuse

#### Grade C: Fair/Utility

-   Obvious but non-structural damage acceptable
-   Functional for intended purpose with repairs
-   Suitable for non-visible or secondary applications
-   May require significant cleaning or restoration
-   Use: Non-bearing, utility, internal structures
-   Price: Reduced—but good value for non-critical use

#### Grade D: Poor/Salvage

-   Significant damage or wear
-   Limited functional use—primarily for salvage
-   Use: Component salvage, material recycle, scrap
-   Price: Minimal—value in material content only

### Moisture Content Testing

Critical for wooden materials and some masonry.

#### Moisture Meter Method

-   Non-invasive meter useful for quick assessment
-   Target range: 10-15% for interior use, 15-20% for exterior
-   Above 20%: Material may not be stable; allow drying
-   Test multiple locations on each piece—moisture distribution varies

#### Weight Assessment

-   Experienced evaluators can assess moisture by weight
-   Green (wet) wood is significantly heavier than dried wood
-   Requires comparative knowledge of species and size

#### Visual and Tactile Assessment

-   Smooth, dry surface indicates low moisture
-   Damp surface or musty smell indicates high moisture
-   Checking (surface cracks) is normal in drying wood and acceptable

### Load-Bearing Capacity Estimation

Critical for materials being reused in structural applications.

#### Visual Inspection for Defects

-   **Knots:** Reduce load capacity. Large knots (>2 inches) in critical load paths are concerning.
-   **Cracks and checks:** Surface checking is acceptable; through-cracks are serious.
-   **Rot or insect damage:** Any void reduces effective cross-section and capacity.
-   **Warping:** Affects load distribution; bow of more than 1/4 inch per 8 feet is significant.

#### Conservative Rating Approach

-   Without testing, rate salvaged materials at 80% of nominal capacity
-   Materials with obvious defects: 60% of nominal capacity
-   Materials with significant history of good performance (proven in original structure): 90% of nominal
-   When actual capacity is critical, engage structural engineer for evaluation

#### Species Identification

-   Old-growth timber is often stronger than modern equivalents of same species
-   Wood species greatly affects load capacity (old-growth pine vs. hardwoods)
-   When reusing in structural application, conservative approach is safest if species is uncertain

## Structural Demolition Safety

Safe, methodical demolition ensures best material recovery while protecting workers. Rushing creates hazards and damages salvage.

### Sequence of Demolition

Work from top to bottom, outside to inside, removing non-structural before structural.

#### Phase 1: Remove Interior Elements

-   Fixtures, hardware, and finishes first (electrical, plumbing, appliances)
-   Interior walls and non-load-bearing partitions
-   Doors, windows, and frames
-   Flooring and underlayment
-   Ceilings and acoustic materials

#### Phase 2: Remove Roofing and Upper Structure

-   Roofing materials, flashing, gutters
-   Upper floor systems and joists
-   Roof framing and trusses
-   This phase creates the most safety risk—fall hazards are extreme

#### Phase 3: Remove Non-Load-Bearing Walls

-   Exterior sheathing and cladding
-   Interior non-load-bearing walls
-   Infill walls between primary structural members
-   Verify non-load-bearing status before removal

#### Phase 4: Remove Load-Bearing Elements

-   Remove only after all dependent structures are secured or removed
-   Wall frames and structural posts
-   Beams and primary structural members
-   This phase requires experienced personnel and proper shoring

#### Phase 5: Foundation and Final Debris

-   Remove brick/stone from basement
-   Clear final debris
-   Leave foundation (usually not salvageable)

### Shoring and Load Transfer

As you remove elements, loads must be transferred to remaining structure or temporary support.

#### Identifying Load Paths

-   Loads flow from roofs down through walls to foundations
-   Understand which walls and posts support which loads
-   Remove non-load-bearing elements first to simplify remaining work
-   When in doubt, assume a wall is load-bearing and protect it

#### Temporary Shoring

-   Install temporary posts and bracing before removing structural supports
-   Use adequate posts (4x4 or larger) on firm foundations
-   Shoring must support all loads above without settling
-   Diagonal bracing prevents shoring from toppling
-   Consult structural engineer for complex demolition

### Tool Requirements

-   Pry bars (4-8 feet) for leverage during deconstruction
-   Reciprocating saw with varied blades for cutting fasteners and materials
-   Cordless drill/driver for screw removal
-   Hammer and cold chisel for masonry and mortar
-   Wire cutters for electrical work (after power is off)
-   Adjustable wrenches for bolt removal
-   Work gloves, safety glasses, and respiratory protection (dust hazard)
-   Hard hat if work is overhead or under structures
-   Fall protection if working on elevated structures

### Team Coordination

Large demolition requires multiple skilled workers with clear communication.

:::warning
Demolition is the most dangerous phase of salvage operations. Coordination failures directly cause injuries and deaths. Clear communication and strict adherence to roles is non-negotiable.
:::

#### Roles and Responsibilities

-   **Foreman/Lead:** Overall coordination, load path understanding, safety oversight
-   **Salvage team:** Careful material removal, sorting, stacking
-   **Debris crew:** Removal of non-salvageable material, site cleanup
-   **Safety monitor:** Continuous monitoring for hazards, worker safety

#### Communication Protocol

-   Pre-work briefing on the day's sequence and hazards
-   Hand signals and radio communication for overhead work
-   Stop-work authority for any person identifying unsafe condition
-   Regular check-ins to verify shoring and structural integrity

### Hazard Prevention

#### Fall Prevention

:::danger
Falls are the leading cause of death in demolition operations. Fall protection is absolutely required for any work above 6 feet. No exceptions. No "it's just a quick job." Falls kill people regularly.
:::

-   Guard all floor openings (hole covers)
-   Use fall protection when above 6 feet
-   Avoid working near edges of elevated structures
-   Clear debris regularly to prevent trip hazards

#### Struck-By Prevention

-   Never work directly under active work areas
-   Secure all materials being removed
-   Use chutes or controlled lowering rather than dropping materials
-   Post spotters to prevent tools or materials from hitting workers

#### Electrical Hazards

-   Shut off power at main breaker before starting interior work
-   Verify power is off with voltage tester before touching electrical components
-   Remove or isolate power lines if building is still connected to grid
-   Consider overhead power lines during upper structure removal

#### Dust and Air Quality

-   Wear appropriate respirators (N95 minimum for dust, P100 for hazardous materials)
-   Wet methods reduce dust during demolition
-   Avoid creating dust clouds that affect workers or neighbors
-   Test for hazardous materials (asbestos, lead) before disturbing materials

### Material Sorting and Staging

Proper staging improves efficiency and material preservation.

#### On-Site Organization

-   Establish separate areas for different material types (lumber, brick, hardware, etc.)
-   Stack materials appropriately to prevent damage and enable easy access
-   Protect materials from weather—cover temporarily if extended storage expected
-   Keep pathways clear for safety and efficiency

#### Quality Control During Recovery

-   Inspect materials immediately upon removal while condition is fresh
-   Grade materials as they're recovered
-   Sort non-salvageable from salvageable during removal process
-   This prevents mixing grades and simplifies later organization
