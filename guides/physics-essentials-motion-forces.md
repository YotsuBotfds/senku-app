# Physics Essentials: Motion, Forces & Simple Machines

<section id="introduction">

Physics is the fundamental science of how things move, interact, and change. For survivors rebuilding civilization, understanding motion and forces is essential—from constructing buildings and machines to managing resources efficiently. You don't need calculus or complex mathematics; you need intuition backed by simple principles. This guide covers Newton's laws in practical terms, types of forces, and the six simple machines that amplify human effort across countless survival tasks.

</section>

<section id="newtons-laws">

## Newton's Three Laws in Practical Terms

### First Law: Objects in Motion Stay in Motion

An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion, unless acted upon by an external force.

**What this means:** Motion is the natural state. Friction, air resistance, and other forces bring moving objects to a stop. A ball thrown across a field travels farther than a ball pushed across gravel because friction is greater.

**Survival applications:**
- Momentum carries water downhill in aqueducts without continuous input
- A heavy hammer swing continues even after releasing it (momentum converts to impact force)
- A boat coasts longer in water than on land due to less friction
- Spinning tools (flywheels, grindstones) store momentum for repeated work

**Key insight:** Reduce friction where you want motion to continue (smooth surfaces, bearings, lubrication). Increase friction where you want to stop motion (brake pads, rough treads, anchors).

### Second Law: Force Equals Mass Times Acceleration

Force = Mass × Acceleration (F = ma)

**What this means:** The more massive an object, the more force is needed to accelerate it. A heavier hammer accelerates slower than a lighter one under the same effort, but hits harder because momentum (mass × velocity) is greater.

Acceleration can be thought of as change in speed or direction—pushing a ball forward accelerates it; pushing sideways changes direction.

:::info-box
**Practical Examples:**
- Pushing a child on a swing: light mass, small force creates visible acceleration
- Pushing a car stuck in mud: heavy mass, large force needed for any acceleration
- Throwing a rock vs. throwing a boulder: same effort produces different accelerations
- Steering a fast-moving bicycle: small force on handlebars accelerates the bike's direction significantly
:::

**Survival applications:**
- Larger tools (heavy hammers, large axes) need more effort to swing but deliver more impact
- Lighter tools are faster and less tiring but less powerful
- A moving log (momentum) requires much less force to guide direction than to start from rest
- Animal yokes: Distributing load across multiple animals multiplies useful force

### Third Law: Action-Reaction Pairs

For every action, there's an equal and opposite reaction.

**What this means:** Forces always occur in pairs. When you push against the ground, the ground pushes back with equal force, propelling you forward. Without the ground's reaction, you wouldn't move.

**Survival applications:**
- Rowing a boat: paddle pushes water backward; water pushes boat forward
- Firing a bow: pulling bowstring backward stores energy; releasing converts to arrow forward (and recoil backward)
- Digging: shovel pushes soil downward and backward; soil pushes shovel upward
- Walking: foot pushes ground backward; ground pushes foot forward
- Climbing a rope: hands/feet pull downward on rope; rope pulls you upward

**Key insight:** Every tool works because of reaction forces. Your effort is transmitted to accomplish work against an equal reaction force.

:::tip
**Design Insight:** The strongest structures exploit action-reaction pairs. A bridge spreads support evenly; a weak bridge concentrates forces at single points (failure mode). Distribute forces across multiple supports.
:::

<section id="types-of-forces">

## Types of Forces You'll Encounter

### Gravity & Weight

Gravity is the fundamental attractive force between all mass. On Earth's surface, it produces a constant acceleration (g = 9.8 m/s²), so weight = mass × 9.8.

- **1 kg** = **9.8 newtons** of gravitational force (or roughly 10 for quick math)
- Heavy objects fall at the same rate as light ones (if air resistance is negligible)
- Gravity always acts downward at the center of mass

**Survival implications:**
- Lifting and carrying: Strength limits depend on gravity's pull. A 50 kg load requires constant effort; set it down and effort ceases
- Water transport: Gravity is free energy; water flows downhill automatically
- Potential energy: Objects at height store gravitational energy; releasing it does work (waterfalls, falling logs)

### Friction

Friction opposes motion between surfaces in contact. Two types exist:

**Static friction** (prevents motion from starting):
- Stronger than kinetic friction
- A heavy box won't slide until you push hard enough to overcome static friction
- Practical: Static friction anchors objects, prevents slipping

**Kinetic friction** (opposes ongoing motion):
- Constant once motion begins
- Weaker than static friction
- Practical: Friction heat, energy loss, braking

:::info-box
**Friction Coefficient Examples:**
- Rubber on dry concrete: 0.7–1.0 (high friction; good grip)
- Steel on steel: 0.4–0.6 (medium; sliding resistance)
- Ice on ice: 0.02–0.05 (very low; skis, sledges)
- Wood on wood (dry): 0.25–0.5 (moderate)
- Wet surfaces: Much lower friction across all materials
:::

Friction = Coefficient × Normal Force (perpendicular pressing force)

Increase friction by increasing pressure (pressing objects together harder). Decrease friction by:
- Smoothing surfaces (polishing)
- Adding lubricants (oil, wax, ice)
- Reducing normal force (lighter load)
- Changing materials (rubber instead of plastic)

**Survival applications:**
- Rope knots work through friction; wet rope knots slip
- Tight grips (high normal force) overcome slippery surfaces
- Lubrication reduces bearing friction, extends tool life
- Friction in fire starting (stick-on-wood, rubbing materials)

### Normal Force

The perpendicular pushing force between two surfaces in contact. A book on a table experiences normal force equal to its weight (perpendicular to surface). On an incline, normal force is reduced because some gravity pulls parallel to the surface.

**Formula:** Normal Force = Weight × cos(angle of incline)

- At 0° (flat): Normal force = full weight
- At 30°: Normal force = 0.87 × weight
- At 45°: Normal force = 0.71 × weight
- At 90° (vertical wall): Normal force = 0

Parallel component = Weight × sin(angle) — this is why objects slide faster down steeper inclines.

### Tension

The pulling force transmitted through ropes, cables, or chains. Tension is always equal throughout a rope (assuming the rope is light and there's no friction). A rope between two people transfers force equally to each person.

**Survival applications:**
- Rope strength (tensile strength) determines safe load limits
- Pulleys redirect tension but don't multiply it without mechanical advantage
- Breaking strength vs. working load: Use 5:1 safety factor (rope rated 5x expected load)
- Taut ropes support loads; slack ropes fail suddenly

### Pressure

Force distributed over an area. High pressure in small area; low pressure over large area.

**Pressure = Force / Area**

- Sharp tools (small area) cut easily; dull tools (same force, larger contact area) require much more effort
- Heels (small area) sink in sand/mud; snowshoes (large area) stay on surface
- Water pressure increases with depth (10 meters = ~2 atmospheres additional pressure)

**Survival applications:**
- Tool sharpness depends on pressure concentration at the edge
- Weight distribution: Spread load to prevent sinking or causing damage
- Pressure vessels: Strong containers resist pressure; weak ones rupture

## Work & Energy

**Work = Force × Distance (in direction of force)**

Work occurs when force moves an object; holding an object takes effort but produces no work (distance = 0).

**Energy = Capacity to do work**

Energy exists in many forms:
- **Kinetic:** Motion energy (moving object)
- **Potential:** Stored energy (height, compression, tension)
- **Thermal:** Heat energy
- **Chemical:** Energy stored in fuel, food, batteries

**Energy is conserved:** Total energy before = total energy after; it converts between forms but doesn't vanish.

:::info-box
**Practical Energy Conversions:**
- Wood burning: Chemical energy → Thermal energy
- Water flowing downhill: Potential energy → Kinetic energy (+ heat from friction)
- Hammer impact: Kinetic energy → Deformation energy (+ heat)
- Compression spring: Applied force → Potential energy (elastic); release → Kinetic energy
- Wind generator: Kinetic energy (wind) → Mechanical rotation → Electrical energy
:::

**Power = Work / Time**

Power measures how fast work is done. A strong person working slowly may produce less power than a weaker person working faster.

**Survival implications:**
- Efficiency: Use energy wisely; avoid wasted motion
- Leverage and machines: Reduce effort by increasing distance (slow down the movement, reduce required force)
- Renewable power: Wind and water are continuous energy sources; use them before stored energy (batteries)

</section>

<section id="simple-machines">

## Six Simple Machines: Multiplying Human Effort

Simple machines amplify force or change direction, following a universal principle: **You never get energy for free. Mechanical advantage trades distance for force.**

## The Six Simple Machines Diagram

![The six simple machines: lever, wheel and axle, pulley, inclined plane, wedge, and screw](../assets/svgs/physics-essentials-motion-forces-1.svg)

A visual reference showing all six simple machines with clear examples of each type and how they function to create mechanical advantage in practical applications.

### 1. Lever

A rigid bar pivoting around a fulcrum. The lever magnifies force by increasing the distance from fulcrum to effort point.

:::info-box
**Mechanical Advantage (MA) = Distance from fulcrum to effort / Distance from fulcrum to load**

Example: 2-meter lever with fulcrum 0.5m from the load.
- Load distance: 0.5m
- Effort distance: 1.5m
- MA = 1.5 / 0.5 = 3
- Therefore: 10 kg load feels like 3.3 kg to lift
:::

**Three lever classes:**

**Class 1 (fulcrum in middle):** Seesaw, crowbar, scissors
- Mechanical advantage possible both directions
- Effort and load on opposite sides

**Class 2 (load in middle):** Wheelbarrow, bottle opener, shovel blade moving soil
- Mechanical advantage always > 1 (force is multiplied)
- Effort and load on same side; effort further from fulcrum

**Class 3 (effort in middle):** Arm lifting an object, baseball bat
- Mechanical advantage always < 1 (force is reduced but speed increases)
- Speed and distance are gained at cost of force

**Survival applications:**
- Prying stuck objects: Class 1 lever (crowbar) magnifies force
- Digging: Class 3 lever (arm + shovel) is long-range; Class 2 (wheelbarrow) multiplies lifting force
- Cutting: Scissors and axes use leverage to concentrate force at a sharp edge
- Rowing: Oar is Class 2 lever; fulcrum is in water; effort point is far from load

### 2. Wheel & Axle

A wheel rotating around a central axle. Similar principle to lever: mechanical advantage equals wheel radius divided by axle radius.

**MA = Wheel diameter / Axle diameter**

Large wheel on small axle magnifies force and distance.

**Survival applications:**
- Pulleys: Wheel around a rope; changes direction and multiplies force
- Windlasses: Large wheel with rope on small axle; one rotation of wheel pulls rope much faster
- Vehicle wheels: Friction at axle is minimized; rolling is far more efficient than dragging
- Gears: Multiple wheels on different axles transfer force between different speeds

### 3. Pulley

A grooved wheel mounted on a fixed or movable support with rope running through.

**Fixed pulley:**
- MA = 1 (no mechanical advantage; only changes direction)
- Advantage: Pull downward instead of upward (easier for some people)
- Application: Lifting objects to height with rope over a branch

**Movable pulley:**
- MA = 2 (load is supported by two rope segments)
- Disadvantage: Requires pulling twice the distance of fixed pulley
- Application: Lifting heavy loads with reduced effort

**Compound pulley systems:**
- Combine multiple fixed and movable pulleys
- MA = number of rope segments supporting the load
- Application: Block-and-tackle systems lift very heavy loads with manageable force

:::tip
**Pulley Practical Math:** A 6-rope block-and-tackle (MA = 6) reduces a 600 kg load to feel like 100 kg of effort, but requires pulling 6 meters of rope to lift 1 meter of load.
:::

### 4. Inclined Plane

A ramp reducing the force needed to lift an object vertically, trading distance.

**MA = Vertical height / Ramp length (or 1/sin(angle))**

:::info-box
**Incline Examples:**
- 30° slope: MA = 2 (half the lifting force; double the distance)
- 45° slope: MA = 1.4 (40% less force; 40% more distance)
- 10° slope: MA = 5.7 (very gentle ramp; large distance; small force)
:::

**Survival applications:**
- Building ramps for lifting heavy loads gradually
- Loading onto vehicles: Ramp is easier than lifting directly
- Road construction: Switchbacks on mountains reduce slope; longer routes, easier climbing
- Wedge (pointed incline): Splits logs, tightens joints

### 5. Wedge

A pointed or sharp inclined plane that enters objects to split or separate them. The narrow angle concentrates force.

**MA = Ramp length / Thickness at base**

A thin, long wedge magnifies force more than a thick, short one.

**Survival applications:**
- Axes and hatchets: Wedge shape splits wood efficiently
- Knives and scissors: Wedge shape separates food and materials
- Doorstops: Wedge prevents motion
- Splitting: Driving wedges into logs separates wood fibers

### 6. Screw

A cylinder with an inclined plane wrapped around it (the thread). Rotation converts to linear motion.

**MA = Circumference / Thread pitch (distance between threads)**

**Survival applications:**
- Fastening: Screws hold objects together with high tension
- Vises: Rotation of screw clamps objects with great force
- Lifts: Car jacks use screws for stable, controllable lifting
- Water pumps: Archimedean screw lifts water using rotation

:::warning
**Screw Failures:** Screws can strip (threads damaged) if overtightened or mismatched to material. Wood screws are different from machine screws. Metal fasteners corrode in wet conditions; replace periodically.
:::

## Compound Machines: Combining Simple Machines

Real tools almost always combine multiple simple machines for optimal effect.

**Bicycle example:**
- Pedal (crank): Class 2 lever
- Gear teeth: Inclined planes (wedges)
- Sprockets: Wheels on different axles
- Chain: Transmits force between sprockets
- Wheels: Reduce rolling resistance
- Brakes: Friction applied to wheels

A bicycle multiplies human pedaling power through multiple mechanical advantages, allowing efficient speed or climbing.

**Plow example:**
- Wedge blade: Cuts soil
- Handles: Levers for control
- Plow body: Inclined plane directing soil
- Wheel axle: Reduces friction

**Windlass (manual lifting device):**
- Wheel: Large radius for ease of rotation
- Axle: Small radius for mechanical advantage
- Rope: Transmits force over distance
- MA = Wheel radius / Axle radius × (number of rope layers on axle)

## Estimating Forces Without Instruments

In survival situations, you won't always have scales or force meters. Use your body as a reference.

### Reference Forces (Your Body)

- **Your weight:** Balance on one leg; your leg/foot supports your weight
- **Arm strength:** How much weight can you lift overhead? This is your rough maximum output force
- **Grip strength:** How hard you can squeeze reveals maximum hand force
- **Leg strength:** How high you can jump or how much you can squat reveals leg power

### Practical Estimation Techniques

**Weighing objects:**
- Compare to your body weight (you know this)
- Estimate by lifting progressively (too light, comfortable, heavy, impossible)
- Distribute across carriers; if two people easily carry an object, it's roughly 50 kg each

**Force measurement:**
- Pull on a rope or object; estimate the effort (easy, moderate, straining, impossible)
- Scale effort to known references (same effort as lifting yourself one-armed = your full arm strength)
- Multiply for mechanical advantage (6:1 pulley = 1/6 of direct lift effort)

**Stress on materials:**
- Bend a branch until it breaks; estimate the force required
- Repeat with different materials; develop intuition
- Safety factor: Use 5–10× the breaking force as safe working load
- Account for age and weathering (wood becomes brittle; metal fatigues)

:::tip
**Build Estimation Practice:** Experiment with known weights (water = 1 kg per liter, common enough to estimate accurately). Lift various objects and compare to water containers. Over time, your intuition improves.
:::

## Center of Gravity & Stability

**Center of gravity:** The point where an object's weight is concentrated for purposes of calculation. For uniform shapes, it's at the geometric center. For irregular shapes, it's where mass is concentrated.

### Stability Principles

A standing object is stable if its center of gravity is within its base of support. Tip it over when center of gravity moves outside the base.

**Wide base = stable** (hard to tip)
**Narrow base = unstable** (easy to tip)
**Low center of gravity = stable** (heavy low, light high)
**High center of gravity = unstable** (heavy high, light low)

**Practical implications:**
- Squat stance (legs wide, center low): Very stable for carrying loads or resisting impact
- Tall structure with narrow base: Easily toppled by wind; needs guy-wires
- Loaded vehicle: Heavy load low is stable; heavy load high is unstable (risk of rollover)
- Arches and domes: Center of gravity within structure; distribute stress efficiently
- Bridges: Weight concentrated at supports; long spans need central support

:::warning
**Structural Failures:** Buildings collapse when load exceeds support strength or when center of gravity moves outside the base during wind, settling, or damage. Design structures with safety margin.
:::

### Balancing & Equilibrium

An object in equilibrium has zero net force and zero net torque (rotational force).

- **Balance a seesaw:** Equal weight at equal distances from fulcrum
- **Balance a load across shoulders:** Center of gravity directly above support (your neck/shoulders)
- **Stacking boxes:** Stack heavier boxes lower; lighter on top

**Practical construction:**
- Ladders: Lean at angle; center of gravity must stay within base
- Scaffolding: Distribute weight evenly; avoid concentrated loads
- Loads on beams: Distribute across beam rather than concentrated at center
- Stable positioning: Wide stance, low center of gravity, balanced load

<section id="practical-calculations">

## Quick Practical Calculations

### Lifting Weight with Mechanical Advantage

**Example:** Lift 300 kg load using 3:1 pulley system.
- Required force: 300 kg ÷ 3 = 100 kg effort (achievable by one person)
- Rope pulled: 3 meters to lift 1 meter
- Time: Depends on pulling speed

### Ramp Length for Incline

**Example:** Lift 100 kg load up 2 meters height with 50 kg effort limit.
- MA needed: 100 kg ÷ 50 kg = 2
- For MA = 2 with incline: Angle = 30°; ramp length = 4 meters
- Trade distance for force (twice the horizontal distance)

### Beam Support

**Example:** Single beam supporting 1000 kg load at center.
- Load on each support: 500 kg
- Force at each support point: 500 kg × 9.8 = 4900 newtons (roughly 500 kgf)

### Simple Wedge Splitting

**Example:** Driving wedge into wood
- Wedge angle: 30° (length-to-width ratio ~2:1)
- Force applied to wedge: ~500 kg effort
- Force pushing wood apart: 500 kg × sin(30°) = 250 kg of spreading force
- The wood fibers must overcome this to resist splitting
