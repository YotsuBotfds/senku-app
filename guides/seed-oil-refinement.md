# Seed Oil Refinement & Grading

**This guide picks up after pressing.** For seed preparation, press construction and operation, cold-pressing vs hot-pressing methods, basic filtration and settling, yield expectations, and press-cake byproducts, see the [Oil Pressing & Extraction guide](oil-pressing.html). That guide owns first extraction; this guide owns everything that happens to the oil after it leaves the press.

Crude seed oil pressed from sunflower, rapeseed, soybean, or linseed contains impurities that limit its shelf life, affect flavor, reduce visual appeal, and diminish performance as a lubricant. Refinement processes separate high-quality food-grade oil from lower-grade machine oil and extend storage life from weeks to years. This guide covers practical refinement methods achievable with hand tools and improvised equipment, quality testing, and storage strategies for long-term stability.

<section id="crude-oil-composition">

## Crude Oil Composition and Why Refinement Matters

### Impurities in Freshly Pressed Oil

**Triglycerides** (~95% of pressed oil): The desired component—three fatty acids bound to glycerin. These are stable and have good nutritional and lubricant properties.

**Free fatty acids** (0.5–4%): Unbound fatty acids liberated by hydrolysis during pressing or storage. High free fatty acids cause:
- Rancidity odor and soapy taste (food use)
- Corrosion of ferrous metals (machine use)
- Reduced viscosity and flow characteristics (lubricant use)

**Phospholipids** (gums, 0.5–1%): Complex molecules with phosphorus-containing head groups. These:
- Create haze and cloudiness in oil (undesirable appearance)
- Oxidize easily, promoting rancidity
- Form sludge when heated
- Can interfere with food quality and shelf life

**Waxes** (0.1–0.5%): Long-chain fatty compounds. In tropical climates, waxes stay dissolved but cloud the oil as temperature drops. Removal is optional for machine oil but important for food-grade (clarity) and extremely cold climates (pour point).

**Pigments** (chlorophyll, carotenoids, oxidized compounds): Color. Natural yellow pigments are benign; brown or dark colors indicate oxidation or contamination.

**Volatiles** (trace): Small molecules from off-flavors or spoilage organisms. Removed by gentle heating.

### Why Food-Grade and Machine-Grade Oils Differ

**Food-grade oil** (cooking, salad, supplement):
- Free fatty acid content <0.3% (low acidity)
- Clear or very pale color
- Odor is mild, nutty, or neutral
- Phospholipids removed (no haze)
- Waxes removed (clear at room temperature)
- Peroxide value <10 (low oxidation)
- Suitable for human consumption

**Machine-grade oil** (lubricant, hydraulic, cutting fluid):
- Free fatty acid content can be 0.5–2% (acidity tolerated)
- Color yellow to brown (pigments acceptable)
- Odor is oily/fatty
- Some phospholipids may remain (improve lubricity)
- Waxes acceptable (pour point control via blending)
- Peroxide value <30 (moderate oxidation acceptable)
- Suitable for engine or bearing lubrication

**The refinement process separates** the crude oil into food-grade (light color, low acid) and machine-grade (darker, higher acid) fractions. Often, the same crude oil batch yields both products depending on how thoroughly it is refined.

<section id="degumming">

## Degumming Process

Removing phospholipids (gums) is the first refining step and creates the most visible improvement (oil transitions from cloudy to clear).

### Water Degumming (Simplest Method)

**Principle**: Phospholipids are polar molecules that have water-soluble head groups. Adding a small amount of hot water causes phospholipids to hydrate and separate from oil.

**Procedure**:

1. **Heat crude oil** to 60–65°C (140–150°F) in a double-boiler or jacketed tank
   - Temperature control: place thermometer in oil; stir every few minutes; do not exceed 70°C (overheating causes oxidation)
   - Heating can be passive (sun in a clear tank) or active (electric heater, steam jacket)

2. **Calculate water addition**: Use 1–3% of oil weight
   - Example: 10 kg crude oil + 100–300 mL hot water (104–107°F)
   - Start with 1% (100 mL); if phospholipids remain, repeat with 2–3% in next batch

3. **Add water slowly** while stirring gently and continuously for 5–10 minutes
   - Stir creates small water droplets dispersed throughout oil
   - This maximizes phospholipid contact with water

4. **Allow to settle** at room temperature for 12–24 hours
   - Gum layer (containing phospholipids + water) separates and sinks to bottom
   - Clear oil layer rises to top
   - Interface between oil and gum is relatively sharp

5. **Separate the layers**:
   - Decant (carefully pour) the clear oil from the top into a clean container
   - Discard gum sludge at bottom (or compost)
   - Oil should now be visibly clearer (if complete, nearly transparent to light yellow)

**Yield**: ~98–99% of original oil weight retained (1–2% lost as gum + water)

**Repeat if necessary**: Visually inspect oil; if still hazy after settling, the oil has residual phospholipids. Repeat water degumming with 2–3% water.

:::tip
Water degumming is reversible—if you over-cool the oil and see a slight haze reappear, gently reheat to 50°C and allow to settle again. The haze will usually clear.
:::

### Acid Degumming (For Stubborn Phospholipids)

If water degumming does not fully clarify oil (typically in soybeans with very high phospholipid content), use **citric acid** or **phosphoric acid**:

**Procedure**:
1. Prepare **citric acid solution**: dissolve 1–2 grams citric acid (or 0.5 grams phosphoric acid) in 100 mL water
2. Heat crude oil to 60°C
3. Add acid solution slowly with stirring; mix for 10–15 minutes
4. Acid hydrolyzes remaining phospholipids, converting them to water-soluble forms
5. Heat to 70°C for 5 minutes (accelerates reaction)
6. Cool to room temperature and allow to settle overnight
7. Decant clear oil from top; discard acidified gum layer

**Yield**: ~98% of oil retained

<section id="neutralization">

## Neutralization and Deacidification

After degumming, remove free fatty acids (which cause rancidity and corrosion) using an alkaline wash.

### Lye Wash Method

**Principle**: Sodium hydroxide (lye/caustic soda) neutralizes free fatty acids, converting them to soap. Soap is water-soluble and separates from oil.

**Safety**: Lye is caustic. Wear gloves, eye protection, and handle carefully. Avoid inhalation of powder.

**Procedure**:

1. **Determine free fatty acid (FFA) content** (see "Acid Value Testing" section below)
   - Example: oil has 1.5% FFA
   - This means 1.5 grams of free fatty acid per 100 grams of oil

2. **Calculate NaOH requirement**:
   - Stoichiometric amount: 1 gram FFA requires ~0.28 grams NaOH
   - Excess safety factor: add 20% extra to ensure all FFA is neutralized
   - Formula: **NaOH (grams) = (oil weight in grams) × (FFA % / 100) × 0.28 × 1.2**
   - Example: 10,000 grams oil with 1.5% FFA:
     NaOH = 10,000 × 0.015 × 0.28 × 1.2 = **50 grams NaOH**

3. **Prepare lye solution**:
   - Dissolve NaOH in hot distilled water (use ~0.5 grams water per 1 gram NaOH; this creates strong but not saturated solution)
   - Example: 50 grams NaOH + 25 mL hot water
   - Solution gets hot due to exothermic dissolution; allow to cool to 50–60°C

4. **Heat degummed oil** to 55–60°C in a large container with drain valve at bottom

5. **Add lye solution slowly** while stirring vigorously, 1–2 minutes addition time
   - Vigorous mixing ensures good contact between oil and lye
   - Stir continuously for 10–15 minutes after all lye is added

6. **Allow to settle** at 55°C for 30–60 minutes
   - Soap layer (containing neutralized FFA + excess lye) settles to bottom
   - Oil floats above

7. **Drain soapstock** (lower layer) into a separate container; retain oil above

8. **Water wash** (remove residual soap and salts):
   - Heat oil to 50–60°C
   - Add hot distilled water (~5% of oil weight; for 10 kg oil, add 500 mL water)
   - Stir gently for 2–3 minutes to allow water-soluble soap to transfer into water
   - Allow to settle 1–2 hours
   - Drain water layer (now containing soap) from bottom
   - Repeat water wash 2–3 times until water drains clear (indicates all soap removed)

**Post-neutralization oil**:
- FFA is now <0.1%
- Oil is noticeably clearer (lighter color)
- Odor is milder
- Suitable for food use if bleaching step follows

**Soapstock yield**: ~1–2% of original oil weight (disposed or can be saponified into soap)

:::warning
Do NOT add too much NaOH. Excess lye causes over-saponification, converting oil to soap directly (loss of product). Use precise calculation; if unsure, calculate for slightly lower FFA content and test with indicator before proceeding.
:::

</section>

<section id="bleaching">

## Bleaching and Decolorization

Bleaching removes pigments (chlorophyll, carotenoids, oxidized compounds) to achieve pale, food-grade appearance. Simultaneously removes odor impurities.

### Activated Clay Bleaching

**Principle**: Activated clay or charcoal adsorbs colored molecules and off-odor compounds on its porous surface.

**Material**:
- Activated bentonite clay (food-grade)
- Activated charcoal (or activated carbon)
- Fuller's earth (bentonite variant)

**Procedure**:

1. **Heat neutralized oil** to 80–90°C (176–194°F) in an open pot with good ventilation

2. **Add activated clay**: 1–2% of oil weight
   - Example: 10 kg oil + 100–200 grams clay
   - Add slowly, stirring to avoid lumps
   - Clay particles should be fully suspended, not settled

3. **Stir occasionally** at elevated temperature for 15–30 minutes
   - As time progresses, oil color becomes progressively lighter
   - Odor is reduced
   - Stop when desired color achieved (usually after 20–25 minutes)

4. **Allow to settle and cool** to room temperature (2–4 hours)
   - Clay particles, now loaded with color, sink to bottom
   - Oil becomes clear and pale above

5. **Filter through cloth**:
   - Pour oil through a fine cotton cloth or cheesecloth multiple layers into a clean container
   - Discard clay residue (compost safe)
   - Yield: ~99% of oil retained

**Alternative: Activated Charcoal**:
- Same procedure, but use 0.5–1% charcoal (more efficient than clay)
- Can leave slight charcoal taste if over-treated; use judiciously for food oil

**Result**: Oil is now pale yellow, nearly odor-free, and suitable for human consumption.

<section id="deodorization">

## Deodorization (Optional for Food Oil)

Complete removal of volatiles (minor off-flavors) requires heating oil under partial vacuum with steam. This is optional but produces premium food oil.

### Simplified Deodorization (Double-Boiler Method)

**Principle**: Steam carries volatile flavor compounds away; vacuum lowers the boiling point of volatiles, allowing removal at lower temperature.

**Equipment**:
- Double boiler or jacketed tank
- Thermometer (0–200°C)
- Manual vacuum pump or aspirator (generates ~0.5 atm vacuum) — optional

**Procedure** (without vacuum):

1. **Heat oil to 140–160°C** (280–320°F) slowly with gentle stirring
   - Temperature must rise gradually to avoid overheating local spots
   - Use indirect heat (hot water jacket) rather than direct flame

2. **Maintain temperature for 30–60 minutes** while oil is exposed to air (allows volatile oxidation/evaporation)
   - Gentle air circulation (natural convection) carries off volatiles
   - Do NOT boil vigorously (causes oxidation and off-flavors)

3. **Occasionally stir and allow foam to settle** (foam carries off light volatiles)

4. **Cool slowly** to room temperature (prevents re-condensation of volatiles into oil)

5. **Result**: Oil is now neutral in odor and taste, suitable for premium food use

**Simplified deodorization result**:
- Removes ~70–80% of remaining volatiles
- Full vacuum deodorization would remove ~95%, but this method achieves good food-grade quality

**Caution**: Heating oil above 160°C without vacuum causes oxidative polymerization (oil becomes thick and develops off-flavors). Avoid excessive temperature.

</section>

<section id="winterization">

## Winterization (Wax Removal for Cold Climates)

In cold climates, wax crystals cloud oil and increase pour point (prevents flowing). Removal is optional for machine oil but important for food oil clarity and cold-climate lubrication.

### Cold Settling Method (Passive)

**Principle**: Wax crystallizes when oil is chilled to low temperature; crystals are denser than oil and settle/can be filtered out.

**Procedure**:

1. **Cool bleached/deodorized oil** to 4–10°C (refrigerator temperature) for 8–24 hours
   - Wax crystals form and settle as small particles at bottom
   - Oil above becomes clear

2. **Decant or filter**:
   - Carefully pour oil into a clean container, leaving wax crystals at bottom
   - Alternatively, chill in a tall container and filter through cloth, allowing crystals to be trapped in cloth

3. **Yield**: ~97–98% of oil retained (2–3% lost as wax)

4. **Result**: Oil remains clear even at cold temperature; pour point decreased by 10–15°C

**Winter-grade oil** (suitable for northern climates or unheated equipment):
- After winterization, oil remains pourable below 0°C
- Without winterization, oil thickens in winter (not suitable for fuel injection or fine bearing passages)

<section id="acid-testing">

## Acid Value Testing (Titration Method)

The acid value (AV) quantifies free fatty acid content. Knowing AV guides refinement decisions.

### Simple Titration Setup

**Materials**:
- Potassium hydroxide (KOH) or sodium hydroxide (NaOH) solution, ~0.1 M (molar) concentration
- Phenolphthalein indicator (or other acid-base indicator)
- Buret or calibrated dropper
- Test tube or small beaker
- Ethanol (denatured or pure)
- Distilled water

**Preparation**:
- If not pre-made, prepare 0.1 M KOH solution: dissolve 5.6 grams KOH in 1 liter distilled water (approximately)
- Standardize solution against a known acid (optional but more accurate)

### Testing Procedure

1. **Measure 5 grams of crude oil** into a test tube

2. **Add 50 mL ethanol** (oil solvent; makes titration easier)
   - Stir or shake to dissolve oil in ethanol
   - Mixture should be clear or slightly hazy

3. **Add 2–3 drops phenolphthalein indicator**
   - Solution is colorless initially

4. **Titrate with KOH solution**:
   - Add KOH dropwise from buret or dropper, stirring continuously
   - Solution turns from colorless to pale pink when endpoint reached (oil is neutralized)
   - Record volume of KOH used (e.g., 3.2 mL)

5. **Calculate acid value**:
   **Acid Value (mg KOH/g oil) = (mL KOH used × M KOH × 56.1) / (weight of oil in grams)**

   For 0.1 M KOH:
   **AV = mL KOH × 0.561 / oil weight**

   Example: 3.2 mL KOH for 5 grams oil:
   AV = 3.2 × 0.561 / 5 = **0.36 mg KOH/g oil**

### Interpretation

- **AV < 0.3**: Food-grade; minimal rancidity risk
- **AV 0.3–0.5**: Acceptable for food; beginning of rancidity
- **AV 0.5–1.0**: Machine-grade; not suitable for food
- **AV 1.0–2.0**: Low-grade lubricant; acceptable for machinery
- **AV > 2.0**: Poor quality; unsuitable for most uses; should be re-refined or discarded

**For example crude oil** (before refinement):
- Freshly pressed sunflower: AV ≈ 1–2
- Poorly stored crude: AV ≈ 3–5
- After water degumming: AV unchanged (phospholipids removed, not FFA)
- After lye neutralization: AV ≈ 0.1–0.2

:::tip
Track acid value over time for stored oil. If AV increases, oil is oxidizing and should be used soon or antioxidants added.
:::

</section>

<section id="peroxide-testing">

## Peroxide Value Testing (Oxidation Indicator)

Peroxide value measures oxidative rancidity. High peroxides indicate spoilage or pending rancidity.

### Simplified Iodometric Method

**Principle**: Peroxides oxidize iodide to iodine, which reacts with starch (turns blue). The depth of blue color or iodine released indicates peroxide level.

**Materials**:
- Iodine tincture (5% iodine in ethanol, available at pharmacy)
- Potassium iodide (KI) solution (~10% in water)
- Starch solution (1 gram starch + 100 mL water, heated and cooled)
- Sodium thiosulfate solution (~0.01 M, for titration—optional)
- Ethanol

**Simplified test** (qualitative, no titration):

1. **Measure 2 mL crude oil** into a test tube

2. **Add 5 mL ethanol** and mix

3. **Add 5 drops of KI solution** and mix gently

4. **Observe color change**:
   - No change (colorless): Low peroxide (PV < 5)
   - Pale yellow: Moderate peroxide (PV 5–15)
   - Orange or brown: High peroxide (PV > 15)
   - Add 5 drops starch to deepen blue color if iodine present

5. **For food-grade oil**: PV should be < 10 (preferably < 5)

**Quantitative method** (titration):
- After adding KI and observing color, titrate with sodium thiosulfate (Na₂S₂O₃) until blue color fades
- Peroxide value = (mL thiosulfate × M thiosulfate × 500) / (grams oil)
- More precise but requires titration equipment

### Interpretation

- **PV < 5**: Excellent; no oxidation
- **PV 5–10**: Good; acceptable food-grade
- **PV 10–20**: Marginal; rancidity beginning; should be used soon
- **PV > 20**: Poor; spoiled; unsuitable for food; marginal for lubricant use

<section id="oil-separation">

## Separating Food-Grade from Machine-Grade Oil

**Key insight**: The same crude oil batch produces both grades depending on refinement depth.

### Practical Separation Strategy

1. **Initial crude oil** (e.g., 20 liters):
   - AV ≈ 1.5, PV ≈ 8, hazy appearance

2. **Water degumming**: 20 liters (retains ~99%)
   - Result: ~19.8 liters; AV still ~1.5, PV ~8, but clear

3. **Light lye neutralization** (0.5–0.75× stoichiometric amount):
   - Uses less lye than full neutralization
   - Removes ~40–50% of FFA
   - Result: 19.6 liters; AV ≈ 0.7–0.8, PV still ~8
   - This portion is machine-grade oil; reserve separately

4. **Second lye neutralization** on the remaining 19.6 liters (full stoichiometric):
   - Removes remaining FFA
   - Result: 19.4 liters; AV ≈ 0.1–0.2, PV still ~8
   - This portion is becoming food-grade

5. **Light bleaching** (0.5% clay, short contact, <50°C):
   - Removes ~30–40% of color
   - Result: 19.2 liters; color is pale yellow, suitable for machine use
   - Reserve as premium machine-grade oil

6. **Intensive bleaching** (1–2% clay, 30 minutes at 80°C) on remaining oil:
   - Result: 19 liters; nearly colorless, suitable for food use
   - **Food-grade oil**: 19 liters total (95% of original crude)

**Yield summary** (from 20 liters crude):
- Machine-grade 1 (partial neutralization): 0.4 liters, AV 0.7–0.8
- Machine-grade 2 (light bleached): 0.2 liters, AV 0.1–0.2, pale yellow
- Food-grade (fully refined, bleached): 19 liters, AV <0.1, color nearly clear

This approach maximizes value: both food and machine oil are obtained from the same pressing.

<section id="oil-profiles">

## Specific Oil Properties and Best Uses

### Sunflower Oil

**Crude composition**:
- FFA: 0.5–2%
- Phospholipids: 0.3–0.5%
- Peroxide value: 5–10 on pressing
- Pour point (crude): ~−5°C

**After full refinement** (food-grade):
- AV: <0.1, PV: <5, color: pale gold
- Taste: mild, slightly nutty
- Smoke point: 450°F (232°C) — good for cooking
- Stability: moderate; oxidizes in ~6 months without antioxidants
- Best use: frying, salads, cooking oil

**Machine-grade**: Not commonly used as lubricant (lighter oils preferred); acceptable as light hydraulic oil after partial refinement.

### Rapeseed / Canola Oil

**Crude composition**:
- FFA: 0.8–2%
- Phospholipids: 0.4–0.8% (higher than sunflower)
- Peroxide: 6–12
- Pour point: −10°C (waxier than sunflower)

**After full refinement**:
- AV: <0.1, PV: <5, color: pale yellow
- Taste: mild, slightly bitter (removed by deodorization)
- Smoke point: 400°F (204°C)
- Winterization important if used in cold climates
- Best use: cooking oil, margarine base, salad oil

**Machine-grade**: Excellent machine/bearing oil after partial refinement; good viscosity and lubricity.

### Soybean Oil

**Crude composition**:
- FFA: 1.5–3% (highest among common seeds)
- Phospholipids: 1–2% (highest; requires multiple degummings)
- Peroxide: 8–15
- Pour point: −5 to 0°C

**After full refinement**:
- AV: <0.1, PV: <5
- Taste: bland (requires deodorization to remove "beanie" off-flavor)
- Smoke point: 450°F (232°C)
- Oxidation: moderate; prone to linoleic acid oxidation
- Best use: cooking oil, margarines, baking

**Machine-grade**: Moderate viscosity; acceptable general-purpose oil after partial refinement.

### Linseed Oil

**Crude composition**:
- FFA: 1–2%
- Phospholipids: 0.5–1%
- Peroxide: 10–20 (high; oxidizes easily)
- Pour point: −5°C

**After full refinement**:
- AV: <0.1, PV: <10 (hard to lower below 10; naturally high peroxide)
- Taste: bitter, unpleasant; NOT suitable for food even when refined
- Smoke point: 450°F (232°C)
- Drying properties: Linseed oxidizes and hardens (polymerizes) — ideal for paint/varnish
- Best use: wood finishing (oil paint base), waterproofing, NOT food

**Machine-grade**: Industrial oil, wood treatment, NOT bearing lubricant (dries/hardens in machinery).

:::warning
Do NOT consume linseed oil as food. Linseed's natural high peroxide and oxidative polymerization make it unsuitable and potentially toxic for human ingestion. Use for wood treatment and finishing only.
:::

## Oil Storage and Stabilization for Long-Term Preservation

Refined oil remains good for 6–12 months without additives; with proper storage and antioxidants, shelf life extends to 2–3 years or more.

### Environmental Factors Affecting Storage

**Oxygen**: Accelerates oxidation (rancidity). Minimize air exposure.

**Light**: UV radiation causes photo-oxidation. Store in dark containers.

**Temperature**: Higher temperature accelerates oxidation (roughly doubles reaction rate per 10°C increase). Store cool.

**Moisture**: Water promotes hydrolysis (breaking ester bonds) and microbial growth. Keep oil dry.

**Metals**: Iron and copper catalyze oxidation. Use glass, food-grade plastic, or ceramic storage; avoid bare metals.

### Storage Container Selection

**Best options**:
- Dark glass bottles (1–2 liter size for food oil; seals well, visible contents)
- Food-grade plastic bottles (HDPE, marked #2) for bulk storage (5–20 liters)
- Ceramic or stainless steel drums for large quantities (50+ liters)

**Avoid**:
- Clear glass (light exposure)
- Thin plastic (oxygen permeability)
- Reactive metals (iron, copper)
- Previously used containers (contamination risk)

### Antioxidant Additions

**Natural antioxidants** (boost shelf life without synthetic additives):

**Vitamin E (tocopherol)**:
- Extract from wheat germ or purchase pure tocopherol
- Add 0.01–0.05% by weight (10–50 mg per liter of oil)
- Effective and food-safe
- Cost: moderate

**Rosemary extract**:
- Dried rosemary leaves macerated in ethanol, then extract filtered and added to oil
- Add 0.02–0.1% by weight
- Slightly alters flavor (rosemary taste)
- Cost: low (rosemary grows wild)

**BHA (Butylated Hydroxyanisole)** or **BHT (Butylated Hydroxytoluene)**:
- Synthetic antioxidants, food-approved
- Add 0.02–0.05% by weight
- Very effective; minimal flavor impact
- Cost: moderate; availability may be limited

**Method of addition**:
1. Dissolve antioxidant powder in a small amount of warm oil
2. Add solution to main oil bulk while mixing
3. Stir thoroughly for 1–2 minutes
4. Store immediately

**Result**: Oil remains stable and edible for 2–3 years; peroxide value remains <10 if stored cool and dark.

### Storage Conditions

**Temperature**: 10–15°C (50–60°F) is ideal. Ambient room temperature (~20°C) is acceptable if no alternative. Do NOT refrigerate food oil (high viscosity and cloudiness from wax). Avoid temperatures above 25°C.

**Light**: Store in dark location (closet, basement, opaque cabinet). Avoid sunlight and bright artificial light.

**Oxygen**: Seal containers tightly. For large drums, use a bunghole cover and cap. For small bottles, ensure cap seals completely. Small air pockets in headspace are acceptable.

**Humidity**: Store in dry location. If ambient humidity is high, add a desiccant packet (silica gel) to storage container.

**Duration**: Inspect oil every 6 months for rancidity smell, cloudiness, or color change. If no off-odors or visible changes, oil is likely still good. Perform acid value test if quality questionable.

### Nitrogen Blanketing (Optional for Extended Storage)

For oils stored 2+ years, exclude oxygen by "blanketing" with inert nitrogen gas:

1. Fill container with oil, leaving 0.5 inch headspace
2. Purge headspace with nitrogen gas (from cylinder or simple generator) to displace air
3. Cap immediately, trapping nitrogen atmosphere
4. Oil is protected from oxidation for extended periods

This is advanced but useful for precious seed oil reserves.

</section>

<section id="quality-assessment">

## Lubricant Quality Assessment for Machine Oil

Machine oil intended for bearing, engine, or hydraulic use requires additional property verification beyond food-oil testing.

### Viscosity Measurement (Saybolt Viscosity Index)

Viscosity (thickness) affects how well oil flows and clings to moving parts.

**Simple saybolt method** (DIY, no precision equipment):
1. Heat oil to 40°C or 100°C (use hot water bath)
2. Time how long it takes 60 mL of oil to flow through a viscosity tube or small orifice
3. Compare to standard: <50 seconds = thin oil (good for tight bearings, cold weather), >500 seconds = thick oil (good for high-load gears)

**Saybolt Viscosity (SUS) = seconds for 60 mL to flow**

**Classification** (ISO and SAE grades):
- ISO VG 32: thin motor oil, <50 seconds at 40°C
- ISO VG 46: general machinery, 46–56 seconds at 40°C
- ISO VG 68: thicker industrial oil, 68–84 seconds at 40°C

**Refined seed oils typically fall into ISO VG 32–46** (medium viscosity), suitable for general-purpose machinery.

### Demulsibility (Water Separation)

Machine oil must release water (from humidity or wash water) to avoid rust and sludge formation.

**Simple test**:
1. Pour 100 mL oil into a 250 mL graduated cylinder
2. Add 30 mL distilled water
3. Shake vigorously for 1 minute; set down
4. Observe how quickly the oil/water mixture separates
5. After 5 minutes, measure volume of water at bottom (should be >28 mL for good demulsibility)

**Good demulsibility**: Water settles to bottom within 5–10 minutes, leaving clear oil above. Vegetable oils are generally good at demulsibility (better than mineral oil).

### Corrosion Prevention (Copper Strip Test)

Machine oil should not corrode ferrous metals or copper.

**Simple test** (qualitative):
1. Clean a copper strip with fine sandpaper
2. Immerse copper strip in 50 mL oil
3. Heat to 100°C for 30 minutes in a water bath
4. Remove copper strip and inspect: no darkening (good), slight oxidation (acceptable), significant black/green corrosion (oil is unsuitable)

**Refined vegetable oils** typically show slight oxidation (acceptable). Vegetable oils inherently contain natural compounds that inhibit corrosion (better than mineral oil in this regard).

### Pour Point (Cold-Flow Property)

The lowest temperature at which oil flows like a liquid (not a sludge or solid).

**Simple test**:
1. Place 50 mL oil in a test tube
2. Chill in freezer (−10°C, then −15°C, then −20°C, etc.)
3. Remove and tilt; if oil flows (does not adhere to tube), temperature is below pour point
4. Continue chilling in 5°C increments until oil solidifies
5. Pour point is the temperature just above where it solidifies

**Typical pour points** (without winterization):
- Sunflower: −5 to 0°C
- Rapeseed: −10 to −5°C (naturally lower due to wax content)
- Soybean: −5 to 0°C

**After winterization** (wax removal): pour point drops by 10–15°C, making oil suitable for cold climates.

| Property | Test Method | Acceptable Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Acid Value | Titration | <1.0 mg KOH/g | Corrosion risk if >1.5 |
| Peroxide | Iodometric | <30 | Higher tolerance than food |
| Color | Visual | Yellow to pale brown | Dark = oxidation |
| Viscosity | Flow time | ISO VG 32–68 | Depends on application |
| Pour point | Freezing test | >−5°C (or −15°C if winterized) | Colder climates need lower |
| Demulsibility | Water separation | >90% water separation in 5 min | Rust prevention |
| Copper corrosion | Strip test | Max light oxidation | Should not darken significantly |

</section>

## Quality Record-Keeping and Documentation

Maintain records of all refinement batches for traceability and continuous improvement.

### Batch Log Template

**Log entry** (for each pressing/refinement cycle):
- **Batch ID**: Date + sequential number (e.g., 2026-02-22-01)
- **Source**: Oil type (sunflower, rapeseed, etc.), pressing date, volume pressed
- **Crude properties**: AV, PV, color (visual), appearance (clear/hazy)
- **Refinement steps**:
  - Degumming: water volume, settling time, yield
  - Neutralization: NaOH used, settling time, yield
  - Bleaching: clay/charcoal type, amount, temperature, contact time, yield
  - Deodorization: max temperature, time, yield
  - Winterization (if done): chill temperature, settling time, yield
- **Final properties**: AV, PV, color, viscosity (if tested), yield (total %), date
- **Grading**: Food-grade or machine-grade
- **Storage**: Container type, location, antioxidant added (if any), amount added
- **Notes**: Any issues, unusual observations, deviations from procedure

### Example Log Entry

```
Batch ID: 2026-02-22-01
Source: Sunflower, pressed 2026-02-20, 20 liters

Crude: AV 1.8, PV 9, pale yellow, slightly hazy

Degumming: 200 mL water at 65°C, settled 18 hours → 19.8 L, clear

Neutralization: 56 grams NaOH (0.1 M × 1.25 stoich), 55°C, 15 min mix, settled 45 min → 19.6 L
Water wash: 3× with 500 mL water each, final wash clear

Bleaching: 196 grams clay, 85°C, 25 min → 19.2 L, pale yellow, filtered

Final: AV 0.08, PV 4, color pale gold, viscosity ~ISO VG 40 (timed flow)
Yield: 96%
Grade: Food-grade, excellent

Storage: Dark glass bottles (10× 2L), +100 mg/L vitamin E, cool cabinet at 15°C

Notes: Excellent batch. Viscosity good for cooking. No off-odors.
```

### Trend Analysis (Monthly or Quarterly)

Review multiple batches over time:
- **AV trend**: Increasing AV indicates deteriorating storage conditions or oxidation during pressing (fix storage or pressing method)
- **PV trend**: Increasing PV suggests light exposure or elevated storage temperature
- **Yield trend**: Declining yield may indicate less careful procedure or poorer-quality crude

Use trends to optimize process and storage conditions.

Seed oil refinement transforms crude pressed oil into separate food-grade and machine-grade products. Even with simple equipment and hand tools, passive degumming, careful neutralization, and proper storage can extend shelf life from weeks to years and increase the usability and value of the pressing batch. Careful testing and record-keeping ensure consistency and quality, especially important in low-connectivity scenarios where replacement supplies may be difficult to obtain.
