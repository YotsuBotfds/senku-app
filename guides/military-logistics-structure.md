## Overview

Military organizations are optimized for coordinated combat operations. Success requires clear hierarchy (who reports to whom), standardized procedures (so soldiers know their role without constant orders), and robust logistics (so fighters have ammunition, food, shelter). A 1,000-soldier army that cannot feed itself or move ammunition collapses within days; a 100-soldier army with excellent logistics can operate for weeks.

This guide covers organizational structure (squad through battalion), command systems, supply accounting, medical evacuation, and field operations. Understanding these principles applies to civilian emergency management, large work crews, and organized communities.

</section>

<section id="unit-structure">

## Unit Organization: Squad, Platoon, Company, Battalion

Military units are nested hierarchies. Larger units are composed of smaller units, each with clear responsibilities.

### Squad (8–12 soldiers)

Smallest operational unit; typically commanded by a sergeant (NCO, non-commissioned officer).

**Typical organization:**

```
Sergeant (squad leader)
  ├─ Deputy (senior enlisted, assists leadership)
  ├─ Rifleman (2–3)
  ├─ Light machine gunner (1–2)
  ├─ Grenadier/specialist (1)
  └─ Medic (1)
```

**Tasks:**

- Clear and hold a specific objective (building, road section, fortified position).
- Assault objectives with coordinated suppressive fire.
- Patrol and reconnaissance.

**Autonomy:** Squad can operate independently for 1–2 days if supplied (ammunition, food, water).

### Platoon (24–40 soldiers)

Composed of 3–4 squads; commanded by a lieutenant (officer) or senior sergeant.

**Typical organization:**

```
Platoon Leader (officer or senior NCO)
  ├─ Sergeant (second-in-command)
  ├─ Squad 1 (sergeant + 10 soldiers)
  ├─ Squad 2 (sergeant + 10 soldiers)
  ├─ Squad 3 (sergeant + 10 soldiers)
  └─ Support (machine gunner, medic, radio operator)
```

**Tasks:**

- Clear and defend a larger area (village section, defensive line).
- Conduct company-level operations under orders.
- Operate independently for 2–3 days.

**Command:** Platoon leader makes tactical decisions (route, timing, when to advance or withdraw); squad leaders execute within platoon intent.

### Company (80–150 soldiers)

Composed of 3–4 platoons; commanded by a captain (officer) with first sergeant (senior NCO).

**Typical organization:**

```
Company Commander (captain)
  ├─ First Sergeant (senior NCO, oversees discipline, supply, training)
  ├─ Platoon 1 (lieutenant + 30 soldiers)
  ├─ Platoon 2 (lieutenant + 30 soldiers)
  ├─ Platoon 3 (lieutenant + 30 soldiers)
  └─ Headquarters (radio operator, runner, supply sergeant, medic, cook)
```

**Tasks:**

- Occupy and defend a town, fortress, or extensive position.
- Launch company-sized assault operations.
- Operate independently for 3–5 days if resupplied daily.

**Self-sufficiency:** Company is the smallest unit with organic supply (supply sergeant manages ammunition, rations, water for all 150 soldiers).

### Battalion (400–800 soldiers)

Composed of 3–4 companies plus support. Commanded by a major (officer); operates as combined-arms unit (riflemen, machine gun section, mortar section, engineers).

**Typical organization:**

```
Battalion Commander (major)
  ├─ Executive Officer (senior officer, deputy)
  ├─ Battalion Sergeant Major (senior NCO, administration)
  ├─ Company A (captain + 100 soldiers)
  ├─ Company B (captain + 100 soldiers)
  ├─ Company C (captain + 100 soldiers)
  ├─ Support Company: Machine guns, mortars, engineers (75 soldiers)
  └─ Battalion Headquarters: Medical, supply, communications, intelligence (25 soldiers)
```

**Tasks:**

- Execute major operations (assault fortified positions, defend territory).
- Coordinate with other battalions (brigade level).
- Operate independently for 5+ days if supplied.

**Combat power:** Battalion-sized force can assault a lightly defended position, capture a town, or hold against battalion-sized opposing force for extended period.

**Table: Unit summary**

| **Unit** | **Size** | **Commander** | **Composition** | **Autonomy** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Squad | 8–12 | Sergeant | 2–3 firegroups | Hours–1 day |
| Platoon | 24–40 | Lieutenant/Sergeant | 3–4 squads | 1–2 days |
| Company | 80–150 | Captain | 3–4 platoons + HQ | 3–5 days |
| Battalion | 400–800 | Major | 3–4 companies + support | 5–10 days |

## Command Hierarchy and Decision Authority

Each rank has defined authority (can make which decisions) and responsibility (held accountable for outcomes).

**Officer ranks** (by seniority):

| **Rank** | **Insignia** | **Typical Role** | **Authority** |
|---|---|---|---|
| Lieutenant | Single bar or star | Platoon leader | Tactical decisions within company framework |
| Captain | Two bars or star + bar | Company commander | Operational decisions; leads 80–150 soldiers |
| Major | Oak leaf or equivalent | Battalion commander | Strategic decisions for 400–800 soldiers |
| Colonel | Eagle or equivalent | Regiment/battalion group | Commands multiple battalions |
| General | Stars | Corps/army level | Strategic command of 5,000+ soldiers |

**Non-commissioned officer (NCO) ranks** (by seniority):

| **Rank** | **Role** | **Authority** |
|---|---|---|
| Corporal | Team leader | Leads 2–4 soldiers; executes orders; reports to sergeant |
| Sergeant | Squad leader | Leads 8–12 soldiers; makes tactical adjustments; ensures orders understood |
| Staff Sergeant | Senior squad leader or platoon sergeant | Assists lieutenant; oversees 3–4 squads; discipline and morale |
| First Sergeant | Company senior NCO | Administers company; manages supply, discipline; second-in-command to captain |

**Command authority principle:** Every soldier has one direct superior (chain of command). Orders flow downward; information/requests flow upward. This prevents confusion and ensures accountability.

### Communication Protocol

Orders given in standard format to ensure clarity:

```
"Listen up. This is [Sender rank/name].

Situation: [Current enemy/environmental status]
Task: [Specific objective]
Execution: [How to accomplish task; timing; formation]
Logistics: [Supply, medical, evacuation routes]
Command & Control: [Radio frequency, signals, chain of command during operation]

Questions?"
```

**Running an operation:**

1. **Receive order** from superior (battalion commander to company commander).
2. **Issue order** to platoons (company commander to lieutenants, in standard format above).
3. **Execute** (lieutenants lead platoons in attacking objective).
4. **Report** (lieutenants report success/casualty count to company commander every 30 minutes during operation).
5. **Consolidate** (platoons regroup, account for all soldiers, prepare for next task).

## Supply Lines and Ammunition Accounting

An army marches on its stomach. Without reliable supply, even a well-trained force collapses.

### Supply Categories

**Class I (Food and water):**

- Ration packs: 2,000–2,500 calories per soldier per day.
- Fresh food: Vegetables, bread (resupply daily if available).
- Water: 1 gallon (4 liters) per soldier per day (drinking, cooking); 2–3 gallons in hot climates or heavy combat.

**Class II (Ammunition):**

- Rifle cartridges: 100–300 per soldier (100 for holding defensive position; 300 for assault operations).
- Machine gun ammunition: 1,000–3,000 rounds per gun.
- Grenades: 4–6 per grenadier.
- Mortar rounds: 50–200 per platoon.

**Class III (Fuel):**

- Vehicle fuel: Consumed based on distance and terrain.
- Cooking fuel (if generators, camp stoves): 10–20 gallons per day for 100-soldier company.

**Class IV (Construction):**

- Timber, sandbags, tools for fortifications.

**Class V (Medical):**

- Bandages, splints, medication.
- Field hospital supplies (stretchers, splints for 10% of force).

### Resupply Schedule

**Daily resupply (holding position):**

```
Company (100 soldiers) daily needs:
Food: 100 × 2,500 cal = 250,000 calories ≈ 100 lbs of ration packs
Water: 100 × 1 gallon = 100 gallons (400 lbs)
Ammunition: 100 × 150 rounds = 15,000 rounds (400 lbs, assuming 0.3 lbs per cartridge)

Total: ~900 lbs per day delivered by supply wagons or carriers.
```

**Ammunition accountability:**

Each squad maintains an ammunition count (how many rounds per soldier, per weapon). At end of each day:

```
Squad of 10 soldiers
Distributed ammunition: 2,000 total rounds
Expended in day: 300 rounds
Remaining: 1,700 rounds
Resupply needed: 300 rounds to restore to 2,000

Record: Squad sergeant reports "300 rounds expended, 1,700 on hand, requesting 300 rounds resupply" to platoon sergeant daily.
```

**Supply discipline prevents:**

- Soldiers expending ammunition wastefully (knowing it must be accounted for).
- Surprise shortages (platoon leader knows ammunition status in advance).
- Hoarding (individual soldiers secreting ammunition; known totals prevent it).

### Supply Convoy Organization

A supply convoy (wagons, pack animals, or vehicles) carries multiple tons of supplies. Escort is essential (vulnerable to ambush).

**Typical convoy (50-soldier company resupply, 3-day delay):**

```
Total supplies needed: 900 lbs/day × 3 days = 2,700 lbs

Convoy composition:
- Supply wagon or 4–6 pack animals (2,000 lbs capacity)
- 2–3 soldiers (drivers/handlers)
- 10–15 escort soldiers (protection against ambush)
- 1 sergeant (convoy leader; coordinates with receiving company)

Departure time: Dawn (arrives at company by midday)
Return time: Afternoon (returns before dark)

Frequency: Daily if resupply point within 1 day march; every 3 days if farther.
```

## Field Kitchens and Feeding Operations

A field kitchen serves 100–300 soldiers. Quality food improves morale and combat effectiveness (hungry soldiers fight poorly).

**Kitchen equipment:**

| **Item** | **Quantity** | **Purpose** |
|---|---|---|
| Large pot (10–20 gallon) | 2–3 | Boiling water, cooking stews |
| Griddle/pan | 2–4 | Frying bread, meat |
| Utensils (spoon, ladle, knife) | 4–6 | Food prep |
| Fuel (wood or camp stove) | 50 lbs/day | Heating water and food |
| Tables | 2 | Food service and prep |
| Buckets (water) | 4–5 | Water hauling |

**Daily meal preparation (100 soldiers):**

**Breakfast (0600 hours):**

```
Per soldier: Hard bread (8 oz) + butter (2 oz) + hot tea or coffee (8 oz) = ~500 calories
Preparation: 3 hours (starting 0300)
Fuel needed: 20 gallons water × 8 Btu to boil + heat loss = 400 Btu (1/4 gallon equivalent fuel)
Labor: 3 cooks + 2 helpers = 5 soldiers
```

**Midday (1200 hours):**

```
Per soldier: Dried meat or salted fish (4 oz) + beans (6 oz) + bread (4 oz) = ~600 calories
Preparation: 4 hours (stew must simmer; beans soak overnight)
Fuel needed: 50 gallons water, extended cooking
Labor: 3 cooks + 2 helpers
```

**Evening (1800 hours):**

```
Per soldier: Soup (meat/vegetable, 12 oz) + bread (6 oz) + water = ~700 calories
Preparation: 3 hours
Fuel needed: 40 gallons water
Labor: 3 cooks + 2 helpers
```

**Daily total:**

```
Calories provided: 500 + 600 + 700 = 1,800 calories (less than 2,500 ideal; troops supplement with personal ration packs)
Fuel: ~2 gallons per day (20 gallons water × 0.1 gallon fuel equivalent)
Labor: 15 soldier-days per 100 soldiers = 15% of force in supply/kitchen duties
Cost: ~$1–2 per soldier per day (assuming local ingredients)
```

**Kitchen location:** Rear of camp, downwind of troops (smoke and smell don't bother fighters). Near water source (stream for drinking, cooking, washing). 200+ meters from fighting positions (if supply point is overrun, kitchen can be relocated quickly).

## Medical Evacuation and Treatment Chain

Combat produces casualties. How quickly wounded reach medical care determines survival rates.

**Treatment levels:**

1. **Point-of-care (squad):** Self-aid, buddy aid. Every soldier carries tourniquet, hemostatic gauze, elastic bandage.
2. **Squad medic:** Trained to stop bleeding, stabilize fractures, assess wounds. Carries medications (antibiotics if available).
3. **Company aid station:** Sergeant-level first aid; IV fluids, oxygen if available; sorting (who can return to duty vs. needs evacuation).
4. **Battalion aid station:** 2–3 experienced medics; basic surgery (clearing airways, closing severe wounds). Can keep 10–20 casualties for 24 hours.
5. **Field hospital:** Surgeon-led; may do amputations, blood transfusions if available. 50–100 bed capacity; coordinates with rear-area hospital.

**Evacuation chain:**

```
Wounded soldier → Squad medic (1–5 minutes)
                → Company aid station (15–30 minutes)
                → Battalion aid station (1–2 hours)
                → Field hospital (4–8 hours)
                → Rear-area hospital (12–24 hours)
```

**Casualty ratio planning:**

In heavy combat, expect 10–20% of force become casualties (killed, wounded, missing) per day.

```
100-soldier company in assault: 10–20 casualties
Required medics (1 per 10 soldiers): 10 medics for full company
Actual (limited by training): 1 medic per squad ≈ 3 medics for 100 soldiers
Gap: Medics are overwhelmed; some wounds receive delayed treatment.
```

**Sorting criteria (at aid station):**

- **Immediate:** Severe hemorrhage, airway obstruction, shock. Treat now or evacuate urgently.
- **Delayed:** Moderate wounds (fractures, burns, moderate bleeding). Treat after immediates.
- **Minor:** Walking wounded; treat at company level or return to duty.
- **Expectant:** Unsurvivable injuries (catastrophic trauma, massive burns); comfort care only.

:::warning
Overwhelmed medical systems must prioritize. In field hospitals with 20 casualties and 5 doctors, 15 patients wait hours. Avoid assuming your unit will receive immediate treatment; prevention (body armor, helmets, fortifications) is more effective than treatment.
:::

## Communication Structure and Signals

Orders, intelligence, and casualty reports must flow rapidly. Communication failure causes friendly fire, missed objectives, and disaster.

**Messenger system:**

Primary communication for tactical forces (runners, mounted couriers). Fast, unreliable.

```
Time to deliver: 30 minutes (squad to company, nearby)
Typical message: "Company A is moving to defend ridge; standby for additional orders"
Risk: Messenger captured/killed; message lost
Mitigation: Send 2 messengers independently (one usually gets through)
```

**Radio system:**

Fast, reliable if equipment available. Range: 1–10 km depending on power and antenna.

```
Format: "Alpha One, this is Charlie Six. Sitrep: Taking fire from north ridge. Request artillery. Out."

Chain: Squad leader (Alpha One) → Platoon sergeant → Company commander (Charlie Six) → Battalion operations officer

Speed: <1 minute from squad to battalion commander
```

**Visual signals:**

Flag signals, hand signals, smoke/flares. Useful for silent communication or when radio unavailable.

```
Raised arm (fist): Stop
Raised arm (waving): Move forward
Prone: Take cover
Two fingers pointed: Direction of enemy
Smoke grenade: Marker (assembly point, casualty position, etc.)
```

**Signal discipline:** Minimize radio traffic (enemy can triangulate transmitter position). Use brief, coded messages when possible.

```
"Bravo Two, this is Bravo One. All clear." (brief)
vs.
"All friendlies in second squad, we have checked our area and found no hostile forces." (too verbose; reveals unit size and position)
```

## Camp Layout and Defense

When stationary, troops must organize camp defensively (reduce attack vulnerability) and functionally (effective operations).

**Typical company camp (100 soldiers):**

```
                North (toward enemy)

              [Entrance guard post]

    [Machine gun emplacement left]    [Machine gun right]

              [Vehicle park]

    [Ammunition   [Field      [Water
     depot]      kitchen]     source]

    [Tents 1–4]  [Latrine]  [Tents 5–8]

    [Company
     HQ tent]

     [Supply]    [Medical]  [Storage]

                  South (rear)
```

**Defense layout:**

- **Perimeter guard:** 15–20% of force on watch (rotating 4-hour shifts so guards are alert).
- **Machine gun positions:** Covering approaches; interlocking fields of fire.
- **Obstacles:** Wire, stakes, or ditches; slows attackers, funnels them into killzone.
- **Alternate positions:** If main position overrun, soldiers retreat to secondary fortification.

**Space requirements:**

```
100-soldier company
Tents (2 soldiers per tent): 50 tents × 40 sq feet = 2,000 sq feet
Vehicles/animals: 1,000 sq feet
Kitchen/supply: 500 sq feet
Entrances, roads: 1,000 sq feet
Total: ~4,500 sq feet ≈ 0.1 acres

Defensible shape: Square or rectangle (easier to defend perimeter than scattered layout)
```

**Sanitation:**

- **Latrine:** 30+ meters from water source; downhill/downwind.
- **Garbage pit:** 50+ meters from camp.
- **Animal waste:** Collected and buried (disease vector).

Poor sanitation causes dysentery, typhus, cholera—diseases kill more soldiers than combat in many historical conflicts.

## March Order and Convoy Organization

Moving an army requires careful coordination. Disorder (scattered soldiers, slow pace, ambush vulnerability) can cause disaster.

**March formation (company of 100 soldiers):**

```
[Scout squad ahead, 500m] ← Eyes and ears; detects ambush early

[Advance guard: 1 squad] ← Fights initial resistance; buys time

[Main body: 3 squads in column] ← Bulk of force; organized for rapid deployment

[Rear guard: 1 squad] ← Protects flank; catches stragglers

[Supply wagons] ← Ammunition, food, ammunition; moved between advance guard and main body for protection
```

**March discipline:**

- **Interval:** 2 meters between soldiers (allows rapid deployment if ambushed; prevents single casualty from killing multiple soldiers).
- **Pace:** 3 mph sustained; 1 mile per 20 minutes (allows rest after 1 hour march = 3 miles).
- **Rest:** 10 minutes per hour (water, readjustment of gear).
- **Duration:** 6–8 hours per day (leaves afternoon/evening for camp setup, maintenance, recovery).

**Daily march:**

```
0600 Breakfast
0700 Begin march
0800 10-minute rest
0900 Continue march
1000 10-minute rest
1100 Continue march
1200 Lunch (1-hour halt)
1300 Begin march
1400 10-minute rest
1500 Continue march (total: ~15 miles covered by 1600 hours)
1600 Arrive camp, setup (1–2 hours)
1800 Evening meal
2000 Rest (night watch rotation)
```

**Ambush response (if enemy fires during march):**

```
1. Take cover immediately (nearest terrain: ditch, building, trees).
2. Return fire (suppress enemy, prevent follow-on attacks).
3. Squad leader assesses: Can we win? Should we retreat?
4. If defensible: Hold position, call for reinforcement (rear guard moves forward).
5. If untenable: Retreat to better ground, prepare defense.
6. Report: "Ambushed on road at [location]. 2 casualties. Engaging enemy. Over."
```

## Example: Company-Sized Operation

**Scenario:** Company of 100 soldiers must assault and hold a fortified village.

**Plan (approved by battalion commander):**

```
Date: Tomorrow, 0600 hours
Objective: Village of Highfield (5 km north)
Task: Assault village, capture commander's residence, establish defensive perimeter

Personnel:
- Company A: 100 soldiers (4 platoons)
- Company B: 50 soldiers (support/reserve)
- Artillery: 2 mortars, 6 rounds per position

Logistics:
- Supply point: 2 km south of village
- 2-day supply at assembly point (ammunition, rations, water)
- 1 field medic with battalion aid station at supply point
- Communication: Radio frequency 47.5 MHz, callsign "Delta Six" (company commander)

Timeline:
- 0400: Wake, breakfast, final checks
- 0500: March begins (Company A, 5 km at 3 mph = 1 hour 40 min)
- 0640: Arrive assembly point (2 km south of objective); reconnoiter with scouts
- 0700: Issue assault order to platoons
- 0730: Mortar prep fire (10 minutes; softens enemy, triggers ambush positions)
- 0740: Assault begins (Company A advances from west; Company B flanks from east)
- 0800: Secure village edge; consolidate positions
- 0830: Platoon-by-platoon assault (house-by-house, room-by-room clearing)
- 0900: Objective secured; establish defensive perimeter
- 0930: Consolidation (account for casualties, redistribute ammunition, prepare for counterattack)
- 1200: Resupply wagons arrive; meals and ammunition distributed
- Ongoing: Garrison duty (hold village; resist counterattack; await orders from battalion)
```

**Execution notes:**

- **Casualty expectation:** 5–10 soldiers (5–10%) killed or wounded in assault.
- **Ammunition expenditure:** 10,000–15,000 rounds (150–200 per soldier vs. normal 150 = 33% more than expected; resupply needed immediately).
- **Duration:** 4–6 hours assault; 1–3 days garrison (until relieved).
