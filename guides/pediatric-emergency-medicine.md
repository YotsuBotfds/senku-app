## Overview

Children are not small adults. Pediatric emergency medicine requires understanding of developmental differences in anatomy, physiology, and medication metabolism. This guide provides evidence-based protocols for managing life-threatening emergencies in infants and children (birth to 18 years) in resource-limited settings.

**Critical disclaimer:** This guide is educational and intended for austere environments where pediatric emergency care is unavailable. In any acute emergency, call professional emergency services immediately. These protocols assume basic drugs and equipment (oxygen, IV fluids, epinephrine, anticonvulsants) but not intensive care unit capabilities.

The principles outlined follow Pediatric Advanced Life Support (PALS) guidelines, adapted for field and wilderness medicine.

</section>

## Weight Estimation and Dosing

Accurate weight is essential for all pediatric drug dosing. When actual weight is unknown, use age-based estimation.

### Broselow Tape Method

The Broselow tape is a color-coded length-based system that provides weight estimates, drug doses, and equipment sizes simultaneously. If unavailable, use age-based formulas.

### Age-Based Weight Estimation

**Infants (birth–12 months):**
- 0–3 months: 3–6 kg
- 3–6 months: 6–8 kg
- 6–12 months: 8–10 kg

**Toddlers (1–3 years):** Weight = (age in years + 9) / 2 kg
- 1 year: 10 kg
- 2 years: 12 kg
- 3 years: 14 kg

**Preschool (4–6 years):** Weight = (age in years + 7) × 2 kg
- 4 years: 16 kg
- 5 years: 18 kg
- 6 years: 20 kg

**School age (7–12 years):** Weight = age in years × 3 kg (±10%)
- 8 years: 24 kg
- 10 years: 32 kg
- 12 years: 36 kg

**Adolescent (13–18 years):** Adult-range weight; use adult dosing for most drugs.

:::tip
When uncertain, overestimate weight slightly rather than underestimate. Underdosing may result in treatment failure in life-threatening situations.
:::

### Critical Drug Doses (per kg)

| Drug | Dose | Max Single Dose | Indication |
|------|------|-----------------|-----------|
| Epinephrine IV (cardiac arrest) | 0.01 mg/kg | 1 mg | Cardiac arrest, anaphylaxis |
| Epinephrine IM (anaphylaxis) | 0.01 mg/kg | 0.5 mg | Anaphylaxis, bee sting, food allergy |
| Amiodarone IV | 5 mg/kg | 300 mg | Ventricular fibrillation, pulseless VT |
| Atropine IV | 0.02 mg/kg | 0.5 mg (child), 1 mg (adolescent) | Bradycardia, anticholinergic poisoning |
| Benzodiazepine (lorazepam) IV | 0.05–0.1 mg/kg | 4 mg | Seizure |
| Diazepam rectal | 0.5 mg/kg | 20 mg | Febrile/prolonged seizure, no IV access |
| Ibuprofen (fever/pain) | 10 mg/kg | 400 mg | Fever, pain |
| Acetaminophen (fever/pain) | 15 mg/kg | 1000 mg | Fever, pain |
| Hydrocortisone IV | 50 mg/kg | 500 mg | Septic shock, anaphylaxis |
| Dextrose (hypoglycemia) | 0.25–0.5 g/kg IV | 12.5 g | Symptomatic hypoglycemia |

For detailed dosing information on all emergency medications, refer to the <a href="../medications.html">Medications guide</a>.

<section id="airway-anatomy">

## Pediatric Airway Anatomy

Children have significant anatomic differences from adults. These differences affect airway management and risk of obstruction.

### Key Anatomic Differences

**Proportionally larger head:** Infants have a relatively large occiput (back of skull). When supine, the head flexes the neck; use a shoulder roll under the shoulders to extend the neck and align the airway.

**Larger, more anterior (forward) epiglottis:** The epiglottis is U-shaped and high in the pharynx. It is easily obscured during intubation attempts.

**Narrowest point of the airway:** In adults, the narrowest point is the vocal cords (glottis). In children <8 years, it is the subglottic region (just below the vocal cords). This is why cuffless endotracheal tubes are traditionally used in young children.

**Larger tongue and tonsils:** These structures are relatively large and easily obstruct the airway, especially when the child is unconscious or sedated.

**Shorter neck:** There is less distance between the mandible (lower jaw) and the sternal notch, limiting the space to work.

### Jaw Thrust vs Head Tilt–Chin Lift

**Jaw thrust:** Preferred in suspected cervical spine injury. Position behind the child's head; place fingers on the angle of the mandible and lift forward. Do not tilt the head backward.

**Head tilt–chin lift:** For non-trauma patients. Place one hand on the forehead, tilt the head back slightly; place fingers under the bony part of the chin and lift.

### Opening the Airway—Size Estimates

- **Infant (0–6 months):** Size 0 or 1 oropharyngeal airway; 2.0–2.5 mm endotracheal tube
- **Infant (6–12 months):** Size 1 or 2 oropharyngeal airway; 3.0 mm endotracheal tube
- **Toddler (1–3 years):** Size 2 oropharyngeal airway; 3.5–4.0 mm endotracheal tube
- **Preschool (4–6 years):** Size 3 oropharyngeal airway; 4.5–5.0 mm endotracheal tube
- **School age (7–12 years):** Size 3 or 4 oropharyngeal airway; 5.5–6.0 mm endotracheal tube

**Formula:** Endotracheal tube size (mm) = (age in years / 4) + 4

## Febrile Seizure Management

Febrile seizures are the most common type of seizure in children (ages 6 months to 5 years). They occur with fever >38.5°C (101.3°F) and, while frightening, usually resolve spontaneously.

### Febrile Seizure Classification

**Simple febrile seizure:** Single seizure episode lasting <15 minutes, generalized tonic-clonic, no focal features. Occurs in ~4% of febrile children. Excellent prognosis; recurrence risk ~30%.

**Complex febrile seizure:** Multiple seizures in 24 hours, focal features (movements on one side), or duration >15 minutes. Higher recurrence risk; slightly elevated risk of later epilepsy.

### Acute Management

**During the seizure:**
1. **Protect the airway:** Turn child's head to side to prevent aspiration; do not force anything into the mouth.
2. **Time the seizure:** Note the exact time of onset.
3. **Give lorazepam IV** at 0.05–0.1 mg/kg (max 4 mg) if seizure lasts >5 minutes.
4. **If no IV access,** use rectal diazepam at 0.5 mg/kg (max 20 mg) or midazolam IM.

**After the seizure stops:**
1. **Position for recovery:** Place child on side in recovery position.
2. **Check oxygen saturation:** Give oxygen if SpO2 <94%.
3. **Reassess airway and breathing.**
4. **Get history** from parents: fever onset, illness exposure, previous seizures.

:::warning
Not all febrile children with seizures require admission. Most simple febrile seizures resolve and do not require EEG or imaging unless there are atypical features (focal seizure, age <6 months, or recurrence within 24 hours).
:::

### Fever Management

Fever itself does not prevent recurrence; anticonvulsant prophylaxis is more effective than aggressive fever control. However, manage comfort:

- **Ibuprofen:** 10 mg/kg every 6–8 hours (max 400 mg)
- **Acetaminophen:** 15 mg/kg every 4–6 hours (max 1000 mg)
- **Tepid sponging:** Gentle bathing with room-temperature water (see Hydrotherapy guide)

### Prophylaxis

**Continuous prophylaxis (phenobarbital, valproic acid) is rarely used** because febrile seizures have excellent prognosis and medication side effects may outweigh benefits.

**Intermittent prophylaxis (diazepam given at fever onset)** is reasonable for high-risk children or parental anxiety. Dose: 0.3 mg/kg per dose at sign of fever, repeat every 8 hours (max 5 mg per dose).

<section id="dehydration-assessment">

## Dehydration Assessment and Categorization

Dehydration in children can rapidly progress to hypovolemic shock. Early assessment and classification guide fluid replacement.

### Clinical Assessment of Dehydration

| Finding | Mild (5%) | Moderate (10%) | Severe (≥15%) |
|---------|-----------|----------------|---------------|
| Behavior | Normal or slightly decreased | Decreased, irritable | Lethargic, altered mental status |
| Thirst | Mildly increased | Moderate | May not be able to drink |
| Capillary refill | <2 seconds | 2–3 seconds | >3 seconds |
| Eyes | Normal | Slightly sunken | Markedly sunken |
| Tears | Present | Decreased | Absent |
| Fontanelle (infant) | Normal | Slightly depressed | Markedly depressed, sunken |
| Skin turgor | Normal | Slightly decreased | Markedly decreased (tenting) |
| Mucous membranes | Moist | Slightly dry | Dry |
| Extremities | Warm | Cool | Cold, mottled |
| Urine output | Near normal | Decreased | Minimal or absent |
| Systolic BP | Normal | Normal | Low |

### Fontanelle Assessment (Infants <18 Months)

The anterior fontanelle is the soft area on the top of the infant's head between the frontal and parietal bones.

- **Normal:** Slightly convex, soft, pulsatile
- **Dehydrated:** Sunken below the level of the skull
- **Overhydrated or raised intracranial pressure:** Bulging, tense

Press gently and note depression below skull level (dehydration) or prominence above (elevated pressure).

### Shock in Dehydrated Children

Early shock is compensated: normal blood pressure, increased heart rate, cool extremities, delayed capillary refill. Uncompensated shock is life-threatening: hypotension, altered mental status, severe shock.

**Signs of uncompensated shock:**
- Systolic BP <70 mmHg (infant), <80 mmHg (toddler), <90 mmHg (school age)
- Weak or barely palpable pulses
- Severe altered mental status
- Skin pale, cold, mottled

Uncompensated shock requires immediate IV fluid bolus and evacuation.

Vital signs change significantly with age. Heart rate decreases and blood pressure increases as children grow.

| Age | Heart Rate (bpm) | Respiratory Rate (breaths/min) | Systolic BP (mmHg) | Diastolic BP (mmHg) |
|-----|------------------|--------------------------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| 0–3 months | 100–160 | 40–60 | 50–70 | 25–45 |
| 3–6 months | 100–160 | 40–60 | 60–80 | 30–50 |
| 6–12 months | 90–150 | 30–50 | 70–100 | 40–60 |

<section id="pediatric-shock-recognition">

## Pediatric Shock Recognition Timeline

Shock in children progresses through early (compensated) and late (decompensated) phases. Recognition of early shock is critical—intervention in the compensated phase can prevent progression to irreversible shock.

### Early (Compensated) Shock Timeline: 0–30 Minutes

In compensated shock, the body maintains blood pressure through sympathetic nervous system activation (increased heart rate, peripheral vasoconstriction). Blood pressure appears normal, but tissues are underperfused.

**Clinical signs of early shock (0–10 minutes):**
- **Tachycardia:** Heart rate elevated beyond normal range for age (see vital signs table)
- **Tachypnea:** Respiratory rate elevated; increased work of breathing
- **Delayed capillary refill:** >2 seconds when blanching skin on sternum or fingertip
- **Cool, pale skin:** Decreased perfusion to extremities; skin color pale or mottled
- **Altered mental status:** Anxiety, irritability, or decreased responsiveness
- **Oliguria:** Urine output decreased or absent
- **Normal blood pressure:** May be near-normal or at lower end of normal range for age

**Time-critical intervention window (10–30 minutes):**
- **Action required:** Initiate IV fluid bolus immediately
- **Fluid type:** Normal saline or Ringer's lactate, 20 mL/kg bolus
- **Administration:** Over 15–30 minutes
- **Monitor:** Reassess perfusion after bolus; repeat if signs persist

:::danger
**CRITICAL:** Many clinicians miss early shock because blood pressure is still "normal." Do NOT wait for hypotension to initiate resuscitation. Tachycardia + delayed capillary refill + cool extremities = shock until proven otherwise.
:::

### Late (Decompensated) Shock Timeline: 30+ Minutes

If early shock is not treated, decompensation occurs. In decompensated shock, the body's compensatory mechanisms fail and blood pressure drops—a pre-arrest state.

**Clinical signs of late shock (decompensation, 30+ minutes):**
- **Hypotension:** Systolic BP <70 mmHg (infant), <80 mmHg (toddler), <90 mmHg (school age), <100 mmHg (adolescent)
- **Severe altered mental status:** Lethargy, unresponsiveness, or decreased ability to interact
- **Severe tachycardia:** May slow as metabolic acidosis develops (ominous sign)
- **Weak or thready pulses:** Barely palpable; weak peripheral perfusion
- **Severe oliguria or anuria:** Minimal or no urine output
- **Skin:** Cold, clammy, mottled, or cyanotic (blue-tinged)
- **Lactic acidosis:** Rapid, deep breathing (Kussmaul respirations) as body compensates for metabolic acid

**Time-critical intervention window (30–60 minutes):**
- **Action required:** This is a medical emergency requiring immediate evacuation
- **Fluid resuscitation:** Continue IV fluids; repeat 20 mL/kg boluses
- **Medications:** Consider vasopressors (epinephrine, norepinephrine) if available and trained
- **Airway management:** Prepare for intubation if airway reflexes are lost
- **Evacuation:** Transport to ICU-capable facility immediately

:::danger
**CRITICAL WINDOW:** Once decompensated shock develops, reversibility decreases rapidly. If decompensated shock is present at field encounter, the child has likely been in shock for 30+ minutes. Outcomes are poor without ICU-level care (vasopressors, blood products, inotropic support).
:::

### Shock Etiology Recognition

Different causes of shock present with characteristic findings:

**Hypovolemic shock (most common in pediatrics):**
- Caused by: Dehydration (gastroenteritis, burns), hemorrhage, third-spacing
- Early signs: Tachycardia, delayed capillary refill, oliguria
- Responsive to: IV fluid boluses
- Characteristic: Cool extremities, dry mucous membranes (dehydration)

**Distributive shock (sepsis, anaphylaxis):**
- Caused by: Sepsis (bacterial/viral), anaphylaxis, toxic shock syndrome
- Early signs: Tachycardia, warm extremities (initially), bounding pulses
- Responsive to: Fluids + antibiotics (sepsis) or epinephrine (anaphylaxis)
- Characteristic: Rapid progression; rash (petechial in meningitis; urticarial in anaphylaxis)

**Cardiogenic shock (rare in children):**
- Caused by: Myocarditis, arrhythmia, pericardial effusion/tamponade, severe anemia
- Early signs: Tachycardia, pulmonary edema (crackles, wheezing), hepatomegaly
- Responsive to: Inotropes, diuretics; depends on etiology
- Characteristic: Fluid overload signs; history of cardiac disease

**Obstructive shock (tension pneumothorax, pericardial tamponade):**
- Caused by: Tension pneumothorax, pericardial tamponade, pulmonary embolism
- Early signs: Tachycardia, tracheal deviation (tension pneumothorax), muffled heart sounds (tamponade)
- Responsive to: Needle decompression (tension pneumothorax), needle pericardiocentesis (tamponade)
- Characteristic: Acute onset; may follow trauma

### Shock Assessment Flowchart

| Sign | Normal | Early Shock | Late Shock | Action |
|------|--------|-------------|-----------|--------|
| Heart rate | Age-normal | ↑ (>normal for age) | ↑↑ or ↓ (slowing is ominous) | Start fluids if HR elevated |
| Capillary refill | <2 sec | 2–3 sec | >3 sec | Fluid bolus 20 mL/kg |
| Skin | Warm, normal color | Cool, pale | Cold, mottled, cyanotic | Evacuate now if decompensated |
| BP | Normal for age | Normal (early) | ↓ (low for age) | Fluids + evacuation if low |
| Mental status | Appropriate | Anxious, irritable | Lethargic, unresponsive | Intubation readiness |
| Urine output | Normal (1 mL/kg/hr) | Oliguria (<0.5 mL/kg/hr) | Anuria (none) | Continuous monitoring |

</section>

### Respiratory Rate Assessment

Respiratory rate is one of the earliest signs of respiratory distress or inadequate oxygenation. Assess:

- **Retractions:** Indrawing of skin at the ribs, sternum, or suprasternal notch with breathing (sign of increased work of breathing)
- **Grunting:** Expiratory sound indicating respiratory distress
- **Nasal flaring:** Widening of nostrils with each breath
- **Head bobbing:** Neck extension and flexion with breathing (severe distress)
- **Accessory muscle use:** Muscles of the neck and shoulders engaged in breathing

Any combination of increased respiratory rate + retractions indicates respiratory distress and warrants supplemental oxygen and airway support.

<section id="common-pediatric-emergencies">

## Common Pediatric Emergencies

### Status Epilepticus (Prolonged or Recurrent Seizures)

Seizure lasting >5 minutes or recurrent seizures without return to baseline consciousness.

**Management:**
1. **Position:** Side-lying; protect airway.
2. **Oxygen:** Provide if available.
3. **Lorazepam IV:** 0.05–0.1 mg/kg (max 4 mg). If no IV access, **diazepam rectal:** 0.5 mg/kg (max 20 mg).
4. **Repeat every 10–15 minutes** if seizure continues.
5. **Second-line:** Fosphenytoin or phenobarbital if available.
6. **Airway:** Be prepared to manage airway if seizure continues; consider intubation.
7. **Evacuate urgently.**

### Croup (Laryngotracheobronchitis)

Viral infection causing barky, seal-like cough and inspiratory stridor. Usually self-limited.

**Mild-to-moderate croup:**
- Dexamethasone 0.15 mg/kg (max 6 mg) as single dose
- Supportive care: humidified air, hydration
- Most improve within 24 hours

**Severe croup with respiratory distress:**
- Nebulized epinephrine (1:1000) 0.5 mg/kg (max 5 mg) in 3 mL saline
- May repeat after 30 minutes
- Dexamethasone as above
- Consider oxygen
- Evacuation if not responding

:::warning
**Dexamethasone Prescription Warning:** Dexamethasone is a potent corticosteroid that should only be used with a specific medical indication (croup, bacterial meningitis adjunct, severe allergic reactions). It should NOT be used prophylactically or as a general anti-inflammatory without a clear diagnosis. Contraindicated in patients with active untreated infections (bacterial, fungal, parasitic) without concurrent antimicrobial therapy — corticosteroids suppress immune function and can allow infections to become fulminant. Even short courses (>48 hours) can cause hyperglycemia, adrenal suppression, immunosuppression, and GI bleeding risk. In pediatric patients, use the lowest effective dose for the shortest duration. Taper gradually if used for more than 2-3 days.
:::

### Epiglottitis

Bacterial infection causing swollen epiglottis; rare since Hib vaccination but still life-threatening. Child presents with drooling, difficulty swallowing, tripod positioning (sitting forward, propped on hands).

:::danger
Do NOT examine the throat or manipulate the airway unnecessarily. Avoid agitation. Prepare for emergency airway management (intubation or cricothyrotomy). This is a surgical emergency—evacuate immediately while maintaining airway.
:::

**Management:**
1. Keep child calm and upright.
2. Prepare for airway management.
3. Administer oxygen if available.
4. IV access only if child is stable and does not resist.
5. Antibiotics if available (ceftriaxone 80 mg/kg/day divided in 4 doses).
6. Evacuate emergently.

### Anaphylaxis

Immediate allergic reaction to food, insect sting, medication, or latex.

**Signs:** Rapid onset (seconds to minutes) of urticaria (hives), angioedema (swelling), stridor or wheezing, hypotension, altered mental status.

**Management:**
1. **Epinephrine IM immediately:** 0.01 mg/kg of 1:1000 solution (max 0.5 mg) into the lateral thigh.
2. **Oxygen:** Provide if available.
3. **IV access and fluids:** 20 mL/kg bolus of normal saline.
4. **Antihistamine:** Diphenhydramine 1 mg/kg IV or IM (max 50 mg).
5. **Corticosteroid:** Hydrocortisone 50 mg/kg IV (max 500 mg).
6. **Monitor:** Symptoms can recur; observe for at least 4–6 hours.
7. **Evacuate** for ongoing observation.

### Meningitis

Viral or bacterial infection of meninges. Presents with fever, neck stiffness, headache, rash (petechial), altered mental status.

**Red flags:**
- Petechial (small red/purple) rash that does not blanch with pressure
- Altered consciousness
- Seizure
- Severe headache with fever

**Management:**
1. **High-dose antibiotics immediately** (do not delay for diagnostics):
   - Ceftriaxone 80–100 mg/kg/day divided in 2 doses (max 4 g/day) **OR**
   - Cefotaxime 150 mg/kg/day divided in 4 doses (max 12 g/day)
2. **Supportive care:** IV fluids, fever control.
3. **Dexamethasone:** 0.15 mg/kg (max 10 mg) given with or just before the first antibiotic dose improves outcomes in bacterial meningitis.
4. **Isolation:** Respiratory precautions (mask for all contact) to prevent spread.
5. **Evacuate urgently** for lumbar puncture and ICU care.

### Intussusception

Invagination (telescoping) of one segment of bowel into another, typically in infants 6–36 months. Presents with intermittent severe abdominal pain, palpable "sausage-like" mass in the right upper quadrant, and "currant jelly" stools (blood-tinged).

**Management:**
1. **Nothing by mouth:** Patient may need surgery.
2. **IV access and fluids:** Fluid resuscitation.
3. **Imaging:** Ultrasound can confirm diagnosis; sometimes used therapeutically (hydrostatic reduction).
4. **Surgery:** Definitive treatment if imaging reduction unsuccessful or perforation suspected.
5. **Evacuate urgently.**

### Gastroenteritis with Severe Dehydration

Common in children; can rapidly progress to hypovolemic shock.

**Mild-to-moderate dehydration:**
- Oral rehydration solution (ORS): 50 mL/kg over 4 hours for mild; 100 mL/kg over 4 hours for moderate
- Small, frequent meals once tolerating fluids

**Severe dehydration or shock:**
- IV normal saline: 20 mL/kg bolus over 30 minutes; repeat if no improvement
- Nothing by mouth initially
- Once stabilized, transition to oral rehydration

**Antimotility agents (loperamide):** Avoid in children <2 years and in bloody diarrhea (risk of toxic megacolon).

### Foreign Body Airway Obstruction (FBAO)

Choking is a leading cause of unintentional injury in infants and young children. Management differs by age. For general choking safety and prevention, see the <a href="../first-aid.html">First Aid guide</a>.

**Infants (0–12 months) with FBAO:**
1. **Back blows + chest thrusts:**
   - Support the infant's jaw with one hand; position face-down on your forearm
   - Deliver 5 sharp back blows between the scapulae with heel of hand
   - Turn infant face-up (supporting head and neck); deliver 5 rapid chest thrusts with 2 fingers on lower sternum
   - Repeat cycle until object is expelled or infant becomes unresponsive
2. **If unresponsive:** Begin CPR; open mouth gently before each breath to visualize object for removal
3. **Do NOT perform abdominal thrusts** in infants (risk of visceral injury)

**Children (>12 months) with FBAO:**
1. **Abdominal thrusts (Heimlich maneuver):**
   - Stand behind the child; place fist above navel, below xiphoid process
   - Grasp fist with other hand; deliver rapid upward thrusts
   - Repeat 5–10 times until object expelled
2. **Back blows:** May be alternated with abdominal thrusts if child is very young (12–36 months)
3. **If unresponsive:** Begin CPR; open mouth before each rescue breath to look for object

:::warning
Do NOT perform blind finger sweeps. Only remove visible objects. Blind sweeping can lodge the object deeper in the airway.
:::

**After successful clearance:** Observe for signs of persistent airway obstruction, esophageal perforation (subcutaneous emphysema, chest pain), or aspiration pneumonia. Evacuate if respiratory distress develops.

### Burns Assessment and Initial Management

Burn severity in children is assessed using percentage of body surface area (BSA) affected. Children have different BSA proportions than adults.

**Modified Rule of Nines for children:**
- Head and neck: 18% (vs. 9% in adults)
- Anterior trunk: 18%
- Posterior trunk: 18%
- Each upper extremity: 9%
- Each lower extremity: 14%
- Perineum/genitalia: 1%

**For infants and very young children, use the Lund-Browder chart** (a more accurate age-adjusted diagram). Estimate percentages vary with age—the head is relatively larger, legs smaller.

**Burn severity classification:**
- **Superficial (1st degree):** Erythema only; no blistering. Painful; heals in 3–5 days
- **Partial-thickness (2nd degree):** Blisters, wet appearance, blanches with pressure; very painful
- **Full-thickness (3rd degree):** Charred, leathery, does not blanch; painless (nerve destruction)
- **Deep full-thickness (4th degree):** Extends to muscle/bone

**Critical thresholds:**
- >10% BSA partial-thickness or full-thickness burn in children = transfer to burn center
- >5% BSA in infants <1 year = transfer (infants have less fluid reserve)
- Any full-thickness burn = transfer
- Any burn on face, hands, feet, genitalia, joints, or circumferentially = transfer

**Acute burn management:**
1. **Stop the burn:** Remove from heat source; cool with room-temperature water (not ice) for 10–15 minutes
2. **Remove contamination:** Non-adherent clothing; leave adherent clothing in place
3. **IV access:** Two large-bore IVs; draw blood for baseline labs
4. **Fluid resuscitation:** <a href="../burn-treatment.html">Refer to Burn Treatment guide</a> for detailed protocols; typically use Parkland formula modified for pediatric age
5. **Pain control:** IV opioids (morphine 0.1 mg/kg); avoid IM in dehydrated children
6. **Tetanus prophylaxis:** If burned area is tetanus-prone or vaccination status unknown
7. **Antibiotics:** Topical agents (silver sulfadiazine, bacitracin) to prevent infection
8. **Monitoring:** Reassess for inhalation injury (singed nasal hairs, soot in mouth, stridor, wheezing), compartment syndrome (circumferential burns), rhabdomyolysis

**Inhalation injury:** Check for carbonaceous sputum, bronchorrhea, and respiratory distress. Anticipate difficult airway; consider early intubation before edema worsens.

### Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) in New-Onset Type 1 Diabetes

DKA is a medical emergency and is the first presentation of type 1 diabetes in ~25% of children. Can occur at any age but peaks at 5–7 years.

**Recognition:**
- Polyuria, polydipsia, polyphagia
- Rapid weight loss
- Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain
- Fruity-smelling breath (acetone)
- Rapid, deep breathing (Kussmaul respirations)
- Altered mental status, lethargy, or coma
- Tachycardia, hypotension (in severe cases)

**Immediate assessment:**
- **Blood glucose:** Usually >250 mg/dL; may be >600 mg/dL
- **Venous or capillary blood gas:** pH <7.30, bicarbonate <15 mEq/L
- **Serum electrolytes:** Check for hyponatremia, hypokalemia, hyperchloremia
- **Urinalysis:** Large ketones, glycosuria
- **Anion gap:** >12 mEq/L

**Management:**
1. **IV access:** Two large-bore IVs; continuous cardiac monitoring
2. **Fluid resuscitation:** Start with 10 mL/kg of normal saline over 1 hour; then continue at slower rate (0.5–1 mL/kg/hr) over next 24–48 hours
3. **Insulin:** Begin after potassium is checked (insulin drives K+ into cells, worsening hypokalemia)
   - Initial bolus: 0.1 unit/kg IV
   - Infusion: 0.05–0.1 unit/kg/hr
4. **Potassium replacement:** Crucial—even though total-body K+ is depleted, serum K+ may be normal or high initially
   - If K+ <3.5 mEq/L: Add 20–40 mEq/L to IV fluids
   - Do NOT give insulin until K+ is checked
5. **Sodium bicarbonate:** Reserved for pH <6.9 and severe acidosis (rare; usually supportive care sufficient)
6. **Monitoring:** Check glucose every 1–2 hours; electrolytes every 2–4 hours until stable
7. **Transition to subcutaneous insulin:** Once DKA resolved (glucose <200 mg/dL, pH >7.30, tolerating oral intake)
8. **Evacuation:** All DKA cases require hospital care; watch for cerebral edema (rare but fatal), rhabdomyolysis, and acute kidney injury

:::danger
Cerebral edema is a rare but devastating complication of DKA treatment, especially with rapid correction or excessive hypotonic fluid administration. Use isotonic fluids; avoid overly rapid glucose correction.
:::

</section>

## Pediatric Fluid Resuscitation Protocols

Accurate fluid management is critical in pediatric emergencies. Calculations are weight-based, and over- or under-resuscitation both carry serious consequences.

### Maintenance Fluid Requirements (Holliday-Segar Formula)

Used for ongoing hydration in non-critical patients:

- **First 10 kg of body weight:** 4 mL/kg/day
- **Second 10 kg of body weight:** 2 mL/kg/day
- **Each additional kg above 20 kg:** 1 mL/kg/day

**Example:** A 25 kg child requires (10 × 4) + (10 × 2) + (5 × 1) = 40 + 20 + 5 = 65 mL/day total, or ~2.7 mL/kg/hr average

**In acute settings,** administer maintenance fluids at 1.5–2 times normal rate if significant ongoing losses (diarrhea, vomiting, fever).

### Bolus Fluid Resuscitation for Shock

**Initial bolus:**
- **Volume:** 20 mL/kg of isotonic crystalloid (normal saline 0.9% or Ringer's lactate)
- **Rate:** Rapid infusion over 15–30 minutes
- **Re-assess:** After bolus, check perfusion (capillary refill, skin temperature, mental status, urine output)
- **Repeat:** If signs of shock persist, give second bolus of 20 mL/kg
- **Third bolus:** If still in shock after two 20 mL/kg boluses, patient may need vasopressor support and ICU care; evacuate immediately

:::tip
In hemorrhagic shock, use 1:1:1 ratio of packed RBCs to FFP to platelets if available. Minimize initial crystalloid—crystalloid-only resuscitation in hemorrhage increases mortality.
:::

### Deficit Replacement

For dehydration (non-hemorrhagic), calculate the fluid deficit and replace over 4–12 hours depending on severity:

**Deficit = % dehydration × body weight (kg) × 10**

- 5% dehydration = 5% of body weight lost
- Example: 10 kg child, 10% dehydration = 0.10 × 10 × 10 = 10 liters × 10 = 1000 mL deficit

Administer half the deficit over the first 8 hours, then the second half over the next 16 hours, **plus** ongoing maintenance and replacement of continuing losses.

### Signs of Fluid Overload

Excessive fluid administration can cause pulmonary edema, congestive heart failure, and cerebral edema (especially in DKA).

**Warning signs:**
- Respiratory crackles (pulmonary edema)
- Increasing oxygen requirement
- Hepatomegaly
- Jugular venous distension
- Weight gain >10% in 24 hours
- Altered mental status (cerebral edema)
- Hypertension (unusual for pediatric shock)

**Management of overload:**
- Slow infusion rate
- Switch to maintenance fluids only
- Consider diuretics (furosemide 1 mg/kg IV) if pulmonary edema present
- Elevate head of bed if conscious
- Monitor daily weights

## Neonatal Emergencies

Neonates (birth to 28 days) have unique physiology and require specialized approaches. Immediately after birth, the infant transitions from fetal to neonatal circulation—a process that can be disrupted by asphyxia, infection, or anatomic abnormality.

### Birth Asphyxia and Resuscitation

Perinatal asphyxia occurs when the infant does not receive adequate oxygen before, during, or immediately after birth.

**Risk factors:**
- Prolonged labor, maternal infection (chorioamnionitis)
- Placental abruption or preeclampsia
- Meconium staining of amniotic fluid
- Abnormal fetal heart rate patterns
- Prematurity

**Recognition at birth:**
- **Apgar score <7** at 1 minute (assesses Appearance, Pulse, Grimace, Activity, Respiration)
- Cyanosis, pallor, limp muscle tone
- Apneic (no respiratory effort) or gasping
- Bradycardic (<100 bpm)
- Seizure activity

**Immediate resuscitation (birth-1 minute):**
1. **Dry the infant** thoroughly; remove wet blankets (prevent heat loss)
2. **Position:** Neutral head position on warmed surface
3. **Clear airway:** Gentle suctioning of mouth then nose (if meconium-stained, deep suctioning only if infant is vigorous; non-vigorous infants should go directly to resuscitation)
4. **Tactile stimulation:** Dry rubbing of back and limbs to stimulate breathing
5. **Provide oxygen:** If cyanotic despite drying/positioning, start oxygen (100% via mask or bag)

**Bag-mask ventilation (if no spontaneous breathing by 1 minute):**
- **Rate:** 40–60 breaths/min
- **Pressure:** Just enough to see chest rise
- **Mask seal:** Ensure good contact; adjust head position
- **If no improvement by 5 minutes:** Prepare for intubation

**Chest compressions (if heart rate <100 bpm by 1 minute or remains <60 bpm by 10 minutes):**
- **Technique:** Two thumbs or two fingers (3rd and 4th) on lower sternum, just below nipple line
- **Depth:** ~1/3 chest depth
- **Rate:** 100–120 compressions/min
- **Ratio:** 3:1 (3 compressions: 1 ventilation) in neonates
- **Continue until:** Heart rate >100 bpm or 10 minutes of no response (consider terminating resuscitation)

**Medications (if available):**
- **Epinephrine:** 0.01 mg/kg IV umbilical vein (push rapidly, flush with saline)
- **Sodium bicarbonate:** 1 mEq/kg IV (only after 10+ minutes of good ventilation and compressions; improves refractory acidosis)

**Thermoregulation:** Keep newborn warm (goal 36.5–37.5°C). Hypothermia worsens outcomes; use radiant warmers, warm blankets, cap if available.

### Meconium Aspiration Syndrome (MAS)

Meconium (fetal stool) in amniotic fluid suggests fetal stress. If aspirated into the lungs, it causes chemical pneumonitis and airway obstruction.

**Recognition:**
- History of meconium-stained amniotic fluid
- Tachypnea, grunting, retractions within hours of birth
- Hyperinflation on chest X-ray (if available)
- Hypoxemia despite supplemental oxygen

**Management:**
- **Oxygen:** Titrate to SpO2 >94%
- **Respiratory support:** CPAP or mechanical ventilation if severe
- **Monitor:** Watch for air leak (pneumothorax)—most common complication
- **Treat secondary bacterial infection** if signs of sepsis develop (fever, lethargy, poor feeding)
- **Do NOT routinely use antibiotics** unless infected
- **Do NOT routinely use exogenous surfactant** unless diagnosis confirmed and severe

### Neonatal Sepsis Recognition

Sepsis in newborns presents nonspecifically: fever, hypothermia, poor feeding, lethargy, or irritability. High index of suspicion is critical.

**Risk factors:**
- Maternal fever during labor or prolonged rupture of membranes (>18 hours)
- Maternal GBS colonization (group B streptococcus) not treated
- Chorioamnionitis
- Prematurity

**Clinical signs:**
- Fever (temperature >38°C rectal) or hypothermia (<36°C)
- Poor feeding, weak suck
- Lethargy or irritability
- Jaundice appearing <24 hours of birth (suggests hemolysis or infection)
- Vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal distension
- Seizure (rare, but sign of meningitis)
- Petechial rash (signs of meningococcemia; rare in neonates but grave)

**Immediate management:**
1. **IV access:** Peripheral IV or umbilical line
2. **Cultures:** Draw blood culture before antibiotics if possible
3. **Labs:** CBC, CRP, procalcitonin, blood glucose
4. **Empiric antibiotics** (do NOT delay for cultures):
   - Ampicillin 50 mg/kg IV every 12 hours (birth–7 days) or every 8 hours (>7 days)
   - PLUS Gentamicin 7.5 mg/kg IV every 18 hours (birth–7 days) or every 12 hours (>7 days)
   - OR Ceftotaxime if meningitis suspected (100 mg/kg IV every 12 hours)
5. **Supportive care:** IV fluids, maintain temperature, monitor for complications
6. **Evacuation:** All presumed neonatal sepsis requires hospital admission and ICU observation

### Umbilical Venous Line Access (UVC) in Neonatal Arrest

If peripheral IV cannot be obtained quickly in a resuscitating newborn, umbilical vein catheterization is life-saving.

**Technique (field/austere setting):**
1. Visualize umbilical cord; gently clean with saline or antiseptic if available
2. Identify three vessels: two small arteries (thicker walls) and one larger vein
3. Grasp the base of umbilical stump with forceps or clamp
4. Insert a 3.5 or 5 Fr catheter (or IV catheter) into the umbilical vein at a 45° angle
5. Advance until blood returns; secure with tape or suture
6. Use for emergency medications and fluids only (not for ongoing infusions once peripheral access obtained—risk of hepatic necrosis from hypertonic solutions)

**Complications:** Infection, vessel perforation, liver necrosis (if hypertonic fluid infused). Remove as soon as peripheral access established.

### Neonatal Thermoregulation

Newborns lose heat rapidly and cannot generate sufficient heat to compensate.

**Prevention of hypothermia:**
- Dry immediately after birth
- Use radiant warmer or body-to-skin contact (mother's chest)
- Apply warm blankets and hat
- Avoid cold surfaces, cold fluids, prolonged air exposure
- Keep ambient temperature 22–25°C

**Consequences of hypothermia:**
- Increased metabolic rate and oxygen consumption
- Worsening of asphyxia
- Hyperglycemia or hypoglycemia
- Coagulopathy and increased infection risk

**Mild hypothermia (33–36°C) management:** Passive rewarming with blankets
**Moderate hypothermia (30–33°C):** Active external rewarming (warm fluids, radiant heat)
**Severe hypothermia (<30°C):** Slow active rewarming; avoid rapid external warming (peripheral vasodilation → afterdrop)

## Pediatric Trauma Assessment (ABCDE Approach)

Pediatric trauma requires modification of the standard adult approach. Children have different anatomy, compensate longer, and may have injuries in unexpected locations.

### A — Airway and Cervical Spine Protection

**Airway assessment:**
- Listen for spontaneous vocalization (cry, words)
- Look for obstruction: blood, saliva, foreign material, tongue
- Assess for stridor (high-pitched breathing indicating upper airway obstruction)

**Cervical spine precautions:**
- Assume cervical injury until proven otherwise in any significant trauma
- Use jaw thrust (no head extension) if airway intervention needed
- Apply C-spine collar; immobilize with rolled blankets or backboard
- All children with head injury, loss of consciousness, or high-mechanism trauma warrant C-spine imaging

**Airway management (if needed):**
- Suction airway gently (not deeply into pharynx—risk of vagal response and bradycardia)
- Place small towel roll under shoulders (not under head) to align airway
- Size-appropriate oropharyngeal airway if unconscious and no gag reflex
- Prepare for intubation if decreased level of consciousness

### B — Breathing and Ventilation

**Assessment:**
- Count respiratory rate (should be normal for age; see vital signs)
- Listen bilaterally for breath sounds (asymmetry suggests tension pneumothorax or hemothorax)
- Observe for retractions, nasal flaring, accessory muscle use
- Palpate chest for crepitus (subcutaneous emphysema), flail segments, rib fractures

**Common pediatric thoracic injuries:**
- **Pneumothorax/hemothorax:** Decreased breath sounds, tracheal deviation (tension), hypotension. Treat with needle decompression (2nd intercostal space, midclavicular line) followed by chest tube if available.
- **Rib fractures:** Usually associated with significant force; suspect underlying lung contusion or pneumothorax.
- **Pulmonary contusion:** Develops over hours; monitor for progressive respiratory distress and hypoxemia. Provide supplemental oxygen; avoid overload (risk of pulmonary edema).
- **Flail chest:** Multiple rib fractures in segment. Pain control is critical to prevent hypoventilation. Consider epidural analgesia (if available) or regional blocks.

:::warning
In children, rib fractures suggest high-impact force and carry higher risk of underlying visceral injury. Suspect pulmonary contusion, liver/spleen laceration, and cardiac injury.
:::

### C — Circulation and Hemorrhage Control

**Assessment:**
- Check distal pulses (femoral, radial, pedal) for presence and quality
- Assess skin perfusion: color, warmth, capillary refill
- Check for external bleeding

**Hemorrhage control:**
- Apply direct pressure to bleeding wounds with gauze or cloth
- Elevate extremity if possible
- Apply tourniquet **proximal to wound** if bleeding uncontrolled by pressure (thigh or arm); note time of application
- Do NOT remove impaled objects—stabilize in place with bulky dressings

**Shock assessment:** Use shock recognition timeline (see Pediatric Shock Recognition section). Begin fluid resuscitation immediately if signs of shock.

**Specific pediatric hemorrhage:**
- **Intra-abdominal bleeding (spleen, liver):** Often managed conservatively with bed rest and transfusion rather than surgery if stable. Watch for deterioration.
- **Pelvic fracture:** Can cause massive retroperitoneal hemorrhage. Apply pelvic binder or wrap tightly with sheet to stabilize.
- **Femur fracture:** Potential for 1.5–2 L blood loss into thigh. Apply traction splint if available to reduce bleeding and pain.

### D — Disability (Neurologic Assessment)

**AVPU scale:**
- **Alert:** Awake, oriented, responsive to voice
- **Verbal:** Responds to voice but not oriented
- **Painful:** Responds only to painful stimuli
- **Unresponsive:** No response

**Glasgow Coma Scale (modified for children):**
- Same as adults; assess eye opening, verbal response, motor response
- GCS <8 = consider intubation to protect airway

**Pupil assessment:**
- Size: Normal 2–5 mm; dilated >5 mm suggests increased intracranial pressure or drug effect
- Reactivity: Should constrict to light
- Asymmetry: Blown pupil (>5 mm, non-reactive) suggests uncal herniation—neurosurgical emergency

**Head injury management:**
- Assume concussion if any trauma to head region
- Observe for deterioration (vomiting, lethargy, seizure)
- Elevate head 30° to promote venous drainage
- Avoid hyperthermia and hypoxemia (worsen outcomes)
- Neuroimaging indicated for: GCS <15, focal neurologic signs, vomiting, loss of consciousness >1 minute

### E — Exposure and Environment

**Exposure:**
- Remove all clothing to assess for occult injuries
- Log-roll to examine back and buttocks
- Look for abrasions, bruising, penetrating injuries

**Hemorrhage reassessment:**
- Re-count wounds and re-apply pressure
- Monitor for ongoing bleeding

**Environmental considerations:**
- Prevent heat loss (cover child in blankets after assessment)
- Protect privacy and dignity
- Note time of injury, mechanism, and estimated blood loss

### Pediatric-Specific Trauma Considerations

**Abdominal trauma in children:**
- Liver and spleen injuries are most common; children tolerate these conservatively if hemodynamically stable
- Duodenal hematoma and pancreatic injury can present with vomiting/abdominal pain days after injury
- Assess for lap-belt sign (bruising across lower abdomen)—associated with hollow viscus injury

**Estimating blood loss by age/weight:**
- **Infant (3 kg):** ~240 mL total blood volume; 10% loss = 24 mL; pale, tachycardia, weak pulses if >20% lost
- **Toddler (12 kg):** ~960 mL total blood volume; 10% loss = 96 mL; tachycardia, cool extremities if >15% lost
- **School age (30 kg):** ~2400 mL total blood volume; 10% loss = 240 mL; normal BP if <20% lost

**Rule of thumb:** Children can lose up to 15–20% blood volume before showing signs of shock; loss of 25% or more is immediately life-threatening.

## Drowning and Submersion Injuries

Drowning is the leading cause of unintentional death in children ages 1–4 in many developed nations. Submersion can result in complete recovery or devastating brain injury depending on duration and water temperature.

### Pathophysiology of Submersion

**Cold water (≤15°C):** Triggers diving reflex (bradycardia, breath-hold, peripheral vasoconstriction). Can allow some protection if submersion very brief. Hypothermia develops rapidly and may paradoxically slow metabolism, reducing oxygen demand.

**Warm water (>15°C):** No protective reflex; hypoxemia and CO2 retention develop quickly. Panic and aspiration follow rapidly.

**Mechanism of injury:**
1. **Initial breath-hold:** Hypoxemia and hypercapnia build
2. **Gasping reflex:** Water aspirates into lungs (5–10% children have dry drowning—laryngospasm prevents aspiration)
3. **Loss of consciousness:** Cerebral hypoxemia; continued aspiration
4. **Cardiac arrest:** Profound hypoxia and acidosis; asystole develops

**Duration thresholds:**
- **<5 minutes:** Good chance of full recovery
- **5–10 minutes:** Increasing risk of brain damage
- **>10 minutes:** Usually fatal or severe neurologic injury

### Recognition of Drowning vs. Distress

**Active drowning (child in water):**
- Mouth at water level; unable to call for help
- Head tilted back with mouth open
- Eyes glazed, closed, or rolling back
- Vertical body position; little or no leg movement
- Gasping or no sound
- Appears to be treading water but making no progress

**Passive drowning (child unconscious in water):**
- Submerged or face-down at water surface
- Unresponsive
- No spontaneous breathing

**Do NOT wait for the "obvious" drowning—many silent drownings occur in seconds.**

### Immediate Management

**Rescue and removal from water:**
1. **Reach, throw, go:** Use reach pole or throw flotation device before entering water yourself
2. **If you must enter water:** Use floatation; approach from behind to avoid panic grab
3. **Spine precautions:** Assume cervical spine injury in any significant trauma (shallow-water dives); use jaw thrust only
4. **Place in recovery position:** On side if breathing spontaneously and with intact gag reflex

**CPR for drowning:**
- If not breathing, begin CPR immediately (5 rescue breaths, then compressions)
- Continue until: Rescuer exhausted, signs of life return, advanced help arrives, or 1 hour of continuous CPR (in hypothermic children, resuscitation may be continued longer as they can tolerate prolonged hypoxemia)
- **Hypothermic children:** May appear lifeless but recover after rewarming; do NOT declare deceased until warmed and checked

**Airway management:**
- Remove any visible debris/seaweed gently
- Do NOT perform abdominal thrusts to remove water (increases aspiration risk)
- Suction gently once conscious level stable

### Post-Drowning Management

**All submersion victims warrant evaluation and observation,** even if they appear to recover spontaneously. Delayed complications include:

1. **Secondary drowning / Dry drowning (24–72 hours post-event):**
   - Pulmonary edema develops from water aspiration
   - Progressive respiratory distress, cough, wheezing
   - May require supplemental oxygen, CPAP, or intubation

2. **Hypoxemic encephalopathy:**
   - Altered mental status, lethargy, seizure
   - Poor prognosis if prolonged submersion
   - May take weeks to determine outcome

3. **Aspiration pneumonia:**
   - Fever, infiltrates on chest X-ray
   - Treat with broad-spectrum antibiotics if waterborne contamination suspected

**In-field management post-resuscitation:**
- Keep child warm (prevent hypothermia)
- Provide supplemental oxygen; target SpO2 >94%
- Obtain IV access; monitor heart rate and blood pressure
- Observe for seizure; have anticonvulsant ready
- Evacuate all submersion victims for hospital monitoring and evaluation

### Hypothermia Considerations

**Cold-water drowning (water <15°C):**
- Hypothermia often accompanies submersion
- Bradycardia is expected; do NOT assume asystole means death
- Aggressive rewarming may be needed; some "cold-water drowning" victims recover after prolonged resuscitation

**Rewarming protocol:**
- **Mild hypothermia (32–35°C):** Passive external rewarming; warm blankets and warm environment
- **Moderate hypothermia (28–32°C):** Active external rewarming; warmed IV fluids, warm inhalation
- **Severe hypothermia (<28°C):** Extracorporeal rewarming (ECMO if available) for best outcomes

:::tip
The saying "No one is dead until they are warm and dead" applies to cold-water drowning victims. Continue resuscitation during evacuation, even if arrest prolonged.
:::

### Prognostic Factors

**Favorable:**
- Submersion <5 minutes
- Age <5 years (better neurologic resilience)
- Cold water (<15°C)
- Early CPR and advanced life support
- Initial GCS >5

**Unfavorable:**
- Submersion >10 minutes
- Witnessed apnea >25 minutes
- pH <7.0 on presentation
- No pupillary light reflex
- Absent motor response on exam

Children with good prognostic indicators can recover fully; those with poor indicators have high mortality or severe neurologic disability.

## Evacuation Priorities for Pediatric Emergencies

### Immediate Evacuation (Call Emergency Services / Helicopter if Available)

- Respiratory distress, stridor, or altered airway
- Shock (compensated or uncompensated)
- Altered mental status or severe lethargy
- Seizures (status epilepticus)
- Severe trauma
- Suspected meningitis or epiglottitis
- Anaphylaxis
- Severe dehydration unresponsive to fluids

### Urgent Evacuation (Within 1–2 Hours)

- Moderate dehydration with ongoing losses
- Febrile seizure with complex features
- Suspected intussusception
- Severe croup unresponsive to steroids and epinephrine
- Possible foreign body ingestion

### Routine Evacuation (Can Wait, But Should Not)

- Mild dehydration
- Simple febrile seizure, first occurrence
- Mild croup
- Minor trauma without concerning features
