## Overview

If you are asking, "Can I save this animal for meat after a disaster?" start with the triage block above: healthy stock gets water and shelter, salvageable injuries get fast slaughter, and decomposed or obviously unsafe carcasses are discarded.

Disaster (flood, earthquake, war) often leaves livestock stranded, injured, or without feed and water. Quick action salvages meat before spoilage, removes disease vectors, and prevents suffering. Unlike routine butchering (controlled, sanitary), emergency salvage operates in chaotic conditionsâ€”limited tools, no refrigeration, contaminated water, disease risk. Success requires rapid decision-making, basic anatomy knowledge, and improvisation.

This guide covers livestock recovery, emergency slaughter, field butchery procedures, and preservation under duress. Principles apply to both domestic animals and feral/wild animals encountered in post-disaster zones.

## Livestock Recovery After Disasters

**Early assessment** (first 24 hours post-disaster) determines livestock survival and salvageability.

### Livestock Status Evaluation

**Living animals:**

- **Healthy:** Feed, water, basic care. Can recover.
- **Injured but mobile:** Assess injury severity. If treatable, isolate and treat. If severe (broken leg, severe laceration), immediate slaughter recommended (prevents suffering, secures meat before spoilage).
- **Severely injured or unable to move:** Immediate slaughter; salvage meat before death (decomposition begins within hours in warm weather).
- **Drowned or decomposing:** Do not consume (bacterial contamination, toxins).

**Assessment checklist:**

| **Condition** | **Sign** | **Action** |
|---|---|---|
| Shock/dehydration | Rapid pulse (>120 bpm), dry mucous membranes | Give water + electrolytes; rest 2 hours before deciding |
| Severe injury | Wound infection smell, pus, heat around injury | Slaughter if infection >1 day old; recover meat immediately |
| Fracture | Obvious deformity, unwillingness to bear weight | Slaughter (cannot heal without veterinary care) |
| Fever/illness | Elevated temperature, lethargy, discharge | Slaughter if meat is salvageable (illness in early stage < 48 hrs) |
| Starvation | Visible ribs, protruding hip bones, lethargy | Feed and water; assess 24 hours later |

### Livestock Recovery and Stabilization

**Water and feed** (immediate priority):

- **Water:** Animals need water within 2 hours (critical in heat). Offer frequently in small amounts (prevents bloat if severely dehydrated; give electrolyte solution if availableâ€”salt + sugar in water).
- **Feed:** Offer forage (hay, grass) or grain if available. Small amounts first (prevents colic).

**Shelter:** Protect from further weather (rain, extreme cold/heat). Group animals together (reduces stress; allows body heat sharing in cold).

**Injury stabilization:**

- **Bleeding:** Apply pressure with cloth; elevate limb if possible.
- **Fracture:** Immobilize with splint or support; minimize movement.
- **Burns:** Cool with water if available; do not apply ice (causes further damage).

**Quarantine:** Isolate sick animals from healthy ones (prevents disease spread). Mange, foot rot, respiratory diseases spread rapidly in crowded conditions.

## Feral Animal Management

In disaster zones, domestic animals become feral (revert to wild behavior), and wild animals move into human areas seeking food/shelter. Both are dangerous and potentially diseased.

### Feral Dog and Cat Packs

**Risk assessment:** Feral dogs in packs are dangerous (will attack humans and livestock). Feral cats less dangerous but can carry rabies and other diseases.

**Management:**

- **Avoid confrontation:** Do not engage; keep distance (>100 meters).
- **Secure livestock:** Pen animals; do not leave unattended.
- **Remove food sources:** Dispose of garbage, dead animals; eliminate attraction.
- **Lethal control (last resort):** If pack is attacking livestock or threatening people, organized hunt with multiple people and weapons.

### Feral Livestock

Domestic animals left for weeks/months revert to feral behavior: cattle become aggressive, pigs become dangerous, horses are unpredictable.

**Recovery approach (if animal is valuable):**

1. **Corral:** Herd into enclosed area (pen, barn, canyon).
2. **Isolate:** Separate from herd if mixed species.
3. **Approach carefully:** Use ropes, not physical contact. Animal is afraid and may attack.
4. **Reassess:** Once in pen for 24 hours, behavior usually improves.

**If recovery is not practical:** Slaughter for meat (see next section).

### Wild Animals in Human Areas

Deer, boar, rabbits may forage near settlements post-disaster. Risk: Disease (especially tick-borne illnesses), unpredictability, property damage.

**Management:**

- **Avoid:** Do not attract or feed wild animals.
- **Secure crops/shelter:** Prevent foraging access.
- **Hunting:** If meat is needed, organized hunts with trained shooters. Avoid eating organs (disease vector); use only muscle meat.

:::warning
Disease in stressed animals is high. Assume all wild and feral animals carry parasites and pathogens. Cook meat thoroughly (internal temperature 160â€“165Â°F for all game). Boil or filter water after animal contact.
:::

## Emergency Slaughter Procedures

**Goal:** Render animal unconscious instantly, then kill quickly, minimizing suffering and preserving meat quality.

### Method Selection by Animal Size

**Small animals (goats, sheep, pigs <150 lbs):**

**Neck cut method:**

1. **Restraint:** Have person hold animal securely (headlock or rope).
2. **Positioning:** Animal's head slightly extended (exposes neck).
3. **Cut:** Sharp knife across windpipe and carotid artery (major vein on side of neck). Both sides severed simultaneously = rapid unconsciousness (10â€“20 seconds) followed by death (loss of blood pressure to brain).
4. **Bleed out:** Allow blood to drain into container (can be used for blood sausage or fertilizer).

**Stunned-then-cut method (preferred if captive bolt available):**

1. **Stun:** Captive bolt gun or blow to forehead (point of intersection between eye and ear lines) renders animal unconscious instantly.
2. **Cut:** Once unconscious, cut neck as above.

**Advantages:** Faster, less stress on animal (no awareness of dying).

**Large animals (cattle, horses >500 lbs):**

**Stunning is essential** (difficult to cut neck on large animal without restraint). Options:

1. **Captive bolt gun (large caliber):** .45 or larger. Aimed at specific point (center of forehead, midway between eyes and ears). Renders unconscious instantly.
2. **Axe blow:** Requires skill and strength; less reliable.
3. **Rifle shot:** If available and person is trained; shot placement critical (aim for brain).

**Procedure:**

1. **Stun:** Captive bolt or shot; animal drops unconscious.
2. **Bleed:** Immediately cut neck; blood loss causes death within 1â€“2 minutes.

### Post-Slaughter Handling

**Immediate actions:**

1. **Bleed out:** Allow 5â€“10 minutes for complete blood drainage (improves meat quality and shelf life).
2. **Eviscerate:** Remove internal organs quickly (bacterial contamination accelerates if stomach/intestines remain).
3. **Chill:** Move to shade or cool location if refrigeration unavailable.

**Field evisceration:**

```
1. Place animal on back (use ropes to position).
2. Cut abdomen from breastbone to groin, carefully (do not puncture organs).
3. Remove organs: First, separate intestines and place in separate container (can be used for sausage casings).
4. Remove liver (check for discoloration or abscesses; if present, discard entire liver).
5. Remove heart and lungs (discard unless clearly healthy).
6. Separate offal (heart, kidneys, liver) for separate use or disposal.
7. Rinse body cavity with clean water if available; allow to drain.
```

**Timeline:** Complete evisceration within 30 minutes of slaughter in warm weather (accelerates spoilage).

## Field Butchery and Meat Salvage

**Objective:** Separate edible muscle from bone, hide, and organs; maximize meat yield.

### Basic Anatomy Reference

**Cattle/large animals sections:**

| **Section** | **Location** | **Muscle Quality** | **Use** |
|---|---|---|---|
| Chuck (shoulder) | Front shoulder to 4th rib | Medium; high connective tissue | Stews, ground meat |
| Rib | Ribs + loin over ribs | High quality | Steaks, roasts |
| Short loin (back) | Behind rib to hip | Excellent (tender, flavorful) | Steaks (premium cuts) |
| Sirloin (hip) | Hip joint area | Good | Steaks, roasts |
| Round (rear leg) | Rear quarter below hip | Medium; lean | Roasts, stew meat |
| Breast/brisket | Lower front | Tough; high connective tissue | Braised, ground meat |
| Shank | Lower leg | Very tough; connective tissue/marrow | Bone broth, soup |
| Offal | Organs | Variable | Liver, heart, kidneys edible if healthy |

**Approximate yield:**

- Carcass weight: 60% of live weight (carcass after hide/organs removed).
- Meat yield: 65â€“75% of carcass weight.
- Example: 1,000 lb live cattle â†’ 600 lb carcass â†’ 400 lb meat + 200 lb bone/waste.

### Butchery Process

**Step 1: Hide removal**

Use hide for leather (see tanning guide); if time-critical, skip and bury hide.

**Step 2: Limb separation**

Cut around joints; use minimal knife work (wasting meat). Pull limb back; cut through joint capsule.

```
Forelegs: Cut vertically through shoulder joint; front legs separate with 15â€“20 lb meat.
Rear legs: Cut through hip joint; rear legs separate with 30â€“40 lb meat each.
```

**Step 3: Spine separation**

Cut along backbone; separate ribs from spine.

**Step 4: Muscle separation**

Follow natural muscle seams (anatomy minimizes cutting):

```
Chuck (shoulder): Separate from ribs along natural line; yields 20â€“30 lbs.
Rib section: Remove ribs; separate back strap (tender, premium cut).
Loin: Remove from underneath ribs; separate into steaks or roast.
Round (rear leg): Separate into 3â€“4 parts based on muscle groups.
```

**Timeline:** 30â€“45 minutes for experienced butcher; 1â€“2 hours for inexperienced person. Minimize time (heat = bacterial growth).

### Meat Contamination Prevention

**Sanitation in field conditions:**

- **Wash hands/knife frequently:** Use clean water (boil if unsure).
- **Separate tool usage:** Use one knife for hide, another for meat (prevents hide bacteria on edible meat).
- **Keep meat clean:** Place on clean cloth/leaves; avoid ground contact.
- **Cover meat:** Protect from insects and dust; use cloth or shade.

**Meat inspection for disease:**

- **Discoloration:** Gray-green = bacterial breakdown; discard.
- **Odor:** Sour smell = decomposition; discard.
- **Unusual color:** Red/brown staining in deep muscle = blood oxidation (normal); pale white = possible disease (discard).
- **Lesions:** White spots or abscesses = infection (trim away; if extensive, discard).
- **Liver inspection:** Should be dark red/brown; pale color or spots indicate disease (discard entire organ; muscle may be salvageable).

## Meat Preservation Without Refrigeration

In disaster, refrigeration is unavailable. Preservation methods must be rapid.

### Drying (Jerky)

**Principle:** Remove water; bacteria cannot grow without moisture.

**Process:**

1. **Slice meat:** Cut thin (1/4 inch) along the grain (easier to chew when dry).
2. **Salt application:** Coat each slice heavily with salt (draws out water; preserves).
3. **Drying:** Hang on rope/line in sun and wind; takes 2â€“5 days depending on humidity and temperature.
4. **Test:** Meat is done when it bends but does not snap; moisture has reduced from 70% to 10â€“20%.

**Yield:** 10 lbs fresh meat â†’ 2.5 lbs jerky (75% water loss).

**Shelf life:** 1â€“3 months if kept dry; longer if stored in sealed container.

### Salting (Salt Pork)

**Principle:** Salt prevents bacterial growth; used historically for ship-board preservation.

**Process:**

1. **Cut into chunks:** 2â€“4 inch cubes.
2. **Pack with salt:** Layer meat and salt in barrel or container (salt must contact all surfaces).
3. **Weight down:** Press meat to extract water; cover with cloth.
4. **Storage:** Keep cool and dry; meat will last 3â€“6 months.

**Preparation for eating:** Soak in water for 6â€“12 hours to remove excess salt before cooking.

**Limitation:** High salt content may not be acceptable if fresh water is scarce (salt requires water to rinse/cook).

### Smoking

**Principle:** Smoke deposits preservative compounds (acetic acid, phenols) and dries meat.

**Process:**

1. **Build smoke fire:** Oak, hickory, or other hardwood; avoid softwood (too resinous).
2. **Hang meat:** At height where smoke is warm (~100â€“150Â°F) but animal is not cooking.
3. **Smoke duration:** 6â€“12 hours for light smoking (cold smoking); 1â€“2 days for heavy smoking.
4. **Test:** Meat surface should be brown/tan and firm.

**Shelf life:** 1â€“2 months if meat is also dried; longer if heavily smoked.

**Advantage:** Improves flavor; smoky taste often preferred.

### Rendering (Fat Preservation)

For fatty meats (pork, beef brisket):

1. **Cut fat into small pieces.**
2. **Heat slowly:** Render out liquid fat; strain through cloth.
3. **Cool in containers:** Fat solidifies.
4. **Store:** Sealed container, cool location, lasts 3â€“6 months.

**Uses:** Cooking medium, candle wax, leather conditioning.

### Cold Chain Improvisation

In cold climate or during cool season:

- **Bury in snow:** Wrap meat; bury in snow or ice; insulate with more snow/straw. Lasts weeks if temperature stays below 35Â°F.
- **Spring/stream:** Place wrapped meat in cool flowing water. Current prevents stagnation; temperature stays ~50Â°F even in summer.
- **Root cellar:** If available (cave, buried shelter), natural coolness (50â€“55Â°F) extends shelf life to weeks.

**Duration:**

```
Above 50Â°F: 2â€“3 days before spoilage visible
40â€“50Â°F: 4â€“7 days
30â€“40Â°F: 10â€“14 days
Below 30Â°F (near freezing): Weeksâ€“months
```

## Disease Risk Assessment in Stressed Animals

Disaster stress increases disease in livestock. Some diseases are transmissible to humans; others compromise meat quality.

### Common Post-Disaster Diseases

| **Disease** | **Signs in Animal** | **Risk to Humans** | **Salvage Meat?** |
|---|---|---|---|
| Anthrax (spores) | Sudden death, no visible signs | High (spore infection fatal) | No; discard entire carcass |
| Foot-and-mouth | Blisters on hooves/mouth, lameness | Low (cooking kills virus) | Yes if blisters localized; remove affected tissue |
| Blackleg (gas gangrene) | Swelling, gas crackling under skin, dark discoloration | Low if cooked | Only if lesion limited; trim away affected tissue |
| Septicemia (bloodstream infection) | Fever, lethargy, rapid deterioration | High; toxins in meat | No; discard |
| Pneumonia | Cough, nasal discharge, fever | Low if cooked | Yes; avoid lungs; cook thoroughly |
| Parasites (intestinal) | Thin appearance, rough coat, diarrhea | Low (cooking kills); depends on parasite | Yes; internal organs higher risk; avoid |

### Inspection Checklist

Before consuming salvaged meat:

1. **Smell test:** Sour/rotten smell = discard.
2. **Color test:** Normal meat color (red to dark purple depending on age); gray-green = discard.
3. **Texture:** Firm muscle; slimy surface = discard.
4. **Liver:** Dark red-brown (normal); pale/white/spotted = discard entire organ; muscle may be ok if otherwise healthy.
5. **Bone marrow:** Should be red/pink; white/yellow = possible disease (discard).
6. **Muscle lesions:** White pockets (abscess) = trim away; if extensive throughout, discard entire muscle.

### Cooking Safety

**Temperature requirements (kills most pathogens):**

- **Beef, pork, lamb, goat:** 160Â°F internal temperature minimum.
- **Poultry:** 165Â°F.
- **Game (deer, wild boar):** 160â€“165Â°F.

**Test:** Use clean stick/knife as thermometer (internal color reaches no pink); or cooking time (well-done appearance throughout).

**Boiling:** High heat cooking (soups, stews) at 212Â°F kills most pathogens within 10â€“15 minutes.

## Tools and Equipment

**Minimum tools for field butchery:**

| **Tool** | **Purpose** | **Alternative** |
|---|---|---|
| Large knife (8â€“10 in blade) | Primary butchering | Machete, sharp rock |
| Small knife (4â€“5 in blade) | Detail work, offal removal | - |

**Hygiene:**

- **Wash tools:** After slaughter, before butchering (prevent hide bacteria on meat).
- **Sharpen frequently:** Dull knives require more force, more likely to slip (injury risk).
- **Cover meat:** Cloth or shade prevents insect/dust contamination.

## Example: Salvaging Cattle Herd After Flood

**Scenario:** Flood recedes; 10 cattle remain in enclosure, mixed condition.

**Day 1 assessment:**

- 3 cattle: Healthy, recovered from flood stress.
- 5 cattle: Dehydrated, stressed, but able to stand/eat.
- 2 cattle: One has severe leg injury (cannot stand); one showing signs of respiratory illness (cough, nasal discharge).

**Action plan:**

**Healthy cattle (3):** Feed and water; monitor for illness; keep for breeding/milk if applicable.

**Stressed but salvageable (5):** Provide water (electrolyte if available), forage, shelter. Reassess in 24 hours.

**Severely injured (1):** Cannot recover without veterinary surgery (not available). Decision: Slaughter for meat immediately (before spoilage).

**Ill cattle (1):** Isolate; observe for 12 hours. If condition improves, treat. If worsens (fever >105Â°F, severe respiratory distress), slaughter for meat.

2. **Stun:** Captive bolt to forehead or rifle shot (if trained).
3. **Bleed:** Cut neck; blood drains into container (5â€“10 min).
4. **Hide removal:** (optional; time-critical, skip if necessary)
5. **Eviscerate:** Remove organs within 30 minutes.
6. **Butcher:** Separate into sections (chuck, rib, loin, round, shank).
7. **Preserve:** Dry or salt immediately.
