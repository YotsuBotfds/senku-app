<section id="cold-shock">

## Cold Shock Response (0–3 minutes)

Cold water submersion triggers immediate physiological responses independent of hypothermia.

### Gasp Reflex and Breath-Hold Breakpoint

**Cold shock response:**
1. **Gasp and gasping:** Immersion in water <15°C triggers uncontrollable gasping for air. Facial immersion worsens response.
2. **Hyperventilation:** Respiratory rate spikes to 20–30 breaths/min, reducing blood CO2.
3. **Aspiration hazard:** Water enters mouth and airway; drowning risk high even in shallow water.
4. **Loss of cognitive control:** Panic overrides normal breathing control.

**Breath-hold time:**
- Most humans can hold breath 30–90 seconds voluntarily.
- Cold water immersion reduces this to 5–15 seconds (loss of voluntary control).
- Hyperventilation before immersion does NOT extend breath-hold (myth); it reduces CO2 until syncope occurs underwater.

### Cardiovascular Response

**Diving reflex (mammalian dive response):**
- Facial immersion triggers parasympathetic response: **bradycardia** (slower heart rate), vasoconstriction (blood shunted to vital organs).
- Useful evolutionary mechanism; becomes problematic with panic + elevated heart rate from cold water.

**Initial tachycardia:** Cold water triggers sympathetic response (adrenaline), raising heart rate 20–30 bpm above normal. Arrhythmias possible, especially in pre-existing cardiac disease.

### Immediate Actions

- **If conscious in water:** Do not swim hard (exhaustion risk). Assume survival floating position (HELP or huddle).
- **If face submerged:** Hold breath; surface immediately for controlled breathing.
- **Avoid panic:** Recognize gasp reflex as involuntary. Train breathing control before immersion in cold water.

<section id="swimming-failure">

## Swimming Failure Phase (3–30 minutes)

After cold shock subsides, immersion hypothermia begins, reducing muscle function and cognitive control.

### Mechanism

**Peripheral cooling:**
- Muscles in extremities cool rapidly (hands/feet reach ~10°C within minutes in 4°C water).
- Cold reduces muscle contractility and coordination.
- Lactic acid accumulation; metabolic exhaustion accelerates.

**Core temperature decline:**
- Water removes heat 25× faster than air.
- Core temperature drops 0.1–1°C per minute in very cold water (<4°C).
- Shivering thermogenesis insufficient to maintain core.

**Loss of muscle control:**
- Fine motor control fails first (grip, precise movements).
- Gross motor control degrades (swimming becomes uncoordinated).
- Stumbling and flailing ("cold water immersion shock").

### Swimming Failure Symptoms

- **Stroke inefficiency:** Swimming speed drops 50% in minutes.
- **Incoordination:** Uncontrolled arm/leg movements; can't follow intended path.
- **Cognitive decline:** Difficulty making decisions, confusion, loss of memory.
- **Loss of grip:** Hands numb; can't hold rescue equipment.

### Survival Strategy During Swimming Failure

1. **Assume survival position immediately** (HELP or huddle) rather than attempting to swim.
2. **Avoid vigorous swimming** — increases heat loss and exhaustion.
3. **Conserve energy** — Remain still or move minimally.
4. **Call for help** — If witnesses present, vocalize location.
5. **Tread water minimally** — Only vertical bobbing motion; horizontal swimming wastes energy.

<section id="hypothermia-phases">

## Hypothermia Progression (30+ minutes)

Hypothermia is defined as core body temperature <35°C (95°F). Progression has distinct phases:

<div class="cold-timeline">

### Phase 1: Shivering (32–35°C / 90–95°F)

**Duration:** 30 min to 2 hours (depends on water temp, body composition).

**Symptoms:**
- Intense uncontrollable shivering
- Mild confusion, difficulty concentrating
- Mild tachycardia and tachypnea (elevated breathing)
- Vasoconstriction (pale, bluish lips/extremities)
- Reduced dexterity

**Survival action:** Keep patient awake, prevent further heat loss, consume warm liquids if conscious and able to swallow.

### Phase 2: Decreased Shivering (28–32°C / 82–90°F)

**Duration:** 1–4 hours (variable).

**Symptoms:**
- Shivering may paradoxically decrease (paradoxical undressing: patient removes layers—dangerous).
- Significant confusion, irrational behavior, apathy
- Reduced speech coherence; slurred speech
- Drowsiness; loss of protective reflexes
- Muscle rigidity; slow reflexes

**Survival action:** Assume rescue will occur. Keep patient horizontal (prevent sudden movement, which can trigger cardiac arrhythmias). Gentle rewarming.

### Phase 3: Severe Hypothermia (<28°C / <82°F)

**Duration:** Hours; some survive after extreme hypothermia (8+ hours).

**Symptoms:**
- Extreme confusion, loss of consciousness
- Shivering absent
- Fixed, dilated pupils (may appear dead)
- Bradycardia (heartbeat slow and irregular; may be undetectable)
- Breathing shallow, possibly undetectable
- Muscle rigidity
- **Appearance:** Patient appears dead

**Critical concept — "No one is dead until they are warm and dead":**
Many severely hypothermic patients have survived after hours of apparent death (core temp <20°C) due to neuroprotective effects of cold. Resuscitation has succeeded in cases with no vital signs for 3+ hours.

**Survival action:** Assume patient is alive. Begin gentle rewarming (see rewarming section). Do not apply rough handling or CPR unless clearly indicated.

**Variables affecting survival time:**
- **Body composition:** Fatty tissue insulates; lean individuals cool 30% faster.
- **Age:** Children and elderly cool rapidly; young adults typically slower.

- **Fitness:** Better cardiovascular fitness extends survival slightly.
- **Prior cold exposure:** Acclimatization possible (diving reflex enhancement).

<section id="immersion-positions">

## Immersion Survival Positions

### HELP Position (Heat Escape Lessening Posture)

Used in cold water with lifejacket/buoyancy aid. Minimizes heat loss while maintaining flotation.

**Technique:**
1. **Assume fetal position** while floating on front.
2. **Cross arms over chest** (embrace lifejacket or barrel).
3. **Thighs crossed,** knees to chest as much as possible.
4. **Head back, face clear of water.**

**Advantage:** Reduces heat loss from core body compared to horizontal floating. Suitable for single person.

**Time:** Sustainable indefinitely if buoyancy maintained and no other hazards.

### Huddle Position (Group Survival)

Used when multiple people are immersed in very cold water.

**Technique:**
1. **Three or more people** form tight circle.
2. **Children placed in center** (adults insulate).
3. **Arms around each other's shoulders.**
4. **Torsos touching; faces inward.**
5. **All wear lifejackets or maintain buoyancy.**

**Advantage:** Group body heat shared; heat loss from each individual reduced by 25–50% compared to solo floating.

**Disadvantage:** Requires coordination and multiple people.

**Time:** Extends survival time by 1–2 hours in very cold water.

### Dead Man's Float (Passive)

If exhausted and no lifejacket:

1. **Lie face down in water.**
2. **Hang arms and legs limp.**
3. **Every 10–30 seconds, lift head, breathe, return to face-down.**

**Advantage:** Minimal energy expenditure. Reduces panic.

**Disadvantage:** Risk of aspiration if unconscious; only use if alert.

<section id="rescue">

## Cold Water Rescue Techniques

### Self-Rescue (If Near Shore)

1. **Assume HELP position** — Do not thrash.
2. **Move hands and feet minimally** — Use survival floating for 20–30 min while reviewing escape routes.
3. **Identify exit point** — Nearest shore, obstacle, or debris.
4. **Plan path** — Minimize distance; take longest rest stops in shallow water.
5. **Exit water gradually** — Climb out slowly (prevent post-rescue collapse, see below).

### Assisted Rescue (Bystander or Trained Responder)

**Throw rescue:**
1. **Throw floatable object** (rope, ring buoy, board, log) to victim.
2. **Victim grasps object** and floats passively.
3. **Rescuer pulls to shore** slowly and steadily.

**Wading rescue (if water <chest depth):**
1. **Rescuer extends pole or branch** to victim.
2. **Victim grasps and is guided to shore.**

**Swimming rescue (trained only):**
1. **Approach victim from behind** (exhausted swimmers panic and may pull rescuer under).
2. **Grasp under arms or chin** for tow.
3. **Swim to shore** using side stroke.

**Boat rescue:**
1. **Approach from downwind** (easier maneuvering).
2. **Use boarding ladder** or low gunwale to minimize victim's effort.
3. **Two persons assist** if possible.

### Immobilization After Rescue

**Prevent post-rescue collapse:**
- Do NOT allow victim to stand immediately after rescue.
- Keep supine or semi-reclined (horizontal position).
- Sudden movement or vertical position can trigger cardiovascular collapse ("afterdrop") — delayed fatal arrhythmia after rescue.

</section>

## Rewarming Protocols

Rewarming speed depends on severity of hypothermia and available resources.

<div class="rewarming-steps">

### Mild Hypothermia (32–35°C) — Active Rewarming

**Conscious, shivering, able to generate heat:**

1. **Remove wet clothing** — Evaporative cooling stops only when dry.
2. **Passive external rewarming:** Dry shelter, blankets, warm beverages if alert and able to swallow.
3. **Active external rewarming:** Apply external heat sources (not direct to skin, risk burns):
   - Warm water bottles (not >40°C) to groin, armpits, neck.
   - Heat lamp or radiant heater (maintain distance).
   - Skin-to-skin contact in sleeping bag (controversial; works if both persons thermoregulate well).
4. **Warm fluids:** Sweet drinks, broth, or warm (not hot) water. Avoid alcohol (dilates vessels, worsens heat loss).
5. **Monitoring:** Watch for shivering cessation (sign of deterioration).

**Expected rewarming rate:** 1–2°C per hour.

**Duration:** 2–6 hours to full recovery.

### Moderate Hypothermia (28–32°C) — Gentle Rewarming

**Conscious but confused, minimal shivering:**

1. **Remove wet clothing carefully** — Minimize movement.
2. **Passive external rewarming:** Dry shelter, blankets, warm (not hot) beverages.
3. **Avoid vigorous external heat** — Peripheral vasodilation can cause "afterdrop" (paradoxical core temperature drop).
4. **Monitor vitals:** Heart rate, breathing; watch for arrhythmias.
5. **No CPR unless indicated** — Hold CPR if patient has detectable pulse/breathing.

**Expected rewarming rate:** 0.5–1°C per hour.

**Duration:** 4–12+ hours.

### Severe Hypothermia (<28°C) — Extracorporeal Rewarming (Hospital Only)

**Unconscious, no detectable pulse, fixed pupils:**

**Field management:**
1. **Do not attempt active rewarming** — Risk of afterdrop and arrhythmia.
2. **Keep horizontal** — Minimize movement.
3. **Dry clothing** — Prevent further heat loss.
4. **Gentle transport** to hospital with ECMO/bypass capability.
5. **Assume patient alive** — Resuscitation has succeeded in cases with core temp <20°C after prolonged submersion.

**Hospital management:** ECMO (extracorporeal membrane oxygenation) or venoarterial bypass provides active core rewarming by circulating blood through external warmer. Only available in specialized centers.

**Survival rate:** 50–70% if rewarmed via ECMO, even with prolonged cardiac arrest (up to 8 hours in some cases).

<section id="near-drowning">

## Near-Drowning and Secondary Drowning

**Near-drowning:** Surviving water submersion with respiratory compromise.

### Submersion Incident

**Immediate (0–30 min after rescue):**
- Aspiration (water in lungs) causes hypoxia and pulmonary edema.
- Breath-holding underwater can lead to hypoxic seizures.
- "Dry drowning" — Laryngospasm prevents water entry but also prevents breathing; hypoxia results.

**Symptoms:**
- Coughing, gasping, frothy sputum
- Chest tightness, wheezing
- Agitation, restlessness
- Altered consciousness

### Field Management

1. **Airway:** If unconscious, position on side to drain fluid.
2. **Oxygen:** Administer if available (high-flow oxygen, 10–15 L/min).
3. **Monitor breathing:** Watch for deterioration (respiratory distress, altered voice quality).
4. **Do not remove from water too rapidly** — If severely hypothermic, slow rewarming takes priority over immediate extrication.

### Secondary Drowning (Delayed)

**Pulmonary edema onset 4–48 hours post-rescue:**
- Water in lungs triggers inflammatory response.
- Fluid accumulation worsens hypoxia.
- Can be fatal even if initial rescue successful.

**Warning signs:**
- Progressive difficulty breathing (dyspnea)
- Rales/crackles in lungs on auscultation
- Frothy pink sputum
- Cyanosis
- Altered consciousness

**Management:** Oxygen, positive pressure ventilation (CPAP or intubation), diuretics to reduce pulmonary edema. Hospital care essential.

**Prevention:** Hospitalize all drowning victims for 24–48 hr observation, even if initially alert.

</section>

<section id="ice-rescue">

## Ice Rescue (Cold Water Under Ice)

Submersion under ice is universally fatal without rapid rescue (10–15 min before unconsciousness).

### Prevention

- **Avoid thin ice** — Stay >10 cm thickness minimum (palm-sized hand sinks through thinner ice).
- **Use ice picks** — Specialized picks with chain help self-rescue.
- **Buddy system** — Never cross ice alone.

### Self-Rescue (If Ice Breaks)

1. **Do not panic** — Thrashing accelerates exhaustion.
2. **Reach toward solid ice edge** — Do not climb up; ice likely breaks under pressure.
3. **Kick legs behind** — Create horizontal position to distribute weight.
4. **Slide belly onto ice** — Drag yourself forward using arm strength (legs trailing in water).
5. **Roll away from hole** — Once partially on ice, roll sideways away from opening.
6. **Crawl to shore** — Do not stand; weight spreads over wider area if crawling.

**Time:** 30–60 seconds to escape.

### Assisted Rescue

1. **Do not approach hole directly** — Rescuer may fall through.
2. **Use long pole or ladder** — Extend from solid ice at 45° angle.
3. **Lay ladder or plank flat** — Rescuer lies flat (distributes weight), reaching victim.
4. **Pull victim horizontally** onto ice.

### Submersion Under Ice

If victim goes under ice:
1. **Mark hole location** — Pace along ice edge to estimate drift (current carries underwater body).
2. **Check downstream** — Move 1–2 meters downwind/downstream; often body drifts.
3. **Probe ice** — Use pole to feel for body beneath ice.
4. **Chisel hole** — Break ice in search area to retrieve body.
5. **Resuscitate** — Begin CPR immediately; hypothermia provides neuroprotection (patients have survived 45 min submerged).

**Key:** Even if unresponsive and apparently dead, resuscitate all cold-water submersion victims. Do not declare death until patient is warm.
