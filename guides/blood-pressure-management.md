:::warning
High blood pressure (hypertension) is often asymptomatic but damages vessels and organs over time. Hypertensive crisis (very high BP with symptoms) requires emergency intervention. Low blood pressure can indicate shock or medical emergency. Proper measurement technique is essential for accurate diagnosis.
:::

<section id="measurement">

## Blood Pressure Measurement: The Auscultatory Method

Accurate measurement requires proper technique. Errors are common and lead to misdiagnosis.

### Auscultatory Technique (Gold Standard)

**Equipment needed:**
- Sphygmomanometer (manual cuff + pressure gauge + stethoscope)
- Appropriately-sized cuff (bladder circumference 80% of arm circumference; too small reads high, too large reads low)

**Procedure:**

1. **Patient position:** Sitting upright, arm at heart level, feet flat on floor. Avoid talking during measurement.
2. **Cuff placement:** Wrap cuff snugly around upper arm, ~2 cm above elbow crease. Bladder of cuff over brachial artery.
3. **Palpate pulse:** Find radial pulse (wrist) with two fingers.
4. **Inflate cuff:** Squeeze hand bulb while watching pressure gauge. Inflate to ~20 mmHg above point where radial pulse disappears.
5. **Place stethoscope:** Position earpieces in ears, diaphragm over brachial artery in antecubital fossa (elbow crease).
6. **Slowly deflate:** Open valve to release air slowly (~2–3 mmHg per second). Listen intently.
7. **Record systolic:** Note pressure at which first **Korotkoff sound** (tapping sound) appears. This is systolic pressure.
8. **Record diastolic:** Note pressure at which Korotkoff sounds disappear completely. This is diastolic pressure.
9. **Record BP:** Write as "systolic/diastolic" (e.g., "120/80 mmHg").

**Korotkoff sounds (5 phases):**
- **Phase 1:** First sharp tapping sound (systolic)
- **Phase 2:** Soft whooshing sound
- **Phase 3:** Crisp tapping sound
- **Phase 4:** Muffled, fading sound (diastolic in some populations)
- **Phase 5:** Silence (diastolic in most populations, standard)

### Common Measurement Errors

- **Cuff too small:** Reads falsely high
- **Cuff too large:** Reads falsely low
- **Arm unsupported:** Isometric muscle contraction raises BP artificially
- **Full bladder:** Raises BP slightly
- **Talking during measurement:** Talking raises BP
- **Deflating too fast:** May miss systolic; re-inflate and repeat
- **Deflating too slow:** Causes discomfort; diastolic may artificially rise

**Always measure both arms:** Normal variation <10 mmHg. If >10 mmHg difference, use higher reading; may indicate vascular disease.

### Electronic Blood Pressure Monitors

Automated oscillometric devices are convenient but less accurate than auscultatory method, especially in arrhythmias or severe shock. If available, validate against manual measurement.

<section id="normal-ranges">

## Blood Pressure Ranges: Classification

<div class="bp-ranges">

| Category | Systolic | Diastolic | Action |
|----------|----------|-----------|--------|
| **Optimal** | <120 | <80 | Continue healthy lifestyle |
| **Normal** | 120–129 | <80 | Continue healthy lifestyle; recheck annually |
| **Elevated** | 130–139 | <80 | Lifestyle modifications; recheck in 3 months |
| **Stage 1 HTN** | 140–159 | 90–99 | Lifestyle + medication; recheck in 1–3 months |
| **Stage 2 HTN** | ≥160 | ≥100 | Medication; recheck within 1 month |
| **Hypertensive Crisis** | >180 | >120 | Immediate medical intervention |
| **Hypotension** | <90 | <60 | Assess for shock or medical condition |

**Note:** Only ONE reading is needed to diagnose: If systolic OR diastolic exceeds threshold, patient is in that category.

## Hypertensive Crisis and Urgency

### Hypertensive Urgency

**Definition:** BP >180/120 without symptoms or end-organ damage.

**Management:**
- Do NOT treat acutely (lowering BP too fast risks stroke)
- Recheck BP in 5–10 min (may be white-coat effect; elevated from stress)
- If persistently elevated: Initiate or increase regular antihypertensive medication
- Follow up with physician within 24 hours

### Hypertensive Emergency (Crisis)

**Definition:** BP >180/120 WITH symptoms of end-organ damage.

**Symptoms (organ damage signs):**
- **Neurologic:** Severe headache, confusion, seizures, stroke symptoms
- **Cardiac:** Chest pain, dyspnea, acute coronary syndrome
- **Renal:** Hematuria (blood in urine)
- **Retinal:** Vision changes, visual loss
- **Pulmonary:** Dyspnea, orthopnea (shortness of breath when lying down)

**Management:**
1. **Immediate evaluation:** Assess for end-organ damage (neuro exam, chest exam, urinalysis if available)
2. **IV access:** Establish large-bore IV
3. **Antihypertensive medication (if available):**
   - **Labetalol:** IV 20 mg bolus, repeat every 10 min (max 80 mg per dose)
   - **Nitroglycerin:** SL nitrate tablet (0.3–0.6 mg), repeat every 5 min; or IV nitroprusside (requires ICU)
   - **Nifedipine:** Immediate-release 10 mg SL (controversial; variable absorption)
4. **Target:** Lower BP by ~10% in first hour (avoid excessive rapid lowering, which worsens stroke/MI risk)
5. **Hospital transport:** Hypertensive emergency requires ICU-level monitoring and titrated antihypertensive therapy

**Prognosis:** With prompt treatment, most hypertensive emergencies resolve without permanent organ damage. Delays increase risk of stroke, MI, or kidney damage.

<section id="antihypertensives">

## Antihypertensive Medications

### Pharmaceutical Options (If Available)

| Class | Example | Mechanism | Dose | Side Effects |
|-------|---------|-----------|------|--------------|
| **ACE inhibitor** | Lisinopril | Blocks angiotensin II | 10–40 mg daily | Dry cough, hyperkalemia |
| **ARB** | Losartan | Blocks angiotensin II receptor | 50–100 mg daily | Hyperkalemia, dizziness |
| **Beta-blocker** | Metoprolol | Reduces heart rate/contractility | 50–100 mg daily | Fatigue, bradycardia, bronchospasm |
| **Calcium channel blocker** | Nifedipine, amlodipine | Dilates vessels | 30–60 mg daily (extended-release) | Headache, edema, constipation |
| **Thiazide diuretic** | Hydrochlorothiazide | Increases sodium/water excretion | 12.5–25 mg daily | Hypokalemia, hyperglycemia, gout |

**Combination therapy:** Often requires 2–3 drugs for adequate control.

<section id="lifestyle-modification">

## Lifestyle Modification: Non-Pharmacologic Management

Lifestyle changes can lower BP 10–20 mmHg and prevent progression to medication.

### Dietary Interventions

**DASH diet (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension):**
- **Emphasize:** Vegetables, fruits, whole grains, lean proteins, low-fat dairy
- **Avoid:** Processed foods, red meat, high-fat dairy
- **Effect:** 8–14 mmHg reduction in BP

**Sodium restriction:**
- **Target:** <2300 mg/day (1 teaspoon salt = 2300 mg sodium)
- **Strict limit:** <1500 mg/day for maximal effect
- **Sources to avoid:** Processed foods, canned goods, bread, cheese, deli meats
- **Effect:** 5–10 mmHg reduction in sensitive individuals

**Potassium and magnesium:**
- **Potassium:** Counteracts sodium; increases vasodilation. Target 3500–4700 mg/day (found in bananas, potatoes, leafy greens, beans)
- **Magnesium:** Relaxes vascular smooth muscle. Target 400–500 mg/day (found in nuts, seeds, whole grains, leafy greens)
- **Effect:** 4–8 mmHg reduction

### Physical Activity

- **Target:** 150 min moderate aerobic activity weekly (brisk walking, cycling, swimming)
- **Resistance training:** 2–3 sessions/week
- **Effect:** 5–7 mmHg reduction

### Weight Loss

- **Target:** 5–10% body weight reduction
- **Effect:** 2.5–5 mmHg per 5 kg lost

### Stress Reduction

- **Relaxation techniques:** Deep breathing, meditation, yoga
- **Effect:** 2–5 mmHg reduction

### Alcohol Limitation

- **Limit:** <1 drink/day (females), <2 drinks/day (males)
- **Excess alcohol** raises BP; moderation lowers it

### Smoking Cessation

- **Smoking doesn't directly raise chronic BP** but accelerates vascular damage
- **Essential** for cardiovascular health

**Cumulative effect:** Combining multiple lifestyle modifications can lower BP 30+ mmHg, equivalent to medication.

</section>

<section id="herbal-antihypertensives">

## Plant-Based Antihypertensives

In austere settings without pharmaceuticals, herbs may provide modest BP reduction.

### Garlic (Allium sativum)

- **Active compound:** Allicin (sulfur compound)
- **Mechanism:** Vasodilatory; slight ACE inhibitor effect
- **Preparation:** Fresh garlic (1–2 cloves daily) or powder
- **Effect:** 5–10 mmHg reduction (modest; variable efficacy)
- **Caution:** Anticoagulant effect; avoid if bleeding risk

### Hawthorn (Crataegus spp.)

- **Mechanism:** Improves cardiac contractility; vasodilatory
- **Preparation:** Dried berries or leaves; simmer in water 5–10 min, drink as tea
- **Typical dose:** 1–2 cups daily
- **Effect:** 5–8 mmHg reduction
- **Caution:** May interact with cardiac medications; use cautiously if on other heart drugs

### Hibiscus (Hibiscus sabdariffa)

- **Mechanism:** ACE inhibitor-like effects; diuretic
- **Preparation:** Dried calyces; steep in hot water for tea
- **Typical dose:** 1–3 cups daily
- **Effect:** 7–10 mmHg reduction (shown in some trials)
- **Caution:** Can cause urinary frequency (diuretic effect)

### Omega-3 Fatty Acids (Fish Oil, Flaxseed)

- **Mechanism:** Anti-inflammatory; improves endothelial function
- **Sources:** Fatty fish (salmon, mackerel), flaxseeds, walnuts
- **Typical dose:** 2–3 g daily (fish) or 1–2 T ground flaxseed
- **Effect:** 2–4 mmHg reduction

### Celery (Apium graveolens)

- **Mechanism:** Contains phthalides (vasodilatory compounds)
- **Preparation:** Eat fresh or drink juice
- **Typical dose:** 1–2 stalks daily or small glass juice
- **Effect:** Modest; 5–10 mmHg (variable)

**Note:** Herbal antihypertensives are slower-acting than pharmaceuticals and effect more variable. Suitable for prevention/mild HTN, not emergency management.

## Hypotension and Shock Differential

### Hypotension

**Definition:** Systolic BP <90 mmHg or MAP (mean arterial pressure) <65 mmHg.

**Difference between low BP + asymptomatic vs. hypotensive shock:**
- **Chronic low BP (hypotension):** Some individuals naturally have low-normal BP (e.g., athletes). If asymptomatic and perfusing well, not dangerous.
- **Acute hypotension/shock:** Sudden drop in BP causing inadequate organ perfusion. Presents with symptoms.

### Shock Types and Recognition

| Shock Type | Cause | BP | Heart Rate | Skin | Mental Status |
|-----------|-------|----|----|------|--------------|
| **Hypovolemic** | Blood loss, dehydration | Low | High | Cold, clammy | Anxious, then lethargic |
| **Cardiogenic** | Heart failure, MI | Low | High | Cold, clammy | Confused |
| **Septic** | Infection | Low (or initially high) | High | Warm, flushed (early) or cold (late) | Confused, disoriented |
| **Anaphylactic** | Allergic reaction | Very low | Very high | Flushed, then pale | Confused, loss of consciousness |

### Management of Hypotensive Shock

**Immediate actions:**
1. **Lay patient flat:** Elevate legs ~30° (improves venous return)
2. **Large-bore IV access:** ×2 if possible
3. **Identify cause:** Blood loss? Infection? Allergic reaction? Cardiac event?
4. **Rapid fluid resuscitation:**
   - **Hypovolemic shock:** Normal saline bolus 1–2 liters over 10–20 min; repeat if BP not improving
   - **Septic shock:** Similar fluid bolus, then antibiotics
   - **Anaphylactic:** Epinephrine FIRST (see anaphylaxis guide); fluids second
   - **Cardiogenic:** Cautious fluids (overload worsens pulmonary edema); needs vasopressor support
5. **Treat underlying cause:** Control bleeding, antibiotics for infection, epinephrine for anaphylaxis

**Prognosis:** Shock is immediately life-threatening; mortality increases with delay. Survival depends on rapid recognition and treatment of underlying cause.

## Improvised Sphygmomanometer Construction

In austere settings without commercial BP cuff, improvised measurement is possible (less accurate, but functional).

### Simple Palpatory Method (No Cuff Needed)

1. **Find radial pulse** at wrist with index/middle finger
2. **Apply gentle pressure** until pulse disappears
3. **Note approximate pressure** at which pulse disappears (this is approximate systolic)
4. **Limitation:** Does not measure diastolic; less accurate

**Accuracy:** ±20 mmHg; suitable for trending BP changes, not diagnosing hypertension.

### Improvised Cuff + Pressure Measurement

**Materials needed:**
- Elastic material (bicycle inner tube, elastic band)
- Transparent tube or bag (vinyl, plastic)
- Ruler or graduated measuring device
- Water-filled column for pressure (see below)

**Construction:**
1. **Make cuff:** Wrap elastic around upper arm to form bladder
2. **Attach tubing:** Connect to pressure vessel or manometer

### DIY Manometer (Water Column Method)

1. **Clear tube:** Transparent plastic tube (aquarium air line, or drip tubing)
2. **Fill with water:** Vertical tube filled with water
3. **Calibrate:** Water rises in tube; each 1 cm height ≈ 7.4 mmHg pressure
4. **Formula:** Height (cm) × 7.4 = pressure (mmHg)
5. **Mark scale:** Write mmHg measurements along tube using marker

**Procedure:**
1. Inflate cuff while monitoring pressure (water height)
2. Use stethoscope at brachial artery
3. Record systolic + diastolic as water level drops

**Accuracy:** ±10 mmHg (reasonable for field measurement).

**Alternative:** If no materials available, palpatory method (pulse disappearance) gives systolic estimate only.
