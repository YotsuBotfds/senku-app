## Overview: Oral Anatomy in Survival Context

The oral cavity is one of the most densely innervated regions of the human body, with 5-10 times more sensory nerve endings per unit area than fingertips. Oral infections rank among the most dangerous in low-resource settings due to rapid spread toward the brain and airway. This guide covers tooth and jaw anatomy essential for identifying pathology, performing emergency extractions, managing infections, and providing pain relief in survival scenarios.

Use this guide for structure, terminology, and context. Use `preventive-dental-hygiene` for routine care, `dental-prosthetics` for fit or appliance questions, and `emergency-dental` for swelling, infection, or trauma.

**Prevalence:** Globally, 3.6 billion people suffer from untreated caries; 1 billion experience severe periodontal disease. In survival situations without preventive care, oral infection emerges within 2-4 weeks of onset.

**Urgency Levels:**
- **Immediate (0-6 hours):** Facial swelling with airway compromise, uncontrolled bleeding post-extraction, acute periorbital cellulitis
- **Urgent (6-24 hours):** Severe abscess with fever >38.5°C, spreading cellulitis, difficulty swallowing
- **Delayed (24-72 hours):** Moderate pain with no systemic signs, cracked teeth, displaced but vital teeth
- **Preventive (>72 hours):** Routine cavity assessment, periodontal maintenance, tooth preservation strategies

:::info-box
Survival Fact: A single untreated infected tooth has killed individuals through airway obstruction or sepsis in 5-14 days without antibiotic access. Early recognition and decisive action prevent death.
:::

## Tooth Structure and Types

Teeth are complex organs with multiple layers, each serving distinct functions. Understanding anatomy is crucial for diagnosis, treatment, and emergency care.

### Tooth Anatomy Layers

:::card
-   **Enamel:** Outermost protective layer, translucent white, hardest substance in body (96% mineral). Non-living, cannot regenerate. Thickness 0.5-2.5mm at crown margin.
-   **Dentin:** Yellow layer beneath enamel, softer than enamel, contains tubules with fluid. Living tissue with sensory sensation. Exposure causes sensitivity.
-   **Pulp Chamber:** Central cavity containing blood vessels and nerves. Pulp extends from crown (pulp chamber) to root tip (apical foramen).
-   **Cementum:** Covers root surface, lighter yellow than dentin. Softer than enamel, resorbs with age. Periodontal ligament attaches here.
-   **Periodontal Ligament:** Suspensory ligament connecting cementum to bone. 200-500 micrometers thick. Contains nerve fibers for proprioception.
-   **Alveolar Bone:** Socket bone supporting tooth. Density varies; density indicates health and healing capacity.
:::

### Crown vs Root Ratio

Clinical crown extends from gingival margin to occlusal surface. Root extends from gingival margin to apex. Ideal ratio 1:1 or slightly favor crown. Recession exposes root (darker, softer, sensitive). Short clinical crown reduces retention for restorations.

:::warning
**Root Exposure Risk:** Exposed root (cementum) decays 4-5 times faster than crown enamel and erodes with acidic foods. Survival priority: prevent further recession through gentle hygiene and dietary modification.
:::

## Numbering Systems

### Universal Numbering System (FDI)

International standard using two digits. First digit indicates quadrant (1-4 permanent, 5-8 primary). Second digit is tooth position (1-8 for permanent, 1-5 for primary). Quadrants: 1=upper right, 2=upper left, 3=lower left, 4=lower right. Examples: tooth 11 = upper right central incisor, tooth 46 = lower right first molar.

### Palmer Notation

Uses symbols (┘└ for lower, ┐┌ for upper) with numbers 1-8 from midline. Historical, less used in modern practice but important for records.

### Clinical Significance

Accurate numbering critical for treatment planning, patient communication, medical records, emergency identification, and treatment sequencing in survival scenarios.

## Jaw Anatomy and Temporomandibular Joint (TMJ)

### Maxilla (Upper Jaw)

Fixed bone forming roof of mouth and upper face. Articulates with 13 other bones. Contains maxillary sinuses (air-filled cavities above teeth). Palate divides oral from nasal cavity. Hard palate is bony anteriorly, soft palate posteriorly. Incisive foramen enters nasal cavity, allows superior alveolar nerve passage.

### Mandible (Lower Jaw)

Only movable bone of skull. U-shaped, horseshoe configuration when viewed from above. Condyles (two rounded processes) articulate with temporal bone. Coronoid processes provide muscle attachment. Body is thickest, strongest part. Angle (posterior corner) receives masseter muscle. Mental foramen (chin area) exits inferior alveolar nerve.

### Temporomandibular Joint (TMJ)

Complex hinge and sliding joint between mandibular condyle and temporal bone. Disc of fibrocartilage separates articular surfaces. Allows three motions: opening/closing (hinge), protrusion/retrusion (forward/backward), and lateral movement. Supported by ligaments and muscles. Normal opening 35-50mm interincisal distance. Posterior mandibular height: 50-70mm from lower border to occlusal surface.

**TMJ Disorders:** Pain with function, restricted opening, clicking/popping, muscle tenderness. Causes include trauma, malocclusion, stress, grinding (bruxism). Management: soft diet, NSAIDs (ibuprofen 400-600mg every 6-8 hours, max 2400mg/day), warm compresses (15-20 minutes, 3-4 times daily), jaw rest, muscle relaxation techniques.

:::tip
**TMJ Recovery Timeline:** Acute TMJ pain typically resolves in 3-7 days with conservative management. If pain persists beyond 2 weeks or opening remains <30mm, suspect disc displacement or fracture and immobilize jaw with soft diet.
:::

### Masticatory Muscles

-   **Masseter:** Closes jaw, strongest muscle (can exert 200+ lbs force)
-   **Temporalis:** Elevates mandible, adds retraction
-   **Medial Pterygoid:** Assists jaw closure
-   **Lateral Pterygoid:** Opens jaw, protrudes mandible, aids disc movement

### Major Salivary Glands

### Saliva Composition and Functions

-   **Amylase:** Enzyme initiating carbohydrate digestion
-   **Lysozyme:** Antibacterial protein, immune defense
-   **IgA:** Immunoglobulin providing specific immunity
-   **Mucins:** Glycoproteins providing lubrication and protection
-   **Calcium & Phosphate:** Remineralize enamel, prevent decay
-   **pH Buffering:** Bicarbonate neutralizes acids, protective

### Xerostomia (Dry Mouth)

Reduced saliva production causes increased decay, candidiasis, difficulty swallowing, burning sensation. Normal resting saliva flow 0.3-0.5 mL/min; stimulated flow 1-3 mL/min. Xerostomia occurs at <0.1 mL/min resting flow. Causes: medication, radiation, dehydration, Sjögren's syndrome, stress. Management: frequent sips of water (250-500 mL every 2 hours), sugar-free gum (5-15 minutes, 3-4 times daily), saliva substitutes, increased humidity, glycerin swabs (apply to oral mucosa 2-3 times daily).

:::warning
**Dehydration + Dry Mouth Crisis:** In survival situations, dehydration dramatically accelerates dental decay and candidiasis. Prioritize water intake and oral hygiene; even 500mL water daily prevents catastrophic mouth degradation.
:::

## Common Oral Pathologies

### Dental Caries (Cavity)

Bacterial acid attack on enamel/dentin. Streptococcus mutans primary pathogen. Demineralization begins within minutes of sugar exposure; remineralization possible if fluoride/calcium present. Progression: pit/fissure formation → enamel penetration → dentin involvement → pulp exposure. Risk factors: poor hygiene, frequent sugar consumption, low saliva, acidic foods.

### Periodontal Disease

Gingival inflammation (gingivitis) progressing to bone loss (periodontitis). Red, swollen, bleeding gums are early signs. Anaerobic bacteria colonize periodontal pocket. Untreated leads to tooth mobility, abscess, eventual tooth loss. Plaque removal and antimicrobial rinses halt early disease; advanced cases need professional care.

### Endodontic Infection (Pulpitis/Abscess)

Pulp inflammation from decay, trauma, or crack. Sharp pain on chewing (8-10/10 severity), sensitivity to temperature lasting >30 seconds after stimulus removed. **Reversible pulpitis:** mild pain, responds to anti-inflammatory treatment. **Irreversible pulpitis:** severe pain, spontaneous episodes, requires root canal or extraction. Abscess forms at root tip within 3-7 days of pulp necrosis; presents with swelling, facial cellulitis risk, fever >38.5°C.

:::danger
**Spreading Periorbital Cellulitis Signs:** Swelling of eyelid, chemosis (conjunctival swelling), vision changes, fever, or inability to close eye = hospital emergency. Brain abscess risk increases daily. Extract tooth immediately and start IV antibiotics if available. Position airway securely.
:::

Emergency treatment: drainage, antibiotics (amoxicillin 500mg every 8 hours × 7 days, or clindamycin 300-450mg every 6-8 hours if penicillin-allergic), extraction if root canal impossible in survival scenario.

### Cracked Tooth Syndrome

Crack from trauma, large restoration, chewing hard objects. Pain on release (not pressure). Partial cracks may only cause pain if they reach pulp. Crack line propagates with each chew. Management: avoid chewing on that side, bonded restoration to stabilize crack, or extraction if pulp damaged.

### Bruxism (Teeth Grinding)

Unconscious grinding/clenching, often stress-related. Wears enamel, causes muscle pain, TMJ dysfunction. Night guard provides mechanical barrier. Stress management, muscle relaxation, avoiding stimulants essential.

### Erosion vs Attrition

**Erosion:** Chemical (acid) loss of enamel from GERD, acidic foods/drinks, eating disorders. Smooth, rounded appearance. **Attrition:** Mechanical wear from grinding or overaggressive brushing. Sharp, faceted wear patterns. Both cause sensitivity and require management to halt progression.

## Clinical Assessment Methods

### Recognition & Assessment Criteria

<table><thead><tr><th>Finding</th><th>Assessment Technique</th><th>Clinical Significance</th><th>Survival Action</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Tooth Color</td><td>Visual inspection in daylight</td><td>Yellowing = dentin exposed, dark gray = pulp death</td><td>Monitor for abscess; avoid extraction if vital</td></tr><tr><td>Cavitation</td><td>Visual + explorer (catches on pit)</td><td>Discoloration, pitting = progression toward pulp</td><td>Arrest decay immediately; fill if possible</td></tr><tr><td>Gum Appearance</td><td>Visual inspection</td><td>Pale = poor circulation, red = inflammation, dark = necrosis</td><td>Red gums + swelling = extract soon; dark = infection spreading</td></tr><tr><td>Swelling/Drainage</td><td>Visual + palpation</td><td>Pus/swelling = active infection/abscess</td><td>Immediate: drainage required, antibiotics critical</td></tr><tr><td>Mobility Grade I</td><td>Grasp tooth, move side-to-side</td><td><1mm movement = early bone loss</td><td>Monitor; may still be salvageable</td></tr><tr><td>Mobility Grade III</td><td>Tooth moves >2mm or vertically</td><td>Advanced bone loss or acute trauma</td><td>Extract within 24 hours; pain management essential</td></tr></tbody></table>

### Visual Examination

-   Assess tooth color (yellowing indicates dentin exposure or staining; gray = pulp death)
-   Look for cavities (discoloration, pitting, rough texture)
-   Check gum color (pale=unhealthy, red=inflamed, dark=necrotic, dark red=serious infection)
-   Note any swelling or drainage (pus indicates abscess)
-   Inspect for cracks or fractures
-   Evaluate alignment and occlusion

### Tactile Examination

-   Use dental explorer or sharpened stick to detect cavities (catches on defects)
-   Probe periodontal pockets with blunt probe (depth >5mm indicates disease)
-   Gently tap teeth (percussion) to assess vital response and pain
-   Palpate jaw structures for masses, asymmetry, tenderness
-   Check TMJ by palpating condyles during opening/closing (clicking = disc displacement)

:::tip
**Field Assessment Without Tools:** Press tooth firmly with finger; sharp pain on release (not pressure) = cracked tooth. Tap with spoon handle; pain = periapical infection. Slow gentle movement <1mm = early disease; >2mm mobility = extract.
:::

### Thermal Testing

Cold stimulus (ice chips, cold water, ethyl chloride if available) indicates vital pulp if sensation detected within 5-10 seconds. Lack of response suggests necrotic pulp or pulp damage. Exaggerated response (pain lasts >30 seconds after stimulus) indicates pulpitis (inflammation).

### Percussion Response

Light tapping on tooth crown using spoon handle or small hammer. Pain indicates periapical inflammation or periodontal involvement. Helps differentiate pulpal from periapical pathology. Sharp pain = periapical; dull ache = periodontal.

### Mobility Assessment

**Grade I:** <1mm horizontal movement = early-stage periodontal disease
**Grade II:** 1-2mm horizontal movement = moderate bone loss (≥50% attachment loss)
**Grade III:** >2mm or vertical mobility = advanced disease or trauma
Mobility indicates bone loss or trauma. Record baseline and monitor every 24-48 hours in survival setting.

## Equipment & Materials

**Essential Supplies (improvised alternatives in parentheses):**
- Sterile gloves (clean cloth wraps)
- Gauze/cotton (cloth strips, plant fiber)
- Topical anesthetic: lidocaine 2% or benzocaine spray (clove oil, ice)
- Ibuprofen 400-600mg tablets (willow bark, tree bark teas)
- Amoxicillin 500mg or clindamycin 300mg (honey, turmeric paste as antimicrobial)
- Dental explorer/probe (sharpened stick, thin metal)
- Extraction forceps (not improvised—critical tool if available)
- Saline solution (salt water: 1/2 teaspoon salt per 8oz water)
- Temporary filling material (zinc oxide eugenol paste, beeswax, clay-based compounds)
- Splinting material for displaced teeth (composite, wire, dental floss, cloth tape)
- Gauze packing (cloth, plant fiber, honey-soaked)

## Emergency Procedures in Survival Scenarios

### Step-by-Step Pain Relief Protocol

**1. Initial Assessment (0-5 minutes)**
- Determine pain severity (1-3 = mild, 4-6 = moderate, 7-10 = severe)
- Identify tooth location and symptoms
- Check for swelling, fever, difficulty swallowing
- Assess airway status

**2. Immediate Topical Treatment**
- Rinse mouth with warm salt water (1/2 tsp salt per 8oz water, repeat every 2 hours)
- Apply topical clove oil directly to tooth/gum (eugenol provides 1-2 hours anesthesia)
- Apply cold compress to outside of cheek (15-20 minutes, repeat every 2 hours for first 24 hours)

**3. Systemic Pain Management**
- **NSAIDs (first choice if no contraindications):** Ibuprofen 400-600mg every 6-8 hours (max 2400mg/day), or Naproxen 220mg every 8-12 hours (max 660mg/day)
- **Acetaminophen (alternative):** 500-1000mg every 4-6 hours (max 3000mg/day)
- Take with food to prevent gastric upset
- Continue for 7-10 days if abscess present

**4. Recovery Positioning**
- Elevate head 30-45 degrees to reduce facial swelling
- Avoid hot foods/drinks for first 48 hours
- Restrict diet to soft foods; avoid chewing on affected side

:::warning
**Pain That Worsens Despite Treatment:** If pain escalates to 9-10/10 despite maximal ibuprofen dosing within 12 hours, assume irreversible pulpitis or abscess progression. Extraction becomes necessary to prevent sepsis. Delay increases infection spread risk exponentially.
:::

### Abscess Drainage Protocol

**Diagnostic Signs:** Facial swelling (often unilateral), pus/discharge from gingival margin, severe pain localized to one tooth, possible fever.

**Procedure:**
1. Rinse mouth thoroughly with warm salt water
2. Locate point of maximal fluctuance (soft, moveable swelling)
3. If pus visible at surface, gently encourage drainage by pressing swollen area with sterile gauze—do NOT crush tissue
4. Apply warm compresses (15-20 min, 3-4 times daily) to encourage continued drainage
5. Rinse with salt water after each drainage episode
6. Monitor for airway compromise if swelling extends to floor of mouth or extends bilaterally

:::danger
**Rapid Airway Compromise Warning:** Swelling of floor of mouth, difficulty swallowing saliva, muffled speech, or neck stiffness = Ludwig's angina (life-threatening cellulitis). Lay patient flat, clear airway, prepare for emergency airway management. Antibiotics must start immediately: Clindamycin 600mg IV every 6 hours or Ampicillin-sulbactam 3g IV every 6 hours.
:::

**Post-Drainage Antibiotics:** Essential even if drainage successful. Amoxicillin 500mg every 8 hours × 7-10 days, or Clindamycin 300-450mg every 6 hours × 7-10 days (if penicillin-allergic).

### Fractured Tooth Management

**Immediate (0-30 minutes):**
1. Locate tooth fragment if possible (wrap in saline-soaked gauze if found)
2. Rinse tooth gently under cool water (do NOT touch root surface)
3. Assess fracture depth: above gumline (enamel/dentin) vs below (pulp likely involved)

**For Enamel-Dentin Fracture (no pulp exposure):**
- Smooth sharp edges with fine file or sandpaper if available
- Apply topical fluoride if available to exposed dentin
- Avoid hard chewing; diet of soft foods for 2-4 weeks
- Monitor for sensitivity; if pain develops, assume pulp inflammation

**For Pulp Exposure (bleeding from center):**
- DO NOT probe or touch exposed pulp
- Rinse very gently with cool water
- Apply calcium hydroxide paste or zinc oxide eugenol if available
- Cover with temporary filling (beeswax, clay-based compound)
- Pain will develop within 12-48 hours; plan extraction or root canal attempt
- Monitor for abscess formation (swelling, fever within 3-7 days)

:::tip
**Saving Fractured Segments:** Locate fragment and reattach if fracture is horizontal and fragment is <1/3 crown. Etch edges with acid (if available), apply adhesive/composite, and splint. Most effective within 4 hours of fracture.
:::

### Displacement/Luxation Management

**Immediate (within 5-10 minutes):**
1. Do NOT touch root surface; grasp by crown only
2. Rinse tooth gently under cool water
3. Reposition to original location immediately (suturing to socket preferred, but bite on gauze acceptable)
4. Bite on gauze/cloth for 30 minutes to hold position

**Immobilization (first 7-14 days critical):**
- Splint with temporary bonded material (composite, wires, dental floss looped around adjacent teeth)
- Restrict diet to liquids/soft foods for 2 weeks
- Avoid touching or wiggling tooth
- If splinting impossible, extraction may be only viable option

**Pain Management:**
- NSAIDs as above (ibuprofen 600mg every 6 hours)
- Avoid ice; use warm compresses instead

**Monitoring (observe every 24 hours):**
- Test for nerve damage: light touch sensation should be present
- Assess tooth color (gray = pulp death likely; extract within 7-14 days)
- Check for infection signs (swelling, discharge, fever)
- Progressive mobility = extraction indicated

:::warning
**Tooth Survival Factors After Luxation:** Prognosis declines rapidly with time. Within 5-30 minutes post-injury = 80% survival chance. 30-60 minutes = 50-60% survival. >4 hours = <10% survival. Extract and preserve root for potential replantation later if professional care becomes available.
:::

## Complications & When to Stop

### Life-Threatening Danger Signs

:::danger
**IMMEDIATE AIRWAY EMERGENCY:** Any of the following requires patient repositioning, airway clearance, and consideration of emergency tracheostomy:
- Inability to swallow saliva
- Stridor (high-pitched breathing)
- Neck/submental swelling that restricts mouth opening
- Cyanosis or severe respiratory distress
- Loss of gag reflex or drooping of soft palate

**SEPSIS PROGRESSION:** Fever >39°C, tachycardia >120/min, altered mental status, rapid spreading of facial swelling = systemic infection. Antibiotics alone insufficient; extraction usually required. Death from oral sepsis can occur within 7-14 days without intervention.
:::

### Complications During Treatment

**Uncontrolled Bleeding Post-Extraction:**
- Apply pressure with sterile gauze for 10-15 minutes continuously
- Replace gauze without disturbing clot if bleeding continues
- If still bleeding after 30 minutes: suspect coagulopathy or arterial damage
- Rinse with ice-cold water, pack tightly with gauze, bite for 30 minutes more
- If bleeding persists: underlying bleeding disorder likely; restrict activity, monitor for shock

**Dry Socket (Alveolar Osteitis):**
- Occurs 2-4 days post-extraction
- Presents with throbbing pain in extraction site, foul odor, visible bone
- Cause: premature clot dissolution, smoking, poor hemostasis
- Treatment: rinse gently, pack with medicated gauze (eugenol), NSAIDs, warm salt water rinses after day 3

**Root Fragment Left Behind:**
- If tooth breaks during extraction and fragment remains in socket
- Monitor for fever, persistent pain, or drainage
- Small fragments (1-2mm) may be left if removal causes excessive damage
- Document location if possible for future professional retrieval
- Large fragments (>3mm) should be removed to prevent chronic abscess

:::warning
**Nerve Damage Risk:** Extraction of lower molars risks inferior alveolar nerve damage (numbness of lower lip, chin, lower teeth on affected side). Damage usually temporary (2-8 weeks) but can be permanent (5-10% of difficult extractions). Most important: avoid excessive force and use proper angulation.
:::

## Recovery & Aftercare

### Post-Extraction Recovery Timeline

**Hours 0-6 (Acute Phase):**
- Bite on gauze continuously for 30-45 minutes post-extraction
- Apply ice to outside of face (20 minutes on, 10 minutes off × 6 hours)
- Head elevated 30-45 degrees, minimal talking
- Soft diet (broth, yogurt, mashed food), cool drinks
- No rinsing, spitting, or touching extraction site
- Monitor for excessive bleeding (should slow within 15-30 minutes)

**Days 1-3 (Inflammation Phase):**
- Continue NSAIDs: ibuprofen 400-600mg every 6 hours
- Swelling peaks at 48-72 hours (normal; indicates healing response)
- Gentle warm salt water rinses starting Day 2 (after meals, 4-6 times daily)
- Soft diet continued; avoid hot foods
- Monitor temperature daily (fever >38.5°C = infection; start/restart antibiotics)
- Sleep with head elevated on 2-3 pillows

**Days 4-10 (Early Healing Phase):**
- Reduce ice application; switch to warm compresses if swelling persists
- Continue salt water rinses (warm, 1/2 tsp salt per 8oz, 3-4 times daily)
- Gradually return to normal diet as comfort allows
- Monitor for dry socket symptoms (sharp pain starting day 3-4)
- If antibiotics prescribed, complete full course even if feeling better

**Weeks 2-12 (Late Healing Phase):**
- Bone fills extraction socket over 3-6 months; gingival healing mostly complete by 3 weeks
- Avoid hard/crunchy foods over extraction site until full integration (~12 weeks)
- Gently brush adjacent teeth; avoid site until gingival surface heals
- Return to normal diet after 3 weeks if no complications

:::info-box
**Healing Milestones:** Gingival epithelialization = 7-14 days. Bone formation begins immediately but takes 3-6 months to complete. Avoid strenuous exercise for first week (increases bleeding risk). Smoking delays healing by 50%; absolute contraindication.
:::

### Monitoring for Deterioration

**Signs Indicating Infection (Requires Antibiotics):**
- Fever >38.5°C (101.3°F)
- Increased swelling after day 3
- Purulent drainage (pus)
- Red streaking up arm or neck
- Lymph node swelling below jaw (regional lymphadenitis)
- Foul odor from extraction site after day 2

**Signs Indicating Systemic Infection (Critical):**
- Fever >39°C (102.2°F)
- Confusion, disorientation
- Difficulty swallowing saliva
- Bilateral swelling (both sides of face/neck)
- Rapid spread of swelling over 4-6 hours
- Shortness of breath

## Troubleshooting: When Treatment Isn't Working

### Pain Persists Despite NSAIDs + Ice (>24 hours)

**Likely Cause:** Irreversible pulpitis or early abscess
**Check For:** Swelling? Fever? Pus drainage?
**Action:**
- If no swelling/fever: continue NSAIDs (increase to 600mg ibuprofen every 6 hours, total 2400mg/day max); try topical clove oil
- If swelling present: begin antibiotics (amoxicillin 500mg × 3 daily) + NSAIDs; monitor for spread
- If pain worsens despite all measures: extraction likely necessary within 24-48 hours

**Recovery Timeline:** 2-3 weeks post-extraction for return to normal function

### Swelling Spreads Despite Antibiotics

**Critical Decision Point:** Antibiotics alone failing; tooth source must be eliminated
**Immediate Actions:**
- Start ibuprofen 600mg every 6 hours to reduce swelling/pain
- Extract affected tooth as soon as possible (do NOT delay >12 hours)
- If extraction impossible, encourage aggressive drainage by pressing on abscess site
- Continue antibiotics post-extraction for 7 days minimum
- Monitor airway closely; position semi-upright

:::danger
**Spreading Cellulitis Decision:** If swelling spreads >2cm per 6 hours OR patient develops difficulty swallowing = emergency extraction mandatory. Delay >4 hours significantly increases abscess, sepsis, or airway emergency risk.
:::

### Tooth Mobile After Treatment (Won't Stop Moving)

**Likely Cause:** Progressive bone loss, trauma, or failed splinting
**Assessment:** Grade the mobility (I, II, or III as outlined above)
**Action:**
- **Grade I:** Monitor every 2-3 days; if stable, may preserve
- **Grade II:** Attempt stronger splinting; if already splinted, may need extraction
- **Grade III:** Extract within 24 hours; continued mobility indicates advancing bone loss/trauma

### Displaced Tooth Turns Gray Within Days

**Indication:** Pulp has died from traumatic injury
**Action:** Extract immediately (within 24 hours). Dead tooth = infection source; will abscess within 3-7 days.
**Do NOT attempt to save:** Gray discoloration = irreversible pulp necrosis; conservation efforts futile.

### Fractured Tooth Tip Still Causes Sharp Pain

**Likely Cause:** Micro-fracture extending deeper into dentin or pulp
**Check For:** Can you feel where pain originates? Pinpoint location with explorer.
**Action:**
- Apply topical anesthetic (eugenol, clove oil) directly to sharp area
- Smooth edge further with file or sandpaper
- If pain persists >24 hours: likely pulp involvement = extract
- Do NOT leave rough edge; resharpening occurs with chewing

### Abscess Drainage Slows But Pain Continues

**Likely Cause:** Incomplete drainage or secondary loculation
**Action:**
- Encourage further drainage by gentle pressure + warm compresses
- Increase salt water rinses (6-8 times daily)
- NSAIDs at maximum dose (ibuprofen 600mg every 6 hours)
- If drainage reopens and pulses: continue management
- If drainage ceases but pain remains: extract tooth immediately

## Common Mistakes & Their Consequences

### Mistake 1: Ignoring Early Cavity Signs

**What Happens:** Small cavity, no pain, patient skips treatment. Decay reaches dentin (1-2 weeks), then pulp (3-4 weeks). Abscess forms with fever, swelling.
**Consequence:** Preventable decay becomes emergency extraction + antibiotics + recovery time.
**Correction:** Arrest decay immediately with whatever materials available (temporary filling, topical fluoride, dietary modification). Early treatment saves tooth and prevents infection.

### Mistake 2: Incomplete Extraction (Broken Root Tips)

**What Happens:** Root fragments left behind are invisible but viable sources for infection. Abscess develops 5-14 days later in seemingly healed socket.
**Consequence:** Repeated infection cycle; must extract again; patient loses confidence in treatment.
**Correction:** After extraction, probe socket with instrument to detect remaining fragments. Use indirect X-ray shadow (if any light source available) to visualize tips. Remove all root structure visible before closing.

### Mistake 3: Starting Antibiotics Without Source Control

**What Happens:** Patient given antibiotics for abscess but tooth remains. Antibiotics provide 2-5 days symptomatic relief, then pain/swelling returns. Develops antibiotic resistance.
**Consequence:** Wasted antibiotics; eventual extraction anyway + prolonged suffering.
**Correction:** Extraction is primary treatment. Antibiotics are supportive only. Tooth must be removed within 24-48 hours of abscess diagnosis.

### Mistake 4: Inadequate Anesthesia During Extraction

**What Happens:** Insufficient topical anesthetic or no systemic pain management. Patient jerks during extraction; root breaks, soft tissue tears, excessive bleeding.
**Consequence:** Incomplete extraction, tissue trauma, infection, prolonged healing.
**Correction:** Allow topical anesthetic 3-5 minutes to work. Use NSAIDs preoperatively (ibuprofen 600mg × 1 hour before). Work quickly and deliberately; hesitation increases pain perception.

### Mistake 5: Poor Splinting of Displaced Teeth

**What Happens:** Loose temporary splint; tooth continues to move during healing. Periodontal ligament fails to reattach; tooth becomes progressively mobile.
**Consequence:** Tooth cannot be saved despite appearing vital. Must eventually extract.
**Correction:** Immobilization must be firm, absolutely preventing any micro-motion. Use composite bonding or wire + gauze; maintain splint for 3-4 weeks minimum.

### Mistake 6: Aggressive Rinsing After Extraction

**What Happens:** Patient rinses or spits vigorously within 24 hours. Blood clot dislodges → dry socket (severe pain days 2-4).
**Consequence:** 7-10 day recovery instead of 3-5 days; requires secondary treatment.
**Correction:** NO rinsing for first 24 hours. Gentle rinses only after day 2. Do NOT spit forcefully; let liquid drip from mouth.

### Mistake 7: Continuing Hard Diet Too Soon

**What Happens:** Patient eats hard/crunchy foods on extraction side before healing begins. Clot disrupted or underlying socket traumatized.
**Consequence:** Delayed healing, increased infection risk, possible complications.
**Correction:** Soft diet for minimum 2 weeks post-extraction. Avoid extraction site when chewing.

## Field Adaptations: Operating Without Ideal Equipment

### Extraction Without Forceps

**Improvised Tools:**
- Thick, sturdy stick (wood hardness critical; will break if soft)
- Spoon handle (best leverage if thick enough to not bend)
- Flat rock with smooth edge (leverage only; high tissue trauma risk)
- Wrapping adjacent tooth with cloth to gain leverage

**Technique (Principle: Rock Motion)**
1. Use improvised tool as lever; fulcrum on adjacent tooth or gum
2. Rock tooth side-to-side (NOT straight vertical pull)
3. Gradually increase amplitude of motion as periodontal ligament stretches
4. Once mobile, gentle rotation combined with upward movement
5. Extract when tooth lifts freely with moderate force

**Hemorrhage Control Without Forceps:**
- Direct pressure with gauze × 15 minutes
- Ice-cold water rinses to promote vasoconstriction
- Elevation of head 45 degrees
- Bite on gauze if patient able to hold position

### Pain Management With Limited Medications

**If No Ibuprofen:**
- Willow bark tea (contains salicylates, ancestor of aspirin): steep 2-3 tsp bark 10 minutes, drink 2-3 times daily
- Turmeric paste (curcumin = anti-inflammatory): mix turmeric powder with honey, apply topically
- Ice application remains effective even with no oral medication
- Clove oil or crushed cloves for topical anesthesia (potent; use sparingly)

**If No Lidocaine/Benzocaine:**
- Clove oil dabbed on gum (eugenol = natural anesthetic; 1-2 hours coverage)
- Ice held against gum area until numb (10-15 minutes; causes temporary numbness)
- Topical alcohol if available (stings initially, then numbs)
- No ideal substitute exists; extraction may require patient fortitude and assistant restraint

### Antibiotic Alternatives (Emergency Use Only)

**If No Amoxicillin/Clindamycin:**
- Honey: topical application to infected wounds (Manuka honey >20+ UMF most effective); some evidence for oral infection control
- Turmeric paste: curcumin has antimicrobial properties; apply topically 2-3 times daily
- Garlic: mild antimicrobial; chew or apply paste
- Salt water rinses: minimum 6-8 times daily (reduces bacterial load but not replacement for systemic antibiotic)

**Critical Caveat:** These alternatives slow infection progression but do NOT replace antibiotics for serious abscess/cellulitis. Extract tooth as primary intervention.

### Splinting Without Composite

**Options:**
- Cloth tape + adjacent tooth: wrap cloth around tooth being splinted and adjacent stable tooth
- Dental floss: loop around tooth and adjacent stable tooth; tie securely
- Wax (beeswax if available): mold around tooth and adjacent neighbor; firm enough to prevent motion
- Improvised elastic band: loop between teeth (less ideal, but provides some immobilization)

**Principle:** Immobilization must prevent ANY perceptible micro-motion. Test by attempting to move tooth side-to-side; should move <0.5mm.

### Assessment Tools Without Instruments

- **Cavity detection:** Inspect visually; probe with sharpened wood/bone
- **Mobility grading:** Grasp tooth gently, attempt side-to-side motion; compare to opposite side
- **Pocket depth:** Gently probe with sharpened stick; bleeding indicates >3mm pockets
- **Thermal response:** Ice chips to tooth; note patient response (delay indicates pulp death)
- **Percussion:** Tap with spoon handle or small stone; note pain vs dull response

:::tip
**Field Examination Protocol Without Tools:** Visual assessment + gentle probing + cold/heat response + percussion = sufficient diagnostic capacity for extraction decisions in 80% of emergency cases. Prioritize pain relief and infection control over perfect diagnosis.
:::
