## Introduction

Sewage treatment is necessary when a community has sufficient density and infrastructure to collect wastewater in pipes. Unlike dispersed latrines, centralized collection allows treatment to occur at a single facility before discharge to the environment. This guide covers treatment options ranging from simple septic systems serving a few households to larger plants treating thousands of liters daily, suitable for towns, camps, or industrial facilities. All designs prioritize pathogen removal, nutrient recovery where possible, and environmental protection.

## Understanding Sewage Composition

### Wastewater Types and Characteristics

**Domestic sewage:**
- Source: household toilets, showers, sinks, laundry
- Composition: feces, urine, soap, food scraps, paper
- Characteristics: high organic content (BOD 100–300 mg/L), high pathogens, variable flow

**Septic effluent:**
- Partially treated liquid from septic tanks
- Contains 80–90% of original pathogens but much lower suspended solids
- Suitable for secondary treatment before final disposal

**Combined sewage:**
- Mix of domestic sewage and stormwater/rainwater runoff
- Highly variable composition and volume (increases 5–10× during heavy rain)
- More difficult to treat; requires large retention capacity

### Quality Parameters

Key measurements of sewage and treated effluent:

| Parameter | Raw Sewage | Septic Effluent | Safe for Discharge |
|-----------|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **BOD₅** (organic matter) | 100–300 mg/L | 50–100 mg/L | <10 mg/L |
| **TSS** (suspended solids) | 100–250 mg/L | 20–50 mg/L | <10 mg/L |
| **Nitrogen** | 40–80 mg/L | 30–60 mg/L | <10 mg/L (variable) |
| **Phosphorus** | 5–15 mg/L | 4–12 mg/L | <1–2 mg/L (variable) |
| **Fecal coliforms** | 10⁷–10⁸ CFU/100 mL | 10⁵–10⁶ CFU/100 mL | <1,000 CFU/100 mL |
| **E. coli** | 10⁶–10⁷ CFU/100 mL | 10⁴–10⁵ CFU/100 mL | <100 CFU/100 mL |

## Gravity-Fed Collection Systems

Before treatment begins, sewage must be collected and transported to the treatment facility.

### Piping Design

**Pipe sizing:**
- Pipes should slope gently (minimum 0.5% slope, or drop 0.5 m per 100 m of pipe length)
- Slope ensures sewage flows by gravity; no pumping required
- Avoid flat or upslope sections where sewage stagnates, causing odor and blockage

**Pipe diameter:**
- For small communities or individual facilities: 75–100 mm (3–4 inch)
- For towns or large camps: 150–300 mm (6–12 inch) depending on daily flow
- Pipes should be sized such that flow velocity remains 0.6–1.0 m/second (prevents settling and odor)

**Pipe material:**
- Concrete, PVC, or clay pipes are durable in most conditions
- Avoid galvanized steel (rusts quickly in acidic sewage)
- Ensure joints are sealed to prevent groundwater infiltration or seepage into soil
- Lay pipes below the deepest groundwater table to prevent contamination

**Maintenance access:**
- Install cleanout ports (vertical pipes with removable caps) every 30–50 m
- Allows mechanical cleaning if blockages occur
- Position cleanouts at changes in slope or direction

### Collection Points and Force Mains

**Low-pressure collection:**
- For dispersed settlements without gravity slope, install small-diameter pipes at shallow depth
- Solids collect at lowest points; regularly flush with water to clear blockages
- Simpler and cheaper than conventional deep piping

**Force mains (pressurized):**
- If gravity slope is not available, use a pump and pressurized pipes
- Requires reliable electricity or manual/solar pumping
- Higher maintenance; limit use to critical situations
- Pumps should be sized to push flow at minimum 0.9 m/second to prevent settling

:::info-box
**Design Principle:** Gravity-fed systems are far more reliable and sustainable than pump-dependent systems in low-infrastructure settings. When possible, design collection systems to slope naturally from households to the treatment facility, even if this requires burying pipes at considerable depth or routing them circuitously.
:::

## Septic Tank Systems

The simplest on-site treatment system for individual households or small groups (5–20 people).

### Design and Function

A septic tank is a watertight chamber where sewage undergoes preliminary treatment:

1. **Solids separation:** Heavy solids (feces, paper) settle to the bottom; lighter grease and oils float to the top
2. **Bacterial decomposition:** Anaerobic bacteria break down settled solids (sludge), reducing volume by 30–50%
3. **Retention time:** Sewage remains in the tank 2–3 days, allowing settling and anaerobic digestion
4. **Liquid effluent:** Clarified liquid exits into a soil absorption system (drainfield, septic drainfield, or constructed wetland)

### Sizing and Construction

**Tank volume:**
- For a household of 5 people: 1,000–1,500 liters (minimum)
- Rule of thumb: 150–200 liters per person for primary treatment
- Larger tanks improve treatment but require more space

**Typical dimensions (1,200-liter tank):**
- Length: 2.2 m, Width: 1.2 m, Depth: 0.8 m

**Construction:**
1. **Excavation:** Dig a pit to accommodate the tank with 30–50 cm soil cover above
2. **Tank structure:**
   - Concrete (poured or precast) is most durable
   - Brickwork or stone with cement mortar is acceptable if properly sealed
   - Avoid wood or fiberglass in very old designs; replace if deteriorating
3. **Compartments:** Divide the tank into two or three chambers with partitions (perforated or with overflow pipes):
   - **Chamber 1 (settling):** Receives incoming sewage; solids settle, liquid overflows to Chamber 2
   - **Chamber 2 (digestion):** Slower-moving liquid where sludge is further decomposed
   - **Chamber 3 (outlet):** Filtered liquid exits to drainfield; outlet is 50–100 cm above tank bottom to allow sludge accumulation
4. **Inlet and outlet pipes:**
   - Inlet: Submerged pipe extending 30 cm below liquid surface; prevents turbulence that would stir settled solids
   - Outlet: Baffle or outlet T-pipe positioned to allow liquid exit while retaining sludge
5. **Vent pipe:** 5–10 cm diameter vent pipe rises through roof, extending 0.5 m above it, with fine mesh screen to prevent insect entry

**Inlet and outlet design (critical):**
- If these are not properly designed, treated effluent will be of poor quality
- Use baffles or submerged T-pipes to minimize current and turbulence
- Ensure outlet draws from 30–50 cm below liquid surface (clarified layer)

### Maintenance

**Pumping schedule:**
- Frequency depends on tank size and number of users
- A 1,200-liter tank serving 5 people requires pumping every 3–5 years
- More frequent use (larger households) requires more frequent pumping
- Monitor sludge depth visually (if accessible) or hire a service to measure

**Inspection and upkeep:**
- **Monthly:** Visually inspect for cracks or leaks; repair promptly
- **Annually:** Check that the vent pipe is not blocked; clear if necessary
- **Every 2–3 years:** Inspect outlet baffle; if damaged, repair or replace
- **Before pumping:** Have a professional assess tank condition; may identify leaks, damaged baffles, or corrosion

**What not to put in septic tanks:**
- Large solids (diapers, feminine hygiene products, paper towels)
- Grease or oil (clogs pipes and prevents treatment)
- Medications, pesticides, or harsh chemicals (kill bacteria needed for decomposition)
- Excessive water (floods the tank, reducing retention time)

**Grease traps:**
- Install a simple trap upstream of the septic tank if households generate significant grease (kitchens, food businesses)
- A 50–100 liter tank with a settling chamber captures grease before it enters the main septic tank
- Clean grease traps monthly

### Drainfield Design

Treated effluent from the septic tank must be absorbed into soil or further polished.

**Soil absorption (subsurface drainfield):**
1. **Soil assessment:**
   - Test soil permeability: dig a hole 30 cm deep, fill with water, and measure how quickly it drains
   - If water drains in <1 hour, soil is good for absorption (sandy loam)
   - If water drains in 1–4 hours, soil is acceptable (clay-rich loam)
   - If water takes >4 hours, soil is poor (heavy clay); use alternative systems (raised bed, constructed wetland, or additional treatment)

2. **Drainfield layout:**
   - Position 15+ meters downhill from the septic tank and 30+ meters from water sources
   - Lay perforated pipes (5–10 cm diameter) in trenches 30–40 cm deep and 1–2 m apart
   - Pipe slope: 1% (0.5–1 cm per meter) to encourage gentle flow
   - Backfill trenches with gravel (10–15 cm) above pipes, then soil
   - Vegetate with water-tolerant plants (grass, reeds, shallow-rooted shrubs)

3. **Drainfield maintenance:**
   - Do not build on or drive over the drainfield (compacts soil, reduces absorption)
   - Avoid root intrusion; keep trees away from trenches
   - Monitor for soggy areas or sewage smell; indicates failure
   - If the drainfield fails, construct a new one at least 10 m away

### Septic System Failure and Repair

**Warning signs:**
- Toilet backup or slow draining
- Odor near the septic tank or drainfield
- Soggy soil or pooling water above the drainfield
- Sewage surfacing in nearby wells or streams

**Common causes:**
- Full tank (sludge exceeds 50% of tank volume); requires pumping
- Damaged baffle (allows sludge into effluent); replace
- Clogged drainfield (accumulated solids or roots); usually requires construction of a new drainfield
- High groundwater or poor drainage (unsuitable site); may require alternative treatment

**Remediation:**
1. Pump the tank immediately
2. Inspect the tank interior (if safe) or hire a professional to assess damage
3. Repair baffles or tank structure if damaged
4. If drainfield is failing, construct a secondary drainfield or upgrade to constructed wetland treatment
5. Implement stricter household discipline (no grease, reduced paper, fewer solids)

:::warning
**Groundwater Contamination Risk:** Septic tanks are not foolproof. If the water table is high, soil is clay-rich, or the drainfield is poorly sited, sewage can contaminate groundwater and wells. Before installing a septic system, have the groundwater depth and soil type assessed by a professional. In unsuitable conditions, use an alternative such as a constructed wetland or mechanical treatment plant with proper discharge standards.
:::

## Waste Stabilization Ponds (Lagoons)

For small towns or camps (500–10,000 people), stabilization ponds provide low-cost, passive treatment.

### Design Principles

Ponds work through natural processes: bacterial action (anaerobic and aerobic), algal photosynthesis, and sunlight disinfection.

**Typical sequence:**
1. **Anaerobic pond:** Deep (2–3 m), dark, where sludge settles and anaerobic bacteria decompose organic matter. High nutrient release but poor pathogen removal.
2. **Facultative pond:** Intermediate depth (1.5–2 m), where aerobic bacteria in the surface layer consume organics; anaerobic processes occur at the bottom. Better pathogen removal through competition.
3. **Maturation pond:** Shallow (0.8–1.2 m), where algae and sunlight provide additional disinfection. Typically 2–3 ponds in series for maximum pathogen kill.

### Sizing and Construction

**Area requirement:**
- 0.5–1.5 m² per person per day (varies by climate and treatment depth)
- For a settlement of 2,000 people: 1,000–3,000 m² total (e.g., three 600 m² ponds in series)

**Location:**
- Flat terrain (minimal excavation cost)
- Downwind and downslope from settlement
- 50+ meters from water sources
- Protected from flooding but with capacity to handle storm surges

**Pond construction:**
1. **Excavation:** Dig basins to specified depth and area
2. **Lining:** Clay lining (30–50 cm, compacted to reduce permeability) or synthetic liner (plastic sheeting) to prevent groundwater contamination
3. **Inlet and outlet:**
   - Inlet: Submerged pipe to prevent short-circuiting (sewage traveling directly to outlet without treatment)
   - Outlet: Overflow weir, adjustable to maintain water level
   - Outlet height controls retention time (longer time = better treatment; typically 20–30 days total)
4. **Embankments:** Sloped sides (1:3 or 1:4 ratio, height:base) for stability and safety
5. **Access:** Simple footpath or bridge for maintenance and monitoring

### Retention Time and Temperature

**Retention time:**
- Calculated as: Total pond volume / Daily flow = retention time (days)
- Example: 12,000 m³ ponds / 40 m³/day flow = 300 days; distributed across three ponds = 100 days per pond
- Longer retention = higher pathogen kill but requires more space and land cost

**Temperature effects:**
- In warm climates (>20°C mean): 20–30 day retention achieves 2–3 log pathogen reduction
- In cool climates (<10°C mean): 30–50 day retention needed; in winter, may achieve only 1–2 log reduction
- Design for the worst-case season (winter in temperate climates)

### Operation and Maintenance

**Weekly:**
- Check inlet and outlet water levels; adjust outlet weir if needed
- Remove floating debris (leaves, branches) if present
- Monitor for algal scum or unusual color (indicates overloading or shock)

**Monthly:**
- Visual inspection of embankments for erosion or seepage
- Monitor inlet and outlet pipes for blockages
- Count duckweed or aquatic plant growth (some is beneficial; excessive growth may indicate nutrient overloading)

**Seasonally:**
- In cold climates, prepare for ice formation (does not harm ponds but complicates access)
- In rainy season, monitor water levels; adjust outlet to prevent overflow or ponding

**Annually:**
- Measure sludge depth in anaerobic ponds (if sediment exceeds 0.5 m, dredging may be needed after 5–10 years)
- Test effluent quality (fecal coliforms, BOD, phosphorus); compare to standards
- Assess vegetation and duckweed coverage; remove if >50% of surface (too much plant growth reduces aerobic zone)

**Sludge management:**
- Accumulated sludge in anaerobic ponds is removed every 5–20 years depending on loading and sludge accumulation rate
- Dredged sludge is dried on drying beds or in ponds (separate area, lined) and used as compost or soil amendment after pathogen inactivation (2+ years storage)

### Safety and Hazards

- Ponds can be deep and may have soft sludge bottoms; post warning signs and prevent unsupervised access
- Algal blooms may produce toxins; avoid allowing livestock or irrigation from ponds with visible blooms
- Effluent is still partially treated; never use directly for drinking or in contact with skin

:::info-box
**Climate Suitability:** Waste stabilization ponds work best in warm, sunny climates with long retention capacity. In cold or cloudy regions, mechanical treatment (activated sludge, trickling filters) may be more effective but require more energy and maintenance expertise.
:::

## Upflow Anaerobic Sludge Blanket (UASB) Reactors

For communities with more technical capacity and some source of energy, UASB systems provide rapid, compact treatment.

### How UASB Works

Sewage flows upward through a suspended "blanket" of anaerobic microorganisms, which decompose organic matter rapidly.

- **Retention time:** 6–8 hours (much faster than ponds)
- **Organic removal:** 80–90% of BOD removed; produces biogas (methane)
- **Pathogen removal:** 1–2 log reduction; not sufficient for final effluent (requires polishing)
- **Biogas production:** 0.2–0.3 m³ biogas per kg BOD removed; can be used for heating or electricity

### Design Considerations

- Requires routine operator training and monitoring
- Needs pH control, sludge wasting schedules, and gas safety procedures
- Suitable for larger communities (1,000+ people) or industrial facilities
- Biogas can be used on-site (heating, generator) or flared
- Effluent from UASB is not fully treated and must undergo secondary polishing (constructed wetland, ponds, or sand filter)

## Trickling Filters

Aerobic treatment system where sewage flows over a bed of rock or plastic media, where bacteria and biofilm treat the sewage.

### Operation

1. **Media:** Rock (limestone or granite, 3–5 cm diameter) or plastic packing (provides large surface area for biofilm growth)
2. **Dosing:** Sewage distributed over the top of the media and allowed to percolate downward, exposing it to air
3. **Biofilm:** Aerobic bacteria colonize the media surface, forming a 1–2 mm layer that oxidizes organic matter
4. **Effluent:** Treated water exits the bottom, mixed with sloughed biofilm solids; requires settling tank (clarifier) before discharge

### Advantages and Disadvantages

**Advantages:**
- Simpler operation than activated sludge systems
- Lower energy requirement than mechanical aeration
- Robust to shock loads

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires large surface area (high land cost)
- Biofilm sloughing produces suspended solids; requires settling
- Requires 3–5 day retention time
- Not suitable for cold climates (biological activity slows)

## Constructed Wetlands for Polishing

A common final treatment step after mechanical or biological treatment.

### Design

- Similar to the greywater wetlands described in the greywater recycling guide
- Effluent from septic tanks, UASB reactors, or trickling filters is applied to a wetland
- Wetland removes remaining nutrients and pathogens through plant uptake, bacterial action, and settling

### Advantages

- Low energy requirement
- Creates aesthetic and wildlife habitat
- Produces some harvestable biomass (reeds for thatching, craft, or fuel)
- Tolerant of variable inflow

### Disadvantages

- Requires significant land area (0.5–2 m² per person)
- Slower treatment than mechanical systems
- Cannot handle toxic or shock loads

## Effluent Monitoring and Discharge Standards

Before discharging treated sewage to the environment, verify that it meets local standards.

### Testing Methods

**Field tests (simple, no equipment):**
- **Color:** Treated effluent should be clear or slightly cloudy; dark brown indicates insufficient treatment
- **Smell:** Should be neutral or earthy; sulfurous or putrid smell indicates anaerobic conditions or high organics
- **Turbidity:** If using a graduated cylinder, turbidity can be estimated visually (clear = <5 NTU, cloudy = 5–10 NTU)

**Laboratory tests (if available):**
- **BOD₅:** Biochemical oxygen demand; target <20 mg/L for discharge to surface water
- **TSS:** Total suspended solids; target <10 mg/L
- **Fecal coliforms:** Target <1,000 CFU/100 mL for discharge; <100 CFU/100 mL if used for unrestricted irrigation
- **Nitrogen and phosphorus:** Varying standards; generally <10 mg/L each to prevent algal blooms in receiving waters

### Typical Discharge Standards

| Parameter | Strict (for potable reuse) | Moderate (surface water) | Agricultural irrigation |
|-----------|----------------------------|--------------------------|-------------------------|
| BOD | <10 mg/L | <20 mg/L | <30 mg/L |
| TSS | <5 mg/L | <10 mg/L | <20 mg/L |
| Fecal coliforms | <1 CFU/100 mL | <1,000 CFU/100 mL | <1,000 CFU/100 mL* |
| E. coli | Absent | <100 CFU/100 mL | <100 CFU/100 mL* |

*For restricted irrigation (non-food crops); unrestricted use requires stricter standards.

### Sampling Protocol

1. Collect samples in sterile bottles from the effluent outlet
2. If possible, collect samples under aseptic conditions (disinfect bottle opening with alcohol, avoid touching interior)
3. Keep samples cool (4°C) and transport to laboratory within 24 hours
4. Analyze for BOD, TSS, pathogens, and nutrients
5. Document results and compare to standards

## Maintenance and Long-Term Operation

### Record-Keeping

Maintain a log documenting:
- Daily inflow volume (if meters are available; estimate if not)
- Observations on odor, color, and algae or biofilm growth
- Maintenance performed (cleaning, repair, chemical addition)
- Test results (if monitoring is done)
- Staff attendance and training

### Troubleshooting

| Problem | Cause | Solution |
|---------|-------|----------|
| Overflow from pond | Inflow exceeds capacity or outlet blocked | Clear outlet; reduce inflow if possible; enlarge pond or improve upstream retention |
| Algal blooms | High nutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus) | Reduce retention time (increase outlet level); harvest algae; increase water exchange |
| Foul smell | Anaerobic conditions or shock load | Improve aeration; reduce inflow; check for blockages |
| High effluent turbidity | Underloading or biofilm sloughing | Increase retention time; adjust sludge wasting |
| Low treatment efficiency | System design inadequate for actual population | Consider expansion; add treatment stages; increase retention time |

### Sustainability

Long-term success requires:
- **Community investment:** Local participation in design, construction, and operation
- **Financial sustainability:** Fee collection or government subsidy to cover energy, maintenance, and staff
- **Operator training:** Qualified personnel trained to monitor and adjust operations
- **Spare parts supply:** Access to replacement pipes, valves, and equipment
- **Environmental monitoring:** Regular testing to ensure standards are met
- **Adaptive management:** Willingness to modify operation based on experience and climate changes

## Conclusion

Sewage treatment systems scale from small household septic tanks to large municipal plants. All share the goal of removing pathogens and reducing organic matter before discharge to the environment. The choice of system depends on population density, available land, climate, technical capacity, and budget. Gravity-fed collection, simple ponds or septic systems, and passive polishing through constructed wetlands provide affordable, low-maintenance options for most communities. More complex mechanical systems offer faster treatment but require sustained investment in energy and skilled operation. Careful design, consistent maintenance, and regular monitoring ensure that treatment systems protect both human health and environmental integrity.
