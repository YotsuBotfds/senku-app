:::warning
**Crop failure from seed/biome mismatch:** Growing cold-tolerant varieties in tropical zones or humidity-sensitive crops in monsoon regions leads to total crop failure. Test new cultivars on small plots before committing large areas. Verify seed provenance and growing requirements match your specific microclimate. Consult local farmers on proven varieties before planting.
:::

<section id="overview">

## Why Biome-Specific Agriculture Matters

Agricultural failure in austere conditions kills faster than direct violence. The difference between starvation and food security often comes down to:

1. **Crop selection aligned to climate** (cold-hardy in mountains, drought-resistant in deserts, heat-tolerant in tropics)
2. **Genetic diversity** (multiple varieties of staple crops, so single failure doesn't eliminate food source)
3. **Seed security** (reliable seed supply from year to year, or ability to save seeds)
4. **Adaptation to local conditions** (altitude, rainfall, soil, growing season length)

This guide provides frameworks for **selecting and maintaining crop varieties** appropriate to specific biomes.

</section>

## Cold-Hardy Varieties: Mountain & Northern Climates

Cold-hardy agriculture focuses on:
- **Short growing season** (90–120 days frost-free)
- **Cold tolerance** (frosts in shoulder seasons)
- **Quick maturity** (essential to complete before hard freeze)
- **Nutrient density** (grow what's most calorie/nutrient-dense in limited season)

<div class="crop-card">

### Staple Crops for Cold Climates

**Potatoes** (Most important)
- **Why:** High yield/acre, nutritionally complete, stores 6+ months
- **Cold-hardy varieties:** Elba, Kennebec, Red Norland, Yukon Gold, Butte
- **Planting:** Late April/early May (after last frost)
- **Days to harvest:** 90–120 depending on variety
- **Yield:** 10–15 tons/acre in good conditions
- **Storage:** Cool, dark (40–50°F ideal); lasts 4–6 months

**Turnips & Rutabagas** (High-yield root crops)
- **Why:** Cold-tolerant (can harvest into November), good nutrition, long storage
- **Varieties:** Gilfeather, Laurentian (rutabaga); Purple-top White Globe, Hakurei (turnip)
- **Planting:** June-July
- **Days to harvest:** 70–90 (turnips), 90–110 (rutabaga)
- **Yield:** 15–20 tons/acre

**Cabbage** (Long storage, good nutrition)
- **Why:** Survives light frosts; stores 4–6 months in cool conditions
- **Varieties:** Savoy Ace, Danish Ballhead, Winterbor (kale)
- **Planting:** April (transplants) or June (seed)
- **Days to harvest:** 65–110 depending on variety
- **Yield:** 20+ tons/acre
- **Bonus:** Kale is extremely cold-hardy (survives to -10°F); can harvest all winter in some zones

**Grains** (Foundation for calories)
- **Why:** Most calorie-efficient; long storage; can save seed
- **Cold-hardy varieties:**
  - **Oats:** Robust, yields 40–50 bu/acre, very cold-tolerant
  - **Winter wheat:** Planted fall, harvested early summer; higher yield than spring wheat
  - **Barley:** Cold-hardy, good for beer/brewing or animal feed
  - **Rye:** Extremely hardy, tolerates poor soil, good bread grain
- **Yield (grain):** 40–60 bushels/acre (varies by moisture, fertility)

**Legumes** (Nitrogen fixation & protein)
- **Why:** Add nitrogen to soil (reducing fertilizer need); high protein content
- **Varieties:** Dried beans (Northern Kidney, Pinto), peas (Alaska, Oregon Sugar Pod)
- **Planting:** Late April-May
- **Days to harvest:** 60–90
- **Yield:** 1000–2000 lbs/acre

---

### Example Cold-Climate Rotation (Mountain Zone, 130 frost-free days)

**Year 1 - Field A:** Winter wheat (fall planted, summer harvest) + legumes as cover crop after harvest

**Year 1 - Field B:** Potatoes (May–September)

**Year 1 - Field C:** Cabbage, turnips, kale (may extend into October with mulch)

**Year 2 - Rotate fields:**
- A → Potatoes
- B → Grain + legume
- C → Root crops

**Result:** Staggered harvests; diverse calories from grains + potatoes + roots; soil maintained via legume rotation

## Desert-Adapted Crops: Arid Climates

Desert agriculture centers on:
- **Drought resistance** (deep roots, waxy leaves, efficient water use)
- **Heat tolerance** (survive 100–120°F)
- **Water efficiency** (produce food with minimal irrigation)
- **Salt tolerance** (if irrigation from saline sources)

<div class="crop-card">

### Staple Crops for Deserts

**Millets** (Ancient grains, extremely drought-resistant)
- **Why:** 2–3x more drought-tolerant than wheat; quick maturity; nutritious
- **Varieties:** Pearl millet, sorghum, teff
- **Planting:** After last frost (typically late April-May)
- **Days to harvest:** 60–90
- **Yield:** 20–40 bu/acre (lower than wheat, but drought-proof)
- **Bonus:** Can be intercropped with legumes

**Sorghum** (Drought king)
- **Why:** Requires 50% less water than corn; survives heat >110°F; grain + syrup potential
- **Varieties:** Sweet sorghum (grain + juice), grain sorghum (Milo, Kaffir)
- **Planting:** After last frost, when soil warm (60°F)
- **Days to harvest:** 80–120
- **Yield:** 30–60 bu/acre depending on moisture
- **Note:** Allelopathic (secretes compounds inhibiting other plants); rotate crops after

**Chickpeas & Lentils** (Legumes + heat tolerance)
- **Why:** Protein-rich, drought-resistant, nitrogen-fixing
- **Varieties:** Desi chickpea (small, harder), Kabuli (larger, creamy)
- **Planting:** Early spring (cool weather crop even in desert)
- **Days to harvest:** 100–150
- **Yield:** 800–1200 lbs/acre

**Amaranth & Quinoa** (Pseudo-cereals, extreme hardiness)
- **Why:** Highly nutritious; extremely drought-tolerant; fast-growing
- **Varieties:** Amaranth (K432, Plainsman), Quinoa (Tempi, Real)
- **Planting:** After soil warms
- **Days to harvest:** 70–100
- **Yield:** 1000–2000 lbs/acre grain

**Date Palms & Tamarisks** (Long-term investment)
- **Why:** Minimal water after establishment; calorie-dense fruit; multi-year producer
- **Varieties:** Medjool, Deglet Noor (dates); tamarisk for forage
- **Planting:** Year 1 establishment; bearing Year 3–5
- **Yield:** 150–300 lbs fruit/mature tree/year

---

### Water-Efficient Techniques (Desert)

1. **Mulching:** 4–6 inches straw/compost around plants (reduces evaporation 50%)
2. **Drip irrigation:** Water directly to soil (not overhead); reduces water loss to evaporation
3. **Hugelkultur:** Mounding with buried wood/mulch (retains moisture longer)
4. **Swale systems:** Catch runoff, slow infiltration, allow soil to absorb
5. **Planting density:** Closer spacing increases shade between plants, reducing water stress

## Altitude Adaptation: Growing at Elevation

Altitude creates unique challenges:
- **Shorter growing season** (frost danger extends further into spring/fall)
- **Cold nights** (even in summer, nights <50°F common)
- **Intense UV radiation** (thinner atmosphere)
- **Low humidity** (faster evaporation)
- **Thin soil** (if recently glaciated terrain)

<div class="crop-card">

### Crop Selection by Elevation

**High Altitude (8000–12,000 ft):**
- **Growing season:** 60–90 days frost-free
- **Best crops:** Potatoes, cold-hardy root crops (turnips, beets), leafy greens, quick grains
- **Specific varieties:**
  - Potatoes: Elba, Russet Norkotah (bred for high altitude)
  - Greens: Spinach, kale, chard (tolerant of cool nights)
  - Beans: Snap beans (bush varieties only; pole take too long)
  - Grains: Barley, rye (very hardy)
- **Special consideration:** Use cold frames or mulch to extend season

**Mid Altitude (5000–8000 ft):**
- **Growing season:** 90–140 days
- **Best crops:** Mix of cold-hardy and heat-loving
- **Varieties:**
  - Standard potatoes + some corn (quick varieties like Olathe)
  - Cool-season (spring/fall): Cabbage, turnips, greens
  - Warm-season (mid-summer): Beans, squash, tomatoes (in protected areas)
- **Strategy:** Plant cool-season crops early spring; harvest before summer heat; replant heat-tolerant in summer; harvest again in fall

**Low Altitude (3000–5000 ft):**
- **Growing season:** 120–170 days
- **Best crops:** Diverse; most temperate crops thrive
- **Varieties:** Standard seed catalogs are reliable guide

---

### High-Altitude Soil Building

Altitude often means thin, rocky, infertile soil. Preparation takes years:

**Year 1–2:**
- Remove rocks (tedious but necessary)
- Add 3–4 inches compost/aged manure
- Plant cover crops (hairy vetch, rye) in off-season
- Expect modest yields

**Year 3–5:**
- Soil biology active; organic matter breaking down
- Add 2 inches more compost/manure annually
- Yields improving; can expand planting area
- Continue cover cropping

**Year 5+:**
- Soil dark, rich, earthworms present
- Standard yields possible
- Reduce compost inputs (maintenance only)

## Seed Selection Criteria for Austere Conditions

When building seed stocks, prioritize **reliable performers** for your specific biome.

<div class="selection-criteria">

### Core Selection Criteria

**1. Adaptability to your climate**
- Research average first/last frost date
- Choose varieties rated for your zone or 1 zone colder (margin of safety)
- Example: If zone 5, choose seeds rated zone 4 or 5 (avoid zone 6)

**2. Daylength sensitivity**
- Some crops ("photoperiodic") respond to day length
- **Critical for:** Potatoes, grains, some legumes in extreme latitudes
- High-latitude gardeners should choose varieties bred for similar latitude (not southern varieties)
- Example: If you're 50°N, avoid varieties bred for 35°N

**3. Disease resistance**
- Select varieties with marked resistance (usually noted V/F/N for tomatoes; PM for mildew, etc.)
- Reduces need for fungicides/sprays in austere conditions
- Example: Choose cabbage varieties resistant to yellowing disease if that's endemic in your region

**4. Yield & storage potential**
- Prioritize calorie/nutrient yield per acre (potatoes, grains, pulses over ornamental squashes)
- Select storage varieties (winter squash, root crops, dried beans) over summer vegetables
- Long-season providers (perennials, multi-harvest) over annuals requiring replanting

**5. Seed-saving capability**
- Avoid F1 hybrids (seeds don't breed true next generation)
- Choose heirloom or OP (open-pollinated) varieties
- Select "true breeding" traits (self-pollinating beans/peas, isolatable crops like lettuce)
- Some crops harder to save (corn = cross-pollinated, needs isolation; carrots = biennial; brassicas = insect-pollinated)

**6. Cost & availability**
- In austere conditions, resupply may be impossible
- Stock seed from multiple suppliers (redundancy)
- Buy 2–3 year supply of critical crops
- Save seed from best performers (multiplies seed stock over time)

## Genetic Insurance Strategy: Seed Banking & Diversity

**Genetic insurance** = maintaining multiple varieties of each staple crop to prevent total loss from disease/pest/poor season.

<div class="genetic-strategy">

### Example: Potato Genetic Insurance

**Austere setting:** Community of 100 people needs ~15 tons potatoes/year (150 lbs/person)

**Single-variety risk:** Choose only "Russet Burbank"
- Year 1: Good yield (20 tons)
- Year 2: Late blight (fungal disease) devastates Russet plants → 2 ton crop → famine

**Multi-variety insurance:** Maintain 4 varieties in rotation

**Selected varieties & planting:**
- **Kennebec** (40% of planting): Disease-tolerant, stores well, all-purpose
- **Elba** (30%): Very cold-hardy, good for short-season areas
- **Red Norland** (20%): Fast-maturing, some disease resistance
- **Yukon Gold** (10%): Distinctive variety (cultural preference) + backup if others fail

**Seed requirement:** 2 tons seed potatoes (1% of crop)

**Year 1 crop diversity:**
- Kennebec: 8 tons (from 0.8 tons seed)
- Elba: 6 tons (from 0.6 tons seed)
- Red Norland: 4 tons (from 0.4 tons seed)
- Yukon Gold: 2 tons (from 0.2 tons seed)
- **Total: 20 tons**

**Year 2 scenario: Late blight hits**
- Kennebec heavily affected: 3 ton yield (down from 8)
- Elba shows resistance: 5 ton yield (similar to year 1)
- Red Norland partially affected: 2 ton yield (down from 4)
- Yukon Gold resistant: 1.5 ton yields (stable)
- **Total: 11.5 tons** (below target but not famine; can supplement with other foods)

**Without diversity (single Russet):** 2 tons = starvation-level

---

### Building Genetic Insurance Over Time

**Year 1 (Foundation):**
- Choose 4–6 varieties for each staple crop
- Plant 30–50% of normal area with each variety (assess local performance)
- Prioritize cold-hardy, disease-resistant options for your biome

**Year 2:**
- Increase plantings of best-performing varieties
- Save seed from strongest plants of each variety
- Reduce reliance on external seed supply

**Year 3+:**
- Stabilize plantings (25–30% each major variety)
- Save significant seed stock (1–2 years supply of each)
- Reduce further from external seed supply
- Add new varieties only if previous ones show weakness

---

### Seed Storage & Viability

**Short-term (1–2 years):**
- Cool (50–60°F), dry (30–40% humidity), dark location
- Sealed containers (glass jars with desiccant, or vacuum-sealed bags)
- Germination testing: Plant 10 seeds, count sprouting; if <80% germinating, variety at end of life

**Long-term (5+ years):**
- Very cool (32–40°F), very dry (10% humidity)
- Root cellar, ice house, or specially constructed cold storage
- Check annually; discard if germination drops <60%

**Seeds requiring special handling:**
- **Beans/peas:** Can last 5+ years in cool storage
- **Brassicas (cabbage, broccoli):** 3–5 years
- **Root crops (carrot, parsnip):** 1–3 years (shorter-lived)
- **Grains (wheat, barley):** 5+ years if dry
- **Potatoes:** Not seed; store tubers in cool/dark (4–6 months typical)

## Checklist: Building Biome-Appropriate Agriculture

**Pre-Event (1–2 years before potential collapse):**
- [ ] Research your biome (frost date, altitude, precipitation, soil type)
- [ ] Identify 4–6 staple crops suitable to your climate
- [ ] Select 3–4 varieties of each staple crop
- [ ] Source seeds from reliable suppliers (save catalogs/contacts)
- [ ] Build seed inventory (aim for 2–3 year supply of critical crops)
- [ ] Store seeds in cool, dry, dark location
- [ ] Test germination of oldest seeds annually

**During Transition (as standard supply chains fail):**
- [ ] Prioritize cold-hardy, drought-resistant, high-yield crops
- [ ] Establish rotation: diverse plantings of each staple
- [ ] Save seed from best-performing plants
- [ ] Teach seed-saving skills to 2+ community members
- [ ] Document your successes/failures for next year

**Ongoing (year 2+):**
- [ ] Increase proportion of self-saved seed
- [ ] Rotate crops to maintain soil fertility
- [ ] Monitor for pests/diseases; select resistant varieties next year if problems
- [ ] Share seed with neighbors (builds community resilience)
- [ ] Expand genetic insurance if one variety underperforms
