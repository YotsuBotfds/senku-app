## Why Documentation Matters

Visual documentation is one of humanity's most powerful tools for understanding and preserving the world. Whether using cameras or sketches, recording what we see serves fundamental needs across agriculture, medicine, law, history, and communication. In uncertain times, the ability to create lasting visual records becomes even more critical.

### Core Purposes of Visual Documentation

#### Record Keeping & Inventory

A single photograph or detailed drawing preserves information that would take paragraphs to describe. Document your tools, buildings, livestock, gardens, and stores. Create visual inventories for insurance, memory, and knowledge transfer. A photograph taken today becomes precious documentation in years to come.

#### Evidence & Verification

Visual records provide irrefutable proof of condition, time, location, and process. Medical practitioners use photographs to track wound healing or disease progression. Farmers document pest damage and crop health. Surveyors create maps with photographic reference. In disputes or emergencies, photographs establish facts that words alone cannot.

#### Mapping & Spatial Understanding

Photographs and sketches create maps that guide others. A drawing of your property with water sources, shelter locations, and resource caches is invaluable. Aerial or elevated photographs reveal landscape patterns invisible from ground level. Sketch maps of local terrain, routes, and landmarks preserve geographic knowledge.

#### Botanical & Medical Reference

Growing conditions, plant diseases, and herbal remedies are documented through careful photography and illustration. A photograph of a diseased leaf or infected wound helps identify the problem and track treatment effectiveness. Pressed specimens with supporting photographs create reference collections for identification. Medical photography documents injuries, illnesses, and recovery.

#### Communication & Instruction

A sequence of photographs illustrates a process more effectively than written instructions. Document techniques for building, cultivating, preserving, and crafting. Share these visual records to teach others. Step-by-step photography of construction, repair, or procedure becomes invaluable knowledge.

#### Historical Preservation

Photographs record the present for the future. Document your people, places, and way of life. Images taken today become primary historical sources for future generations. What seems ordinary now becomes precious heritage. Create visual archives of your community, skills, and achievements.

:::tip
Start documenting immediately. Even simple photographs are better than nothing. Focus on capturing clear, well-lit images with context. Include scale references (hand, ruler, coin) and annotations (date, location, what is being shown).
:::

## Camera Obscura: Optical Projection

The camera obscura (Latin for "dark room") is the foundational principle of all photography. Light passes through a small opening and projects an inverted image on a surface. Before photographic film existed, artists used camera obscura to trace accurate images for drawing and painting.

### The Principle

Light rays from an object pass through a pinhole and converge on the opposite wall, creating an inverted image. This is pure optics—no chemicals or electricity required. The image quality improves with a smaller pinhole and longer focal length (deeper box).

![Camera Obscura optical diagram showing light ray path through pinhole to projection surface](../assets/svgs/photography-documentation-1.svg)

Figure 1: Camera Obscura Principle — Light from the object passes through a pinhole and projects an inverted image on the screen inside.

### Building a Camera Obscura

A camera obscura can be as simple as a darkened room or as small as a box. The basic requirements are:

-   **Light-tight enclosure:** A room, box, or tent where all external light is blocked
-   **Pinhole opening:** A small hole (typically 0.5-1mm) in one wall
-   **Viewing surface:** A white wall, translucent screen, or paper on which to trace the image

#### Simple Room Camera Obscura

Black out a room or large box completely. Make a small pinhole in one wall or shutter. The brighter the exterior and the darker the room, the clearer the image. The focal length is the distance from pinhole to wall. A 12-24 inch deep box works well for detailed work.

#### Pinhole Sizing

The pinhole diameter affects image brightness and sharpness. A smaller hole (0.3-0.5mm) gives sharper images but requires more light. A larger hole (1mm) is brighter but softer. Optimal pinhole diameter is calculated as:

Pinhole diameter (mm) = √(2 × focal length in mm × wavelength of light)
Typical result: 0.3–0.5mm for focal lengths of 100–300mm

#### Tracing from Camera Obscura

The primary use of camera obscura is architectural and artistic. Project the image on paper or thin cloth and trace the outlines. This creates geometrically accurate drawings without needing artistic skill. The inverted image can be flipped later.

:::tip
Camera obscura is superior for accuracy. A lens or adjustable pinhole improves image quality. For drawing complex buildings, landscapes, or scenes, invest time setting up a good camera obscura—the resulting accuracy is worth it.
:::

### Advantages & Limitations

#### Advantages

-   Purely optical—no chemicals
-   Extremely accurate projection
-   Reusable indefinitely
-   Easy to build from common materials
-   No electricity required

#### Limitations

-   Image is dim without strong light
-   Requires complete darkness
-   Image is inverted (must flip drawing)
-   No permanent record created directly
-   Only works outdoors or through windows

## Pinhole Camera: Portable Photography

A pinhole camera is a light-tight box with photographic paper or film inside and a tiny pinhole in front. Light exposes the light-sensitive material directly, creating a photograph. No lens is needed. Exposure times range from seconds in bright sun to minutes in shade.

### Construction

A pinhole camera can be built from a simple box, tin can, or wooden frame. The critical requirements are:

-   Light-tight construction (no light leaks except through the pinhole)
-   Flat, black interior to absorb stray light
-   Pinhole precisely made in a thin metal sheet
-   Film or photographic paper held flat on the back wall
-   Light-tight shutter to control exposure

![Pinhole camera cross-section showing light path, film placement, and shutter mechanism](../assets/svgs/photography-documentation-2.svg)

Figure 2: Pinhole Camera Construction — Components and assembly of a functional pinhole camera.

#### Making a Pinhole

The quality of your pinhole determines image sharpness. Use a thin sheet of brass, copper, or aluminum foil. Drill or carefully punch a hole, then smooth and enlarge it to exactly 0.5-1.0mm using sandpaper or a jeweler's file. Mount it in a cardboard or metal ring that fits your box opening.

#### Shutter Mechanism

A simple shutter can be:

-   **Sliding cardboard:** A black cardboard panel that slides in/out of a groove
-   **Lens cap:** A removable cap over the pinhole opening
-   **Tape flap:** Black tape that you peel back and reapply
-   **Mechanical:** A spring-loaded or string-controlled flap

### Calculating Exposure Time

Exposure time depends on light, film sensitivity, and f-stop. The f-stop of a pinhole camera is calculated as:

f-stop = focal length ÷ pinhole diameter

Example: 200mm focal length ÷ 0.5mm pinhole = f/400
This is a very slow lens, requiring long exposures.

#### Exposure Time Guidelines

:::info-box
**Quick Reference: Pinhole Camera Exposure**
- Faster film (ISO 400) exposes in 1/4 to 1/10 the time of ISO 100
- Overcast days multiply times by 4–10 compared to bright sun
- Bracket exposures by trying 1x, 0.5x, and 2x your calculated time
- Vibration and subject motion are more visible with long exposures; stabilize the camera and avoid busy scenes
:::

:::tip
Always bracket exposures: try the calculated time, then double and half it. Wind, vibration, and light variations affect results. Keep detailed notes of settings and results to improve future attempts.
:::

### Film Loading & Development

Load photographic paper or film in complete darkness. A darkroom or changing bag is essential. Mount the paper flat against the back wall, securing it with small pieces of tape or clips. Ensure the pinhole is unobstructed and the shutter works smoothly.

After exposure, seal and store the camera away from light. Develop the paper using standard photographic chemistry (see Developing & Darkroom section). Results are usually contact prints—same size as the negative or paper.

Pinhole cameras create characteristic images: soft focus, vignetting (darkening at edges), and interesting distortions. These qualities are often aesthetically appealing and complement documentary photography.

## Chemical Photography: Silver Halide to Cyanotype

Chemical photography uses light-sensitive compounds to create permanent images. The most common traditional methods—daguerreotypes, calotypes, and prints—all exploit the chemical behavior of silver compounds. Simpler processes like cyanotype (blueprint) are accessible with minimal equipment.

### Silver Halide Process: The Foundation

The standard black-and-white photographic process is based on silver halide (silver combined with bromine, chlorine, or iodine). These compounds darken when exposed to light, allowing images to be captured and developed.

#### Historical Approach: Coating Paper with Silver Nitrate

Before commercial photographic paper existed, photographers coated plain paper with silver nitrate solution (15-20% concentration). The process:

1
#### Prepare Paper

Use high-quality paper (watercolor paper works well). Brush or float it on a silver nitrate solution. The silver ions bind to the paper fibers.

2
#### Sensitize

Expose the coated paper to dilute iodine or bromine vapor, which converts soluble silver nitrate to insoluble silver halide—the light-sensitive form.

3
#### Expose

Place a negative on the sensitized paper and expose to bright sunlight or diffuse light. Where light passes through the negative, silver halide reduces to metallic silver (darkening).

4
#### Develop (Gallic Acid)

Brush the paper with gallic acid solution. This amplifies the exposure, developing the latent image into visible silver deposits. The longer you brush, the darker the print.

5
#### Fix (Sodium Thiosulfate)

Immerse the print in a solution of sodium thiosulfate ("hypo"). This dissolves remaining silver halide, stopping development and stabilizing the image. Wash thoroughly after fixing.

:::warning
Silver nitrate stains skin and clothes permanently (dark brown/black). Wear rubber gloves and old clothes. Work over paper. Keep vinegar nearby to neutralize spills. Do not ingest silver compounds.
:::

### Daguerreotype: Mirror-Like Images

Daguerreotypes, invented in 1839, use a polished silver plate coated with silver halide. The process is complex and toxic (mercury vapor is involved in development), but results in extraordinarily detailed, long-lasting images. Daguerreotypes require:

-   Polished copper or silver plate
-   Iodine vapor to sensitize
-   Mercury vapor for development (dangerous)
-   Gold or other toning for permanence

Given the mercury hazard and complexity, daguerreotype is better studied than attempted without proper ventilation and safety equipment. Modern practitioners use modified, less hazardous procedures.

### Cyanotype: The Simplest Chemical Process

Cyanotype (blueprint) is the most beginner-friendly chemical photographic process. It requires only two inexpensive, non-toxic chemicals and produces striking blue-and-white images. The process is stable and long-lasting.

#### Materials Needed

#### Potassium Ferricyanide

Available from photography suppliers, chemical suppliers, or online. A small container (25-50g) costs little and lasts a long time.

#### Ferric Ammonium Citrate

Also available from suppliers. Ensure you get the brown/green form, not the red form (different chemistry).

#### High-Quality Paper

Watercolor paper, cotton rag paper, or smooth paper stock. The coating will soak in, so porous papers work best.

#### Sunlight or UV Light

Direct sunlight is ideal. UV lamps (blacklight) work for indoor use.

#### Negative

A photographic negative or any transparent film with an image (printed on acetate sheet).

#### Water for Washing

Running water or distilled water in a tray. The wash removes unreacted chemicals.

#### Cyanotype Procedure

1
#### Prepare Sensitizer Solution

Mix equal parts potassium ferricyanide and ferric ammonium citrate solutions (e.g., 25ml each). A common recipe is 1.5g of each chemical dissolved in 45ml distilled water. The solution is yellow-green and light-sensitive—prepare just before use.

2
#### Coat Paper

In dim light or under red safelight (optional), brush the sensitizer solution evenly onto the paper. Use a wide brush or foam applicator. Work quickly and smoothly to avoid streaks. Allow to dry completely in darkness (1-2 hours or more). The paper will be light yellow-green when dry.

3
#### Expose

Place the negative directly on the dried, sensitized paper. Lay both on a flat surface in bright sunlight or under UV light. The exposure time varies: bright sunlight typically requires 5-20 minutes; UV lamps may need 5-30 minutes. You can peek at corners to monitor exposure (it darkens from yellow-green to blue-green).

4
#### Wash

Remove the negative and immediately rinse the paper under running water. The unreacted sensitizer is soluble and will wash away in seconds. Rinse thoroughly for 3-5 minutes, agitating the paper. The image will appear as bright blue on white paper, fully visible after the first minute of washing.

5
#### Dry

Hang or lay the paper flat to dry. Cyanotype images can continue to strengthen slightly as they dry (a process called "clearing"). Once dry, they are stable and permanent.

#### Cyanotype Characteristics

Cyanotypes produce white images on brilliant Prussian blue backgrounds—the inverse of standard photographs. The tone is striking and distinctive. With experimentation, you can:

-   Reduce exposure for lighter blues
-   Tone the image with tea, coffee, or other solutions to shift colors
-   Bleach portions with dilute hydrogen peroxide to create partial images
-   Use alternate papers (fabric, leather) for unusual effects

:::tip
Cyanotype is forgiving and teaches the fundamentals of chemical photography without toxicity or complexity. Start with cyanotype to build confidence, then explore more advanced processes.
:::

### Making Negatives for Chemical Printing

All chemical printing processes require a negative—a transparent image where blacks are clear (light passes through) and whites are opaque. Negatives can be created by:

-   **Photographic film:** Traditional black-and-white film developed normally produces negatives
-   **Digital printing:** Print digital images on transparent acetate sheets using an inkjet printer (high black density required)
-   **Drawing:** Draw or paint the image directly on transparent film or glass
-   **Photocopy:** Photocopy images onto transparent acetate sheets

Higher contrast in the negative produces sharper, more saturated prints. For cyanotype, a dense negative (heavy black areas) is ideal.

## Developing & Darkroom Methods

Darkroom work is the crucial step between exposure and final photograph. Whether using traditional film or sensitized paper, chemical development transforms the latent (invisible) image into a visible, stable photograph.

### Essential Darkroom Setup

A functional darkroom is simply a light-proof space where you control the light to your advantage. It can be a dedicated room, a large closet, or even a lightproof tent or changing bag.

#### Light Safelight

Some photographic materials are sensitive only to certain wavelengths. Black-and-white printing paper is least sensitive to red and amber light, so a red safelight allows you to see while working. A safelight typically consists of:

-   A 25-40 watt bulb in a fixture
-   A red or amber filter to block blue and green light
-   Mounting 3-4 feet above your work surface

Without a safelight, you work entirely in darkness or with a flashlight under your shirt. A simple red safelight greatly improves efficiency and safety.

#### Chemical Trays

Standard darkroom work uses three trays for black-and-white prints:

#### Developer Tray

A tray holding diluted developer (e.g., D-76, HC-110). Temperature should be 20°C (68°F) or as specified. Keep temperature consistent.

#### Stop Bath Tray

A tray holding diluted acetic acid solution (or plain water). This stops development immediately, halting the reaction. Temperature less critical.

#### Fixer Tray

A tray holding sodium thiosulfate ("hypo") solution. This dissolves remaining light-sensitive material, making the print stable to light. Work here can be done in normal light once the print has fixed for ~30 seconds.

#### Chemical Temperature Control

Developer temperature profoundly affects development speed and image contrast. Keep chemicals within ±2°C of the recommended temperature:

-   Warm water bath under the developer tray
-   Ice water bath if developer is too warm
-   Use an accurate thermometer
-   Adjust development time if temperature deviates

### Contact Printing: Simplest Method

Contact printing produces a print the same size as the negative. It requires minimal equipment and is the foundation of all printing:

1
#### Place Negative on Paper

In darkness, lay the negative flat on the photographic paper (emulsion side down). The emulsion is the dull side; the shiny side is the base.

2
#### Expose

Place a glass or clear plastic sheet on top to hold the negative flat. Expose to light (safelight, enlarger, or flashlight) for 5-30 seconds depending on negative density and light intensity. Start with 10 seconds and adjust.

3
#### Develop

Quickly slide the paper into the developer tray. Use tongs to agitate gently every few seconds. The image appears gradually. Development time is typically 1-2 minutes.

4
#### Stop Bath

Remove from developer with tongs and immediately place in the stop bath tray. Agitate for 30 seconds to stop development completely.

5
#### Fix

Transfer to the fixer tray. After ~30 seconds in fixer, you can turn on the lights. Continue fixing for the time specified on the fixer label (typically 5-10 minutes for prints).

6
#### Wash & Dry

Rinse thoroughly under running water for 10+ minutes (or use a wash tray with running water). Hang or lay flat to dry. Proper washing prevents yellow staining over time.

### Enlarger Basics: Making Bigger Prints

An enlarger is a device that shines light through a negative mounted on a stage, projecting an enlarged image onto paper below. This allows you to make large prints from small negatives or to crop and adjust composition.

#### Basic Enlarger Components

-   **Light source:** Bulb or LED illuminating the negative uniformly
-   **Negative stage:** Holder for the film negative
-   **Lens:** Projects the image onto the baseboard
-   **Baseboard:** Flat surface where the paper is placed and exposed
-   **Bellows or rail:** Allows focus and size adjustment

#### Enlargement Process

-   Insert the negative into the enlarger
-   Set lens aperture (f/5.6 or f/8 is typical)
-   Adjust height and focus to get desired image size and sharpness
-   Use a test strip (small piece of paper) to determine exposure time
-   Expose full-size paper, then process as in contact printing

:::tip
Start with contact prints to understand development chemistry and timing. Learn to expose and develop before investing in an enlarger. Many contact prints from 4x5 or larger format negatives are already substantial in size.
:::

## Digital Camera Salvage: Recovering Images Without Grid Power

If digital cameras or smartphones survive an emergency, recovering and preserving the images is critical. Modern devices rely on batteries and electricity; knowing how to manage them without the grid is essential.

### Battery Management

#### Solar Charging

Small solar panels can trickle-charge device batteries. A 5-10 watt solar panel with appropriate USB adapter can charge most phones and cameras. In sunlight, expect a full charge in 4-8 hours depending on panel size and battery capacity. Battery packs with built-in solar charging are widely available.

#### Mechanical Charging (Manual)

Hand-crank chargers and pressure-activated chargers exist but are slow. They're useful for topping up a device or running in emergencies when solar isn't available. Expect 30 minutes of cranking to provide 1-2 hours of phone use.

#### Power Optimization

Extend battery life by:

-   Turning off wireless (WiFi, Bluetooth, mobile data)
-   Reducing screen brightness to minimum usable level
-   Disabling location services and background app refresh
-   Putting the device in low-power or airplane mode
-   Turning off camera features like flash when not needed

### Memory Card Management

Memory cards (SD, microSD, CompactFlash) are the most reliable form of image storage. They hold data indefinitely without power and are incredibly durable. If a camera battery dies, the images on the memory card are safe.

#### Safe Card Extraction

-   Power off the camera before removing the card
-   Never remove the card during file transfer
-   Keep cards in protective cases
-   Store cards in a cool, dry location
-   Create backups of critical images immediately

#### Card Readers

A USB memory card reader is invaluable. Even if the camera is broken, a working card reader allows you to access images. Keep a compact card reader with your survival supplies. Readers are small, cheap, and work with any computer or device with USB capability.

### Image Backup & Data Duplication

The golden rule of data preservation: no backup is a single copy. Create redundant backups in different locations and on different media:

-   **Original:** Keep the original memory card, safely stored
-   **Computer backup:** Transfer to a local computer, external drive, or NAS
-   **Physical prints:** Print critical images on archival paper
-   **Secondary storage:** Copy files to a second external drive or different location

If you have electricity (solar, wind, hydro, or grid), prioritize transferring images to reliable storage immediately. Don't rely on camera batteries or memory cards alone.

### Improvised Printing Without Electricity

If you cannot print images normally, you can create a basic display and record it:

#### Screen Projection & Tracing

-   Display the image on a phone, tablet, or computer screen
-   Place a glass or transparent material over the screen
-   Tape paper or transparent film to the glass
-   Manually trace the image outlines using the screen as a light source
-   Add details by hand, referring to the screen image

This is slow but creates a hand-drawn record of the digital image. For important documentation, the effort is worthwhile.

#### Digital-to-Cyanotype Method

-   Adjust digital images to high contrast using any available software
-   Print the inverted, high-contrast image on transparent acetate or thin plastic
-   Use this as a negative for cyanotype or silver halide printing
-   Produces a physical, archival-quality print

:::warning
Never rely solely on digital storage for irreplaceable photographs. Create physical prints or archival copies. Digital files are vulnerable to: data corruption, storage media failure, format obsolescence, and power loss.
:::

## Printing Photographs: Creating Physical Records

A photograph on paper is more permanent, shareable, and robust than one stored digitally or only viewed on a screen. Printing creates tangible records that can be shared, archived, and survived intact.

### Contact Printing (Recap with Emphasis)

Contact printing is the simplest and most direct method. The negative is placed directly on photographic paper and exposed to light. The resulting print is the same size as the negative. This method:

-   Requires no enlarger
-   Uses minimal chemicals and paper
-   Produces sharp images (no lens required)
-   Works with any light source (sun, lamp, safelight with extended exposure)

For 4x5 negatives or larger, contact prints are already substantial in size. Combined with a good negative, contact printing is fully capable of producing exhibition-quality images.

### Solar Printing: Using Sunlight

Solar printing is the most electricity-free method. Sunlight alone provides sufficient energy to expose photographs on appropriately sensitized paper.

#### Cyanotype by Sunlight

As detailed earlier, cyanotype requires only sunlight and two simple chemicals. The exposure times are:

-   **Direct sunlight:** 5-20 minutes
-   **Bright shade:** 20-60 minutes
-   **Overcast day:** 60+ minutes or all day

#### Silver Halide by Sunlight

Historically, silver halide prints were exposed by sunlight alone. A negative was placed on sensitized paper and left in sunlight until development. This is slower than modern printing but entirely feasible:

-   Dense negatives require shorter exposure (faster)
-   Thin negatives require longer exposure
-   Can be monitored by peeking at corners to judge exposure progress

### Toning for Permanence & Color Variation

Toning changes the color and improves the permanence of black-and-white photographs. Common toning methods include:

#### Tea & Coffee Toning

A simple and accessible method. After fixing and washing:

-   Prepare strong brewed tea or coffee
-   Allow it to cool slightly (warm, not hot)
-   Immerse the wet print in the solution for 5-15 minutes
-   The print gradually shifts from black to warmer sepia/brown tones
-   Longer soaking produces more dramatic color shifts
-   Rinse and dry as normal

#### Selenium Toning

Professional method using selenium toner solution. Improves image permanence and shifts blacks to slightly warmer tones. Requires careful handling (selenium fumes are toxic) but is more reliable than organic toning.

#### Gold Chloride Toning

Creates rich purple and red tones. Expensive but produces beautiful, very permanent results. Historically used for museum-quality prints. Minimal chemical hazard; main concern is cost.

#### Iron & Copper Toning

Various iron and copper compounds create different color shifts (blue, green, red). Recipe-based methods exist but require specific chemistry knowledge.

:::tip
Start with tea or coffee toning—it's safe, cheap, and produces pleasant warm tones. If you want maximum permanence without fancy equipment, use archival-quality paper and proper fixing and washing (no toning required, though it helps).
:::

### Printing onto Unusual Surfaces

Photographic emulsion can be applied to almost any surface. Possibilities include:

-   **Fabric:** Coat cotton or linen with emulsion, expose through negative, develop
-   **Wood:** Coat sanded wood with emulsion for rustic, artistic prints
-   **Leather:** Apply emulsion to untreated leather for unique surfaces
-   **Plaster:** Coat wet plaster with emulsion, expose, and the image sets as the plaster dries

These require experimentation but offer creative alternatives when paper is scarce or when unique surfaces are desired for record-keeping.

### Making Enlargements Without Electricity

If electricity is unavailable, you cannot use a traditional enlarger. Alternatives include:

#### Optical Enlargement (Camera Obscura)

Use a camera obscura to project a negative and trace the enlargement. The focal length and distance from pinhole to wall determine magnification.

#### Mechanical Enlargement

Use a grid method: divide the negative into squares, then draw a larger grid on the paper and copy each square. Labor-intensive but produces accurate enlargements.

#### Optical Printing

A lens focused on the negative and projected onto paper can enlarge the image. This requires optical skill but is doable with careful setup and a bright light source.

## Documentary Methods Without Cameras

Cameras are not the only way to document. Detailed sketching, botanical illustration, measurement drawing, and plaster casts are time-tested methods that preserve information with precision. Many of these methods were primary documentation tools for centuries and remain invaluable.

### Detailed Field Sketching

A careful pencil or pen sketch captures information as effectively as a photograph and often more accurately (since the artist interprets and emphasizes important features). Field sketching is essential for:

-   Recording structures or landscapes in detail
-   Documenting conditions that photographs miss (internal structure, materials, scale)
-   Creating permanent records without chemicals or electricity
-   Developing observation and drawing skills

#### Field Sketch Techniques

-   **Outline first:** Sketch the overall shape and proportions lightly
-   **Add details:** Work inward with increasingly fine detail
-   **Include scale:** Draw a ruler, hand, or familiar object for size reference
-   **Note materials:** Label textures, colors, and compositions in the sketch or notes
-   **Annotate:** Write measurements, dates, locations, and other critical information directly on or beside the sketch
-   **Use perspective:** If depicting a structure or landscape, establish a perspective grid for accuracy

### Botanical Illustration

Botanical drawings must be accurate and precise. They document species identification, growth patterns, and characteristics that matter for identification, cultivation, or medicine.

#### Essential Elements of a Botanical Drawing

-   **Overall form:** The habit of the plant (tall, spreading, bush-like)
-   **Leaf shape and arrangement:** Critical for identification
-   **Flowers or seeds:** The reproductive parts, often with enlarged detail drawings
-   **Scale:** Actual size or clearly marked magnification ratios
-   **Specimen details:** Notes on location, date, season, and any unusual features

#### Pressed Specimens

Combine drawings with preserved specimens:

-   Press plants between paper or cloth to dry and preserve
-   Mount the dried specimen on archive paper alongside detailed notes
-   Include identification, location, date, and uses
-   Store in acid-free folders in a cool, dry place

A pressed specimen plus supporting photograph or drawing creates a durable reference collection.

### Measurement Drawing & Technical Illustration

Measurement drawing produces accurate records of objects or structures by recording precise dimensions and proportions.

#### Procedure

-   **Measure all critical dimensions:** Use a ruler, tape measure, or calipers
-   **Record proportions:** Note relationships (height is 3x width, etc.)
-   **Sketch to scale:** Draw the object using the measurements, scaled proportionally on paper
-   **Include views:** Show front, side, and top views (engineering-style drawings)
-   **Detail important features:** Enlarged drawings of joints, connections, or intricate areas
-   **Annotate fully:** Label all dimensions, materials, and features

#### Grid Method for Accuracy

For complex or irregular shapes:

-   Overlay a grid of squares on the subject (using chalk, tape, or imagined)
-   Draw a proportionally scaled grid on paper
-   Copy the contents of each grid square from subject to drawing
-   This method guarantees proportional accuracy

### Plaster Casts & Molding

Creating plaster casts preserves 3D information. Uses include:

#### Animal Tracks & Footprints

-   Find a clear print in soil or sand
-   Build a simple dam around it using card or clay
-   Mix plaster of Paris to a thick slurry and pour into the dam
-   Allow to set fully (1-2 hours or overnight)
-   Remove carefully—the cast is a reverse (negative) of the print
-   Oil-based clay molds are preferable for repeated casting

#### Artifact Casts

Casts document pottery, coins, carved stones, or other artifacts:

-   Make a flexible mold (silicone, rubber, or oil-based clay)
-   Pour plaster or resin into the mold
-   Allow to set and remove carefully
-   The cast preserves all surface details

#### Advantages

-   3D records that show detail inaccessible to 2D drawings
-   Allow handling and study without damaging originals
-   Portable reproductions for sharing knowledge
-   Can be made indefinitely from molds

:::tip
Combine multiple documentation methods: sketch the object, measure and record dimensions, take a photograph, and make a cast if appropriate. Redundancy in documentation ensures information survives intact.
:::

### Comparison: Drawing vs. Photography

#### Advantage of Drawing

-   No equipment required
-   Emphasizes important details
-   Artist interpretation adds information
-   No chemical dependencies
-   Creates engaging, clear records

#### Advantage of Photography

-   Captures ALL details accurately
-   Faster to document many items
-   No artistic skill required
-   Records color and tone precisely
-   Can capture context and scale

## Safety & Chemical Handling

Photography involves chemicals that demand careful handling. Understanding hazards and implementing safety practices protects your health and ensures quality results.

### Darkroom Ventilation & Safety

#### Air Circulation

Darkroom work produces chemical fumes—particularly from developer, stop bath, and fixer vapors. Adequate ventilation is essential:

-   Install a window fan or exhaust fan venting to the outside
-   Never work in a sealed room for extended periods
-   Use a respirator (dust mask) if ventilation is limited
-   Take breaks, especially during long printing sessions

#### Lighting Hazards

Safelights and darkroom lighting can generate heat:

-   Keep safelights away from flammable materials
-   Use LED or low-heat bulbs when possible
-   Avoid extended exposure to safelight heat (can degrade prints or film)
-   Ensure darkroom electrical wiring is properly grounded to prevent shock hazard in wet environments

:::warning
Chemical fumes accumulate in poor ventilation. Symptoms of overexposure: headaches, dizziness, nausea, respiratory irritation. If symptoms occur, leave the darkroom immediately and get fresh air. For chronic darkroom use, ensure continuous ventilation and use respiratory protection when needed.
:::

### Chemical Safety Essentials

#### Silver Nitrate

-   Causes permanent dark stains on skin and clothes (brown/black)
-   Wear nitrile gloves and old clothes
-   Work over absorbent paper
-   Keep vinegar nearby to neutralize spills (changes brown to white temporarily)
-   Do not ingest; toxic if swallowed

#### Potassium Ferricyanide & Ferric Ammonium Citrate

-   Low toxicity, generally safe for cyanotype
-   Wear gloves to prevent skin irritation
-   Rinse thoroughly after contact
-   Ensure proper disposal (do not dump unlimited quantities down the drain)

#### Selenium Toner

-   Selenium vapor is toxic; avoid inhalation
-   Use only with adequate ventilation (fume hood preferred)
-   Wear gloves and eye protection
-   Professional-use chemical; requires care

#### Acetic Acid (Stop Bath)

-   Corrosive; causes skin burns
-   Wear gloves and avoid splashing
-   Ventilation needed to reduce vapor inhalation
-   Keep water nearby for accidental splashes

#### Sodium Thiosulfate (Fixer)

-   Low toxicity when diluted
-   Wear gloves; prolonged contact can irritate skin
-   Ensure good ventilation

:::warning
Never mix chemicals or create your own formulas without thorough knowledge. Incompatible chemicals can produce toxic gases (e.g., mixing oxidizers with organic materials). Stick to established recipes. Keep chemicals in labeled, clearly marked containers. Store incompatible chemicals separately. Follow product safety data sheets (SDS) for all chemicals.
:::

### Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

#### Essential Equipment

-   **Nitrile gloves:** Chemical-resistant, replace when damaged
-   **Apron or old clothes:** Protects from stains and splashes
-   **Eye protection:** Goggles or glasses to prevent splashes
-   **Footwear:** Closed-toe shoes or boots to protect from spills

#### Optional but Recommended

-   **Respirator:** Dust or fume respirator for extended darkroom sessions
-   **Apron with pockets:** For keeping notes and timers at hand

### Emergency Preparedness

#### Chemical Spill Response

-   **Silver Nitrate:** Wipe up immediately with paper towels (wear gloves). Rinse with water. The stain on skin will fade over days but can be accelerated with vinegar.
-   **Other chemicals:** Contain with absorbent material, then rinse with water (unless otherwise specified on SDS).
-   **Eye contact:** Rinse immediately with clean water for 15+ minutes. Seek medical attention.

#### First Aid

-   Keep first aid supplies in the darkroom or nearby
-   Have a phone available for emergency calls
-   Know the location of the nearest poison control center number

### Waste Disposal

#### Safe Disposal Methods

-   **Photo chemicals:** Many communities have hazardous waste collection services. Collect used chemicals in sealed, labeled containers and drop off at designated facilities.
-   **Fixer solution:** Can be recycled or neutralized according to local regulations. Do not pour unlimited quantities down the drain.
-   **Silver recovery:** Silver from photographic solutions can be recovered and recycled by professional services (economically viable for high-volume users).

Consult local environmental regulations for specific requirements in your area.

## Archival Storage: Protecting Photographs Long-Term

Creating a photograph is only half the work. Preserving it requires understanding the threats to photographic materials and implementing storage methods that protect against degradation. Photographs can last centuries if stored properly—or decay to complete ruin in decades if not.

### Primary Threats to Photographs

#### Light

Both visible and ultraviolet light fade photographs. Blue, green, and UV wavelengths are most damaging. Store photographs in darkness:

-   Use opaque storage boxes
-   Keep in closets or rooms with minimal light
-   Avoid framing prints under glass in sunlit locations (except with UV-protective glass)
-   Display only archival copies; keep originals in storage

#### Moisture & Humidity

Excessive moisture causes:

-   Fungal growth and mold (green/black spots)
-   Emulsion softening and lifting
-   Paper warping and buckling
-   Ink or dye bleeding and fading

Low humidity can cause brittleness and cracking. Ideal relative humidity is 30-50% for most photographs. Maintain consistent humidity:

-   Store in climate-controlled areas
-   Use desiccant packets (silica gel) in storage boxes
-   Monitor humidity with simple hygrometers
-   Avoid basements (damp) and attics (temperature extremes)

#### Temperature

High temperatures accelerate all chemical degradation. Cool storage significantly extends photograph life:

-   Store at 15-21°C (59-70°F) if possible
-   Stable temperature more important than absolute temperature
-   Avoid freezing (causes moisture and brittleness)
-   Never store in hot attics or car trunks

#### Pests & Insects

Silverfish, cockroaches, and other insects eat photographic emulsion and paper. Prevention:

-   Use sealed, pest-proof storage boxes
-   Keep storage areas clean and sealed
-   Use cedar blocks or diatomaceous earth (food-grade) near storage areas
-   Never use chemical pesticides near photographs (can cause staining)

#### Chemical Degradation

Acidic storage materials cause photographs to deteriorate. Acid comes from:

-   Cardboard (unless acid-free)
-   Wooden shelving
-   Old paper or adhesives
-   Air pollutants (especially in industrial areas)

Use only acid-free storage materials.

### Archival Storage Standards

#### Acid-Free Materials

All materials in contact with photographs must be acid-free:

-   **Acid-free tissue paper:** Separating prints in storage
-   **Acid-free cardboard:** Storage boxes and dividers
-   **Acid-free paper:** For backing or mounting
-   **Photo-safe sleeves:** Polyester or acid-free paper sleeves for 35mm slides or negatives
-   **Archival plastic:** Polyester (Mylar) or polypropylene, NOT PVC (vinyl)

:::warning
Never store photographs in regular plastic bags, vinyl sleeves, or cardboard boxes. These leach acids and degradation products that destroy images. Use only materials labeled "acid-free" or "archival quality."
:::

#### Storage Containers

#### Print Storage Box

Acid-free cardboard or wood box, appropriately sized for prints. Store prints flat, separated by acid-free tissue. Drawers or compartments prevent stacking pressure.

#### Negative Sleeves

Polyester or acid-free glassine sleeves for film negatives. Four or six compartments per sleeve. Allows viewing without handling the negative directly.

#### Slide Storage

Acid-free slide boxes or metal storage cabinets. Keep slides cool (15-21°C) and dry. Use glassine sleeves to prevent dust.

#### Card Index

Create a catalog on acid-free cards noting: image subject, date, location, negative number, storage location. Store this index with the collection.

### Mounting & Framing for Display

If displaying photographs, use archival-quality mounting and framing materials:

-   **Acid-free mat board:** For mounting prints
-   **Archival adhesive:** pH-neutral, reversible adhesives (not rubber cement or super glue)
-   **UV-protective glass:** Blocks ultraviolet light that fades images
-   **Archival frame backing:** Acid-free cardboard behind the image
-   **Proper spacing:** Never allow the glass to touch the print (moisture and pressure damage)

#### Mounting Methods

-   **Dry mounting:** Heat-seal prints to backing (requires dry mounting press)
-   **Hinged mounting:** Small acid-free tape hinges attach print at the top edge only, allowing natural movement
-   **Window mat:** Print is placed behind a cut mat, held in place by hinges
-   **Encapsulation:** Print is sealed between two sheets of Mylar, visible but protected

### Multiple Copies in Different Locations

The most important preservation rule: never keep a single copy. A disaster (fire, flood, theft, mold) can wipe out your entire collection. Create geographic redundancy:

**3-2-1 Backup Strategy for Photographs:**
Keep 3 copies of critical photographs. Store 2 copies locally (for access) and 1 copy in a geographically distant location. Use different storage media (prints, negatives, digital files) to ensure at least one survives any single failure mode.

#### Distribution Strategies

-   **Home archive:** Primary copies in your residence, properly stored
-   **Distant family/friends:** Keep duplicate prints or negatives with trusted relatives in different locations
-   **Community archive:** Some communities maintain historical photograph archives; contribute copies of local documentation
-   **Waterproof container:** Copies in a sealed, waterproof box buried or hidden on your property
-   **Digital backups:** Files on encrypted external drives kept in multiple locations

### Restoration & Damage Repair

Photographs that show signs of damage (mold, fading, tears, creases) can often be partially restored or prevented from further deterioration:

#### Mold & Fungal Growth

-   Increase ventilation and reduce humidity immediately
-   Allow photographs to air-dry in a dark, well-ventilated space
-   Do not aggressively scrub (damages the image)
-   Once dry, store in desiccant-rich containers to prevent recurrence

#### Creases & Tears

-   Do not attempt to iron or flatten (heat and pressure cause permanent damage)
-   Gently flatten under weight in a dry environment (slow process)
-   Small tears can be stabilized with archival tape on the back
-   Large tears may require professional conservation

#### Fading & Discoloration

-   Cannot be reversed; focus on preventing further fading
-   Store in darkness, low humidity, and cool temperatures
-   Make duplicate prints from negatives while quality allows
-   Use toning (sepia, etc.) to enhance faded images

:::tip
The best preservation strategy is prevention. Excellent storage conditions make restoration unnecessary. Invest time and minimal resources setting up proper storage now rather than attempting expensive restoration later.
:::

### Cataloging & Documentation

A well-organized archive is only useful if you can find images when needed. Create a catalog:

-   **Numbering system:** Assign a unique number to each photograph or negative
-   **Card or digital index:** Record subject, date, location, photographer, condition, storage location
-   **Thumbnail images:** Small reference photos showing what each file contains
-   **Physical organization:** Arrange storage to match the index (by date, subject, or number)
-   **Backup catalog:** Keep a copy of the index separate from the photographs

A poorly archived collection is effectively lost—you have images but cannot find them. Spend time cataloging.

## Digital Preservation Methods

In modern survival scenarios, digital photographs may be created on phones, cameras, or computers before grid power is lost. Unlike physical prints and negatives, digital files face unique preservation challenges: storage media failure, format obsolescence, data corruption, and eventual power loss. A comprehensive digital preservation strategy combines multiple approaches to ensure long-term accessibility.

### Digital Storage Media Longevity

Different storage media have vastly different expected lifespans:

**Solid-State Drives (SSDs):**
- **Lifespan:** 10-20 years under ideal conditions
- **Failure mode:** Flash memory cells degrade; data loss accelerates at end of life with little warning
- **Advantage:** No moving parts; resistant to shock and vibration
- **Disadvantage:** Requires occasional power to refresh cells (best practice: power once yearly)
- **Cost:** Moderate
- **Recovery difficulty:** Difficult once failed; professional recovery expensive

**Hard Disk Drives (HDDs):**
- **Lifespan:** 5-10 years under ideal conditions
- **Failure mode:** Mechanical failure (head crash) or electronic control board failure
- **Advantage:** Large capacity; can store thousands of photographs
- **Disadvantage:** Moving parts; vulnerable to shock, vibration, and dust
- **Cost:** Low
- **Recovery difficulty:** Possible but expensive; depends on failure type

**Optical Media (DVDs, Blu-rays):**
- **Lifespan:** 20-50 years (varies by manufacturing quality; archival-grade media better)
- **Failure mode:** Dye layer degrades; data becomes unreadable
- **Advantage:** Stable if stored properly; readable by basic drives; doesn't require power for storage
- **Disadvantage:** Slow to write; requires careful handling; susceptible to scratches; format may become obsolete
- **Cost:** Very low
- **Recovery difficulty:** Moderate; scratches can be polished; dye damage is permanent

**Magnetic Tape (LTO, archival formats):**
- **Lifespan:** 30-50+ years (LTO specification claims 100+ years for archival-grade)
- **Failure mode:** Binder degrades; tape sheds; oxide layer separates
- **Advantage:** Extremely durable; does not require power; excellent for cold storage
- **Disadvantage:** Requires specialized drives to read; not suitable for frequent access; bulky
- **Cost:** Moderate (drives expensive, but tape media cheap)
- **Recovery difficulty:** Low; can be cleaned or digitally recovered if readable

**Flash Memory Cards (SD, microSD, CompactFlash):**
- **Lifespan:** 10-15 years
- **Failure mode:** Flash memory cells degrade; controller failure
- **Advantage:** Very small; no power required for storage; easy to transport
- **Disadvantage:** Easy to lose; susceptible to physical damage; file system can become corrupted
- **Cost:** Very low
- **Recovery difficulty:** Moderate; professional recovery possible for mechanical damage

:::warning
**No single storage medium is truly permanent.** All media degrades over time. Digital preservation requires redundancy, format migration, and periodic refreshing (re-copying files to new media). Never rely on a single copy.
:::

### Data Redundancy Strategies

The cardinal rule of digital preservation: **3-2-1 Rule** — maintain 3 copies, 2 on different media types, 1 in a geographically separate location.

**Example 3-2-1 strategy for a 50GB photograph collection:**
1. **Local SSD (primary working copy):** 256GB SSD in your computer
2. **Local optical backup:** Burned to archival-grade DVDs or Blu-rays (multiple discs); stored in acid-free boxes
3. **Remote backup:** Copy on encrypted external drive or hard disk stored with trusted person/location 100+ miles away
4. **Optional 4th copy:** Tape archive for long-term cold storage (if drive available)

**Benefits of this approach:**
- Local copies for immediate access and editing
- Different media types ensure at least one survives technology-specific failures
- Geographic separation protects against local disaster (fire, theft, flood)
- Multiple redundancy protects against media failure

### File Format Preservation

Digital file formats become obsolete. Proprietary formats from obscure manufacturers may become unreadable in 20 years when hardware and software are no longer available.

**Recommended formats for long-term preservation:**

**For photographs:**
- **TIFF (Tagged Image File Format):** Uncompressed or lossless compression; widely supported; excellent for long-term storage
- **PNG (Portable Network Graphics):** Lossless compression; excellent color support; widely supported; good for archival
- **JPEG:** Lossy compression; universal support; acceptable for documents; NOT recommended for archival photography (quality loss is permanent)

**Avoid:**
- **Proprietary manufacturer formats:** RAW files from specific camera brands may require proprietary software
- **Obsolete formats:** Old digital camera formats (early TIFF variants, manufacturer-specific formats) may become unreadable
- **Heavily compressed formats:** Excessive JPEGs, MP4, or other lossy formats lose quality permanently

**Format migration strategy:**
1. Store original files in TIFF (maximum quality, uncompressed or lossless)
2. Create working copies in JPEG for easy viewing and sharing
3. Every 10-15 years, re-examine file formats and consider migrating to newer standards if originals are at risk
4. Keep metadata (date, location, photographer, copyright) in standardized formats (EXIF, XMP tags)

### Physical Print Archiving

Despite digital preservation advantages, **the most reliable long-term preservation is physical prints**. A well-stored photograph print can survive 100+ years without electricity or format migration.

**Strategy: Create archival prints from digital files**

1. **Select critical images:** Not every photograph needs to be printed; prioritize irreplaceable, historically important, or sentimental images
2. **Print on archival paper:** Use papers explicitly labeled "archival" or "acid-free" (museum quality)
3. **Use archival inks:** Pigment-based inks (not dye-based) are more permanent; matched ink+paper combinations last 100+ years
4. **Proper exposure and development:** Color balance and contrast affect longevity; slightly darker prints fade better than light prints
5. **Protective storage:** Use acid-free sleeves, boxes, and mounting materials (see Archival Storage section)

**Recommended workflow:**
- Annual review: Select 10-20 favorite photographs
- Print on archival-quality materials using quality printer (or professional lab)
- Store in acid-free boxes, climate-controlled location
- Distribute copies to trusted locations (geographically diverse)

This creates a hybrid system: digital files for convenience and accessibility; prints for long-term, technology-independent preservation.

### Metadata & Documentation for Digital Files

Digital files without accompanying metadata are orphaned—future readers won't know when, where, or why photographs were taken.

**Essential metadata to embed or document:**

| Metadata Field | Format | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Date Created | EXIF DateTimeOriginal (YYYY-MM-DD) | 2026-02-19 |
| Time Created | EXIF DateTime (HH:MM:SS) | 14:32:45 |
| Location | EXIF GPS or text note | 40.7128° N, 74.0060° W (or "Smith Farm, North Field") |
| Subject Description | EXIF ImageDescription, keywords | "Heirloom tomato crop, blight damage, June 2026" |
| Photographer | EXIF Artist | "Jane Smith" |
| Copyright | EXIF Copyright | "Copyright Jane Smith, 2026" |
| Keywords | EXIF Keywords | "garden, pest, documentation, agriculture" |
| Camera/Device | EXIF Model | "iPhone 13" or "Canon EOS R5" |
| Altitude | EXIF GPS Altitude | "1,200 feet elevation" (useful for weather/growing zones) |
| Environmental Notes | Embedded text file or printed index | "Temp 78°F, Humidity 65%, Pest: Japanese beetles" |

**Embedding metadata:**
- **Modern phones/cameras:** Automatically embed EXIF data (verify location services are on if you want GPS coordinates)
- **Manual entry:** Use free tools (ExifTool, XnView) to add metadata to existing files
- **Card index or text file:** Create a text file in each directory documenting images if embedding is not feasible

**Printed index strategy:**
Print or write a simple catalog:
- Filename or number
- Subject
- Date
- Location
- Notes on content

Store this index with the image files (digital and printed). If your file system becomes corrupted, the printed index preserves knowledge of what photographs you have.

### Power Loss & Off-Grid Digital Access

Once grid power is lost, accessing digital files becomes difficult. Plan ahead:

**Offline access devices:**
- **E-reader with PDF files:** Transfer photographs as PDF documents to e-reader; accessible without charging
- **Printed index/catalog:** Hard copy list of images and their locations
- **Paper prints:** Critical images printed and stored physically
- **Mechanical viewers:** Early digital cameras with small screens; low power consumption if powered by manual charging

**Solar charging for digital devices:**
- Small solar panels (5-10 watts) can charge phones or portable drives over many days
- Use only for critical file access/transfer; limit to necessary tasks
- Printout key information so you don't have to power devices repeatedly

**Legacy of knowledge:**
In a true SHTF scenario, printed photographs and indexes are more valuable than digital files if infrastructure has collapsed. Prioritize creating physical archives.
