## Overview

Agroforestry is the deliberate integration of trees with crops or pasture. Silvopasture (a subset) combines livestock grazing with tree production. Trees provide shade, windbreaks, fodder, timber, and nut production while reducing soil erosion and improving water infiltration. Animals improve soil via manure and trampling. The combination is more productive per square foot than monoculture and more resilient to climate extremes.

Two primary models exist: (1) alley cropping—rows of crops between tree alleys; (2) silvopasture—pasture or understory vegetation beneath trees, with rotational grazing. This guide covers system design, canopy spacing for light penetration, fodder tree species and management, windbreak design, riparian buffers, and animal rotation schedules.

## Agroforestry System Designs

**Alley Cropping**

Trees planted in rows create "alleys" of open space between rows where crops are grown. Trees provide shade, windbreaks, nitrogen fixation (if legume trees), and eventual timber or fruit. Crops occupy the alley strips. Spacing varies dramatically by species based on mature crown spread.

**Spacing:**
- Tree rows: 30–100 feet apart depending on species (larger trees like walnut require 45–60 ft spacing between rows due to 40–60 ft crown diameter; smaller trees like fruit trees can use 20–30 ft spacing).
- Trees within rows: Species-dependent—walnut 30–45 feet apart; fruit trees 15–25 feet apart.
- Alley width: Minimum 10 feet (allows tractor width); typical 15–20 feet for smaller species. For walnut and large timber trees, alleys may need 40+ feet for adequate light.

**Species combinations:**
- **Temperate**: Walnut or oak rows (nitrogen-fixing black locust for alley fertility) + corn, beans, or lettuce in alleys. Walnuts are allelopathic; isolate them or use allelopathy-tolerant crops (corn, beans).
- **Subtropical**: Mango or avocado rows + ground crops or pasture. Acacias or tagasaste as nitrogen-fixing rows.
- **Tropical**: Coconut or cacao rows + understory crops (cacao itself, plantain, legume cover).

**Yield timeline:**
- Years 1–2: Full alley production (minimal tree competition).
- Years 3–5: Tree canopy expands; alley yields decline as shade increases. Shade-tolerant crops (lettuce, spinach, ferns) replace sun-demanding crops.
- Years 5–10: Trees mature; alleys become dappled-light zones. Perennial shade crops and grazing predominate.
- Years 10+: Trees are productive (mast, fruit, timber); alley use shifts to permanent pasture, shade-tolerant perennials, or herbaceous layers of a food forest.

**Windbreak Design**

Windbreaks reduce wind speed, protecting crops, reducing water loss, and improving livestock comfort. A good windbreak is 50% permeable (blocks wind but allows some flow; 100% solid causes turbulence on the lee side).

**Design:**
- **Height**: Trees should be 1.5–2× the height of protected crops. A 30-foot tree protects 60–90 feet downwind.
- **Width**: 3–5 rows of trees (multiple species, staggered heights) create a buffer. Single-row windbreaks are less effective.
- **Composition** (temperate):
  - **North row** (windward): Fast-growing, dense shrubs (privet, hawthorn, willow).
  - **Middle rows** (shelter): Medium trees (apple, oak, ash).
  - **South row** (leeward): Tall trees (walnut, hickory, pine if evergreen needed).
- **Spacing**: 8–12 feet between trees to allow crown expansion without crowding.
- **Orientation**: Run windbreaks perpendicular to prevailing winds (north-south if winds are from the west).

**Effectiveness:**
A well-designed windbreak reduces wind speed by 50% at 3× its height, and 25% at 10× its height. Wind reduction increases crop yields (less water stress, less mechanical damage) and reduces heating needs in winter.

**Riparian Buffers (Streamside Agroforestry)**

Riparian buffers are tree/shrub corridors along streams, protecting water quality and providing ecological benefits.

**Design:**
- **Width**: 30–100 feet (wider is better; minimum 30 feet for meaningful filtration).
- **Composition** (temperate):
  - **Herbaceous layer** (streamward, 0–10 ft from water): Native grasses, sedges. Filter runoff sediment.
  - **Shrub layer** (10–30 ft): Willow, dogwood, elderberry. Stabilize banks, provide shade (cools water for fish), fix nitrogen (if legumes).
  - **Tree layer** (30–100 ft): Oak, ash, maple, sycamore. Large trees stabilize soil, moderate water temperature, provide timber potential.
- **Management**: No livestock grazing in streamside zone (hooves compact soil, eliminate vegetation). Upland areas can be grazed or cropped.

**Benefits:**
- Filters sediment and excess nutrients from runoff (reduces algal blooms downstream).
- Stabilizes streambanks (prevents erosion).
- Cools water (tree shade); beneficial for cold-water fish.
- Creates wildlife habitat (birds, aquatic insects).
- Provides timber and food crops in mature years.

## Canopy Spacing and Light Penetration

The most critical design parameter in agroforestry is managing shade. Too much shade kills understory crops; too little reduces windbreak and environmental benefits.

**Light availability by shade:**

| **Shade Level** | **% Full Sun** | **Suitable Crops** |
|----------------|---------------|-------------------|
| Full sun | 80–100% | Corn, tomato, beans, squash, most vegetables |
| Partial shade | 50–80% | Lettuce, spinach, some berries (blueberry), herbs (mint, parsley) |
| Dappled shade | 30–50% | Shade-tolerant perennials (asparagus, shade-loving herbs), forest-garden layer |
| Heavy shade | <30% | Ferns, shade-tolerant grasses, minimal production |

**Measuring shade:**
Use a solar pathfinder tool or smartphone app to estimate shade percentage. Alternatively, observe the system during the growing season (e.g., if lettuce leaves are pale and stretched, shade is too heavy).

**Spacing formula (simplified):**

For a tree with mature height H and crown spread W:
- **Alley width** = W + 2 × (tree height / 2) = tree crown diameter + tree height (this ensures the sun angle at mid-day reaches the alley).

Example: A walnut tree grows 50 feet tall with a 40-60 foot crown spread (walnuts have large crowns). Shade length at summer sun angle (45°) is ~50 feet. To ensure 50–80% sun in the alley at mid-day:
- Alley width should be 50-60 feet (crown spread) to maintain adequate light. Tree-to-tree spacing for walnuts: 60–80 feet (allowing for the large crown and sufficient light in alleys between rows).

**Shade management over time:**

| **Year** | **Canopy Spread** | **Alley Sun** | **Management** |
|---------|-----------------|---------------|---------------|
| 1–3 | 20–30 ft | 80–100% | Full-sun crops throughout alley |
| 3–7 | 30–40 ft | 50–80% | Plant shade-tolerant crops near trees; full-sun crops in alley center |
| 7–10 | 40–50 ft | 30–50% | Shift to shade-tolerant perennials; reduce alley width |
| 10+ | 50+ ft | <30% | Alley becomes permanent pasture or forest-garden understory |

:::info-box
In temperate zones, south-facing alleys (crops on the south side of trees) capture more light than north-facing alleys. If choosing between north and south alleys, prioritize south for high-light crops.
:::

## Fodder Trees and Management

Fodder trees are pruned or browsed to provide livestock feed. They reduce dependence on purchased hay and improve soil via animal manure.

**Best fodder trees (temperate):**

| **Species** | **Nutritional Quality** | **Yield** | **Management** | **Notes** |
|-----------|----------------------|---------|---------------|----------|
| Willow (coppice) | Moderate protein, low fiber | 5–10 tons dry matter/acre/year | Annual coppice (cut to ground, regrows from stumps) | Fast-growing; multiple cuts per year possible; good browse for goats, sheep |
| Poplar (hybrid) | Moderate | 4–8 tons DM/acre/year | Coppice or pollard (cut at 6–8 ft, regrows from branches) | Soft wood; fast growth; regenerates multiple years from stumps |
| Alder (black, nitrogen-fixer) | Moderate, nitrogen-enriched | 2–4 tons DM/acre/year | Coppice; allow 2–3 years between cuts | Nitrogen-fixing (enriches manure for crops); slower regrowth |
| Ash (white) | Excellent protein; palatable | 2–4 tons DM/acre/year | Pollard; can be browsed by animals directly (if fenced below 8 ft) | High quality; palatable to all livestock. Long lifespan. |
| Oak (red or white) | Moderate; high tannin | Variable | Acorn production primary; browsing secondary | Acorns are nutritious but high in tannins (leach by soaking). Limited live-tree browsing. |
| Hazelnut | Excellent; nuts | Nut crop primary, leaves/twigs secondary | Coppice after nut harvest; regrowth serves as winter browse | Nuts for human and animal food. Slower to establish. |

**Fodder tree management:**

1. **Coppicing**: Trees are cut close to the ground (2–4 inches). Stumps regrow multiple shoots; these are harvested annually or every 2 years.
   - Best for: Willow, poplar, alder, hazelnut.
   - Regrowth: Takes 2–3 years to establish high yield; years 4+ produce heavily (2–4 tons/acre/year).
   - Rotation: Cut section A, let section B rest and regrow, cut section C. Rotate 3–4 sections so each rests 2–3 years.

2. **Pollarding**: Trees are cut at a height of 6–8 feet (above livestock browse height). Branches regrow from the head.
   - Best for: Ash, oak, poplar, willow (if space allows).
   - Longevity: Same tree can be pollarded for 100+ years.
   - Regrowth: 2–3 years between harvests; branches are thick and useful for timber as well as fodder.

3. **Direct browsing** (if livestock is fenced to below browse height): Ash and other palatable trees can be browsed directly by goats, sheep, cattle.
   - Fencing: Exclude livestock until trees are 6–8 feet tall (prevents damage to young trees).
   - Monitoring: Ensure browsing does not exceed sustainable limits (typically <50% of new growth per year).

**Yield and nutrition:**

Fodder trees provide 2–10 tons of dry matter per acre annually (depending on species, management, and climate). This is equivalent to 200–500 bales of hay (small square bales) per acre—a significant supplement to pasture-based livestock systems.

**Nutritional comparison**:
- High-quality hay: 10–12% crude protein, 60–70% digestible energy.
- Willow/poplar fodder: 8–12% crude protein, 55–65% digestible energy.
- Ash leaves: 12–15% crude protein, 70% digestible energy (excellent quality).

:::warning
Oak leaves and acorns are high in tannins, which reduce digestibility and can cause constipation in livestock. Leach acorns by soaking in water for several days before feeding to reduce tannins. Limit oak fodder to 20–30% of total diet.
:::

## Silvopasture Design and Animal Rotation

Silvopasture combines tree production with pastoral grazing. Trees are spaced to allow pasture growth beneath and grazing animals improve soil.

**Silvopasture spacing:**

Trees are spaced 30–50 feet apart (wider than alley cropping, allowing 50–80% sun for pasture). Typical densities:
- **Open silvopasture**: 15–25 trees per acre (100–150 ft spacing).
- **Dense silvopasture**: 40–60 trees per acre (50–75 ft spacing).
- **Very dense** (forest garden): 100+ trees per acre (20–30 ft spacing); minimal pasture, mostly shade/forest management.

**Pasture species selection** (temperate):

| **Species** | **Sun Requirement** | **Shade Tolerance** | **Forage Quality** | **Notes** |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|-------------------|----------|
| Timothy grass | 80–100% | Low | High | Excellent for horses; declines in shade |
| Orchard grass | 70–100% | Moderate | High | More shade-tolerant than timothy; common in silvopasture |
| Tall fescue | 70–100% | Moderate | Moderate | Deep-rooted; drought-tolerant; tolerates heavy grazing |
| Clover (red/white) | 70–100% | Moderate | Excellent | Nitrogen-fixing; palatable; declines in heavy shade |
| Sainfoin | 70–100% | Low | Excellent | Legume; mineral-rich; slow establishment |
| Shade-tolerant pasture mix | 50–80% | Good | Moderate | Custom blends (fescue, bluegrass, clover) for silvopasture |

**Animal rotation schedule** (silvopasture with 4 paddocks):

Rotational grazing (moving animals among paddocks) prevents overgrazing, allows pasture recovery, and improves soil uniformly.

**4-paddock system example (30 acres, 4 paddocks, 7.5 acres each, 20 cattle):**

```
Week 1: Paddock A (20 cattle, 7.5 acres)
        Paddocks B, C, D resting/recovering

Week 2: Cattle move to Paddock B
        Paddock A recovers

Week 3: Cattle move to Paddock C
        Paddocks A, B recovering

Week 4: Cattle move to Paddock D
        Paddocks A, B, C recovering

Week 5: Return to Paddock A (now recovered, 4 weeks rest)
```

**Grazing period and rest period:**
- **Grazing period**: 7–14 days per paddock (cattle graze, not overgraze).
- **Rest period**: 28–42 days per paddock (grass recovers, grows new leaves, roots strengthen).
- Ratio: 1:4 (if grazing 7 days, rest 28 days, requiring 5 paddocks). Typical is 1:3–1:4 ratio.

**Stocking rate** (adjusted for silvopasture tree density):

In open silvopasture (25 trees/acre), stocking rates are ~80–90% of full pasture rates due to reduced forage area.

- **Open pasture (no trees)**: 1–2 cattle per acre (depending on forage quality and rainfall).
- **Light silvopasture (15 trees/acre)**: 0.8–1.5 cattle per acre.
- **Moderate silvopasture (40 trees/acre)**: 0.6–1.0 cattle per acre.
- **Dense silvopasture (100 trees/acre)**: 0.3–0.6 cattle per acre (minimal pasture).

**Multi-species grazing** (integrating different animals):

Goats, sheep, and cattle have different browsing heights and forage preferences. Mixing species increases forage utilization.

```
Cattle: Graze grass 4–6 inches height
Sheep: Graze 2–4 inches; eat forbs (herbaceous non-grasses)
Goats: Browse shrubs and low tree growth (<6 ft); clear brush

Rotation: All three species graze Paddock A for 7 days together, then move to B.
```

Multi-species grazing increases production per acre by 20–30% vs. single-species.

## System Design Example: 20-Acre Silvopasture

**Goal**: Graze 25 beef cattle year-round; produce timber and fodder.

**Site**: 20 acres, 70% productive pasture, 30% existing forest (retained as shelter, nut trees).

**Tree plan:**
- Plant 40 acres-equivalent trees at 25 trees/acre = 500 trees total (spacing: 40 feet apart in rows 40 feet apart).
- Species: 70% oak or walnut (timber), 30% willow and hazelnut (fodder).
- Existing forest: Keep as windbreak and wildlife shelter.

**Paddocks** (rotational grazing):
- Divide 14 acres productive pasture into 4 paddocks (3.5 acres each).
- Rotation: 7 days grazing, 21 days rest (4-week cycle, 4 paddocks).

**Stocking rate:**
- 25 cattle ÷ 3.5 acres = 7 cattle/acre during grazing week.
- Over 4-week cycle, pasture is grazed 1 week per month, rested 3 weeks; effective stocking = 7 ÷ 4 = 1.75 cattle/acre/year (accounting for silvopasture tree shade, this is realistic).

**Fodder supplementation** (winter, 120 days):
- Cut willow/hazelnut in fall; cure to hay.
- 500 trees at 3 tons DM/acre/average = 1500 lbs dry matter total annually.
- 25 cattle × 15 lbs DM/day × 120 days = 45,000 lbs DM needed.
- Silvopasture fodder supplies ~3% of winter needs; remainder from pasture hay (baled in summer) or purchased hay.

**Revenue** (long-term, 15+ years):
- Timber (oak/walnut): 500 trees ÷ 30-year rotation = ~17 trees harvested/year.
  Average board feet per tree: 500–1000.
  Total: 8,500–17,000 board feet/year × $0.50–$1.00/bf = $4,250–$17,000/year (variable by timber market).
- Livestock: 25 cattle × 500 lbs annual weight gain × $1.50/lb = $18,750/year (selling feeder cattle or beef).
- Nuts/fodder: Secondary income (hazel nuts, willow baskets).

**Management labor**: 5–10 hours/week (move cattle weekly, maintain fences, monitor trees).

## Getting Started: Small Silvopasture

**Phase 1 (Year 0):**
1. Identify a 2–5 acre pasture with good drainage.
2. Space 20–30 trees at 40-foot intervals (willow, poplar, or fast-growing natives).
3. Plant 1–2-year-old saplings (faster establishment than seeds).

**Phase 2 (Years 1–2):**
1. Fence paddocks (divide pasture into 3–4 sections of 0.5–1 acre each).
2. Monitor tree growth; ensure they establish before heavy grazing (protective fencing or grazed section exclusion until trees are 6+ feet tall).
3. Manage pasture (weed control, overseeding if needed).

**Phase 3 (Years 3–5):**
1. Trees are 10–15 feet tall; shade increases.
2. Begin rotational grazing (move animals weekly among paddocks).
3. Manage forage species (lettuce→shade-tolerant varieties in heavy shade areas).
4. Begin fodder harvest (willow coppice, first year after establishment).

**Phase 4 (Years 5+):**
1. System is productive; trees provide timber, shade, wind protection.
2. Grazing management is routine (weekly moves, seasonal adjustments).
3. Tree maintenance (thinning, fodder harvesting) becomes regular.

A simple 2-acre silvopasture with 15 trees and 2–3 grazing paddocks can support 5–8 goats or 2–3 cattle and provide supplemental tree fodder, requiring 3–5 hours of management per week.
