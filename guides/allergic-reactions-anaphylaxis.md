## Quick-Triage: What Do You See?

Use this table first. Match the **patient complaint or visible sign** to decide severity and immediate action before reading the rest of this guide.

| Patient says / You see | Severity | Escalation? | Immediate action |
|---|---|---|---|
| "Throat swelling", "throat closing", "lump in throat", difficulty swallowing | **ANAPHYLAXIS** | Give epinephrine NOW | Epinephrine IM → lie flat → call 911 |
| "Can't breathe", wheezing, stridor, shortness of breath, chest tightness | **ANAPHYLAXIS** | Give epinephrine NOW | Epinephrine IM → lie flat → call 911 |
| "Face swelling", lip/tongue swelling, swollen eyes — especially after sting or food | **ANAPHYLAXIS** if airway involved | Give epinephrine if any breathing difficulty or dizziness | Epinephrine IM if airway signs; otherwise antihistamine + observe |
| Dizziness, faintness, rapid heartbeat, cold clammy skin after allergen exposure | **ANAPHYLAXIS** (cardiovascular) | Give epinephrine NOW | Epinephrine IM → lie flat → elevate legs → call 911 |
| Hives, itchy welts, rash — no breathing trouble, no swelling of throat/face | **Mild–moderate** | Observe; do NOT give epinephrine unless progressing | Antihistamine → cool compress → monitor 4–6 h |
| "Face swelling after sting" but breathing is normal, no dizziness | **Moderate (anaphylactoid)** | Observe closely; have epinephrine ready | Antihistamine + corticosteroid → monitor for 4–6 h |
| Mild itching mouth/throat, nausea after eating | **Mild** | Watch for progression | Oral antihistamine → observe |
| Hives PLUS vomiting, abdominal pain, or flushing | **Moderate–severe** | Prepare epinephrine; give if breathing or circulation changes | Antihistamine + prednisone → observe 4–6 h |

**Mild vs severe — the hard rule:**
- **Any breathing difficulty, throat/tongue swelling, or dizziness/fainting after allergen exposure = anaphylaxis until proven otherwise → give epinephrine first.**
- **Skin-only symptoms (hives, itch, rash) with normal breathing and normal consciousness = mild/moderate → antihistamine, observe, keep epinephrine nearby.**
- **When in doubt, give epinephrine.** It is far safer to give an unnecessary dose than to withhold a needed one.

:::danger
Anaphylaxis is a life-threatening allergic emergency. Epinephrine is the only reliably effective first-line medication. Delays or use of other drugs (antihistamines, steroids alone) can be fatal. Recognize anaphylaxis immediately; administer epinephrine without delay.
:::

## Allergy Spectrum: Mild to Anaphylaxis

Allergic reactions range from minor annoyance to fatal in minutes. Understanding the spectrum helps triage and treat appropriately.

<div class="allergy-spectrum">

### Localized Allergic Reactions (Non-Life-Threatening)

**Urticaria (hives):** Itchy raised welts, typically on skin only. Pruritus (itching) severe. Usually self-limited (24–48 hours).
- Cause: Mast cell degranulation releasing histamine, tryptase
- Treatment: Give antihistamine (diphenhydramine 25–50 mg or equivalent), apply cool compress, avoid triggers
- Prognosis: Excellent; resolves without intervention

**Allergic rhinitis:** Nasal congestion, sneezing, rhinorrhea (runny nose)
- Cause: Inhaled allergen (pollen, dust, mold)
- Treatment: Give antihistamine and decongestant; avoid allergen
- Prognosis: Self-limited seasonal or chronic; quality-of-life issue

**Allergic conjunctivitis:** Itchy, watery, red eyes
- Cause: Ocular allergen exposure (pollen, pet dander)
- Treatment: Apply antihistamine drops, rinse eyes, avoid allergen
- Prognosis: Self-limited; cosmetically bothersome

**GI symptoms:** Itching mouth/throat, mild GI upset, nausea
- Cause: Ingested allergen (typically food)
- Treatment: Give oral antihistamine, manage nausea, observe patient
- Prognosis: Self-limited; monitor for progression to anaphylaxis

### Anaphylactoid Reactions (Moderate, Reversible)

**Definition:** Allergic symptoms involving multiple systems without yet being life-threatening (respiratory and cardiovascular stable).

**Symptoms:**
- Systemic flushing (red skin, sensation of warmth)
- Urticaria + angioedema (swelling of lips, tongue, throat)
- Gastrointestinal: abdominal pain, vomiting, diarrhea
- Bronchospasm (mild): wheezing without severe shortness of breath
- Hypotension precursor: dizziness, lightheadedness, weakness

**Treatment:**
- Give antihistamine (first-line for moderate reaction)
- Give corticosteroid (oral prednisone 0.5–1 mg/kg or IV dexamethasone)
- Observe patient 4–6 hours for progression to anaphylaxis
- Do NOT give epinephrine unless clear respiratory/circulatory compromise

**Prognosis:** Excellent if recognized and treated early. 10–20% progress to anaphylaxis.

### Anaphylaxis (True Emergency)

**Definition:** Rapid-onset allergic reaction with respiratory AND/OR cardiovascular compromise. Onset typically 5–30 minutes (can be <5 min).

**Classic symptom cluster:**
- **Respiratory:** Wheezing, stridor (high-pitched breathing), dyspnea (difficulty breathing), feeling of throat closure
- **Cardiovascular:** Hypotension (dizziness, loss of consciousness), tachycardia (rapid heartbeat), shock (cold, clammy skin)
- **Dermal:** Flushing, widespread urticaria
- **Gastrointestinal:** Nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramping, diarrhea

**Diagnosis:** Any respiratory or cardiovascular symptom + suspected allergen exposure = anaphylaxis until proven otherwise.

**Treatment:** Epinephrine, FIRST PRIORITY (see next section).

**Prognosis:** Without epinephrine, mortality 30–50%. With epinephrine, mortality <1%.

## Epinephrine: First-Line Treatment

Epinephrine (adrenaline) is the ONLY reliably effective first-line medication for anaphylaxis. No alternatives exist. Antihistamines and steroids are ADJUNCTS, not replacements.

<div class="epi-protocol">

### Why Epinephrine Works

Epinephrine acts on three receptor types:
- **Alpha-1 adrenergic:** Causes vasoconstriction (increases blood pressure, combats shock)
- **Beta-1 adrenergic:** Increases heart rate and contractility (combats cardiovascular collapse)
- **Beta-2 adrenergic:** Relaxes airway smooth muscle (opens airways, combats wheezing/stridor)
- **Mast cell stabilization:** Prevents further degranulation (stops reaction from worsening)

Antihistamines and steroids do NOT stabilize mast cells or reverse ongoing bronchoconstriction and hypotension. **Always give epinephrine first.**

### Epinephrine Dosing

**Adults:**
- **0.3–0.5 mL of 1:1000 solution intramuscularly** in lateral thigh (fastest absorption; large muscle mass)
- Typical auto-injector (EpiPen): 0.3 mg
- Can repeat every 5–15 minutes if symptoms persist

**Children:**
- **0.01 mg/kg of 1:1000 solution IM** (max 0.5 mg per dose)
- Typical auto-injector (EpiPen Jr.): 0.15 mg
- Can repeat every 5–15 minutes

**Dose routes (in order of preference):**
1. **Intramuscular (IM):** Lateral thigh muscle (vastus lateralis). Fastest, most effective. Needle inserted perpendicular, full depth.
2. **Intravenous (IV):** For profound shock or when IM fails. Requires dilution and careful administration (risk of arrhythmia if too rapid).
3. **Subcutaneous (SC):** Slower than IM; only if IM unavailable (rare).

**Typical auto-injector use:**
1. **Remove EpiPen** from carrier tube.
2. **Swing to blue safety release** (orient correctly; blue = safety).
3. **Place orange tip against skin** of lateral thigh (can inject through clothing).
4. **Push down firmly** until click heard; hold 3 seconds.
5. **Remove and massage** injection site for 10 seconds (encourages absorption).
6. **Have second auto-injector available** for repeat injection.

### Post-Injection Protocol

1. **Place patient supine** (raises blood pressure, assists cardiac output).
2. **Elevate legs** if hypotensive (gravity assists venous return).
3. **Call for emergency help** (even if symptoms resolve, biphasic reaction risk exists).
4. **Monitor vital signs** (blood pressure, heart rate, breathing) every 1–2 minutes.
5. **Reassess respiratory status** at 5 minutes:
   - If improved: Give epinephrine dose 2 (second auto-injector) only if symptoms return or if first dose >5 min ago.
   - If unchanged/worsening: Give second epinephrine dose immediately.
6. **Transport to hospital** even if symptoms resolve (overnight observation standard; biphasic reaction possible).

### Epinephrine-Resistant Anaphylaxis

Rarely, anaphylaxis does not respond to one or two epinephrine doses. This is anaphylactic shock, a surgical emergency.

**Signs:** Profound hypotension, loss of consciousness, no pulse, apnea despite 15–20 minutes of treatment.

**Management:**
- Give epinephrine every 3–5 minutes (IV if available)
- Give IV fluids if available (bolus 500 mL–1 L rapid infusion)
- Manage airway: Keep airway open; intubate if apneic
- Place in prone position if no cervical spine injury (may improve perfusion)
- Evacuate urgently to ICU-level care (pressors, advanced airway management)

## Biphasic Reactions

Anaphylaxis can be biphasic: Initial symptoms resolve, then recur 1–72 hours later without further allergen exposure.

**Mechanism:** Initial mast cell release (first wave) responds to epinephrine. Later, newly activated mast cells release mediators again (second wave).

### Biphasic Reaction Incidence

- **Occurs in 5–15% of anaphylaxis cases**
- **Risk factors:** Severity of initial reaction, delayed epinephrine use, insufficient epinephrine dose
- **Timing:** Second wave typically 4–12 hours; can extend to 72 hours

### Management

1. **Observe patient for minimum 4 hours** after initial anaphylaxis resolution (6–8 hours if severe or delayed presentation).
2. **Keep epinephrine available** during observation period.
3. **Monitor vital signs** every 30 min.
4. **If second wave occurs:** Give epinephrine dose immediately.
5. **Admit to hospital** after anaphylaxis; overnight observation allows identification of biphasic reaction.

<section id="field-epinephrine">

## Improvised Epinephrine Auto-Injector (Austere Settings)

In austere conditions without commercial auto-injectors, improvised epinephrine administration may be attempted. This is not standard medical practice but may be life-saving if commercial EpiPens unavailable.

### Obtaining Epinephrine Solution

**Prescription epinephrine (if available):**
- Some people carry multiple EpiPens for emergencies
- Some hospitals/clinics stock vials of 1:1000 epinephrine (1 mg/mL)
- Veterinary epinephrine is chemically identical to human drug (available at farm supply/veterinary clinics)

### Improvised Administration

**Materials needed:**
- Syringe (any size; larger syringe easier to measure)
- Needle (27–30 gauge; smaller for injection comfort)
- 1:1000 epinephrine solution (1 mg/mL)
- Rubbing alcohol (antiseptic)

**Dose:** 0.3–0.5 mL for adult (0.01 mL/kg for child, max 0.5 mL)

**Procedure:**
1. **Draw dose into syringe** carefully (measure accurately; overdose risk exists).
2. **Wipe injection site** (lateral thigh) with alcohol.
3. **Insert needle perpendicular** to thigh (fully insert into muscle).
4. **Inject dose** slowly over 1–2 seconds.
5. **Withdraw needle** and massage site 10 seconds.
6. **Position supine**, elevate legs, monitor vital signs.

**Risks:**
- **Infection:** Non-sterile technique risks abscess/cellulitis.
- **Overdose:** Excessive dose causes hypertension, arrhythmias, coronary ischemia.
- **Needle injury:** May hit vessel or nerve (though low probability in thigh).

This technique is dangerous and should only be attempted when patient is dying from anaphylaxis AND no alternatives available.

</section>

<section id="antihistamines">

## Antihistamines: Role and Plant-Based Alternatives

Antihistamines are ADJUNCTS to epinephrine, not replacements.

### Conventional Antihistamines

**H1 blockers (most useful in allergic reaction):**
- **Diphenhydramine:** 25–50 mg oral or IV (sedating)
- **Cetirizine:** 10 mg oral (non-sedating)
- **Loratadine:** 10 mg oral (non-sedating)

**H2 blockers (less useful, but may help GI symptoms):**
- **Cimetidine:** 300–400 mg
- **Ranitidine:** 150 mg (if available; discontinued in many countries due to impurity concerns)

**Use in anaphylaxis:** Give AFTER epinephrine, as adjunct. Typical dose diphenhydramine 25–50 mg IM or IV.

### Plant-Based Antihistamines

**Quercetin (flavonoid antioxidant):**
- Found in: Apples, berries, tea, onions, leafy greens
- Mechanism: Inhibits mast cell degranulation; anti-inflammatory
- Typical dose: 500–1000 mg 2–3× daily (preventative; less effective during acute reaction)
- Efficacy: Modest; about 25–50% of pharmaceutical antihistamine strength

**Stinging nettle (Urtica dioica):**
- Found: Herbaceous plant; common in temperate regions
- Mechanism: Possibly inhibits histamine release; anti-inflammatory
- Preparation: Boil dried leaves (or fresh leaves) in water 10 min; drink as tea
- Typical dose: 1–2 cups tea 3× daily (preventative)
- Efficacy: Mixed scientific evidence; historical use suggests mild benefit
- Caution: Allergic individuals may react to nettle itself; test small amount first

**Ginger (Zingiber officinale):**
- Mechanism: Anti-inflammatory; mild antihistamine properties
- Preparation: Fresh or dried root; boil in water or add to food
- Typical dose: 1–2 g daily (preventative)
- Efficacy: Modest; primarily anti-inflammatory benefit

**Limitations of plant-based options:**
- Slower onset than pharmaceutical antihistamines
- Lower peak efficacy
- Suitable for prevention of mild allergies; NOT treatment of anaphylaxis
- Quality and potency variable

## Desensitization and Immunotherapy

**Desensitization** is gradual exposure to increasing amounts of allergen, training immune system to tolerate it.

### How Desensitization Works

1. **Initial immune response:** IgE antibodies bind to mast cells. Allergen exposure causes mast cell degranulation and allergic symptoms.
2. **Gradual exposure:** Small, increasing doses of allergen trigger weak IgE response (mild symptoms).
3. **Immune tolerance:** Over weeks/months, immune system shifts from IgE (allergic) to IgG (non-allergic) response. Mast cells become less reactive.
4. **Result:** Patient tolerates previously allergenic substance with minimal or no response.

### Desensitization Protocol (Example: Pollen Allergy)

**Week 1:** Exposure to very dilute pollen extract (1:10,000). Minimal symptoms.
**Week 2:** 1:1000 dilution.
**Week 3:** 1:100 dilution.
**Week 4–6:** Progressively less dilute until full-strength exposure tolerated.

Each step causes mild allergic response (itching, sneezing, mild swelling) but not severe reaction (no anaphylaxis).

### Desensitization Risks

- **Accidental high-dose exposure:** Can trigger anaphylaxis if progression too fast.
- **Requires medical supervision:** Must be done in setting where epinephrine immediately available.
- **Slow process:** Takes weeks to months.

### Field-Expedient Desensitization (Austere Settings)

In survival situations with known allergy (e.g., insect sting, specific food):

1. **Controlled exposure:** Introduce very small amount of allergen (1/100 normal dose if possible).
2. **Wait 15 min:** Observe for reaction.
3. **Observe 1 hour:** Monitor for late-phase reaction.
4. **Repeat next day:** If first exposure tolerated, introduce slightly larger dose.
5. **Escalate gradually:** Over 1–2 weeks, work up to normal exposure.
6. **Keep epinephrine available:** In case progression too fast.

This is risky and should only be attempted if long-term exposure to allergen is unavoidable (e.g., keeping bee for honey production in remote location).

## Allergy Identification and Avoidance

### Testing for Allergies (Clinical Settings)

**Skin prick test:**
- Allergen solution applied to skin; needle creates small puncture.
- Wheal (raised bump) = positive reaction (allergy likely present).
- Quick, inexpensive; limited to 20–40 allergens per session.

**Radioallergosorbent test (RAST):**
- Blood test measuring IgE against specific allergens.
- Can test many allergens simultaneously.
- Less sensitive than skin test but less operator-dependent.

### Field Identification (Without Testing)

**Documentation:**
1. **Record reaction:** Symptoms, onset time, severity, allergen suspected.
2. **Identify pattern:** Did reaction always occur with specific food/environment? Does avoidance prevent it?
3. **Temporal relationship:** Did reaction occur within minutes-to-hours of exposure?

**Example:** Every time patient eats shellfish, develops oral itching and hives within 30 min. Conclusion: Shellfish allergy likely.

### Avoidance Strategies

**Complete avoidance (safest):**
- Eliminate allergen from diet/environment completely.
- Read food labels for hidden allergens.
- Inform family/friends of allergy.

**Controlled exposure:**
- In austere settings where complete avoidance impossible, use desensitization (risky; requires planning).

**Cross-reactivity awareness:**
- Shellfish allergy: May cross-react with certain molds, crustacean shells.
- Tree pollen allergy: May cross-react with certain fruits (oral allergy syndrome).
- Latex allergy: May cross-react with certain fruits (banana, avocado).
