:::tip
Menstrual health is indicator of overall health. Irregular cycles, excessive bleeding, or absence of menses often signal underlying health issues. Understanding reproductive physiology empowers women to recognize problems early and advocate for themselves in austere healthcare settings.
:::

:::warning
**Infection risk from improper hygiene:** Reusable pads and menstrual products in austere conditions risk bacterial infection if not cleaned thoroughly with safe water. Menstrual cup insertion by untrained hands can cause tissue tears or toxic shock syndrome (TSS). Prolonged use of any internal product without cleaning causes TSS (fever, shock, organ failure—potentially fatal). Always clean hands and equipment thoroughly before insertion. Never leave products in longer than manufacturer guidance.
:::

## Reviewed Boundary Card

Use this reviewed answer-card boundary for routine menstrual support only: cycle intake, dated bleeding and pain logs, hygiene and supply planning, privacy and dignity support, and handoff preparation.

Start by asking what is routine for this person: cycle day or last period, usual cycle length, bleeding duration, current bleeding rate, pain location and severity, fever or discharge symptoms, possible pregnancy or missed period, postpartum status, recent trauma or assault, product access, safe water access, privacy needs, and whether symptoms are new or worsening.

For routine support with no danger signs, keep actions practical and non-diagnostic: make a dated log of bleeding rate, clots, pain location, pain severity, fever or discharge, dizziness or fainting, products used, fluid access, and what changed from baseline; plan enough clean dry pads, underwear, storage bags, soap, and safe water; change menstrual products regularly; wash hands before and after changing products; wash externally only; dry reusable products fully before storage; and preserve privacy, disposal access, and nonjudgmental support.

Do not use this card for diagnosis, contraception or fertility treatment, abortion guidance, medication or dosing, procedures, infection treatment, pregnancy management, legal or reproductive-rights decisions, return-to-work or school clearance, or safety certification.

Handoff urgently to a clinician, midwife, emergency service, or public-health contact when there is possible pregnancy with pelvic or one-sided pain, shoulder-tip pain, fainting, severe or worsening pelvic pain, heavy bleeding, repeated large clots, weakness, pallor, cold skin, confusion, fever, chills, foul-smelling discharge, pelvic tenderness, postpartum bleeding or pain, trauma or assault, or symptoms that are new, severe, recurrent, or not improving.

## Menstrual Cycle Physiology

Understanding the cycle helps predict fertility, recognize problems, and manage menstruation in resource-limited settings.

![Menstrual cycle phases and hormonal changes diagram](../assets/svgs/menstrual-cycle-1.svg)

<div class="repro-section">

### Four Phases

| Phase | Duration | Hormones | Endometrium | Ovary Status | Symptoms |
|-------|----------|----------|-------------|--------------|----------|
| **Menstruation** | 3–7 days | Low estrogen, progesterone | Shedding (bleeding) | Hormones at nadir | Cramps, bleeding |
| **Follicular** | 10–14 days | Rising estrogen | Thickening | Follicle develops | Energy ↑, mood improving |
| **Ovulation** | 1 day | LH surge triggers release | Peak estrogen | Egg releases | Possible ovulation pain, cervical mucus |
| **Luteal** | 12–16 days | Progesterone rising | Maintains thickness | Corpus luteum produces progesterone | Mood changes, bloating, fatigue |

### Hormonal Control

**Pituitary gland** produces:
- **FSH (follicle-stimulating hormone):** Triggers ovary to develop follicle
- **LH (luteinizing hormone):** Surge triggers ovulation

**Ovarian hormones:**
- **Estrogen:** Rises during follicular phase; triggers LH surge
- **Progesterone:** Dominates luteal phase; prepares uterus for pregnancy

**Feedback:** If pregnancy doesn't occur, hormone drop triggers menstruation; cycle repeats.

### Typical Cycle Characteristics

- **Length:** 28 days average (range 21–35 normal)
- **Bleeding duration:** 3–7 days
- **Flow volume:** 30–40 mL average (soaks 5–10 pads per cycle)
- **Regularity:** Cycles should be relatively consistent month-to-month; +/- 3 days variation is normal

## Reusable Menstrual Pad Construction

In austere settings, commercial pads may be unavailable. Cloth pads are sustainable, cost-effective, and comfortable.

### Materials

**Core (absorbent) layers:**
- Old cloth diapers (most absorbent)
- Flannel sheets (old, soft)
- Cotton T-shirts (layered, adequate)
- Cotton fleece blankets
- Bath towels (cut to size)

**Waterproof layer (prevents leaking):**
- Old rubber shower curtain (cut to size)
- Cloth-backed waterproof fabric (if available)
- Plastic grocery bags (less comfortable but functional)

**Fastening:**
- Elastic bands (old bra straps, elastic hair ties)
- Snap buttons (if sewing tools available)
- Adhesive fabric strips

### Construction Steps

1. **Cut fabric:**
   - Base layer: 7 cm wide × 15–20 cm long
   - Absorbent layers: 5–6 cm wide × 12–15 cm long (tapered at ends)
   - Waterproof layer: 7 cm × 15 cm

2. **Layer assembly:**
   - Plastic/waterproof layer first (against underwear)
   - 4–6 absorbent layers stacked (flannel, cloth diaper, etc.)
   - Top layer (soft cotton or flannel, faces body)

3. **Stitch or knot edges:**
   - Simple running stitch around perimeter (holds layers together)
   - Or tie knots at corners (no sewing required)

4. **Add fasteners:**
   - Fold top edges and sew on elastic strips (wraps around underwear sides)
   - Or sew snap buttons to pad and underwear waistband
   - Or use safety pins (traditional method, less comfortable)

5. **Make multiple pads:**
   - Aim for 5–7 pads per person (rotate through wash/dry cycle)
   - Ensures clean pad available every day

### Washing and Care

1. **Rinse immediately:**
   - Cold water (hot water sets blood stains)
   - Rub gently; don't wring
   - Blood rinses out quickly if not dried

2. **Wash:**
   - Soap and cold water; agitate gently
   - Boil periodically (kills bacteria) if water available
   - Air dry (or sun dry if possible)

3. **Storage:**
   - Keep in dedicated container or pouch
   - Ensure fully dry before storing (moisture promotes bacterial growth)

4. **Longevity:**
   - Pads last 2–5 years with proper care
   - Replace when elastic stretches, waterproof layer fails, or absorbent layers compress

<section id="menstrual-cup">

## Menstrual Cup and Alternatives

If available commercially, menstrual cups are highly sustainable (reusable for 5–10 years).

### Menstrual Cup Basics

**How it works:**
- Small silicone or rubber cup inserted into vagina
- Sits below cervix; collects menstrual blood
- Capacity: 30 mL (lasts 12 hours or more)
- Emptied, rinsed, reinserted

**Advantages:**
- Reusable for years (cost-effective long-term)
- Less surface area in contact with body than pads/tampons (fewer infections)
- Can be worn during swimming, exercise
- Environmentally sustainable

**Disadvantages:**
- Learning curve (first few cycles may be messy)
- Cost upfront ($25–40)
- Requires clean water to rinse

### Insertion Technique

1. **Fold cup:**
   - "C-fold": Fold rim in half
   - "Punch-down fold": Press center in, creating small bundle

2. **Relax and insert:**
   - Find comfortable position (sitting, squatting, standing)
   - Insert folded cup pointing downward/backward (toward tailbone)
   - Once inside, release fold; cup pops open

3. **Check placement:**
   - Run finger around rim; should feel opened circle
   - Cup sits below cervix (no strings hanging out)

4. **Removal:**
   - Pinch base to break seal
   - Gently wiggle and remove
   - Empty blood into toilet
   - Rinse with water; reinsert

### Alternative to Menstrual Cups (DIY)

If commercial cups unavailable:
- Folded cloth (cotton square folded into triangle; insert in underwear) — similar to tampon
- Sponges (sea sponges or natural fibers) — inserted like tampon; absorb blood
- Neither is ideal (higher infection risk, messier) but functional in crisis

<section id="hygiene-austere">

## Hygiene in Austere Conditions

Maintaining reproductive hygiene prevents infection in low-resource settings.

### Daily Hygiene

1. **External wash:**
   - Clean water (not contaminated)
   - Hands clean before touching genitals
   - Gently wipe front-to-back (prevents fecal bacteria entering vagina)
   - Pat dry (moisture promotes yeast growth)

2. **Underwear:**
   - Change daily, more often if menstruating
   - Wash after use in clean water
   - Air dry (sunlight is disinfectant)

3. **Menstrual product change:**
   - Change every 4–8 hours during heavy flow
   - Every 12 hours during light flow
   - More frequent change = reduced infection risk

4. **Douching (avoid):**
   - Do NOT douche (wash out vagina)
   - Disrupts normal vaginal flora (bacterial balance)
   - Increases infection risk
   - Water wash externally only

### Infection Prevention

**Signs of infection:**
- Unusual discharge (thick, discolored, malodorous)
- Itching, burning, pain
- Pelvic pain
- Fever

**If infected:**
- Increase product changes (every 2–3 hours)
- Wash frequently with clean water
- Seek antibiotics if available (tetracycline, azithromycin for likely bacterial causes)
- If severe (fever, pelvic tenderness): Seek emergency care

## Irregular Cycles and Amenorrhea Management

Irregular menstruation often signals health issues. Recognition allows early intervention.

### Irregular Cycles (Oligomenorrhea)

**Definition:** Cycles <21 days or >35 days; unpredictable timing.

**Causes:**
- **Stress/trauma:** Affects hypothalamus (hormone control center)
- **Malnutrition:** Insufficient fat/calories for reproductive hormones
- **Excessive exercise:** Depletes body fat, suppresses estrogen
- **PCOS (polycystic ovary syndrome):** Androgen excess, insulin resistance
- **Thyroid dysfunction:** Hyper- or hypothyroidism disrupts cycle
- **Intrauterine device (IUD):** Hormonal IUDs may cause irregular periods

**Management:**
1. **Reduce stress:** Meditation, adequate sleep, social support
2. **Nutritional assessment:** Ensure 1800+ kcal/day, adequate protein
3. **Moderate exercise:** Intense training may suppress cycle; reduce to moderate levels
4. **Thyroid assessment:** If available, TSH testing (evaluates thyroid)
5. **Track cycle:** Chart periods over 3 months to establish pattern

### Amenorrhea (Absent Menses)

**Definition:** No menstruation for 3+ months (or absence of menarche by age 15).

**Causes (same as irregular, but more severe):**
- Pregnancy (always rule out first if sexually active)
- Severe malnutrition/eating disorder
- Over-training (athletes)
- Stress (trauma, loss, major life change)
- Thyroid disease
- Hormonal contraceptives
- Pituitary/ovarian tumors (rare)

**Management:**
1. **Pregnancy test:** If positive, prenatal care needed
2. **Nutritional rehabilitation:** Increase calories, protein, healthy fats
3. **Reduce physical demands:** Allow body to recover
4. **Stress reduction:** Therapy, community support
5. **Medical evaluation:** If available, thyroid testing; endocrinology referral if needed

**Concern:** Prolonged amenorrhea risks bone loss (osteoporosis later in life); restoration of menses important.

## Fertility Awareness Methods (Natural Family Planning)

Women tracking cycles can predict fertile window (time when pregnancy is possible) for contraception or conception planning.

### Fertile Window Identification

**When pregnancy is possible:**
- Ovulation occurs ~14 days before next period (±2 days; variable)
- Sperm survive 3–5 days; egg survives ~24 hours
- **Fertile window:** 5 days before ovulation + day of ovulation + 1 day after
- **Example:** If ovulating on day 14 of 28-day cycle, fertile window is days 9–15

### Ovulation Prediction Methods

**1. Basal body temperature (BBT):**
- Temperature rises 0.5–1°F after ovulation (progesterone effect)
- Track morning temperature (before getting out of bed)
- Chart over 3 months to identify pattern
- Useful for confirming ovulation but not predicting it (rise occurs after fertile window passes)

**2. Cervical mucus:**
- Pre-ovulation: Clear, stretchy (like egg white); sperm-friendly
- Post-ovulation: Thick, sticky; sperm-hostile
- Monitoring mucus changes helps predict ovulation
- Requires observation and tactile assessment

**3. Ovulation pain (mittelschmerz):**
- Mild pain or pressure on ovulation day
- Not all women experience it
- Useful if present but unreliable alone

**4. Ovulation predictor kits:**
- Urine test detecting LH surge (occurs 24–36 hours before ovulation)
- Predicts ovulation within 1–2 days
- Useful if affordable/available

### Using for Contraception

- **Avoid unprotected intercourse** during fertile window (days 9–15 of typical 28-day cycle)
- **Combine methods:** BBT + mucus observation + calendar tracking most reliable
- **Requires planning:** Not suitable for women with irregular cycles or those who have difficulty tracking

### Using for Conception

- **Plan intercourse** during fertile window (every other day from day 9–16)
- **Track months:** Usually 3–6 months to achieve pregnancy (sooner if already fertile)
- **Monitor health:** Nutritional optimization, stress reduction improve fertility

## Menopause Management

Menopause (cessation of menses) typically occurs ages 45–55. Natural transition but may cause uncomfortable symptoms.

### Menopausal Symptoms

| Symptom | Duration | Severity |
|---------|----------|----------|
| **Hot flashes** | 30 sec–10 min | Mild to severe; disruptive sleep |
| **Night sweats** | Same as hot flashes | Soaking; disrupts sleep quality |
| **Vaginal dryness** | Months to years | Discomfort, painful intercourse |
| **Mood changes** | Variable | Irritability, anxiety, depression possible |
| **Sleep disturbance** | Months to years | Insomnia, frequent waking |
| **Joint/muscle pain** | Variable | Mild to moderate |
| **Weight gain** | Gradual | 5–10 lbs typical |
| **Brain fog** | Variable | Forgetfulness, difficulty concentrating |

### Management

**Non-pharmacologic:**
1. **Dress in layers:** Easy removal during hot flashes
2. **Cool environment:** Keep room cool; use fans
3. **Hydration:** Drink plenty of water (helps temperature regulation)
4. **Exercise:** Reduces symptom severity; improves sleep and mood
5. **Stress reduction:** Meditation, yoga, breathing techniques
6. **Vaginal lubrication:** Coconut oil, aloe vera, or commercial products for dryness
7. **Nutrition:** Adequate calcium, vitamin D (bone protection post-menopause)

**Herbal options:**
- **Black cohosh:** 40–80 mg daily; modest reduction in hot flashes
- **Red clover:** Isoflavones; variable efficacy in studies
- **Sage tea:** Traditional use for hot flashes; minimal research but safe
- **Vitamin E:** 800 IU daily; may reduce hot flash frequency
- **Phytoestrogens:** Plant-based estrogens (soy, flaxseed); modest benefit

**Pharmaceutical (if available):**
- **Hormone replacement therapy (HRT):** Most effective but carries risks (increased breast cancer, stroke, clots); used short-term
- **SSRIs:** Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (antidepressants) reduce hot flashes; side effect profile better than HRT for some

## Reproductive Health Education Framework

Teaching menstrual/reproductive health empowers young people and women to understand and advocate for themselves.

### Key Topics for Menarche/Adolescence

1. **Cycle basics:** Length, phases, hormone changes
2. **Menstrual symptoms:** Cramping (normal), heavy bleeding (abnormal), tracking
3. **Menstrual products:** Options (pads, tampons, cups, reusable)
4. **Hygiene:** Changing products, external washing, avoiding infection
5. **Emotional changes:** Mood fluctuation; not "crazy," hormonally-driven
6. **Sexual health:** Contraception options, consent, STI prevention
7. **When to seek help:** Heavy bleeding, severe pain, irregular cycles, infection signs

### Community Education Programs

**Effective formats:**
- **Youth workshops:** Ages 9–14, before menarche (increases comfort and knowledge)
- **Parent-child discussions:** Open communication reduces shame
- **Peer education:** Adolescent girls teaching peers (increases engagement)
- **Visual aids:** Diagrams, models, videos aid understanding
- **Hands-on:** Product demonstrations (how to use pad, cup, etc.)
- **Q&A:** Anonymous questions reduce embarrassment

### Reducing Stigma

- **Normalize menstruation:** "All half of population menstruates; it's normal"
- **Avoid shame language:** "Girly," "dirty," "unclean" perpetuate harm
- **Inclusive language:** Include transgender/non-binary people who menstruate
- **Access equity:** Ensure all have menstrual products (period poverty is real)
- **Open discussion:** Normalize talking about periods; destigmatizes
