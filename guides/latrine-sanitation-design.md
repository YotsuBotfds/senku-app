## Introduction

Also covers toilet and outhouse setup, plus safe poop and human-waste disposal in household and camp settings.

Access to safe sanitation is one of the most critical survival necessities. Contaminated human waste is the leading cause of waterborne disease and pathogenic transmission in low-infrastructure settings. This guide covers the design, construction, and maintenance of latrine systems ranging from emergency field latrines to more sophisticated long-term installations. Whether you are building for a household, settlement, or displacement camp, proper planning prevents disease outbreaks and protects both groundwater and surface water resources.

## Why Sanitation Matters

Before construction begins, understand why proper sanitation infrastructure is non-negotiable:

- **Disease prevention:** Human feces contain 10 billion pathogenic organisms per gram. Exposure leads to dysentery, typhoid, cholera, hepatitis A, and parasitic infections.
- **Groundwater protection:** Contaminated latrines within 15 meters of wells or boreholes can render water sources unusable and spread infection across an entire community.
- **Dignity and social structure:** Adequate latrines improve attendance at schools, workplaces, and community activities—especially for women and girls.
- **Household health:** Families with latrines report 30–50% fewer gastrointestinal infections than those practicing open defecation.

## Site Selection and Planning

![Latrine siting diagram showing water source distances and soil infiltration patterns](../assets/svgs/latrine-sanitation-design-1.svg)

### Choosing a Location

The location of a latrine determines its safety and effectiveness. Follow these guidelines:

**Distance from water sources:**
- Minimum 15 meters from wells, boreholes, or surface water in porous soils
- 30+ meters if soil is sandy or highly permeable
- 50+ meters from wells in karst (limestone) terrain where water infiltrates rapidly
- Place latrines downhill from all water sources, never uphill
- If the user is asking "where should it go?" treat setback and separation as the first decision, then pick the pit type.

**Terrain and drainage:**
- Avoid waterlogged or flood-prone areas—seasonal water tables can compromise integrity
- Select high ground or a slight slope to facilitate drainage of wash water
- Ensure the pit will not intercept subsurface water flow toward communal water sources
- Check groundwater depth by digging a test hole; the water table should be at least 1.5 meters below the pit bottom

**Social and practical considerations:**
- Located at least 6 meters from cooking or eating areas
- Place away from animal shelters and feed areas
- Position for privacy (screening, distance from paths)
- Accessible to all intended users, including children, elderly, and disabled individuals
- Orient the entrance away from prevailing winds to reduce odor drift to settlement areas
- When the complaint is about proximity, answer with separation first and fixture design second.

**Community coordination:**
- In group settings (camps, settlements), stagger latrine placement across multiple sites rather than concentrating them
- Agree on 1 latrine per 20 people for emergency camps (or 1 per 50 in non-emergency), split by gender if culturally required
- Mark reserved zones on community maps

### Soil and Hydrogeology Assessment

Inspect the soil before construction:

- **Sandy/gravelly soil:** Highly permeable; good drainage but rapid pathogen infiltration. Requires greater distance from water sources or lining.
- **Clay-rich soil:** Poor drainage but slower water movement. Risk of lateral spreading if drainage is blocked.
- **Rock or shallow bedrock:** Dig test holes to confirm depth. If bedrock is <1.5 m, consider alternative systems (raised tanks, chemical/portable toilets).

## Pit Latrine Types

### Simple Pit Latrine

The most basic and lowest-cost option for individual households.

**Construction steps:**
1. Dig a cylindrical pit 1.0–1.5 m in diameter and 2–3 m deep (or until 1.5 m above water table)
2. Reinforce walls with locally available materials:
   - Woven branches and mud
   - Stacked unburnt brick
   - Cut logs laid horizontally
   - Rough stone, mortared or unmortered
3. Prevent collapse with a simple wooden or concrete slab at ground level, leaving a hole for use
4. Build a basic superstructure:
   - Four vertical poles supporting a roof frame
   - Thatch, metal sheets, or woven branches for walls and roof
   - A simple door for privacy
5. Install a hand-washing station nearby (a suspended water container with a tap or release mechanism)

**Advantages:**
- Minimal cost and materials
- Culturally acceptable in many settings
- No electricity or water required
- Can be excavated by hand with basic tools

**Disadvantages:**
- Attracts flies and mosquitoes if poorly covered
- Difficult to empty (pit fills in 5–10 years)
- Can contaminate groundwater if soil is permeable
- No pathogen treatment; material remains infectious

**Maintenance:**
- Ensure the slab remains intact; repair cracks promptly
- Whitewash walls occasionally to reduce fly breeding
- Empty when fill reaches 0.5 m below the slab (hire a manual pit-emptying service or abandon if not feasible)

### Ventilated Improved Pit (VIP) Latrine

A simple pit with additions that dramatically reduce flies and odor.

**Key improvements:**
1. **Ventilation pipe:** A 10 cm diameter pipe (PVC or bamboo) rises from the pit through the superstructure roof, 0.5–1 m above the highest point of the structure. The open end is screened with fine mesh to prevent fly entry and allow airflow.
2. **Dark interior:** Walls are opaque and the squat hole is small, so flies are discouraged from entering.
3. **Wind effect:** Passing wind creates pressure differences that pull air up the vent pipe, creating an updraft that removes odor and CO₂.

**Construction steps:**
1. Dig the pit as for a simple latrine (1.5 m diameter, 2–3 m depth)
2. Line walls with brick, stone, or branches to prevent collapse
3. Install a concrete or wooden slab with a 25 cm diameter hole
4. Set a short vertical wooden or metal tube (30–40 cm tall) over the hole, angled slightly backward for ergonomics
5. Attach the ventilation pipe (10 cm diameter, PVC or bamboo) to the back or side of the superstructure, extending at least 1 m above the roof
6. Seal the joint between the pipe and slab with mud or cement
7. Cover the pipe top with a fine mesh screen (mosquito netting works)
8. Build the superstructure around the pit, ensuring walls are opaque and the door closes for privacy

**Advantages:**
- Eliminates >99% of fly breeding because flies cannot enter the ventilation pipe
- Odor is minimal if the vent pipe is properly installed
- Minimal operational cost once built
- Still uses no water or electricity
- Can last 10+ years if well maintained

**Disadvantages:**
- Slightly higher initial cost than a simple pit
- Requires understanding of how ventilation works (may not be adopted without proper education)
- Still requires pit emptying eventually
- Effectiveness depends on climate (better in windy, sunny locations)

**Maintenance:**
- Check that the vent pipe screen is not blocked by debris; replace if damaged
- Ensure the slab and pipe junctions remain sealed
- If odor persists, verify that the vent pipe extends above the roof and is unobstructed

### Pour-Flush Latrine

A water-sealed latrine that uses a small amount of water for flushing, suitable for households with access to reliable water.

**Design concept:**
- Instead of a squat hole directly over the pit, a sealed bowl is connected to the pit via a U-shaped or S-shaped bend (trap)
- Water poured into the bowl seals the trap and flushes waste downward
- No mechanical valve needed; gravity and the water seal prevent odors and flies

**Construction steps:**
1. Dig and line the pit as before (1–1.5 m diameter, 2–3 m deep)
2. Construct a reinforced concrete slab with a depression for the squat bowl or install a ceramic squat pan
3. Connect the bowl outlet to a 5–10 cm diameter pipe that curves downward (trap) and then into the pit
4. Ensure the trap always contains at least 5 cm of water—this is the seal
5. Build a simple superstructure with a roof and door
6. Position a water container (jerrycan or small tank) for flushing near the latrine entrance; typically 1–2 liters per flush

**Water requirements:**
- 1–2 liters per flush
- In water-scarce areas, collect greywater from washing for flushing
- In dry climates, ensure reliable water access before choosing this design

**Advantages:**
- Eliminates flies and odor completely (sealed system)
- More hygienic and pleasant to use than open-pit latrines
- Can last 10+ years with minimal maintenance
- Compatible with underground collection and treatment systems

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires water for every flush; not feasible where water is scarce
- If the water seal breaks (trap dries out), odor and flies return
- Requires understanding of plumbing principles
- More expensive than simple pits

**Maintenance:**
- Check weekly that the trap contains water; top up if evaporating
- If the pan cracks, repair with cement quickly to prevent seepage
- Periodically pour extra water to flush the lower part of the trap

:::warning
**Groundwater Contamination Risk:** Pour-flush and simple pit latrines can contaminate groundwater in sandy soils or high water tables. Before constructing, verify groundwater depth (minimum 1.5 m below pit bottom) and distance from water sources (minimum 15 m, preferably 30 m). If soils are very permeable, consider lining the pit with clay or plastic, or use an alternative system such as a composting toilet.
:::

## Emergency Field Latrines for Displacement Camps

In rapid-onset emergencies (floods, conflict, earthquakes) when permanent latrines cannot be built quickly, field latrines provide temporary sanitation for displaced populations.

### Shallow Trench Latrine

**Suitable for temporary occupation (weeks to a few months):**
1. Dig a trench 30–50 cm deep and 50–100 cm wide, oriented perpendicular to the main settlement pathways
2. Place logs or branches across the trench at 30 cm intervals to prevent users from falling through
3. Cover the trench edges with cloth or plastic for minimal privacy
4. Move the trench every 2–3 weeks or when fill reaches 20 cm below ground level
5. Cover the old trench with soil and plant trees on top (reclamation measure)

### Borehole-Style Emergency Latrine

**For larger camps (200+ people):**
1. Dig multiple deep holes (pits) in a line, spaced 10 m apart
2. Each hole: 1 m diameter, 4–6 m deep (stop 1.5 m above water table; if water table is too high, use shallow trench latrines instead)
3. Build simple superstructures over each pit (bamboo frame, tarp, or wooden door for privacy)
4. Post clear signs with pictograms showing usage (especially if serving illiterate populations)
5. Assign community caretakers to clean and maintain each latrine daily
6. As pits fill (after 4–6 weeks), abandon and dig new ones downwind from camp

### Communal Segregation

In large camps:
- Designate separate latrines for men, women, and children (reduces harassment and improves use)
- Target: 1 latrine per 20 people
- Ratio of women's to men's latrines: 3:1 (women use longer, more frequently)
- Place women's latrines closer to living areas for safety and accessibility

### Handwashing and Hygiene

Every latrine cluster must have handwashing facilities:
- Simple water containers (jerrycans with a spigot or pierced hole near the bottom)
- Soap if available; ash, sand, or soil are alternatives
- A trough or collection basin to prevent contamination of the ground
- Drying stations (cloth or logs to prevent muddy ground)
- Target: 1 handwashing point per 2 latrines

:::info-box
**Camp Sanitation Rule of Thumb:** In displacement camps, every day of delay in building latrines increases disease risk exponentially. Prioritize emergency latrine construction in the first 48 hours, then transition to more permanent VIP designs as soon as possible.
:::

## Fly and Odor Control

### Preventing Fly Breeding

Flies are the primary vector for latrine-associated disease. Control them by:

1. **Keeping the pit sealed:** Ensure the slab has no gaps and the squat hole is covered when not in use
2. **Installing a vent pipe (VIP design):** Acts as a one-way exit for gases but prevents fly entry
3. **Regular cleaning:** Whitewashing pit walls with lime (calcium hydroxide) every 3–6 months discourages fly breeding
4. **Maintaining drainage:** Prevent water pooling around the latrine, which creates mosquito breeding grounds
5. **Vegetation management:** Clear tall grass or reeds within 10 m of the latrine; they harbor flies

### Odor Management

Odor attracts flies and indicates poor ventilation or anaerobic conditions:

1. **Ensure airflow:** Check that vent pipes are unobstructed and extend above the roof
2. **Use lime:** Sprinkle unslaked (quicklime) or slaked (hydrated) lime into the pit monthly. Use cautiously with pour-flush systems (can damage concrete); safer to use soil or ash in those cases
3. **Add carbon material:** Sprinkle dry leaves, straw, or sawdust to absorb odors and speed decomposition
4. **Improve drainage:** If the pit is waterlogged, odor worsens; ensure water does not enter from above or the sides

## When to Close and Relocate a Latrine

A latrine reaches the end of its useful life when:

- The fill level reaches 0.5 m below the slab
- The structure becomes unstable or collapses
- Groundwater rises and contaminates the pit
- Health risks outweigh the benefit of continued use

**Closure procedure:**
1. Dig a new latrine at least 20 m away and downhill from the old one
2. Stop using the old latrine and move users to the new one
3. Fill the old pit with soil, clay, or ash (not organic waste or additional feces)
4. Compact the fill and seal the top with a layer of clay
5. Plant trees or vegetation on top to reclaim the land
6. Wait at least 2 years before digging in the same spot

:::danger
**Do Not Dig Close to Old Latrines:** Pathogenic organisms (bacteria, viruses, parasites) can survive in the soil for months or years. Digging a new latrine within 5 m of an old one risks re-contamination. Allow at least 20 m distance and ensure the new pit is downhill from the old.
:::

## Handwashing Stations

Handwashing is one of the most effective interventions to break disease transmission. Every latrine must have a handwashing facility.

### Basic Handwashing Station

**Materials (minimal cost):**
- A 20-liter jerrycan or recycled container
- A small tap, valve, or a hole pierced near the bottom (4 cm above ground)
- Soap or ash
- A trough or basin underneath to catch water
- A small platform to elevate the container

**Design:**
1. Fill the container with water daily
2. Position the tap 30–40 cm above ground for ease of use
3. Place a trough or basin beneath to catch runoff (prevents muddy standing water)
4. Provide a hanging cloth for hand drying (communal cloths should be washed regularly)
5. Stock soap or ash in a small container affixed to the stand

### Greywater Management

Handwashing generates greywater that can be reused:
- Direct runoff to a small soakage pit or mulch basin downhill from the latrine
- Plant water-tolerant vegetation (banana, papyrus, or bamboo) to absorb excess moisture
- This reduces standing water and improves the sanitation zone

### Handwashing Behavior Change

Building infrastructure alone does not ensure use. Promote handwashing through:
- Community education on disease transmission (visuals showing 5 moments: before eating, before cooking, after using the latrine, after touching feces, before feeding children)
- Assigning community health volunteers to monitor and motivate use
- Making soap accessible; if soap is scarce, distribute it in small quantities weekly
- Celebrating families or groups with the best hygiene practices

## Monitoring and Maintenance

### Daily Tasks

- **Cleanliness:** Sweep the latrine area and hose down walls if water is available
- **Handwashing:** Refill water containers and check that soap/ash is available
- **Accessibility:** Ensure the latrine door opens and the squat hole is not blocked
- **Safety:** Check for structural damage or deterioration

### Weekly Tasks

- **Deep clean:** Scrub the squat pan or bowl with water and a brush
- **Drainage:** Ensure water does not pool around the superstructure
- **Pest control:** Inspect for signs of rodents or insects; seal any gaps in the structure

### Monthly Tasks

- **Lime or ash application:** Sprinkle lime or soil into the pit to reduce odor and suppress fly breeding
- **Ventilation check (VIP):** Verify that the vent pipe is not blocked and the screen is intact
- **Water seal (pour-flush):** Check that the trap contains water; top up if necessary
- **Structural inspection:** Look for cracks in the slab or walls; repair promptly

### Annual Tasks

- **Full assessment:** Estimate fill level, check groundwater status, assess structural integrity
- **Emptying plan:** If the pit is approaching capacity, arrange manual emptying or plan relocation
- **Maintenance record:** Keep simple notes on repairs and cleaning (helps predict future needs)

:::tip
**Community Latrine Committees:** In settlements of 20+ households, establish a latrine committee responsible for maintenance and repairs. Rotate cleaning duties among users to spread responsibility. Consider a small monthly fee (in cash or labor) to fund maintenance materials.
:::

## Safety Considerations and Warnings

### Pathogen Survival

Human feces remain infectious for extended periods:
- Bacteria (E. coli, Salmonella, Shigella): 1–3 months in moist soil
- Viruses (norovirus, hepatitis A): up to 6 months
- Parasitic eggs (hookworm, roundworm): 1–5 years in soil
- Therefore, avoid touching pit contents and wait at least 2 years before reclaiming old latrine sites for food production

### Collapsed Pits

Pit walls can collapse, especially in sandy soils or when walls are not lined:
- Never enter a pit for cleaning or emptying
- If collapse occurs, refill the pit with soil and dig a new one
- Always line walls with concrete, brick, stone, or compacted clay

### Methane and Asphyxia

Anaerobic decomposition in deep pits produces methane and CO₂. These gases are toxic and can cause sudden collapse and death if inhaled:
- Never climb into a latrine pit for any reason
- If gas escapes (smell of rotten eggs), do not approach; ventilate by opening all doors and windows
- Ensure adequate ventilation in the superstructure

### Chemical Hazards

Some chemicals should never be disposed of in latrines:
- Pesticides: contaminate groundwater and kill beneficial soil microorganisms
- Bleach: interferes with anaerobic decomposition and creates toxic chlorine gas
- Petroleum products: contaminate water and resist decomposition
- Medications: can leach into groundwater and affect community health

## Conclusion

Latrines and sanitation systems are foundational infrastructure in any settlement. Proper site selection, construction, and maintenance prevent disease outbreaks and protect water resources. Whether you are building a single household latrine or designing sanitation for a large camp, the principles are the same: isolate human waste, prevent odor and flies, protect groundwater, and promote handwashing. Regular maintenance and community engagement ensure that these systems remain effective for years to come.
