## Overview

Pigs are the most food-efficient livestock, converting feed to meat at a 3:1 ratio (3 lbs feed → 1 lb gain). They also process food waste and kitchen scraps, improving resource efficiency. A single gilt (young female) produces 2 litters per year of 8–12 piglets each. Individual feeder pigs reach butchering weight (250–300 lbs) in 6–8 months on pasture + grain supplementation. Pork is highly versatile: fresh meat, cured ham, bacon, sausage, lard, and rendered fat for cooking. This guide covers breed selection, housing (pasture and pen systems), feeding, disease prevention, farrowing (birth) management, and processing.

## Breed Selection

Pig breeds differ in growth rate, feed efficiency, meat quality, and litter size. Market demand and climate also influence selection.

### Meat Breeds (Fast Growth, Lean Meat)

| **Breed** | **Weight at 6 mo** | **Feed Efficiency** | **Temperament** | **Notes** |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------------|-----------------|-----------|
| **Duroc** | 250–300 lbs | Good | Calm, docile | Red color; fast growth; good meat quality |
| **Hampshire** | 240–280 lbs | Excellent | Active, alert | Black/white; lean muscle; good feeders |
| **Landrace** | 250–300 lbs | Good | Calm | White; long body; good mothering ability |
| **Yorkshire** | 250–300 lbs | Good | Alert, active | White; versatile; good mothers and meat |
| **Large Black** | 200–250 lbs | Moderate | Calm, docile | All black; slower growth; excellent meat flavor; hardy |
| **Berkshire** | 220–260 lbs | Moderate | Calm | Dark with white markings; excellent meat; heritage breed |

### Dual-Purpose and Heritage Breeds

- **Gloucestershire Old Spots**: Calm, good mothers, slower growth, excellent meat flavor.
- **Red Wattle**: Large (400+ lbs mature), excellent meat, outdoor hardy, slow growth.
- **Tamworth**: Active foragers, lean meat, independent, good on marginal feed.

**For a family operation:**
- Duroc or Hampshire for efficient meat production (ready at 6–8 months).
- Large Black or Berkshire if flavor and heritage genetics are priorities (slower growth, higher meat quality).

:::info-box
One breeding sow (gilt) + occasional access to a boar (borrowed, ~$50 fee, or rented for season) can produce 16–24 piglets/year = 8000–12000 lbs pork annually—sufficient for a large family or small commercial operation.
:::

## Housing and Grazing Systems

Pigs thrive in both pasture and confined systems. Pasture reduces feed costs; confinement allows higher stocking density.

### Pasture System (Low Infrastructure)

**Setup:**
- **Movable pen**: Simple electric fence enclosure (portable panels + hot wire), ~100 sq ft per pig.
- **Shelter**: 3-sided hut (100–200 sq ft per 4–6 pigs) for shade and rain.
- **Water**: Trough or wallows (pigs cool via mud/water, not sweat).
- **Rotation**: Move pen every 1–2 weeks to fresh forage.

**Advantages:**
- Pigs forage for roots, grubs, insects; reduce feed costs.
- Manure distributed on pasture (soil building).
- Low infrastructure; minimal daily labor.
- Better health (outdoor conditions, exercise).

**Disadvantages:**
- Less control over diet (forage quality varies).
- Predation risk (coyotes, wild dogs).
- Weather-dependent (heavy rain makes mud difficult).

**Feed requirement (pasture system):**
- 1.5–2.5 lbs supplemental grain/day per 100 lbs body weight.
- Pasture provides 30–50% of calories in good conditions.
- Example: 200 lb pig needs 3–5 lbs grain/day + forage.

### Pen System (Higher Density, Confined)

**Setup:**
- **Housing**: Insulated shelter (200–300 sq ft for 4–6 pigs); concrete or slatted floor for drainage.
- **Space**: 8–10 sq ft per pig minimum (confined pigs need less space than pasture due to lower activity).
- **Ventilation**: Prevent ammonia buildup (causes respiratory disease).
- **Water**: Nipple drinkers or troughs; 1 per 4 pigs.
- **Feeding**: Trough or floor feeding (pigs eat 1–2 lbs at a time; feed 3–4 times daily or use self-feeders).

**Advantages:**
- High stocking density; more pigs per acre.
- Better feed control; precise nutrition.
- Less labor for moving/rotating.
- Better parasite control (off-ground living).

**Disadvantages:**
- Higher infrastructure costs.
- Waste management (manure collection, composting, odor).
- Less exercise; higher disease risk if hygiene is poor.

**Feed requirement (pen system):**
- 2–3 lbs grain/day per 100 lbs body weight.
- All calories from grain; no forage.
- Example: 200 lb pig needs 4–6 lbs grain/day.

### Farrowing Pen (Breeding Sows)

**Setup** (for pregnant/nursing sows):
- **Size**: 8×8 ft minimum (sow + nursing piglets).
- **Heat lamp**: For piglets (pigs need warm environment; newborn piglets susceptible to chilling).
- **Bedding**: Deep straw (~12 inches) for comfort and nest-building.
- **Guard rails**: Prevent sow from crushing piglets (rails at ~8 inches from floor; piglets shelter in this space).
- **Separation**: Creep pen (small area accessible only to piglets) with heat lamp for sleeping.

**Feeding** (farrowing sow):
- 4–5 lbs grain/day (higher protein, ~16% CP).
- Fresh water ad libitum (nursing drains fluids).
- Farrowing lasts ~2 hours; nursing period 8–12 weeks.

<section id="feeding">

## Feeding Requirements and Feed Costs

Pigs are omnivorous and opportunistic eaters. Feed costs dominate production expenses.

### Grain-Based Feeding

**Primary grains** (by availability):
- **Corn**: High energy (~3500 kcal/lb), low protein (~8% CP). Cheapest; often only grain fed.
- **Barley**: Moderate energy, moderate protein (~11% CP). Better balance than corn alone.
- **Oats**: High fiber; useful for piglets (gentle on stomach).
- **Soybeans**: High protein (~38% CP); expensive; used for supplementation.

**Balanced ration** (example, for growing pigs 100–250 lbs):
- 70% corn + 25% barley + 5% minerals/vitamin mix = ~10% protein (adequate for growth).
- Or: 50% corn + 30% barley + 20% soybean meal = ~14% protein (optimal for faster growth).

**Daily feed requirement** (by weight):
| **Weight** | **Daily Feed** | **Growth Rate** | **Time to Butcher** |
|-----------|----------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| 50 lbs | 2 lbs/day | 0.5 lbs/day | ~16 months from birth |
| 100 lbs | 3 lbs/day | 1 lb/day | ~12 months from birth |
| 150 lbs | 4 lbs/day | 1.5 lbs/day | ~8 months from birth |
| 200 lbs | 5 lbs/day | 1.5 lbs/day | ~6 months from birth |
| 250–300 lbs | 5–6 lbs/day | <1.5 lbs/day | Butcher when ready |

**Cost example** (corn at $3/bushel = ~$0.12/lb):
- 200 lb pig eating 5 lbs/day × 180 days = 900 lbs feed = $108 feed cost + startup cost of piglet (~$50–150) = ~$160–260 total input per pig.
- Meat yield: 250–300 lbs meat = ~$0.50–1/lb cost (excluding labor).

### Food Waste and Scraps

Pigs efficiently convert kitchen waste to meat.

**Suitable waste:**
- Vegetable scraps, stale bread, fruit waste, whey (cheese byproduct).
- Cooked meals (rice, pasta, stew leftovers); cooked is safer than raw.

**Unsuitable/risky:**
- Meat/bone scraps (disease transmission risk; may carry pathogens).
- Moldy feed (mycotoxin risk; causes illness).
- High-salt foods (excess salt is harmful).

**Feeding waste:**
- Supplement, not replace, grain.
- Example: 5 lbs grain + 2 lbs food waste/day for 200 lb pig.
- Reduces grain cost by 20–40%.

### Water

**Daily requirement:**
- 1–1.5 gallons per 100 lbs body weight.
- 200 lb pig: 2–3 gallons/day.

Provide fresh water at all times. In winter, prevent freezing with heated troughs or frequent refill.

## Disease Prevention and Common Issues

### Erysipelas

Bacterial infection (Erysipelothrix) causing diamond-shaped skin lesions, fever, joint swelling, and sudden death.

**Prevention:**
- Vaccination (available; consult veterinarian).
- Sanitation; reduce stress.

**Signs:**
- Raised, diamond-shaped lesions on skin.
- Fever, lethargy.
- Joint swelling; lameness.
- Death if untreated.

### Mange and Lice

External parasites causing itching, hair loss, scabby skin.

**Prevention:**
- Clean housing; frequent bedding change.
- Minimize stress.
- Treat if detected.

**Signs:**
- Intense scratching.
- Hair loss; red, scabby skin.
- Poor growth.

**Treatment:**
- Antiparasitic spray or injectable (consult veterinarian).
- Isolate affected pigs.

### Respiratory Disease (Pneumonia)

Viral/bacterial pneumonia affecting pigs in confined, poorly ventilated housing.

**Prevention:**
- Good ventilation (remove ammonia, excess moisture).
- Avoid temperature extremes.
- Minimize dust.
- Vaccination (if available).

**Signs:**
- Coughing, nasal discharge.
- Fever, depression.
- Reduced feed intake.

**Treatment:**
- Antibiotic (consult veterinarian).
- Improve ventilation immediately.

### Bloat

Gas accumulation in stomach from rapid grain consumption or overeating.

**Prevention:**
- Feed in portions; don't overload feeders.
- Avoid sudden diet changes.
- Grain should be moistened (reduces bloat risk).

**Signs:**
- Abdominal distension.
- Distress, difficulty breathing.
- Death if severe.

**Emergency:**
- If severe, emergency veterinary care (may require decompression).

### Parasites (Internal)

Roundworms and other intestinal parasites reduce growth and cause diarrhea.

**Prevention:**
- Clean housing; rotate pasture (if pasture system).
- Deworming every 6–8 weeks or as needed.
- Good sanitation.

**Signs:**
- Poor growth, pot-bellied appearance.
- Diarrhea or constipation.
- Anemia (pale mucous membranes).

## Farrowing (Birth) and Piglet Care

### Farrowing Timeline

**Gestation:** 114 days (3 months, 3 weeks, 3 days).

**Signs of imminent farrowing** (within 24 hours):
- Udder swelling and filling with colostrum (first milk).
- Restlessness; nest-building behavior (straw rearrangement).
- Vaginal mucus discharge.
- Appetite loss; isolation.

### Farrowing Process

1. **Early labor**: Restlessness, pacing. Lasts 2–12 hours.
2. **Active labor**: Straining and pushing. First piglet born within 30 minutes.
3. **Expulsion**: Piglets born head-first; interval between births 10–30 minutes.
4. **Duration**: Total farrowing 2–4 hours for litter of 8–12 piglets.
5. **Placenta**: Passes within 2 hours of last piglet.

### Piglet Care After Birth

1. **Dry and warm**: Remove piglets from sow; dry with cloth; place under heat lamp (95°F initial).
2. **First nursing**: Ensure piglets nurse within 1 hour; colostrum is critical (antibodies, energy).
3. **Iron injection**: Newborn piglets are anemic; inject iron supplement (100–200 mg iron dextran) within 48 hours.
4. **Tail docking and castration** (if intended; most operations do this at 3–7 days):
   - Tail docking: Prevents tail-biting behavior in confined pigs. Remove bottom 1 inch of tail.
   - Castration (males): Prevents boar odor in meat. Use sterile knife; monitor for infection.
5. **Identification**: Notch ears or apply tattoos for record-keeping.

**Nursing period:** 8–12 weeks. Sow nurses piglets; they also eat supplemental creep feed (starter grain) beginning at 2–3 weeks of age.

**Weaning:** At 8–12 weeks, separate piglets from sow. Wean into nursery pen (smaller enclosure, ~3 weeks) before moving to grower pen.

:::warning
Monitor for piglet mortality (chilling, crushing by sow, starvation). Ensure heat lamps work, sow behaves normally, and piglets are nursing. High mortality (>15%) suggests management issues.
:::

## Processing Pork

### Butchering Timeline and Weight

**Feeder pig** (purchased at 8–12 weeks, 40–60 lbs):
- Feed 6–8 months on pasture + grain.
- Ready to butcher at 250–300 lbs (18–24 months of age total).

**Home butchering** (if legal in your jurisdiction):
1. Stun pig (captive bolt or rifle shot; must be instant and humane).
2. Exsanguinate (bleed out).
3. Scald in hot water (130–140°F) to loosen hair.
4. Hair removal (scrape with knife or wire brush).
5. Evisceration (remove organs).
6. Chill immediately to <45°F.
7. Butcher into primals: ham (hind leg), loin, belly (bacon), shoulder, trim (sausage).

**Meat yield:**
- 250 lb live pig = ~185 lbs carcass (74% dressing percentage).
- ~140–160 lbs retail cuts (75% of carcass).

### Lard Rendering

Lard (rendered pork fat) is valuable for cooking, baking, and soap-making.

**Rendering process:**
1. Collect fat trim from butchering; dice into small cubes (~1/2 inch).
2. Heat gently in a large pot over low-medium heat (~250°F). Fat melts; solids (cracklings) sink.
3. Strain through cheesecloth into jars (save cracklings as high-fat snack or animal feed).
4. Cool; lard solidifies at room temperature.
5. Storage: Refrigerate or freeze; lasts 6+ months.

**Yield:** 250 lb pig produces 10–20 lbs of rendered lard (depending on fatness).

**Uses:**
- Cooking/frying (high smoke point; excellent for pie crust and biscuits).
- Soap-making (combined with lye).
- Candles (if tallow is also available).

### Curing Ham and Bacon

**Dry cure (salt-curing; traditional):**

Ingredients (per 1 lb meat):
- 0.5 oz salt (10–12% of weight).
- 0.1 oz sugar (optional, balances salt).
- 0.01 oz curing salt (sodium nitrite; prevents botulism; use caution, toxic in excess).

Process:
1. Mix salts and sugar.
2. Rub cure mixture into meat; place in covered container.
3. Refrigerate 7–10 days (ham/bacon shoulder), massaging and turning every 2–3 days.
4. Rinse thoroughly; pat dry.
5. Optionally smoke (see below) or hang to air-dry.
6. Slice thin; cook as needed or eat as-is (dry-cured ham is shelf-stable).

**Smoking (after curing):**

Equipment:
- Simple smoker (barrel, box, or purchased smoker).
- Cold smoke (70–90°F) for flavor without cooking.
- Hot smoke (180–225°F) for cooking and flavor.

Process:
1. Place cured meat on racks in smoker.
2. Use oak, hickory, or applewood (avoid softwoods; turpentine smell).
3. Cold smoke for 4–8 hours daily over 2–4 weeks.
4. Or hot smoke for 6–12 hours at 180–225°F (meat cooks during smoking).
5. Hang to air-dry after smoking.

**Storage:**
- Dry-cured/smoked ham: Hangs at room temperature, lasts months or years (develops mold surface; this is normal; brush off).
- Cooked/cooled bacon: Refrigerate or freeze; lasts weeks to months.

### Sausage-Making

**Simple sausage (ground pork + seasoning):**

Ingredients (per 1 lb meat):
- 1 lb pork trim (shoulder, belly, etc.).
- 1 tsp salt.
- 0.25 tsp black pepper.
- 0.125 tsp garlic powder (or other herbs).

Process:
1. Grind or finely chop pork.
2. Mix with seasonings.
3. Form into patties or stuff into casings (purchased from butcher supply; animal intestine or synthetic).
4. Cook immediately or freeze for storage.
5. Yields ~1 lb sausage per lb pork.

**Fermented/cured sausage** (more complex, requires cultures and cure):
- Omitted here due to complexity; consult traditional sausage recipes.

<section id="summary">

## Summary: Pig Production and Processing

- **Breed selection**: Duroc, Hampshire for fast growth; Large Black, Berkshire for flavor.
- **Housing**: Pasture system (lower cost) or pen system (higher density).
- **Feeding**: 2–3 lbs grain/day per 100 lbs + pasture forage or food waste.
- **Breeding cycle**: 114-day gestation; 2 litters/year per sow = 16–24 piglets/year.
- **Growth to butcher**: 6–8 months from feeder pig (40–60 lbs) to butcher weight (250–300 lbs).
- **Processing**: Home butchering yields 140–160 lbs retail cuts + 10–20 lbs lard per pig.
- **Preservation**: Dry-cure and smoke ham/bacon; render lard; make sausage.

A single gilt (young sow) with seasonal access to a boar can produce 16–24 piglets annually, providing 8000–12000 lbs of pork—sufficient for a large family or small business. Pasture-based systems minimize feed costs; processing on-farm adds value and utilization.
