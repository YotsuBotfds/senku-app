## Routing: Road Rules, Mixed Traffic, and Safety Complaints

Use this guide when the problem is how people and vehicles should share a road: right-of-way, speed, bridge limits, signage, intersection behavior, enforcement, and mixed-mode traffic safety. If the task is to build, repair, or choose the transport system itself, hand off to <a href="../transportation.html">Transportation &amp; Infrastructure</a>.

### Quick routing cues

- If the user is asking who must yield, how fast to go, or how to post limits, stay here.
- If the user is asking how to build roads, bridges, carts, boats, rails, or vehicle systems, switch to transportation first.
- If the complaint is "people keep getting hurt on the road," answer with operating rules here and refer the build-side work to transportation.

<section id="overview">

## Overview: Traffic in a Post-Infrastructure World

Modern traffic management depends on painted lines, traffic lights, electronic signs, professional law enforcement, and millions of standardized motor vehicles operating on maintained roads. When this infrastructure fails, traffic does not stop — people still need to move goods and themselves from one place to another. What changes is the composition of traffic and the systems governing it.

Post-infrastructure traffic is inherently mixed-mode. Pedestrians, animal-drawn carts, bicycles, horseback riders, and occasional motor vehicles share the same road surfaces. These modes travel at vastly different speeds, occupy different amounts of road width, have different stopping distances, and respond differently to road surface conditions. Without deliberate rules, this mix produces collisions, road damage, injuries, and death.

Traffic fatalities in low-regulation environments are historically high. Before modern traffic laws, urban horse-and-pedestrian accidents killed thousands annually in major cities. Adding motor vehicles to a mixed environment without rules dramatically increases the danger.

This guide covers practical traffic governance for communities managing mixed-mode transportation on degraded road infrastructure. For road construction and repair, see <a href="../road-networks-logistics.html">Road Networks & Logistics</a> and <a href="../transportation.html">Transportation &amp; Infrastructure</a>. For vehicle construction and maintenance, see <a href="../cart-wagon-construction.html">Cart & Wagon Construction</a> and <a href="../bicycle-construction.html">Bicycle Construction</a>.

<section id="right-of-way">

## Right-of-Way Conventions

Right-of-way rules determine who yields to whom when paths cross or road width is insufficient for simultaneous passage. Without clear conventions, every intersection and narrow point becomes a negotiation — or a collision.

### The Vulnerability Hierarchy

The most universally applicable right-of-way system prioritizes the most vulnerable road users:

1. **Pedestrians** — always have right-of-way over all other modes
2. **Livestock being driven** — animals are unpredictable; other users must give way
3. **Animal-drawn vehicles** — limited steering and braking capability
4. **Cyclists** — exposed, limited stability
5. **Horseback riders** — higher position gives visibility advantage, but horses can spook
6. **Motor vehicles** — most capable of stopping and yielding; most dangerous in collisions

This means motor vehicles yield to everything, and pedestrians yield to nothing. The logic is straightforward: the user with the greatest capacity to cause harm bears the greatest responsibility to prevent it.

:::info-box
**Historical precedent:** This vulnerability hierarchy was the dominant traffic principle worldwide before the 1920s. The concept of "jaywalking" — blaming pedestrians for being in the road — was invented by automobile industry lobbying groups. In a post-infrastructure context, roads serve all users, and the most dangerous users bear the duty of care.
:::

### Intersection Rules

At intersections without signals or signs:

**Uncontrolled intersections (equal roads):**
- The user arriving first proceeds first
- If two users arrive simultaneously, the user to the right has priority
- All users slow to walking speed when approaching

**Main road vs. side road:**
- Users on the main road (wider, more traveled) have priority
- Side-road users must stop and wait for a clear gap
- Mark main roads with painted rocks, posts, or cleared sightlines at intersections

**T-intersections:**
- Through traffic on the continuing road has priority
- The user on the terminating road must stop

:::tip
Physical intersection design matters more than rules. Forcing users to slow down through physical constraints — narrowing the road at intersections, placing obstacles that require turns, or using raised surfaces — is far more effective than relying on rule compliance. People obey physics even when they ignore rules.
:::

### Narrow Passages

When a road, bridge, or path is too narrow for two-way traffic:

- **Uphill traffic has priority** over downhill traffic (harder to restart on a grade, especially for loaded carts)
- **Loaded vehicles have priority** over empty vehicles
- **If equal, the user who entered the narrow section first proceeds**
- At bridges: establish a visible signal system (a flag or sign on each end indicating whether the bridge is occupied)

<section id="speed-management">

## Speed Management

Speed limits in a post-infrastructure environment are not about radar guns and tickets. They are about physics: stopping distance, reaction time, road surface grip, and the survivability of impacts at different speeds.

### Speed Zones

<table class="speed-table">
<tr><th>Zone</th><th>Max Speed</th><th>Applies To</th></tr>
<tr><td>Settlement core (market, school, clinic)</td><td>Walking pace (~5 km/h)</td><td>All vehicles</td></tr>
<tr><td>Residential areas</td><td>Trotting pace (~10 km/h)</td><td>All vehicles</td></tr>
<tr><td>Open road, good condition</td><td>~30 km/h for motor vehicles</td><td>Motor vehicles only</td></tr>
<tr><td>Open road, poor condition</td><td>~15 km/h for motor vehicles</td><td>Motor vehicles only</td></tr>
<tr><td>Bridges and fords</td><td>Walking pace</td><td>All vehicles</td></tr>
</table>

**Why these limits matter:**

- A pedestrian struck at walking pace (5 km/h) is unlikely to be seriously injured
- A pedestrian struck at 30 km/h has approximately a 10% fatality risk
- A pedestrian struck at 50 km/h has approximately a 50% fatality risk
- On degraded road surfaces, vehicles lose control at speeds that would be safe on maintained pavement

:::warning
Motor vehicles are the most dangerous element of mixed-mode traffic. A 1,000 kg vehicle at 30 km/h carries roughly 35,000 joules of kinetic energy — enough to be fatal on impact. Communities must enforce strict speed limits in populated areas. The consequences of non-enforcement are measured in deaths and permanent disabilities.
:::

### Speed Enforcement Without Technology

- **Physical calming measures:** Chicanes (alternating obstacles forcing S-curves), raised crossings, narrowed lanes, and surface texture changes physically prevent high speeds
- **Community enforcement:** Designated road wardens who record violations and report to the governance council
- **Consequence system:** First offense — warning. Second offense — vehicle impounded for a period. Third offense — driving privileges revoked. Serious injury caused by speed — criminal proceedings per <a href="../justice-law.html">Justice & Law</a>

<section id="road-maintenance">

## Road Maintenance Priorities

With limited labor and materials, communities cannot maintain all roads equally. A triage system ensures critical routes remain passable.

### Road Classification

**Primary routes:** Connecting the community to essential external resources (water sources, agricultural land, neighboring communities, trade routes). Receive highest maintenance priority.

**Secondary routes:** Internal community roads connecting residences to central facilities (market, clinic, school, workshop). Maintained for daily use.

**Tertiary routes:** Access roads to outlying areas, storage sites, salvage zones. Maintained to minimum passable standard.

**Abandoned routes:** Roads serving no current purpose. Marked as unmaintained and used at own risk.

### Maintenance Tasks by Priority

1. **Drainage** — Water damage destroys roads faster than traffic. Clearing ditches, culverts, and drainage channels is the single highest-impact maintenance activity
2. **Pothole and surface repair** — Fill with gravel, broken brick, or compacted earth. Small repairs prevent large failures
3. **Vegetation control** — Roots crack surfaces, overgrowth narrows passable width, reduces sightlines at intersections
4. **Shoulder maintenance** — Soft shoulders trap vehicles and cause rollovers when wheels leave the surface
5. **Signage and marking** — Maintain intersection markers, speed zone indicators, bridge weight limit postings

:::tip
Organize road maintenance as a community labor obligation. Each household contributes a set number of labor-hours per month to road upkeep, similar to historical corvee systems. This distributes the burden and ensures consistent attention. For detailed road construction techniques, see <a href="../road-networks-logistics.html">Road Networks & Logistics</a>.
:::

<section id="bridge-management">

## Bridge Weight Limits and Management

Bridges are the most vulnerable and valuable pieces of road infrastructure. A failed bridge can cut off a community from essential resources. Managing bridge loads is critical.

### Assessing Bridge Capacity

Without engineering analysis, use conservative rules:

- **Concrete and steel bridges in good condition:** Likely safe for any vehicle that can reach them, but inspect for rebar corrosion (rust staining on concrete surface), cracking, and spalling
- **Wooden bridges:** Inspect all structural members for rot, insect damage, and cracking. Load capacity degrades over time. When in doubt, restrict to pedestrians and light carts
- **Improvised bridges:** Treat as having the lowest possible capacity. Load test incrementally — send a person across first, then a light cart, then progressively heavier loads

:::danger
**Bridge collapse is lethal.** A bridge that appears sound may have hidden structural damage — corroded internal rebar, rot inside timbers, erosion of abutment foundations. Never assume a bridge is safe because it looks intact. Inspect thoroughly before allowing heavy loads. If a bridge shows any signs of distress (sagging, cracking, unusual sounds under load), restrict it immediately and consult someone with construction experience.
:::

### Weight Limit Posting

Post weight limits at both ends of every bridge in the community's road network:

- Express limits in terms people understand: "Pedestrians and bicycles only," "Light carts (under 500 kg)," "All vehicles under 2,000 kg," "No restrictions"
- Include a visual indicator — a pictogram showing allowed vehicle types
- Update limits after inspection or after significant weather events (flooding weakens foundations)

### Bridge Inspection Schedule

- **After every major storm or flood:** Check foundations, deck, and structural members
- **Monthly:** Visual inspection from above and below (if accessible)
- **Annually:** Detailed inspection with probing of wood members and close examination of concrete/steel

<section id="mixed-mode-management">

## Managing Mixed-Mode Traffic

The core challenge of post-infrastructure traffic is the simultaneous presence of road users with vastly different characteristics.

### Separation Where Possible

The safest approach is physical separation of incompatible modes:

- **Pedestrian paths:** Designate walking routes alongside but separated from vehicle roads using berms, ditches, or fencing
- **Animal lanes:** Livestock being driven to market or pasture should use designated routes or travel at specific times (early morning, late evening) to minimize conflicts
- **Vehicle-only sections:** Steep grades, sharp curves, and narrow passages where pedestrians would be endangered should be marked as vehicle-only with pedestrian bypass paths

### Time-Based Separation

When physical separation is not possible, time-based rules can reduce conflicts:

- **Market days:** Vehicles restricted from the settlement core during market hours
- **School hours:** Streets near schools restricted to pedestrian traffic during arrival and dismissal times
- **Night travel:** Motor vehicles must carry lights (lanterns, battery-powered lights) and reduce speed by half at night

### Animal-Specific Rules

Animals introduce unique hazards:

- **Horses can spook** at unexpected noises, motor vehicles, or sudden movements. Motor vehicle operators must stop and idle (not rev the engine) when passing horses
- **Draft animals pulling loads** have very limited ability to stop or steer. Other users must give them wide clearance and predictable behavior
- **Loose livestock** on roads is a serious hazard. Communities should establish fencing requirements or herding obligations for livestock owners
- **Animal waste** on roads is a slip hazard and sanitation issue. Owners should remove waste from settlement core areas; on open roads, waste naturally degrades but may need clearing from bridges and intersections

For detailed guidance on working with draft animals on roads, see <a href="../draft-animals.html">Draft Animals</a> and <a href="../animal-husbandry-transportation.html">Animal Husbandry & Transportation</a>.

<section id="signage">

## Road Signage and Communication

Without manufactured signs, communities must create their own road communication systems.

### Essential Sign Types

**Warning signs:** Hazards ahead — bridge with weight limit, steep grade, sharp curve, falling rock area. Use a consistent shape (triangle) and color (red/orange paint on wood or metal).

**Regulatory signs:** Speed zones, right-of-way assignments, one-way sections. Use a consistent shape (rectangle) and color (black on white).

**Informational signs:** Distances to destinations, community boundary markers, road names or numbers. Use a consistent shape (rectangle) and color (green background or natural wood).

### Sign Materials

- **Painted wood:** Most accessible. Repaint annually. Use oil-based paint for weather resistance
- **Salvaged metal signs:** Repurpose existing road signs by painting over with new information
- **Stone cairns:** For trail markers and route indicators in areas where signs would be vandalized or blown away
- **Carved wood:** More durable than painted, but slower to produce

:::tip
Consistency matters more than beauty. A community with uniform, readable signs using consistent shapes and colors communicates far more effectively than one with elaborate but varied signage. Establish a sign standard early and apply it uniformly. See <a href="../measurement-standards.html">Measurement Standards</a> for establishing consistent units and conventions.
:::

<section id="enforcement">

## Enforcement and Compliance

Traffic rules are only effective if they are followed. In a post-infrastructure community, enforcement relies on social mechanisms rather than professional police forces.

### Community Road Wardens

Appoint rotating road wardens — community members who observe traffic behavior in settlement areas and at critical points (intersections, bridges, school zones). Wardens:

- Note violations and report to governance council
- Have authority to verbally warn violators
- Do not physically enforce (no stopping vehicles, no confiscation)
- Are identifiable (armband, vest, or other marker)

### Graduated Consequences

1. **Verbal warning** from road warden or witness
2. **Written warning** posted at community board and delivered to violator
3. **Community service** — road maintenance labor hours assigned
4. **Vehicle impoundment** — vehicle held for a set period (days to weeks)
5. **Driving privilege revocation** — individual prohibited from operating vehicles in community territory
6. **Compensation and criminal proceedings** — for violations causing injury or death, handled through the community justice system per <a href="../justice-law.html">Justice & Law</a>

### Building Compliance Culture

Enforcement is a backstop. The goal is voluntary compliance through:

- **Education:** Ensure all community members understand the rules and the reasoning behind them
- **Visible consequences:** When violations are addressed, the community sees that rules have meaning
- **Leading by example:** Governance council members and community leaders must scrupulously obey traffic rules
- **Children's education:** Teach road safety early. Children who grow up with traffic awareness become adults who follow traffic rules instinctively

For broader community governance structures, see <a href="../governance.html">Governance</a> and <a href="../community-organizing.html">Community Organizing</a>.

<section id="emergency-vehicles">

## Emergency Vehicle Priority

Even in a post-infrastructure context, emergency situations require rapid movement — medical emergencies, fire, security threats. Establish a clear emergency priority system:

- **Emergency vehicles** (marked with a specific color or flag — red is the universal default) have right-of-way over all other traffic
- **All users must yield** by moving to the edge of the road and stopping when an emergency vehicle approaches
- **Emergency vehicles are exempt** from speed limits but are still responsible for avoiding collisions
- **Abuse of emergency markings** (using emergency flags for non-emergency travel) should carry serious consequences

:::warning
Emergency vehicle priority only works if people recognize emergency vehicles reliably. Use a simple, visible, and consistent marking system — a red flag on a pole is effective and requires no special equipment. Do not allow multiple incompatible marking systems to develop; standardize early.
:::

<section id="implementation">

## Implementation Steps

1. **Survey the road network.** Map all roads, bridges, intersections, and known hazards within community territory
2. **Classify roads and bridges.** Assign primary/secondary/tertiary status to roads. Assess and post bridge weight limits
3. **Draft rules.** Using this guide as a starting framework, adapt rules to local conditions (terrain, traffic volume, available vehicle types)
4. **Community review.** Present proposed rules at a community meeting. Incorporate feedback. Adopt by consensus per <a href="../governance-democratic-systems-frameworks.html">Governance & Democratic Systems</a>
5. **Install signage.** Mark speed zones, intersections, bridge limits, and emergency routes
6. **Appoint wardens.** Establish a road warden rotation schedule
7. **Educate.** Hold a community session explaining all rules. Ensure every new arrival is briefed
8. **Review quarterly.** Assess effectiveness, accident rates, compliance, and road conditions. Adjust rules as needed

Traffic safety is an ongoing process, not a one-time establishment. As the community's transportation mix evolves — more vehicles repaired, new routes opened, trade increasing — the rules must evolve with it. The goal is not perfection but a system that meaningfully reduces injuries and deaths compared to unregulated traffic.
