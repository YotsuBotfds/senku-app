:::danger
Hurricanes (Atlantic/Eastern Pacific) and cyclones (Indian Ocean/Western Pacific) are among Earth's most powerful storms. Wind speeds exceed 250 km/h; storm surge can reach 6+ meters. Preparation must begin weeks before season, be finalized 72 hours before landfall, and completed by 12 hours before arrival.
:::

## Preparation Timeline

Hurricane/cyclone season planning spans months, but critical actions occur in final 72 hours.

<div class="prep-timeline">

### 12 Weeks Before Season (May, for Atlantic Hurricane Season June 1)

- **Document property:** Photos/video of home and contents for insurance claims.
- **Create evacuation plan:** Identify primary and secondary evacuation routes. Plan destinations (shelters, relatives' homes, hotels inland/elevation).
- **Stock supplies:** Non-perishable food, water (1 gallon per person per day, 2+ weeks), medications (30-day supply minimum), batteries, flashlights, first aid.
- **Maintain HVAC/generators:** Service air conditioning units; test backup generators if available.
- **Identify safe room:** Identify interior room with no windows (bathroom, hallway, closet). Plan temporary reinforcement.

### 4–6 Weeks Before Season

- **Trim trees:** Remove large branches overhanging house or structure. Hire professionals if necessary.
- **Check roof:** Repair loose shingles, gutters, flashing. Replace damaged sections.
- **Check foundation:** Repair cracks in foundation or walls. Poor foundation compromises entire structure.
- **Secure loose items:** Fence repair, patio furniture storage, garden structures.

### 2 Weeks Before Season

- **Check shutters/protection:** Test storm shutters or plywood panels. Ensure fasteners are accessible.
- **Stock fuel:** Fill gas cans; keep generator fuel on hand. (Note: Avoid storing excessive fuel; safety hazard.)
- **Medications:** Refill prescriptions; ensure adequate supply.
- **Plans finalization:** Confirm evacuation destination, pets' needs, important document location.

</div>

### 72 Hours Before Landfall (When Track Known)

<div class="prep-timeline">

**Friday (if Monday arrival expected):**

1. **Evacuate if in flood zone** — Storm surge is most dangerous hazard. Act early; traffic becomes gridlocked hours before impact.
2. **Board windows:** Install storm shutters or secure plywood panels with proper fasteners (not nails; nails bend under wind pressure).
3. **Fill bathtub, large containers with water** — Backup supply if taps contaminated by surge.
4. **Stock final groceries:** Focus on shelf-stable, ready-to-eat foods (canned goods, peanut butter, crackers, etc.). Avoid items requiring refrigeration or cooking.
5. **Charge all devices:** Phones, laptop, flashlights, batteries.
6. **Top off fuel tank** — Stations may close; plan for possible evacuation departure.
7. **Bring in outdoor items:** Lawn furniture, potted plants, trash cans (become projectiles in high wind).
8. **Move car to covered location** or away from trees.

</div>

### 48 Hours Before Landfall

<div class="prep-timeline">

**Saturday (if Monday arrival expected):**

1. **Finalize evacuation:** If ordered to leave, depart by now. Roads become impassable as storm nears.
2. **Secure property:** Close interior doors (limits wind damage spread). Open some roof vents slightly (prevents pressure buildup that can lift roof).
3. **Take important documents:** Deeds, insurance policies, passports, medical records. Store in waterproof bag.
4. **Pets: plan evacuation.** Most shelters do not accept animals; arrange hotels, relatives, or pet-friendly shelters in advance.
5. **Final weather check:** Confirm track and intensity.

</div>

### 24 Hours Before Landfall

<div class="prep-timeline">

**Sunday (if Monday arrival expected):**

1. **If evacuating, confirm departure** — Do not delay.
2. **If sheltering in place:**
   - Review safe room plan.
   - Move supplies (water, food, medications, flashlights) into safe room.
   - Charge all devices fully.
   - Fill bathtub.
   - Close all interior doors.
3. **Stock final items:** Keep phones charged. Know how to turn off gas if emergency repair needed.

</div>

### 12 Hours Before Landfall (Last Chance)

<div class="prep-timeline">

**Monday morning (if Monday afternoon arrival expected):**

1. **Final evacuation decision.** After this point, roads become impassable and rescue impossible.
2. **If sheltering:** Assume no power for 1–3 weeks. Complete all final tasks:
   - Charge all devices.
   - Fill tubs/containers with water.
   - Eat perishable food (refrigerator will not work).
   - Confirm medication supply accessible.
   - Have battery radio on for final weather updates.
3. **Move to safe room 1–2 hours before storm arrival.** Bring supplies, close interior doors, lock bathroom/safe room door.
4. **Stay put:** Do not venture outside for any reason once wind becomes dangerous (gusts >50 km/h).

</div>

<section id="shelter-hardening">

## Shelter Hardening: Windows, Doors, Roof

Standard residential structures cannot withstand Category 3+ wind speeds. Hardening focuses on preventing envelope breach (window/door failure).

### Window Protection

**Storm shutters:**
- Metal rolling shutters (installed before season) provide excellent protection but are expensive.
- Hinged plywood panels pre-built and stored for deployment. Fastening takes 30–60 min per window.
- Proper fasteners: ¼-inch lag bolts into frame every 30 cm, not nails. Nails bend under pressure.

**Plywood paneling:**
1. **Measure windows** and cut plywood (½ inch thick, ½ inch larger than window opening).
2. **Pre-drill holes** (½ inch, spaced 30 cm apart) through plywood and framing.
3. **Store panels with fasteners** (lag bolts, washers) accessible.
4. **Installation:** Position panel, insert bolts, tighten firmly. Takes 5 min per window with practice.
5. **Duct tape edges** after bolts tightened to seal gaps.

**Interior options (less effective):**
- Heavy interior shutters
- Heavy curtains/drapes
- Duct tape window panes (minimal effect; mostly psychological)

### Door Reinforcement

**Exterior doors:**
- Install reinforced door frames with multiple bolts (minimum 3 bolts per door).
- Ensure hinges are bolted to frame (not screwed).
- Add diagonal bracing (2×4 or 4×4 lumber diagonally from top hinges to opposite wall stud).

**Sliding glass doors:**
- Install metal bars or grilles.
- Or remove door entirely, board opening.

### Roof Strapping and Reinforcement

**Critical point:** Roofs fail in two ways: sheathing failure (plywood lifts off) or connection failure (rafters/trusses tear from walls).

**Roof straps (hurricane ties):**
- Pre-installed metal straps connect roof trusses to walls.
- If not present, retrofitting is expensive and requires roofer expertise.
- Ask structural engineer if home needs roof-to-wall connections.

**Roof inspection:**
- Loose shingles, cracked plywood, missing flashing = water intrusion risk after wind damage.
- Repair before season critical.

**Gable end bracing:**
- Gable ends (triangular sections at house sides) are weak. Temporary cross-bracing with plywood helps.

<section id="storm-surge">

## Storm Surge: Coastal Hazard

Storm surge is the rise in sea level due to wind and pressure; not the same as waves.

### Surge Height and Evacuation Zones

- **Category 1 storm, shallow bay:** 1–1.5m surge
- **Category 3 storm, flat coastline:** 2.7–3.7m surge
- **Category 5 storm, funnel-shaped bay:** 5.5m+ surge

Evacuation zones (A, B, C, or numerical) are drawn by emergency management based on historical surge modeling. If in evacuation zone, **leave by 72 hours before landfall.** Rescue is impossible once surge arrives.

### Surge Flooding

- **Saltwater contamination:** Destroys crops, contaminates wells for months.
- **Floating debris:** Logs, vehicles, building materials are lethal at high speed.
- **Inland penetration:** Surge can travel 10+ km inland in flat areas, affecting people far from coast.
- **Duration:** Water may remain 24–72 hours, trapping residents on rooftops or in attics.

### Personal Flotation

If trapped above water (attic, roof):
- Wear life jacket or improvised flotation (sealed plastic containers, empty buckets tied to body).
- Keep signaling materials (bright cloth, mirror, whistle) accessible.
- Wave from roof/window to attract rescuers.

</section>

<section id="during-storm">

## During-Storm Protocol

Once hurricane arrives, being outside is universally fatal. All preparation must be complete before conditions deteriorate.

### Safe Room Occupancy

1. **Move to safe room** 2–4 hours before strongest winds. Interior room with no windows (bathroom, hallway, under stairs, interior closet).
2. **Bring supplies:** 2-week water and food, medications, medications, battery radio, flashlights, first aid, mattresses/blankets for cushioning.
3. **Close interior doors** to safe room to isolate from wind penetration.
4. **Stay down:** Lie on mattresses/cushions on floor. Protects from flying debris if room's ceiling breaches.
5. **Keep battery radio on** to track storm progress and local damage reports.
6. **Do not venture outside.** Even brief exposure to Category 2+ winds risks being knocked down or struck by debris.

### Power Loss Expectations

Assume 1–3 weeks without electricity:
- Refrigerators: Food spoils within 4 hours if unopened; 2 hours if opened. Plan non-perishable diet.
- Water pumps: Electric pumps fail; wells become inaccessible.
- Gasoline: Service stations close; fuel unavailable for 1+ week.
- Cell towers: Widespread outages; communication impossible.

### Water/Sanitation

- **Tap water:** May be contaminated by surge or system failure. Assume unsafe until authorities declare safe.
- **Toilets:** Flush if water available; otherwise, use portable toilet, bucket-and-bag method, or pre-dug pit toilet.
- **Drinking water:** Only use stored, bottled, or boiled water.

<section id="post-storm">

## Post-Storm Assessment and Debris Clearing

Once wind subsides and visibility returns, assessment begins. Safety is paramount.

### Initial Inspection (First 12 Hours)

1. **Check for gas leak:** Smell gas? Do not use electricity (sparks ignite gas). Open windows and doors, leave building immediately, contact gas company from distance.
2. **Check for electrical hazards:** Downed power lines are lethal. Assume all lines are live. Stay 10+ meters away.
3. **Walk perimeter:** Visually inspect for structural damage (shifted foundation, wall cracks, roof penetration).
4. **Document damage:** Photos for insurance claims.
5. **Locate emergency shut-offs:** Gas valve (wrench needed), electrical main breaker, water main.

### Structural Damage Assessment

**Survivable damage:**
- Missing shingles (roof leaks, but structure intact)
- Broken windows (temporary plywood repair)
- Siding/fascia damage (cosmetic)

**Urgent damage:**
- Roof collapse (risk of further collapse; vacate building)
- Wall cracks (foundation problem; evacuate, assess with engineer)
- Separation at roof/wall (structural failure; evacuate)

### Debris Clearing Safety

**Hazards:**
- Nails and metal hidden in debris (tetanus risk; keep wounds clean).
- Broken glass (wear gloves, boots).
- Downed power lines (assume live; do not touch).
- Heavy debris (trees, logs, building materials; risk of crushing).
- Mold growth (within 24–48 hours in wet conditions; respiratory hazard).

**Clearing practices:**
1. **Wear protection:** Heavy gloves, boots, long pants, eye protection, respirator (if working in moldy conditions).
2. **Clear roads first:** Prioritize routes for emergency vehicles and restoration crews.
3. **Separate salvageable/hazardous:** Wood can be salvaged; treat chemical/fuel-soaked material as hazardous.
4. **Burning debris:** Check local regulations; often prohibited post-hurricane due to air quality concerns.
5. **Heavy lifting:** Use proper technique; back injuries are common post-disaster. Use levers, dollies, help from others.

### Insurance Claims

- **File early:** Adjusters become overwhelmed; early filing prioritizes your claim.
- **Documentation:** Photos of all damage, list of damaged items with replacement cost.
- **Receipts:** Keep receipts for repairs and replacements made.
- **Keep all paperwork:** Save estimates, invoices, and correspondence.

### Restoration Timeline Expectations

- **1 week:** Roads passable; some water/power restoration begun.
- **1 month:** 50–70% power restored; water system functioning in many areas.
- **3 months:** Most services restored; repair materials becoming available (initially scarce).
- **6–12 months:** Most structural repairs complete; landscaping and cosmetic repairs ongoing.
