## Overview

Water quality directly impacts crop yield, livestock health, and soil condition. Contaminated irrigation water can reduce productivity or damage soil structure through salt accumulation or pathogen presence. This guide covers practical field tests for the most critical water parameters: turbidity (clarity), pH (acidity/alkalinity), bacterial contamination, chemical contaminants, hardness, and dissolved oxygen. Organized testing schedules and record-keeping ensure early detection of problems.

Testing does not require laboratory equipment; most methods use simple indicators, household chemistry, or observations. Severity thresholds for agriculture are less stringent than drinking water standards but critical for safety.

## Quick Routing for Water Testing Questions

Use this guide when the user needs an assessment, not a treatment recipe.

- `is this safe`, `can I use this source`, or `what should I test first` -> start with the source and follow the testing sections below
- `clear`, `normal odor`, `tests in range`, or `routine check` -> safe to continue using for irrigation or livestock; keep the normal testing schedule
- `cloudy`, `recent rain`, `slightly off pH`, `minor odor`, or `elevated iron/hardness` -> questionable; settle, pre-filter, retest, and monitor trends
- `sewage smell`, `coliform positive`, `algae bloom`, `foaming`, or `arsenic/lead/pesticides suspected` -> follow-up needed; stop using for leafy greens or drinking and hand off to water-purification for treatment selection
- `how do I make this drinkable` or `what treatment should I use` -> hand off to water-purification after you identify the problem

This guide answers whether the source is acceptable and what is wrong with it. When the question becomes how to disinfect or make the water potable, switch to [Water Purification](water-purification) for the treatment decision.

## Turbidity Testing

Turbidity is the cloudiness of water caused by suspended particles (clay, silt, algae, sediment). High turbidity (>5 NTU—Nephelometric Turbidity Units) clogs irrigation emitters and indicates water carries sediment that may clog soil pores over time.

### Secchi Disk Method (Free, Visual)

A Secchi disk is a simple 20 cm (8-inch) white disk. Lower it into a clear tube or bucket of water on a string until it disappears from view, then raise it until you see it again. The depth at which it disappears is the Secchi depth.

**Construction:**
1. Cut a 20 cm diameter white disk from PVC plastic or wood.
2. Divide into alternating black and white quadrants (paint or use tape).
3. Drill a center hole and attach a rope with knots every 10 cm for depth measurement.

**Interpretation:**
- **Secchi depth >40 cm**: Clear water (low turbidity, <1 NTU). Safe for irrigation.
- **Secchi depth 20–40 cm**: Slightly turbid (1–5 NTU). Acceptable; monitor emitters for clogging.
- **Secchi depth 10–20 cm**: Moderately turbid (5–10 NTU). Pre-filter before irrigation (settling pond, sand filter).
- **Secchi depth <10 cm**: Highly turbid (>10 NTU). Do not use for drip irrigation; use for flood irrigation only, or treat.

:::warning
High turbidity after heavy rain suggests erosion upstream or bank slump. Test again after 24–48 hours. If turbidity remains high, investigate the source.
:::

### Jar Test (Quick Visual Check)

Fill a clear glass jar with sample water. Hold it against a white background in daylight. If you cannot read text through the jar, turbidity is >5 NTU.

**Grain observation:**
Look for fine sediment grain size:
- **Sand (gritty)**: Settles in <1 minute; can be removed by screening and settling ponds.
- **Silt (fine powder)**: Settles in 1–2 hours.
- **Clay (very fine)**: May take days to settle; requires chemical flocculation (alum) or filtration.

If water is gray-green or brown, algae or organic matter is present. Test for dissolved oxygen and biological activity (see below).

## pH Testing: Acidity and Alkalinity

pH indicates if water is acidic (<7), neutral (7), or alkaline (>7). Most crops prefer pH 6.0–7.5 for irrigation. Acidic water (pH <6) corrodes metal pipes and may leach heavy metals from soil. Alkaline water (pH >8) can precipitate phosphorus and iron, making them unavailable to plants.

### Litmus Paper Test (Cheap, ~0.05 per test)

Litmus paper changes color in the presence of acids or bases.

**Method:**
1. Dip a strip of red or blue litmus paper into the water sample.
2. Red litmus paper turns blue in alkaline conditions.
3. Blue litmus paper turns red in acidic conditions.
4. Match the color to the reference scale (typically 0–14 pH).

**Accuracy**: ±1 pH unit (adequate for field use).

### Red Cabbage Juice Indicator (Free)

Red cabbage contains anthocyanins, a natural pH indicator that changes color from red (acidic) to green (neutral) to yellow (alkaline).

**Method:**
1. Chop 1/4 of a red cabbage finely.
2. Boil in 200 ml water for 10 minutes.
3. Strain and cool.
4. Add 10 ml cabbage juice to 40 ml water sample.
5. Compare color:
   - **Red**: pH <5 (very acidic)
   - **Pink**: pH 5–6 (acidic)
   - **Purple**: pH 6–7 (neutral)
   - **Green**: pH 7–8 (alkaline)
   - **Yellow**: pH >8 (very alkaline)

**Storage**: Cabbage juice keeps 2–3 weeks in a sealed, cool bottle.

### pH Strips (Low cost, ~0.20 per test)

Commercial pH strips (0–14 range) from any pharmacy are reliable to ±0.5 pH units.

**Method:**
1. Dip strip into water for 2 seconds.
2. Remove and allow to dry for 30 seconds.
3. Compare color to reference card on the box.

| **pH** | **Classification** | **Irrigation Suitability** |
|--------|-------------------|---------------------------|
| <5 | Very acidic | Corrosive; add agricultural lime (CaCO₃) to raise pH |
| 5–6 | Acidic | Acceptable; monitor for metal corrosion |
| 6–7.5 | Neutral, slightly alkaline | Ideal |
| 7.5–8 | Alkaline | Acceptable; monitor for phosphorus/iron precipitation |
| >8 | Very alkaline | Poor; adds bicarbonate; causes mineral issues |

:::info-box
If pH is consistently outside 6–7.5, treat the water or select crops tolerant to that pH. Blueberries prefer pH 4.5–5.5; alfalfa prefers pH >7.0.
:::

## Bacterial Contamination Testing

Pathogenic bacteria (E. coli, Salmonella) and parasites (Cryptosporidium, Giardia) contaminate water from human/animal waste. Testing for total coliform bacteria (an indicator of fecal contamination) is more practical than testing for pathogens directly.

### Coliform Testing: Lactose Broth Method

This is a simplified home version of the standard coliform test. Coliforms are gram-negative bacteria common in animal feces. Their presence indicates potential fecal contamination and risk of pathogens.

**Equipment needed:**
- Sterile test tube (boiled or purchased).
- Lactose broth (prepared below or purchased as dehydrated powder from lab supply).
- Incubator (oven set to 35°C / 95°F works; alternatively, water bath or warm area).

**Prepare lactose broth:**
1. Dissolve 5 grams of beef extract, 5 grams of peptone, and 5 grams of lactose in 1 liter of distilled water.
2. Adjust pH to 7.0–7.2 (optional, but helps).
3. Dispense 10 ml into sterile test tubes.
4. Boil/autoclave for 15 minutes to sterilize, or boil in a pressure canner at 10 PSI for 10 minutes.
5. Cool before use. Store in refrigerator.

**Test:**
1. Add 1 ml of water sample to a tube of lactose broth.
2. Incubate at 35°C (95°F) for 24–48 hours (keep tube in a warm location, or use an oven at lowest setting; temperature must stay within 35–37°C).
3. **Result:**
   - **No gas production (no bubbles)**: Likely no coliforms (or very low numbers). Safe for irrigation.
   - **Gas in tube (bubbles trapped at top of tube)**: Presumptive positive for coliforms. Water is potentially unsafe; do not use for vegetable irrigation. Treat or find alternative source.

**Limitations**: This test is presumptive only. Confirmation requires Gram staining or further culturing, which requires lab equipment. Assume any gas production means contamination is present.

### Visual/Smell Indicators

- **Odor**: Rotten or sewage smell strongly suggests recent contamination.
- **Foam**: Detergent or organic matter indicates human/animal runoff.
- **Color**: Brown or orange water may indicate iron bacteria or acid mine drainage (test pH).
- **Visible sediment**: Suggests mechanical runoff; do not assume microbial contamination without testing.

:::danger
Do NOT use water with clear signs of sewage (smell, visible feces, foam) for crop irrigation, even if you plan to cook the produce. Pathogens are robust; heat from cooking may not kill all of them.
:::

## Chemical Contaminant Testing

Chemical contamination includes heavy metals (arsenic, lead, cadmium), pesticide residues, and mining/industrial runoff.

### Iron Testing (Field Strip Test)

Excess iron (>0.3 mg/L) causes brown staining on equipment and soil, and can form biofilms in irrigation systems.

**Simple observation:**
1. Fill a clear glass with water.
2. Let stand for 1 hour.
3. If brown sediment or rust color appears at the bottom or on the container walls, iron is present.

**Iron test strip** (purchased from lab supply, ~0.50 per test):
1. Dip into water.
2. Compare color to reference scale (0–3 mg/L typically).
3. **<0.3 mg/L**: Safe.
4. **0.3–3 mg/L**: Elevated; monitor for staining; use settling pond to allow precipitation.
5. **>3 mg/L**: Very high; treat with aeration and settling, or use alternative source.

### Arsenic (Field Test Kit, ~$10–20)

Arsenic contamination is common in areas with natural arsenic deposits or historical mining/pesticide use. Arsenic is odorless and colorless; field testing requires a kit.

**Arsenic field kit (purchased):**
1. Follow kit instructions (typically involve mixing water sample with reagent powder and comparing color change to reference).
2. **<0.01 mg/L (10 ppb—parts per billion)**: Safe (US drinking water limit).
3. **0.01–0.05 mg/L**: Elevated; limit use; boil or filter if for drinking; okay for irrigation if <0.05 mg/L and crops are not leafy greens (arsenic concentrates in aboveground tissue less).

:::warning
If arsenic is detected, send a sample to a lab for confirmation. Do not rely on a single field test. If confirmed, treat via reverse osmosis or activated charcoal filtration, or find an alternative source.
:::

### Lead (Field Test Kit, ~$5–10)

Lead is common in water near old mines, smelters, or from corroded plumbing.

**Lead test kit (purchased):**
1. Follow instructions (typically involve adding reagent and observing color change).
2. **<0.015 mg/L (15 ppb)**: Safe.
3. **>0.015 mg/L**: Risk; do not use for drinking or vegetable irrigation.

### Pesticide Contamination (Observation + Lab Confirmation)

Field testing for pesticides is impractical; suspect it if:
- Water is downstream of agricultural land with recent pesticide application.
- Smell or taste is unusual (bitter, sweet, chemical).
- Fish or frogs are absent from the water body.
- Vegetation near the water body is damaged or dying.

**Response:** Send a sample to a lab for multi-residue testing (covers 100+ common pesticides). Cost is $100–300. If pesticides are detected, do NOT use for crop irrigation.

## Hardness Testing

Water hardness is the concentration of dissolved calcium and magnesium. Hard water (>200 ppm—parts per million—total hardness) can precipitate minerals on soil pores, reducing water infiltration. Over years, this causes soil compaction and poor drainage.

### Soap Test (Free)

1. Fill a clear bottle halfway with water sample.
2. Add 5–10 drops of liquid dish soap.
3. Shake vigorously for 10 seconds.
4. Count the number of suds (foam) layers:
   - **Many suds (1+ inch thick)**: Soft water (<60 ppm). Ideal.
   - **Some suds (0.5–1 inch)**: Moderately hard (60–120 ppm). Acceptable.
   - **Few suds (0–0.5 inch)**: Hard (>120 ppm). May cause issues over time.

### Hardness Titration (Simple Lab Method)

**Equipment:**
- Buret or syringe (0–50 ml).
- Test tube.
- Ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA) standard solution (0.01 M, purchased or prepared).
- Eriochrome Black T indicator (powder, purchased from lab supply).
- Ammonium hydroxide (ammonia, household strength works).

**Method:**
1. Measure 20 ml water sample into test tube.
2. Add 5 drops ammonia (to buffer pH to 10).
3. Add 2–3 grains of Eriochrome Black T powder (color should turn red).
4. Fill buret with EDTA solution.
5. Titrate (slowly add EDTA from buret, swirling tube) until red color changes to blue. Record ml of EDTA used.
6. **Calculation**: ml EDTA × 2.5 = ppm CaCO₃ (hardness as calcium carbonate equivalents).
   - **<60 ppm**: Soft.
   - **60–120 ppm**: Moderate.
   - **120–250 ppm**: Hard.
   - **>250 ppm**: Very hard.

### Management of Hard Water

- **<120 ppm**: No treatment needed.
- **120–200 ppm**: Monitor soil; use a leaching fraction (apply 20–30% excess water to flush salts from root zone).
- **>200 ppm**: Use a settling pond to allow some minerals to precipitate; or blend with softer water source; or apply agricultural gypsum (CaSO₄) to soil annually to improve structure.

| **Hardness (ppm CaCO₃)** | **Irrigation Impact** | **Management** |
|------------------------|---------------------|---------------|
| <60 | Ideal | None |
| 60–120 | Acceptable | Monitor soil structure annually |
| 120–250 | Elevated | Increase leaching fraction; aerate soil; amend with gypsum |
| >250 | Problematic | Find softer source or treat |

## Dissolved Oxygen Testing

Dissolved oxygen (DO) is important for aquatic life (fish, aquatic plants). Low DO (<3 mg/L) indicates stagnant water with high biological oxygen demand (BOD)—organic matter decomposing and consuming oxygen. This suggests recent contamination or algal blooms (both indicators of water quality issues).

### Simple Observation (Non-numeric)

- **Healthy stream**: Clear, moving, no odor, fish present. DO assumed adequate (>5 mg/L).
- **Stagnant pond**: Murky, no flow, may smell of rotten eggs (hydrogen sulfide). DO likely <3 mg/L.
- **Algal bloom** (green water, surface scum): Often indicates high nutrients and variable DO—may be high during day (algae photosynthesizing) and low at night (algae respiring). Not recommended for irrigation if algae are thick.

### Dissolved Oxygen Test Kit (Purchased, ~$15–30)

Commercial DO test kits (electronic or chemical) measure milligrams per liter:

**Test method (varies by kit; instructions on package):**
- Typically involve adding reagent to water sample and measuring color change or using electrode.
- **>5 mg/L**: Healthy.
- **3–5 mg/L**: Marginal; avoid if possible.
- **<3 mg/L**: Poor water quality; do not use.

:::info-box
If your source shows low DO, increase aeration by splashing water, using a fountain, or installing a small aerator. Aeration also helps oxidize iron and removes some volatile compounds.
:::

## Source Assessment: Springs, Wells, and Surface Water

Different water sources carry different contamination risks.

### Spring Water

Springs emerge from groundwater and are generally clean if the spring outlet is protected.

**Advantages:**
- Cold, typically clear.
- Less turbidity than surface water.
- Some natural filtration through soil layers.

**Risks:**
- If the spring is close to surface (limestone karst areas), contamination can reach the spring within days.
- Seasonal variability; may dry in summer.

**Testing frequency:** Monthly during rainy season; quarterly in dry season.

### Well Water

Wells tap groundwater at depth, offering better protection from surface contamination.

**Advantages:**
- Buffered from seasonal changes.
- Protected from most surface contaminants.
- Generally consistent temperature and chemistry.

**Risks:**
- If well is shallow (<30 feet) or poorly constructed, surface contamination can seep in.
- Historic contamination (arsenic, nitrates from past agricultural use) may be present.
- Long-term mining or industrial activity upstream can affect groundwater.

**Testing frequency:** Annually; immediately after heavy rain if well is shallow.

**Well construction check:**
- Inspect well cap; ensure it is sealed and elevated above ground.
- No standing water around well.
- No obvious cracks in well casing.

### Surface Water (Rivers, Ponds, Ditches)

Surface water is most vulnerable to contamination from runoff and livestock.

**Advantages:**
- Abundant.
- Easy to access.

**Risks:**
- High turbidity after rain.
- Seasonal bacterial and algal blooms.
- Pesticide and fertilizer runoff.
- Livestock access and fecal contamination.

**Testing frequency:** Before each use if possible; minimum monthly; daily during heavy rain.

**Protection:**
- If taking from a stream, sample upstream of pastures and settlements if possible.
- Use the top 6–12 inches of water (avoid bottom sediment where bacteria concentrate).
- Let water settle in a bucket for 10 minutes before use to allow some sediment to drop.

## Testing Schedule and Record-Keeping

### Recommended Testing Frequency

**By source type:**

| **Parameter** | **Spring** | **Well (Deep)** | **Well (Shallow)** | **Surface** |
|---------------|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|------------|
| Turbidity | Monthly | Quarterly | Monthly | Before each use |
| pH | Quarterly | Annually | Quarterly | Monthly |
| Coliform | Monthly | Quarterly | Monthly | Before use |
| Iron | Annually | Annually | Quarterly | Monthly |
| Hardness | Annually | Annually | Annually | As needed |
| Dissolved oxygen | If stagnant | N/A | If stagnant | Monthly |

**By season:**
- **Wet season/monsoon**: Increase frequency (turbidity and pathogen risk rises).
- **Dry season**: Check for drying trends; monitor well levels and pH changes.

### Record-Keeping Template

Maintain a simple table in a notebook or spreadsheet:

| **Date** | **Source** | **Turbidity (Secchi cm)** | **pH** | **Coliform (Y/N)** | **Color** | **Odor** | **Notes** |
|----------|-----------|--------------------------|--------|-------------------|-----------|---------|----------|
| 2026-02-01 | Well A | 45 cm | 7.0 | N | Clear | None | Normal |
| 2026-02-15 | Spring B | 30 cm | 6.8 | N | Slightly cloudy | None | Post-rain |
| 2026-03-01 | Pond | 15 cm | 7.5 | Y | Green tint | Algae | Bloom; do not use |

**Action triggers** (test immediately if observed):
- Turbidity suddenly increases.
- Odor or color changes.
- Equipment corrosion or staining.
- Animals avoiding the water.
- Visible algal bloom or foam.

### Response to Problem Results

| **Issue** | **Action** |
|-----------|-----------|
| High turbidity (>5 NTU) | Build settling pond or pre-filter; test again after treatment |
| Low pH (<5.5) | Add agricultural lime (CaCO₃); retest weekly until stable >6 |
| Coliform positive | Boil water if for drinking, or hand off to [Water Purification](water-purification) for treatment selection; do not use for leafy greens; treat with bleach (0.5–2 ppm) and retest |
| High iron (>0.3 mg/L) | Aerate; install settling pond; monitor emitters for clogging |
| High hardness (>200 ppm) | Apply gypsum to soil; increase leaching fraction; blend with softer source if available |

## Quick Treatment Methods

### Settling/Sedimentation

Allow water to sit in a still pond or tank for 24 hours. Sediment and some pathogens settle to the bottom. Withdraw water from the top 80% of the tank; discard bottom.

**Effectiveness:** Removes 70–90% of suspended solids; some pathogens (sinks with sediment).

### Boiling

Heat water to a rolling boil for 1 minute (3 minutes above 2000 m elevation). Kills all pathogens.

**Use for:** Drinking water or high-value crops if contamination is confirmed.
**Limitation:** Not practical for irrigation of large areas; energy-intensive.

### Bleach (Chlorination)

Add household bleach (5% sodium hypochlorite) to disinfect water:
- **Turbid water (>5 NTU)**: 2 ppm chlorine residual (add 0.4 ml bleach per 100 liters).
- **Clear water (<5 NTU)**: 0.5 ppm chlorine residual (add 0.1 ml bleach per 100 liters).
- Let sit for 30 minutes before use.

**Effectiveness:** Kills most bacteria and viruses; less effective against Cryptosporidium.

### Sand Filtration (Home-Made)

Build a simple sand filter using a plastic barrel:
1. Cut the bottom out of a barrel (or use a barrel with a spigot).
2. Layer (bottom to top): gravel (5 cm), coarse sand (5 cm), fine sand (10 cm), gravel (5 cm).
3. Pour turbid water into top; it filters through sand and emerges clearer from the bottom/spigot.
4. **Effectiveness:** Removes 70–90% of particles >10 micrometers; reduces some pathogens.
5. **Maintenance:** Backflush (reverse water flow) weekly to clean sand; replace sand annually.

## Summary Checklist

- **Start with visual inspection** (turbidity, color, odor) and simple tests (Secchi disk, litmus paper).
- **Test coliform bacteria monthly** if water quality is uncertain.
- **Establish a testing schedule** based on source type (springs quarterly, wells annually, surface water monthly or before use).
- **Keep records** to track trends and identify problems early.
- **Respond quickly** to high turbidity, pH extremes, or coliform positives.
- **Use settling ponds or simple filtration** to improve surface water quality.
- **Test for iron and hardness annually** to prevent long-term soil damage.

Regular, simple testing prevents crop damage and maintains livestock health. The tools are inexpensive and reusable; the time investment is minimal compared to the value of reliable water access.
