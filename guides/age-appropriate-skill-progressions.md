## Developmental Framework

Effective knowledge transfer requires matching teaching to cognitive development. Children at age 6 cannot understand abstract reasoning; children at 14 can. Attempting to teach advanced concepts too early wastes time. Scaffolding skills appropriately accelerates learning and builds confidence.

This guide maps core survival skills to developmental stages, preventing both under-stimulation (boring) and overwhelming (too-hard). Each age band reflects research on child cognitive development and practical application to post-collapse skill transmission.

:::warning
**Critical principle:** Don't skip foundational stages. A child who never learned basic fire safety (ages 5-7) will struggle with advanced fire management (ages 14-16). Progression builds cumulatively—each stage provides foundation for the next.
:::
</section>

<section id="ages-5-7">

## Ages 5-7: Foundation & Safety Awareness

### Cognitive Abilities at This Stage

- Concrete thinking (can't handle abstractions)
- Short attention span (5-15 minutes)
- Learning through play and imitation
- Developing basic safety awareness
- Strong observation skills
- Beginning to follow multi-step instructions

### Core Skills to Introduce

| Skill Domain | Specific Competencies | Teaching Method | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Fire Safety** | Understand fire is hot/dangerous, stay back from flames, never touch, know where adults work with fire | Observation + storytelling + supervised observation | Ongoing |
| **Water Safety** | Don't drink untreated water without adult, drowning danger, cold water danger | Direct instruction + demonstration | 2-3 weeks |
| **Basic Food Safety** | Wash hands before eating, don't eat unknown plants, spoiled food makes sick | Habit training + modeling | Ongoing |
| **Tool Awareness** | Sharp tools are dangerous, heavy tools cause injury, learn tool names | Observation + labeled tools + stories | 2-3 weeks |
| **Animal Safety** | Some animals are safe, some dangerous, how to recognize and avoid | Stories + controlled observation of animals | Ongoing |
| **Shelter Awareness** | Sun is hot (need shade), cold nights happen (need blankets), rain is wet (need cover) | Lived experience + discussion | Ongoing |

### Teaching Approach

**Immersion and imitation.** Don't teach fire safety in abstract lecture—show children how adults work with fire safely. They observe, ask questions, learn through participation in real activities. Direct instruction is brief (2-3 minute explanations), followed by observation and guided practice.

**Storytelling.** Children at this age learn through story. "The child who touched fire burned his hand" is memorable. "Fire has a hot zone where we can't go" is abstract and forgotten. Stories stay with children; lectures don't.

**Habit formation.** Core safety habits (wash hands before eating, don't touch sharp tools alone) established through daily repetition. After 3-4 weeks of consistent practice, behavior becomes automatic.

### Difficulty Level

**Beginner.** These are awareness and avoidance competencies, not active skills. Success means child understands danger and follows safety rules. Competency demonstrated through observation of safe behavior in everyday activities.

### Example Progression

**Month 1:** Introduce concept through stories. "Adults work with fire here. Fire is hot. We stay back there [gesture to safe distance]. When we're older, we'll learn to use fire."

**Month 2-3:** Child participates in activities near but not with fire. Observes. Asks questions. Adult explains. "See the color? That tells us the metal is ready. Feel the heat from here—that's enough danger, we stay back."

**Month 4+:** Behavior is habituated. Child automatically maintains safety distance. Child can explain to younger children why fire is dangerous.

<section id="ages-8-10">

## Ages 8-10: Basic Competency & Supervised Practice

### Cognitive Abilities at This Stage

- Concrete thinking still dominant, but beginning abstract reasoning
- Attention span: 15-30 minutes
- Can follow multi-step instructions (3-5 steps)
- Beginning to understand cause-and-effect
- Learning moves from pure observation to active participation
- Pride in demonstrated competency

### Core Skills to Introduce

| Skill Domain | Specific Competencies | Teaching Method | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Water Procurement** | Basic water gathering (bucket, rope into well), testing water quality by sight/smell, boiling process | Supervised practice + explanation | 4-6 weeks |
| **Fire Making** | Striking flint safely, gathering tinder/kindling, building small fires under supervision, extinguishing properly | Hands-on practice with supervision, guided steps | 8-12 weeks |
| **Basic First Aid** | Bandaging minor cuts, recognizing injury severity, basic cleanliness, calling for help | Practice with demonstrations, mock scenarios | 6-8 weeks |
| **Plant Identification** | Learning 5-10 safe local plants, edibility testing, seasonal recognition | Field walks + identification practice + drawing | 12+ weeks |
| **Agriculture Basics** | Planting seeds, watering, weeding, simple harvesting, observing growth | Active participation in garden | Seasonal (ongoing) |
| **Tool Use** | Safe handling of knife, axe, basic saw, basic hammering, tool maintenance | Supervised hands-on practice, observation | 4-6 weeks per tool |
| **Basic Cooking** | Measuring, mixing, heating water safely, simple meal assembly, kitchen organization | Supervised food preparation | Ongoing |

### Teaching Approach

**Scaffolded independence.** Adult demonstrates, child replicates with guidance, adult provides immediate feedback. Gradually reduce oversight as competency grows. "Watch me. Now you try. Good—your grip is correct. Now try alone. I'll watch."

**Structured practice.** Skills practiced repeatedly in slightly varied contexts. Not just one fire-starting session—many fires, different conditions (windy day, cold day, damp wood). Variation prevents fragile learning that works only in one context.

**Safety boundaries.** Child cannot harm themselves or others during learning. Tools are appropriate size/weight for child's capability. Adult is always present during potentially dangerous activities. As competency and judgment develop, independence increases.

### Difficulty Level

**Beginner to Intermediate.** These are foundational practical skills. Competency means child can perform basic task with occasional guidance, with low failure rate and high safety. Child can explain why each step matters.

### Example Progression: Fire Starting

**Week 1-2:** Observation. Child watches adult start fires multiple times. Learns name of each materials (tinder, kindling, fuel wood). Learns the sequence. Learns what "ready to light" looks like.

**Week 3-4:** Assisted practice. Adult strikes flint while child holds tinder bundle. Child arranges kindling while adult watches. Child lights very small tinder while adult is right there.

**Week 5-6:** Guided independence. Child does entire process with adult present. Adult gives cues but doesn't help. "What's your next step?" "Is that dry enough?" Child corrects own mistakes (too much tinder, kindling arranged wrong) with prompting.

**Week 7-8:** Independent practice. Child starts fires alone. Adult watches from distance. Child calls if stuck. Adult intervenes only if safety threatened.

<section id="ages-11-13">

## Ages 11-13: Intermediate Skills & Apprenticeship Readiness

### Cognitive Abilities at This Stage

- Abstract reasoning developing
- Peer relationships important
- Can understand systems and consequences
- Seeking responsibility and respect
- Motivation shifts from "adult says" to "I want to"
- Beginning specialization interests

### Core Skills to Introduce

| Skill Domain | Specific Competencies | Teaching Method | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Food Preservation** | Smoking/drying meat, salting, fermentation basics, proper storage, recognizing spoilage | Supervised practice, document seasonal cycles | 12-20 weeks |
| **Advanced First Aid** | Treating burns, deep cuts, bone injuries, recognizing infection, herbal remedy preparation | Structured lessons + scenarios + supervised practice | 8-12 weeks |
| **Carpentry Basics** | Measuring accurately, sawing straight, hammering nails, basic joints, tool care | Guided projects (build a box, shelf, stool) | 12-20 weeks |
| **Advanced Plant Knowledge** | Identification of 20+ species, medicinal vs. edible vs. poisonous, harvesting timing, preservation | Field studies + documentation + practice | 20+ weeks |
| **Leather Working** | Tanning basics, cutting, stitching, tool use, simple item construction | Hands-on workshop instruction | 8-12 weeks |
| **Textile Basics** | Spinning fibers, basic weaving, simple tool use, finishing techniques | Structured instruction + repeated practice | 12-20 weeks |
| **Metal Basics** | Forge safety, tool handling, heat recognition, simple joining (nails, hardware) | Workshop apprenticeship style | 12-20 weeks |
| **Water Management** | Finding water sources, filtering/boiling purification, simple storage, contamination prevention | Problem-solving approach + field work | 8-12 weeks |

### Teaching Approach

**Apprenticeship model begins.** Youth is assigned to a master practitioner in chosen skill. Master demonstrates, youth assists, youth practices. Master provides feedback and increases responsibility gradually. This is formal knowledge transmission, not casual observation.

**Specialization.** Not all youth learn all skills. Each youth chooses specialization (or 2-3 related skills) based on interest and community need. Depth beats breadth at this stage.

**Project-based learning.** Rather than abstract skill drills, youth complete meaningful projects. Carpenter learns through actually building items. Food preserver learns through actually preserving foods for storage. Projects have real value and motivation.

**Responsibility emphasis.** Youth at this stage want respect. Acknowledge their growing competency publicly. Trust them with increasingly important work. "You're trusted to manage the fermenting station" conveys respect.

### Difficulty Level

**Intermediate.** Skills are more complex, requiring both technical precision and judgment. Competency means youth can perform task independently with acceptable quality and safety, can problem-solve when conditions change, and can explain reasoning.

### Example Progression: Apprenticeship Structure

**Month 1:** Observation phase. Youth shadows master daily (4-6 hours), watches all processes, asks questions, learns theory (why materials chosen, what sequences must happen, what indicators show readiness).

**Month 2-4:** Assisted practice. Youth performs simple aspects under master's supervision. Master demonstrates, youth replicates with guidance. Master inspects all output before use.

**Month 5-8:** Guided independence. Youth works more independently. Master reviews completed work and provides feedback. Youth is making decisions (materials chosen, timing, process variations) with master's approval.

**Month 9+:** Independent practice. Youth handles majority of work independently. Master available for consultation but not hovering. Youth beginning to teach newer apprentices (consolidates learning).

## Ages 14-16: Advanced Skills & Journeyman Path

### Cognitive Abilities at This Stage

- Abstract reasoning well-developed
- Can understand complex systems
- Questioning authority (normal—listen to questions respectfully)
- Peer group highly important
- Beginning to see themselves as "almost adult"
- Capable of teaching younger youth

### Core Skills to Target

By this age, youth should be pursuing 1-2 specializations intensely. Not trying to learn everything—deepening expertise in chosen domains.

| Specialization Path | Advanced Competencies | Timeline to Journeyman |
|---|---|---|
| **Food Preservation** | Master all preservation methods (smoking, drying, salting, fermentation, canning), troubleshooting spoilage, training apprentices | 18-24 months to journeyman |
| **Carpentry** | Complex joinery, accurate measurement, reading plans, structural knowledge, tool mastery, training apprentices | 24-36 months to journeyman |
| **Medicine/Herbal Practice** | Advanced diagnosis, herbal remedy creation, wound care, childbirth assistance, record-keeping | 24-36 months to journeyman |
| **Metalworking** | Forge operation, tool creation, weapon smithing, decorative work, troubleshooting | 36-48 months to journeyman |
| **Agriculture** | Soil management, crop selection, pest control, harvest optimization, teaching younger farmers | 18-24 months to journeyman |
| **Textiles/Leatherwork** | Complex weaving patterns, leather finishing, tool creation, business/production management | 24-36 months to journeyman |
| **Water Systems** | Well maintenance, water testing, storage design, contamination troubleshooting, teaching | 18-24 months to journeyman |

### Teaching Approach

**Near-peer mentorship.** At this stage, youth can learn effectively from slightly older journeymen, not just masters. Journeyman (age 18-25, 2-3 years more experience) often teaches better than master—they remember learning difficulties.

**Increasing autonomy.** Youth makes decisions independently. Master/mentor available for consultation on difficult problems, but youth is expected to solve most problems. "What would you do?" becomes normal question instead of direct instruction.

**Teaching responsibility.** Advanced apprentices teach younger apprentices. Teaching deepens their own understanding and prepares them for potential master status. Recognize this as legitimate skill—teaching well is as valued as technical competency.

**Possible accelerated paths.** Exceptionally talented youth may progress faster. Don't artificially slow them. If youth demonstrates advanced competency, allow acceleration toward journeyman status.

### Difficulty Level

**Intermediate to Advanced.** Skills require significant judgment, problem-solving in novel situations, and often teaching ability. Competency means youth can handle most real-world situations independently, troubleshoot problems, and guide others.

### Example Advancement Timeline

**Year 1 (ages 14-15):** Advanced apprentice. Works independently on most projects. Makes decisions with minimal master oversight. Begins teaching newer apprentices (younger children). Completes complex projects. Developing specialization depth.

**Year 2 (ages 15-16):** Senior apprentice. Handles majority of work independently. Teaches regularly. Takes on leadership within apprenticeship. May be ready for journeyman advancement near year 2 end depending on competency.

**Advancement criteria to journeyman:**
- Consistent competency on complex tasks
- Can teach concepts effectively
- Demonstrated problem-solving in novel situations
- Peer journeymen confirm competency
- Completion of masterwork (substantial project demonstrating full skill range)
- Community recognition/celebration

:::info-box
**Masterwork requirement:** Before advancement to journeyman, youth creates substantial work demonstrating mastery. Carpenter might build a large furniture piece or small structure. Food preserver might create diverse preserved foods for winter. Metalworker might craft complete toolset. Masterwork is examined by multiple masters and becomes community record of achievement.
:::

## Ages 17-18: Journeyman & Adult Transition

### Status and Roles

Youth advancing to journeyman have completed apprenticeship and demonstrated significant competency. They're recognized as skilled practitioners capable of independent work and teaching.

### Skills and Responsibilities

| Path | Competencies | Roles |
|---|---|---|
| **Recent Journeyman** | Independent competent work, teaching ability, beginning specialization within craft | Performs work for community, mentors apprentices, contributes to master council decisions |
| **Advanced Journeyman** | Approaching master-level expertise, consistent excellence, strong teaching record | Potential path to master status, may specialize or cross-train in related fields |
| **Multi-skilled Journeyman** | Competency in 2-3 related skills (e.g., carpentry + basic masonry, food preservation + herbal medicine) | Fills knowledge gaps, increased community value, flexibility |

### Continuing Development

Journeymen don't stop learning. Common pathways:

**Specialization deepening:** Journeyman in carpentry might specialize in furniture, building, or fine detail work. Continued practice and study over years deepens expertise toward master level.

**Cross-training:** Journeyman in carpentry might apprentice briefly in masonry (accelerated—maybe 12 months instead of 24, building on transferable skills). Creates redundancy and flexibility.

**Master preparation:** Journeymen demonstrating exceptional skill, strong teaching commitment, and recognized potential might be invited into master-track advanced study, typically 2-3 additional years focusing on teaching methodology, innovation, and leadership.

**Specialization beyond original craft:** Some journeymen discover stronger interest in teaching or documentation than continuing technical work. They might transition to educator roles (teaching younger apprentices) or archivist roles (documenting procedures and knowledge).

### Teaching Approach

**Peer mentorship.** Journeymen learn primarily from other journeymen and masters through discussion and shared problem-solving, not structured instruction. "We have this problem—how would you solve it?" becomes primary learning mode.

**Innovation allowed.** Journeymen can experiment with technique variations, new tool applications, novel approaches. Masters provide guidance but encourage creative problem-solving within bounds of safety and quality.

**Teaching mastery.** Journeymen developing master potential receive coaching in teaching methodology. How do you explain complex concepts? How do you assess apprentice competency fairly? How do you balance patience with standards? These are learned through practice and feedback.

### Integration into Adult Life

By age 17-18, youth are economically productive community members. Journeymen:
- Perform paid work (food, goods, or labor compensation)
- May have family/partnership responsibilities
- Participate in community decisions affecting their work
- Have status and respect based on competency
- Are expected to begin thinking about knowledge transmission (training next generation)

## Recognizing Readiness for Acceleration

Not all children progress at age-based pace. Some are ready for advanced skills earlier; others need more time. Watch for:

### Signs of Readiness to Accelerate

- **Mastery demonstrated:** Consistently performs current skill level without errors or reminders
- **Seeking challenge:** "Can I try the harder version?" "Can I do this solo?" indicates readiness
- **Teaching others:** When apprentice naturally begins explaining to younger kids, they're consolidating—ready for next level
- **Problem-solving:** When apprentice starts solving problems independently rather than asking, they're ready for more autonomy
- **Safety consciousness:** When apprentice remembers safety rules without reminding, understands why they matter, ready to increase independence

### Signs of Need for Slower Pace

- **Safety breaches:** Repeatedly forgetting or ignoring safety—not ready for independence. Return to supervised practice.
- **Repeated mistakes:** Making same error despite multiple corrections—may need different teaching approach or prerequisite skills
- **Stress/anxiety:** If accelerated learning creates anxiety or resistance, slow down. Confidence matters more than speed.
- **Attention issues:** Difficulty focusing—may indicate task is too hard (need scaffolding) or too boring (need differentiation)
- **Motivation loss:** Interest declining—reassess approach. Is teaching method matching learning style? Does youth understand relevance?

:::tip
**Principle:** Not all children learn same speed. Some learn faster, some slower—neither implies different future capability. Some learn better with hands-on practice (kinesthetic), others through observation (visual), others through explanation (auditory). Different pace and style doesn't equal difference in potential. Respect individual variation while maintaining high expectations for eventual mastery.
:::
