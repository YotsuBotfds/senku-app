<section id="overview">

## Overview

Indoor and hydroponic farming represent modern approaches to food production suitable for survival and resilience scenarios. These systems grow plants without soil, using nutrient solutions in controlled environments. Unlike traditional agriculture, hydroponic systems operate year-round, require 90% less water, and produce significantly higher yields per square foot. This guide covers five primary hydroponic systems (DWC, NFT, Ebb & Flow, vertical towers, and aquaponics), microgreens and sprouting for rapid nutrient-dense crops, mushroom cultivation requiring minimal equipment, and lighting systems optimized for different growth stages.

:::info-box
Indoor farming systems produce harvestable crops within 30–90 days depending on crop type. A single 4×4 foot vertical shelf system with 3 tiers can yield 100–200 plants per month during continuous harvest cycles.
:::

</section>

<section id="hydroponics">

## Hydroponic Fundamentals

Hydroponics grows plants in nutrient solution without soil. More efficient than soil cultivation, requires less water, and produces higher yields in limited space.

### Hydroponic Advantages

-   **Water efficiency:** Uses 90% less water than soil farming (recirculating systems). A lettuce crop requiring 100 gallons in soil needs only 10 gallons hydroponically.
-   **Space efficient:** Vertical stacking maximizes production per square foot. 60+ square feet of growing surface fits within 4 square feet of floor space.
-   **Year-round production:** Controlled environment produces continuously regardless of season. Lettuce grows in 30–45 days in any climate.
-   **Reduced pests:** Absence of soil eliminates many pest habitats. Soilless systems eliminate 70–80% of typical soil-based pest pressure.
-   **Predictable yields:** Controlled nutrients and environment ensure consistent quality and harvest timing.
-   **No tilling/weeding:** Eliminates labor-intensive soil work. Systems require 5–10 hours monthly maintenance versus 20+ hours for equivalent soil gardens.

:::tip
Start with Deep Water Culture (DWC) systems if new to hydroponics. A single 5-gallon bucket system costs under $50, requires no electronics, and succeeds with minimal troubleshooting.
:::

### Components Required

-   **Growing medium:** Rockwool, hydroton, perlite, or coconut coir (replaces soil). Most cost $0.50–2.00 per plant depending on medium.
-   **Nutrient solution:** All-in-one hydroponic fertilizer (simplest) or individual nutrient components (nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium). Cost $0.10–0.50 per liter depending on formulation.
-   **Water reservoir:** Food-grade container holding nutrient solution. 5-gallon buckets ideal for DWC; larger systems need 20–50 gallon tanks.
-   **Air pump & stones:** Oxygen delivery for roots (essential for most systems). Aquarium-grade pumps (500–1500 mL/min) cost $15–40.
-   **pH test kit:** Maintains 5.5-6.5 pH range (optimal nutrient availability). Digital meters cost $20–50; test strips cost $3–8.
-   **EC/TDS meter:** Measures nutrient concentration (prevents over/under fertilization). Typical range 500–2000 PPM depending on crop.
-   **Lighting:** Grow lights providing 12-16 hours daily light. LED fixtures cost $100–500 depending on coverage area.
-   **Environment control:** Thermometer, hygrometer for monitoring conditions. Ideal temperatures 65–75°F; humidity 50–70%.

:::warning
Never use treated lumber, copper, or galvanized metal in nutrient solutions. These leach toxic compounds. Stick to food-grade plastics, stainless steel, or untreated wood only.
:::

<section id="nft">

## NFT (Nutrient Film Technique) Systems

Thin film of nutrient solution flows continuously past plant roots. Highly water-efficient, low-cost, but requires reliable power.

### System Setup

![Indoor Farming Techniques diagram 1](../assets/svgs/indoor-farming-1.svg)

#### Construction Steps

1.  Obtain food-grade channels or construct from PVC gutters (2-4 inch diameter)
2.  Install at slight angle (1-2% slope) toward drain end
3.  Mount reservoir below channels to use gravity return
4.  Install pump delivering nutrient solution to channel inlet
5.  Install drip line or drip emitter at inlet distributing solution evenly
6.  Solution flows as thin film over roots, drains back to reservoir
7.  System requires 8-16 hours daily operation (timer-controlled)

#### Advantages

-   Minimal water usage (continuously recirculated)
-   Simple construction using readily available materials
-   No growing medium cost (roots contact channel directly)
-   Excellent drainage prevents waterlogging

#### Disadvantages

-   Pump failure causes immediate plant death (no water buffer)
-   Power dependency—requires reliable electricity
-   Root rot if pump stops for extended period
-   Channel cleanup required between crops

:::warning
NFT systems require uninterrupted power. A 2-hour pump failure kills most crops. Install battery backup or use manual feed systems in off-grid scenarios. Always keep spare pump and tubing on hand.
:::

<section id="dwc">

## Deep Water Culture (DWC) Systems

Plants suspended above reservoir with roots submerged continuously in oxygenated nutrient solution. Simple, reliable, excellent for beginners.

### System Setup

![Indoor Farming Techniques diagram 2](../assets/svgs/indoor-farming-2.svg)

#### Construction Steps

1.  Use food-grade container (5-gallon bucket, bin, or tank)
2.  Cut holes in lid sized for net pots (2-3 inches diameter)
3.  Place growing medium (hydroton or rockwool) in net pots
4.  Install air pump with air line to air stone in reservoir
5.  Fill reservoir with nutrient solution (8-12 inches depth)
6.  Place seedlings in net pots, roots suspended in solution
7.  Maintain continuous aeration—oxygen essential for root health

#### Advantages

-   Simple construction using basic supplies
-   Highly reliable—works without complex equipment
-   Excellent for small-scale production (home use)
-   Inexpensive startup cost (bucket system under $50)
-   Easy troubleshooting and maintenance

#### Disadvantages

-   Larger plants require frequent water changes (nutrients depleted)
-   No nutrient recirculation (water must be changed regularly)
-   Limited capacity (single bucket holds 4-6 plants typically)
-   Temperature control important (warm solution reduces oxygen)

### Maintenance Schedule

-   **Daily:** Check water level (add distilled or reverse-osmosis water only; minerals cause salts to accumulate), verify pump operation
-   **Weekly:** Monitor plant growth, adjust lighting height (maintain 18–24 inches above canopy), remove dead leaves
-   **Bi-weekly:** Test pH and nutrient concentration (EC/TDS). Record readings in a log to track trends.
-   **Every 3-4 weeks:** Partial water change (25-50%) maintaining nutrient balance. Drain only 1/4–1/2 tank, refill with fresh nutrient solution.
-   **Harvest to replant:** Complete reservoir drain and refill. Clean all surfaces with 10% bleach solution to eliminate algae and pathogens.

:::tip
DWC systems work without electricity if you accept slower plant growth. Use air stones connected to manual air pumps (hand pumps, foot pumps) operated 2–3 times daily. This is ideal for off-grid survival scenarios.
:::

<section id="ebb-flow">

## Ebb & Flow Systems

Periodic flooding and draining of growing bed. Water flows in at high tide, drains at low tide exposing roots to air. Good for varied crops.

### System Components

-   **Growing bed:** Container holding plants and growing medium
-   **Reservoir:** Holds nutrient solution (below growing bed)
-   **Pump:** Cycles solution into growing bed
-   **Timer:** Controls flood/drain cycles (typically 15-30 minutes flood, drain)
-   **Fill line:** Delivers solution from reservoir to bed
-   **Drain line:** Returns solution from bed to reservoir

### Operation

1.  Timer activates pump; nutrient solution floods growing bed (water level rises)
2.  Plants absorb solution while submerged; oxygen-loving roots exposed to air
3.  After flood period (15-30 min), timer deactivates pump
4.  Solution drains back to reservoir via gravity (bed must slope slightly)
5.  Roots exposed to air for 15-30 minutes before next flood cycle
6.  Typically 2-4 flood/drain cycles daily

### Advantages

-   Works well with various growing media (rockwool, perlite, clay pebbles)
-   Roots get oxygen exposure—prevents anoxic conditions
-   Good for larger/longer-season crops
-   Can support diverse crop types simultaneously

#### Disadvantages

-   Pump failure during flood period causes root death by suffocation
-   Timer malfunction risks
-   More complex than DWC
-   Requires careful system design to prevent flooding errors

## Microgreens Production

Harvesting plants at 1-2 weeks old when cotyledon leaves fully developed. Nutritious, fast-growing, excellent home production.

### Growing Steps

1.  **Select seeds:** Fast-germinating varieties (radish, mustard, broccoli, sunflower, pea shoots)
2.  **Prepare trays:** Shallow containers (1-2 inches) with drainage holes
3.  **Add medium:** 1 inch growing medium (coconut coir, perlite mix works well)
4.  **Moisten medium:** Water until saturated but not waterlogged
5.  **Spread seeds:** Scatter seeds densely (goal is dense mature crop)
6.  **Blackout period:** Cover with dark cloth 2-3 days (forces upward growth)
7.  **Misting:** Twice daily misting keeps seeds moist during germination
8.  **Light exposure:** Once sprouted, remove cover and provide 12-16 hours light
9.  **Harvest:** Cut above soil line when first true leaves appear (7-14 days)

<section id="sprouting">

## Sprouting Methods

Germinating seeds to consume when nutrients are highest. Sprouting takes 3-7 days and requires minimal equipment.

### Jar Sprouting (Simplest Method)

1.  Place 1-2 tablespoons seeds in wide-mouth jar
2.  Add water to cover; let soak 8-12 hours
3.  Drain water completely (very important)
4.  Cover jar mouth with cheesecloth or sprouting lid
5.  Rinse 2-3 times daily with lukewarm water, drain completely each time
6.  Keep jar tilted or inverted to maintain moisture while allowing air circulation
7.  Keep away from direct sun (green color develops if exposed to light—still edible but slightly bitter)
8.  Sprouts ready when tails 1/4 to 1/2 inch long (3-7 days depending on seed)
9.  Drain final time and refrigerate—keep 1-2 weeks in sealed bag

### Sprouting Suitable Seeds

-   **Alfalfa:** 3-5 days, mild flavor, most versatile
-   **Mung beans:** 4-5 days, tender, nutty flavor
-   **Lentils:** 3-4 days, crunchy, slight mineral taste
-   **Fenugreek:** 4-5 days, maple flavor, medicinal properties
-   **Broccoli:** 4-6 days, crunchy, anti-inflammatory compounds
-   **Radish:** 4-6 days, spicy kick, nutrient-dense

### Sprouting Safety

:::danger
Raw sprouts carry salmonella and E. coli risk. The warm, moist sprouting environment favors pathogenic bacteria. Keep equipment scrupulously clean—use hot water and soap after every rinse cycle. Discard any sprouts showing mold, slime, or off-odors. Always cook sprouts if immunocompromised or serving to vulnerable populations. Source seeds from reputable suppliers (seed-grade, not agricultural-grade; agricultural seeds are treated with fungicides).
:::

<section id="mushrooms">

## Mushroom Cultivation

Growing mushrooms requires different approach than plants. Easy, high-yield, minimal space required.

### Mushroom Growing Basics

-   **Growing medium:** Substrate (straw, wood chips, sawdust, coffee grounds) inoculated with spawn
-   **Spawn:** Mushroom mycelium grown in grain or sawdust—purchased or home-propagated
-   **Fruiting conditions:** High humidity (80-95%), fresh air exchange, light (not required)
-   **Temperature:** Varies by species (oyster 55-75°F, shiitake 55-70°F)

### Oyster Mushroom Growing (Easiest)

#### Bucket Method

1.  Fill 5-gallon bucket with straw, wood chips, or mixed hardwood sawdust (4 quarts)
2.  Boil substrate 30 minutes, cool completely
3.  Mix in oyster mushroom spawn (1/2 cup per 4 quarts substrate)
4.  Layer substrate and spawn in bucket (alternate layers)
5.  Cover with breathable cloth and rubber band
6.  Keep in dark 50-70°F location for 2-3 weeks (colonization period)
7.  Once fully colonized (white mycelium visible), move to humid location with light
8.  Mist 2-3 times daily to maintain 85%+ humidity
9.  Mushrooms fruit within 7-14 days
10.  Harvest by cutting at base when caps flatten

#### Yield & Timeline

-   **Colonization:** 2-3 weeks at 60-70°F
-   **Fruiting:** 1-2 weeks once moved to humid area (80°F+ with misting)
-   **Yield per bucket:** 1-2 pounds fresh mushrooms per flush
-   **Flushes:** 3-4 flushes (harvests) per bucket cycle possible (one flush every 10-14 days)
-   **Total time:** 4-8 weeks from inoculation to final harvest
-   **Cost per pound:** $1-3 in materials; retail value $8-15 per pound

:::tip
Oyster mushroom spawn tolerates cool temperatures better than shiitake. If room temperature fluctuates 50-75°F, oyster species will colonize reliably. Shiitake requires more stable warmth (65-70°F).
:::

### Shiitake Mushroom Growing

-   **Substrate:** Hardwood logs (oak preferred, 3-6 inches diameter) or sawdust blocks compressed in bags
-   **Spawn:** Shiitake spawn more expensive than oyster ($20-40 per pound versus $10-15)
-   **Colonization:** 3-6 months (slower than oyster; patience required)
-   **Fruiting trigger:** Cold shock—soak logs in 50°F water for 12-24 hours stimulates fruiting
-   **Yield:** 1-2 pounds per log annually for 3-5 years if properly maintained
-   **Spacing:** Logs spaced 4-6 inches apart to allow air circulation and moisture

:::info-box
Shiitake logs produce intermittently (spring and fall flushes) unless cold-shocked monthly. More effort than oyster mushrooms, but far superior flavor and higher market value ($15-25/lb fresh).
:::

### LED Selection

-   **Full spectrum:** 5000-6500K provides balanced growth. Lower spectrum (3000K red) during fruiting stage increases yield.
-   **Dimmable:** Allows adjustment for different growth stages. Seedlings need 40-50% intensity; mature fruiting crops need 100%.
-   **Spacing:** 18-24 inches above plants prevents burning; adjust as plants grow. Monitor leaves for bleaching (too close) or stretching (too far).
-   **Coverage:** Calculate light spread—typically 1,000W LED covers 4×4 feet. Most LED fixtures specify coverage area at 18-24 inch height.
-   **Power efficiency:** Quality LEDs consume 1.5-2 watts per square foot for optimal growth. Count watts: 1,000W fixture covers 500-600 square feet at minimum intensity.

:::warning
Cheap LED fixtures claim high wattage but deliver poor spectrum or low actual power. Buy from manufacturers who publish spectral distribution charts and PPFD (photosynthetic photon flux density) measurements. Low-cost fixtures waste electricity and stunt growth.
:::

## Vertical Growing Systems

Stacking growing beds vertically maximizes production per square foot of floor space. Essential for small-space farming.

### Vertical Hydroponic Tower

Stacking cups or pockets vertically with nutrient solution cycling through center tube.

#### DIY PVC Tower Construction

1.  Use 4-inch PVC pipe (6-10 feet tall depending on space)
2.  Drill 2-3 inch holes every 6-8 inches for net pot pockets
3.  Mount vertically with drip line to center hole (solution flows down through PVC)
4.  Nutrient solution enters at top, drains at bottom to reservoir
5.  Plant in net pots inserted in holes
6.  Typically holds 16-24 plants per tower

#### Advantages

-   Maximizes space (60+ square feet of growing surface in 4 sq ft floor area)
-   All plants receive equal water/nutrient distribution
-   Excellent for lettuce, herbs, and compact plants
-   Visually impressive (good for demonstrations)

#### Challenges

-   Lower plants may not receive adequate light
-   Pump failure affects entire tower
-   Root access difficult for troubleshooting

### Vertical Shelf Systems

Stacking traditional hydroponic trays on shelving with independent light for each level.

#### Setup

-   Industrial shelving (metal, adjustable height)
-   Waterproof trays on each shelf level
-   Independent DWC or NFT system per shelf
-   Individual LED light fixture above each shelf
-   Drain into collection tray or central reservoir

#### Advantages

-   Each shelf independent (failure doesn't affect others)
-   Each shelf customizable for different crops
-   Excellent light exposure to all plants
-   Easy access for maintenance

#### Space Efficiency

-   3-tier shelf system produces 9× the plants in same footprint
-   6 square feet floor space × 3 tiers = 18 square feet growing area
-   Typical home system: 100–200 plants per month harvest cycle
-   Cost: $200-400 shelving + $300-900 LED fixtures + $150-300 hydroponic components = $650-1600 total startup

:::info-box
Vertical shelf systems break even in 4-6 months for households that would otherwise buy $30+ of fresh produce weekly. A 3-tier system growing lettuce and herbs produces 40-60 lbs annually—equivalent to $300-600 retail value.
:::

## Water & Nutrient Management

Nutrient solution is the lifeblood of hydroponic systems. Balance and monitoring directly determine yields.

### Nutrient Solution Composition

Standard hydroponic nutrients contain three macronutrients and six micronutrients:

- **Macronutrients (N-P-K):** Nitrogen (promotes leaf growth), Phosphorus (root and flower development), Potassium (overall plant health). Ratio typically 7-9-5.
- **Secondary nutrients:** Calcium, Magnesium, Sulfur—often deficient in homemade solutions.
- **Micronutrients:** Iron, Manganese, Boron, Zinc, Copper, Molybdenum—required in small quantities but critical.

Two-part or three-part commercial solutions simplify mixing. One-part "complete" solutions are easier but less flexible for customization.

### Water Quality

-   **Source water:** Use distilled or reverse-osmosis water if tap water contains high dissolved minerals. Hard water (>200 PPM calcium/magnesium) causes nutrient imbalances.
-   **pH buffering:** Water pH should be 5.5-6.5. Use pH down (phosphoric acid, $5-10/liter) or pH up (potassium hydroxide) to adjust. Test after 2 hours to allow stabilization.
-   **Temperature:** Maintain 65-72°F. Warm water (>76°F) holds less dissolved oxygen and promotes root rot. Cool water (<60°F) slows growth.
-   **EC/TDS monitoring:** Track electrical conductivity or total dissolved solids. Most crops prefer 1000-1500 PPM. Test twice weekly.

:::warning
High salts (PPM >2000) cause nutrient lock—roots cannot absorb nutrients despite presence. Dilute immediately with 25-50% water change. For recovery, reduce feeding for 1 week and test daily until PPM drops below 1200.
:::

### Nutrient Adjustment Schedule

- **Week 1-2 (seedling):** 600-800 PPM; pH 6.0-6.5; daily water level checks
- **Week 3-6 (vegetative growth):** 1000-1200 PPM; pH 5.8-6.2; every 3 days water change (25%)
- **Week 7+ (fruiting/flowering):** 1200-1500 PPM; pH 5.5-6.0; weekly 25-50% water change to prevent salt buildup
- **Final 2 weeks before harvest (flushing):** 400-600 PPM; plain water only; removes excess nutrients from tissue

<section id="pest-disease">

## Pest & Disease Management

Hydroponics reduces pest pressure compared to soil farming, but problems still occur.

### Prevention Strategies

-   **Cleanliness:** Disinfect all equipment with 10% bleach before starting. Pathogens survive on tools and surfaces.
-   **Isolation:** Keep new plants separate for 2 weeks before integrating into main system. Monitor for pests.
-   **Air quality:** Ensure 1-2 air changes per minute using fans. Stagnant air promotes powdery mildew and fungal diseases.
-   **Humidity:** Maintain 50-70% relative humidity (RH). Above 75% RH encourages mold; below 40% RH stresses plants and invites spider mites.
-   **Water quality:** Treat incoming water with UV sterilization or hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) at 50 PPM to kill pathogens.

### Common Pests

| Pest | Symptoms | Control |
|------|----------|---------|
| Spider mites | Fine webbing, yellow speckled leaves | Spray neem oil or insecticidal soap; increase humidity to 70%+; release predatory mites (*Phytoseiulus*) |
| Aphids | Curled leaves, sticky residue, sooty mold | Spray insecticidal soap 2-3 times weekly; neem oil; remove affected leaves |
| Whiteflies | White flying insects on leaf undersides, yellowing | Yellow sticky traps; spray pyrethrin or neem oil every 5 days for 3 weeks |
| Fungus gnats | Tiny flies in growing medium; weak roots | Allow growing medium to dry between waterings; mosquito dunks in reservoir; sand layer on top |

### Common Diseases

-   **Powdery mildew:** White powder on leaves. Increase airflow, reduce humidity, apply sulfur dust (if allowed) or baking soda solution (1 tbsp per gallon water).
-   **Pythium (root rot):** Brown slimy roots, sudden wilting. Remove affected plants immediately. Increase oxygen (aerate more), lower temperature, change 50-75% of water. Treat with hydrogen peroxide (15 mL of 3% H2O2 per gallon).
-   **Fusarium:** Yellowing leaves starting from base, internal browning of stems. No cure—remove plant immediately and sterilize system. Never compost infected plant material.
-   **Leaf spotting (bacterial/fungal):** Dark lesions on leaves. Remove affected leaves, increase air circulation, reduce leaf wetness during misting.

:::tip
Hydrogen peroxide (food-grade, 3% dilution) is your emergency fix for oxygen-related problems. Add 1-2 mL per gallon weekly to boost dissolved oxygen and suppress anaerobic pathogens. Safe for edibles; breaks down into water and oxygen.
:::

<section id="harvest-storage">

## Harvest & Storage

Timing harvest at peak nutrition and using proper storage extends shelf life and nutrient value.

### Harvest Timing by Crop

| Crop | Harvest Indicator | Timing | Storage Method | Shelf Life |
|------|-------------------|--------|-----------------|------------|
| Lettuce/Greens | Leaves 4-6 inches long; harvest outer leaves first | Morning when turgid; 30-45 days | Plastic bags in crisper, mist daily | 5-10 days |
| Herbs (basil, parsley) | Pinch growing tip when 6-8 inches tall; promotes bushiness | Morning before heat; harvest 30% max at once | Crisper in damp paper towels | 3-7 days |
| Tomatoes | Fully colored but still slightly firm; snap off stem | Pick at first color for ripening off vine | Ripen at room temp away from direct sun | 5-10 days if unripe; 2-3 days if ripe |
| Peppers | Full size, glossy skin; some color change | Can harvest green or wait for color change | Room temperature, ventilated area | 7-14 days |
| Microgreens | First true leaves fully expanded; 1-2 inches tall | 7-14 days; morning harvest best | Damp cloth in sealed container, refrigerate | 3-5 days |
| Mushrooms | Caps flattening (before releasing spores); sliced stem attached | 20-30 minutes before full spore release | Paper bag in crisper (not plastic) | 3-5 days |

### Post-Harvest Handling

-   **Cooling:** Place harvested crops in cool area (50-55°F) immediately. Most greens wilt within hours at room temperature.
-   **Hydration:** Mist leafy greens with cool water immediately after harvest. Hydrated leaves store 2-3× longer.
-   **Drying:** Pat microgreens and mushrooms dry gently before storing. Excess moisture promotes decay.
-   **Ethylene separation:** Keep tomatoes and peppers separate from leafy greens. Ethylene gas they emit accelerates greens aging.

:::info-box
Harvest in early morning (6-8 AM) when plant tissues are fully hydrated. Night temperatures cool plants, making harvest samples last 1-2 days longer in storage.
:::

## Yield Expectations & Productivity

Understanding realistic yields helps plan production and food security.

### Annual Production by System Type

-   **Single 5-gallon DWC bucket (4 plants):** 15-30 lbs lettuce/year with continuous 30-45 day harvest cycles; or 10-15 lbs tomatoes/peppers per year (single season)
-   **3-tier vertical shelf system (6 sq ft floor):** 60-120 lbs lettuce/herbs/year; or 30-50 lbs mixed vegetables per year
-   **NFT system (12 linear feet):** 80-150 lbs leafy greens per year depending on crop rotation
-   **Mushroom bucket (3 flushes):** 3-6 lbs fresh mushrooms per bucket per cycle (30-50 lbs annually with restocking)

### Factors Affecting Yield

-   **Light:** Insufficient light (< 400 PPFD) cuts yields 40-50%. Optimal light doubles growth rate.
-   **Temperature:** 5°F drop from optimal slows growth 10-15%. Systems below 60°F or above 80°F reduce yields 25-40%.
-   **Nutrient concentration:** Underfeeding (PPM <800) reduces yield 30-50%. Overfeeding (PPM >2000) causes nutrient lock and similar yield loss.
-   **CO₂:** Elevated CO₂ (800-1000 PPM) increases yields 20-30%, but requires sealed space with ventilation (not practical in open indoor spaces).
-   **Crop rotation:** Continuous cropping (harvest one plant, replace immediately) maintains full production. Week-long gaps between plantings reduce annual output 15-20%.

## Troubleshooting Common Problems

### Nutrient Deficiencies

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Symptom</th><th scope="row">Nutrient Deficient</th><th scope="row">Solution</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Yellow lower leaves, green veins</td><td>Iron</td><td>Check pH (too high reduces iron availability), add chelated iron if needed</td></tr><tr><td>Purple/reddish discoloration</td><td>Phosphorus</td><td>Add phosphorus supplement, check EC (may be nutrient lock)</td></tr><tr><td>Yellowing between veins, brown edges</td><td>Potassium</td><td>Add potassium supplement, increase nutrient concentration</td></tr><tr><td>Stunted growth overall</td><td>Nitrogen</td><td>Increase nutrient solution strength (check EC reading)</td></tr></tbody></table>

### Root Issues

-   **Root rot (brown, slimy roots):** Low oxygen (increase aeration), high temperature (cool solution), or high nutrient concentration (dilute)
-   **Root algae growth:** Light penetrating roots (block light from reservoir), excess nutrients (reduce concentration)
-   **Wilting despite moisture:** Root damage from pH extremes (adjust to 5.5-6.5), temperature shock (stabilize), or disease

### Pest/Disease Issues

-   **Fungal diseases:** Increase air circulation, reduce humidity to 60-70% at night, ensure good drainage
-   **Spider mites/aphids:** Neem oil spray (caution on edibles), predatory insects, or insecticidal soap
-   **Powdery mildew:** Increase airflow, reduce humidity, sulfur dust (if permitted), or sulfur spray

### System Problems

-   **Pump failure:** Keep spare pump and power cord; check power source, replace if worn
-   **Algae bloom:** Block light from solution (opaque containers/covers), reduce nutrient concentration if excess
-   **pH drift:** Test twice weekly, adjust with pH up (potassium hydroxide) or pH down (phosphoric acid)
-   **EC reading too high:** Partial water change, don't feed further until reduced

## Common Mistakes & How to Avoid Them

New growers repeat certain errors. Learning from others prevents crop loss.

### Setup Mistakes

1. **Using untreated wood or galvanized metal:** These leach toxins into nutrient solution over weeks. Use only food-grade plastic, stainless steel, or sealed wood. Cost to fix: complete water change ($20-50) and equipment replacement.

2. **Insufficient aeration:** DWC and ebb & flow systems fail without constant oxygen. Air stone must bubble continuously. Even 15 minutes of pump failure can damage roots. Backup air pump costs $20-30 and prevents total crop loss.

3. **Inadequate lighting distance:** Keeping lights too far away (>36 inches) causes stretching and weak growth. Plants become tall but spindly. Reset light height to 18-24 inches immediately.

4. **Wrong growing medium:** Rockwool retains water longer than hydroton. Mixing media in same system causes some plants to get too much/too little water. Use consistent medium per system.

### Nutrient Mistakes

1. **Overfeeding from the start:** New growers assume more nutrients = faster growth. Excess nutrients (>1500 PPM) lock up absorption and burn roots. Start conservatively (900 PPM) and increase by 100 PPM weekly while monitoring plant response.

2. **Never changing water:** Nutrients deplete unevenly. Potassium depletes before nitrogen. After 3-4 weeks, salt accumulation exceeds 2000 PPM even with conservative feeding. Change 25-50% water every 2-3 weeks minimum.

3. **Ignoring pH drift:** pH drifts toward 8+ in tap water systems within 1 week. Out-of-range pH (below 5 or above 7) locks nutrients. Test pH twice weekly and adjust daily if needed.

4. **Mixing nutrient brands:** Different brands use different chelation methods and nutrient ratios. Mixing causes imbalances. Stick to one brand; switch only by doing complete water change.

### Environmental Mistakes

1. **High heat without cooling:** Temperatures >80°F cause root rot despite aeration. If summer brings heat, add submersible chillers ($80-300), or relocate system to cool area (basement, shade).

2. **Excessive humidity indoors:** Humidity >75% in poorly ventilated space causes mold within days. Install exhaust fan (4-6 inch $40-80) drawing moist air outside. Fan should exchange air 3-4× per hour.

3. **Lights on 24/7:** Plants need 4-8 hours darkness for metabolic processes. 24-hour light stresses plants and increases electricity cost. Use timer running 12-16 hours per crop.

4. **Inconsistent light schedule:** Changing light hours disrupts flowering. Use simple digital timers (electricity costs aside) set to same hours daily.

:::warning
Never add new nutrients on top of stale solution "just to boost." This creates toxic salt concentrations. Always calculate total PPM from existing nutrients + additions before dosing. Better to dilute first and add conservatively.
:::

## Climate Adaptations & Seasonal Adjustments

Indoor farming bypasses outdoor weather, but climate still influences cooling/heating costs and optimal setups.

### Cold Climates (Below 50°F Outdoor Winter Temps)

**Challenges:** Heating costs rise; water temperature drops.

**Adaptations:**
- Insulate reservoir with foam wrap (R-10 minimum). Reduces heating 30-40%.
- Use submersible heater maintaining 68°F (thermostat-controlled; $25-50). Cheaper than system cooling later.
- Relocate growing space to interior room, away from exterior walls. Reduces heating load 15-20%.
- Increase light hours to 16+ hours. Longer photoperiod compensates for low outdoor light in winter.
- Choose cold-tolerant crops (lettuce, microgreens, mushrooms). Peppers and fruiting crops suffer in cool climates.

### Hot Climates (Above 80°F Outdoor Summer Temps)

**Challenges:** Cooling is expensive; evaporative cooling less effective in humid regions.

**Adaptations:**
- Relocate to underground space or air-conditioned area during summer. Basements naturally stay 55-65°F year-round.
- Install submersible chiller if system stays indoors. Costs $200-500; prevents root rot from warm solution.
- Reduce light intensity (dim LEDs to 70%) to lower heat generation. Slightly reduced yield beats crop loss.
- Increase nutrient aeration—warm water holds 30% less oxygen. Double air pump output in summer.
- Grow heat-tolerant crops: basil, certain herbs, microgreens, mushrooms. Avoid tomatoes/peppers in high heat.

### Variable Climates (Swings >30°F Between Seasons)

**Challenges:** Sudden temperature shifts stress systems; fungal diseases increase during humidity spikes.

**Adaptations:**
- Maintain stable room temperature with HVAC (most cost-effective). Even 5°F variance triggers disease pressure.
- Install humidity-controlled exhaust fan (hygrostat $30-50 triggers at 70% RH). Prevents mold during humid seasons.
- Choose hardy crops tolerant of 60-75°F range: lettuce, herbs, microgreens, oyster mushrooms. Avoid temperamental fruiting crops.
- Plan crop rotation: cool-season crops (lettuce, spinach) in winter/spring; heat-tolerant herbs in summer.

### Off-Grid & Low-Power Scenarios

**Challenges:** Pump failure causes crop loss; consistent power unavailable.

**Adaptations:**
- Use Deep Water Culture (DWC) manual aeration instead of electric pumps. Hand pump operated 2-3 times daily sustains lettuce (slower growth, but viable).
- Use Kratky method (passive hydroponics): no pump, no electricity required. Nutrient solution remains static; works 4-6 weeks for fast crops (lettuce, microgreens).
- Choose crops with 30-45 day cycle (lettuce, microgreens) over slow ones (tomatoes, peppers). Reduces total electricity need and extends before pump failure becomes critical.
- Collect rainwater (filtered) for water changes if tap water unavailable. Reduces dependency on treated water.
- Solar-powered LED lights (with 12V battery backup) provide 8-10 hours growth light per day. Adequate for lettuce; marginal for fruiting crops.

:::info-box
Kratky hydroponics requires zero electricity: plants float on styrofoam, roots suspended in static nutrient solution. A 5-gallon bucket system costs under $30, produces 4-6 heads of lettuce in 35 days. Perfect for disaster scenarios with no power access.
:::
