:::danger
Young children do not recognize danger the way adults do. A child can climb, touch, taste, or wander into trouble faster than a caregiver can react. On a homestead, the main job is not to explain danger after the fact. It is to arrange the home so that common hazards are hard to reach, easy to notice, and simple to supervise.
:::

<section id="scope">

## Scope

### My Child Keeps Getting Into Things

This guide covers the practical hazards that most often injure children in homes, cabins, yards, barns, gardens, and shared family spaces. It focuses on childproofing a homestead, not on formal law, school policy, or child protection reporting systems.

Use it when you need answers to questions like:

- How do I keep a toddler away from a stove, fire, well, or pond?
- Where should tools, poisons, and chemicals be stored?
- What home rules should children learn at different ages?
- What daily checks prevent the most common accidents?

If a child has already been exposed to fire, poison, drowning, electricity, or a serious fall, treat it as an emergency and use the related hazard guides.

## The Main Principle

Children are safest when three things are true:

1. The hazard is blocked or moved out of reach.
2. An adult can see or hear the child quickly.
3. The child has simple rules that match their age and ability.

If any one of those three fails, the risk rises sharply. If two fail, the home is not safely childproofed.

<section id="highest-risk-hazards">

## Highest-Risk Hazards

These are the hazards that deserve the first round of changes in any homestead with children.

### 1. Fire and Stove Hazards

Fire is one of the fastest ways a child can be badly hurt. Hot stoves, open flames, embers, matches, candles, lanterns, and ash buckets all need special attention.

**What to do:**

- Keep children out of the cooking zone unless an adult is actively teaching them.
- Mark a no-cross line around the stove or fire area.
- Keep handles turned inward on pots and kettles.
- Use a stable cooking surface with no loose stools or stepping blocks nearby.
- Store matches, lighters, fire starters, ash, and fuel where small hands cannot reach them.
- Never leave a child alone near a lit stove, ember bucket, candle, or fireplace.

**Red flags:**

- A child carries sticks, paper, cloth, or fuel near a fire.
- Ash is stored in an open bucket on the floor.
- Pot handles stick out where a child can grab them.
- Children play “cooking” with real heat, knives, or fire tools.

For indoor stove and chimney safety, see <a href="../cookstoves-indoor-heating-safety.html">Cookstoves, Indoor Heating & Safety</a> and <a href="../fire-safety-compartmentalization.html">Fire Safety and Compartmentalization</a>.

### 2. Water and Drowning Hazards

Wells, ponds, barrels, troughs, cisterns, buckets, rain tanks, and floodwater all create drowning risk. Children can drown in surprisingly shallow water if they fall face-down and cannot get up.

**What to do:**

- Cover wells and tanks securely.
- Empty buckets and tubs after use.
- Fence or block access to ponds, ditches, and steep banks when possible.
- Teach children that water is only for adults or supervised play.
- Keep bath time and water carrying under close watch.
- Never treat floodwater as a play area.

**Red flags:**

- Children are found near water without an adult nearby.
- A child knows how to open tank lids or climb fences.
- A bucket, trough, or tub is left outside where toddlers roam.

For rescue and post-rescue steps, see <a href="../drowning-prevention-water-safety.html">Drowning Prevention & Water Safety</a>.

### 3. Tools, Blades, and Work Areas

Tools are normal on a homestead, but children should not have free access to them. Axes, saws, knives, pruning shears, chisels, nails, wire, and power tools can injure in seconds.

**What to do:**

- Store sharp tools high, locked, or in a designated adult-only area.
- Return every tool to the same place after use.
- Keep a clean floor so children do not step on nails, screws, or wire.
- Give children only age-appropriate tools under direct supervision.
- Do not leave blades in grass, on benches, in sink water, or in work pockets.

**Red flags:**

- Children are “helping” near sharp tools without a clear boundary.
- A tool shed or workbench can be entered freely by small children.
- Nails, wire, glass, or scrap metal are left on the ground.

### 4. Poisons, Chemicals, and Medicines

Many homestead supplies look like food, drink, or harmless household items to a child. Lye, bleach, fuel, solvents, pesticide, medicines, alcohol, and cleaning mixtures must be treated as poison by default.

**For detailed storage rules, chemical compatibility, and where to keep specific cleaners and reagents, see <a href="../chemical-safety.html">Chemical Safety</a> (section 6). This guide covers the childproofing layer — keeping hazards out of reach — not chemical storage specifics.**

**What to do:**

- Keep all chemicals in original containers when possible.
- Label everything clearly.
- Store poison, fuel, and medicine separately from food.
- Never pour chemicals into food jars or drink bottles.
- Keep cleaning solutions out of reach after every use.
- Count medicine doses so children cannot take extra by mistake.

**Red flags:**

- A child has access to unlabeled bottles or jars.
- Medicine is kept in a kitchen drawer or on a low shelf.
- Fuel, bleach, or lye is stored beside flour, sugar, or cooking oil.

### If a child is exposed right now

If a child may have swallowed medicine, bleach, lye, fuel, solvent, or an unknown substance:

1. Take the substance away from the child.
2. Keep the container or label so you know what it was.
3. Do **not** force vomiting.
4. If there is chemical on the skin, remove contaminated clothing and rinse the skin with plenty of water.
5. If it got in the eyes, flush with clean water right away and keep flushing while help is arranged.
6. If the child is sleepy, confused, coughing, drooling, vomiting repeatedly, or having trouble breathing, treat it as an emergency now.
7. Call poison-control or emergency help if available.

**For active ingestion triage — "my child swallowed cleaner," "toddler drank something under the sink," "child got into drain cleaner" — use <a href="../unknown-ingestion-child-poisoning-triage.html">Unknown Ingestion &amp; Child Accidental Poisoning Triage</a> immediately. This section covers the prevention layer only.**

Fuel, solvent, and caustic products such as lye or strong cleaners are not "wait and see" exposures.

For broader hazard handling, see <a href="../chemical-safety.html">Chemical Safety</a>.

### 5. Animals and Livestock

Animals can bite, kick, scratch, trample, or spread disease. Even friendly animals become dangerous if startled, guarding young, eating, or confined.

**What to do:**

- Teach children not to run into pens, coops, stalls, or animal feeding areas.
- Keep children out of the path of moving livestock.
- Never allow children to tease, poke, or ride animals without a capable adult.
- Separate children from aggressive, protective, or sick animals.
- Give children simple rules for dogs, cats, birds, goats, chickens, pigs, and larger stock.

**Red flags:**

- A child is left alone with a large animal.
- Animals crowd feeding areas where children play.
- Children sleep or play where animals enter freely.

For companion animals specifically, see <a href="../companion-animal-care.html">Companion Animal Care</a>.

### 6. Climbing and Fall Risks

Fences, ladders, trees, haylofts, loft beds, porches, roofs, stacked wood, rocks, and machinery become injury points when children climb without limits.

**What to do:**

- Make “no climbing without an adult” a household rule for ladders, roofs, and high stacks.
- Keep furniture away from windows and ledges.
- Use guard rails on loft beds and raised sleeping platforms.
- Store heavy items low so children cannot pull them down.
- Keep play away from piles that can collapse.

**Red flags:**

- Children use stools or boxes to reach shelves.
- A child climbs to “help” with chores in high places.
- Hay, lumber, or firewood is stacked where children play.

### 7. Sleep-Space Safety

Children are vulnerable when they sleep because they cannot react quickly to fire, smoke, heat, entrapment, or wandering animals.

**What to do:**

- Keep infant and toddler sleep spaces clear of cords, loose blankets, small objects, and soft pile-ups that can block breathing.
- Keep candles, lanterns, and heaters away from beds and bedding.
- Make sure children can get out easily if there is fire or smoke.
- Avoid sleep spaces near open water, tool storage, or chemical storage.
- Check that older children do not crowd the bed with unsafe items.

**Red flags:**

- Loose cords or ropes hang near a child’s sleeping area.
- A baby sleeps in a place where an adult cannot see them quickly.
- Heat sources are too close to bedding.

### 8. Bathroom and Diaper-Change Safety

Bathroom routines and diaper-changing routines can create their own hazards for small children.

**What to do:**

- Keep toilet lids, potty chairs, diaper pails, cleaners, and wipes where children cannot open or spill them easily.
- Use non-slip footing near tubs, buckets, and wet floors.
- Never leave a toddler alone near a toilet, tub, bucket, or wash basin.
- Keep diapering supplies close so you do not have to leave a baby unattended.
- Keep medicines, wipes, soaps, and cleaning fluids away from where a child can grab them during diaper changes.

**Red flags:**

- A child can reach toilet cleaner, bleach, or trash from the bathroom floor.
- A diaper-change area has sharp corners, slippery floors, or loose cords.
- A toddler is left alone while the caregiver is fetching wipes, clothes, or soap.

## Daily Quick Checks

Run this short check every morning and again before bed:

1. Are matches, knives, medicines, fuel, and chemicals put away?
2. Are stoves, fires, heaters, and candles supervised or extinguished?
3. Are water containers covered and doors/gates closed?
4. Are tools, nails, wire, and scrap metal off the floor?
5. Are animals contained or supervised?
6. Are climbing hazards, ladders, and stacked materials secured?
7. Is the child’s sleep space clear of cords, heat, and small objects?
8. Do all caregivers know where each child is supposed to be?

If one of these checks fails, fix it before moving on to work.

## Supervision Systems That Work

Good supervision is not constant panic. It is a system.

### Assign Zones

Every child should have a normal play zone and a restricted zone. Adults should know which places require direct watching: kitchens, wells, fuel storage, animal pens, tool sheds, roads, and work areas.

For how to shape the play zone itself, see <a href="../playground-child-play-area-safety.html">Playground & Child Play-Area Safety</a>.

### Use Named Adults

Children should know which adult is responsible at a given time. Do not rely on “someone is watching them.” Use clear handoffs like:

- “I am on water watch.”
- “You are on stove watch.”
- “After lunch, Mara takes the yard and pond area.”

### Keep Sight and Sound Rules

Younger children should be within sight or easy hearing distance most of the time. If a child is too quiet, too far away, or too close to a hazard, check immediately.

### Build a Check-In Habit

Have children check in:

- when they go outside
- when they return inside
- before climbing
- before going near water
- before helping with tools

This is especially important when several adults are busy with chores.

## Age-Appropriate Home Rules

Children can follow safety rules when the rules are short, repeated, and consistent.

### Toddlers and Preschoolers

- Stay with an adult near water, fire, animals, and tools.
- Do not touch matches, knives, medicine, or chemicals.
- Do not climb furniture, fences, or ladders alone.
- Ask before going outside or leaving the room.

### Early School Age

- Do not enter the kitchen when an adult says the stove area is closed.
- Do not pick up unknown bottles, packets, or powders.
- Do not feed animals unless an adult says yes.
- Do not climb higher than your body length without permission.
- Tell an adult right away if you find glass, wire, pills, or dead animals.

### Older Children and Teens

- Learn where the hazards are and keep younger children away from them.
- Use tools only after training.
- Close gates, covers, lids, and latches after use.
- Report broken railings, loose boards, exposed wires, and bad storage immediately.
- Do not supervise younger children near water, fire, ladders, roofs, tools, or animals unless an adult has clearly handed off that job and is still close enough to take over.

### Practical autonomy rule

Do not decide by age alone. Decide by:

- whether the child follows rules every time, not just sometimes
- whether they can explain the hazard back to you
- whether an adult can see or hear them quickly
- whether the task can hurt them badly in one mistake

If a task involves open water, fire, knives, ladders, chemicals, livestock, engines, or electricity, use direct adult supervision until the child has both the skill and the judgment to stop when something changes.
- Help with supervision, but do not replace adult responsibility.

## When To Escalate Right Away

Treat these as urgent:

- A child was near open water and cannot be accounted for.
- A child touched poison, fuel, lye, medicine, or unknown chemicals.
- A child may have swallowed a chemical, fuel, or medicine, even if they still look normal.
- A child was burned, shocked, or trapped.
- A child fell from height and is confused, vomiting, or hard to wake.
- A child was bitten by an animal and the wound is deep or bleeding heavily.
- A child has smoke exposure, trouble breathing, or fainting after a fire or heater incident.

If you are unsure, act early. Children can worsen quickly after a hazard exposure.

</section>

<section id="a-practical-childproofing-order">

## A Practical Childproofing Order

If you can only fix a few things today, do them in this order:

1. Move poisons, medicines, fuel, and sharp tools out of reach.
2. Block access to water, fire, and stove areas.
3. Fix sleep-space hazards and clear cords, heaters, and loose objects.
4. Secure animal areas and keep children out of feed and handling zones.
5. Remove climbing opportunities near windows, roofs, and stored materials.
6. Write down the home rules and make sure every caregiver uses the same ones.

</section>

## Final Rule

If a rule is too complicated for a child to remember, it is too complicated for the home to rely on. Better child safety comes from simpler spaces, clearer routines, and fewer reachable hazards.
