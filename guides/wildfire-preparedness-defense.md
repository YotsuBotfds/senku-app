**Wildfire Danger:** Wildfires are unpredictable and move quickly. Personal safety always takes priority over property. Evacuation orders must be obeyed. This guide provides community-scale preparation; it does not overcome the fundamental danger of high-intensity wildfires.
:::

<section id="overview">

## Why Wildfire Preparedness Matters

Wildfires pose catastrophic threat to communities. Unlike hurricanes or earthquakes (which strike briefly then pass), wildfires can burn for weeks, threatening multiple communities sequentially.

**Wildfire impact factors:**
1. **Community location** (proximity to wildland-urban interface)
2. **Fuel availability** (dry vegetation, buildings provide fuel)
3. **Preparedness** (defensible spaces, evacuation plans)
4. **Response coordination** (community defense, firefighting capability)

In austere conditions without professional firefighting infrastructure:
- Wildfires move unchecked
- Communities must self-defend or evacuate
- Preparation = difference between survival and total loss

This guide covers **community-scale preparation and defense** strategies.

<section id="fire-behavior">

## Fire Behavior & Community Threat Assessment

<div class="fire-basics">

### Fire Triangle & Suppression Principles

**Fire requires three elements (fire triangle):**
1. **Fuel** (wood, vegetation, structures)
2. **Oxygen** (air)
3. **Heat** (ignition source; sustained by burning fuel)

**Suppression removes one element:**
- **Remove fuel:** Clear dead wood, thin forests, mow vegetation
- **Remove oxygen:** Smother (water, retardant), suffocation
- **Remove heat:** Cool with water, reduce temperature below ignition point

**Community implication:** You cannot eliminate oxygen. Focus on fuel reduction + heat management (water).

### Fire Spread Factors

| Factor | Effect on Spread | Community Control |
|---|---|---|
| **Wind direction** | Pushes flames forward | Evacuate upwind; prepare firebreaks downwind |
| **Slope** | Fire travels faster uphill | Evacuate uphill communities early |
| **Fuel moisture** | Wet fuel slows fire; dry fuel accelerates | Clear dead vegetation; maintain green buffer around community |
| **Vegetation type** | Dense brush burns faster; hardwoods slower | Clear underbrush; promote low-density spacing |
| **Structural density** | Close buildings burn simultaneously (firestorm) | Space buildings; remove adjacent fuel |

### Community Threat Zones

**Zone 1 (Immediate threat):** Wildfire within 5 miles, moving toward community
- Action: Begin evacuation preparations
- Coordinate with firefighting resources (if available)
- Pre-position water supplies for structure protection

**Zone 2 (High threat):** Wildfire within 10 miles, conditions may change
- Action: Ready evacuation equipment/supplies
- Alert population to prepare for evacuation
- Establish perimeter patrols (watch for spot fires—embers ahead of main flame)

**Zone 3 (Distant threat):** Wildfire >10 miles away, moving away
- Action: Continue normal operations; maintain alert
- Use time to clear fuel, harden structures
- Plan exercises

<section id="defensible-space">

## Defensible Space: Structure-Level Preparation

**Defensible space** = cleared area around buildings reducing fuel available to fire.

### Three Zones of Defensible Space

<div class="defensible-space">

**Zone 0: Immediate (0–5 ft from structure)**
- **Goal:** Remove all dead vegetation; eliminate mulch/wood debris
- **Actions:**
  - Remove dead leaves, needles from gutters + roof
  - Clear dead plants/shrubs within 5 ft
  - Thin tree branches overhanging roof (cut to 10+ ft above roof)
  - Remove vines growing on structure
  - Eliminate wood piles (move >30 ft away, if keeping)
  - Remove decorative wood chips (replace with rock/gravel)
- **Timeline:** Spring + fall (seasonal maintenance)

**Zone 1: Intermediate (5–30 ft from structure)**
- **Goal:** Reduce fuel density; increase spacing between plants
- **Actions:**
  - Thin trees: Space canopies 10+ ft apart (light should filter through)
  - Remove lower tree limbs (prune up to 6–8 ft height)
  - Remove shrubs between trees (reduces fuel continuity)
  - Remove dead trees, standing or fallen
  - Clear grass/ground vegetation (mow to <4 inches)
  - Space remaining shrubs 2× their height (e.g., 4 ft shrub → 8 ft spacing)
- **Timeline:** Annual (summer before fire season)

**Zone 2: Extended (30–100+ ft from structure)**
- **Goal:** Create thinned forest; break up fuel continuity
- **Actions:**
  - Thin trees (every other tree removal typical; creates gaps)
  - Remove ladder fuels (shrubs connecting ground to tree canopy)
  - Clear fallen wood (remove if fire fuel; leave if wildlife habitat)
  - Widen roads/pathways (for evacuation, fire truck access)
- **Timeline:** Multi-year project; prioritize based on fuel density

### Structural Hardening

**Roof:**
- **Material:** Class A fire-rated (metal, composition shingle, tile)
- **Avoid:** Wood shingles, cedar, untreated wood
- **Maintenance:** Clear debris annually; repair holes immediately
- **Cost:** Moderate (replacement roof ~$10–20k for house)

**Siding:**
- **Material:** Fire-resistant (brick, stucco, metal, fiber-cement)
- **Avoid:** Wood or vinyl siding (both burn easily)
- **Gaps:** Seal between siding + foundation (prevent ember intrusion)
- **Cost:** High (full replacement); moderate (new additions/repairs)

**Windows & Doors:**
- **Windows:** Dual-pane tempered glass resists radiant heat better than single-pane
- **Shutters:** Metal (not wood) exterior shutters block ember entry
- **Doors:** Metal or solid core wood doors; metal preferred
- **Seals:** Weather stripping around doors (prevents flame intrusion)

**Gutters & Vents:**
- **Gutters:** Install 1/8-inch mesh screens (exclude large debris while allowing drainage)
- **Roof vents:** Install spark arrestors (1/8-inch mesh) or metal vents (no gaps)
- **Attic ventilation:** Balance fire safety with cooling (discuss with builder/fire professional)

**Defensible space economics:** For modest home:
- Zone 0 clearance: $500–$1500 (professional crew; less if DIY)
- Zone 1 thinning: $1000–$3000
- Zone 2 forest thinning: $5000–$20,000+ (large acreage)

**Community approach:** Organize group projects (volunteers clear each property sequentially) to reduce costs.

## Firebreak Design & Community Implementation

**Firebreak** = cleared line (no vegetation) intended to slow/stop wildfire spread.

### Firebreak Effectiveness

Firebreaks work best when:
1. **Wide enough:** 100+ ft stops most fires (requires significant labor)
2. **Fuel-free:** Completely cleared vegetation + dead wood
3. **Maintained:** Vegetation regrows; must clear annually
4. **Strategic location:** Place between wildfire source + community

**Limitations:**
- Very intense fires (high winds, extreme heat) can jump even wide firebreaks
- Embers blown ahead of fire can start new fires beyond firebreak
- Requires ongoing maintenance (labor-intensive)

### Firebreak Construction

<div class="firebreak-design">

**Location selection:**
- Map prevailing wind direction (fire typically comes from specific direction)
- Identify terrain between community and likely fire source (hilltops are good barriers)
- Choose natural features to augment (rivers, rocky areas, cleared roads)

**Community-scale firebreak (50–100 ft wide):**

**Phase 1: Vegetation removal**
- Clear all trees, shrubs, grass >4 inches height
- Remove dead standing trees (snags)
- Remove fallen wood (chip if available; remove if not)
- Goal: Bare soil visible for full width

**Phase 2: Maintenance**
- Spring: Clear winter-blown debris
- Summer (pre-fire season): Mow any grass regrowth to <2 inches
- Fall: Clear autumn leaves/needles

**Labor requirement:**
- 100-ft line × 50-ft wide × removal depth 2 ft = ~12,000 cubic ft material
- Professional crew (5 people): ~1 week
- Volunteer crew (20 people): ~2–3 days
- Annual maintenance: Volunteer crews, 1 day/season

**Tools needed:**
- Chainsaws + fuel
- Brush cutters (for dense undergrowth)
- Shovels
- Axes (for logs)
- Trucks (for hauling)

</div>

### Alley/Road Widening

Roads can serve as firebreaks if cleared properly.

**Community road preparation:**
- Width: 40+ ft between tree canopies (allows fire trucks, reduces ember spread)
- Canopy clearance: Trim tree limbs up to 12 ft height (allows truck passage; reduces fuel above)
- Roadside vegetation: Mow/clear within 15 ft of roadway edge
- Annual maintenance: Spring & summer

<section id="evacuation-plan">

## Community Evacuation Planning & Execution

<div class="evacuation-plan">

### Evacuation Decision-Making

**Alert Levels:**

| Level | Wildfire Status | Community Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Advisory** | Wildfire detected 20+ miles away, moving away | Prepare to evacuate; update emergency kit |
| **Watch** | Wildfire 10–20 miles away, moving toward community; conditions may change | Ready evacuation vehicles; monitor weather/fire updates |
| **Warning** | Wildfire <10 miles away, moving toward community; evacuation expected | Begin staged evacuation (elderly, disabled first) |
| **Evacuation Order** | Wildfire <5 miles, imminent threat to life/property | Mandatory evacuation; leave immediately |

### Evacuation Routes & Capacity

**Pre-event planning:**
- Identify 2–3 primary evacuation routes (different directions if available)
- Identify secondary routes (if primary blocked)
- Calculate vehicles needed:
  - Community of 100: ~25–30 vehicles (assume 3–4 people/vehicle)
  - Community of 1000: ~250–300 vehicles
  - Communities rarely have enough private vehicles for full population
- **Solution:** Organize community evacuation centers (gathering points) + shared transport

**Evacuation execution:**
- Announce evacuation (radio, door-to-door, emergency signal)
- Establish staging areas (assembly points per neighborhood)
- Coordinate transportation (drivers move people in shifts if necessary)
- Establish safe destination (town 50+ miles away with community shelter space)
- Account for all persons (check-in at destination; reunite separated families)

### Personal Preparation

**Each household should have "go-bag" ready (during fire season):**
- Important documents (IDs, property deeds, insurance info)
- Medications (week supply minimum)
- Water + food (72-hour emergency supply)
- Clothing + sturdy shoes
- Cash (ATMs may not function after disaster)
- Photos/irreplaceable items (if room; property can be replaced)

**Vehicle preparation:**
- Full fuel tank (during fire season; especially if evacuation imminent)
- Vehicle in good repair (evacuation is no time to discover mechanical problems)
- Plan for elderly/disabled household members (accessible transportation)

### Sheltering Evacuees

**If evacuation brief (24–48 hours):**
- Community members can stay with distant family/friends
- Host community may allow tents in parks, parking lots

**If evacuation extended (days to weeks):**
- Set up evacuation shelters:
  - Schools, gymnasiums, community centers
  - Capacity: ~50 sq ft/person minimum
  - Supplies: Cots/bedding, food, water, hygiene facilities, medical aid
- Organize daily activities (reduces anxiety, maintains morale)
- Reunite families; establish communication with community back home

## Community Fire Suppression & Defense

In austere conditions without professional firefighting, community-organized defense may be necessary.

<div class="community-defense">

### Water Source Development

**Critical for any defense attempt:**
- Identify water sources (ponds, lakes, wells, streams)
- Ensure adequate volume for sustained drawing
- Access routes: Roads allowing trucks/equipment to reach water source
- Pumping capability: Hand-pump (if low-tech) or electric/gas pump (if fuel available)

**Water storage (pre-positioned):**
- Tanks or pools filled before fire season (~1000 gallons minimum per neighborhood)
- Positioned at elevation (gravity flow to fire zones)
- Regularly maintained (remove algae, prevent mosquito breeding)

### Equipment & Supply Positioning

**Per 100-person community:**
- **Pumps:** 1–2 (hand or powered)
- **Hoses:** 500–1000 feet (1–2 inch diameter)
- **Nozzles:** 2–3 (various spray patterns)
- **Shovels:** 10–15
- **Rakes:** 10–15
- **Buckets:** 20–30
- **Protective gear:** Gloves, goggles, fire-resistant clothing (if available)
- **First aid supplies:** Burns are common; stock extra bandages, pain medication

**Storage:** Centralized location, accessible to defense team

### Defense Team Organization & Training

**Selection & roles:**
- **Team leader:** Coordinates overall strategy; communicates with evacuation command
- **Water team:** Operates pumps + hoses
- **Clearing team:** Removes immediate fuel, creates defensive perimeter
- **Safety officer:** Watches for spot fires, changing wind, team fatigue
- **Medical:** Treats burns, smoke inhalation, heat exhaustion

**Size:** 10–20 people per 100-person community (larger communities = multiple teams)

**Training:**
- How to operate pumps (practice annually)
- Basic hose operation (water pressure hazards)
- Fuel clearing safety (chainsaw operation, not relevant for untrained)
- Fire behavior (understand spread patterns)
- Team communication (clear signals, radio if available)

### Defense Strategy

**IF wildfire approaching community:**

**Phase 1: Evacuation + Strategic withdrawal (Days 1–2)**
- Evacuate non-essential population (elderly, children, medical patients)
- Defense team remains; prepares structures + positions water sources
- Clear immediate fuel zones (Zone 0 around structures)
- Identify fallback position (secondary defense line if first overrun)

**Phase 2: Active defense (During fire arrival)**
- Position personnel at key structures
- Wet structures + immediate surroundings as fire approaches
- Clear spot fires (embers blowing ahead of main flame)
- Maintain hydration + safety (smoke inhalation hazard; heat exhaustion risk)

**Phase 3: Retreat if necessary (If overwhelmed)**
- Do NOT fight fire if it's too intense (recognize limits)
- Evacuate remaining personnel immediately (lives more important than property)
- Fall back to predetermined safe zone
- Regroup at designated meeting point

**Critical rule:** If fire intensity exceeds defensive capability, evacuate. No property is worth lives.

<section id="post-fire">

## Post-Fire Community Recovery

### Immediate Aftermath (Hours–Days)

**First tasks:**
- Account for all community members (reunite families)
- Assess injuries (deploy medical response if needed)
- Identify safe water source (verify not contaminated)
- Establish temporary shelter if homes damaged
- Begin information gathering (scope of damage)

### Debris Management

**Burned wood/ash:**
- Hazardous if ash still hot (can reignite)
- Cannot be used for construction (weakened by heat)
- May be chipped/composted (if labor available) or hauled to disposal site
- Volume overwhelming; plan extended cleanup

### Rebuilding Phase

**Inspections:**
- Structures that appear intact may have internal damage
- Hazmat concern: Burned materials may contain toxins
- Professional inspection (if available) guides repair decisions

**Reconstruction priorities:**
- Shelter first (houses, communal buildings)
- Water/sanitation
- Food production/supply
- Infrastructure (roads, power if applicable)

### Lessons Learned / Re-preparation

**Community debrief (weeks after fire):**
- What worked in evacuation planning?
- What bottlenecks occurred?
- How to improve defensible space?
- How to improve water positioning?
- Training gaps for defense team?

**Update plans based on experience:**
- Revise evacuation procedures
- Expand defensible space in vulnerable areas
- Increase water storage capacity
- Training schedule for next season

<section id="checklist">

## Checklist: Wildfire Preparedness & Community Defense

**Pre-Fire Season (Spring):**
- [ ] Assess community location risk (proximity to wildland)
- [ ] Map evacuation routes (2+ options)
- [ ] Identify water sources + verify accessibility
- [ ] Check pumps, hoses for functionality
- [ ] Perform firebreak maintenance (clear regrowth)
- [ ] Train defense team (annual drill)
- [ ] Distribute evacuation plan to community
- [ ] Verify emergency alert system working (radio, runners, etc.)

**Fire Season Preparation (Summer):**
- [ ] Daily fire outlook (any fires in region?)
- [ ] Maintain alert levels (Advisory → Watch → Warning → Evacuation)
- [ ] Inspect defensible spaces (structures, community areas)
- [ ] Stock evacuation shelters (if fire >1 week away)
- [ ] Fuel generators, vehicles (ready for rapid evacuation)
- [ ] Verify evacuation destination is available/prepared

**During Evacuation:**
- [ ] Announce evacuation level + routes
- [ ] Begin staged evacuation (vulnerable first)
- [ ] Deploy defense team (if choosing to defend)
- [ ] Account for all persons at destination
- [ ] Establish daily communication with evacuated community

**Post-Fire Recovery:**
- [ ] Account for injuries/deaths; establish care
- [ ] Assess damage scope
- [ ] Begin debris management + rebuilding planning
- [ ] Debrief community on what worked/didn't
- [ ] Update preparedness plan for next season
