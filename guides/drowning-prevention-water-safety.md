Drowning can happen fast and quietly. A person can be in severe trouble with almost no splashing or shouting, especially children, tired swimmers, cold-water victims, and anyone wearing heavy clothes or gear. Prevention is the main goal of this guide. If a rescue is needed, use the safest option first: call for help, reach, throw, or use a boat if available. Do not become a second victim.

## Scope

This guide is for day-to-day water safety around rivers, lakes, wells, ponds, canals, floodwater, and small boats. It is written to help prevent drowning, spot danger early, and respond without taking unnecessary risks.

It does not replace local rescue training, lifeguarding, or emergency care. If someone is unresponsive or not breathing normally, treat it as an emergency right away.

## The Main Rules

1. Stay out of water you do not need to enter.
2. Supervise children and weak swimmers closely, within arm's reach when they are near water.
3. Never rescue by jumping in unless you are trained and it is the only way to save a life.
4. Cold water is dangerous even when the surface looks calm.
5. Floodwater is not safe water.
6. A person who is rescued may still need urgent medical care even if they seem awake.

## Where Drowning Risk Is Highest

### Rivers and Streams

Fast current, hidden drop-offs, slippery banks, and debris make rivers dangerous even for strong swimmers.

Watch for:
- Brown or turbulent water after rain
- Branches, logs, or wire in the current
- Steep banks with no easy exit
- Water that looks shallow but moves fast

### Lakes and Ponds

Lakes can have sudden depth changes, cold pockets, weeds that entangle legs, and no easy way out from the shore.

Watch for:
- Muddy bottoms that trap feet
- Weed beds and lily pads
- Steep or slippery shorelines
- No nearby adult supervision

### Wells, Cisterns, and Tanks

Open wells and storage tanks are a fall-and-drown hazard, especially for children and animals.

Prevention:
- Cover openings securely
- Fence or mark dangerous edges
- Keep buckets, ropes, and tools from creating trip hazards
- Never let children play near open water containers

### Floodwater

Floodwater can hide holes, sharp debris, open manholes, live electrical hazards, and strong currents.

Rules:
- Do not wade if you can avoid it
- Do not drive through floodwater
- Assume floodwater is contaminated
- Stay away from moving water, even if it looks shallow

## Supervision and Child Safety

Children need close adult supervision near any water, even buckets, tubs, ditches, and shallow ponds.

Good habits:
- Assign one adult to watch children at all times near water
- Count heads before and after swimming or boating
- Keep infants and toddlers within arm's reach
- Empty buckets, tubs, and kiddie pools after use
- Fence wells, tanks, and ponds where possible

Do not assume older children can watch younger ones safely. A child who is confident in water may still miss a smaller child slipping under.

## Safe Swimming Basics

Swim only where the depth, current, and exit route are understood.

Before anyone enters the water:
- Check the weather and the water temperature
- Look for current, weeds, submerged objects, and drop-offs
- Decide how everyone will get out
- Keep a helper on shore

Safer swimming choices:
- Use supervised areas when available
- Swim with another person, not alone
- Avoid alcohol and drugs before swimming or boating
- Use life jackets for weak swimmers and children around open water

## Cold Water Risk

Cold water can cause trouble before a person even gets tired. The first danger is often a gasp or loss of breathing control, not hypothermia alone.

Cold-water warning signs:
- Sudden gasping or panic
- Can no longer coordinate breathing and swimming
- Numb hands or weak grip
- Confusion or poor judgment

Extra caution:
- Fall-in risk is higher in cold weather and near spring runoff
- Heavy clothing and boots can drag a person down
- A person pulled from cold water may seem to recover and still crash later

## Boating Basics

If you use boats, canoes, or rafts:
- Wear life jackets where possible
- Do not overload the craft
- Keep weight low and balanced
- Stay seated unless the craft is designed for standing
- Carry a bailer, paddle, and basic repair gear if practical
- Avoid boating alone in rough water or at night

Treat small boats as fragile, not as rescue platforms for excitement or standing.

## Rescue Priorities

### 1. Call for help

If someone is in serious trouble, shout for help and call emergency services if available.

### 2. Reach

Use a pole, branch, rope, strap, towel, or paddle to reach the person from shore, pier, or boat.

### 3. Throw

Throw a flotation aid or anything that floats and can be held onto.

### 4. Row or paddle

If a boat is the safest available option, go carefully and keep the craft stable.

### 5. Go only if trained and necessary

Entering the water is the last choice. A panicked person may grab and pull a rescuer underwater.

If you must enter the water:
- Use a flotation aid if possible
- Approach from behind if trained to do so
- Keep your own exit route in mind
- Do not attempt a heroic rescue that creates a second casualty

## Post-Rescue Immediate Steps

After the person is out of the water:

1. Check responsiveness and breathing.
2. Call for emergency help if available.
3. If they are not breathing normally, start CPR right away. If you are trained and able, use rescue breaths with chest compressions because drowning is primarily a breathing and oxygen problem. If you are untrained or unable to give breaths, give chest compressions until help arrives.
4. Remove wet clothing if safe and keep the person warm.
5. Watch closely for vomiting, confusion, worsening cough, chest pain, or trouble breathing.
6. Do not let the person "sleep it off" without observation if the rescue was significant.

Important:
- A person who inhaled or aspirated water can worsen later
- Cold-water rescue can look deceptively mild at first
- Children should be watched especially closely after any immersion event

## Red Flags

Get urgent medical help if the rescued person has any of the following:
- Not breathing normally
- Blue, gray, or very pale skin
- Persistent cough or foam at the mouth
- Chest pain
- Confusion, fainting, or severe sleepiness
- Seizure
- Repeated vomiting
- Worsening shortness of breath
- Severe cold exposure or uncontrolled shivering

If the person lost consciousness at any point, assume the event was serious.

## Practical Prevention Checklist

- Keep children supervised near all water
- Fence or cover wells, tanks, and other deep openings
- Avoid swimming alone
- Do not enter floodwater unless absolutely necessary
- Use life jackets for weak swimmers and small boats when possible
- Learn local hazards before using a new river, lake, or canal
- Keep rescue tools near shore: rope, pole, flotation aid
- Do not mix swimming or boating with alcohol, fatigue, or poor visibility

## When To Treat It As An Emergency

Treat the situation as an emergency if:
- A person is missing near water
- Someone is face down, silent, or motionless in water
- A child disappears from sight near a pond, pool, tank, or ditch
- A boater is not responding
- The person has breathing trouble after rescue
- The person was in cold water long enough to seem weak, confused, or disoriented

## Short Version

If someone is at risk of drowning:
- Warn others and call for help
- Reach or throw first
- Do not jump in unless you must
- Watch children closely around any water
- Treat floodwater and cold water as serious hazards
- After rescue, keep observing for breathing problems and red flags
