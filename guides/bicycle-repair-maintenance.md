## 1. Why Bicycle Maintenance Matters

Bicycle maintenance is the most cost-effective and skill-essential aspect of bicycle ownership. A well-maintained bicycle lasts decades; a neglected one becomes unusable within months. In low-resource or post-collapse scenarios, the ability to repair a bicycle becomes as important as riding one.

### The Economics of Maintenance vs. Replacement

A single new bicycle costs $200-1000+. A complete repair kit costs $20-50. Routine maintenance adds 5-10 years to a bicycle's working life. Learning to repair your bicycle means never being stranded due to preventable failures.

### Common Failure Modes and Prevention

Most bicycle failures fall into three categories:

- **Pneumatic failures:** Flat tires, failed tubes, valve leaks — preventable through pressure monitoring and puncture resistance
- **Drivetrain failures:** Rusty chains, worn cogs, loose bolts — preventable through cleaning and lubrication
- **Steering/braking failures:** Loose brakes, bent rims, worn pads — preventable through adjustment and inspection

Regular inspection catches these before catastrophic failure. A 10-minute check per week can prevent 90% of roadside emergencies.

## 2. Essential Tools & Materials

### Minimum Tool Kit for Field Repair

A complete roadside repair kit costs under $30 and fits in a small pouch:

- **Tire levers (3x):** Plastic or metal; essential for removing tires from rims
- **Patch kit:** Glueless or traditional patches; carry 10-15 patches per kit
- **Pump:** Frame-mounted or hand pump (12-inch minimum); floor pumps stay at home
- **Multi-tool with Allen wrenches:** 3mm, 4mm, 5mm, 6mm covers 95% of adjustments
- **Spoke wrench:** Single tool; weighs <1 ounce
- **Chain tool/breaker:** Drives out pins to split chains; <2 ounces
- **Spare tube:** One full-size tube plus patch kit provides redundancy
- **Rag or cloth:** For cleaning and drying
- **Lubricant:** Thin oil in small bottle; do not use grease (attracts dirt)

### Home Workshop Tools

For serious maintenance:

- **Floor pump with gauge:** Ensures accurate pressure
- **Cone wrenches (17mm, 15mm, 14mm):** For bearing repacking
- **Cable cutters:** Clean cuts prevent fraying
- **Screwdrivers (Phillips, flat-head):** Various sizes for brake adjustment
- **Grease cup:** For bearing repacking
- **Waterstone or file:** For smoothing sharp edges
- **Vise or work stand:** Holds bike during repair
- **Chain cleaner or brush:** Removes embedded dirt

:::warning
**Tool Safety:** Never use tools beyond their designed purpose. A wrench slipping under load can cause hand injury. Always pull toward yourself rather than pushing. Wear eye protection when using files or grinding tools.
:::

## 3. Flat Tire Repair

Flat tires are the most common bicycle problem and the most repairable. Understanding both patch and tube replacement ensures you can fix any puncture.

### Pre-Repair Inspection

Before removing the tire, determine if the puncture is in the tire or the tube:

1. **Visual inspection:** Look for thorns, glass, or metal debris embedded in the tire
2. **Pressure check:** Depress the tire; a healthy tire has moderate resistance to thumb pressure
3. **Deflation speed:** A slow leak suggests a small puncture; rapid loss suggests a rupture or pinhole

### Step-by-Step Patch Repair

**For small punctures (pinhole or small tear in tube):**

1. **Remove the wheel:** Release the brake (squeeze brake arms or unclip lever). Open the quick-release or use an Allen wrench to loosen the axle nuts. Lift the derailleur chain clear of the cassette and pull the wheel away.
2. **Extract the tire from the rim:** Insert the tire lever under the bead (edge of tire). Pry upward gently; do not force. Work around the rim with 2-3 levers spaced equally, creating slack in the tire. One side of the tire should pull free; you do not need to remove the entire tire.
3. **Remove the tube:** Unscrew the valve core (if removable) or open the valve. Pull the tube free. Note the valve type (Presta, Schrader).
4. **Locate the puncture:** Inflate the tube slightly. Listen for hissing or feel for air escaping. If you cannot locate it, submerge the tube in water and watch for bubbles. Mark the puncture with a pen or chalk.
5. **Prepare the surface:** Rub the area with the sandpaper from the patch kit (small abrasive sheet). Roughen the surface in a circular motion covering an area slightly larger than the patch.
6. **Apply adhesive (traditional patches only):** Spread a thin, even layer of patch adhesive over the sanded area. Allow 3-5 minutes for the solvent to partially evaporate (the surface should feel tacky, not wet).
7. **Place the patch:** Peel the backing from the patch carefully. Press the patch firmly onto the puncture, applying pressure from the center outward to expel air bubbles. Hold for 30-60 seconds. Do not peel the patch to check your work immediately; allow 5 minutes to fully cure.
8. **Test the patch:** Inflate the tube slightly and listen/feel for leaks. If the patch holds, proceed to reinstallation.
9. **Reinstall the tube:** Insert the valve through the rim hole first. Work the tire back onto the rim, beginning opposite the valve. Use your hands before resorting to tire levers; lever use risks puncturing the new tube. Press the tire fully onto the rim on both sides; the bead should seat evenly.
10. **Inflate fully:** Use the pump to reach the target pressure (marked on the tire sidewall). Inspect that the tire is seated evenly on the rim.

:::tip
**Finding a Hidden Puncture:** If the location is not obvious, inflate the tube to full pressure and submerge it section-by-section in a bucket of water. Punctures will produce visible bubbles. Pinhole leaks may require close inspection — feel for dampness or listen carefully. Some slow leaks come from the valve stem; test by applying soapy water around the valve.
:::

### Tube Replacement

**When to replace instead of patch:**

- Multiple punctures in the same tube
- Large tears or ruptures (>5mm)
- Valve damage
- Severe age-related degradation (cracking, hardening)

**Replacement steps:** Follow steps 1-3 above, but skip patch repair. Install a new tube of the correct size (marked on the tire, e.g., 26×1.5-2.125). Ensure the valve type matches your rim. Proceed with steps 9-10.

### Preventing Flats

- **Maintain pressure:** Check pressure weekly. Under-inflated tires pinch-flat; over-inflated burst.
- **Inspect tires regularly:** Remove thorns, glass, and debris before they penetrate deeper.
- **Use puncture-resistant tires:** Thicker rubber or Kevlar belts reduce flat rates by 70%.
- **Avoid riding through debris:** Straw, nails, and broken glass concentrations cause flats.
- **Replace worn tires:** Tire tread wears thin; worn tires suffer more flats. Replace when rubber is <2mm thick.

## 4. Chain Maintenance

The chain is the bicycle's most frequently maintained component. A well-lubricated chain reduces energy loss by 5-10% and lasts 2-3 years; a neglected, rusty chain slips, stretches, and fails within months.

### Understanding Chain Wear

Bicycle chains are measured in links. Each link consists of inner and outer plates held together by rivets (pins). Chains measure 1/8 inch (single-speed) or 3/32 inch (multi-speed) in width. Standard chain length is measured in links; a typical road bike runs 110-116 links; a mountain bike runs 114-120 links.

**Chain stretch:** As a chain wears, the pins and inner plates wear, causing the chain to elongate (stretch). A chain stretches approximately 0.5% per 1000 km of use. Once stretch exceeds 1%, the chain misaligns with the cogs, causing wear and slipping.

### Cleaning the Chain

A dirty chain loses efficiency and wears faster due to embedded grit acting as an abrasive. Clean chains every 100-200 km or after riding in wet/muddy conditions.

**Method 1: Brush cleaning (fastest)**

1. Place the bicycle in a work stand with the rear wheel elevated
2. Backpedal slowly while brushing the chain with an old toothbrush or soft wire brush
3. Wipe the chain with a dry cloth
4. Apply lubricant

**Method 2: Solvent soaking (most thorough)**

1. Remove the chain using the chain breaker (see Chain Replacement, below)
2. Soak the chain in a container of mineral spirits or degreaser for 10-30 minutes
3. Agitate gently; use a brush to remove stubborn dirt
4. Hang the chain vertically and allow to drip-dry
5. Reinstall and lubricate

### Lubricating the Chain

Lubrication reduces friction between plates, pins, and cogs. The lubricant must be light enough to penetrate the chain but not so light it washes away in rain.

**Technique:**

1. Backpedal slowly while applying lubricant to the inner chain (between the plates)
2. Aim for the top section of the chain (moving toward the front chainring)
3. Apply one drop per link; avoid over-lubrication (excess lubricant attracts dirt)
4. Wipe excess with a cloth after 10 minutes
5. Lubricate every 50-100 km or after wet rides

**Lubricant types:**

- **Thin mineral oil:** Inexpensive, effective, but washes away in heavy rain; reapply after wet rides
- **Light synthetic oil:** More water-resistant; cost is 2-3x mineral oil
- **Chain-specific lubricants:** Engineered for bicycle chains; excellent performance; highest cost

### Chain Replacement

Chains wear and require replacement every 1500-3000 km depending on conditions and maintenance.

**Identifying a worn chain:**

1. **Measure stretch:** Chain elongates 0.5% per 1000 km. A 114-link chain is 114 inches; a stretched chain measures 114.6+ inches. Use a ruler: place it on the chain so the "0" mark aligns with a pin. Count forward 24 pins (a standard chain checker covers this span). If the 24th pin does not align with the "24-inch" mark, the chain is stretched and requires replacement. A stretched chain (>1% elongation) damages the cassette and chainring teeth, making replacement more expensive.
2. **Visual inspection:** Rough, dull chains with visible rust are worn. Shiny, smooth chains are serviceable.

**Replacement steps:**

1. **Measure the old chain:** Count the links or measure end-to-end. Most chains are 108-120 links. The packaging will specify the correct length.
2. **Select the correct chain:** Match by width (1/8" for single-speed, 3/32" for multi-speed) and length. Many chains come slightly long; you will trim to fit.
3. **Use the chain breaker:** Place the old chain on the chain breaker tool. Align a pin with the breaker's punch. Turn the handle to push the pin out. Do not drive the pin completely through — stop when the pin is nearly free (the chain link should separate without the pin dropping out). This allows reuse of the pin if needed.
4. **Remove the old chain:** Once broken, slide the chain free of the chainring and cogs.
5. **Thread the new chain:** Starting at the largest chainring in front, thread the chain over all cogs in the rear cassette, then back to the chainring. Ensure the chain is not twisted.
6. **Set chain length:** The chain should have minimal slack on the smallest cog in front and rear (climbing gear). Add or remove links using the chain breaker tool. Reconnect the chain by linking the ends and driving a pin through with the chain breaker, or use a quick-link connector (if the chain is quick-link compatible).
7. **Test the chain:** Manually spin the pedals through a full rotation. The chain should run smoothly without hesitation, noise, or contact with the frame.

:::warning
**Chain Safety:** A broken chain mid-ride can cause a crash. Inspect chains before long rides. Never ride a chain with more than 1% stretch — damage to the cassette and chainring will compound costs. When installing a new chain, check that the quick-link is fully seated (if used) by pulling the chain hard.
:::

## 5. Brake Adjustment & Maintenance

Effective brakes are a safety necessity. Poorly adjusted brakes reduce stopping power and increase fall risk. Most brake adjustments require only Allen wrenches and take <10 minutes.

### Rim Brake Systems

**Rim brakes** press brake pads against the sides of the rim, creating friction to slow the wheel. They are the most common type on utility bicycles.

**Adjustment procedure:**

1. **Inspect pad wear:** Brake pads should be at least 3mm thick. If pads are <2mm or worn unevenly, replace them.
2. **Center the brake:** Loosen the bolt holding the brake caliper to the frame (usually a 5mm Allen wrench). Squeeze the brake lever fully; the pads should touch the rim evenly. Tighten the caliper while holding it centered.
3. **Adjust pad clearance:** If the brake rubs even when not engaged, loosen the mounting bolt slightly. Turn the barrel adjuster (on the brake cable) counterclockwise to reduce rubbing. Each turn adds ~0.5mm of pad clearance.
4. **Check pad-to-rim distance:** Pads should be 1-2mm from the rim when the brake is off. Spin the wheel; it should not drag or hit the pads anywhere around the rim.
5. **Test braking force:** Squeeze the brake lever; it should be firm (not mushy or hitting the bar). If it's too soft, pull the brake cable tighter by loosening the cable anchor bolt and moving the cable toward the frame before retightening.
6. **Toe-in (advanced):** If pads squeal, adjust the angle so the front of the pad contacts the rim slightly before the rear (toe-in). Use shims (small washers) under the mounting bolt on the rear side of the pad arm.

### Drum Brakes

**Drum brakes** are sealed units that house braking friction inside a drum attached to the wheel. They are maintenance-free for most users and more effective than rim brakes in wet conditions.

**Adjustment:** Drum brakes require virtually no field adjustment. If stopping power decreases, the brake may need internal servicing (requires disassembly by a skilled technician). In low-resource scenarios, accepting reduced braking performance may be necessary.

### Cable Replacement

Brake cables wear and fray, reducing braking responsiveness and introducing resistance.

**When to replace:** If the cable shows visible rust, fraying at either end, or sudden loss of responsiveness, replace it.

**Replacement steps:**

1. Disconnect the cable anchor bolt at the brake lever
2. Pull the cable free from the brake body
3. Measure the length and cut a new cable to the same length using cable cutters (sharp, clean cuts prevent fraying)
4. Thread the new cable through any ferrules (metal sleeves) or guides
5. Attach the cable to the brake lever anchor and the brake body anchor, ensuring the cable is taut but not under tension when the brake is relaxed
6. Apply lubricant to the inner cable (if you split the outer sleeve to access it)

## 6. Wheel Truing & Spoke Maintenance

A true wheel is essential for safe braking and efficient pedaling. A wobbly wheel indicates spoke problems that, if left unaddressed, lead to complete wheel failure.

### Understanding Wheel Geometry

A wheel consists of the rim (outer hoop) connected to the hub (center axle) by spokes under tension. Spokes typically run 32-40 per wheel, radiating from the hub flanges. The tension in each spoke pulls the rim toward the hub center.

A "true" wheel has:

- **Radial true:** The rim is centered around the hub; no runout (wobble) when spun
- **Lateral true:** The rim does not bend side-to-side; spoke tension is balanced
- **Dished true:** For rear wheels, the rim is offset to one side to center it between the chainstay ends

### Minor Wobble Repair

For small wobbles (<5mm), truing by hand is possible without a truing stand.

**Procedure:**

1. Spin the wheel and observe where it wobbles relative to the frame
2. If the rim touches the brake pad on one side, the wobble is toward that side
3. If the rim sits away from the brake, the wobble is away from that side
4. Use a spoke wrench (appropriately sized for the spoke nipples, typically 0.136", 0.156", or 0.188" depending on the wheel)
5. Turn the spoke wrench counterclockwise to loosen spokes, clockwise to tighten
6. **Tightening** pulls the rim toward that spoke; **loosening** allows the rim to move away
7. Make small adjustments: 1/4 turn per spoke; recheck wobble
8. Spokes on the left side of the rim (facing forward) tighten to move the rim right; right-side spokes move it left
9. Work around the wheel in a balanced manner; do not over-tighten (broken spokes are expensive)

### When to Rebuild

If wobbling exceeds 10mm, if spokes are broken or severely bent, or if the wheel has been involved in a collision, rebuild may be necessary. Rebuilding requires complete spoke replacement and professional expertise in most cases.

**Signs a wheel needs rebuilding:**

- Multiple broken spokes
- Severe permanent bend in the rim
- Spoke tension extremely uneven (some tight, some loose)
- Rim contact with brake pads despite spoke adjustment

### Spoke Replacement (Single Spoke)

**For a single broken spoke:**

1. Remove the tire and brake caliper if necessary to access the spoke
2. Remove the spoke nipple (nut on the rim end) using a spoke wrench; careful unthreading prevents stripped nipples
3. Remove the old spoke, noting its angle and routing (some spokes cross others in a specific pattern)
4. Insert the new spoke, routing it identically to the removed spoke
5. Thread the nipple onto the new spoke; hand-tighten first
6. Adjust spoke tension to match neighboring spokes (use touch to gauge relative tightness)
7. Reinstall the brake and tire; test for wobble

:::warning
**Over-Tightening Spokes:** Excessive spoke tension breaks spokes and cracks rims. If a spoke breaks during tensioning, the wheel has been over-stressed. Never exceed finger-tight plus 1/4 turn from the "just snug" position without professional guidance.
:::

## 7. Bearing Repacking & Maintenance

Bicycle bearings (at the hubs, headset, and bottom bracket) are sealed assemblies that require occasional maintenance as grease ages and water enters.

### When to Repack Bearings

- **Headset bearings:** If steering feels gritty, clunky, or has play (movement) when braking hard
- **Hub bearings:** If the wheel wobbles, has excessive side-play on the axle, or spins slowly before coasting
- **Bottom bracket:** If pedaling feels rough or there is play in the crankset

### Headset Bearing Repacking

**Tools needed:** 15mm and 17mm cone wrenches, grease, cloth

**Procedure:**

1. Loosen the stem cap bolt (top of the head tube) one turn
2. Using two appropriately-sized cone wrenches, turn the top cone counterclockwise to loosen (one wrench steadies the lower cone)
3. Remove the upper cone and dust cover
4. Lift out the ball bearing retainer (usually a metal or plastic cage holding 10-15 balls)
5. Wipe away old grease with a cloth; inspect balls for pitting or flat spots (signs of damage requiring replacement)
6. Apply fresh grease to the bearing race (the cup seated in the head tube)
7. Reinstall the bearing retainer and upper cone
8. Tighten the upper cone by hand until snug, then back it off 1/4 turn (this creates the correct play)
9. Tighten the stem cap bolt firmly to lock the cone position
10. Check steering: the fork should rotate freely without play (no clunking when you grab the front wheel and rock it toward and away from you)

### Hub Bearing Repacking

**Front hub procedure:**

1. Remove the wheel from the bicycle
2. Using cone wrenches (typically 15mm and 17mm), loosen and remove the cone on one side of the hub
3. Pull the axle free from the opposite side
4. Wipe away old grease
5. Inspect the bearing balls; if pitted, replace them (usually sold in sets of 8-12)
6. Apply fresh grease to the race
7. Reinstall the axle, cone, and retainer in reverse order
8. Adjust cone tension so the axle spins freely with no play (no side-to-side wobble at the rim)

**Rear hub:** Repeat the procedure on both sides if necessary, noting that the cassette cogs sit on the non-drive side; remove the cassette using a cassette tool before repacking.

### Bearing Adjustment

The critical adjustment is **cone tension.** Too loose: axial play and failure; too tight: grinding and premature bearing failure.

**Correct adjustment:** Turn the axle by hand (for hubs) or fork (for headset). Movement should be smooth and free, without grinding. Remove the wheel and check for side-play by grabbing the rim and rocking it toward the frame; there should be zero movement. If there is play, tighten the cone by 1/4 turn and recheck.

## 8. Improvised Tools & Field Repairs

In scenarios where commercial tools are unavailable, improvisation is critical.

### Tire Levers

**Improvised:** Large, smooth pebbles, hardwood wedges, or carefully-bent flat steel stock (flattened metal rod or old knife blade). The key is a smooth edge that does not tear the tire or pinch the tube.

### Pump

**Improvised:** A syringe (large medical syringe, 20-50 mL capacity) can inflate a tube slowly through the valve. Multiple people can use a syringe in sequence to reach usable pressure. A foot pump can be fabricated from PVC pipe and a leather or rubber piston.

### Chain Tool

**Improvised:** A heavy hammer and a nail can drive out a chain pin (place the chain on an anvil or block of wood, align the pin with the nail tip, and strike gently). This is slow and risks damage; reserve for emergencies only.

### Bearing Lubricant

**Improvised:** Any oil (vegetable, mineral, animal fat) works in an emergency, though it will not provide the cushioning effect of bicycle grease. In extremely low-resource scenarios, clean animal fat mixed with fine sand (filtered off before use) can serve as a low-quality bearing lubricant.

### Brake Adjustment

**Improvised without wrenches:** Use flat stones or wood blocks to support the brake body while loosening bolts by hand (if bolt heads are large enough to grip). This is slower but functional.

## 9. Safety Essentials

### Helmet Use

A bicycle helmet reduces head injury risk by 70% in falls. In post-collapse scenarios where hospital access is limited, head injury prevention becomes critical. Always wear a helmet, even for short trips.

**Helmet care:** Inspect after falls; cracks reduce protective ability. Replace helmets every 3-5 years; foam degrades over time.

### Visibility & Lighting

- Use reflectors on wheel spokes, pedals, and seat stays
- Carry a flashlight or implement a generator light powered by the wheel rotation
- Wear light-colored clothing when riding at dusk or night
- Paint the bicycle frame in high-visibility colors (white, yellow, orange)

### Road Awareness

Bicycle crashes most commonly result from:

- Collision with vehicles or stationary objects (guard against fatigue and distraction)
- Sudden stops (maintain proper braking distance from obstacles)
- Loss of control on wet or loose surfaces (reduce speed in poor conditions)
- Mechanical failure (perform pre-ride inspection)
