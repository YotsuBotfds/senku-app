## Introduction to Poultry Keeping

Chickens are the most accessible livestock for most people. A small flock (3–10 birds) can provide eggs, meat, pest control, and fertilizer for gardens while requiring minimal space, feed, or specialized equipment. A larger flock can supply a household's entire protein needs and generate surplus for barter or sale in communities where regulations permit.

This guide covers everything from selecting breeds suited to your climate and purpose, designing predator-proof housing, managing daily routines, formulating balanced feed from available ingredients, recognizing and treating health issues, butchering and processing poultry, brooding chicks, and integrating poultry with garden ecosystems.

## Part One: Breed Selection

### Egg-Producing Breeds (Layers)

These breeds prioritize egg production over meat quality.

**Best for all-purpose use:**
- **Leghorn:** 280–300 eggs per year; lean meat; active foragers; poor broods (rarely go broody)
- **Rhode Island Red:** 200–250 eggs yearly; dual-purpose quality meat; calm, good mothers; disease-resistant
- **Wyandotte:** 200+ eggs yearly; dual-purpose; excellent winter layers; broody; cold-hardy
- **Sussex:** 250 eggs yearly; curious foragers; friendly disposition; good meat potential

**Heritage/ornamental layers:**
- **Araucana/Ameraucana:** 200+ eggs yearly; colored eggs (blue, green); friendly; relatively cold-hardy
- **Cochin:** 150 eggs yearly; excellent mothers; broody; excellent meat; exceptional winter hardiness

**Exotic/specialty layers:**
- **Malay:** Deep yellow yolks; excellent meat; cold-hardy but slower in reaching lay
- **Butcher's favorite heritage breeds (excellent meat-to-egg balance):** Orpington, Australorp

### Meat Breeds

These maximize growth rate and meat quality.

**Standard meat breeds:**
- **Cornish Cross (hybrid):** Reaches market weight in 8–10 weeks; poor foraging; requires feeding; cannot produce own replacement chicks
- **Cornish (heritage):** 12–14 weeks to market weight; can reproduce naturally; dual-purpose meat and eggs (slower layer)
- **Cochin:** Excellent meat; broody; very cold-hardy; smaller than other meat birds

### Dual-Purpose Breeds (Balanced Egg and Meat Production)

These are ideal for small households and subsistence farms.

- **Orpington:** 180–200 eggs yearly; excellent meat (large frame); friendly; broody; cold-tolerant
- **Australorp:** 250+ eggs yearly; good meat; cold-hardy; calm; black plumage in many varieties
- **Wyandotte:** 200 eggs yearly; excellent meat; broody; cold-hardy; broody
- **Rhode Island Red:** 200–250 eggs; good meat; cold-hardy; disease-resistant
- **Sussex:** 250 eggs yearly; friendly; good meat; excellent foragers

### Climate and Regional Considerations

**Cold climates (winters below 0°F / –18°C):**
- Wyandotte, Cochin, Orpington, Australorp, Rhode Island Red
- Look for small combs and wattles (less frostbite risk)
- Avoid breeds with large combs (Leghorn) in severe cold

**Hot climates (summers regularly above 95°F / 35°C):**
- Mediterranean breeds (Leghorn, Araucana)
- Malay, Cochin (air pockets in feathering provide insulation)
- Avoid heavy-feathered breeds; ensure shade and water

**Wet/damp climates:**
- Any breed, but prioritize good ventilation and dry coops
- Avoid breeds prone to respiratory issues (large dusty feathers)

**Predator-heavy areas:**
- Docile, alert breeds; avoid flighty breeds that may scatter
- Preferably breeds that stay near coop (Orpington, Wyandotte)
- Fast, alert types (Sussex, Rhode Island Red) can escape predators

### Sourcing Birds

**Hatcheries:** Ship chicks via mail; breeds are standardized but may carry disease; offer sexed chicks (female-only hatchery birds are 95%+ accurate).

**Local breeders:** Higher cost; access to heritage genetics; can verify bird health; opportunity to learn care practices.

**Farm/feed store:** Some stores have local chick days (spring); can inspect birds before purchase.

**Breeder clubs:** Many heritage breeds have regional clubs; members often have chicks or young birds available.

**Minimum recommendation:** Start with 3 birds (provides redundancy if one dies); 6–10 birds suit a household of 4–5 people.

## Part Two: Coop Design and Housing

### Ventilation

Proper ventilation prevents moisture buildup, respiratory disease, and ammonia accumulation.

**Ventilation requirements:**
- Air should circulate without drafts flowing directly on roosting birds
- Minimum: 1 square foot of ventilation opening per bird (passive air exchange)
- Ventilation should be at roof/upper walls; roosts should be below it
- Drafts are worst 6 inches above the ground; roosting birds 2+ feet high avoid this

**Passive ventilation design:**
- Install a vent or opening under the roofline on opposite walls
- Use louvers to prevent rain entry while allowing air flow
- Ensure openings can be partially closed in extreme wind/snow

**Active ventilation:**
- In very cold climates, minimize ventilation in winter (keep moisture out); maximize in summer (hot-weather cooling)
- Some keepers use small solar fans for summer cooling

### Predator-Proofing

Predators include foxes, raccoons, opossums, hawks, snakes, and weasels.

**Structural requirements:**
- **Coop walls:** ½-inch hardware cloth (not chicken wire—predators tear through) extending 6 inches below ground or buried 6 inches deep
- **Roof:** Predator-proof (raccoons can pry open plywood); use ½-inch hardware cloth or solid wood
- **Door frame:** Tight-fitting with slide bolt (not latch hook—raccoons open these)
- **Run (attached outdoor area):** Overhead netting or hardware cloth (prevents hawk attacks)

**Run design for predators:**
- Completely enclosed top (hawks and owls hunt from above)
- Bury fencing 6 inches deep or bend outward 6 inches at ground level (prevents digging)
- If predators active, avoid letting birds free-range unsupervised

**Deterrents:**
- Motion-activated lights or sound devices in extreme predator situations
- Roosters provide alarm but cannot fight large predators
- Guardian animals (dog, llama, guinea fowl) provide protection if well-trained

### Nesting Boxes

One nesting box per 3–4 hens; boxes should be dark and private.

**Standard box dimensions:**
- 12 inches deep × 12 inches wide × 12 inches tall
- Minimum 10 inches × 10 inches for bantams
- 14 inches × 14 inches for large breeds (Cochin, Orpington)

**Placement:**
- Lower than roosts (hens prefer roosting above boxes; otherwise they sleep in boxes, fouling them)
- In the darkest part of the coop if possible
- Filled with 4–6 inches of straw or wood shavings

**Collecting eggs:**
- Check twice daily in hot weather (eggs deteriorate quickly)
- Once daily in cool weather
- Collect immediately after a hen leaves to reduce broodiness

### Roosts

Roosting bars should be smooth hardwood or saplings.

**Dimensions:**
- 1.5–2 inches diameter (hens should grip loosely; too thin causes bumblefoot)
- Spaced 12 inches apart horizontally and 12–18 inches vertically
- 2–4 feet above the floor (provides security and keeps droppings away from living space)

**Design:**
- Use hardware cloth or screens below roosts to catch droppings (easier to clean than floor)
- Ensure roosts are 12+ inches from walls (hens huddle more efficiently with open roosts)
- Provide one linear foot of roost per large hen, 8 inches per bantam

### Flooring

**Options:**
- **Deep litter (wood shavings 12+ inches deep):** Reduces smell; microbes heat decomposing material; must be turned occasionally; winter use is ideal
- **Sand:** Excellent dust bathing medium; drains well; cold in winter; requires regular cleaning
- **Concrete (sealed): Excellent for wet climates; coldest; hardest on feet/joints; must be scraped regularly
- **Dirt with straw:** Lowest cost; drains in most soils; requires frequent straw replacement

**Best practice:** Mix of materials—sand or dirt base with 4–6 inches wood shavings/straw on top. Remove heavily soiled material weekly.

### Space Requirements

Minimum (indoor coop): 3–4 square feet per bird

Recommended (indoor): 4–5 square feet per bird

Outdoor run: 8–10 square feet per bird (provides room to dust bathe and forage without overcrowding)

Free-range (open foraging): 1/4 to 1/2 acre per bird (depends on pasture quality and local predators)

Undersized housing leads to feather pecking, fighting, and disease.

### Coop Size Examples

**3-bird flock:** 12×8 foot coop with 40-square-foot run (or free-range)

**6-bird flock:** 8×10 foot coop with 60-square-foot run (or free-range)

**10-bird flock:** 10×12 foot coop with 100-square-foot run (or managed free-range)

## Part Three: Daily Care and Routine

### Morning Routine (15–20 minutes)

1. Open coop to attached run or free-range area
2. Top off water (essential in hot weather—check twice)
3. Inspect birds for signs of illness (discharge, drooping, reluctance to move)
4. Listen for abnormal sounds (rattling, wheezing)
5. Count birds; note any missing or injured
6. Provide fresh feed (1/4 pound per bird daily as a baseline)

### Evening Routine (15 minutes)

1. Collect eggs (prevents broodiness and breakage)
2. Provide fresh water for the night
3. Shut coop and bolt door (predators hunt dusk to dawn)
4. Quick health check
5. Remove any uneaten feed (prevents rodents)

### Water Management

**Daily water needs:** 250 ml (8 oz) per bird, more in hot weather

**Systems:**
- **Gravity waterers:** Inverted jar systems; simple but require daily refilling and cleaning
- **Trough systems:** Easy to fill; require frequent cleaning (algae and contamination build quickly)
- **Nipple drinkers:** Minimal spillage and contamination; require 50°F water (freezes in winter)

**Winter water:**
- Insulate waterers with straw/insulation
- Use heated bases (heated by kerosene or electricity if available)
- Or provide water 2–3 times daily at room temperature
- Snow is insufficient; birds need liquid water

**Water quality:**
- Change daily if not continuously flowing
- Provide clean, uncontaminated water (not runoff or stagnant)
- Remove algae and debris

### Coop Cleaning

**Daily:**
- Remove wet or soiled litter from roosting areas
- Remove uneaten feed (attracts rodents)

**Weekly:**
- Replace soiled straw/shavings in nesting boxes
- Scrape droppings from under roosts
- Top off deep litter bedding

**Monthly:**
- Full litter replacement in warm months
- Inspect walls and roof for damage/predator entry
- Clean windows/ventilation areas of dust and condensation

**Seasonal deep clean:**
- Spring/summer: Replace all bedding, disinfect coop, wash feeders and waterers thoroughly
- Fall: Prepare for winter (repair drafts, ensure ventilation is unblocked)

## Part Four: Feeding and Nutrition

### Feed Types and Formulations

**Commercial layer pellets/crumbles:**
- 16% protein; balanced mineral content; simplest option
- Drawback: requires continuous supply; may carry pathogens

**Commercial meat grower pellets:**
- 20–24% protein; formulated for rapid weight gain
- Used from week 1 until processing (8–10 weeks)

**Scratch grains (home-formulated):**
- Equal parts cracked corn, oats, wheat, barley
- 10–12% protein; lacks minerals and vitamins
- Use as supplement only (20% of diet)
- Add oyster shells for calcium if birds are laying

**Kitchen and garden scraps:**
- Vegetable trimmings, cooked grains, garden insects
- Excellent supplements (30% of diet)
- Avoid: avocado, chocolate, green potatoes, onions, moldy feed

**Forage and pasture:**
- Free-ranging birds consume 30%+ of diet as insects, plants, seeds
- Reduces purchased feed by 20–40%
- Excellent for egg and meat quality (darker yolks, better flavor)

### Nutritional Requirements

**Laying hens:**
- Protein: 16–18%
- Calcium: 3–3.5% (for shell formation)
- Phosphorus: 0.6–0.7%

**Meat birds:**
- Protein: 20–24%
- Calcium: 0.8–1.2%
- Phosphorus: 0.6–0.8%

**Growing chicks (0–8 weeks):**
- Protein: 24% (weeks 0–4); 18% (weeks 4–8)
- Balanced minerals for bone development

### Building a Complete Feed (No Commercial Input)

For 6 birds:

**Daily ration (3 pounds total):**
- 1 pound grains (corn, oats, wheat, barley—equal parts)
- 0.5 pound legumes (dried peas, lentils, soybeans—soybeans are high protein)
- 0.5 pound vegetables (garden scraps, kitchen waste)
- 1 pound forage (grass clippings, garden insects if free-ranging)
- 1 ounce oyster shells (for laying hens)

**Mix:**
1. Combine grains and legumes; store dry
2. Each feeding, moisten with water to prevent dust and aid digestion
3. Mix in fresh vegetables
4. Provide separately (allows birds to choose and reduces waste)

**Protein supplementation:**
- Insects (mealworms, crickets, grasshoppers): excellent if available
- Scraps from kitchen (meat, fish, bones): birds will consume and gain nutrition
- Worms and invertebrates from garden: natural foraging

### Oyster Shells and Grit

**Oyster shells (calcium):** Offer free-choice to laying hens; meat birds need less

**Grit (digestion aid):** Granite grit helps pulverize food in the gizzard; offer free-choice

Both can be offered in separate containers or mixed into feed.

### Water-to-Feed Conversion

As a rule, birds consume roughly 2 pounds of water for every 1 pound of dry feed. In hot weather, this increases to 3:1. Adequate water is critical; dehydration drops egg and meat production rapidly.

## Part Five: Health Management

### Common Diseases and Treatment

:::warning
**Biosecurity critical:** Isolate sick birds immediately; wash hands between birds; disinfect equipment. Many poultry diseases are highly contagious and fatal if not contained.
:::

**Coccidiosis (intestinal parasite):**
- **Symptoms:** Drooping, blood in droppings, pale appearance, death in chicks
- **Cause:** Eimeria parasites (common in dirty litter)
- **Treatment:** Amprolium (feed additive) or sulfamethoxazole (if available); clean coop thoroughly
- **Prevention:** Maintain dry litter; limit chick crowding; rotate pasture

**Newcastle Disease (viral):**
- **Symptoms:** Green diarrhea, twisted neck, tremors, death
- **Cause:** Highly contagious virus
- **Treatment:** None; euthanasia recommended
- **Prevention:** Isolation; vaccination if available; no visitors with contact to other birds

**Marek's Disease (herpes virus):**
- **Symptoms:** Paralysis (legs pulled backward), blindness, tumors, death
- **Cause:** Extremely contagious virus; lifelong carriers shed virus
- **Treatment:** None; affected birds must be culled
- **Prevention:** Vaccination at hatch; isolation of chicks from older birds

**Chicken Pox (fowlpox):**
- **Symptoms:** Scabby, warty lesions on face and legs; respiratory form causes wheezing
- **Cause:** Virus; spreads via mosquitoes and direct contact
- **Treatment:** Supportive care; vaccination available for respiratory form
- **Prevention:** Mosquito control; isolation

**Respiratory infections (infectious bronchitis, laryngotracheitis):**
- **Symptoms:** Wheezing, sneezing, watery discharge, low egg production
- **Cause:** Viral or bacterial
- **Treatment:** Improve ventilation; antibiotics if bacterial (tetracycline, sulfamethoxazole)
- **Prevention:** Good ventilation; isolation of coughing birds

**Parasites (external—lice, mites; internal—worms):**
- **Symptoms:** Feather loss, pale comb, reduced production, diarrhea
- **Treatment:** Dust with diatomaceous earth (DE) or approved pesticides; deworming if available
- **Prevention:** Regular dust bathing areas; good sanitation; rotate pasture

### Parasite Management

**External parasites (lice and mites):**
1. Provide dust bathing areas (5-gallon bucket filled with sand + 10% diatomaceous earth)
2. Birds bathe weekly in dust; natural behavior controls parasites
3. If infestation heavy: dust birds with DE or sulfur (if available)

**Internal parasites (worms):**
1. Rotate pasture (every 2 weeks if possible) to break lifecycle
2. Dewormer (fenbendazole, levamisole) if clinical symptoms or quarterly preventive
3. Good sanitation and dry litter reduce parasite load

### Bumblefoot (Staphylococcal infection)

**Symptoms:** Swollen, scabby foot; limping; heat in foot

**Cause:** Bacteria entering foot through cuts or rough roost edges

**Prevention:**
- Smooth roost bars (minimum 1.5 inches diameter)
- Prompt treatment of foot wounds

**Treatment:**
1. Soak foot in warm water (Epsom salt if available) for 10 minutes
2. Clean and dry
3. Wrap in sterile bandage with antibiotic ointment
4. Isolate bird if severe; consider systemic antibiotics (tetracycline)

### Egg Binding

**Symptoms:** Hen sits hunched, strains without laying, may become paralyzed if untreated

**Cause:** Oversized egg, calcium deficiency, lack of exercise, genetic predisposition

**Treatment:**
1. Isolate in warm area (75–85°F)
2. Provide calcium-rich water (crushed oyster shells + water)
3. Gentle abdominal massage (downward, toward vent)
4. Warm bath (10–15 minutes) relaxes muscles
5. If no relief in 12–24 hours, egg may need surgical intervention (rare in subsistence settings)

**Prevention:** Adequate calcium, exercise, multiple birds to minimize stress

## Part Six: Butchering and Processing

:::info-box
**Legal considerations:** Check local regulations. Some areas have restrictions on home butchering or require facility permits for any meat sales.
:::

### Pre-Butcher Preparation

- Select birds 8–10 weeks old (meat birds) or end-of-lay hens
- Fast for 12 hours before processing (empty digestive tract reduces contamination)
- Provide water
- Minimize stress (quiet, familiar handlers)

### Killing Methods

**Most humane method: cervical dislocation**
1. Hold bird securely behind wings
2. Place head between thumb and forefinger, neck stretched
3. Quick, firm downward motion while holding body still
4. Death is instantaneous if done correctly
5. Allow body to bleed out (3–5 minutes); nerves continue firing briefly after death

**Alternative: Stunning then bleeding**
1. Stun with sharp blow to back of head (baseball bat or specialized tool)
2. Immediately cut carotid arteries on both sides of neck
3. Allow complete bleeding (5+ minutes)

### Plucking

**Wet plucking (easiest for home processing):**
1. Heat water to 140–145°F (60–63°C)
2. Submerge carcass for 1–2 minutes (loosens feathers)
3. Pull feathers quickly; pin feathers may require second dipping
4. Complete removal takes 10–15 minutes per bird

**Dry plucking:**
1. Pluck without water (more difficult; can tear skin)
2. Requires more time but leaves less moisture

### Cleaning and Gutting

1. Remove head and feet (if keeping for stock)
2. Place bird breast-up; cut vertically through abdomen from breastbone to vent
3. Carefully remove intestines, gizzard, liver, heart, and lungs
4. Reserve liver (check for spots or discoloration; discard if abnormal)
5. Save gizzard and heart for cooking or stock
6. Rinse thoroughly under cool, clean water
7. Cool rapidly (ice bath if possible)

### Quartering

1. Remove wings (cut upward from rib cage)
2. Remove legs (bend hip joint until it pops; separate thigh from body)
3. Separate thighs from drumsticks
4. Remove breast halves (follow rib bones)
5. Trim excess skin and fat

### Cooling and Storage

- Cool to 40°F or below within 2 hours of processing
- Refrigerate at 32–40°F; use within 3 days
- Freeze at 0°F or below; keeps 6–12 months

**Making stock:**
- Boil bones, feet, and neck in water with aromatics (onion, carrot, herbs)
- Simmer 2–4 hours
- Strain and cool; fat solidifies on top and can be removed
- Freeze in containers

## Part Seven: Brooding Chicks

### Natural Brooding

A broody hen provides warmth, protection, and learning of pecking behavior.

**Encouraging broodiness:**
- Select breeds known to go broody (Cochin, Wyandotte, Orpington, Sussex)
- Leave eggs in nest boxes longer than usual (raises hormone levels)
- Provide secluded, private nesting area

**Managing a broody hen:**
1. Collect eggs under her; allow her to sit 7–10 days before introducing eggs
2. Move her and eggs to a separate, predator-proof brooding box
3. Provide feed and water within reach (she will leave briefly to eat)
4. Lockdown period (last 3 days): Do not move eggs; she will turn them less frequently
5. Hatch takes 21 days; allow hen to care for chicks 6–8 weeks

**Advantages:**
- Hen teaches chicks natural behaviors, socialization
- Chicks learn from mother (feeding, roosting)
- No equipment or heat source required

### Artificial Brooding

Necessary if no broody hens available.

**Equipment:**
- Heat lamp (250-watt infrared or heat lamp) or brooding plate
- Thermometer
- Brooding box or area (3×3 feet minimum for 25 chicks; scale up accordingly)
- Feed and water containers
- Bedding (wood shavings, sand, or paper towels—avoid cedar/pine)

**Temperature management:**
- Week 1: 95°F (35°C) under lamp; ambient 75°F (24°C)
- Week 2–3: 85°F (29°C)
- Week 4–5: 75°F (24°C)
- Week 6+: Ambient temperature (transition to outdoor run if weather permits)

**Chick-to-brooder ratio:**
- 1 chick per 1 square foot initially; expand as they grow
- Watch for crowding behavior (piling causes overheating/suffocation)

**Feed for chicks:**
- Starter feed: 24% protein for weeks 0–4
- Grower feed: 18% protein for weeks 4–8
- Provide chick-sized feeders and waterers
- Fresh water constantly
- Introduce chick grit if feeding anything besides commercial chick feed

## Part Eight: Predator Management and Free-Ranging

### Predator Identification and Strategies

**Hawks and owls:**
- Hunt during day (hawks) or dusk/night (owls)
- Strategy: Overhead netting over run; bring birds to coop by dusk
- Guardian: Roosters provide alarm; can defend against smaller raptors

**Foxes:**
- Nocturnal; prey on complete birds (leave no remains)
- Strategy: Secure coop completely; no ground-level gaps
- Guardian: Large dog; Livestock Guardian Dog breeds (Anatolian, Great Pyrenees) are ideal

**Raccoons:**
- Nocturnal; highly intelligent; can open latches
- Kill multiple birds (traumatic); often leave corpse
- Strategy: Secure coop with slide bolts, not latches; bury fencing; remove food sources
- Guardian: Dog with raccoon experience

**Weasels (stoat, mink, ermine):**
- Nocturnal; extremely vicious; kill for sport
- Kill entire flock quickly
- Strategy: ½-inch hardware cloth (can fit through larger gaps); eliminate ground holes
- Guardian: Dog or cat may deter

**Opossums:**
- Opportunistic; slow; play dead as defense
- Strategy: Same as raccoon
- Guardian: Dog will chase; unlikely to establish residency if dog present

**Snakes:**
- Eat eggs and chicks
- Strategy: Eliminate rodents (snake food source); keep grass short; remove hiding spots
- Remove snake by hand or trap if possible (some are beneficial)

### Free-Ranging

**Advantages:**
- Reduce feed costs (30–50% reduction possible)
- Improved egg yolk color and nutrition
- Pest control (insects, slugs, ticks)
- Manure distribution in pasture
- Exercise and natural behavior expression

**Disadvantages:**
- Increased predation risk
- Crop/garden damage
- Parasite exposure
- Reduced production in very hot weather

**Best practices:**
- Supervise during vulnerable hours (dusk/dawn)
- Ensure secure coop for night confinement
- Rotate pasture (every 2–4 weeks) if possible
- Provide shade and water in foraging area
- In predator-heavy areas, restrict free-range to daytime only under supervision

### Integrating with Gardens

**Benefits to garden:**
- Insect and pest control (grasshoppers, beetles, slugs, tick larvae)
- Fertilizer deposition
- Cultivation of soil (scratching releases compacted layers)

**Risks:**
- Dust bathing in garden beds (creates wallows)
- Eating seedlings and transplants
- Overfeeding on vegetables (reducing crop)

**Strategies:**
- Fence garden beds during seeding/transplant phase
- Allow access after plants are established and resilient
- Provide vegetable scraps to redirect from growing plants
- Rotate free-ranging areas to prevent overuse

## Part Nine: Seasonal Management

### Spring

- Increase light for early laying (if using artificial light)
- Introduce new chicks or pullets
- Check coop for winter damage; repair before predator season
- Begin free-range grazing if weather permits

### Summer

- Provide shade and cool water
- Watch for heat stress (panting, lethargy)
- Increase ventilation and airflow
- Move run frequently to provide fresh forage and shade
- Expect reduced egg production in heat

### Fall

- Prepare for winter (check weatherproofing, insulation, heating)
- Collect and store feed
- Reduce lighting-based stimulation (if used) gradually
- Molt occurs; egg production drops naturally
- Plan butchering schedule for meat birds

### Winter

- Monitor water (prevent freezing)
- Ensure adequate ventilation without drafts
- Provide supplemental heat if temperatures drop below 0°F (–18°C) for extended periods
- Expect reduced egg production (natural response to shorter days)
- Use deep litter method for warmth

## Conclusion

Poultry keeping is accessible, rewarding work that connects people to food production and animal husbandry. A small flock provides protein, reduces food costs, produces excellent fertilizer, and integrates naturally into homestead and garden ecosystems. From selecting breeds suited to your climate, designing a secure and healthy coop, managing daily routines, solving health challenges, and processing your birds, the skills in this guide establish a foundation for self-sufficient poultry production at any scale.
