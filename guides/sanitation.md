## 1\. Germ Theory & Disease Prevention

### How Diseases Spread

Understanding disease transmission is fundamental to preventing epidemics. Pathogens spread through several routes:

:::card
#### Fecal-Oral Route

The most dangerous transmission pathway in poor sanitation conditions. Microscopic fecal matter contaminates hands, food, or water supplies. This causes cholera, typhoid, dysentery, hepatitis A, and giardia.
:::

:::card
#### Droplet Transmission

Respiratory pathogens spread through coughs, sneezes, or talking within 2 meters. Causes influenza, tuberculosis, COVID-19, measles, and mumps. Controlled through masks, ventilation, and distancing.
:::

:::card
#### Vector-Borne Transmission

Insects and animals transmit pathogens: mosquitoes carry malaria and dengue; ticks carry Lyme disease; flies transmit dysentery and cholera. Requires insect control and protective measures.
:::

:::card
#### Contact Transmission

Direct skin-to-skin contact or contaminated surfaces. Causes scabies, ringworm, staph infections, and many viral infections. Spreads by touching infected persons or contaminated objects.
:::

### Historical Context

Before modern sanitation, waterborne diseases were civilization's greatest killers:

-   **Cholera (1854):** John Snow identified contaminated water source in London by mapping deaths. Single pump elimination stopped outbreak, proving sanitation's life-saving importance.
-   **Typhoid Fever:** Killed millions before sanitation improvements. Mary Mallon spread typhoid through food as a kitchen worker while herself immune to symptoms.
-   **Dysentery:** Called "The Bloody Flux," killed more soldiers in wars than combat. Proper sanitation eliminated this scourge.

:::warning
**Critical:** In a post-collapse scenario, a single outbreak can devastate a community. Sanitation infrastructure is more valuable than medicine because it prevents disease rather than treating it.
:::

## 2\. Latrine Construction

### Pit Latrine Fundamentals

The simplest sanitation technology that prevents fecal-oral disease transmission. Human waste is safely isolated from water sources, food, and living areas.

:::danger
**GROUNDWATER CONTAMINATION**: Improperly placed latrines contaminate wells and water sources with fecal pathogens (cholera, typhoid, hepatitis, parasites). This is the #1 cause of waterborne disease outbreaks in communities.
:::

### Minimum Setback Distances
| Condition | Minimum Distance from Water Source |
|-----------|----------------------------------|
| Sandy/gravel soil (high permeability) | 30 meters (100 feet) minimum |
| Clay/loam soil (low permeability) | 15 meters (50 feet) minimum |
| Fractured rock/karst | 60+ meters (200+ feet) — consult geology |
| Sloped terrain | Always DOWNHILL from water source |
| Flood zone | Never place latrines in flood-prone areas |

### Soil Permeability Test (Percolation Test)
1. Dig a test hole 30 cm × 30 cm × 30 cm (1 ft cube)
2. Fill with water and let drain completely (pre-soak)
3. Refill to 15 cm (6 inches) depth
4. Measure time for water level to drop 2.5 cm (1 inch)
- Under 5 minutes: HIGH permeability (sandy) → Use maximum setback distances
- 5-30 minutes: MODERATE permeability → Standard setback distances
- Over 30 minutes: LOW permeability (clay) → Minimum setback distances acceptable, but poor drainage may cause other issues

### Pit Depth Limits
- Pit bottom must be at least 1.5 meters (5 feet) ABOVE the highest seasonal groundwater level
- In areas with high water tables, use raised/mound latrines or composting toilets instead of pit latrines
- If you hit water while digging, STOP — the location is unsuitable for a pit latrine

:::warning
**DOWNHILL RULE**: Groundwater flows downhill. Always place latrines downhill AND downstream from wells, springs, and surface water sources. If terrain is flat, maximize distance. When in doubt, dig a test well between the proposed latrine site and water source to check for contamination.
:::

![Sanitation &amp; Public Health diagram 1](../assets/svgs/sanitation-1.svg)

### Pit Latrine Construction Steps

1.  Choose location: Minimum 30m downhill from water source, 20m from dwelling. Ensure natural drainage away from well/spring.
2.  Excavate pit: 2-3m deep depending on groundwater level. Must be above water table to prevent contamination.
3.  Line the pit: Use brick, stone, or concrete rings to prevent collapse. Don't line if soil is clay (natural seal).
4.  Install slab: Concrete or wood platform at ground level with single squat hole. Slab must be stable and cleanable.
5.  Build superstructure: Simple shelter with roof and door for privacy. Protects users from weather and insects.
6.  Add ventilation: Pipe (10cm diameter) running from pit to roof height with fly screen. Allows methane escape and reduces smell.
7.  Final sealing: Small sand trap beneath hole to dry out fecal matter. Improves safety and delays pit filling.

### VIP (Ventilated Improved Pit) Latrine

Improved version with enhanced ventilation and fly control. The vent pipe extends above the roof with a fly screen at the top. Black pipe absorbs solar heat, creating airflow that pulls odors and flies away from the squat hole. Reduces smell by 99% and prevents fly breeding. Preferred for densely populated areas.

### Composting Toilet

Uses organic material (sawdust, leaves, ash) to create aerobic decomposition. Waste breaks down safely without water or pit. Produces usable compost. Requires carbon/organic material after each use. Best for areas with high groundwater or water scarcity. Can be above-ground and relocated.

### Pour-Flush Toilet

Uses water seal to prevent smell and flies. Requires 1-2 liters of water per flush. Wastewater flows to septic tank or infiltration pit. Better than pit latrine but requires reliable water supply. Handwashing station and soap critical nearby.

### Handwashing Station Design

:::card
Essential location: Immediately adjacent to latrine, visible and convenient.

Simple design: Clay pot with tap at bottom (or hole with plug), elevated on stand. Water + soap/ash.

Daily maintenance: Refill water, replace soap, prevent contamination of handwashing water.

Capacity: Minimum 10 liters for hand hygiene before leaving latrine area.
:::

## 3\. Sewage & Wastewater Systems

### Septic Tank Design

Decentralized sewage system for communities with piped water. Receives all household wastewater and separates solids from liquids for safe treatment.

![Sanitation &amp; Public Health diagram 2](../assets/svgs/sanitation-2.svg)

### Septic System Components

:::card
#### Tank Size Calculation

Minimum 1000 liters (264 gallons) for 5-10 people. Add 500 liters per additional household member. Larger tanks (2000-3000L) recommended for reliability and reduced maintenance frequency.
:::

:::card
#### Tank Construction

Can be concrete (preferred), brick/stone with cement render, or fiberglass. Must be watertight and have internal baffles to slow water flow. Inlet pipe is submerged; outlet pipe is slightly lower. Creates settling chamber where solids drop and fats rise.
:::

:::card
#### Drain Field Design

Disperses treated wastewater into soil. Trenches 30-45cm deep, 1m apart, backfilled with gravel and perforated pipe. Area depends on soil percolation rate (larger for clayey soil). Minimum 30m from water source. Never plant trees directly over drain field (roots clog pipes).
:::

### Constructed Wetlands

Engineered ecosystem for gray water treatment. Shallow basin (30-60cm) with aquatic plants (cattails, reeds) and gravel. Wastewater filters through roots and rhizomes where beneficial bacteria remove nutrients and pathogens. Produces clean water for reuse. Requires 2-5m² area per person. Visually attractive and provides habitat.

### Cesspool vs. Septic Tank

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Feature</th><th scope="row">Septic Tank</th><th scope="row">Cesspool</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Treatment</td><td>Biological + physical separation</td><td>No treatment, only storage</td></tr><tr><td>Output</td><td>Treated, can infiltrate soil</td><td>Untreated, must pump when full</td></tr><tr><td>Contamination Risk</td><td>Low if properly built</td><td>High if near water sources</td></tr><tr><td>Maintenance</td><td>5-10 year pump intervals</td><td>Every 1-2 years (expensive)</td></tr><tr><td>Cost</td><td>Higher initial</td><td>Lower initial, higher ongoing</td></tr><tr><td>Post-Collapse Viability</td><td>Excellent</td><td>Poor (needs external pump)</td></tr></tbody></table>

### Pipe Sizing & Slope Calculations

Main sewer line: 100mm (4") minimum from house to tank. Slope of 1-2% (1cm drop per meter). Slope is critical—too flat causes clogs, too steep causes water to separate from solids.

Drain field laterals: 50mm (2") diameter perforated pipe. Minimum slope 0.3%. Equal distribution between multiple trenches is crucial.

Velocity formula: Minimum 0.6 m/s to prevent settling in pipes. For gravity flow with 1.5% slope, 100mm pipe works for 40+ people per day.

## 4\. Waste Management

### Solid Waste Disposal

In post-collapse scenarios, waste management prevents disease vectors and contaminates. Separate waste streams reduce long-term problems.

:::card
#### Organic Waste

Food scraps, yard waste, animal manure. Best composted in designated area away from living space (20m+). Can feed chickens/pigs or become fertilizer. Never put meat into open compost (attracts predators). Composting kills most pathogens with proper heat and time.
:::

:::card
#### Inert Waste

Metal, glass, stone, concrete. Store separately for potential reuse. Metals can be recycled or remade. Glass is valuable for containers. Take minimal space compared to organic waste.
:::

:::card
#### Hazardous Waste

Batteries, paint, chemicals, pesticides. Must be isolated in sealed containers away from water sources and living areas. Bury deep or store long-term. Never burn—releases toxic fumes. Mark clearly to prevent accidental exposure.
:::

### Composting Organic Waste

1.  Create compost bin: Enclosed structure (wood, wire, or brick) to contain materials and prevent rodent access. Minimum 1m³ volume.
2.  Layer materials: Alternate "brown" (dry leaves, straw, paper) with "green" (food scraps, fresh manure). Ratio 3:1 brown to green.
3.  Maintain moisture: Keep as moist as a wrung-out sponge. Add water if too dry; turn pile if too wet.
4.  Turn regularly: Every 1-2 weeks accelerates decomposition. Center of pile reaches 60-70°C, killing pathogens in 2-3 months.
5.  Harvest compost: When dark, crumbly, and cool (6-12 months). Use for gardens and fields to rebuild soil.

### Burn Pit Safety

For non-compostable waste: plastic, rubber, textiles. Burn pits must be:

-   Located 50m+ downwind from living areas
-   In cleared ground away from dry vegetation
-   Below ground level in a pit (contains sparks)
-   Attended and extinguished completely
-   Never for hazardous materials (batteries, paint, chemicals)

:::warning
**Never burn:** Plastics (dioxin release), rubber (toxic fumes), paint cans, batteries, aerosols. Only wood, paper, and natural fibers.
:::

### Recycling & Repurposing

In sustainable communities, waste becomes resources. Metal containers can store water or become tools. Glass jars preserve food. Plastic bottles become planters or containers. Textiles patch clothing or become cleaning rags. Rope and cord can be remade into fishing line or nets.

### Medical Waste Handling

Used bandages, syringes, contaminated materials. Must be incinerated (if safe) or deeply buried (1m+ depth) in designated pit away from water sources. Do not mix with general waste. Sharps containers prevent needle stick injuries. In resource-limited settings, reusable instruments must be sterilized rather than discarded.

## 5\. Water Supply Protection

### Buffer Zones: Critical Distances

Contamination from latrines spreads through soil and groundwater. Establish protection buffers:

:::card
Minimum 30 meters from latrine to well/spring downhill. This is the absolute minimum—50m is safer.

Groundwater gradient: Water flows downslope. Latrines must ALWAYS be downhill from wells. Even slight slope matters in clay soils.

Seasonal consideration: Rainy season increases flow speed. In monsoonal climates, increase distance in wet season.
:::

### Spring Protection

Natural springs often provide safe water if protected from external contamination. Steps for protection:

1.  Identify spring source: Find where water emerges naturally. This is the cleanest point.
2.  Build collection box: Enclose spring origin in concrete or stone box to prevent surface contamination.
3.  Install overflow pipe: Excess water flows downhill away from collection point, preventing stagnation.
4.  Add protection wall: Build mound around spring to direct surface runoff away. Keep 10m radius clean.
5.  Install fence: Keep livestock and people away from spring zone. Only for water collection, not washing.
6.  Install tap/outlet: Allow water extraction without people entering box. Prevents contamination.
7.  Regular maintenance: Clean collection box quarterly. Remove sediment and algae. Check overflow function.

### Well Sanitation

Hand-dug wells require multiple protection measures:

:::card
#### Well Construction Standards

Casing: Sealed pipe extending 2m above ground prevents contaminated surface runoff from entering well.

Lining: Solid casing to at least 3m depth. Gravel filter below to prevent sand in water.

Platform: Concrete apron (2x2m minimum) around well base to slope water away. Prevents puddles that breed mosquitoes.

Sanitary seal: Cap well when not in use. Install hand pump (not rope bucket) to prevent contamination.

Water extraction: Only use clean containers. Do not drop objects into well. Test water quarterly.
:::

### Watershed Management

Protect the entire area that drains to your water source:

-   Keep livestock away from streambanks (erosion + contamination)
-   Maintain forest cover to filter and slow runoff
-   Avoid pesticide and chemical use uphill from water source
-   If agricultural, use buffer strips of vegetation between fields and water
-   Manage erosion with terraces and mulch to prevent silt in water

### Water Safety Testing

Simple visual test: Water should be clear, colorless, and odorless. Cloudiness indicates sediment or pathogens. Brown water suggests iron or tannins. Smell indicates organic decomposition.

Boiling test: Boil sample for 1 hour. If cloudiness doesn't settle, indicates hardness (safe but tastes bad).

Chlorine test: Add 2 drops pool chlorine per liter. If no smell after 30 minutes, bacteria were present. If strong chlorine smell, water is now safe (chlorine has disinfected it).

Presumptive test: Observe well area. If latrines nearby, heavy use in wet season, or stagnant areas, assume water is contaminated and must be treated.

### Advanced Multi-Barrier Water Treatment

Beyond basic boiling and chemical treatment, multiple-barrier systems improve water safety and reduce disease transmission risk in larger settlements.

**Principle:** No single treatment is 100% effective. Combining methods provides redundancy. If one barrier fails, others catch contaminants.

**System design:**

1. **Pre-filtration:** Sediment filter (removes particles >5µm, reduces load on subsequent barriers). Strain through cloth 2-3 times.

2. **Chemical coagulation:** Add alum powder (potassium aluminum sulfate, 4-8 mg/L dosage) to water. Allows suspended particles to clump and settle. Let settle 2 hours. Alum slightly acidifies water—acceptable for drinking.

3. **Sand/gravel filtration:** Slow sand filter system (1 meter depth, stone layers) filters particles, provides biological filtration. Multiple cells in series improve efficiency.

4. **Biofilm development:** Sand filters develop beneficial biofilm (layer of organisms on sand surface) that breaks down organic matter, consumes pathogens, acts as additional filtration barrier. **Timeline:** Biofilm develops over 4-6 weeks. Filtration effectiveness poor initially; improves with time as biofilm matures. Continue boiling water from new filters until biofilm established and water quality obviously improves.

5. **Activated charcoal:** Removes chemicals, improves taste/odor (not effective against pathogens alone). Layer of fine charcoal in filtration system.

6. **Boiling:** Final thermal treatment kills any remaining pathogens (rolling boil 1 minute, 3 minutes at elevation).

7. **Chemical disinfection:** Chlorine or iodine as final barrier (1-2 ppm for clear water, 3-5 ppm for cloudy water).

**Most effective post-collapse approach:** Sediment filtration + boiling. Additional barriers add complexity but improve safety margin if boiling fails.

### Rainwater Harvesting with First-Flush Diverter

Rainwater is cleanest natural source but requires protection during collection.

**First flush diverter:** First rain washes roof debris (bird droppings, dust) accumulating since last rain. Discard first 10-15 minutes of rain (removes dust/contamination from air and roof). For roof catchment, install simple diverter: PVC pipe from gutter with manual valve, direct initial flow to ground or waste tank. After 10-20 minutes of flow, switch valve to direct flow to storage. Prevents contamination of stored water with first-flush water.

**Roof material:** Avoid asbestos roofing (historical), lead-painted surfaces (old buildings). Metal and tile roofs most acceptable.

**Collection and storage:** Sealed storage tank prevents mosquito breeding and dust entry. Covered tanks keep algae from growing. If algae develops, drain tank completely and clean interior with brush.

## 6\. Personal Hygiene

### Handwashing: The Most Critical Practice

Handwashing prevents 50% of diarrheal diseases and 30% of respiratory infections. Simple but must be done correctly.

:::card
#### Proper Handwashing Technique

1.  Wet hands with clean water (flowing is best, but immersion works)
2.  Apply soap (or ash/salt mixture) and lather thoroughly
3.  Wash palms, backs, between fingers, under nails, wrists (minimum 20 seconds)
4.  Rinse completely under running water until no soap remains
5.  Dry with clean cloth or air dry (cloth must be washed daily)
:::

### When to Wash Hands (Absolutely Critical Moments)

-   After using latrine (before leaving toilet area)
-   Before food preparation and eating
-   Before childcare or touching children
-   After contact with feces (diapers, animals)
-   Before treating wounds and after
-   After touching animals or animal products
-   When hands are visibly dirty

### Handwashing Without Running Water

Tippy tap: Water container with tap at bottom. Press lever to release water for handwashing. Refill once daily.

Basin system: Three basins—wash, rinse, final rinse. First basin gets dirtiest, change daily. Reduces water use by 80% compared to running water.

Alternative soap: Wood ash (2 parts) mixed with water (1 part) creates lye that cleans. Add fat scraps to make bar soap. Salt also works as mild cleaner.

### Bathing with Limited Water

Daily bathing is important for disease prevention. With water scarcity:

1.  Heat minimal water (1-2 liters) by sun or fire
2.  Pour over body to wet skin
3.  Use soap/ash to lather entire body
4.  Rinse with available clean water
5.  In cold climates, once weekly bathing prevents hypothermia while maintaining hygiene

Face, hands, genitals, and feet should be washed daily even if full body wash is infrequent. Focus on high-contact areas.

### Dental Hygiene Alternatives

Commercial toothpaste unavailable? Use natural alternatives:

:::card
#### Salt & Baking Soda

Mix 1:1 with water to form paste. Cleans teeth and slightly abrasive. May sting gums initially. Prevents plaque buildup.
:::

:::card
#### Activated Charcoal

Powder from burned wood is mildly abrasive and absorbs odors. Mix with water and small amount of salt. Whitens teeth with regular use.
:::

:::card
#### Chew Sticks

Branches from certain trees (neem, salvadora, miswak) contain natural antimicrobials. Chew end to fray, then use as brush. Prevents cavities and gum disease.
:::

:::card
#### Alcohol-based Rinses

Strong alcohol (liquor) kills mouth bacteria. Rinse with small amount, don't swallow. Works as antiseptic for gum disease.
:::

### Nail & Hair Care

Fingernails: Cut short every 1-2 weeks. Long nails harbor bacteria and parasites (schistosomiasis, hookworm). Keep clean with nail brush.

Hair: Regular washing removes lice and mites. Comb with fine-tooth comb to remove nits. In lice outbreaks, shave head or treat with natural oils (neem, coconut).

Foot care: Inspect daily for cuts, blisters, infections. Wash between toes—fungal infections thrive in moisture. Trim toenails straight across to prevent ingrown nails. In parasitic regions, always wear shoes.

## 6a. Nutritional Hygiene and Deficiency Prevention

Survival situations often restrict food variety, leading to nutritional deficiencies which weaken immune system and impair healing. Proper nutrition directly impacts disease resistance and survival outcomes.

### Common Deficiencies in Survival

**Vitamin C deficiency (scurvy):** Prevents collagen formation, impairs wound healing and immune function. Symptoms: bleeding gums, loose teeth, poor wound healing, extreme fatigue. Prevention: consume fresh plants (leaves, berries, evergreen needles). Pine needle tea high in vitamin C—steep handful of needles 5-10 minutes (contains 4-5 times more vitamin C than citrus). Eat any raw vegetables available (some ascorbic acid retained even in cooked foods).

**Vitamin A deficiency:** Impairs vision, immune function, and skin health. Symptoms: night blindness, poor wound healing, skin lesions. Prevention: consume orange/yellow plants (carrots, pumpkins, sweet potato leaves), dark leafy greens, animal liver if available. Liver is extremely concentrated source—even small amounts (50-100g) provide daily requirements.

**Iron deficiency (anemia):** Reduces oxygen carrying capacity, causes fatigue, impairs wound healing and cognitive function. Symptoms: extreme fatigue, pale skin/nails, shortness of breath with exertion. Prevention: consume meat (red meat especially iron-rich), legumes (beans, peas), dark leafy greens (spinach, kale). Vitamin C increases iron absorption dramatically—eat citrus or vitamin C-rich plants with iron-rich foods. Clay and ash consumption (pica behavior) indicates iron deficiency—requires intervention.

**Calcium deficiency:** Weakens bones over months, affects muscle function. Symptoms: muscle cramps, brittle nails, slow fracture healing. Prevention: consume leafy greens, seeds/nuts (if available), small bones in fish (if fishing possible), eggshells ground into powder (calcium carbonate, mix into grains).

**Iodine deficiency:** Impairs thyroid function, causes lethargy, mental fog, intellectual disability (cretinism in infants if maternal deficiency untreated). Symptoms: enlarged thyroid (goiter), fatigue, cold intolerance. Prevention: consume seaweed if coastal access (extremely high iodine), iodized salt if available. Seaweed is concentrated source—small amount (2-3 cm piece) daily sufficient. Landlocked regions without seaweed access face serious iodine deficit over months.

### Supplementation from Plants

**Stinging nettle:** Extremely nutritious (iron, calcium, vitamins, minerals). Boil leaves in water 10 minutes, drink as tea. Tastes grassy. Multiple cups daily safe and beneficial. Nutritional value rivals cultivated vegetables.

**Dandelion greens:** Highly nutritious (similar to spinach), young leaves less bitter than mature. High vitamin A and C. Can be eaten raw or cooked. Entire plant useful—roots roasted as coffee substitute contain inulin (prebiotic).

**Watercress:** One of most nutrient-dense plants available (more iron per ounce than beef). Eat raw or cooked. Grows in clean streams year-round in temperate regions.

**Chickweed and other wild greens:** Various species high in vitamins and minerals. Learn local edible greens. Foraging adds dietary diversity.

### Signs of Malnutrition

Watch for: fatigue disproportionate to exertion, slow wound healing (despite good care), weakened immunity (constant infections), hair loss, bleeding gums, edema (swelling from protein deficiency), apathy or depression. Address by increasing food variety and quantity if possible. Prioritize calorie-dense foods (nuts, seeds, animal fat) before micronutrient variety if choosing between them—starvation is more immediately dangerous than deficiency.

## 6b. Child and Infant Hygiene in Survival

Infants and young children have weaker immune systems and higher disease risk. Special considerations prevent catastrophic illness and improve survival outcomes for vulnerable group members.

### Diaper Sanitation and Waste Management

Modern disposable diapers unavailable in survival. Cloth diapers essential: use cotton cloth (doubled for absorbency), fastened with plant fiber cord or animal skin strips. Change frequently (every 3-4 hours, immediately if soiled). Wash in boiling water daily to sterilize—critical for preventing diaper rash and infections.

**Alternative: no-diaper approach** (common in many traditional cultures): Hold infant over waste area for elimination. Requires attentiveness to infant cues but reduces diaper maintenance burden and infection risk. Infants signal readiness (fussiness, changes in body position). This method is faster than diaper changes and improves parent-infant bonding in observation.

**Diaper rash prevention:** Keep area dry and clean. Air exposure accelerates healing. Change wetted diapers within hours. Use soft absorbent materials next to skin. Powders from ground dried plants (plantain, yarrow) or charcoal powder absorb moisture and reduce rash.

### Infection Prevention in Children

Children prone to ear infections, respiratory infections, and skin infections due to immature immune systems.

**Ear hygiene:** Prevent moisture in ear canals after bathing (risk of infection). Gently dry ears with cloth. Address respiratory symptoms early with herbal teas, steam inhalation—prevents progression to ear infections.

**Respiratory infections:** Monitor for persistent cough or fever. Steam inhalation (breathing vapor from hot water pot) loosens secretions. Herbal tea with ginger, honey, lemon provides comfort. Watch for rapid breathing, retractions (skin sucking inward with breathing), or stridor (wheezing sound)—signs requiring aggressive intervention.

**Diarrhea in infants:** Extremely dangerous in infants under 6 months (dehydration rapidly fatal). Aggressive rehydration essential. **Oral rehydration solution critical:** Mix 1 liter clean water + 6 level teaspoons sugar + 1/2 level teaspoon salt. Give small frequent amounts (5-10 ml every few minutes using spoon/syringe). If vomiting, wait 10 minutes after vomiting, then resume slowly. Continue rehydration until diarrhea stops plus 12 additional hours.

**Signs of dangerous dehydration in children:** Extreme thirst, dry mouth, no urination for 6+ hours (normal is every 3-4 hours), dizziness or lethargy, weak pulse, sunken fontanelle (soft spot on baby's head), sunken eyes. These require immediate ORS administration—if possible seek advanced medical care.

### Breastfeeding Support

Breastfeeding provides best nutrition and passive immunity transfer (antibodies from mother protect infant). Ensure nursing mother gets adequate calories and hydration (high caloric cost of nursing—mother needs 500+ extra calories daily). Support nursing duration (minimum 6 months, longer if possible).

**Lactation concerns:** Ensure proper latch (pain-free feeding). Blocked ducts cause mastitis (breast infection, fever develops). Treat with frequent nursing, warm compresses, or gentle massage. Cracked nipples—apply purified fat (lard, coconut oil) between feedings.

**Weaning timeline:** At 6 months introduce soft foods (mashed roots, grains, proteins). Continue breastfeeding 2+ years if possible—provides nutrition and disease protection.

### Immunization Gap

Without access to vaccines, children at high risk for preventable diseases (measles, whooping cough, tetanus, polio). Extra vigilance required.

**Isolation from sick individuals:** Critical—prevent exposure to infectious disease. Isolate sick family members from children when possible.

**Aggressive treatment of respiratory symptoms:** Early intervention prevents serious complications. Cough lasting >2 weeks may indicate whooping cough (particularly dangerous in infants)—requires special care with frequent small meals, oxygen support if available, observation for apnea (stopped breathing).

**Focus on nutritional support:** Strengthen immunity through nutrition. Prioritize protein and vitamin intake. Vitamin A deficiency increases measles severity—ensure adequate orange/yellow vegetables and liver.

**Early recognition of serious illness:** Children progress rapidly from well to critical. Daily health checks. Fever >39°C, difficulty breathing, lethargy, unusual rashes (particularly if petechiae—small red spots that don't blanch), or convulsions require immediate treatment.

</section>

## 6c. Hygiene Materials and Improvisation

Modern hygiene products (soap, toilet paper, toothbrush) unavailable in survival situations. Improvisation from natural materials maintains adequate hygiene while conserving limited resources.

### Soap and Cleaning Solutions

**Wood ash lye solution:** Collect clean wood ash from hardwood fires (softwood ash less effective—contains less potassium). Add water (approximately 1 part ash to 3 parts water), let settle 24 hours. Carefully pour off clear liquid (lye water—highly caustic, pH >13) without disturbing sediment. Wear gloves when handling lye. Use diluted (1 part lye to 10 parts water) for washing—removes oils effectively without chemicals.

**Bar soap from lye and fat:** Render animal fat (heat until liquid), cool slightly to lukewarm, slowly add lye water while stirring constantly. Exothermic reaction produces soap. Mix reaches paste consistency, spreads on cloth to cool and harden. Cut into bars. This crude soap effective for bathing and laundry. Shelf-life indefinite if kept dry.

**Soapwort plant:** Wild plant (Saponaria officinalis) containing saponins—natural surfactants. Crush roots or leaves, steep in hot water 15-30 minutes, strain liquid. Use for washing clothes. Mild, safe for delicate fabrics. Can be cultivated as perennial herb.

### Toilet Paper Alternatives

**Leaves:** Soft leaves (plantain, dock, lamb's quarters) function adequately. Gather bundles, store dry. Test beforehand for irritant plants.

**Water systems:** Traditional "bum gun" or water vessel—more hygienic than dry wiping, uses less material. Water stream (from pot with spout or bamboo tube) for cleansing. Dry with cloth or leaves.

**Cloth wraps:** Designate reusable cloth strips for wiping, store in bucket of water with ash solution, wash in boiling water daily. More comfortable than leaves, fully reusable.

### Menstrual Products

**Cloth pads:** Sew two layers of soft cloth (linen, cotton), insert compressed absorbent layer (cloth inner fold). Wash in boiling water to sterilize after use. Reusable indefinitely with proper care. Design allows for longer wear without discomfort.

**Leaf padding:** Use soft absorbent leaves (inner birch bark, cattail fluff, kapok plant fibers) inside cloth wrapping. Change 2-3 times daily. Dispose by burying in designated waste area away from camp.

**Blood management:** Menstrual blood odor attracts predators (bears, large carnivores) to camp. Take precautions: bury menstrual materials 100+ meters from camp in sealed bag, double-bury materials and protect camp perimeter during menstruation, increase nighttime security. Research predator behavior in specific region (seasonal patterns vary).

### Deodorant and Scent Management

**Wood ash:** Dusted on skin absorbs moisture and odors. Crude but effective. Mix with water to create paste that adheres longer.

**Baking soda alternative:** Wood ash serves similar function—absorbs moisture and odor compounds.

**Natural scent plants:** Crush dried lavender, sage, rosemary, or other aromatic herbs. Rub on skin to mask odors. Psychological benefit significant even if minor odor reduction—morale important in survival groups.

**Herbal deodorants:** Create herbal powders mixing ash with crushed aromatic plants (dried sage, thyme, lavender). Store dry, apply to underarms and feet. Reapply after bathing.

### Toothbrush and Tooth Care

**Chew sticks:** Branches from certain trees (neem, salvadora, miswak, willow) contain natural antimicrobials. Chew end to fray fibers into brush-like form, then use as brush. Prevents cavities and gum disease better than many commercial toothbrushes.

**Activated charcoal toothpaste:** Burn hardwood completely, grind ash with water and small amount of salt, add crushed herbs. Creates tooth-whitening paste. Regular use slightly whitens teeth and improves gum health.

**Tooth cleaning cloth:** Clean fine cloth wrapped around finger for rubbing teeth removes plaque and debris. Less effective than brush but functional for maintenance.

</section>

<section id="food">

## 7\. Food Safety & Hygiene

### Safe Food Handling

Foodborne illness causes 30% of post-disaster deaths. Proper handling prevents contamination:

:::card
#### Prevent Cross-Contamination

Keep raw meat separate from ready-to-eat foods. Use different cutting boards for meat and vegetables. Don't reuse marinades. Handle meat over sink to prevent drips. Wash hands after touching raw meat.
:::

:::card
#### Cooking Temperatures

Poultry (chicken, duck): 74°C (165°F) internal temperature. White color throughout, juices run clear.

Beef/pork: 63°C (145°F) for medium rare. Fully cooked pork reaches 71°C (160°F).

Fish: 63°C (145°F). Flesh flakes easily with fork.

Eggs: Yolk and white solid, not runny. 71°C (160°F) if cooking scrambled.

Leftovers: Reheat to 74°C (165°F) throughout before eating. If stored >2 hours without refrigeration, discard.
:::

### Dishwashing Without Running Water

Three-basin system:

1.  Basin 1 (hot soapy water): Remove food residue and grease
2.  Basin 2 (clean hot water): Rinse soap
3.  Basin 3 (boiling water or chlorine solution): Final sanitization
4.  Air dry on clean cloth or rack (don't use towel—spreads bacteria)

Solar disinfection: Place wet dishes in sun for 6+ hours. UV radiation kills pathogens. Effective and free.

### Food Storage Hygiene

Protect from insects: Store in sealed containers (glass jars, boxes). Keep in cool, dark place. Check regularly for pest damage.

Prevent mold: Dry grains and legumes completely before storage. Use charcoal or salt to absorb moisture in storage containers. High humidity causes aflatoxins (toxic fungal byproduct).

Animal protection: Elevate storage on shelves or hanging. Rodents contaminate 10x the food they eat with feces/urine.

Rotation system: FIFO (First In, First Out). Label storage date. Use oldest first.

### Fly Control

Flies transmit dysentery, cholera, typhoid by contaminating food with fecal bacteria. Prevention is critical:

-   Screens: Install on windows and doors (12 mesh per inch minimum)
-   Netting: Cover food during preparation and storage
-   Cleanliness: Remove food scraps immediately. Sweep floors daily.
-   Traps: Bottle trap with rotting fruit and sugar. Flies enter, drown in water at bottom.
-   Latrine maintenance: Properly sealed latrine eliminates fly breeding. Most important single measure.

</section>

<section id="vector">

## 8\. Vector Control

### Mosquito Control

Mosquitoes transmit malaria, dengue, yellow fever, Zika, and chikungunya. Elimination requires water elimination and personal protection:

:::card
#### Eliminate Standing Water

Female mosquitoes breed only in standing water. Even small pools (bucket-sized) breed millions of mosquitoes in one week. Daily inspection of camp required for standing water. Empty water containers weekly. Fix gutters and downspouts. Fill puddles with soil. Keep water barrels sealed or screened. Even bottle caps with rainwater can breed mosquitoes.

**Breeding timeline:** Mosquito larvae develop from egg to adult in 7-10 days under warm conditions (warm stagnant water accelerates development). A single breeding site can produce thousands of adults in 1-2 weeks. Conversely, elimination of standing water eliminates breeding in 10 days.
:::

:::card
#### Source Reduction Checklist

-   Drainage ditches must flow continuously (mosquitoes need stagnant water)
-   Plant vegetation at water's edge (dragonflies eat mosquito larvae)
-   Stock ponds with fish (they eat mosquito larvae—mosquito fish, tilapia)
-   Clean rain gutters monthly
-   Cover water storage completely or use screen
-   Daily walks around settlement checking for new standing water
-   Drain low spots, fill depressions, keep ground sloped for drainage
:::

:::card
#### Bed Nets

Insecticide-treated bed nets prevent 80% of malaria infections. Hang net securely before sleep. Repair holes immediately. Nets last 2-3 years. In endemic areas, bed net is single most important prevention tool.
:::

:::card
#### Smoke & Natural Repellents

Smoke: Mosquitoes dislike smoke. Smoke fires at dawn and dusk when mosquitoes are active. Use dried herbs (eucalyptus, sage, citronella, lavender).

Topical repellents: Mix coconut oil with neem oil or citronella essential oil. Apply to exposed skin. Reapply every 2 hours.

Clothing: Long sleeves and pants, especially at dawn/dusk, physically blocks mosquitoes.
:::

### Fly Control

Common houseflies transmit dysentery, cholera, typhoid, salmonella through food contamination. Control through multiple strategies:

**Prevention (most effective):**
-   Window screens: 12 mesh per inch minimum. Flies cannot enter sealed space.
-   Food protection: Mesh food tent covers meals during preparation and eating.
-   Latrine sealing: Covered latrine is best fly prevention. Flies breed in exposed feces.
-   Keep garbage buried and decomposing far from living areas

**Fly traps:**
-   **Sugar + vinegar trap:** Container with small opening, inside contains mixture of sugar water (1:10 sugar to water ratio) + 1 teaspoon vinegar. Flies enter, cannot escape. Empty daily.
-   **Fermentation trap:** Container with small opening, inside contains fermenting fruit/vegetables (banana peels, vegetable scraps). Flies attracted to fermentation smell, enter, cannot escape. Fermentation occurs in 2-3 days naturally. Highly effective and uses food waste. Empty weekly or when full of flies.
-   **Sticky traps:** Yellow cards coated with adhesive. Flies attracted to color, land, stick. Effective for monitoring populations and control in small spaces. Flies suffocate when trapped.

**Manual control:**
-   Swatter: Manual killing with fly swatter reduces population in small spaces. Morning kills more flies (slower-moving). Target sitting flies.

### Rodent Control

Rats contaminate food, damage structures, transmit plague and hantavirus. Control requires multiple strategies:

:::card
#### Trapping

Snap traps: Bait with peanut butter or fish. Immediate, humane kill. Cost-effective. Check daily.

Box traps: Cage trap allows capture alive. Transport far away or dispatch humanely. Reusable indefinitely.

Bucket trap: Rotating platform over water-filled bucket. Rat falls in, drowns. Automatic, requires monitoring.
:::

:::card
#### Prevention (Most Important)

Storage: Keep food in sealed metal containers. Rats cannot chew through metal. Elevate above ground level.

Sealing: Rats enter holes as small as 1cm. Seal cracks in buildings with caulk, metal plates, or concrete.

Sanitation: Remove food scraps immediately. Don't leave garbage exposed. Proper waste management eliminates rat food.

Cats: One cat kills 20+ rats per year. Effective, renewable rodent control.
:::

### Lice & Flea Treatment

Lice: Highly contagious. Treatment: shave head or apply neem oil daily for 1 week. Comb with fine-tooth comb to remove nits. Wash clothing in hot water. All household members must treat simultaneously or reinfestation occurs.

Fleas: Treat animals with natural oils (neem, lavender) or dust (food-grade diatomaceous earth). Wash bedding frequently. Fleas cause plague transmission—priority elimination in rats.

### Tick Removal

Proper removal: Using tweezers, grasp tick as close to skin as possible. Pull straight out with steady, firm pressure. Don't twist or jerk. Place tick in alcohol to kill. Never squeeze—can inject pathogen into wound.

Prevention: Wear long pants in tick areas. Check body and animals after being outside. Tuck pants into boots. Apply permethrin to clothing for protection.

</section>

## 9\. Quarantine & Epidemic Response

### Identifying an Outbreak

Early detection prevents epidemic escalation. Watch for:

-   Cluster of similar symptoms: Multiple people with fever, cough, diarrhea in short timeframe
-   Symptom onset timing: Many people sick within incubation period (usually 1-14 days)
-   Geographic concentration: Cases clustered in one area or institution
-   Unusual severity: More deaths than normal, faster progression

### Community Layout for Epidemic Response

Spatial separation prevents disease spread:

![Sanitation &amp; Public Health diagram 3](../assets/svgs/sanitation-3.svg)

### Isolation Procedures

1.  Separate sick individuals: Isolate 30+ meters from main camp. Can be tent, hut, or room in separate building.
2.  Restrict movement: Sick persons don't leave isolation area. Essential supplies brought to them.
3.  Dedicated caregivers: Assign specific people to care for sick (typically immune or previously infected). Minimizes exposure.
4.  Separate supplies: Isolated area has own water bucket, dishes, waste container. Nothing shared with clean zone.
5.  Protective equipment: Caregivers wear masks, cover exposed skin. Wash hands/clothes immediately after.
6.  Duration: Minimum 10-14 days after symptom resolution. Some diseases (measles) require 21 days.
7.  Post-isolation disinfection: Boil all clothing, bedding. Disinfect surfaces with bleach solution.

### Contact Tracing Basics

Identify and monitor people exposed to sick individuals. This stops outbreaks:

:::card
Interview patient: "Who were you near for >15 minutes in past 10 days?" Record names, relationships, dates of contact.

Monitor contacts: Check temperature twice daily. Watch for symptom onset. Restrict movement until incubation period passes.

Quarantine if symptomatic: Move to isolation immediately. Continue monitoring.

Release from monitoring: After incubation period (usually 10-14 days) with no symptoms, person is safe.
:::

### Mask Making

In disease outbreaks, masks reduce transmission by 50-75%. Homemade masks are imperfect but better than nothing:

1.  Use 100% cotton fabric (old t-shirt, cloth)—this filters better than polyester
2.  Cut rectangular piece approximately 20x10cm
3.  Fold in half, sew three sides, leave top open for elastic
4.  Insert elastic (from underwear waistband) or use cloth ties at top and bottom
5.  Sew opening closed
6.  Wear with seal around nose and mouth. Adjust so no gaps.
7.  Wash mask daily with hot soap and water
8.  Replace when elastic weakens or fabric tears

### Disinfection Protocols for Outbreak Areas

Surfaces: Clean with soap first, then disinfect with bleach (1 part bleach to 10 parts water) or boiling water. Focus on frequent-touch surfaces (handles, latrine seats).

Bedding & clothes: Boil for 20+ minutes. Fever-infected items must reach 65°C minimum.

Dishes: Boil in water for 10 minutes. Handles all pathogens.

Waste: Bury deeply (1m+) away from water. Burn if non-compostable. Prevent access by animals.

## 11\. Camp & Community Layout

### Ideal Community Layout Principles

Spatial organization prevents disease transmission by separating contamination sources from clean areas:

![Sanitation &amp; Public Health diagram 4](../assets/svgs/sanitation-4.svg)

### Layout Principles Explained

:::card
#### 1\. Water Source Highest

Gravity provides natural separation. Water source must be upslope from all other activities. This prevents contamination from flowing downhill into water supply.
:::

:::card
#### 2\. Living Area Below Water

Dwellings and storage are first cluster downslope from water. Sufficient distance (50m+) maintains water safety while keeping living area convenient to water.
:::

:::card
#### 3\. Cooking Area Separate

Food preparation in dedicated area maintains hygiene. Windward side from living area to prevent smoke bothering residents. Safe distance from animal areas.
:::

:::card
#### 4\. Washing Area Before Waste

Washing facilities downslope from living but upslope from latrines. Used water drains away from living area but doesn't enter groundwater near latrines.
:::

:::card
#### 5\. Latrine & Waste Lowest

Fecal contamination risk is managed by physical separation. 30m minimum from water source. Downslope and downwind from living areas. Natural drainage away from settlement. Can be >100m away.
:::

:::card
#### 6\. Drainage Planning

Establish swales or drainage paths to direct rainfall away from living area toward waste zone. Prevents surface contamination of living area. Protects against flooding.
:::

:::card
#### 7\. Wind Consideration

Prevailing wind direction matters. Animal pens and waste areas should be downwind from living area. Cooking smoke upwind means it doesn't blow into living areas.
:::

### High-Density Camp Layout

In refugee camps or emergency shelters with limited space, concentric zones replace linear separation:

-   Zone 1 (innermost): Water distribution point and health clinic. Cleanest area.
-   Zone 2: Shelters and cooking areas. Organized rows with spacing to allow air flow.
-   Zone 3: Washing and bathing facilities. Buffer from contamination sources.
-   Zone 4 (outermost): Latrines, waste management, animal pens. Farthest from water.

## 12\. Disinfection & Sterilization

### Boiling

Most reliable, oldest disinfection method. Kills all pathogens including heat-resistant spores at 121°C (requires pressure cooker).

:::card
#### Water Disinfection by Boiling

Rolling boil for 1 minute kills most pathogens at sea level. At high altitude (>2000m), boil for 3 minutes due to lower temperature. Pathogens destroyed: bacteria, viruses, protozoa (giardia, cryptosporidium).

SODIS (Solar Disinfection): Clear plastic bottles filled with water, left in direct sun for 6+ hours. UV radiation kills pathogens. Free method for emergencies.
:::

:::card
#### Instrument Sterilization

Medical instruments can be sterilized by boiling: Place in pot of water, bring to rolling boil, maintain 15-20 minutes. Cool on clean cloth. Store in clean, dry place. Prevents infection in medical procedures.
:::

### Chlorine Solutions

Chemical disinfection using chlorine is rapid and cost-effective. Kills bacteria, viruses, and some (not all) protozoa.

:::card
#### Preparing Chlorine Stock Solution

From household bleach: Most bleach is 5% sodium hypochlorite. Mix 1 part bleach with 10 parts water. This is 0.5% stock solution.

From chlorine powder (calcium hypochlorite): Dissolve 1 kg powder in 10 liters water. Stir well. Let settle 24 hours. Decant clear liquid (this is your stock solution).
:::

:::card
#### Water Disinfection Dosing

For emergency (cloudy) water: Add 2 drops of 0.5% solution per liter. Wait 30 minutes. Should smell of chlorine.

For clear water: Add 1 drop per liter. Wait 30 minutes.

If no smell after 30 minutes: Water was heavily contaminated. Add 2 more drops, wait another 30 minutes.

If strong chlorine smell: Chlorine has killed all pathogens. Water is safe. Can aerate by pouring back and forth between containers to reduce smell.
:::

:::warning
**Important:** Chlorine doesn't effectively kill Cryptosporidium (protozoa with hard cyst). For water with suspected livestock contamination, boiling is more reliable than chlorination.
:::

### UV & Sunlight Disinfection

Direct sunlight: UV radiation (especially UV-B at 280-320nm) damages pathogen DNA/RNA. Effective on clear water in clear bottles. 6+ hours of direct sun kills most pathogens. Works best at high altitude and clear sky.

Limitations: Doesn't work well in cloudy conditions. Slow process. Doesn't provide residual protection like chlorine.

### Alcohol-Based Disinfection

70% ethanol or isopropanol kills many bacteria and viruses. Does NOT kill spores or some resistant organisms (norovirus). Good for surface disinfection and hand sanitizing.

:::card
#### Making Hand Disinfectant

Mix 1 part strong alcohol (≥60% ethanol) with small amount of water and glycerin (for skin health). Apply to hands, rub until dry. Effective against most pathogens.
:::

### Vinegar & Acetic Acid

5% acetic acid (household vinegar) has antimicrobial properties but is weaker than chlorine. Useful for surface disinfection and preventing mold.

:::card
#### Uses

Surface cleaning: Spray undiluted vinegar on surfaces, let sit 10+ minutes, wipe clean. Kills many bacteria and mold.

Food preservation: Vinegar preserves vegetables and prevents pathogenic bacteria growth. Not recommended for water disinfection (too slow and incomplete).
:::

### Lime (Calcium Hydroxide) Disinfection

Quicklime (CaO) or slaked lime (Ca(OH)₂) raises pH to >13, which kills most pathogens including resistant spores.

:::card
#### Making Lime Disinfectant Solution

Add quicklime to water (ratio 1:10). Reaction produces heat—use with caution. Solution becomes opaque white. Let settle. Decant clear liquid is strong disinfectant.

Latrine disinfection: 1 cup lime solution poured down latrine every few days kills pathogens and reduces smell.

Surface disinfection: Spray lime solution on contaminated surfaces. Avoid skin contact (caustic burn risk).
:::

### Surface Disinfection Protocol

1.  Clean first: Remove visible organic matter (feces, blood, mucus) with soap and water. Disinfectant doesn't work well on dirty surfaces.
2.  Apply disinfectant: Use bleach solution (1:10), vinegar, lime, or boiling water. Cover all surfaces.
3.  Contact time: Allow 10-15 minutes for disinfectant to work. Longer is safer.
4.  Wipe/rinse: Remove disinfectant residue with clean water. Prevents future contamination.
5.  Dry: Air dry is best. If cloth, use clean towel changed daily.

### Medical Instrument Sterilization Without Autoclave

Boiling method: Most practical for post-collapse. Place instruments in pot of water, boil for 20 minutes. Cool on clean cloth. Store in clean, dry place.

Chemical sterilization: Soak in bleach solution (1:10 dilution) for 30 minutes, then rinse in sterile water and dry. Good for instruments damaged by heat.

Flame sterilization: Hold instrument in flame until red-hot (kills all pathogens). Cool on clean surface. Don't touch. Risk of oxidation on metal.

Alcohol soak: 70% ethanol for 1-2 hours. Good for between-use disinfection but not as reliable as heat for true sterilization.

### Disinfecting Hands During Disease Outbreak

Primary method: Soap + water + 20-second wash. Most reliable.

When water unavailable: Hand sanitizer (70% alcohol) or vinegar rinse.

Before medical procedures: Scrub with soap 5+ minutes, including under nails. Rinse thoroughly. Dry with clean cloth. If available, apply antiseptic (iodine, alcohol).

## 13\. Drainage Engineering

### Site Grading & Slope Requirements

Water management begins with terrain. Proper slope directs water away from structures, preventing flooding and contamination:

:::card
#### Minimum Slope Standards

2% slope minimum: This means 2cm vertical drop per 1 meter horizontal distance. Sufficient to prevent standing water and pooling. Easier to achieve on larger properties.

Why it matters: Without adequate slope, water pools near buildings. Pools become mosquito breeding grounds and introduce moisture damage to foundations. Soil around foundations becomes saturated, weakening structural integrity.
:::

### French Drain Construction

Underground drainage system for removing subsurface water that threatens foundations:

1.  Dig trench: Along building foundation, 60-90cm deep, sloped toward drainage point. Width 30-45cm.
2.  Perforated pipe: PVC or flexible drain pipe with holes. Pipe diameter 5-10cm depending on drainage volume. Holes face down to catch water.
3.  Gravel bedding: 15cm of coarse gravel (2-3cm stones) beneath pipe. Prevents soil from clogging holes.
4.  Gravel layer above: 30cm of gravel covering pipe. Creates permeable zone around pipe.
5.  Geotextile fabric: Optional but recommended. Separates gravel from soil above, preventing sediment infiltration into drain.
6.  Backfill: Topsoil above geotextile. Slope surface away from building to complement subsurface drainage.
7.  Outlet: Pipe terminates at lower elevation or daylight outlet. Can drain to swale, pond, or downstream area. Must flow by gravity.

### Swale & Berm Systems

Surface water diversion using landscape features:

:::card
#### Swale Design

What it is: Shallow, vegetated ditch that captures and channels surface water. Acts as biofilter, removing sediment and nutrients before water infiltrates.

Dimensions: 30-60cm wide, 15-30cm deep. Length as needed. Bottom width narrower than top to prevent flat bottom (collects water).

Vegetation: Grasses, sedges, rushes. Roots stabilize banks and slow water flow, allowing infiltration and filtration.

Gradient: 1-3% slope to maintain flow without erosion. Too steep causes erosion; too flat causes stagnation.
:::

:::card
#### Berm Design

What it is: Raised earthen ridge that blocks water flow. Creates barrier between clean area and contamination source.

Sizing: 1-2 meters wide at base, 0.5-1 meter tall. Compacted soil, sloped on both sides to prevent erosion.

Use cases: Separate latrine area from living area. Direct flood water around settlement. Separate livestock areas from water supply.

Vegetation: Plant trees/shrubs on berm top to stabilize soil and provide additional barrier. Willow and alder grow well in moist areas.
:::

### Culvert Construction

Underground passages for water crossing through settlements or roads:

:::card
#### Log Culvert (Simplest)

Dig channel across road or settlement area. Place large diameter logs (30+ cm) side-by-side in trench to create tunnel. Cover with gravel, then topsoil. Water flows through gaps between logs. Suitable for small streams and low traffic areas. Degrades over 5-10 years as wood rots.
:::

:::card
#### Stone Culvert (More Durable)

Dry-stack stone walls forming arched channel. Requires skilled stonework but lasts 50+ years. Arch distributes weight evenly. Opening size depends on water volume and stream velocity.
:::

:::card
#### Pipe Culvert (Modern)

PVC, concrete, or metal pipe laid in trench. Cover and backfill. Requires proper diameter sizing to handle peak flow without backing up. Inlet and outlet must be stabilized to prevent erosion.
:::

### Foundation Drainage

Critical for buildings on slopes or in wet areas:

:::card
#### Curtain Drain

Purpose: Intercepts groundwater uphill from building before reaching foundation.

Installation: Trench dug uphill, 60-90cm deep, runs parallel to building. Contains perforated pipe in gravel. Discharges downslope away from building.

Effect: Lowers groundwater table around foundation, preventing saturation and pressure on walls.
:::

:::card
#### Footer Tiles (Foundation Drains)

Placement: At base of foundation wall, along footing.

Function: Collects water seeping through foundation walls. Perforated pipe runs around perimeter, discharges to sump pit or daylight.

Sump pit: Below-grade basin at building's lowest point. Pump or gravity drain removes accumulated water.
:::

### Road Drainage Design

Prevents washout and extends road life:

:::card
#### Road Crown

Shape: Center of road is higher than edges by 2-5cm per meter of road width. Creates slope directing water to edges.

Why it works: Water runs off road surface instead of pooling. Extends road life by preventing saturation of base layers.
:::

:::card
#### Ditches

Placement: Along both sides of road at lower elevation than road surface.

Function: Collects runoff from crown. Channels water away from road toward lower areas.

Maintenance: Must be kept clean (removed debris, sediment). Clogged ditches fail. Depth 30-60cm depending on rainfall.
:::

:::card
#### Cross-Drains

Purpose: Passes water from uphill side to downhill side of road. Prevents water from running along road surface.

Type: Culverts, French drains, or swales cutting across road alignment.

Spacing: Every 50-100 meters on sloped roads. More frequent on steeper grades.
:::

:::card
#### Road Maintenance for Washout Prevention

Regular maintenance prevents total failure. After heavy rains: clear ditches of debris, fill erosion gullies with gravel, reset crown shape if flattened, repair culvert outlets that are eroding.
:::

</section>

## 14\. Sewage Systems: Progression & Design

### Technology Progression: From Pit to Wetland

Different sanitation technologies suit different community sizes and resource levels. Progression reflects improving treatment quality:

:::card
#### Pit Latrine (1 person → 50 people)

Simplest technology. Waste drops into sealed pit. No water infrastructure required. Fills in 3-5 years. Must relocate and dig new pit. Zero treatment of effluent (stays in ground). Suitable only for dispersed populations or temporary use.
:::

:::card
#### VIP Latrine (1 person → 100 people)

Enhanced pit latrine. Ventilation eliminates smell and flies. Reduces health impacts of open pits. Still no treatment. Can serve larger groups before pit fills. Better than pit latrine in dense areas.
:::

:::card
#### Septic Tank (10 people → 300+ people)

Two-stage treatment. Tank separates solids (settle to bottom). Liquids (effluent) overflow to drain field. Primary treatment only—doesn't remove pathogens completely. Requires adequate soil for drain field. Most reliable post-collapse technology where water is piped to buildings.
:::

:::card
#### Constructed Wetland (5 people → 500+ people)

Secondary treatment. Wetland plants and biofilm remove nutrients and additional pathogens. Effluent can be reused for irrigation (if properly treated). Works with septic tank pretreatment. Attractive habitat value.
:::

### Septic Tank Construction Details

The most practical large-scale sanitation system for post-collapse communities with piped water:

:::card
#### Tank Materials

Concrete (preferred): Cast monolithic or poured in-situ. Lasts 30+ years. Must be sealed—cracks allow untreated water to escape. Interior should be smooth (less settling interruption).

Brick or stone (local materials): Double walls with cement mortar. Interior rendered smooth. Takes longer to construct but uses local materials. Lasts 15-25 years.

Fiberglass (if available): Most durable (40+ years). Factory-built with proper baffle system. Expensive. Weight and installation challenges.
:::

:::card
#### Two-Chamber Design

First chamber (settling): 60-70% of tank volume. Wastewater enters, solids sink, fats rise. Most settling occurs here. Time in chamber: 12-24 hours allows good separation.

Baffle system: Vertical wall with inlet near bottom and outlet near top. Prevents scum (floating fats) from flowing to second chamber. Critical for treatment effectiveness.

Second chamber (additional treatment): 30-40% of tank volume. Further settling and anaerobic treatment. Outlet to drain field.
:::

:::card
#### Tank Sizing Formula

Base calculation: 750 gallons (2,800 liters) for 1-2 people. Add 150 gallons (600 liters) per additional person. Example: 5 people = 750 + (3 × 150) = 1,200 gallons (4,500 liters).

Conservative sizing: Larger tanks (50% oversized) reduce maintenance frequency and improve treatment. 2-3 year pump interval becomes 5-7 years.

Peak flow consideration: Total household flow (all toilets + showers running simultaneously) may be 3-5x average flow. Tank must accommodate peak without overflowing.
:::

:::card
#### Baffle Design Critical Details

Inlet baffle: Submerged opening below liquid surface. Directs incoming waste down into settling zone. Prevents churning of settled solids. Pipe extends down 45-60cm.

Outlet baffle: Similar design in second chamber. Outlet 5-10cm below maximum liquid level. Takes cleaner effluent from upper layer of second chamber.

Scum trap: Inverted T-pipe at outlet prevents rising scum (fats, oils) from entering drain field. Critical for drain field longevity.
:::

:::card
#### Effluent Distribution

Simple outlets: Single outlet to drain field works if soil is uniform. Water distributed unevenly—first section receives all flow.

Distribution box: Separate small chamber with baffles directing flow equally to multiple laterals. Ensures all drain field trenches receive equal loading. Recommended for longevity.

Dosing/pumping: For sites with poor soil, pump effluent intermittently (4-6 times daily in small doses). Allows soil absorption time between doses. Extends drain field life 2-3x.
:::

### Drain Field & Leach Field Design

Where treated wastewater infiltrates into soil:

:::card
#### Soil Percolation Test

Why test: Different soils absorb water at different rates. Sand absorbs fast; clay slowly. Percolation rate determines drain field size needed.

How to test: Dig test hole 30cm deep, fill with water, measure how much water level drops per minute after 30 min pre-saturation. Example: 1 inch (2.5cm) per minute = coarse sand (excellent). 0.5 inch per minute = sandy loam (good). 0.1 inch per minute = clay (poor—may need alternative system).

Design standard: Add 1 meter of drain field length per 0.5 person if percolation is <0.5"/min. Very slow soils may require constructed wetland instead of drain field.
:::

:::card
#### Trench Layout

Dimensions: 45-60cm deep, 45-90cm wide, length as calculated. Multiple parallel trenches for larger systems.

Spacing: 1.5-2 meters between trench centerlines. Allows soil between trenches to recover.

Slope: Minimum 0.3% slope in distribution pipes. Prevents water from pooling in pipes. Too much slope (>1%) causes hydraulic separation (water moves too fast).
:::

:::card
#### Trench Construction

Gravel layer (bottom): 10-15cm of coarse gravel. Provides permeability and prevents pipe from settling into soil.

Perforated pipe: 5cm diameter (2") for residential. Holes face down to drain into gravel. 50-80cm long sections with slight slope.

Gravel cover (around pipe): 15-20cm of gravel over pipe. Creates porous zone for water distribution.

Geotextile: Separates gravel from topsoil. Prevents soil from clogging gravel pores.

Topsoil cover: 15-20cm minimum. Should support vegetation (grass, not trees). Protects from surface contamination.
:::

:::card
#### Minimum Distances from Wells

Septic tank to well: Minimum 100 feet (30m) downslope. Safer to extend to 150 feet (45m) if space allows. Protects groundwater from untreated effluent.

Drain field to well: Same 100+ feet distance. Slope crucial—drain field must be downslope in all directions from well.

Seasonal considerations: In wet season, groundwater table rises. May reduce effective distance. Monitor wells for contamination—test annually.
:::

### Constructed Wetland System

Natural wastewater treatment combining plants, gravel, and bacteria:

:::card
#### Design Basics

Bed dimensions: 2-5 square meters per person depending on climate. Deeper in cold regions (longer retention time needed). Bed depth 30-60cm.

Gravel substrate: 3-5cm pea gravel at inlet (catches solids), 2-3cm sand in middle, 3-5cm coarse gravel at outlet. Provides surface area for bacteria.

Water depth: 15-30cm above gravel. "Free water surface" system (water visible)—simplest design.
:::

:::card
#### Plant Selection

Common species: Cattails (Typha), reeds (Phragmites), bulrushes (Scirpus), arrowhead. Must be vigorous growers that tolerate waterlogged soil and high nutrients.

Spacing: Plant density 1-5 plants per square meter depending on species. More plants = faster treatment.

Function: Plants remove nutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus) through uptake. Die-back in winter returns some nutrients to soil, but net removal is significant. Annual harvesting of plant biomass removes more nutrients.
:::

:::card
#### Retention Time & Treatment

Loading rate: Design for 5-10 days retention. Effluent moves slowly through bed. Longer retention = better treatment. Retention time = bed volume ÷ inflow rate.

Treatment performance: Removes 80-90% of biochemical oxygen demand (BOD), 70-85% of nitrogen, 50-80% of phosphorus. Kills 90%+ of pathogens (but not all—treated effluent should not be used directly for drinking without additional treatment).

Gray water reuse: Treated wetland effluent can irrigate crops that aren't eaten raw (grains, fiber). For vegetables eaten raw, additional UV or chlorination recommended.
:::

:::card
#### Wetland Maintenance

Vegetation management: Thin plants if overgrowth blocks water flow. Remove dead biomass annually. Replant in damaged areas.

Inlet/outlet: Prevent clogging. Monitor water level—should be steady. If backing up, sediment is clogging inlet.

Mosquito prevention: Keep water level steady—standing water breeds mosquitoes. Shallow zones (<5cm) attract dragonflies which eat mosquito larvae.
:::

### Composting Toilet Deep Dive

Waterless system using thermophilic composting to safely transform human waste:

:::card
#### Thermophilic Composting Basics

Key temperature: Center of compost pile reaches 131°F (55°C) with proper carbon-nitrogen ratio and moisture. At this temperature, all human pathogens (bacteria, viruses, parasites) are killed within 1-2 hours of exposure.

Time requirement: Full pathogen elimination requires 1+ year of storage after composting, even with heat treatment. Pathogens can survive in cool outer layers during initial heating.

Carbon source: Sawdust, leaves, straw added after each use. Ratio 1:1 to 1:3 carbon-to-feces by weight. Too little carbon = anaerobic smell and poor decomposition. Too much = dry pile.
:::

:::card
#### Humanure Hacienda Two-Bin System

Configuration: Two separate compost bins, each large enough for 1-2 people for 6 months of use. Example: 3ft × 3ft × 3ft (0.9m × 0.9m × 0.9m) cube.

Bin 1 (active): Use for 6-12 months. Each toilet use: feces + urine in bucket (optional—urine can go to separate container), then cover with 1-2 scoops of carbon material (sawdust, leaves). Mix occasionally to maintain aeration.

Transition: When Bin 1 is full, stop using and cover with 30cm of carbon-rich material (leaves, compost). Move toilet seat to Bin 2.

Bin 2 (passive): Used for 6-12 months while Bin 1 sits. Microbes and heat complete decomposition.

Harvest: After 1+ year, Bin 1 contains finished compost—dark, crumbly, no recognizable feces. Can be used on non-food plants (trees, shrubs) or stored 1+ additional year for food crops to meet safety standards.
:::

:::card
#### Urine Diversion

Option 1: Collect urine separately (requires diverting toilet seat). Fresh urine diluted 1:5 water is excellent fertilizer. Can be used on crops immediately (no pathogen risk—human urine is sterile if donor is healthy). Nitrogen-rich liquid fertilizer.

Option 2: Mix urine with feces in composting toilet. Increases nitrogen content, accelerates decomposition.

Hygiene: Hands-free collection reduces contamination risk. Urine container must be sealed and labeled clearly. Never confuse with drinking water.
:::

:::card
#### Safe Handling & Health Precautions

Personal protection: Wear gloves when handling fresh feces. Wash hands thoroughly after. Avoid touching face/eating.

Disease risk: Fresh human waste contains all gastrointestinal pathogens. Only safe to handle with protection. Properly composted material (1+ year) is safe to touch.

Application safety: Apply only finished, fully decomposed material to food gardens. Use only on non-food ornamentals or incorporate 1+ year before planting.
:::

## 15\. Rat & Pest Control

### Why Rats Are Critical to Control

Rats represent a public health crisis beyond direct disease transmission:

:::card
#### Food Consumption & Contamination

Direct loss: Rats consume approximately 10% of total grain production globally. In post-collapse scenarios with limited food, 10% loss is catastrophic—can mean starvation.

Contamination loss: Rats contaminate food they don't eat with feces and urine. Ratio is 10:1—for every grain they eat, they contaminate 10 more with disease-causing microbes. A single rat consumes 30-50g grain/day but contaminate 300-500g.
:::

:::card
#### Disease Transmission

Plague (Yersinia pestis): Carried by rat fleas. Causes bubonic plague (lymph node swelling), pneumonic plague (respiratory—100% fatal untreated), septicemic plague. Single infected rat can start epidemic.

Leptospirosis: Rat urine contaminates water sources. Causes fever, kidney failure, death. Transmitted through cuts/abrasions in skin or mucous membranes.

Hantavirus: Aerosolized virus from dried rat feces/urine. Causes pulmonary syndrome (40-50% fatality). No treatment. Prevention only by rodent elimination.

Typhus: Transmitted via rat fleas. Causes fever, rash, death if untreated.
:::

### Building Design: Rat-Proofing

Prevention through physical barriers is most effective long-term strategy:

:::card
#### Seal Gaps & Cracks

Gap size rule: Rats can squeeze through any opening larger than 1/4 inch (6mm). Norway rats are larger—1/2 inch (12mm) gap blocks them. But assume anywhere you can fit a pencil, rats can fit.

Sealing materials: Hardware cloth (metal mesh), concrete, steel plates. Wood and rubber are easily gnawed through. Mice can chew through newer materials—use permanent barriers.

Common entry points: Pipe penetrations, door frames (gap under door), foundation cracks, roof eaves, vent pipes. Inspect entire building perimeter.
:::

:::card
#### Elevated Food Storage

Principle: Rats can climb vertical surfaces easily, but elevated storage (2m+) prevents ground predators and is harder to access from surrounding objects.

Implementation: Store grain in bins on raised platforms. Ensure no access routes (vines, ladders, stacked objects) that let rats climb. Remove all potential routes.
:::

:::card
#### Metal-Lined Grain Bins

Design: Grain stored in metal drums or bins, lids sealed tightly. Metal prevents gnawing. Sealed lids prevent access.

Sizing: Multiple smaller containers (30-50 liter drums) easier to manage than single large bin. Allows rotation and monitoring.

Inspection: Check seals weekly. Rats will eventually find small gaps—maintain seals.
:::

### Trapping Methods

Active rodent control is essential in high-population areas:

:::card
#### Snap Traps

Design: Simple spring-loaded bar kills rat instantly when triggered. Immediate, humane, quick death.

Bait: Peanut butter most effective (aromatic, sticky—rats must chew to trigger). Alternatives: chocolate, bacon, dried fruit.

Placement: Along walls where rats run. Rats follow established travel routes. Place multiple traps (5-10 per structure) in likely paths. Spacing 2-5 meters apart.

Checking: Daily inspection essential. Dead rat decaying creates smell and disease hazard. Remove promptly. Buried or burned.

Safety: Trigger mechanism is dangerous to fingers. Set carefully away from high-traffic areas. Keep away from children/pets.
:::

:::card
#### Multiple-Catch Traps (Box Traps)

Function: Cage trap allows rat to enter but prevents escape. Can catch multiple rats without resetting.

Bait: Peanut butter or grain. Place inside trap at far end.

Checking: Multiple times daily. Stressed rats can die from shock. Overcrowding causes fights. Remove immediately when full.

Disposal: Drowning (bucket of water inside trap), carbon dioxide (exhaust from vehicle in sealed container), or manual dispatch. Humanely kill before disposal.
:::

:::card
#### Live Traps

Function: Capture rat alive in spring-door box. Can be transported and released far away (1+ km) or humanely killed.

Advantage: Allows live relocation to reduce population pressure without killing. Some communities prefer this ethical approach.

Disadvantage: Time-consuming to set/monitor/transport. Relocated rats may not survive in unfamiliar territory. Often recaptured rats are relocated multiple times.
:::

### Biological Rodent Control

Using predators to maintain population balance:

:::card
#### Domestic Cats

Efficiency: Single cat kills 20+ rats per year in active hunting. More effective in structures than fields.

Behavior: Cats are attracted to high rodent populations. Multiple cats in settlement provide continuous control.

Care: Must be fed adequate food (mice/rats don't provide complete nutrition). Breeding can become problem—consider population management.
:::

:::card
#### Terriers & Hunting Dogs

Function: Jack Russell, rat terrier, dachshund breeds evolved to hunt rats. Small, agile, persistent.

Effectiveness: Multiple terriers can clear rat infestation in buildings/fields. Less efficient than cats in houses but excellent for field work.

Drawback: Require training and discipline. Can kill chickens/other livestock if not managed.
:::

### Poisoning Risks & Safer Alternatives

Chemical poisons are tempting but create serious problems:

:::card
#### Secondary Poisoning Risk

Problem: Modern rodent poisons (anticoagulants like warfarin) kill any predator that eats poisoned rat. Hawks, owls, foxes, cats consume affected rats and die. Ecological collapse of natural predator populations.

Outcome: Removal of natural controls leads to population explosion. Requires more poison, more secondary deaths.
:::

:::card
#### Plaster of Paris Method (Safer Alternative)

Mechanism: Plaster + flour 1:1 ratio mixed with water into putty. Rats eat this as it appears to be food. Plaster hardens in stomach (dehydration), causing internal damage. Non-toxic to larger predators (plaster passes through).

Preparation: Mix 2 parts plaster powder, 2 parts flour, 1 part cocoa powder (bait). Add water to paste consistency. Form small balls, place along rat routes.

Safety: Keep away from children/pets. Plaster is non-toxic if ingested, but causes discomfort. Less effective than modern poisons but much safer ecologically.
:::

### Fly Control

Houseflies transmit fecal pathogens to food:

:::card
#### Fly Traps

Bottle trap: Glass bottle with rotting fruit/sugar water inside. Narrow neck opening. Flies enter, cannot escape. Drown in liquid. Change bait when attracts too many.

Sticky traps: Yellow cards coated with adhesive. Flies attracted to color, land, stick. Effective for monitoring populations and control in small spaces.
:::

:::card
#### Prevention Methods

Screens: 12+ mesh per inch on windows/doors prevents fly entry. Must be maintained—tears allow penetration.

Food protection: Mesh net covers over food during preparation and serving. Prevents fly contact.

Manure management: Compost manure under cover or deep (1m+) burial. Exposed manure is fly breeding ground. Properly composted manure (65°C+ 3+ weeks) kills fly larvae.

Lime dusting: Fresh lime (calcium hydroxide) powder dusted on manure kills fly larvae. Pungent smell also deters flies. Use 1-2 kg per cubic meter of manure.
:::

## 16\. Laundry & Clothes Maintenance

### Washing Without Modern Detergent

Pre-industrial washing methods remain effective with available materials:

:::card
#### Lye Soap from Ash Leaching

Production: Burn hardwood (not softwood) completely to ash. Collect ash in bucket. Add water (approximately 3 liters per kg ash). Stir and let settle overnight. Decant clear liquid (lye water—pH >13, caustic).

Soap making: Lye water + animal fat (rendered lard, tallow). Slowly heat together with stirring. Exothermic reaction produces soap. Pour into molds. Cool 24 hours, cut into bars.

Lye water washing: Use diluted lye water directly for washing (wear gloves—caustic). Very effective at breaking down oils and dirt.
:::

:::card
#### Soapwort Plant

What it is: Wild plant (Saponaria officinalis) containing saponins—natural surfactants. Crushed roots or leaves create suds.

Use: Steep crushed soapwort in hot water 15-30 minutes. Strain. Use liquid for washing clothes. Mild, safe for delicate fabrics.

Availability: Can be cultivated as perennial herb. Self-seeds prolifically.
:::

:::card
#### Fuller's Earth

Material: Clay/mineral powder naturally absorbs oils. Used historically to clean and finish cloth.

Application: Dry powder rubbed into greasy clothes, allowed to sit hours/overnight, then brushed out. Absorbs oils without water. Useful in water-scarce conditions.
:::

### Traditional Wash Day Process

1.  Sort by color & fabric: Separate whites, colors, and delicates. Prevents color bleeding. Different fabrics need different handling.
2.  Soak overnight: Clothes submerged in lye water or diluted ash lye. Loosens dirt and stains. 12 hours is ideal.
3.  Scrub on washboard: Wet cloth scrubbed against ridged washboard surface with soap/lye. Friction removes dirt. Arms get tired—this is work. Focus on stains and seams.
4.  Boil whites: Boiling white linens 30-60 minutes (in separate water) whitens and disinfects. Kills bacteria/parasites. Use small fire to save fuel.
5.  Rinse twice: First rinse removes soap/lye, second rinse removes soap residue. Clothes that are not thoroughly rinsed become stiff and irritate skin.
6.  Wring out: By hand or wringer (mechanical device). Gets water out so clothes dry faster. Manual wringing is good physical exercise.
7.  Hang dry: On lines or bushes. Sun exposure helps whiten and disinfects. Wind aids drying. Takes 1-3 days depending on humidity.

### Blue Bag: Optical Brightener

Traditional technique to keep white linens bright:

:::card
#### How It Works

Material: Ultramarine blue or indigo dye, very finely ground. Creates blue suspension when mixed with water.

Application: During final rinse, add small amount of blue dye suspension. Clothes pick up invisible blue coating. Yellow (oxidized) linens + blue = bright white appearance (optical illusion).

Result: Whites appear brighter without chemical bleach. Traditional/common method in pre-industrial societies.

Preparation: Grind indigo or ultramarine pigment finely. Wrap in muslin cloth. Dip in final rinse water. Remove cloth before drying.
:::

### Stain Removal Guide

Common post-collapse stains and natural removal methods:

:::card
#### Blood Stains

Key: Always use COLD water (heat sets protein). Soak fresh blood immediately in cold water with salt. Rub gently. Repeat until clear. For old stains, use paste of salt + water, scrub on washboard.
:::

:::card
#### Grease/Oil Stains

Method: Apply wood ash paste (ash + water) to grease. Let sit 12+ hours. Ash absorbs oil. Brush off dry, then wash normally with soap. Fuller's earth also works—sprinkle on stain, let sit overnight, brush and wash.
:::

:::card
#### Mildew Stains

Prevention: Dry clothes completely before storing. Mildew grows in damp. If mildew appears, sun exposure kills it—hang in direct sun for several days. Lemon juice + salt also kills mildew—apply, expose to sun.
:::

:::card
#### Grass Stains

Method: Soak in vinegar solution (1 part vinegar, 4 parts water) for 1 hour. Vinegar breaks down chlorophyll. Then wash normally with soap.
:::

:::card
#### Rust Stains

Method: Mix salt + lemon juice into paste. Apply to stain, expose to sun 2-3 hours. Citric acid dissolves rust. Repeat if needed. Alternative: soak in cream of tartar + lemon juice.
:::

### Ironing & Pressing

Smoothing fabrics without electricity:

:::card
#### Flat Iron (Solid Iron)

Tool: Heavy iron plate with handle, designed to be heated on stove. Heats slowly, retains heat well. Weight of iron does the work.

Use: Heat on wood stove (medium heat). Test on cloth—should be hot enough to dry water immediately, not so hot to burn. Smooth clothes on flat surface (wood table with cloth pad).

Technique: Long, smooth strokes following fabric grain. Reheating: return iron to stove when cooling. Two irons allow alternating—one cools while other heats.
:::

:::card
#### Sadiron

Design: Iron on handle frame with removable iron plates. Plates heat on stove, swap for cool ones. More practical for continuous ironing than flat iron.
:::

:::card
#### Smooth Stones

Alternative: Smooth, flat stones heated in fire. Wrapped in cloth to prevent burns. Less effective than irons but workable for light pressing.
:::

:::card
#### Starch from Potatoes

Making: Boil potatoes, mash, add water (ratio 1:4 starch to water). Let settle—starch falls to bottom. Decant cloudy liquid, concentrate by letting settle again. Use liquid to starch clothes before ironing.

Effect: Starch stiffens fabric, repels dirt, gives garments crisp appearance. Protective coating on clothes.
:::

### Clothing Repair

Extending garment life through maintenance:

:::card
#### Darning Socks

Goal: Close holes in knitted garments without patches (maintains integrity of weave).

Technique: Use needle and thread matching sock color. Weave thread back and forth across hole creating tight grid. Thread fills hole, stitches catch surrounding fabric.

Result: Sock remains functional, nearly invisible repair if done well. Skill takes practice.
:::

:::card
#### Patching

Method: Cut patch from matching or contrasting fabric. Place over hole, sew around edges with tight stitches. Can be on outside (decorative/visible) or inside (hidden).

Reinforcement: Patches on stress points (elbows, knees) extend garment life significantly. Double-patch for extra durability.
:::

:::card
#### Reinforcing Stress Points

Prevention: Before garments fail, reinforce areas that tear: pocket corners, button holes, armpit seams. Extra layer of stitching or small patch prevents ripping.
:::

:::card
#### Button Replacement

Tool: Needle, thread, replacement button (salvaged from discarded garments or carved from bone/wood).

Technique: Sew button securely with small space between button and fabric (shank) for ease of buttoning. Knot threads on back, wrap thread around shank for extra strength.
:::

## 17\. Water Quality Testing Without Modern Equipment

### Turbidity: Visual Clarity Assessment

First test for water safety is visual observation:

:::card
#### Hand-Visibility Test

Procedure: Fill clear glass or jar with water. Place hand behind glass. Can you see your hand clearly? If yes, water is relatively clear (low turbidity). If hand is shadowy/blurred, high turbidity.

Why it matters: High turbidity indicates suspended particles (sediment, algae, bacteria). Particles can harbor pathogens. Cloudy water should be filtered or allowed to settle before disinfection.

Rough standard: Turbidity >5 Nephelometric Turbidity Units (visually apparent cloudiness) requires treatment. Water that's slightly cloudy but clear enough to see through is typically <5 NTU.
:::

### Odor Test

Smell provides clues about water quality:

:::card
#### Interpretation Guide

Rotten egg smell (hydrogen sulfide): Indicates anaerobic conditions (no oxygen), bacterial activity, sulfur compounds. Not immediately dangerous but suggests stagnation or organic decomposition.

Earthy smell (geosmin): Harmless compounds produced by algae and bacteria. Indicates biological activity but not necessarily dangerous pathogens.

Chemical smell (chlorine, metallic): May indicate chemical contamination or industrial pollution. Could indicate algal toxins. Should be investigated.

No smell: Good indicator of safety. Water should be odorless or have only slight earthy scent.
:::

### Taste Test (Use Extreme Caution)

Taste is informative but potentially dangerous:

:::card
#### Safe Method

Small sample only: Never swallow test water. Rinse mouth with small amount, spit out. Even tiny taste can detect minerals/salts.

Salty taste: Indicates dissolved minerals (salinity). May indicate seawater contamination or mineral-rich soil. Unsafe for drinking—dehydration.

Metallic taste: Iron, copper, or zinc. Indicates mineral contamination. pH may be off (corrosive water).

Bitter taste: Indicates alkaline water (high pH >8). Not dangerous but unpleasant. Usually from limestone/chalk areas.

Sweet taste: May indicate algal toxins or contamination. Dangerous—do not drink.
:::

### Biological Indicators

Presence/absence of aquatic life indicates water quality:

:::card
#### Mayfly Larvae (Ephemeroptera)

Significance: Mayfly nymphs are extremely sensitive to pollution and low oxygen. If present in stream, water is generally clean and well-oxygenated.

Appearance: Small (<2cm) aquatic insects with gill filaments visible along abdomen. Look on rocks/plants in flowing water.
:::

:::card
#### Stonefly Larvae

Significance: Also pollution-sensitive. Presence indicates excellent water quality.
:::

:::card
#### Absence of Life

Meaning: If water contains no visible aquatic life (no insects, no algae, no fish), indicates severe pollution or toxic conditions. Such water is unsuitable for any use without heavy treatment.
:::

### Simple Chemical Tests

Basic chemistry using common materials:

:::card
#### Turmeric Paper pH Test

Preparation: Soak paper strips in turmeric solution (turmeric powder + alcohol or water). Dry. Turmeric is yellow normally.

Use: Dip turmeric paper in water. If paper turns brown, water is alkaline (pH >8). Maintains yellow if neutral or acidic. Brown color intensity indicates alkalinity level.
:::

:::card
#### Red Cabbage Indicator (pH)

Juice extraction: Chop red cabbage (not purple). Boil in water 10 minutes. Strain liquid. Red cabbage juice contains anthocyanin pigment that changes color with pH.

Color interpretation: Red/pink = acidic (pH <7). Purple = neutral (pH 7). Blue/green = alkaline (pH >7).

Procedure: Add drops of cabbage juice to water sample. Color change indicates pH range. Darker color = more extreme pH.
:::

### Iron & Hardness Indicators

:::card
#### Soap Hardness Test

Method: Add soap solution to water sample in test tube. Shake vigorously 10 seconds. Count bubbles.

Soft water: Forms immediate, stable foam. <60mg/L total hardness.

Hard water: Forms little foam, brown precipitate appears. >120mg/L hardness. Soap reacts with minerals before cleaning.

Very hard water: No foam at all, heavy precipitate. >200mg/L. Boiling water helps (removes some hardness).
:::

:::card
#### Iron Presence (Visual)

Indicator: Rust-colored stain in water container or on rocks. Iron oxidizes and precipitates as reddish compound.

Significance: Indicates iron content. Not dangerous to health in moderate amounts but causes staining. Water tastes metallic.
:::

### Safety Threshold Decision-Making

:::card
Safe water characteristics: Clear (can see hand through), no smell, no taste, contains aquatic life (mayflies), neutral pH (turmeric stays yellow), low soap test foam (soft water).

Suspicious water: Cloudy, foul smell, odd taste, no life, extreme pH, hard water. Requires boiling and/or filtering before drinking.

Reject immediately: Water with rotten smell + no life, or chemical contamination smell. Even boiling may not be sufficient. Use alternative source if possible.
:::

## Community-Scale Greywater Systems & Rainwater Harvesting

In settlements without access to piped water, community-managed greywater treatment and rainwater harvesting provide sustainable supplies for non-potable uses. Properly designed systems can supply 50-70% of a community's water needs for irrigation, cleaning, and livestock, reducing pressure on freshwater sources and improving overall water security. These systems integrate water reuse with public health, requiring understanding of separation, treatment, and distribution.

### Greywater Separation at Community Scale

Greywater (from washing, bathing, laundry) differs from blackwater (from toilets) and can be safely recycled for many non-drinking purposes if properly separated and treated:

-   **Definition and sources:** Greywater consists of water from sinks, showers, baths, laundry (60-80 liters per person daily). Higher in organic matter than freshwater but much lower pathogen load than blackwater. Contains oils, soaps, and minor sediment. Blackwater (toilet water) must be handled separately; mixing blackwater with greywater makes treatment impractical.
-   **Separation at source:** Design settlement with separate drainage for greywater (from kitchens, baths, laundries) and blackwater (from toilets only). Dual drainage requires planning during construction. Retrofit separation is difficult and expensive. Slope greywater lines toward treatment area at 1-2% grade (6-10 meters elevation drop per 300-meter run).
-   **Greywater collection tanks:** Central tank system (500-5000 liters) collects greywater from multiple households. Tank size: 1 day's total community greywater volume. Large capacity (5000+ liters) enables diurnal variation balancing. Tank location: downslope from generation sources, upslope from treatment/use areas. Locate away from drinking water sources (minimum 30 meters horizontal, no upslope sources).
-   **Pre-treatment screening:** Coarse screen (2-5 mm mesh) at tank inlet removes hair, lint, large solids. Clean screens weekly during high-use periods. Prevents buildup that clogs treatment systems. Debris: compost or burn (never return to system).
-   **Storage considerations:** Greywater spoils within 24-48 hours without treatment (anoxic conditions develop, odors form). Either treat immediately or maintain high oxygen levels (aeration). Covered tanks prevent mosquito breeding but allow some air exchange. Temperature below 20°C extends storage (cooler = slower decay).

### Constructed Wetlands for Greywater Treatment

Wetlands provide natural, low-energy treatment by combining biological degradation and plant uptake:

-   **How constructed wetlands work:** Greywater flows slowly through substrate (gravel, sand) colonized by bacteria and planted with water-tolerant vegetation. Microorganisms consume organic matter; plants take up nutrients; solid particles settle. Output is 80-95% cleaner than input. No chemicals, no pumping (gravity-fed). Systems work indefinitely if maintained.
-   **Layout and sizing:** For 100 people (6000 liters/day greywater), construct 400-600 m² of wetland surface (6-10 m² per person). Deeper than wide: 30-50 cm depth. Greywater flows horizontally through substrate rather than free water surface (reduces mosquito breeding). Multiple cells in series improve efficiency: first cell (48 hour residence) primary treatment; second cell polishing.
-   **Substrate layers (bottom to top):** 1) Gravel/stone foundation (10 cm, >5 cm pieces) for stability. 2) Coarse sand or gravel (20-30 cm) for drainage and microbial colonization. 3) Fine sand (10 cm) for added filtration. 4) Soil (10-15 cm) for plant rooting. Total depth: 50-70 cm. Each layer compacts during installation; add 10% extra material.
-   **Vegetation selection:** Plant water-loving species adapted to local climate. Cattails (Typha spp.): high nutrient uptake, grow in cold and warm climates. Reeds (Phragmites): even better nitrogen removal, tall (2+ meters). Rushes (Juncus spp.): colder climates. Papyrus or water hyacinth: tropical. Plant density: 1 plant per 0.1-0.2 m² (5-10 plants per m²). Root systems improve substrate porosity.
-   **Nutrient cycling:** Plants uptake nitrogen (N) and phosphorus (P)—major greywater contaminants. Harvest plant biomass annually (cut cattails, remove excess growth). This removes 20-30% of nutrient load, preventing eutrophication. Harvested biomass: compost, feed to livestock, or dry for fuel.
-   **Maintenance and operation:** Inspect monthly for standing water (indicates clogging). Redistribute flow if uneven. Remove excess sludge (>10 cm accumulated) every 3-5 years. Check outlet for channel erosion or leaks. Keep overflow spillway clear. Total maintenance: 4-8 person-hours per year per 100-person system.

### Rainwater Harvesting for Non-Potable Use

Rainfall provides free water supply when captured and stored properly. Roof catchment is most efficient; ground-level collection requires larger area but viable in humid regions:

-   **Roof catchment efficiency:** One meter of rainfall on 100 m² roof yields 100,000 liters (100 m³). Average annual rainfall varies: dry climates 250-500 mm (25,000-50,000 liters per 100 m²), wet climates 1000-2000 mm (100,000-200,000 liters per 100 m²). Assume 80% collection efficiency (20% loss to gutters, evaporation, debris). Metal roofs collect better than thatch (95% vs. 60%). Dark roofs absorb more summer heat—collect water promptly to minimize algae growth.
-   **First-flush diverter:** First rain washes roof debris (bird droppings, dust) accumulating since last rain. Divert first 2-5 mm of rainfall (from roof area) to waste. For 100 m² roof, first 2 mm = 200 liters waste. Install simple diverter: PVC pipe from gutter with manual valve, direct initial flow to ground or waste tank. After 10-20 minutes, switch valve to direct flow to storage. Prevents contamination of stored water.
-   **Gutter and downspout design:** Slope gutters 1-2% toward downspouts. Size downspouts for local rainfall intensity: 1 cm/hour rainfall needs 1 downspout per 40 m² roof area (larger pipes, larger area). Gutters: 10-15 cm wide for most applications. Leaf screens (5 mm mesh) prevent debris accumulation. Clean gutters quarterly.
-   **Storage tank sizing:** Calculate: monthly water needs vs. monthly rainfall capture. If needs exceed rainfall, size tank for 1-3 months storage to bridge dry seasons. Desert climates: store 3-6 months. Tropical climates with distinct dry season: store 2-4 months. Temperate climates: 1-2 months often sufficient. Tank size (liters) = monthly need (liters) × months storage. For 100 people, 50 liters per person daily = 150,000 liters monthly. In dry climate (500 mm/year = 42 mm/month average), roof area 300 m² captures ~10,000 liters/month. Tank: 3-6 months = 150,000+ liter tank.
-   **Tank construction and location:** Elevated tanks (2-5 meters height) provide gravity pressure for distribution (1 meter height = 0.1 bar pressure). Underground tanks cool water, prevent algae. Covered tanks prevent mosquito breeding and dust. Tank material: sealed plastic (moderate cost, 10-20 year lifespan), concrete (long-lasting, expensive), stone (historic, durable). Locate where water naturally drains toward it; avoid upslope contamination sources.
-   **Water quality monitoring:** Rainwater is generally clean but can contain bird/insect matter, algae (green water). For uses beyond irrigation (livestock drinking, cleaning), filter through sand (simple sand filter: 30-50 cm sand depth, change annually). For higher quality, add settling tank (24-hour hold before use) or slow sand filter (1 m³ per day per m² surface area). Chlorination (1 ppm) optional for additional safety.

### Community-Scale Distribution Systems

Moving treated water from collection/treatment to users requires simple gravity-fed networks or small pumps:

-   **Gravity-fed distribution:** Most sustainable—no energy input. Requires elevation difference (tank upslope of users). Typically 10-50 meter elevation drop needed. Pipe network: main trunk from tank to settlement, branches to districts or neighborhoods. Pipe sizing: 2.5 cm (1 inch) diameter handles 5-10 households; 5 cm handles 20-50 households. Slope pipe 1-2% downslope to prevent air pockets. Locate taps at neighborhood level (20-100 meters from each home), not individual household connections (reduces cost 30-50%).
-   **Public taps and metering:** Design public water points every 50-100 meters walking distance. Each tap serves 20-50 people. Provide basin for filling buckets and overflow drainage. Meter water use if rationing needed (during droughts, flow restricted 50%). Maintenance: monthly inspection of taps for leaks, seasonal cleaning.
-   **Pipe materials and maintenance:** Bamboo (renewable, 5-10 year lifespan), PVC (durable, 20+ years), or clay pipe (historic, fragile). Bury pipes 60+ cm deep to prevent freezing (in cold climates) and contamination. Surface pipes need insulation in winter. Pressurized systems (pipe fully water-filled): can deliver upslope if tank elevated sufficiently. Un-pressurized systems (open channel or gravity, air in pipe): only deliver downslope.
-   **Leak detection and repair:** Walk the entire distribution line monthly checking for wet spots, sounds of flowing water. Repair leaks within 48 hours (major water loss). Patch compound, PVC cement, or replacement sections depending on pipe material. Keep spare pipe sections (10% of system length) for emergency repairs.

### Health Regulations and Quality Standards

Non-potable water supplies require safeguards to prevent accidental ingestion and cross-contamination with drinking water:

-   **Sign and mark non-potable water:** All non-potable taps must be clearly labeled: "Not for Drinking" or "Irrigation Only." Paint pipes purple or distinctive color; never white/grey (drinking water colors). Separate piping fully from drinking water—no connections or shared portions.
-   **Testing and monitoring:** Before system startup, collect water samples for laboratory testing (if available): turbidity, bacteria (E. coli), pH, dissolved solids. For ongoing monitoring: monthly visual inspection (clear vs. cloudy), smell (no sewage odor), basic turbidity test (can see hand at 30 cm depth = acceptable). If testing impossible, over-design treatment (extra wetland cells, more filtration).
-   **Cross-connection prevention:** Install backflow preventers on greywater supply near tank (prevents treated greywater from flowing back into potable supply if pressure drops). Train community that greywater lines and taps are NOT drinking water sources. Prohibit refilling water containers from non-potable taps.
-   **Algae and odor control:** Green water (algae growth) indicates excess nutrients and sunlight. Cover tank, reduce open-water exposure time, harvest vegetation more frequently. Odors (musty, sewage) indicate anaerobic conditions or contamination. Increase aeration (add air bubbles) or reduce water age in system.
-   **Emergency protocols:** If treated water quality appears degraded (cloudy, odorous): stop use, investigate source of contamination, increase treatment (add more wetland time, intensive filtration). Communities should maintain small drinking water reserve (1-2 weeks) independent of greywater system as backup.

A community of 500 people with 10,000 m² available roof area and modest local rainfall (500 mm/year) can supply 25-30% of total water needs through rainwater harvesting, plus additional 40-50% through greywater recycling—reducing freshwater extraction by 65-80% and building water resilience during droughts.

## Understanding Microbial Biomes in Waste and Water

Different microorganisms dominate different waste and water environments. Understanding pathogen characteristics informs effective treatment strategies.

### Fecal Pathogens vs Commensal Bacteria

**Pathogenic bacteria:** Cause disease even at low concentrations (Salmonella, Shigella, E. coli O157). Must eliminate completely or reduce to <1 organism per 100mL (WHO standard).

**Commensal bacteria:** Normal gut flora (E. coli normally, Bacteroides, etc.). May number in billions per gram of feces but cause disease only in immunocompromised. Elimination not necessary if pathogenic species eliminated.

**Distinction:** Field testing cannot differentiate pathogenic from commensal. Must assume all fecal bacteria are pathogenic and treat accordingly.

### Pathogen Survival in Waste and Water

Survival varies by organism type. Understanding helps predict treatment effectiveness:

**Bacteria:** Most survive hours to days in waste, killed by:
- Heat >70°C for 30 minutes
- UV light (direct sunlight 6-8 hours)
- Chlorine (5-10 ppm for 30 minutes)
- Burial depth >1 meter (limited oxygen)

**Viruses:** More resistant than bacteria:
- Survive longer (weeks to months)
- Require higher chlorine (10-20 ppm)
- Less susceptible to UV
- Less susceptible to heat (require >80°C)

**Parasites:** Vary widely in resistance:
- Some (tapeworms, roundworms) survive months/years in cool moist conditions
- Require deep burial (>1 meter prevents transmission)
- Others killed by desiccation

**Prions (misfolded proteins):** Virtually indestructible:
- Cause spongiform encephalopathy (CJD, kuru)
- No practical treatment except incineration at 1000°C
- Extremely rare but not affected by standard sanitation

### Composting for Pathogen Control

Compost temperatures 60-70°C in hot compost pile kill most pathogens within days. Proper composting makes fecal matter safe for reuse.

**Requirements:**
- Carbon-rich material (leaves, wood shavings) mixed 3:1 with fecal matter
- Maintain moisture 60-70% (squeeze test: few drops water if squeezed)
- Turn pile weekly (aerates, speeds decomposition)
- Temperature monitoring (if available, aim for >60°C)
- Duration: 6-12 months at lower temperatures, 2-3 months if temps maintained >65°C

**Result:** Rich, dark material safe for soil amendment in gardens (use for ornamental plants in first application, safer than food crops).

<section id="seasonal-hygiene">

## Seasonal Hygiene Adjustments

Sanitation needs change dramatically with season. Adaptation prevents disease while using available resources efficiently.

### Summer Hygiene (Heat and Insects)

**Increased bathing:** Higher exertion and heat increase sweating. More frequent bathing (daily if water available) prevents skin infections. Fungi thrive in warm, moist environments—keep skin dry. Change into dry clothes immediately after sweating.

**Insect protection:** Peak insect season. Maximize netting, repellents, clothing protection. More frequent latrine inspection (flies proliferate). Empty standing water containers more frequently (mosquito development accelerates in heat).

**Food preservation:** Heat accelerates food spoilage. More frequent food storage inspection. Shorter storage duration acceptable. Fermentation/drying methods preferred for preservation.

**Wound care:** Heat increases infection risk. More frequent wound cleaning and dressing changes. Watch for heat-accelerated infection spread.

### Winter Hygiene (Cold and Isolation)

**Reduced bathing:** Cold makes bathing difficult/dangerous (hypothermia risk). Full bathing 1-2 times weekly acceptable. Increased hand/face washing critical (prevents cold spread despite reduced bathing frequency). Wash hands more frequently in winter.

**Wound care critical:** Cold reduces circulation, slowing wound healing. Increased attention to wound cleanliness. Cold injuries (frostbite) require isolation and careful hygiene to prevent infection. Keep wounds warm (not overheated) and dry.

**Clothing management:** More frequent clothing changes necessary despite cold (prevent moisture accumulation in layers). Drying clothes indoors by fire (slow drying). Rotate clothing so each piece dries completely between wearing. Wet clothing left on body accelerates heat loss.

**Respiratory disease season:** Increased transmission of colds, flu. Isolation protocols for sick individuals. Increased ventilation in shelters (despite cold)—open vents to reduce respiratory pathogen concentration.

### Spring/Fall Transitions

**Respiratory disease season:** Spring/fall transitions show increased respiratory infection transmission. Isolation protocols for sick individuals. Increased ventilation in shelters.

**Water source changes:** Spring snowmelt increases water availability but changes source (glacial melt = high sediment). Adjust filtration methods. Fall drying decreases water availability—storage becomes critical. Plan for dry season before water disappears.

**Parasite season transition:** Spring/early summer peak transmission for parasites (increased outdoor activity, more water/mud exposure). Summer focus on footwear and avoiding barefoot contact with soil.

## Mental Health and Sanitation Connection

Psychological stress impairs immune function and disease resistance. Maintaining sanitation rituals provides psychological benefit beyond physical hygiene benefits.

### Sanitation as Psychological Anchor

Daily sanitation routines provide structure and sense of control in chaotic survival situations. Handwashing ritual becomes meditative, grounding activity. Organized latrines and waste areas signal civilization and normalcy—psychological reassurance.

**Morale impact:** Groups maintaining rigorous sanitation have higher morale than groups with poor practices (beyond disease prevention benefit). Perceived control over environment improves psychological resilience and reduces despair/depression.

**Visible organization:** Establishing visible sanitation structures (latrines, handwashing stations) signals organization/control to group members. Improves confidence in leadership and group survival likelihood.

### Stress Management Through Sanitation

Personal hygiene rituals (daily bathing when possible, clean clothes when possible, neat appearance) maintain self-identity and psychological health. Neglecting personal appearance correlates with depression and hopelessness.

**Recommendation:** Encourage personal hygiene even when water scarce. Small amount of water for face/hand washing boosts morale significantly. Allocate separate container for personal washing (even if greywater), distinct from drinking water. Allows people to maintain dignity and self-image.

**Ritual importance:** Bathing becomes ceremony—structured time for self-care and psychological restoration. Community bathing (group activity) strengthens social bonds.

### Group Dynamics and Cleanliness

Groups with unequal sanitation standards develop resentment (those maintaining standards resent those ignoring sanitation). Establish clear, universal sanitation protocols. Hold community meetings to adjust standards by consensus (increases buy-in).

**Equity in sanitation:** Ensure all group members follow same standards. No special exemptions create division. Assign specific sanitation responsibilities (people feel invested in sanitation if they maintain it).

**Celebrate progress:** Celebrate small victories (day without disease cases, water system working, latrines clean). Maintains morale and demonstrates sanitation importance.

<section id="immunocompromised">

## Hygiene for Immunocompromised Individuals

Individuals with weakened immunity (elderly, chronic illness, malnutrition, taking immunosuppressive medications) require enhanced sanitation protection. These individuals die from infections that healthy people survive.

### Identifying Immunocompromised Members

Signs include: slow wound healing (takes >2 weeks for minor cuts), recurrent infections, difficulty recovering from minor illness (takes >1 week for typical cold), taking immunosuppressive medications. Elderly, very young, malnourished, or chronically ill individuals particularly vulnerable.

### Enhanced Sanitation for Vulnerable Members

**Isolation:** Physically separate from main group when possible (own sleeping area, separate eating space). Minimizes exposure to group pathogens. During disease outbreaks, increase isolation further.

**Dedicated handlers:** Assign specific person to prepare food for immunocompromised individuals. Reduces contamination exposure from multiple people handling food.

**Water quality:** Even more stringent water treatment (boiling + chemical + filtration). Never accept questionable water safety. Provide separately treated water source.

**Wound care:** Daily inspection and treatment. Address minor wounds aggressively before infection develops. Wounds take longer to heal—require extra attention.

**Nutrition:** Prioritize caloric/nutritional intake (malnutrition worsens immunosuppression). Allocate extra food if available. Vitamin supplementation critical (vitamin C, A, zinc).

**Environmental controls:** Keep area clean of dust and pathogens. Minimize contact with others during illness periods in group.

### Medication Management

Immunosuppressive medications require consistent use. In survival: prioritize these medications alongside food stores. If medication runs out, weaning requires medical guidance (abrupt stopping dangerous). Plan for continued medication availability during long-term survival scenarios.

<section id="large-scale-disaster">

## Large-Scale Disaster Sanitation Response

Sudden population displacement (earthquake, flood, hurricane) creates acute sanitation emergency. Rapid response prevents epidemic diseases.

### Emergency Latrine Deployment

Acute phase (first 24-48 hours): Minimum 1 latrine per 50 people. Rapidly built pit latrines (3-4 feet deep, 6x6 feet) suffice for initial period.

**Timeline:**
- **Immediate (hours 0-6):** Deploy portable toilets if available (chemical toilets from event supply), or dig emergency cat holes upwind/downwind from camp
- **Early response (hours 6-48):** Build pit latrines (1 per 50 people minimum)
- **Sustained (days 3+):** Increase to 1 per 20 people as population settles and use increases

### Water Distribution Centers

Establish points where people collect treated water. Advantage: centralized quality control, prevents contamination from multiple sources.

**Design:**
- Elevated tanks (gravity flow reduces contamination)
- Multiple filling stations (reduces congestion, transmission risk)
- Hand-washing station at each distribution point
- Chlorination at point of distribution (1-2 ppm for drinking water)
- Distribution schedule (e.g., 6am and 6pm) prevents overcrowding

**Monitoring:** Sample water daily from distribution points (test for free chlorine residual, turbidity, smell). Adjust chlorination if insufficient (no smell of chlorine = inadequate).

### Disease Surveillance

Assign person to monitor health status. Document any cases of diarrhea, respiratory infection, fever, rashes.

**Protocol:**
- Daily census: how many people, how many sick
- Triage: separate sick individuals physically
- Isolation: contagious cases separated from main population
- Documentation: track date, symptom onset, symptom type

Two cases of same symptoms within 48 hours indicates possible outbreak. Increase sanitation measures aggressively.

### Outbreak Response Framework

**Detection phase:** Identify unusual illness patterns. Two or more people with same symptoms within 24-48 hours indicates possible outbreak.

**Response phase:**
1. Isolate affected individuals from main group
2. Increase sanitation measures (more frequent hand washing, improved latrine maintenance)
3. Boil all water regardless of normal treatment (redundancy)
4. Improve food handling (cook everything thoroughly, cover all food)
5. Clean shared surfaces with soap and water

**Monitoring phase:** Watch for spread to other group members. Most outbreaks peak at 3-5 days then decline as intervention takes effect. Watch for secondary waves (new exposure groups).

### Psychological Resilience

Group morale critical for sanitation compliance. Poor morale → poor hygiene → disease → lower morale (downward spiral).

**Maintaining morale:**
- Establish visible sanitation structures (latrines, handwashing stations) signal organization/control
- Assign clear responsibilities (people feel invested in sanitation if they maintain it)
- Celebrate small victories (day without disease cases, water system working, latrines clean)
- Maintain routines (regular meal times, rest periods, designated prayer/gathering times)

## Disease Vector Control

Disease vectors—insects and animals transmitting pathogens—pose severe risk in unsanitary environments. Mosquitoes, flies, lice, fleas, and ticks transmit malaria, dengue, dysentery, plague, typhus, and relapsing fever. Control requires multiple strategies.

### Mosquito Control

**Habitat elimination:** Mosquitoes breed in stagnant water. Remove or drain breeding sites:

- Eliminate standing water (puddles, water-filled containers, blocked gutters)
- Keep latrines and waste areas dry (drain puddles, improve slope for water runoff)
- Cover water storage tanks with lids or fine mesh (prevents egg-laying)
- Clean water collection systems weekly (remove algae and larvae)
- Stock ponds with fish (tilapia, guppies) that eat mosquito larvae
- Maintain drainage systems (clear vegetation from channels)

**Personal protection:** When mosquito elimination incomplete:

- Wear long sleeves and pants during dawn/dusk (peak feeding times)
- Use bed nets (permethrin-treated nets most effective; even untreated nets reduce transmission 50%)
- Apply natural repellents (DEET-free: neem oil, eucalyptus oil, citronella) though less effective than chemical repellents
- Sleep in sealed shelters during high-risk hours (dusk to dawn)
- Avoid outdoor activities during mosquito hours if possible

**Community-level control:** In larger settlements, designate mosquito control teams:

- Weekly water source inspection and treatment
- Larvicide application (Bti—bacillus thuringiensis israelensis—specific to mosquito larvae, not toxic to humans)
- Community drainage projects (improve sloping terrain to prevent pooling)
- Educational programs emphasizing bed net use and water covering

**Vector surveillance:** Monitor for disease in human population. Any fever cases with headache/joint pain may indicate dengue or malaria. Track geographic clusters (multiple cases in one area suggest ongoing vector activity).

### Fly Control

Flies transmit cholera, typhoid, dysentery, and parasitic infections. Attracted to feces, open food, and garbage.

**Prevention methods:**

- Use covered latrines with sealed squat hole (no fly access to feces)
- Implement VIP (ventilated improved pit) latrines with fly screen at vent pipe
- Maintain high level of latrine cleanliness (removes odor that attracts flies)
- Cover all food and water (screens, cloth covers, containers with lids)
- Remove solid waste daily (flies breed in garbage within 24 hours if undisturbed)
- Compost food waste in enclosed bins (allows decomposition while blocking fly access)

**Active control:** If fly populations high:

- Construct fly traps: container with small holes, baited with fermenting fruit/meat, drowning mechanism (water basin at bottom). Traps catch hundreds daily.
- Deploy sticky traps (commercial or home-made: honey/jam on cardboard strips hung near food preparation areas)
- Natural predators (spiders, wasps) discourage fly reproduction—maintain spiders in food storage areas

### Lice Control

Head lice, body lice, and pubic lice transmit relapsing fever and epidemic typhus (untreated epidemic typhus fatal in 20-30% of cases).

**Prevention:**

- Maintain personal hygiene and clean clothing (wash weekly in hot water)
- Designate individual combs and brushes (never shared)
- Frequent hair inspections (visual lice or nits—egg sacs attached to hair shaft)
- Isolate infested individuals until treated

**Treatment:** If lice detected:

- Apply nit comb (fine-tooth comb) repeatedly to remove eggs and nits (most important—kills future generation)
- Natural oils: coconut oil, neem oil, or herbal preparations smeared on scalp and hair for 8+ hours, then wash out
- Permethrin cream (if available)—apply per instructions, repeat after 7 days
- Shave head if infestation severe (fastest way to eliminate all lice and nits, though socially stigmatizing)
- Treat all close contacts simultaneously (sleeping partners, family members)
- Wash all bedding, clothing, and hats in hot water

**Community surveillance:** If more than one case detected, screen entire population (check hair of all individuals). Multiple cases indicate epidemic louse-borne fever risk—immediate aggressive treatment required.

### Tick Control

Ticks transmit Lyme disease (relapsing fever-like symptoms), Rocky Mountain spotted fever, and other serious pathogens.

**Prevention:**

- Keep grass short around living areas (removes tick habitat)
- Inspect body daily for ticks (check skin creases, hairline, groin—favorite feeding sites)
- Use long sleeves and long pants when in high-risk areas (forests, tall grass)
- Apply repellent to exposed skin (permethrin on clothing, DEET on skin if available)
- Examine pets and livestock daily (remove ticks, prevent spread to humans)

**Tick removal:** If attached:

- Do NOT pull or squeeze (releases pathogen-laden fluid into bite wound, increases transmission risk)
- Apply heat (hot match to back—tick releases) or suffocate (petroleum jelly, tree sap, or oil applied to block spiracles)
- Once detached, save in container for identification if illness develops later
- Wash wound with soap and water

**Disease surveillance:** Any fever cases following tick bite exposure should be treated aggressively for relapsing fever (antibiotics essential if available; high-dose herbal preparations if not).

### Flea Control

Fleas transmit plague, typhus, and tapeworms. Primarily spread by contact with infected animals (rodents, dogs, cats).

**Prevention:**

- Prevent rodent access to food stores and living areas (seal gaps, eliminate nesting sites, maintain clean environment)
- Avoid contact with dead animals (potential plague reservoirs)
- Inspect animals regularly for flea infestations
- Prevent animals from sleeping in human sleeping areas

**Treatment of infested animals:**

- Bathe with herbal preparations (neem oil dilution or lye water—kills fleas)
- Comb repeatedly with fine-tooth flea comb (physically removes fleas)
- Isolate heavily infested animals from human contact until treated
- If animal cannot be treated, remove from camp entirely

**Personal decontamination:** After potential flea exposure:

- Bathe thoroughly (physical removal of fleas)
- Wash all clothing (kills fleas trapped in fabric)
- Monitor for fever/swollen lymph nodes (early signs of plague requiring aggressive treatment)

### Chemical Control Considerations

Commercial insecticides effective but potentially toxic. In post-collapse situations with limited supplies:

- Prioritize DDT or permethrin for high-risk situations (epidemic vector control)
- Reserve synthetic repellents for high-risk groups (healthcare workers, vector control teams)
- Use natural repellents as primary population strategy (neem oil, eucalyptus oil) to extend chemical supplies
- Focus chemicals on high-transmission periods/seasons (monsoon season, peak malaria transmission)

<section id="epidemic-prevention">

## Epidemic Prevention and Infection Control

Epidemic control requires multi-layered approach. Single-layer defenses fail; redundancy saves lives.

### Infection Control Hierarchy

**Tier 1—Prevention (Most Effective):**
- Sanitation (removes pathogens at source)
- Vaccination (creates population immunity—unavailable post-collapse)
- Isolation (separates infected from susceptible individuals)

**Tier 2—Containment:**
- Quarantine (restricts movement of exposed, non-symptomatic individuals)
- Personal protective equipment (masks, gloves reduce transmission)
- Hygiene measures (handwashing, water treatment)

**Tier 3—Treatment (Least Effective):**
- Antibiotics, antivirals, supportive care (treats symptomatic individuals, reduces mortality but doesn't prevent transmission)

Most effective epidemic control combines Tiers 1 and 2 before relying on Tier 3.

### Isolation Protocols

**Identifying sick individuals:** Daily health screening (fever checks, symptom review) identifies symptomatic cases within 24-48 hours of symptom onset.

**Physical separation:** Establish isolation area (separate tent/room/building):

- At least 2 meters from main population
- Designated caregiver (ideally single person, changes clothing/washes before returning to main group)
- Downwind from main camp (disease transmission via air currents)
- Waste disposal in separate latrine or burial pit dedicated to isolation area

**Duration:** Isolation continues until symptomatic recovery plus 24 hours symptom-free (for most infectious diseases). For some diseases (severe forms of cholera, plague), longer isolation necessary.

### Quarantine Protocols

**Exposed but asymptomatic individuals:** If someone had close contact with confirmed case:

- Separate from main group (same isolation setup)
- Monitor daily for symptoms (fever is first sign for most diseases)
- Quarantine duration depends on disease and exposure type:
  - Direct contact diseases (measles): 2-3 weeks from last exposure
  - Cholera/typhoid: 5-7 days (if no symptoms by then, low transmission risk)
  - Respiratory diseases: 10-14 days

**Asymptomatic carriers:** Some people shed pathogens without symptoms (typhoid, hepatitis). Impossible to identify without testing. Emphasize hand washing and hygiene for all population members, not just documented cases.

### Environmental Decontamination

**Surfaces and equipment:** Regularly clean shared items:

- Handwashing stations: wash daily
- Eating utensils: wash in hot water after each use (70°C+ kills most pathogens)
- Clothing of sick individuals: wash in hot water separately from main laundry
- Bedding: wash in hot water or sun-dry (UV kills pathogens)

**Waste containment:** Segregate medical waste:

- Dressings, contaminated materials: burn or deep-bury (1m+ depth)
- Sharps (needles, glass): separate container, bury after use (prevents needle-stick injuries)

**Latrine maintenance:** Even if no outbreak, maintain high standards:

- Clean accessible surfaces (around squat hole) daily
- Maintain handwashing station at latrine entrance (soap, water, cloth)
- Prevent flies (primary disease vector)

### Vaccination-Vulnerable Populations

In post-collapse without vaccines, vulnerable groups at highest risk:

**Children under 5:** Highest mortality from vaccine-preventable diseases. Prioritize:
- Nutrition (strengthens immune response)
- Isolation from sick individuals
- Aggressive early treatment of respiratory symptoms (prevent pneumonia complications)

**Pregnant women:** High-risk for severe infections. Protect through:
- Isolation from infectious diseases
- Enhanced nutrition (extra calories support immune function)
- Early treatment of any infections (untreated infections cause miscarriage/premature birth)

**Immunocompromised individuals:** If known (HIV, cancer patients, those on immunosuppressive drugs), provide maximum protection:
- Separate housing if possible
- Priority access to limited medical resources
- First access to prophylactic treatments (if available)

### Outbreak Investigation Framework

When multiple cases detected (2+ people with same symptoms within 48 hours):

**Step 1—Confirm outbreak:** Determine if cases are truly related (same disease) or coincidence. Interview patients about symptom onset, location at exposure time, food/water sources.

**Step 2—Define case:** Establish case definition (fever >38°C + diarrhea = suspected cholera; fever + cough = suspected respiratory infection). Use this to identify all affected individuals.

**Step 3—Identify source:** Interview cases about common exposures:
- Food source: Did cases eat same food? (Foodborne outbreak)
- Water source: Did cases drink from same water point? (Waterborne outbreak)
- Location: Were cases in same area at same time? (Environmental exposure)
- Person-to-person: Did cases have close contact with ill person? (Contagious disease)

**Step 4—Implement control:** Based on probable source:
- Foodborne: Stop distribution of suspect food, improve food handling, cook all food thoroughly
- Waterborne: Switch water source, boil all water, check alternate water point for contamination
- Environmental: Identify contaminated area, restrict access, improve sanitation in area
- Person-to-person: Isolate ill individuals, screen contacts for early symptoms, provide masks to healthcare workers

**Step 5—Monitor for control:** Track daily case counts. If epidemic curve flattens (fewer new cases daily), control measures working. If still rising, implement additional measures.

### Mental Health and Social Cohesion

Epidemics create stress, fear, and breakdown in social norms. Maintain community function:

**Communication:** Transparent, regular updates reduce fear and conspiracy theories. Daily briefing on case counts, deaths, recovered individuals, and control measures.

**Normalcy:** Maintain routines (meals, rest, work assignments) despite illness crisis. Normalcy provides psychological grounding.

**Support systems:** Designate care for isolated individuals (food delivery, emotional support). Death management (burial rites, grief support) preserves social cohesion.

**Trust in response:** Community leaders must visibly practice the same sanitation/isolation rules they demand of others. Perceived unfairness destroys compliance.
