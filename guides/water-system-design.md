## Overview

A reliable water supply is the foundation of any settlement. Without modern mechanical systems, gravity-fed networks provide the most practical solution: collecting water from higher elevations (springs, streams, rainfall) and distributing it downslope to community members. This guide covers identifying water sources, designing gravity-fed systems, storage solutions, and simple water treatment methods to ensure safe, reliable water availability.

A well-designed water system can supply 10–100 liters per person daily with minimal maintenance, serving households, livestock, agriculture, and firefighting needs.

## Identifying and Assessing Water Sources

The first step in designing a new water system is locating a reliable source. If the system already exists and the issue is contamination, low flow, leaks, or repeated outages, start with [Water System Lifecycle](../water-system-lifecycle.html) and then use [Water System Failure Analysis](../water-system-failure-analysis.html) if you need symptom-by-symptom diagnosis.

### Source Types and Reliability

**Springs:**
- Most reliable year-round source
- Water emerges from ground at consistent flow
- Usually protected from surface contamination
- Can supply 1–100 liters per minute, depending on the spring
- Location: hillsides, valley floors; often marked by vegetation clusters

**Streams and rivers:**
- Reliable if not fully dependent on seasonal rainfall
- Flow varies seasonally (winter: high; summer: lower)
- More susceptible to surface contamination (animals, human activity upstream)
- Can be dammed to create reliable supply during dry periods

**Rainfall collection:**
- Variable by season and region
- Useful supplement but not reliable as sole source
- Can supply 30–50 liters per square meter of roof per 25 mm of rainfall

**Wells:**
- Access groundwater below surface
- More stable than surface sources; less contamination
- Require more labor to construct (digging/boring)
- Flow varies by depth and geology

### Assessing Source Quality and Quantity

**Flow measurement:**
1. Collect water in a container of known volume
2. Measure the time to fill
3. Flow rate (liters/minute) = volume / time
4. Multiply by 60 for liters/hour or 1440 for liters/day
5. Seasonal measurement (dry and wet seasons) reveals reliability

**Source analysis:**
- **Elevation:** How high above the settlement? Determines potential distribution pressure
- **Clarity:** Is water clear or turbid (cloudy)? Cloudiness indicates sediment or contamination
- **Taste and smell:** Unusual taste or smell (sulfur, iron, decay) may indicate contamination
- **Use by local animals:** If animals drink from the source, it may be reasonably safe; if they avoid it, investigate why
- **Distance:** Longer distances mean longer piping/channels; contamination may occur during transport

:::info-box
**Local knowledge:** Ask residents about water sources; they often know from generations of experience which sources are reliable and safe. This is more valuable than any guidebook.
:::

## Gravity-Fed Distribution Systems

Gravity provides free, reliable pressure for water distribution. A system that works downhill requires no pumps.

### System Design Principles

**Elevation difference (head):**
- Water naturally flows downhill
- 1 meter of elevation difference creates about 0.1 bar of pressure (roughly 1 atmosphere per 10 meters)
- 10 meters of elevation = comfortable pressure for distribution
- 20+ meters = very good pressure (enough to supply multiple outlets with strong flow)

**Pipe or channel selection:**
- **Gravity flow open channels:** Ditches, stone-lined channels; simplest; no pressure needed
- **Buried pipes:** More reliable (protected from contamination, freezing); requires pressure-resistant material
- **Elevated systems:** Tanks at high elevation; distribute via gravity to lower areas

**Flow rate estimation:**
- Pipe diameter, material, and elevation drop determine flow
- Larger pipes = higher flow but more expensive
- Rougher pipes (wood, stone channels) = higher friction, lower flow
- Smoother pipes (ceramic, metal) = lower friction, higher flow

### Open Channel Systems (Simplest)

**Gravity flow channel design:**
1. Locate the source and settlement
2. Determine elevation difference
3. Design a channel sloping gently from source to settlement (slope: 0.5–2% is typical; less steep = less erosion, but longer channel)
4. Channel can be:
   - Natural streams/existing watercourses (lowest cost)
   - Constructed trenches lined with stone, clay, or wood
   - Elevated channels (if necessary to cross terrain)

**Channel construction:**
1. Dig a trench from source to settlement (slope consistently downward)
2. Line with clay, stone, or wood to prevent erosion and reduce seepage
3. Width: 30–60 cm; depth: 20–40 cm (sufficient for household-scale distribution)
4. Wider channels distribute over larger areas (less pressure, gentler flow)
5. Narrower channels create faster flow and more pressure

**Advantages:**
- Simplest to construct
- Least maintenance
- Easy to repair

**Disadvantages:**
- Water exposed to contamination
- Algae growth; mosquito breeding
- Water loss from evaporation
- Freezes in winter (in cold climates)

### Pipe Systems (More Reliable)

**Pipe materials:**

**Ceramic or clay pipes:**
- Durable; 50–100 year lifespan
- Moderate cost if ceramic is available locally
- Smooth interior (low friction loss)
- Brittle; requires careful handling and joint protection

**Wood pipes:**
- Split logs with hole bored through center
- Moderate durability (rot over 10–20 years)
- Must be regularly sealed
- Surprisingly effective and widely used historically

**Bamboo:**
- Natural tubes; effective in tropical regions
- 10–20 year lifespan
- Easy to split and connect
- Diameter 3–10 cm

**Stone or slate:**
- Half-pipes or channels lined with slate
- Very durable; lasts centuries
- Rough interior (higher friction); requires larger diameter for equivalent flow
- Labor-intensive to construct

**Metal pipes (if available):**
- Iron or copper; highly durable
- Smooth interior; minimal friction
- Expensive and difficult to produce primitively
- Rust prevention requires maintenance

**Pipe sizing:**
- Household supply: 2–5 cm diameter pipes
- Community supply: 5–10 cm or larger
- Larger pipes have lower friction loss but higher cost

**Pipe junctions and joints:**
- Overlapped and sealed with tar or clay
- Leather or metal collars tightened around joints
- Slip-fits with clay and cordage binding
- Main goal: prevent leaks and ensure tight seals

### Elevated Storage and Pressure Systems

**Elevated tank design:**
1. Build a tank (ceramic, wood-lined, or stone) at high elevation (10–20 meters above the settlement)
2. Fill via gravity from the source
3. Distribute via gravity from the tank
4. Provides constant pressure and allows filtration before distribution

**Tank specifications:**
- Volume: 10–20 liters per person served per day (accounts for daily use and some storage)
- Material: Must hold pressure without leaking (ceramic, wood-lined with clay, or lined stone)
- Overflow: Design a way for excess water to safely exit (prevents overflow damaging the tank)
- Drain: Provide access for cleaning and repair

**Distribution from the tank:**
- Outlet ports at different heights for different uses (upper ports = higher pressure for washing; lower ports = lower pressure for slow fill)
- Multiple small outlets = distributed pressure; reduces pipe sizing requirements

:::warning
Elevated structures require very solid construction. A tank failure can cause flooding and structural damage. Regular inspection is essential.
:::

## Simple Water Treatment Methods

Raw water may contain sediment, pathogens, and other contaminants. Simple treatment improves safety.

### Filtration

**Sand filtration:**
1. Create a container with multiple layers:
   - Bottom: 5 cm coarse gravel
   - Middle: 15–20 cm clean sand
   - Top: 5 cm fine sand or cloth
2. Pour water in the top
3. Water percolates through, leaving sediment behind
4. Collect the filtered water at the bottom
5. Replace sand/gravel when flow becomes too slow (filtered sediment clogs the layers)

**Cloth filtering:**
- Stretch clean cloth (linen or cotton) over a container opening
- Pour water through the cloth
- Removes larger particles but not pathogens
- Quick and simple; limited effectiveness

**Multi-stage filtration:**
1. First stage: coarse screen (removes large debris)
2. Second stage: sand filtration (removes sediment)
3. Third stage: cloth or fine sand (removes finer particles)
4. More effective but slower

### Settling

**Allowing sediment to settle:**
1. Fill a container with cloudy water
2. Allow to sit undisturbed for 24 hours
3. Heavy sediment settles to the bottom
4. Carefully pour off the clearer water from the top
5. Discards the sediment-heavy bottom portion
6. Simple and effective for sediment removal; doesn't kill pathogens

### Boiling

Boiling kills pathogens and makes water safer for drinking.

**Boiling method:**
1. Heat water to a rolling boil
2. Maintain boiling for 1–5 minutes (longer at high altitude where water boils at lower temperature)
3. Cool and use
4. Energy-intensive but very effective

**When to boil:**
- Water from surface sources (streams, questionable wells)
- After unusual weather events or contamination incidents
- For drinking water (cooking water can be less cautious)

### Solar Disinfection (SODIS)

A simple, free method of disinfection using sunlight.

**Process:**
1. Fill clear plastic bottles or containers with water
2. Place in direct sunlight for a full day (8–10 hours)
3. UV radiation from the sun kills pathogens
4. More effective on clear days; less effective on cloudy days
5. Water temperature rising also aids disinfection

**Effectiveness:**
- Kills most pathogens (bacteria, viruses, some protozoans)
- Not 100% effective; provides risk reduction
- Inexpensive and practical in sunny regions

### Chlorination (If Chlorine Available)

Chlorine (produced industrially or from salt) kills pathogens.

**Application:**
1. Chlorine is produced by electrolyzing salt water (requires electrical current—not practical in primitive settings)
2. If chlorine becomes available, use sparingly: 1–2 mg/liter
3. Chlorinated water may have a slight chemical taste

**Historical alternatives:**
- Silver or copper (boiling water in metal vessels) provides some disinfection
- Salt (added to water) provides some preservation (not primary disinfection)

### Combined Treatment

**Most effective approach:**
1. Settling (remove sediment)
2. Filtration (remove remaining particles)
3. Boiling (kill pathogens) or solar disinfection
4. This provides reliable safe water

## Distribution System Components

A complete water system includes components beyond pipes.

### Collection and Entry Points

**Spring boxes:**
- Protective structure around the spring
- Prevents surface contamination
- Allows sediment to settle before water enters pipes
- Simple concrete or stone construction

**Intake structures:**
- Screens to prevent large debris from entering the system
- Multiple intake points (one at low flow level, one at higher level) for consistent supply in varying flow conditions

### Distribution Outlets and Access Points

**Household taps:**
- Simple valves that can be closed to stop water flow
- Positioned at convenient locations
- May include filtration before the tap

**Public water points:**
- Shared access locations for the community
- Usually includes a basin or trough for water collection
- May include laundry or washing areas

**Watering points:**
- For livestock or irrigation
- Usually lower pressure; can be positioned lower in the system
- May have separate, less-treated water

### Maintenance Access Points

**Cleaning ports:**
- Shut-off valves at regular intervals
- Allow draining of the system for cleaning and repair
- Collect sediment that accumulates in pipes

**Air valves:**
- Release trapped air from pipes (air pockets reduce flow)
- Usually at high points in the system
- Manual or automatic

**Pressure relief:**
- Allow excess pressure to safely escape
- Prevent pipe burst from pressure buildup
- Important for elevated systems

## System Design for Specific Needs

### Household System (Single Family)

**Requirements:** 20–50 liters per day per person
- Small spring or stream 10+ meters uphill from the house
- 2–3 cm diameter pipe or channel
- Simple filtration
- Boiling for drinking water

**Setup:**
1. Collect water from source
2. 5–10 meter drop provides adequate gravity pressure
3. Distribute to house via single pipe
4. Simple valve to control flow

### Community Water System (50–100 People)

**Requirements:** 2000–5000 liters per day
- More substantial source (spring with 10+ liters/minute or small stream)
- 5–10 cm diameter pipes
- Elevated storage tank (1000–2000 liters)
- Multiple public distribution points

**Setup:**
1. Collect from source
2. Transport via gravity to elevated tank
3. Distribute from tank to community
4. Multiple taps located throughout settlement
5. Regular maintenance and inspection

### Irrigation System

**Requirements:** Variable; typically 5000–50000 liters per day for productive agriculture
- Reliable water source (stream, well, or large spring)
- Open channel distribution (lower cost than pipes for large volumes)
- Simple on/off gates to direct water to specific fields
- Seasonal adjustment (summer irrigation; winter dormancy)

**Design considerations:**
- Larger channels reduce friction loss over distance
- Terraced distribution (upper fields get water first; lower fields receive overflow)
- Efficient if built with slope matching the terrain

## System Maintenance and Troubleshooting

### Regular Maintenance

**Monthly inspection:**
- Walk the entire system
- Check for leaks or unusual water loss
- Clear debris from channels
- Verify all taps work
- Listen for gurgling or air sounds (indicates air in pipes)

**Seasonal maintenance:**
- Drain and dry system before freezing (in cold climates) to prevent ice damage
- Clean sediment from settling basins and filtration layers
- Replace worn pipes or seals
- Inspect tanks for structural integrity

**Annual major work:**
- Replace filtration sand/gravel
- Deep cleaning of storage tanks
- Repair structural damage

### Troubleshooting

**Problem: Low flow or no flow**
- Cause: Sediment clog in pipes; air lock
- Fix: Drain system; flush with water; bleed air from highest point

**Problem: Water is cloudy or discolored**
- Cause: Pipe corrosion (metal pipes); algae growth; sediment
- Fix: Check source; improve filtration; identify pipe material causing discoloration

**Problem: Leaking joints**
- Cause: Seal failure; thermal expansion; age
- Fix: Tighten binding materials; re-seal with tar or clay

**Problem: Water tastes or smells bad**
- Cause: Stagnant water; algae; chemical contamination
- Fix: Drain and refill; improve source; check for nearby contamination sources

**Problem: System freezes in winter**
- Cause: Still water in exposed pipes
- Fix: Drain system before freeze; insulate pipes; keep water circulating

<section id="conclusion">

## Conclusion

A reliable water system transforms settlement feasibility, enabling sustained population, agriculture, and sanitation. Gravity-fed systems require initial investment in planning and construction but then operate essentially for free, requiring only maintenance. Begin with assessment of local water sources, design a system appropriate to your needs and topography, and construct with durable materials. A well-designed system will serve your community for generations, providing the foundation for health and prosperity.
