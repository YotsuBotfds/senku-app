## Overview

Most coughs, colds, stuffy noses, and sore throats are caused by viral upper respiratory infections and get better with time, rest, fluids, and comfort measures. The goal of home care is to help you breathe, sleep, swallow, and hydrate while the illness runs its course.

This guide is for questions like:

- How do I soothe a sore throat?
- What helps a cough at night?
- How do I make steam inhalation?
- When should I worry?

:::tip Quick routing cue
Use this guide for everyday red-routed symptom questions like: sore throat care, overnight cough relief, steam support, or deciding when chest/fever symptoms need escalation.
:::

If the concern is medication overlap from cold products, use this guide only for ordinary symptom relief and label-checking. If someone is confused, hard to wake, breathing slowly, has pinpoint pupils, or may have taken too much acetaminophen, dextromethorphan, decongestant, or another pill/cough syrup dose, leave home-care mode and use the overdose route instead.

## When Home Care Is Reasonable

Home care is usually appropriate when symptoms are mild to moderate and the person can:

- Breathe comfortably
- Drink fluids and keep them down
- Speak in full sentences
- Swallow saliva without drooling
- Stay awake, oriented, and improving slowly

Common ordinary cold symptoms include:

- Runny or stuffy nose
- Mild sore throat
- Mild cough
- Mild fever
- Body aches
- Sneezing
- Hoarse voice

:::info
If symptoms are steadily improving over several days, that is usually a good sign. If they are worsening after getting better, think about a secondary problem such as sinus infection, pneumonia, asthma flare, or strep throat.
:::

## The Core Home-Care Plan

If you want the shortest possible answer, start here:

1. Rest.
2. Drink enough fluids that urine stays pale yellow.
3. Use warm liquids if they feel soothing.
4. Use honey for cough if the patient is older than 1 year.
5. Use salt-water gargles for throat pain.
6. Use steam or a humidifier for congestion, but avoid burns.
7. Use acetaminophen or ibuprofen for pain or fever if normally safe for that person.

## How Do I Soothe a Sore Throat?

Sore throats usually feel better when the throat stays moist and when irritation is reduced.

### Fast relief

- Sip warm water, tea, broth, or other warm non-irritating fluids.
- Gargle with salt water: about 1/2 teaspoon salt in a cup of warm water.
- Use lozenges or hard candy if the person is old enough to safely suck them.
- Eat soft foods if swallowing is painful.
- Avoid very spicy, acidic, or scratchy foods if they sting.

### Honey for throat and cough

Honey can reduce throat irritation and nighttime cough.

- Give 1 to 2 teaspoons as needed, especially before sleep.
- Do not give honey to children under 1 year old because of botulism risk.
- Honey can be taken plain or mixed into warm water or tea.

### What helps most

The throat often hurts more when the air is dry. A humid room, extra fluids, and throat coating from honey or warm drinks usually help more than “pushing through it.”

## What Helps a Cough at Night?

Night cough is often worse because the throat dries out and mucus pools when lying flat.

### Things that help

- Sleep with the head slightly elevated.
- Drink warm fluids before bed.
- Use honey before bedtime if age-appropriate.
- Run a cool-mist humidifier or use steam from a shower.
- Clear the nose before sleep with saline spray or a gentle rinse.

### Things that make night cough worse

- Dry air
- Smoking or vaping
- Lying completely flat
- Dehydration
- Postnasal drip from congestion

:::tip
If a cough is mainly from postnasal drip, nasal relief often works better than cough medicine.
:::

## How Do I Make Steam Inhalation?

Steam can temporarily loosen mucus and make breathing feel easier. It does not cure the illness, but it can help comfort.

### Safer ways to use steam

- Take a warm shower and breathe the moist air.
- Sit in a bathroom with the shower running hot, but do not stand in scalding water.
- Use a clean bowl of hot water at a safe distance and breathe normally over the rising steam.
- Use a humidifier in the room if available.

### Safe method for a bowl of steam

1. Put hot water in a stable bowl or basin.
2. Set it on a table, not on your lap.
3. Sit back far enough that the steam feels warm, not painful.
4. Breathe gently for 5 to 10 minutes.
5. Stop if you feel dizzy, short of breath, or too hot.

### Important cautions

- Do not put your face directly over boiling water.
- Do not let children use unattended steam bowls.
- Do not use steam if it makes coughing, wheezing, or breathing worse.
- Avoid essential oils if they irritate the nose or throat.

## Warm Fluids, Hydration, and Food

Hydration matters because fever, mouth breathing, and coughing dry you out.

### Good choices

- Water
- Broth
- Warm tea
- Warm water with honey and lemon if lemon does not sting
- Oral rehydration solution if intake has been poor

### Practical hydration target

- Drink regularly throughout the day.
- Aim for light yellow urine.
- If urine becomes dark, concentrated, or rare, increase fluids.

### Eating with a cold

You do not need a perfect diet during a short illness, but calories help recovery. Soft, easy foods are often best:

- Soup
- Oatmeal
- Yogurt if tolerated
- Toast
- Rice
- Bananas
- Applesauce

## Simple Symptom Relief

### For congestion

- Saline nasal spray or rinse
- Steam or humid air
- Sleeping with the head elevated
- Gentle nose blowing, one nostril at a time

### For mild fever and aches

- Rest
- Fluids
- Light clothing
- Acetaminophen or ibuprofen if the person normally can take them

### For cough

- Honey for children over 1 year and adults
- Warm fluids
- Humid air
- Throat lozenges for older children and adults

### For throat pain

- Salt-water gargles
- Warm fluids
- Honey
- Soft foods

### For dry, irritated airways

- Use a humidifier
- Avoid smoke
- Avoid very cold, dry air when possible

## Medicine Basics for Ordinary Cold Symptoms

Over-the-counter medicines can help, but they do not cure the infection.

- Acetaminophen helps fever and pain.
- Ibuprofen helps fever, pain, and inflammation.
- Decongestants may help a blocked nose but can raise heart rate or blood pressure.
- Cough suppressants may help some dry coughs, especially at night.

:::warning
Be careful with combination cold medicines. Many products contain overlapping ingredients, especially acetaminophen. Double-check labels so you do not accidentally take too much of one medicine.
:::

### Important cautions

- Do not give aspirin to children or teenagers with viral illness.
- Use ibuprofen cautiously if the person is dehydrated, has kidney disease, stomach ulcers, or cannot drink well.
- If the person has asthma, chronic lung disease, heart disease, pregnancy, or takes regular medications, check whether a medicine is safe before using it.

## What Not to Do

- Do not use boiling steam directly on the face.
- Do not force food or fluids if the person is choking or cannot swallow safely.
- Do not ignore worsening breathing.
- Do not assume every sore throat is “just a cold.”
- Do not start antibiotics for a routine cold unless a clinician has identified a bacterial cause.

## When Should I Worry?

Seek urgent medical care if any of these are present:

- Trouble breathing, wheezing, stridor, or lips turning blue
- Chest pain
- Confusion, fainting, or hard-to-wake behavior
- Drooling, inability to swallow, or severe throat swelling
- Severe dehydration: very dry mouth, no urine for many hours, dizziness, weakness
- Fever above 103 F / 39.4 C, or fever that keeps rising
- Fever lasting more than 3 days, especially if the person looks worse
- Cough lasting more than 3 weeks
- Coughing up blood
- Severe one-sided throat pain or swelling
- Stiff neck, severe headache, rash, or light sensitivity
- Symptoms that get better and then suddenly worsen again
- A child who is breathing fast, using extra muscles to breathe, or not drinking

:::danger
If the person has breathing difficulty, blue lips, confusion, or cannot swallow liquids, do not keep doing home care. Get emergency help now.
:::

## When It Might Be More Than a Cold

Think about another cause when the pattern does not fit a simple viral cold:

- **Strep throat:** sudden severe throat pain, fever, swollen tender neck nodes, little or no cough
- **Flu:** more abrupt fever, body aches, marked fatigue
- **COVID-19:** can look like a cold, flu, or chest illness
- **Asthma flare:** wheeze, tight chest, cough worse at night or with exercise
- **Pneumonia:** fever, cough, shortness of breath, chest pain, fatigue
- **Croup in children:** barking cough, hoarse voice, noisy breathing
- **Allergies:** itchy eyes, sneezing, clear runny nose, no fever

## A Simple Decision Rule

If the person is:

- Breathing okay
- Drinking okay
- Mentally normal
- Fever is mild or improving
- Symptoms are gradually easing

then home care is usually appropriate.

If the person is:

- Getting worse
- Not drinking
- Struggling to breathe
- Confused
- Or has a red-flag symptom from the list above

then they need medical evaluation.

## Quick Reference

- **How do I soothe a sore throat?** Warm fluids, honey if older than 1 year, salt-water gargles, lozenges, soft foods.
- **What helps a cough at night?** Honey, elevated head, humid air, warm drink, nasal clearing before bed.
- **How do I make steam inhalation?** Use a warm shower, humidifier, or a bowl of hot water kept at a safe distance.
- **When should I worry?** Trouble breathing, drooling, confusion, severe dehydration, chest pain, blue lips, or symptoms that worsen instead of improve.
