<section id="overview">

## Overview

Not all fire victims are burn victims. Many die from **smoke inhalation**, **carbon monoxide (CO)**, or mixed **fire-gas exposure** before flames ever touch them.

Common scenarios:

- Structure fire in an enclosed room
- Generator exhaust in or near a house
- Charcoal stove, brazier, or grill used indoors
- Vehicle or engine exhaust in a garage
- Smoldering fire involving plastics, foam, wiring, or upholstery

This guide is meant for prompts such as:

- "Someone inhaled smoke from a house fire." -> use this guide
- "We used charcoal indoors and now everyone is dizzy." -> use this guide
- "Generator exhaust got into the shelter." -> use this guide
- "What do I do after smoke inhalation?" -> use this guide
- "dizzy or nauseous near a running stove or heater" -> treat as CO exposure until proven otherwise
- "everyone in the room has a headache at the same time" -> suspect CO and use this guide
- "headache that started after lighting a fire indoors" -> stop the fire, get fresh air, and stay with this guide
- "confused or sleepy after using a charcoal burner or generator inside" -> use this guide
- "feeling sick around the stove but no one is burned" -> treat as CO or mixed fire gas exposure until proven otherwise
- "stove making me sick" or "heater making everyone dizzy" -> stop the fire, evacuate, and use this guide first
- "smoke smell indoors", "burning fuel smell", "headache near stove", or "heater making me dizzy" -> stop the fire and stay with this guide
- "headache near fire or heater indoors" -> stop the fire, ventilate if you can do so safely, and follow this guide; for stove fixes after the scene is safe see [`cookstoves-indoor-heating-safety.md`](cookstoves-indoor-heating-safety.md)
- "smoke smell indoors that will not clear" -> if anyone has symptoms or the source is still active, evacuate and stay with this guide; for chimney and draft fixes after the scene is safe see [`cookstoves-indoor-heating-safety.md`](cookstoves-indoor-heating-safety.md)
- "cold smoky room, cough, and headache" -> indoor-air danger first; get to fresh air before troubleshooting heat
- "someone got dizzy after running the stove" or "do we ventilate first or get out first" -> get people out first; ventilate only if it does not delay leaving
- "ventilation is blocked and we are worried about carbon monoxide" -> this guide owns the first move: leave the exposure area and get fresh air
- "room is smoky and stuffy" -> if anyone has headache, cough, dizziness, nausea, confusion, shortness of breath, or unusual sleepiness, use this guide before ventilation design or asthma/wildfire advice
- "charcoal indoors" -> use this guide immediately; do not keep testing the air or stay in the room
- "bad indoor air" or "room feels stale after running a stove" -> if anyone has symptoms, use this guide; if nobody is symptomatic and you need ventilation design help, see [`ventilation-air-systems.md`](ventilation-air-systems.md)

First step: if anyone has headache, cough after smoke exposure, dizziness, nausea, confusion, unusual sleepiness, shortness of breath, or collapse, get out to fresh air and stay with this guide. Do not pause to decide whether it is "just heating trouble," to open windows, or to tune the stove unless doing so does not delay escape. If the room is smoky but nobody is yet symptomatic, use the cookstoves guide for the draft, chimney, and ventilation fix after the scene is safe.

If the problem is general indoor airflow without active smoke or symptoms, hand off to the ventilation guide instead of trying to solve it here.

For stove, chimney, and make-up-air fixes after the scene is safe, see the cookstoves guide. For broader room-air or building-air design when nobody is actively exposed, see the ventilation guide.

For the chemistry of how combustion produces CO, CO2, and other fire gases, see [`combustion-fire-chemistry-basics.md`](combustion-fire-chemistry-basics.md). For safe fuel storage that prevents these scenarios, see [`fuel-storage-handling.md`](fuel-storage-handling.md).

<section id="scene-extraction">

## Scene Extraction Priorities

### 1. Do not create a second victim

- If the scene is still full of smoke or gas, rescuers need respiratory protection or must limit exposure to a rapid extraction.
- Do not re-enter a smoke-filled space without a plan, backup, and a clear exit.
- Multiple collapsed victims in one room strongly suggests toxic gas, not just panic.

### 2. Move the patient to fresh air immediately

- Outside is better than a doorway.
- Shut off the source if you can do it safely: generator, charcoal pan, engine, stove.
- Remove hot or smoldering clothing after the patient is out of danger.

### 3. Triage for airway first, not burns first

In smoke and fire-gas exposure, airway injury can worsen fast. A patient with only small skin burns may still need urgent evacuation if breathing is changing.

If you are only looking for stove troubleshooting or chimney tuning, this is not the right section. Use the cookstoves guide after the scene is safe.

</section>

<section id="recognition">

## Recognition: CO, Airway Burns, and Mixed Fire Gas

### Signs of significant smoke inhalation

- Cough after exposure
- Hoarseness or voice change
- Soot around mouth or nose
- Facial burns or singed nasal hair
- Wheeze, fast breathing, or shortness of breath
- Confusion, agitation, or collapse

### Carbon monoxide clues

CO is colorless and odorless. You diagnose it from the story and the pattern:

- Enclosed-space fire or engine exhaust
- Charcoal heater, grill, or brazier used indoors
- Generator running near doors, windows, crawlspaces, or attached structures
- Multiple people with headache, dizziness, nausea, or confusion at the same time
- Pets also affected

Do **not** rely on "cherry red skin." That classic sign is unreliable in real patients.

### Cyanide clues in fire victims

Suspect cyanide as part of a **mixed fire-gas exposure** when there is:

- Enclosed fire involving plastics, foam, upholstery, or synthetic materials
- Sudden collapse out of proportion to visible burns
- Persistent confusion, low blood pressure, or cardiac arrest after smoke exposure
- Severe lactic-acidosis-type picture if labs exist, but do not wait for labs in the field

Many real fire victims have **both CO and cyanide risk**, plus airway irritation.

</section>

<section id="airway-danger">

## Airway Danger Signs

Treat the airway as unstable if you see:

- Hoarseness
- Stridor
- Increasing work of breathing
- Soot in mouth
- Burns inside the mouth
- Progressive facial swelling
- Repeated coughing with exhaustion

These patients may worsen over the next minutes to hours. Keep them upright if tolerated, minimize exertion, and move toward definitive care early.

</section>

<section id="immediate-management">

## Immediate Management

### Oxygen priorities

- Give **high-flow oxygen** if available to any patient with significant smoke inhalation, suspected CO exposure, altered mental status, shortness of breath, or chest pain.
- If oxygen is limited, prioritize:
  1. altered or collapsing patients
  2. anyone with airway burns or severe breathing difficulty
  3. pregnant patients, children, and elderly patients with symptoms

### Basic supportive care

- Move to fresh air
- Loosen tight clothing
- Position upright unless shock or vomiting requires side positioning
- Monitor breathing rate, mental status, and pulse repeatedly
- Treat wheeze with bronchodilator if available and already part of your kit/protocol

### If the patient stops breathing

- Start rescue breathing / bag-mask ventilation if available
- Give oxygen if available
- Begin CPR if pulseless
- Continue resuscitation while removing from the exposure source

</section>

<section id="differentiating-patterns">

## Useful Pattern Recognition

### More consistent with airway smoke injury

- Cough
- Hoarseness
- Soot
- Wheeze
- Facial burns
- Worsening over the first few hours

### More consistent with carbon monoxide

- Headache
- Dizziness
- Nausea
- Confusion
- Syncope
- Multiple people in the same enclosed environment with similar symptoms

### More concerning for cyanide or severe mixed fire gas

- Sudden collapse
- Severe confusion or coma early
- Low blood pressure
- Cardiac arrest
- Heavy synthetic-material fire

These categories overlap. Do not wait to decide perfectly before giving oxygen and evacuating.

</section>

<section id="delayed-deterioration">

## Delayed Deterioration and Monitoring

Smoke inhalation can look better after fresh air, then worsen later.

Watch closely for 6-24 hours after meaningful exposure if evacuation is delayed:

- worsening cough
- wheeze
- hoarseness
- shortness of breath
- increasing fatigue
- confusion returning after initial improvement

Evacuation is strongly favored for:

- any loss of consciousness
- significant enclosed-space exposure
- chest pain
- pregnancy
- abnormal mental status
- airway burn signs
- persistent cough or low oxygen saturation if you can measure it

</section>

<section id="common-errors">

## Common Errors

- Focusing only on skin burns and missing airway injury
- Letting a "walk and talk" patient rest without repeated reassessment
- Assuming generator or charcoal exposure is safe because the room was "partly open"
- Re-entering a toxic environment without respiratory protection
- Forgetting that several mildly sick people in one room is a carbon monoxide clue

</section>

<section id="field-summary">

## Field Summary

1. Extract to fresh air without creating another victim.
2. Prioritize airway and oxygen, not just visible burns.
3. Think carbon monoxide whenever multiple people become ill in an enclosed space.
4. Think mixed fire gas, including cyanide risk, in enclosed fires involving synthetic materials.
5. Reassess repeatedly because delayed deterioration is common.
