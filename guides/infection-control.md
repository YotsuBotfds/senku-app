## Infection Control Overview

### Why Infection Control Matters in Survival

In healthcare facilities with full infrastructure, infections cause 5-10% of patient deaths. In resource-limited or post-collapse scenarios, this number climbs dramatically. Without antibiotics, clean water, and sterile equipment, a minor surgical infection becomes sepsis, which becomes death. Infection prevention is the single most effective intervention available in any medical scenario—more valuable than most medications because it prevents disease rather than treating it. Mastery of infection control is essential for anyone performing <a href="../surgery-field.html">Field Surgery & Emergency Procedures</a> and for wound management in <a href="../wound-hygiene-infection-prevention.html">Wound Hygiene & Infection Prevention</a>.

:::info-box
**The Core Principle:** Prevent infection before it starts. Once an infection establishes, treating it is exponentially harder and more resource-intensive than preventing it.
:::

This guide covers practical protocols for healthcare workers, family caretakers, and survival group medics to prevent disease transmission in any environment. Topics range from hand hygiene (the most basic, most effective measure) to outbreak management (coordinated community response). For systematic outbreak investigation methods including case definitions, attack rates, epidemic curves, and contact tracing procedures, see <a href="../epidemiology-outbreak-investigation.html">Epidemiology and Outbreak Investigation</a>. For setting up community-level disease surveillance networks and quarantine facilities, see <a href="../public-health-epidemiology.html">Public Health Systems & Epidemiology</a>.

:::tip
**Quick routing for clinic questions:**
- If the question is about setting up a small clinic, patient flow, triage space, exam space, storage, or records, see [Clinic Facility Basics](../clinic-facility-basics.html).
:::

### Key Statistics

- Hand hygiene alone prevents 30-50% of healthcare-associated infections
- Proper sterilization of surgical instruments reduces surgical site infection rates by 60-80%
- Isolation of symptomatic respiratory patients within 24 hours stops 85% of droplet disease transmission
- In low-income countries with strong infection control programs vs. weak programs: mortality from treatable infections differs by 5-fold

## Recognition and Assessment

### Identifying Infection vs. Normal Inflammation

Not every fever is infection. Not every wound drainage is bacterial infection. Learning to distinguish normal healing from true infection is critical when access to culture, bloodwork, and imaging is limited.

**Signs of Normal Healing Wound (First 3-5 Days):**
- Minimal clear or pale yellow drainage (serous exudate)
- Mild pain/tenderness at wound edges
- Slight warmth, redness confined to incision line
- No fever or systemic symptoms
- Surrounding skin normal beyond first 1-2 cm from incision

**Signs of Infection (Bacterial or Fungal):**
- Purulent drainage (thick, yellow, green, or foul-smelling pus)
- Rapidly spreading redness/warmth (expanding beyond immediate incision area)
- Fever (temperature >38.5°C / 101.3°F) or chills
- Systemic symptoms: malaise, fatigue, generalized body aches
- Palpable lymph node enlargement (epitrochlear, axillary, inguinal depending on wound location)
- Red streaking extending from wound toward regional nodes (lymphangitis = medical emergency)
- Wound dehiscence (incision opening/separating)
- Fluctuance (pus pocket palpable under skin)

:::info-box
**Key Assessment Principle:** A wound with fever or systemic symptoms + purulent drainage = bacterial infection until proven otherwise. Treat aggressively.
:::

### Assessment When Diagnostic Testing Unavailable

In resource-limited scenarios, visual and clinical assessment is your primary tool:
- **Wound appearance:** Purulent, necrotic tissue, discoloration
- **Odor:** Foul smell indicates bacterial overgrowth or tissue necrosis
- **Surrounding tissue:** Edema, erythema, warmth, lymphangitis
- **Systemic response:** Fever, tachycardia, altered mental status, hypotension (sepsis signs)
- **Patient history:** When injury occurred, when drainage/fever began

:::danger
**Sepsis Recognition (Medical Emergency):** Fever + any 2 of: heart rate >90, respiratory rate >20, altered mental status, lactate >2 mmol/L (if available), hypotension. Sepsis kills rapidly. Treat with broadest-spectrum antibiotics available, aggressive fluid resuscitation, elevation/drainage of source infection.
:::

<section id="principles">

## Infection Control Principles

### Chain of Infection

Infectious agent (bacteria, virus, fungus, parasite) requires pathway: source → transmission → susceptible host. Breaking any link prevents infection. Control measures target each step: eliminate source (vaccination, hygiene, isolation), block transmission (barriers, disinfection, distance), protect susceptible (vaccination, prophylaxis, isolation from exposed).

:::warning
**Breaking the Chain:** An infected person isolated from others = no transmission. A contaminated surface disinfected = no transmission risk. A vaccinated person exposed = protected. You don't need to eliminate every pathogen; you only need to break one link in the chain to prevent disease.
:::

### Standard Precautions

Apply to all patients, all situations: assume all blood, body fluids, secretions, excretions, non-intact skin, and mucous membranes may contain pathogens. Use gloves for contact with blood/body fluids. Use gown/apron if splashing expected. Use mask/eye protection for splashing. Perform hand hygiene frequently. Properly dispose of sharps and contaminated materials.

:::tip
**Standard Precautions Apply to Everyone:** You don't know who has hepatitis B, HIV, or tuberculosis. Assume all patients potentially infectious. This protects both patient and caregiver. Standard Precautions are baseline; add Transmission-Based Precautions on top for specific diagnoses.
:::

### Transmission-Based Precautions

-   **Contact:** Direct or indirect contact with infected persons/surfaces. Use gloves, gown. Keep patients in separate areas if possible. Examples: MRSA skin infection, scabies, norovirus gastroenteritis. Surface transmission occurs when contaminated hands touch mucosal surfaces; typical incubation 2-14 days depending on organism. Environmental persistence varies: norovirus survives weeks on surfaces, MRSA survives hours to weeks depending on desiccation. Contact precautions most effective intervention; hand hygiene critical (alcohol-based sanitizer less effective for norovirus; requires soap/water).
-   **Droplet:** Respiratory droplets (within 3-6 feet). Use mask/N95 for cough/sneeze. Keep distance from patient if possible. Examples: influenza, parainfluenza, mumps, pertussis, strep pharyngitis. Droplets generated by coughing/sneezing; large particles fall to ground quickly (hence 3-6 feet distance). Droplet precautions typically required 24-48 hours after starting effective treatment (antibiotic for bacteria, antivirals if available for viruses). Mask on patient reduces emission; mask on healthcare worker provides some protection but less than for airborne precautions.
-   **Airborne:** Aerosolized particles (tuberculosis, measles, chickenpox). Use N95 respirator, PAPR if available. Negative pressure room ideal. Isolation critical. Examples: TB (infectious for weeks if untreated, until 2 weeks of appropriate antibiotics), measles (highly contagious; 90% of exposed unvaccinated persons infected), varicella (highly contagious). Airborne particles remain suspended hours; air exchange rate matters (minimum 6 air changes per hour ideal). In survival settings without negative pressure: maximize distance (>6 feet), maximize ventilation (open windows), N95 respirator use mandatory for caregivers. Airborne precautions must continue minimum 24 hours after fever resolution (TB) or lesion crusting (varicella) unless on effective treatment.

:::danger
**Measles Exposure Protocol:** Highly contagious airborne disease. One person infects 12-18 others in unvaccinated population (R0=12-18). If exposed to measles case: unvaccinated/unknown immunity persons must isolate for 21 days (incubation period). Vaccinated persons can work if asymptomatic. Post-exposure vaccination within 72 hours of exposure prevents disease in 95% of recipients. No other protection exists; vaccination is only effective intervention.
:::

<section id="history">

## Historical Context of Infection Prevention

### Pre-Germ Theory Era (Pre-1860s)

High mortality from surgical infections (gangrene, sepsis often fatal). Physicians didn't wash between patients. Contaminated instruments used repeatedly. Childbed fever killed 30% of mothers in hospitals (though rare in home births). Ignaz Semmelweis demonstrated handwashing reduced deaths from 18% to 1.3% (but was ridiculed; died institutionalized). Recognition: invisible agents caused infection, but mechanisms unknown.

:::info-box
**Semmelweis Lesson:** One of history's most powerful infection control stories. Simple handwashing cut maternal mortality by 93%. Yet he was rejected and institutionalized for suggesting doctors' hands were contaminated. The resistance to infection control practices is not new—even obvious interventions face skepticism.
:::

### Germ Theory & Pasteur/Lister Era (1860s-1900s)

Louis Pasteur proved microorganisms caused fermentation/disease. Joseph Lister pioneered antiseptic surgery (carbolic acid on wounds, surgical instruments). Mortality from surgical infection dropped dramatically. Germ theory became accepted. Introduction of sterilization (autoclaving 1884) revolutionized surgery.

### Antibiotic Era (1940s-1980s)

Penicillin discovered 1928, mass-produced 1940s. Antibiotics reduced mortality dramatically. Infection control emphasis reduced (antibiotics "fix" infections). Resulting complacency led to antimicrobial resistance emergence. Modern realization: prevention always superior to treatment.

:::warning
**The Antibiotic Complacency Error:** Availability of antibiotics led hospitals to reduce infection control standards. Many physicians stopped emphasizing hand hygiene, sterilization was deprioritized. Result: healthcare-associated infections skyrocketed. Lesson for survival medicine: antibiotics may not be available post-collapse. Infection prevention must be primary strategy.
:::

### Post-Antibiotic Era & Survival Medicine

Multi-drug resistant bacteria (MRSA, MDR-TB) reduce antibiotic options. Supply chains fragile. Preparedness communities emphasize infection prevention as primary strategy (supplies may be unavailable post-collapse). Survival medicine returns to 19th-century emphasis: meticulous asepsis, sterilization, hygiene as foundation of patient care.

:::danger
**Antimicrobial Resistance Crisis:** MRSA (methicillin-resistant Staph aureus), VRE (vancomycin-resistant enterococci), and carbapenem-resistant gram-negatives are increasingly untreatable. Some infections now have zero effective antibiotics. The margin between infection and death is shrinking. In any collapse scenario, prevention becomes literally lifesaving.
:::

<section id="hand-hygiene">

## Hand Hygiene

### When to Perform Hand Hygiene

-   Before and after patient contact
-   Before eating, drinking, taking medication
-   Before and after handling food
-   After using toilet
-   After contact with blood/body fluids (even with gloves)
-   After removing gloves
-   After touching face/hair/body
-   Before and after preparing/handling medication

### Hand Hygiene Technique

**With soap and water (preferred if visibly soiled):** Wet hands with water, apply soap, rub all surfaces (palms, backs, between fingers, under nails, wrists) for 20+ seconds. Rinse thoroughly. Dry with clean cloth or air-dry. For field expedient: vigorously rub hands with sand or soil (if no water), then rinse with water if available.

**Alcohol-based sanitizer (when soap/water unavailable):** Apply 1/2 teaspoon (3mL), rub all surfaces until dry. Requires 60-95% alcohol to be effective. Less effective on heavily soiled hands.

### Nail Care

Trim fingernails short; bacteria hide under nails. Avoid artificial nails (harbor pathogens). Prevent nail damage which allows bacterial entry. In survival: clean nails with brush and water regularly, use sharp object to remove debris.

### Hand Hygiene in Resource-Limited Settings

Access to clean water is not guaranteed. Prioritize hand hygiene at critical moments: before eating, before patient care, after using toilet, after handling contaminated materials. When water scarce: use collected rainwater, boiled water, or stored supplies for hand hygiene. Alcohol-based sanitizers (if available) extend water resources but should not completely replace water-based washing. In field conditions: sand or soil can mechanically remove contamination; rub vigorously for 20+ seconds, then rinse if water available. Educate caregivers that consistent hand hygiene prevents majority of healthcare-associated infections (60-70% reduction in infection rates documented in low-resource settings).

:::tip
**Hand Hygiene Hierarchy (Most to Least Effective):**
1. Soap + running water (best)
2. Soap + collected water (very good)
3. Alcohol-based sanitizer 60%+ (good for clean hands)
4. Sand/soil mechanical removal (better than nothing)
5. No cleaning (worst)

Even partial intervention is better than nothing. Don't delay care waiting for ideal conditions.
:::

## Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

### Gloves

Latex, nitrile, or vinyl. Gloves reduce hand contamination 99%; do NOT replace hand hygiene. Change between patients/tasks. Remove properly: grasp outside wrist, peel downward inside-out. Don't touch exterior. Use double gloves for high-risk procedures. In shortage: prioritize gloves for high-risk tasks (blood contact, open wounds). Reuse gloves by sanitizing if absolutely necessary (not ideal, increases infection risk).

### Masks and Respirators

-   **Surgical mask:** Protects others from wearer; minimal self-protection. Effective for droplet precautions (wearer is source).
-   **N95 respirator:** Protects wearer from airborne particles; requires fit testing for 95% efficiency. Use for tuberculosis, measles, varicella, COVID-19 (airborne transmission phase).
-   **KN95/FFP2:** International equivalents to N95. Slightly lower efficiency (94-95%) but acceptable alternative.

Field expedient mask: cloth layers (cotton), tied around nose/mouth. Provides some protection, not equivalent to surgical mask. Protect mask by keeping dry, never touch front surface.

### Eye and Face Protection

For procedures with blood/body fluid splash risk: use goggles or face shield (protects mucous membranes). Remove after use, clean if reusable. In field: glasses provide some protection; improvise shield from clear plastic if needed.

### Gowns and Aprons

Protects clothing/skin from splashing. Change after contact with potentially infectious material. If shortage: use plastic bags, cloth, or available waterproof material. Priority: high-splash procedures (surgery, suction, certain nursing care).

### PPE Donning and Doffing Sequence

Proper sequence prevents self-contamination during high-risk care:

**DONNING (Putting On) Order:** Gown first → Mask second → Goggles/Face shield third → Gloves last. This sequence ensures protection layers build from body outward, with contamination risk lowest for innermost layers.

**DOFFING (Removing) Order:** Gloves first → Goggles second → Gown third → Mask last. Reverse donning order minimizes exposure to contamination accumulated during patient care. Gloves removed first because they accumulate highest contamination; remove without touching exterior. Goggles removed second while face still protected by gown/mask. Gown removed third, holding only interior surfaces. Mask removed last, from ear straps only (never touch front). Hand hygiene between removing each PPE layer or immediately after all removal.

:::danger
**Contaminated Mask Contact = Self-Inoculation:** The front of a used mask is covered with pathogens. Touching it then touching your face, eating, or removing your mask without proper precautions transmits infection directly to yourself. This is how healthcare workers with meticulous patient care still become infected—careless mask removal or adjustment.
:::

:::tip
**PPE Doffing Reality Check:** Proper doffing takes 2-3 minutes. Rushing causes contamination. If caring for multiple patients: don clean PPE for each patient if possible. If reusing PPE: remove carefully, store in paper bag (not plastic—plastic traps moisture), wash hands, then don again for next patient.
:::

## Aseptic Technique

### Clean vs Sterile

**Clean:** Minimizes microorganisms; acceptable for most patient care, wound dressing, daily living. **Sterile:** Completely free of microorganisms; required for invasive procedures (surgery, catheter insertion, injection). In survival: prioritize sterile technique for invasive procedures, clean technique for routine care.

### Sterile Field Maintenance

-   Place sterile items on clean, dry surface
-   Keep sterile items above waist level (lower areas easily contaminated)
-   Do NOT reach across sterile field (contaminates)
-   Do NOT touch sterile items with non-sterile hands
-   Only sterile items contact sterile field
-   Sterile field is contaminated if wet, touched by non-sterile object, exposed to air >1 hour

### Handling Sterile Items

If gloved, ensure gloves are sterile. If changing gloves: remove non-sterile gloves without contaminating hands, perform hand hygiene, don sterile gloves. For handling with bare hands: perform hand hygiene thoroughly, handle by edges/outer surfaces only, avoid touching surfaces that will contact patients.

### Environmental Asepsis

Clean environment minimizes infection: clean spills immediately, dispose of contaminated materials in leak-proof containers, keep work surfaces clean, limit traffic in high-asepsis areas, prevent dust generation during procedures. Microorganisms in air settle on surfaces; minimize air currents during sterile procedures (avoid fanning, excess movement). Dust resuspended by sweeping; prefer damp mopping. Design consideration: separate clean/contaminated areas if possible (operating area away from general living space, laundry area away from food prep). In survival: designate clean zone for medical care, decontaminate before and after care. Time-based asepsis: sterile setup valid 1 hour if undisturbed, 30 minutes if any disruption, immediately contaminated if touched by non-sterile object. Mark time of setup; discard after time limit.

:::warning
**Sterile Field Contamination:** Even one non-sterile object touching a sterile field contaminates entire field. You cannot "just that part." Once contaminated, discard everything and start over with fresh sterile items. This is why sterile technique is called sterile—any contamination invalidates it.
:::

### Surgical Site Infection Prevention

Surgical wound infections increase morbidity, mortality, length of stay. Prevention requires multi-factorial approach: preoperative (skin antisepsis with chlorhexidine or iodine if available), intraoperative (aseptic technique, appropriate antibiotic prophylaxis if available, maintain normothermia if possible), postoperative (sterile dressing 24-48 hours, then can be removed; daily inspection for signs of infection, hand hygiene before/after dressing change). In survival: prioritize aseptic technique over antibiotic prophylaxis (not always available); meticulous wound care post-op. Higher infection risk in: contaminated wounds (soil contamination), delayed surgery (>6 hours to wound closure), immunocompromised patients, poor nutritional status. Monitor closely for signs of infection: increased drainage (especially purulent), fever, warmth/erythema around incision, dehiscence (incision opening). Early recognition and treatment (antibiotics, drainage) critical.

:::info-box
**Surgical Site Infection Statistics:** Surgical site infection increases hospital stay by 7-10 days, increases mortality 2-3 fold, costs healthcare $3,000-$20,000 per case. In survival settings without antibiotics: surgical site infection can become life-threatening sepsis. Prevention saves lives and conserves scarce antibiotics.
:::

### Field-Expedient Sterilization Kit

For remote locations without access to electricity or complex equipment, assemble a boiling-based sterilization kit:
- Metal pot with lid (large, heat-resistant)
- Thermometer (ideally shows 100°C / 212°F)
- Sterile forceps or tongs (to remove items without contamination)
- Sterile cloth (to place items on for cooling)
- Timing mechanism (watch, sundial, or marked candle)
- Protective equipment (cloth to handle hot pot)

For high-altitude locations (>5,000 ft elevation), remember water boils below 100°C; add 10 minutes per 3,000 ft elevation to achieve sterilization.

## Step-by-Step Procedures

### Performing Hand Hygiene (Detailed Protocol)

**When Hands Are Visibly Soiled:**

1. Wet hands with flowing water (warm preferred but not required)
2. Apply liquid soap (1 pump/teaspoon)
3. Rub palms together vigorously, 5 seconds
4. Rub back of hands, between fingers, under nails (10+ seconds)
5. Interlock fingers and rub between them (5 seconds)
6. Rub thumbs (often missed area), 5 seconds
7. Rub wrists if contamination risk higher (surgical setting), 5 seconds
8. Rinse under flowing water until soap removed
9. Dry with clean cloth or air-dry (30+ seconds)
10. If faucet manual: use paper towel to turn off (prevents recontamination)

**Total time: 40-60 seconds minimum**

**When Hands Not Visibly Soiled (Alcohol-Based Sanitizer):**

1. Apply 3mL (1/2 teaspoon) sanitizer to palm
2. Rub palms together until dry
3. Rub back of hands
4. Rub between fingers
5. Rub under nails (if contamination likely)

**Total time: 20-30 seconds**

:::tip
**Hand Hygiene Compliance Rates:** Even in hospitals with posters and reminders, compliance averages 40%. In survival groups, assign one person to monitor/prompt others. Make it a communal practice, not criticism.
:::

### Aseptic Dressing Change (Wound Care Protocol)

**Supplies Needed:** Clean gloves, sterile gloves (if available), sterile gauze, non-adherent dressing (Telfa) or clean cloth, antiseptic (alcohol, chlorhexidine, iodine, or clean water), tape/cloth strips, hand hygiene supplies

**Steps:**

1. Perform hand hygiene (20+ seconds with soap/water)
2. Gather supplies on clean surface within reach
3. Don non-sterile gloves
4. Carefully remove old dressing by lifting edges, supporting wound (do not pull directly on wound)
5. Observe dressing for: amount/color of drainage, odor, signs of infection
6. Discard dressing in leak-proof container
7. Remove non-sterile gloves, hand hygiene
8. Don sterile gloves (or clean gloves if sterile unavailable)
9. If wound contaminated: gently irrigate with sterile saline or boiled cooled water using syringe or cloth (do not forcefully spray)
10. Pat dry with sterile gauze (do not rub)
11. Apply antiseptic ointment if available (bacitracin, chlorhexidine) in thin layer
12. Cover with non-adherent dressing or clean cloth
13. Secure with tape or cloth strips (not so tight as to restrict circulation)
14. Remove gloves, perform hand hygiene
15. Document dressing change (time, observations, signs of infection)

**Frequency:** Clean wound: every 24-48 hours if dressing dry, or sooner if saturated. Infected wound: daily or more.

:::warning
**Moisture = Bacterial Growth:** Dressings should keep wound moist enough for healing but not wet enough for bacteria overgrowth. If drainage excessive: change dressing more frequently or consider drainage procedure. If dressing dry and adhering: wet edges with saline before removal to avoid reopening wound.
:::

### Sterilization Methods

### Boiling (Field Expedient Sterilization)

Bring water to rolling boil, submerge instruments for 20 minutes (sea level; add 10 min per 3,000 ft elevation). Remove with sterile forceps, place on sterile surface to cool/dry. Note: boiling does NOT kill spores (C. difficile, B. anthracis); for most survival scenarios, adequate. Cover pot to prevent recontamination. Multiple boiling cycles acceptable.

### Disinfection Levels

-   **Low:** Removes vegetative bacteria; dilute bleach (1:100), ethanol 70%
-   **Intermediate:** Kills most bacteria, viruses, fungi; 70% ethanol, phenol, Lysol
-   **High:** Kills everything including spores; glutaraldehyde, peracetic acid, bleach (1:10 dilution)

### Household Disinfectants

Bleach (sodium hypochlorite 5-6%): 1 part bleach + 9 parts water = 0.5% solution (intermediate disinfection, suitable for regular cleaning). 1:1 ratio with water = 2.5-3% (high-level disinfection, use for visibly contaminated surfaces/spills). Use immediately after mixing; potency decreases over hours. Store bleach in cool dark place (sunlight degrades); even "old" bleach (6-12 months) retains reasonable potency. Vinegar (acetic acid) has limited disinfection; not reliable alone for contaminated surfaces. Alcohol 70% effective against bacteria/viruses if contact time adequate (30 seconds); less effective on some bacteria and ineffective on spores. Isopropyl alcohol (rubbing alcohol) preferred over ethanol (beverage alcohol) for disinfection; must be exactly 70% (too dilute = ineffective, too concentrated = evaporates too quickly to be effective).

### Disinfection Strategy by Contamination Level

**Clean surface (routine maintenance):** Wash with soap/water, dry with clean cloth. No additional disinfection needed unless immunocompromised population. **Potentially contaminated (body fluid exposure unlikely but possible, e.g., bedside table):** Intermediate disinfection (0.5% bleach or 70% alcohol). Clean surface visibly first, apply disinfectant, let contact 10 minutes, wipe clean. **Visibly contaminated (blood/body fluid splash):** High-level disinfection (1:1 bleach or commercial disinfectant). Cover visible contamination with bleach-saturated cloth, let stand 20-30 minutes, scrub with cloth, rinse if desired (bleach odor generally acceptable), air dry. **Unknown contamination (disaster cleanup, abandoned space):** Assume highest contamination; use high-level disinfection. Wear protective equipment (gloves, eye protection, respiratory if visible mold/dust). Consider hiring professional cleanup if extensive biological hazard (deceased body, major spill).

### Handpieces and Complex Equipment

Dental handpieces, surgical power tools, endoscopes present sterilization challenge: complex design traps contamination, heat/moisture sensitivity limits sterilization options. Ideal: autoclave after disassembly per manufacturer guidelines. If autoclave unavailable: soak in high-level disinfectant (glutaraldehyde if available, else 1:1 bleach solution for 30+ minutes), then rinse thoroughly (residual disinfectant damages tissues), air dry. For handpieces unable to tolerate liquid: surface disinfection with alcohol or quaternary ammonium compound, manual cleaning of visible contamination with brush. Document sterilization/disinfection of equipment (shows compliance, aids troubleshooting if infections occur).

### Steam vs Dry Heat Sterilization

**Steam (Autoclaving):** Most reliable, fastest method. Requires pressure vessel, thermometer, pressure gauge. Temperature 121°C (250°F), pressure 15 psi, time 15-20 minutes at temperature achieves sterilization. Advantage: rapid, penetrates fabric/wraps. Disadvantage: requires equipment. **Dry heat:** Open flame (sterilizes forceps/scalpels in seconds but risk of damage), hot water (boiling), or oven (requires electricity/fuel). Dry heat slower (30-45 minutes at 160-170°C) but penetrates less effectively than steam; some organisms more resistant. In survival: boiling is most practical field method, 20+ minutes at rolling boil acceptable for most instruments. Oven sterilization requires consistent temperature control; thermostat check recommended before trusting oven temperatures.

</section>

<section id="wound-care">

## Wound Care Protocols

### Clean Wound Dressing

1.  Perform hand hygiene, don gloves
2.  Remove old dressing; observe for signs of infection (increased drainage, odor, redness, warmth)
3.  Cleanse wound gently with sterile saline or boiled cooled water if contaminated
4.  Pat dry with sterile gauze
5.  Apply antiseptic ointment if available (bacitracin, chlorhexidine)
6.  Cover with sterile non-adherent dressing (telfa) or clean cloth
7.  Secure with tape or cloth strips
8.  Discard contaminated materials in leak-proof container
9.  Perform hand hygiene

### Infected Wound Management

More frequent dressing changes (daily or more). Gentle cleansing to remove exudate without disrupting healing tissue. Consider culture/sensitivity if available (identify organism, antibiotic susceptibility). Systemic antibiotics critical. Elevated temperature on gauze during dressing changes. Monitor for systemic signs (fever, malaise, enlarged lymph nodes). For spreading infection (cellulitis, lymphangitis): treat as medical emergency requiring immediate antibiotics/evacuation.

### Chronic Wound Prevention

Pressure ulcers (decubitus): frequent position changes every 2 hours, padding for bony prominences, moisture management. Diabetic ulcers: strict foot inspection daily (dry, protected, no pressure areas), manage blood glucose. Venous stasis ulcers: elevation, compression if available. Arterial ulcers: keep warm, avoid pressure. Poor circulation = slow healing; optimize nutrition and oxygenation.

</section>

<section id="isolation">

## Isolation Precautions

### Physical Isolation

Separate infected patient from non-infected. Private room ideal; if unavailable, place patients >3 feet apart (droplet), or >6 feet apart (airborne preferred, not always possible in survival). Dedicated equipment when possible (stethoscope, blood pressure cuff, thermometer). Do NOT move patient to different locations; minimizes transmission risk.

### Respiratory Isolation

For TB, measles, varicella (before lesions crust over): respiratory protection (N95 or better if available). Surgical mask on patient if cooperative. Limit movement from room. Continue until patient improves (typically 1-2 weeks on antibiotics for TB, >1 week for measles/varicella). Monitor contacts for symptoms.

### Enteric Isolation

For patients with diarrhea from infectious source (bacteria, viruses, parasites): separate bathroom/commode if possible. Hand hygiene after any contact with feces. Gloves for patient care. Do NOT prepare food for others while caring for diarrhea patient. Monitor contacts for symptom development.

<section id="disease-prevention">

## Disease Prevention Strategies

### Vaccination Program (if Available)

Priority in survival settings: measles (highly contagious, preventable), polio, diphtheria/tetanus, meningococcal meningitis (community spread risk). Vaccination during outbreak response can prevent secondary cases. Even with limited vaccine supply, vaccinate contacts of confirmed cases first.

### Waterborne Disease Prevention

Primary source of disease transmission in survival scenarios. Boil water (rolling boil 1 minute, 3+ minutes at altitude). Filtration (cloth, sand, commercial filters) removes particles but not pathogens; combine with boiling. Chemical treatment: chlorine (2-3 drops household bleach per liter, wait 30 min; cloudy water needs 8 drops), iodine tablets (follow package directions, 30 min wait), bleach tablets. Separate drinking water from greywater; never contaminate water supply with feces.

### Vector Control

Mosquitoes (malaria, dengue, yellow fever), ticks (Lyme disease, Rocky Mountain spotted fever), flies (diarrheal disease): remove standing water (breed mosquitoes), use insect repellent (DEET 20-30%), wear long sleeves/pants in high-risk areas, inspect skin daily for ticks, remove ticks carefully (grasp close to skin, pull straight out, don't twist/crush). Keep food covered to prevent fly contamination.

### Hygiene Promotion

Educate community: hand hygiene is single most effective intervention, safe food handling (cook thoroughly, prevent cross-contamination), sanitation (safe waste disposal, separate toilets from water), respiratory etiquette (cover mouth when coughing/sneezing). In survival groups: assign hygiene roles, maintain supplies, monitor compliance.

</section>

<section id="outbreak">

## Outbreak Recognition & Response

### Recognizing an Outbreak

Cluster of similar illnesses in same location/timeframe. Examples: sudden increase in diarrheal illness (suggest food/water contamination), multiple respiratory cases (suggest airborne pathogen), skin infections (suggest contact/contamination). Response: isolate cases, identify exposure source, implement control measures, monitor for spread.

### Outbreak Investigation Steps

For detailed outbreak investigation methodology — including case definition development, attack rate calculation, epidemic curve construction, and formal investigation reports — see <a href="../epidemiology-outbreak-investigation.html">Epidemiology and Outbreak Investigation</a>.

1.  Confirm outbreak exists (more cases than baseline/endemic level)
2.  Identify case definition (specific symptoms/timeframe)
3.  Count cases, identify pattern (age, location, timeline)
4.  Find exposure source (food, water, person-to-person, environmental)
5.  Implement control measures (isolation, disinfection, behavior change)
6.  Monitor for secondary cases (2+ incubation periods from implementation)

</section>

<section id="environmental">

## Environmental Cleaning Protocols

### Cleaning vs Disinfection vs Sterilization

**Cleaning:** Physically remove dirt, blood, organic material with soap/water and mechanical action. Does NOT kill pathogens but removes soil that harbors them. Prerequisite for disinfection (disinfectants less effective on dirty surfaces). **Disinfection:** Chemical or physical process kills pathogens on surfaces; requires adequate concentration and contact time. **Sterilization:** Kills ALL microorganisms including spores; required only for surgical instruments and invasive items.

### High-Touch Surfaces in Care Settings

Prioritize disinfection of surfaces frequently touched: door handles, light switches, bed rails, bedside tables, IV stands, equipment controls, thermometer sheath holders. In survival settings: assign one person to clean/disinfect daily. Use bleach solution (1:100 dilution for regular cleaning, 1:10 for heavily contaminated). Spray on surface, wipe with cloth, allow 10 minutes contact time if high contamination suspected. For patient equipment (stethoscope, blood pressure cuff): clean after each patient with alcohol or 1:100 bleach solution.

### Body Fluid Spills

Blood, vomit, feces, urine contain high pathogen loads. Immediate response: don gloves and eye protection. Contain spill with absorbent material (paper towels). For large spills (>30mL blood): if available, use commercial spill kits or make expedient kit (bleach-soaked cloth in sealed container). Apply 1:1 bleach:water solution, let stand 10 minutes minimum. Wipe up with disposable cloth. Dispose of all materials in leak-proof container. Clean area again with fresh solution. Monitor for missed spots. Hand hygiene after cleanup essential.

### Laundry and Textiles

Contaminated linens (blood-soaked, feces, vomit) require hot water wash (>140°F if available) with appropriate detergent. Standard detergent kills most pathogens; separate contaminated from regular laundry if possible. If hot water unavailable: soak in bleach solution (1:100) for 30 minutes, then wash with available detergent. Air dry if possible (sun exposure provides germicidal effect). In survival: use cloth/cloth waste rather than disposable, wash thoroughly between uses. Never shake contaminated textiles (aerosolizes particles).

### Bathroom and Toilet Facilities

Primary contamination source in survival settings. Daily disinfection essential: scrub toilet bowl with brush and 1:10 bleach solution, let stand 10 minutes, flush. Wipe down toilet exterior, sink, floor with disinfectant. Provide separate sanitation if possible for patients with diarrhea (infectious diarrhea = high transmission risk). Hand hygiene MANDATORY after any bathroom use. In field: outhouse design critical - locate >100 feet from water source, cover feces with lime/soil daily, designate sole-use facilities if infectious disease present.

### Equipment Decontamination

Reusable medical equipment requires systematic decontamination: 1) wear gloves, remove gross contamination with cloth; 2) wash thoroughly with soap and water; 3) rinse and dry completely; 4) disinfect appropriate to equipment type (alcohol wipe for thermometer, bleach solution for non-electrical equipment, quaternary ammonium solution for delicate equipment). Document cleaning (improves compliance). If reusable equipment becomes extensively contaminated (punctured glove, body fluid inside): if possible, discard; if critical, consider boiling if water/heat available and equipment can tolerate it.

</section>

<section id="water-sanitation">

## Water, Sanitation & Infection Control

### Waterborne Pathogen Risks

Bacteria (E. coli, Vibrio cholerae, Salmonella), viruses (hepatitis A, rotavirus, norovirus), protozoa (Giardia, Cryptosporidium), helminths (worms) contaminate water from human/animal feces. Cholera kills 50% of symptomatic cases if untreated; Giardia causes malabsorption/chronic diarrhea. In post-collapse scenarios without centralized water treatment, waterborne disease becomes leading cause of morbidity/mortality. Investment in water security = infection control foundation.

### Water Treatment Hierarchy

**Best:** Boiling (rolling boil 1 minute, 3+ at altitude) kills all pathogens; requires fuel/time. **Good:** Filtration + disinfection (filter removes particles, chlorine/bleach kills microorganisms); requires filtration device. **Acceptable:** Bleach/chlorine alone (kills bacteria/viruses, less reliable on protozoa); requires accurate dosing. **Field expedient:** Combine methods - simple cloth filter removes sediment, then boil or disinfect chemical. Solar disinfection (SODIS): fill clear plastic bottle, expose to sun 6+ hours (UV kills pathogens); slow but requires no fuel.

### Greywater and Blackwater Management

**Greywater** (dishwater, shower): low pathogen load; drain away from living/water areas, can be used for irrigation if treated. **Blackwater** (human waste): extremely high pathogen load; separate sanitation is critical. Never allow mixing of blackwater with drinking water source. Pit latrines/composting toilets reduce transmission but require proper maintenance (cover, distance from water). Urine-diverting toilets allow pathogen-free urine to be safely used as fertilizer. Treat all fecal matter as if highly infectious.

### Food Contamination Pathways

Fecal-oral route = most common waterborne disease transmission: contaminated hands, utensils, cutting boards → cross-contamination of ready-to-eat food. Prevention: dedicated food preparation area separate from sanitation; handwashing before food prep; raw food cooking (>160°F internal temperature kills pathogens); separate utensils for raw/cooked food; never reuse utensils for raw and cooked without washing. In survival: designate one person for food prep if possible, educate others on contamination risks.

### Field Water Security Assessment

Before using water source: visually inspect for discoloration/odor (indicates contamination), check for upstream human/animal activity (feces, carcasses), note proximity to latrines/dumps. Safest sources: springs (if protected), deep wells (less contaminated than surface), rainwater collected from clean roof. Least safe: stagnant pond, water downstream of settlements, water with algal bloom. When source quality uncertain: always treat (boil/disinfect) before drinking. Distribute information to community about safest available sources.

</section>

<section id="complications">

## Complications and When to Stop/Escalate

### Infection Complications Requiring Immediate Escalation

**Sepsis (Medical Emergency):**

Signs: Fever >38.5°C (101.3°F) + any 2 of: heart rate >90, respiratory rate >20, altered mental status, low blood pressure, lactate >2 mmol/L (if available)

Actions:
- Start broadest-spectrum antibiotic available immediately
- Achieve IV access if possible (aggressive fluid resuscitation)
- Drain source infection (abscess, infected wound)
- Escalate care/evacuation if possible
- Monitor for shock (confusion, cold extremities, very low blood pressure = imminent organ failure)

Survival outcome: Without antibiotics and IV fluids, sepsis mortality >80%. Even with limited resources, aggressive infection source control + empiric antibiotics offer only chance.

:::danger
**Septic Shock Recognition:** Cool extremities, altered mental status, profound weakness, very low blood pressure. This is a death spiral—organ failure cascading. If available: epinephrine, aggressive fluids, immediate evacuation. If no advanced care possible: maximally support patient, keep comfortable, prepare for likely death.
:::

**Spreading Cellulitis/Lymphangitis:**

Signs: Red streaking from wound toward regional lymph nodes, fever, enlarged/tender nodes

Actions:
- Elevate affected limb (gravity reduces swelling)
- Start antibiotics immediately (don't wait for culture)
- Warm compresses may provide comfort
- Monitor for progression to sepsis
- If available: ultrasound/imaging to rule out abscess (abscess requires drainage, not antibiotics alone)

**Invasive Fungal Infection:**

Signs: Black/dark tissue discoloration, rapid spread, tissue necrosis, possible crepitus (gas in tissues = necrotizing infection)

Actions:
- This may be necrotizing fasciitis (streptococcal or mixed bacterial, high mortality)
- Requires aggressive surgical debridement + antibiotics
- If no surgical capability: maximize antibiotics, elevation, keep clean
- Poor prognosis in resource-limited setting without surgery

:::warning
**Necrotizing Fasciitis Warning Signs:** Severe pain out of proportion to examination findings, skin discoloration (purple/black), crepitus (crackling under skin from gas), rapid progression (hours to days), systemic toxicity. High mortality (30-40% even with surgery). Requires immediate evacuation/surgery—not treatable by medical management alone.
:::

**Allergic Reaction to Antiseptic/Antibiotic:**

Signs: Rash, itching, swelling, difficulty breathing, anaphylaxis (airway swelling, wheezing, shock)

Actions:
- Stop offending agent immediately
- For mild rash/itching: diphenhydramine if available
- For anaphylaxis: epinephrine IM 0.3-0.5 mg immediately, IV access, airway support, fluids
- Try alternative antibiotic/antiseptic class if anaphylaxis

### When to Continue vs. Stop Infection Control Measures

**Continue isolation/respiratory precautions:**
- Until patient fever-free x 24 hours on appropriate treatment (TB, measles, varicella)
- Until bacterial respiratory infection shows clinical improvement (48+ hours on antibiotics)
- Until diarrheal illness resolves (contact precautions continue beyond symptom resolution for some viruses)

**Can discontinue precautions (after confirming with patient improving):**
- Droplet precautions: 24 hours after antibiotics started + fever resolution
- Contact precautions: After wound healing or patient clinically improved (organism-dependent; norovirus up to 48 hours post-resolution)
- Airborne precautions: Variable by organism; TB minimum 24 hours on effective therapy

:::tip
**Isolation Burden:** Isolation is psychologically difficult. Balance infection control with patient morale. Once safe to discontinue precautions, do so immediately—even one day earlier improves patient mental health.
:::

</section>

<section id="recovery">

## Recovery and Aftercare

### Post-Infection Wound Healing

**Timeline (in normal conditions):**
- Days 0-3: Inflammatory phase (redness, warmth, drainage normal)
- Days 3-21: Proliferative phase (wound closes, drainage decreases)
- Weeks 3-12: Remodeling phase (scar strengthens, wound contracts)

**Optimization for healing:**
- Nutrition: Protein (rebuilding tissue), vitamin C (collagen synthesis), zinc (immune function)
- Hydration: Dehydration impairs immune response and healing
- Activity: Immobilization initially (protects wound), gradual mobilization as heals
- Wound care: Keep clean and slightly moist (not dripping), change dressing if soiled
- Monitor for infection: Fever, purulent drainage, spreading redness = treat aggressively

**In resource-limited settings:**
If antibiotics unavailable post-infection: focus on support care (fluids, nutrition, wound drainage, fever management) and hope for spontaneous resolution. Many infections self-resolve with time if patient survives acute phase.

### Psychological Impact of Infection

Serious infections cause fear: patient may feel they're dying (sometimes justified), fear of disfigurement/amputation, fear of spread to family. Support:
- Clear, honest communication about prognosis
- Reassurance about prevention (you're doing everything possible)
- Involve family in care (reduces isolation, provides hope)
- Pain management if available
- In terminal cases: focus on comfort and dignity

### Contact Tracing Post-Outbreak

If involved in disease outbreak management (for a comprehensive contact tracing protocol including types of contacts, tracing process, and quarantine duration by disease, see <a href="../epidemiology-outbreak-investigation.html#contact-tracing">Epidemiology and Outbreak Investigation — Contact Tracing</a>):
- Identify all who had contact with confirmed case
- Educate on symptom recognition and when to isolate
- Monitor for 2 incubation periods (typically 7-14 days depending on disease)
- Reinforce hand hygiene, respiratory etiquette
- Document contacts (helps identify transmission chains)

</section>

<section id="adaptations">

## Field Adaptations

### High-Altitude Infection Control

**Challenges:** Lower oxygen, water boils <100°C, reduced personnel, limited supplies

**Adaptations:**
- Boiling time increased (10+ minutes per 3,000 ft elevation above sea level)
- Recognize oxygen-dependent infections may progress faster in hypoxic patients
- Hand hygiene more critical (altitude increases infection risk)
- Storage: Medications, PPE degrade faster in UV exposure (mountain sunlight)

### Tropical/Hot Climate Considerations

**Challenges:** High moisture promotes fungal/bacterial growth, rapid decomposition, arthropod vectors

**Adaptations:**
- Disinfectants: Bleach degrades faster in heat; store in cool/dark location, replace every 3-6 months
- Dressing changes more frequent (increased drainage, moisture, sweat)
- Fungal prevention: Keep skin dry, antifungal powder if available
- Vector control: Insect repellent, netting, covered food, standing water removal (dengue, malaria mosquitoes breed in water)
- Waste: Rapid decomposition may require more frequent burial/incineration to prevent vector breeding

### Cold/Arctic Conditions

**Challenges:** Frostbite risk, reduced circulation, limited water, cold preserves pathogens longer

**Adaptations:**
- Frostbite prevention: Wound infection in frostbitten tissue high (devitalized tissue = bacteria overgrowth)
- Water: Melt snow/ice for hand hygiene (fuel required)
- PPE: Gloves necessary both for infection control and temperature protection
- Preservation: Cold storage actually extends food/antibiotic shelf life (keep supplies cold if possible)

### Austere/Disaster Settings (Limited Supplies)

**Priorities when supplies critically limited:**
1. Hand hygiene (soap/water, alcohol sanitizer)
2. Gloves (prioritize for bloody procedures)
3. Masks (for respiratory cases)
4. Bleach disinfectant
5. Sterile dressings
6. Boiling capability (field-expedient sterilization)

**Reuse protocols (last resort):**
- Gloves: Sanitize with alcohol, air dry, reuse until visible deterioration. Not ideal but better than bare hands in high-risk situations
- Masks: Store in paper bag between uses, allow 5+ days rest if possible (reduces viral load), use different mask for each patient if possible
- Instruments: Boil, dry, store in clean/dry container, reboil before use
- Dressings: Wash cloth dressings with soap/water, boil if possible, air dry, reuse

:::warning
**Reuse Infection Risk:** Every reuse increases contamination risk. Document reuse, monitor closely for infection. Rotate supplies to minimize reuse when possible.
:::

### Rural/Remote Healthcare

**Challenges:** Limited diagnostic capability, longer transport time, limited personnel

**Adaptations:**
- Diagnosis: Clinical assessment becomes primary (visual, palpation, vital signs)
- Prevention: Emphasis shifts to preventing infection (no backup antibiotics available)
- Evacuation protocols: Establish beforehand who can transport critically ill, where to evacuate
- Telemedicine: If available, photograph wounds, describe symptoms to remote physician for guidance
- Training: Focus on infection recognition, preventive measures, when-to-evacuate decision-making

</section>

<section id="water-scarce-hygiene">

## Water-Scarce Hand Hygiene Protocols

In collapse scenarios with water scarcity, traditional hand washing becomes impossible. Understanding alcohol-based, ash-based, and improvised alternatives enables continued infection prevention without requiring water.

### Alcohol-Based Hand Sanitizers

**Preparation:**
- **Commercial formulations:** 60-95% ethanol or isopropyl alcohol kills most bacteria/viruses
- **Improvised preparation (if commercial unavailable):**
  - 70% isopropyl alcohol (from salvaged medical supplies, or denatured alcohol)
  - Mix with glycerin or lotion 3:1 alcohol to glycerin (prevents skin drying)
  - Add fragrance (optional; helps palatability perception for non-drinking use)

**Effectiveness:**
- **Works best on:** Visibly clean hands; kills 99.9% of bacteria in 20-60 seconds
- **Does NOT work on:** Hands visibly soiled/dirty (organic material reduces effectiveness); hands contaminated with fecal material (need water washing)
- **Limitations:** No mechanical removal of organisms; doesn't kill spores (C. difficile, norovirus)

**Application Technique:**
1. Apply 2-3 mL (size of a quarter) to palm
2. Rub hands together vigorously for 20-30 seconds
3. Rub surfaces: palms, back of hands, between fingers, around nails, wrists
4. Allow to air dry (do NOT wipe off with cloth—reduces effectiveness)

**When to Use:**
- Before eating, preparing food, touching medical equipment
- After patient contact, before donning gloves
- After glove removal
- Between patients (if hands visibly clean)

**Cautions:**
- Keep away from eyes (causes irritation)
- Do NOT ingest (toxic)
- Flammable—keep away from flame/heat
- Skin irritation possible with frequent use (use hand lotion between applications)

### Ash-Based Hand Cleansing

For areas with no alcohol availability, wood ash provides mild antimicrobial effect through alkalinity and abrasive action.

**Preparation:**
1. Collect clean ash from wood fires (not charcoal)
2. Sift to remove large particles and charcoal
3. Mix with minimal water to form paste (1:3 ash to water by volume)
4. Strain to remove remaining particles

**Effectiveness:**
- Works through: abrasive action (removes pathogens mechanically), alkalinity (slight antimicrobial effect), and mild soap-like properties
- Kills: ~60-80% of bacteria in 20-30 seconds (less effective than soap, but acceptable alternative)
- Does NOT kill: most viruses, spores; mechanical removal primary mechanism

**Application Technique:**
1. Apply ash paste to hands
2. Rub vigorously for 20-30 seconds
3. Rinse with minimal water if available (small amount to remove ash)
4. If no water available: dry hands with clean cloth and accept ash residue (still provides benefit)

**Limitations:**
- Abrasive texture; can cause hand irritation with repeated use
- Not suitable for patients with skin erosions
- Less effective than soap/water
- Appearance (ash stains hands); cultural resistance possible

**When to Use:**
- When alcohol unavailable and water extremely scarce
- Before eating, preparing food, medical procedures
- After visible contamination (though ash less effective than water for this)

### Combination Approach (Ash + Limited Water)

When small amounts of water available:
1. Use ash paste to remove visible soil/contamination (mechanical action)
2. Rinse with minimal water (50-100 mL per person per day)
3. Follow with alcohol sanitizer if available (kills pathogens paste couldn't remove)

This maximizes effect with minimal water use.

### Hand Hygiene Frequency in Water Scarcity

Prioritize hand hygiene at highest-risk moments:
1. **Critical (non-negotiable):** Before food preparation, before eating, before touching patient wounds/dressings, after toileting
2. **Important:** Before donning gloves, after glove removal, between patients
3. **Defer if water/alcohol very limited:** After non-contact activities (documentation, equipment handling without contamination)

Educate community: not all hand contacts require hygiene; focus on highest-risk scenarios when resources limited.

### Staff Hand Hygiene Monitoring in Water Scarcity

Challenge: ensuring compliance when resources extremely limited. Solution:
- Assign hand-hygiene monitor (peer accountability)
- Provide visible hand hygiene stations (ash paste bowl, alcohol container) near patient care areas
- Document compliance (tally sheets)
- Praise-reinforce (acknowledge correct practice to encourage continued compliance)
- Provide staff hand lotion (prevents excessive drying from frequent hygiene, improves compliance)

</section>

<section id="environmental-disinfection">

## Environmental Disinfection with Improvised Agents

Hospital surfaces contaminate healthcare workers and patients. Regular environmental disinfection reduces transmission significantly. Without commercial disinfectants, improvised solutions from household items maintain acceptable infection control.

### Bleach-Based Disinfection

Sodium hypochlorite (household bleach) is most accessible, cost-effective disinfectant. Effectiveness depends on concentration and contact time.

**Preparation:**

| Bleach Concentration | Water Volume | Use Case |
|---|---|---|
| 1:10 dilution (10% bleach) | 1 part bleach + 9 parts water | Heavy contamination (blood/body fluids) |
| 1:50 dilution (2% bleach) | 1 part bleach + 49 parts water | General surface disinfection (floors, equipment, walls) |
| 1:100 dilution (1% bleach) | 1 part bleach + 99 parts water | Hand hygiene prep (non-drinking use only) |

**Application Protocol:**

1. **Pre-cleaning:** Remove visible organic material (blood, secretions, dirt) with cloth first—bleach less effective on dirty surfaces
2. **Application:** Spray or wipe surface with bleach solution
3. **Contact time:** Let sit 5-10 minutes for general surfaces, 10-15 minutes for heavy contamination
4. **Rinsing:** Wipe with damp cloth or rinse with clean water if surface will contact food/mouth
5. **Drying:** Allow to air dry; UV sunlight provides additional disinfection

**Cautions:**
- Corrosive: damages metal, corrodes certain plastics over time
- Fumes: ensure ventilation; do NOT mix with other cleaners (especially ammonia—creates toxic chlorine gas)
- Skin irritation: wear gloves; do NOT use on skin (use alcohol-based or ash instead)
- Storage: bleach degrades over time (loses potency); store in cool, dark location; replace every 6 months if stored long-term
- Concentration matters: too dilute = ineffective; too concentrated = damage without improved kill rate

**Surfaces to Disinfect:**
- High-touch surfaces: door handles, light switches, bed rails
- Equipment: stethoscope, thermometer, blood pressure cuff
- Flooring: patient room, bathrooms, food preparation areas
- Walls/furniture: if visibly contaminated with blood/body fluids

### Iodine-Based Disinfection

If available, iodine solution (tincture of iodine or Betadine dilution) provides alternative.

**Preparation:**
- 1:10 dilution of iodine solution in water (brown color indicates appropriate dilution)
- Contact time: 5-10 minutes
- Application: same as bleach (pre-clean, apply, contact time, rinse)

**Advantages over bleach:** Less corrosive, less fume irritation, still highly effective
**Disadvantages:** Less available than bleach, more expensive, can stain surfaces

### Heat/Steam Disinfection

When chemical disinfectants unavailable, heat kills pathogens through protein denaturation.

**Dry Heat (oven, fire):**
- 160°C (320°F) for 2 hours: kills most bacteria/viruses
- 121°C (250°F) for 20 minutes: kills most pathogens
- Suitable for: metal instruments, glassware, ceramics
- NOT suitable for: plastics, fabrics, papers (melt/burn)

**Steam/Boiling Water (equipment disinfection):**
- Boiling water (100°C) for 20 minutes: kills most organisms (not spores)
- Pressure cooker (121°C, 15 PSI) for 15 minutes: more effective against spores
- Suitable for: metal instruments, glassware, cloth
- Application: submerge in boiling water, maintain temperature, remove with sterile forceps

**Limitations:** Energy-intensive; requires fuel; slower than chemical disinfection

### Environmental Disinfection Schedule

**High-Risk Areas (Disinfect Daily):**
- Patient care areas (bedside, equipment surfaces)
- Bathrooms and toileting areas
- Food preparation surfaces

**Medium-Risk Areas (Disinfect 2-3x Weekly):**
- Floors (if visibly contaminated, daily; otherwise 2-3x weekly)
- Walls (if contaminated; otherwise monthly)
- Door handles, light switches

**Low-Risk Areas (Monthly or PRN):**
- Storage areas
- Administrative areas
- Hallways

### Waste Handling and Disinfection

Contaminated waste (used dressings, bloodied materials, body fluid-contaminated items) requires careful handling:

1. **Segregation:** Separate potentially infectious waste from regular trash
2. **Containment:** Use puncture-resistant container (plastic-lined if available)
3. **Disinfection:** If incineration unavailable:
   - Soak contaminated waste in bleach 1:10 for 2 hours (if liquid waste/blood)
   - Bury in designated hole >30 meters from water sources (last resort; decomposition time variable)
   - Burn if possible (though less ideal than burial for containment)
4. **Handling:** Wear gloves when handling contaminated waste; never touch face; dispose gloves in contaminated waste, not regular trash

:::warning
Improperly disposed medical waste causes infection outbreaks in communities. Contaminated waste left visible causes flies/rodents to spread pathogens. Careful waste handling is non-negotiable infection control measure.
:::

</section>

<section id="isolation-room">

## Home Isolation Room Setup (Negative Pressure Improvisation)

For managing confirmed or suspected infectious disease patients in home settings, isolation limits transmission to household members.

### Single-Room Isolation Minimum Requirements

**Space Selection:**
- Separate bedroom with closeable door
- Dedicated bathroom if possible (if not, designated toilet basin for patient use only)
- Window for ventilation
- Minimal household traffic (avoid main living areas)

**Creating Negative Pressure Improvisation:**
True negative pressure (air flows INTO room) requires mechanical systems unavailable in homes. Improvisation:

1. **Exhaust/Egress:** Open window and place a fan to blow AIR OUT (creating slight negative pressure by air movement outward)
2. **Entrance filtration:** Place HEPA filter (if available) in window opposite exhaust; incoming air filtered
3. **Door sealing:** Tape cracks around door with plastic sheeting; leave small gap at bottom for air flow
4. **Ventilation rate:** Replace room air 6-12 times daily if possible (leave window open partially, fan running continuously)

**Limitations:** Improvised systems much less effective than true HVAC negative pressure; still provides barrier and reduces aerosol transmission vs. no isolation.

### Isolation Room Protocols

**Entry and Donning:**
1. Establish "donning area" just outside room (hallway or anteroom)
2. Put on N95 mask, gown, gloves, eye protection before entering
3. Touch nothing in room except what necessary for patient care
4. Remove gloves if contaminated; replace with fresh ones between care tasks

**Care Provision:**
- Patient remains in bed; minimize movement around house
- Dedicated equipment: thermometer, blood pressure cuff, stethoscope (do NOT remove from room; disinfect with alcohol daily)
- Meals: deliver to room on disposable plates/utensils; patient returns dishes to kitchen (not hand-to-hand contact)
- Toileting: dedicated basin/bedpan (no shared toilet if possible); disinfect thoroughly after each use with bleach 1:10

**Doffing and Exit:**
1. Remove gloves carefully (turn inside-out as removing, seal in bag)
2. Remove gown (roll up inside-out, seal in bag)
3. Perform hand hygiene (alcohol sanitizer or ash paste)
4. Remove mask LAST (place in bag or dispose)
5. Leave room; close door
6. Perform hand hygiene again before leaving anteroom

**Duration:**
- Respiratory illness: isolation 5-10 days after symptoms start (if fever-free for 24 hours and improving, may end isolation)
- Measles: isolation 4 days after rash appearance
- Chickenpox: isolation until all lesions crusted over (~5-7 days)
- Tuberculosis: isolation 2 weeks after starting appropriate antibiotic therapy

### Household Member Precautions

Non-isolated household members:
- Wear mask around patient (especially during coughing)
- Perform hand hygiene after patient contact
- Avoid touching face, eyes, mouth
- Maintain distance (>6 feet if possible)
- Eat in separate location from patient if feasible
- Do NOT share food, drinks, utensils, towels, bedding

**Monitoring:** Check non-isolated members daily for symptoms; if symptoms develop, move to isolation immediately.

### Terminal Cleaning After Isolation

When patient recovers or dies:
1. Empty trash from room; seal and disinfect outside
2. Wash bedding/clothing in hot water with soap
3. Wipe all surfaces with bleach 1:50 solution (10-minute contact time)
4. Leave windows open 24 hours (UV disinfection from sunlight)
5. Allow 1 week before room reoccupancy if possible

## Wound Irrigation Fluid Preparation

Clean water for irrigation is critical in preventing wound infection. In collapse scenarios, access to sterile saline unavailable. Understanding safe improvisation from basic materials maintains infection prevention for wound care.

### Saline Solution Preparation (Home-Made)

**Goal:** Create solution with osmolarity similar to normal saline (isotonic, doesn't damage tissue).

**Standard Formula:**
- 1 liter boiled water (cooled to room temperature)
- 9 grams salt (sodium chloride) = 0.9% saline = isotonic
- OR 1 teaspoon salt (approximately 5 grams) per 1 liter = approximate field formula

**Preparation Steps:**
1. Boil water in clean pot for 5 minutes (kills most pathogens)
2. Allow to cool to room temperature (do NOT use hot water—damages tissue)
3. Measure salt precisely if possible (scale preferred; if not, use household teaspoon—not as precise but acceptable)
4. Dissolve salt completely (stir well)
5. Transfer to clean glass/plastic bottle
6. Label with: "Normal Saline," date prepared, expiration date (use within 24 hours if room temperature, 48 hours if refrigerated)
7. Store in cool location

**Sterility Compromise:** Homemade saline is NOT sterile (may contain dust, microorganisms). Acceptable for field irrigation but higher infection risk than sterile commercial saline. Mitigate by:
- Using immediately after preparation (don't store)
- Using boiled water (highest pathogen kill)
- Keeping bottle tightly sealed between uses
- Discarding if bottle left open >4 hours

### Boiling Water Protocols for Safe Cooling

Boiled water must cool to room temperature before wound irrigation (hot water causes tissue burns).

**Safe Cooling Methods:**
1. **Room temperature cooling:** Boil water, cover loosely with cloth (prevents contamination while allowing steam escape), leave at room temperature 30-60 minutes until cool to touch
2. **Ice bath:** (if ice available) Place hot water pot in cold water bath; stir frequently; cools in 10-15 minutes
3. **Forced air cooling:** (if fan available) Boil water, transfer to shallow pan, direct fan across surface; cools in 5-10 minutes

**Do NOT:**
- Use ice directly in wound irrigation water (increases infection risk; ice may not be sterile)
- Cool in refrigerator then use (risks hypothermia if large volume used for irrigation)
- Add ice chips to hot water (same contamination risk)

### Alternative Irrigation Fluids

If salt unavailable:
- **Plain boiled water:** Hypotonic (slightly damages tissue cells, but acceptable short-term); use if no salt available
- **Sugar water:** 5% glucose (50 grams sugar per 1 liter boiled water); isotonic; acceptable alternative
- **Tap water (if potable):** Least ideal; chlorinated water may irritate wound; use only if no boiled alternatives

### Wound Irrigation Technique in Field

**Setup:**
1. Clean wound site with antiseptic (iodine, alcohol) to reduce bacterial load first
2. Position patient so irrigation fluid drains away (don't run into eyes)
3. Use clean (non-sterile is acceptable) cloth to catch runoff

**Application:**
1. Use bulb syringe, large syringe (if available), or clean cloth to apply irrigation fluid
2. Pour or inject saline into wound with moderate pressure (enough to flush debris, not enough to force fluid into surrounding tissue)
3. Repeat 3-5 times until debris removed and fluid running clear
4. Volume varies with wound size (small superficial wound = 50-100 mL; large contaminated wound = 500-1000 mL)

**Drying:**
- Pat dry with clean cloth
- Allow to air dry 5-10 minutes before bandaging (helps prevent infection)

**Frequency:**
- Fresh wounds: irrigation daily for first 3-5 days
- Chronic wounds: irrigate before dressing change (daily to every 2-3 days)
- Signs of infection: increase to twice daily (fever, purulent drainage, spreading redness)

:::warning
Inadequate wound irrigation allows bacteria to multiply in devitalized tissue. Use sufficient volume and repeat until fluid runs clear. This is among the most critical infection prevention measures in field wound care.
:::

## Antibiotic Stewardship Basics

### Antimicrobial Resistance Crisis

Overuse/misuse of antibiotics has created multi-drug resistant organisms (MRSA, MDR-TB, vancomycin-resistant enterococci, carbapenem-resistant gram-negatives). In survival settings, antibiotics are finite resource. Resistance development means we're returning to pre-antibiotic era where minor infections = death. Stewardship (using antibiotics wisely) preserves efficacy for critical infections.

### Principles of Antibiotic Stewardship

**Use narrowest spectrum effective:** Culture/sensitivity guides choice; if not available, select agent covering likely pathogen. **Complete full course:** Stopping early when feeling better allows resistant mutants to survive and multiply. **Avoid prophylactic use:** Reserve antibiotics for actual infections, not prevention (except specific surgical prophylaxis scenarios). **Match dosing to infection severity:** Severe infections require higher doses/longer courses; minor infections need minimal treatment. **Monitor response:** Clinical improvement (fever resolution, reduced pain) within 24-48 hours suggests organism susceptible. Continued deterioration suggests resistant organism or wrong antibiotic; change therapy.

### Distinguishing Viral from Bacterial Infection

Virus (no antibiotic effective): fever, myalgia, generalized symptoms, clear secretions. **Bacterial infection:** Localized findings (pneumonia = crackles on lung exam), purulent discharge (yellow/green sputum, pus from wound), elevated WBC if available. Throat culture/rapid tests not always available in survival. General rule: upper respiratory infection (runny nose, cough, sore throat) mostly viral; do NOT use antibiotics. Pneumonia, pus from skin infection, UTI with symptoms = likely bacterial, antibiotics appropriate.

### In-Field Antibiotic Selection

Ideal: culture-directed therapy. Realistic (survival): empiric therapy targeting likely organisms. Sepsis from unknown source: broad-spectrum (if available, amoxicillin-clavulanate or similar covers common community pathogens). Pneumonia: respiratory fluoroquinolone if available, else amoxicillin. Skin/soft tissue infection with pus: beta-lactam (amoxicillin) or fluoroquinolone. Urinary tract infection: fluoroquinolone (penetrates urine well) or trimethoprim-sulfa if available. Diarrhea: avoid antibiotics unless patient toxic/septic (actually worsens outcomes in most viral/toxin-mediated diarrhea). Severe diarrhea with bloody stools: consider fluoroquinolone. Always consider: do I have this antibiotic? Will it damage patient (allergy)? Can I complete full course? If answers are no, defer unless life-threatening.

### Storage and Preservation of Antibiotics

Antibiotics degrade over time, especially in heat. Store in cool, dry place (<70°F ideal). Keep in original container with desiccant if possible. Rotate stock (first-in-first-out). Check expiration dates regularly (expired antibiotics less effective, toxicity concerns rare unless tetracyclines/old aspirin). In survival: prioritize preserving high-value antibiotics (fluoroquinolones, macrolides) for serious infections; use lower-potency agents (penicillin, amoxicillin) for minor infections first.

<section id="quarantine-facility">

## Quarantine Facility Design

For community-scale quarantine facility planning including staffing, supply logistics, and communication systems, see <a href="../public-health-epidemiology.html#section-quarantine">Public Health Systems & Epidemiology — Quarantine Systems</a>.

### Outbreak Response: Isolation Space Requirements

Separating infected from non-infected is critical in community outbreak. Optimal: separate building or closed unit. Minimal: separate room with door that closes, preferably with dedicated bathroom. If impossible (small community, limited space): physical separation >6 feet minimum, barrier (plastic sheeting) between patients ideal, dedicated equipment. Design considerations: entrance area for PPE donning/doffing (prevents contamination spread), negative pressure if possible (air flows IN, reducing exposure to community). For airborne diseases: exhaust air through HEPA filter if available; if not, exhaust away from population.

### Staffing an Isolation Unit

Assign dedicated personnel if possible (reduces cross-contamination risk vs rotating staff). Ideally, isolated staff does NOT work with general population simultaneously. If rotation unavoidable: work isolation shift first, then general care (not reverse). Provide appropriate training on donning/doffing, hand hygiene, equipment handling. Mental health: isolation work is stressful; provide support, monitor staff health, encourage rest periods. In small groups: assign rotating schedule to prevent caregiver burnout.

### Waste Management in Outbreak

Contaminated materials (dressings, body fluids, used equipment) are biohazard. If available, double-bag waste (one person places item in first bag, second person handles sealed bag). Burn waste if possible (high-temperature incineration destroys pathogens). If burning unavailable: bury in designated pit >100 feet from water, cover with lime/bleach-saturated soil. Sharps (needles, lancets) in puncture-resistant container, labeled "BIOHAZARD." Never allow contaminated waste to enter general trash or water supply.

### Supplies in Isolation Unit

Stock dedicated supplies to prevent contamination spread: PPE (gloves, masks, gowns, eye protection), hand hygiene (soap, water, sanitizer), disinfectants (bleach, alcohol, other), cleaning supplies (cloth, paper towels, mops), patient care items (medications, diagnostic supplies, dressings). Minimize item removal from unit. If items must leave: double-bag, mark "contaminated," disinfect exterior before transport. Monitor inventory; resupply regularly (supply shortages lead to protocol breaking).

### Communication in Quarantine Facility

Isolated patients experience psychological distress. Maintain communication with loved ones (phone, visits through barrier if possible). Provide clear information: why isolation necessary, expected duration, how to report worsening symptoms. Educate patient on infection prevention (maintain respiratory etiquette, hand hygiene, wear mask if infectious). Morale = critical factor in compliance; supportive approach increases cooperation and reduces escape attempts.

<section id="troubleshooting">

## Troubleshooting

### Problem: Hands Become Irritated After Frequent Handwashing

**Cause:** Frequent soap/water stripping natural skin oils, alcohol causing drying/cracking

**Solution:**
- Reduce alcohol-based sanitizer use (prioritize water-based washing)
- Pat hands dry rather than rubbing
- Use moisturizing lotion/oil after hand hygiene if available (coconut oil, lanolin, glycerin)
- Consider alternative soaps (some are gentler than others)
- Take "hand hygiene breaks" (short periods without repeated washing)
- If cracked/bleeding: hand hygiene critical; infection risk high; apply antibiotic ointment, cover with gloves

**Prevention:** During high-use periods (outbreak), plan for skin irritation; stock moisturizer supplies.

### Problem: Gloves Tear/Puncture During Patient Care

**Cause:** Fingernails, sharp objects, rough handling

**Solution:**
- Keep nails trimmed
- Handle sharps carefully, establish protocol for sharp passing (avoid hand-to-hand)
- If tear suspected: don't assume safe; perform hand hygiene immediately, change to fresh gloves
- Double-glove for high-risk procedures if possible

**Prevention:** Train team on gentle glove handling, nail trimming, sharp safety.

### Problem: Dressing Becomes Soaked/Saturated Frequently

**Cause:** Excessive wound drainage (infection, inflammatory phase, poor dressing adherence)

**Solution:**
- Increase dressing change frequency (daily or more)
- Use absorbent dressing (gauze vs. non-adherent) if drainage heavy
- Consider drain placement (professional surgical procedure if available)
- Observe drainage character (clear/pale = normal; thick/purulent = infection)
- Elevate limb (gravity reduces swelling/drainage)
- If copious drainage + systemic illness: may indicate deep infection requiring drainage

**Prevention:** Assess expected drainage post-procedure; stock appropriate dressing quantity.

### Problem: Team Not Complying with Hand Hygiene

**Cause:** Habit, rushing, perceived inconvenience, water scarcity

**Solution:**
- Establish culture: hand hygiene is non-negotiable, not optional
- Assign compliance officer: one person monitors, reminds, records
- Make it easy: place hand hygiene stations near care areas
- Positive reinforcement: acknowledge good compliance
- Education: show infection consequences; reinforce why it matters
- Adapt to constraints: if water scarce, use alcohol sanitizer; if time-pressured, reduce hand hygiene time to 20-30 seconds

**Prevention:** Plan hand hygiene supply, train before problems arise.

### Problem: Sterile Field Contaminated Multiple Times

**Cause:** Team unfamiliar with aseptic technique, overcrowding, inadequate training

**Solution:**
- Stop; discard entire setup; start over (contaminated = compromised)
- Identify contamination: who touched it? What touched it?
- Training: review aseptic principles; practice with low-stakes setups
- Logistics: ensure only essential personnel near sterile field; others stand back
- Environment: if room too crowded, move procedure to quieter space

**Prevention:** Train team on aseptic technique before high-stakes procedures (surgery); practice drills.

### Problem: Patient Developing Infection Despite Precautions

**Cause:** Breakthrough infection (bug overwhelms defenses), missed transmission route, unrecognized infection source

**Solution:**
- Escalate care: increase precautions, reassess diagnosis
- Culture if possible: identify organism, guide antibiotic selection
- Reassess asepsis: was technique actually followed? Was equipment actually sterile?
- Environmental assessment: other cases developing? Suggests common source outbreak
- Patient factors: immune compromised? Poor nutrition? Diabetes? These increase infection risk

**Prevention:** Maintain high asepsis standard; monitor for early signs; escalate early.

### Problem: Supply Shortage—Not Enough Gloves/Masks/Disinfectant

**Cause:** Unexpected demand surge, supply chain disruption

**Solution:**
- Prioritize: high-risk procedures use fresh supplies first
- Reuse: gloves/masks sanitized and reused if shortage severe
- Alternatives: plastic bags as gloves (less protection, but better than nothing); cloth masks from linen (not ideal)
- Rationing: establish minimum-use protocols; reduce non-essential supplies
- Sourcing: contact other groups/facilities; pool resources if possible

**Prevention:** Maintain 3-6 month supply buffer during normal times; rotate stock.

<section id="common-mistakes">

## Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

### Mistake 1: Touching Mask/Goggles During Care

**Why it happens:** Unconscious habit; fogging goggles; itching face

**Why it's dangerous:** Front surface covered in pathogens; touching then adjusting mask/touching face = self-inoculation

**How to prevent:**
- Practice removing mask/goggles only at end of care
- If fogging occurs: pre-treat goggles with anti-fog solution, or position slightly away from face
- Scratching face: keep hands busy, or ask colleague to scratch for you
- Training: demonstrate self-inoculation with visible contaminant (food coloring on gloves = shows spread)

### Mistake 2: Hand Hygiene After Glove Removal, Not Before Donning

**Why it happens:** Assumption gloves prevent all contamination; rushing between patients

**Why it's dangerous:** Hands contaminated between glove removal and hygiene; contamination transferred to new gloves; cross-contamination to environment

**How to prevent:**
- Protocol: glove removal → hand hygiene → don new gloves
- Signage: place reminders at hand hygiene stations
- Monitoring: watch for this specific error; correct immediately

### Mistake 3: Assuming Low-Risk Tasks Don't Require PPE

**Why it happens:** Procedure looks low-risk; PPE feels cumbersome; time pressure

**Why it's dangerous:** Unexpected splash exposure; patient more infectious than appeared; complacency leads to infection

**How to prevent:**
- Standard Precautions mandate PPE for all patient contact
- No exceptions—gloves/hand hygiene minimum for all patient touching
- Risk assessment: if any doubt, use PPE

### Mistake 4: Reusing Contaminated PPE Without Decontamination

**Why it happens:** PPE shortage; believing item "not that dirty"; procrastination

**Why it's dangerous:** Pathogens remain on PPE; spread to next patient/environment

**How to prevent:**
- Protocol: all PPE reuse requires decontamination (alcohol wipe, bleach solution, or boiling)
- Document: record which PPE reused; monitor for infections in subsequent patients
- Ideally: use fresh PPE each time (reuse only during shortage)

### Mistake 5: Boiling Instruments But Not Achieving Full Contact Time

**Why it happens:** Not using thermometer; assuming boiling started when pot added; lid removed, lowering temperature; high altitude (water boils <100°C)

**Why it's dangerous:** Spore-forming bacteria (C. difficile, B. anthracis) may survive <20 minutes; organisms not killed = surgical site infection risk

**How to prevent:**
- Protocol: bring to rolling boil, start 20-minute timer after boiling confirmed
- Thermometer: if available, verify 100°C (212°F) at sea level
- Altitude adjustment: add 10 minutes per 3,000 ft elevation
- Keep lid on: reduces heat loss, maintains temperature

### Mistake 6: Not Separating Clean From Contaminated Areas

**Why it happens:** Limited space; poor planning; unclear responsibility

**Why it's dangerous:** Cross-contamination between clean supplies/patient areas and contaminated materials; spreads infection

**How to prevent:**
- Designate zones: clean medical area separate from bathroom/contaminated waste
- Movement protocol: clean supplies brought IN; contaminated materials taken OUT; one-way flow if possible
- Storage: clean supplies elevated and away from potential contamination
- Training: everyone understands what goes where

### Mistake 7: Administering Antibiotics Without Complete Course

**Why it happens:** Patient feels better; supply concern; forgetting doses

**Why it's dangerous:** Resistant mutants survive and multiply; infection returns worse; resistance spreads to others

**How to prevent:**
- Education: explain need to complete full course even when feeling better
- Documentation: mark off each dose; verify completion
- Monitoring: check in with patient; remove barriers to compliance (pain control, food with dose if GI side effects)

:::warning
**The Most Common Infection Control Failure:** Hand hygiene neglect. It's the most powerful intervention but easiest to skip when busy. The moment you think "I'll skip this time" is when contamination occurs. Every. Single. Time. No exceptions.
:::

</section>

<section id="safety">

## Safety Warnings

:::warning
### Critical Infection Control Warnings

-   Needlestick injury = high transmission risk for bloodborne pathogens (HIV, hepatitis B/C). Immediately: wash with soap and water, encourage bleeding, report incident. Post-exposure prophylaxis if available within hours.
-   Biological spill = potential hazard. Contain with absorbent material, disinfect with bleach 1:1 with water, let stand 10+ minutes, then clean.
-   Outbreak recognition = rapidly spreading illness in community. Isolate cases, alert authorities if possible, implement enhanced precautions, monitor contacts.
-   Personal illness = do NOT provide patient care if symptomatic (respiratory, GI, skin infections). Self-isolate to prevent spread to vulnerable patients.
:::

:::warning
### PPE Shortage Crisis Management

-   Extreme PPE shortage risks caregiver infection, outbreak amplification. Prioritization: use conventional PPE for highest-risk activities (intubation, suctioning, procedures). Reuse respirators between patients only if epidemic severe and supply exhausted (store in paper bag between uses, allow 5-day rest if possible). Cloth masks better than nothing for respiratory protection but provide minimal self-protection (mainly protects others).
-   Glove shortage: reserve for direct blood/body fluid contact; use glove liners (cloth glove underneath extends lifespan). Bare-hand technique acceptable for feeding/toileting if hand hygiene meticulous.
-   Never remove PPE before it's truly exhausted (visible deterioration, functional failure). Each reuse increases contamination risk but is preferable to no protection in high-risk situations.
:::

:::tip
### Best Practices for Ongoing Infection Control

-   Regular hand hygiene checks (audit team compliance, provide feedback)
-   Equipment cleaning protocol (schedule, responsibility assignment)
-   PPE inventory management (monitor supply, reorder pre-shortage)
-   Staff health monitoring (sick leave policy, testing when symptomatic)
-   Environmental maintenance (cleanliness, disinfection, waste management)
-   Document infection rates (track nosocomial infections, outbreak progression)
-   Education and training (annual competency, scenario drills, knowledge reinforcement)
:::

:::tip
### Low-Resource Infection Prevention Innovations

-   Cloth masks: 2-3 layer cotton provides ~50% protection when fit properly; more effective than nothing. Wash daily with soap/water, air dry (UV from sun provides disinfection).
-   Disinfectant from household items: bleach (sodium hypochlorite) is most cost-effective, stable disinfectant. Alternative: iodine solution (if available), alcohol (if available), boiling water (100% effective if contact time sufficient).
-   Water conservation: reuse water for non-critical cleaning (wash hands with collected rainwater, store cleaner water for drinking). Boil water in bulk when fuel available, cool and store for multiple uses.
-   PPE improvisation: plastic bags for gown (tie at wrists/ankles), cloth tied around nose/mouth as mask, eyeglasses as goggles (not ideal but better than none).
-   Equipment reuse through meticulous cleaning: thermometer sheaths, blood pressure cuff, stethoscope can be disinfected and reused if cleaned properly between patients.
:::

### Common Infection Control Failures

-   **Hand hygiene neglect:** Most important measure; inconsistent compliance negates other precautions
-   **Cross-contamination:** Touching sterile field, reusing non-sterile items, inadequate hand hygiene between tasks
-   **Improper PPE use:** Touching mask/goggles front surface, dangling gloves over clean surface, reusing contaminated PPE without cleaning
-   **Inadequate disinfection:** Using ineffective agents, insufficient contact time, not accounting for contamination level
-   **Missed exposure risk:** Assuming low-risk activities don't require precautions; all patient contact = potential exposure

## Water-Scarce Hand Hygiene Protocols

In resource-limited scenarios, water scarcity or absence makes traditional hand hygiene (soap and water) impossible. Alternative approaches preserve hand hygiene efficacy using alcohol-based preparations, ash-based methods, or other improvised agents.

### Alcohol-Based Hand Sanitizers (Optimal Alternative)

Alcohol-based sanitizers kill 99.9% of bacteria and many viruses when properly formulated. Effective formulations contain 60-95% ethanol or isopropyl alcohol (optimal 70%).

**Preparation of hand sanitizer from available alcohol:**
1. **Ethanol source:** Medical-grade alcohol, spirits (vodka minimum 80 proof), or improvised fermentation (see pharmaceutical production guides)
2. **Formula:** Mix 70% ethanol + 30% water (or lower-alcohol spirits diluted accordingly)
3. **Additives (optional):** Glycerin 2% (prevents drying), aloe vera gel 2% (skin conditioning)
4. **Storage:** Keep in sealed container; alcohol volatile and loses potency over time

**Application technique:**
- Apply 2-3mL (coin-sized amount) to dry hands
- Rub hands together vigorously 20-30 seconds, ensuring coverage of: palm, back of hand, between fingers, under nails, wrists
- Allow to air dry completely (do not wipe off)
- Effective against most bacteria; less effective against norovirus and some spore-forming bacteria (C. difficile)

**Limitations:** Does not physically remove soil/visible debris. If hands visibly dirty, brush off visible contamination first, then apply sanitizer. Avoid touching eyes/face immediately after application (irritation).

### Ash-Based Hand Cleaning (Improvised Alternative)

When no soap or alcohol available, wood ash provides mild abrasive and alkaline properties that help remove microbes and soil.

**Preparation of ash cleaner:**
1. Collect wood ash from fire (hardwood preferred; avoid coal ash)
2. Sift through fine cloth to remove large particles and charcoal
3. Mix with small amount water to form paste (consistency like wet sand)
4. Alternative: add ash to water to create lye water (see water purification guides)

**Application technique:**
1. Wet hands with available water (small amount sufficient)
2. Apply ash paste and rub vigorously for 30-60 seconds
3. Rub between fingers, under nails, on wrists
4. Rinse with water (or wipe with cloth if water scarce)
5. Repeat if hands remain visibly soiled
6. Allow to air dry or dry on clean cloth

**Efficacy:** Ash removes microbes and soil through mechanical action (abrasion) and alkaline environment. Not as effective as soap, but substantially better than water alone. Studies show 50-70% reduction in pathogenic bacteria.

**Limitations:** Ash particles may irritate skin with repeated use; avoid in people with dermatitis or open hand wounds. Ash efficacy variable depending on source and burn temperature.

### Soap Substitutes (When Soap Unavailable)

**Plant-based alternatives:**
- **Soapnut shells (soapberry):** Natural saponins clean effectively. Boil 4-5 shells in 2 cups water for 10 minutes; strain liquid and use as hand wash (1-2 drops per hand)
- **Aloe vera:** Cut leaf, compress to extract gel; contains natural compounds with mild antimicrobial activity. Mix with small amount ash if available
- **Lye water from ash:** See water purification section; extremely alkaline and removes soil/microbes (caution: irritating to skin; rinse thoroughly afterward)

**Oil-based alternatives:**
- **Animal fat + ash (improvised soap):** Mix rendered animal fat with wood ash lye water (see soap-making guides); produces crude soap if time permits
- **Plant oils with ash:** Similar process; less effective but usable if resources available

### Essential Hygiene Points in Water-Scarce Environments

- **Prioritize hand hygiene before eating/food preparation** — most critical transfer point for pathogens
- **Clean hands after toilet use** — highest microbial load; prevent fecal-oral transmission
- **Use sanitizer/ash method multiple times daily** if touching patients or handling wound care
- **Minimize hand contact to face** — reduces direct transmission route
- **Designate separate cloth/towel for drying hands** after washing (reusable cloth should be air-dried and sun-exposed daily for disinfection)

## Environmental Disinfection with Improvised Agents

Standard disinfectants (hospital-grade, commercial bleach) may be unavailable. Improvised alternatives using household materials provide effective disinfection for most pathogens.

### Improvised Bleach Solutions from Household Materials

**Chlorine-based disinfection:**
- **Commercial household bleach (3-6% sodium hypochlorite):** Most accessible option. Prepare dilutions:
  - 0.5% solution (for routine surfaces): 1 part bleach : 10 parts water
  - 1% solution (for blood/body fluid spills): 1 part bleach : 5 parts water
  - 5% solution (high-level disinfection): dilute minimally or use undiluted if available

- **Calcium hypochlorite (pool chlorine):** Alternative if bleach unavailable. Contains 60-70% available chlorine. Dissolve 1 teaspoon in 1 liter water to achieve ~3% solution. Use at same concentrations as household bleach (divide by half for effective chlorine concentration).

- **Chlorine tablets (water purification tablets):** Each tablet (~30mg) adds ~0.3mg/mL available chlorine. Dissolve 3-4 tablets in 1 liter water for disinfection solution.

**Preparation and safety:**
1. Mix bleach/chlorine in well-ventilated area (fumes irritating)
2. Use glass or plastic containers (avoid metal—chlorine corrodes)
3. Label concentration and date prepared (chlorine solutions degrade over days; prepare fresh)
4. Wear gloves and eye protection
5. Do not mix with other chemicals (especially ammonia—produces toxic chlorine gas)

**Application to surfaces:**
1. Pre-clean surface to remove visible dirt (chlorine ineffective on soiled surfaces)
2. Apply disinfectant solution
3. Allow 10-20 minutes contact time (longer for visible contamination)
4. Wipe with clean cloth or rinse with water
5. Air dry

### Alternative Disinfectants (When Bleach Unavailable)

**Iodine-based disinfection:**
- **Tincture of iodine (2%):** Apply directly to surfaces; effective against bacteria, viruses, fungi
- **Iodine solution (5%):** Prepare by diluting tincture; use at same concentrations as bleach solutions
- **Lugol's iodine:** 5% iodine in potassium iodide solution; effective but staining
- Contact time: 1-5 minutes for most pathogens
- Caution: iodine stains surfaces brown; irritating to skin and eyes; avoid in patients with iodine allergy

**Vinegar/acetic acid (5%):**
- Household white vinegar contains ~5% acetic acid
- Apply undiluted; less effective than chlorine but better than water alone
- Contact time: 10-15 minutes
- Effective against common bacteria; limited virucidal activity
- Advantage: minimal toxicity; safe for most surfaces
- Limitation: slower action than bleach solutions

**Alcohol (70%):**
- Isopropyl or ethanol 70% solution kills bacteria, viruses, and fungal spores
- Apply liberally; allow to evaporate (contact time 30-60 seconds)
- Effective for small surfaces, instruments, hands; not ideal for large area disinfection (cost, volatility)

**Hot water/steam:**
- Boiling water (100°C) kills most pathogens in 10-20 minutes
- Steam from kettles (directly applied or item suspended above) provides disinfection
- Especially useful for cloth, small instruments, dishes
- Safe for all heat-tolerant items

:::warning
Environmental disinfection of blood/body fluid spills requires immediate action. Do not delay disinfection—pathogens remain viable for hours/days on surfaces. Even if resources limited, disinfect with whatever available (water, vinegar, sunlight). Chlorine-based solutions optimal but alternatives acceptable if chlorine unavailable.
:::

### UV Light Disinfection

Ultraviolet (UV) light, particularly UV-C (wavelength 200-280nm), kills microbes through DNA damage. Field applications:

**Sunlight disinfection (UV-B/UVA):**
- Direct sunlight kills vegetative bacteria in 1-3 hours
- Viruses killed in 2-5 hours depending on intensity
- Most effective on transparent/translucent items (water, glass)
- Less effective on opaque surfaces (some penetration loss)
- Effective for: cleaning cloth/laundry, water disinfection, small items
- Method: Spread items in direct sunlight for 3-5 hours; rotate to ensure all surfaces exposed

**Improvised UV lamps (if electricity available):**
- Germicidal UV bulbs (254nm peak) extremely effective but produce ozone—requires ventilation
- Fluorescent blacklight bulbs (365nm, UVA) less effective but safer than germicidal bulbs
- Cost-prohibitive in most collapse scenarios unless salvaged equipment available

## Home Isolation Room Setup (Negative Pressure Improvisation)

When managing a patient with highly transmissible respiratory illness (suspected COVID-19, tuberculosis, measles) in home setting, improvised negative pressure isolation reduces airborne transmission to household contacts.

### Negative Pressure Principles

Negative pressure isolation (room pressure lower than surrounding areas) directs airflow from clean areas into contaminated patient room, preventing contaminated air from flowing out. Pathogens concentrated and exhausted outside reduce transmission.

**Improvised methods without mechanical equipment:**

**Method 1: Window Fan Extraction**
1. Install exhaust fan (or duct fan if available) in window, directing outward
2. Seal window around fan with duct tape to prevent air bypass
3. Keep patient room door closed
4. Create small hole (1-2 inches) in door near bottom with tube extending outside through wall (air entry point)
5. Run exhaust fan continuously
6. Result: continuous air movement from hallway → patient room → outside; 3-5 air changes per hour achievable with strong fan

**Method 2: Bathroom Exhaust Modification**
1. If home has bathroom exhaust venting outside, locate venting path
2. Redirect patient room door slightly ajar
3. Close all other doors in home
4. Open small gap between patient room and bathroom (5-10cm)
5. Activate bathroom exhaust
6. Airflow: patient room → bathroom → outside; less efficient than dedicated fan but functional

**Method 3: Passive Window Pressure Differential**
1. Open windows on far side of home (upwind based on prevailing wind direction)
2. Keep patient room window cracked slightly open (just enough to create air movement)
3. Position patient bed away from window (avoid direct air stream)
4. Relies on natural wind/pressure differences; less reliable but zero energy cost
5. Monitor for adequate air movement (paper strip near window should show gentle movement)

### Practical Isolation Room Setup

**Room selection:**
- Bedroom with window preferred (allows exhaust outlet and natural light)
- Single occupancy if possible (avoid shared room with non-infected person)
- Separate bathroom ideal (reduces shared surface contact)

**Surface preparation:**
1. Cover surfaces patient may touch with plastic sheeting (holds droplets, easier cleanup)
2. Designate dedicated waste container with liner (can be burned or buried)
3. Place cloth under patient if respiratory secretion risk (contains droplets)

**Caregiver protection:**
- Caregiver wears N95 mask or surgical mask (if N95 unavailable) during room entry
- Limit entries to essential care only
- Don and doff PPE outside room (prevents contamination spread)
- Hand hygiene before/after room entry

**Duration of isolation:**
- Respiratory illness: 24-48 hours after fever resolution and symptoms improving
- TB: 2 weeks after antibiotic initiation (patient becomes non-infectious)
- Measles/chickenpox: until lesions crusted or rash resolves
- COVID-19: 5-10 days depending on severity (conservative: until symptoms resolved + 24 hours fever-free)

:::tip
Improvised negative pressure isolation significantly reduces transmission to household contacts. Even non-optimal setups (door closed + opened nearby window + exhaust fan) reduce airborne pathogen exposure by 70-80% compared to no isolation.
:::

## Wound Irrigation Fluid Preparation

Clean irrigation fluids are essential for wound management. Normal saline (0.9% sodium chloride) is optimal, but when unavailable, improvised sterile irrigation solutions provide adequate wound cleaning.

### Normal Saline Preparation from Household Salt

**Formula:** 0.9% sodium chloride (0.9 grams salt per 100mL water)

**Preparation steps:**
1. **Measure salt:** Use clean spoon or measuring cup. 0.9g salt ≈ 1/6 teaspoon (approximately)
2. **Measure water:** 100mL clean water (approximately 6-7 tablespoons)
3. **Dissolve:** Add salt to water, stir until completely dissolved
4. **Sterilization options:**
   - **Boil method:** Bring to rolling boil for 5 minutes after adding salt; allow to cool to room temperature before use
   - **Filter + sun method:** Filter through clean cloth (removes particles), expose to direct sunlight 2-3 hours (UV disinfection)
   - **Chemical disinfection:** Add 1-2 drops household bleach per 100mL (minimal chlorine for disinfection without damaging wounds)

**Storage:**
- Use same day if possible
- If storage necessary, keep in sterile sealed container in cool location
- Discard after 3-5 days (contamination risk increases)
- Mark container with preparation date

### Alternative Irrigation Fluids (If Salt Unavailable)

**Boiled water (plain):**
- Less optimal than saline (hypotonic; causes cell swelling) but acceptable for wound irrigation
- Boil 5-10 minutes, cool to room temperature
- Reduces bacterial load through dilution and mechanical flushing
- Allow to cool completely before use (hot liquid causes pain)

**Dilute antiseptic solutions:**
- **Dilute iodine:** 1 drop tincture iodine per 100mL water (very dilute to avoid toxicity)
- **Dilute vinegar:** 1 tablespoon white vinegar per 500mL water (mild antimicrobial)
- **Dilute honey:** 1 teaspoon honey per 100mL water (honey has mild antimicrobial properties)

**Herbal infusions (if time permits):**
- **Chamomile tea:** Steep 1-2 tea bags in 200mL boiling water; strain through cloth; cool
- **Calendula (marigold):** Steep petals in boiling water; strain; cool; mild antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory
- **Plantain leaf:** Boil leaves 5-10 minutes; strain; cool; used historically for wound cleaning

### Irrigation Technique

1. **Position patient** to allow fluid drainage from wound (use basin/cloth to catch runoff)
2. **Irrigate slowly** with syringe or IV bag tubing (30-60mL per minute typical rate)
3. **Duration:** 5-10 minutes continuous irrigation to thoroughly flush wound
4. **Direction:** Irrigation fluid flows from clean area toward open wound (prevents surface contamination from entering wound)
5. **After irrigation:** Allow wound to air dry or gently pat with clean cloth; cover with sterile dressing if available (or clean cloth if not)

### Boiled Water Cooling Protocols

When preparing irrigation fluids by boiling, cooling is critical for patient safety (prevent thermal injury) and practical speed.

**Accelerated cooling methods:**
1. **Ice bath method:** Place pot of boiled water in larger container of ice/cold water; change ice as it melts; cooling to room temperature in 10-15 minutes
2. **Running water method:** Hold pot under cold running water (if available); stir occasionally; 10-20 minutes to cool
3. **Cloth evaporation:** Wet cloth over pot (evaporative cooling); remove when cooled (~20-30 minutes)
4. **Passive cooling:** Leave uncovered in cool location; allow 30-60 minutes for cooling to room temperature
5. **Fan acceleration:** If mechanical fan available, blow air across pot (evaporative cooling); 15-20 minutes

**Temperature check before use:**
- Test temperature on inside wrist (like checking infant formula)
- Should feel comfortably warm, not hot
- If uncertain, wait additional 5-10 minutes rather than risk thermal injury
