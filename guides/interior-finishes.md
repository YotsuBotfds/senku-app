Interior finishes transform raw structural elements (walls, floors, ceilings) into functional, beautiful living spaces. From traditional lime plaster to modern drywall, from handmade tiles to polished concrete, finish choices affect durability, aesthetics, maintenance, and the health of building inhabitants.

<section id="wall-plaster">

## Wall Plaster Systems

Plaster is applied as successive layers, each with different composition and purpose.

### Lime Plaster

Lime plaster (calcium hydroxide-based) is the historical standard, breathable, and flexible.

**Composition:**
- Lime (calcium hydroxide): Binder
- Sand: Aggregate (provides body, reduces shrinkage)
- Fiber (hair, straw): Reinforcement (reduces cracking)
- Water: Vehicle

**Ratio (typical):** 1 part lime : 2.5–3 parts sand : small amount fiber

**Advantages:**
- Highly breathable — allows walls to dry and vapor to escape
- Flexible — accommodates minor structural movement without cracking
- Beautiful, natural appearance; ages gracefully
- Repairable — new lime plaster bonds well to old
- Long track record (used for centuries)

**Disadvantages:**
- Slow to cure (weeks to months)
- Requires skill to apply; poor application leads to delamination
- Lower compressive strength than gypsum (scratches more easily)
- More expensive than modern alternatives
- Sensitive to moisture in first days (rain damages drying plaster)

**Application (three-coat system):**

<div class="plaster-layers">
**Scratch coat:** First coat applied to substrate (stone, brick, or lath)
- Thickness: 1/2–3/4 inch
- Troweled hard; scratched (scored) horizontally for mechanical key to next layer
- Cures: 1–2 weeks before next coat

**Float coat:** Second coat fills irregularities; provides smooth base
- Thickness: 3/8–1/2 inch
- Floated (smoothed with wooden trowel) to even surface
- Cures: 1–2 weeks before finish coat

**Finish coat:** Final layer; cosmetic and protective
- Thickness: 1/8–1/4 inch
- Troweled smooth or textured
- Cures: 2–4 weeks to full strength
</div>

### Gypsum Plaster

Gypsum (hydrated calcium sulfate) plaster is modern standard: faster, easier, more uniform.

**Advantages:**
- Fast curing (1–7 days)
- Easier to apply; even coating possible with less skill
- Harder surface (resists scratches)
- Good fire resistance
- Less expensive than lime

**Disadvantages:**
- Less breathable than lime; can trap moisture in walls
- Lower flexibility; cracks with movement
- Requires ventilation during curing (gypsum needs water to cure; moisture management critical)
- Not self-healing (cracks remain)

**Application:** Similar three-coat system to lime, but coats are thinner and applied/cured faster.

### Earthen Plaster

Plaster made from clay, sand, and fiber; traditional in arid/semi-arid regions.

**Composition:**
- Clay: Binder (10–30% by weight)
- Sand: Aggregate (65–85%)
- Fiber (straw): Reinforcement

**Properties:**
- Extremely breathable
- Non-toxic; healthiest indoor air quality
- Beautiful matte finish
- Soft; easily dented/scratched
- Not suitable for wet areas (bathroom, kitchen backsplash)

**Best use:** Living rooms, bedrooms, interiors in dry climates

**Issues:** Not widely available commercially; must be custom-mixed or sourced locally. Requires dry climate to cure properly.

<section id="plaster-substrate">

## Preparing Substrate and Substrate Types

Plaster adheres well to porous, rough surfaces but not to smooth materials.

### Acceptable Substrates

- **Masonry (brick, stone, block):** Inherently porous; needs only cleaning
- **Wood lath:** Traditional; thin wood strips provide mechanical key; holds plaster well
- **Wire mesh/expanded metal lath:** Modern alternative; provides mechanical key and some lateral bracing
- **Gypsum board (drywall):** Can be plastered if surface is roughened or primer used

### Preparation

1. **Clean surface:** Remove loose mortar, dust, algae, any non-adhering material
2. **Dampen:** Before applying plaster, dampen substrate (especially absorbent materials like masonry) to prevent rapid water loss and ensure proper curing
3. **Prime if needed:** Glossy surfaces (painted, sealed) need primer for adhesion
4. **Check flatness:** Plaster follows substrate; major irregularities will show (or require thicker, more wasteful applications)

:::warning
Wet plaster on a dry, hot surface can dehydrate too quickly, causing crazing (fine cracks) or failure to cure properly. Always dampen substrate before plastering, and avoid plaster application in direct sun or extreme heat.
:::

## Whitewash and Limewash Finishes

Simple, inexpensive, and historically durable coatings for plaster, masonry, or wood.

### Whitewash (Traditional)

Basic recipe: slaked lime, salt, tallow (or linseed oil), water.

**Basic formula:**
- 1 part slaked lime (calcium hydroxide, wet or powder hydrated to paste)
- 1/2 part salt (helps adhesion)
- 1/4 part tallow or linseed oil (waterproofs slightly)
- 2–3 parts water (adjust consistency)

**Application:**
- Thin consistency (like milk) applied with brush
- May require 2–3 coats for even coverage
- Sets quickly (30–60 min); weathering begins immediately
- Bonds to plaster, masonry, wood

**Properties:**
- Bright white; moderately reflective
- Very breathable; allows walls to dry
- Inexpensive
- Weathers quickly; requires recoating every 1–2 years
- Soft; scratches easily

### Limewash (Modern Lime Paint)

Commercial lime paint (tinted, with added binders and minerals) for more durability and color options.

**Advantages:**
- Available in colors (iron oxide pigments safe for lime)
- More durable than whitewash (lasts 3–5 years)
- High-quality binders improve adhesion
- Wide aesthetic range

**Disadvantages:**
- More expensive than simple whitewash
- Still requires regular recoating
- Less "natural" than homemade whitewash (contains additives)

**Application:** Similar to whitewash; multiple thin coats preferred.

<section id="paint-types">

## Paint Types and Natural Alternatives

### Milk Paint

Casein-based paint (protein from milk), traditional and natural.

**Composition:**
- Casein (milk protein): Binder
- Lime or chalk: Whitening agent and filler
- Earth pigments: Color
- Water: Vehicle

**Making milk paint:**
- Mix powdered casein with hot water and lime, let thicken
- Add earth pigments for color
- Thin with water to brush consistency

**Properties:**
- Naturally matte finish; soft appearance
- Very low toxicity; safe for interiors
- Excellent adhesion to porous surfaces
- Not waterproof (avoid wet areas)
- Weathers naturally; can be left to gray over time
- Expensive if purchased pre-made

### Linseed Oil Paint

Oil-based paint using linseed oil as binder.

**Composition:**
- Boiled linseed oil (oxidized during heating): Binder
- Mineral spirits (solvent): Thins paint
- Pigments (earth minerals, synthetic): Color
- Dryers (cobalt, manganese salts): Accelerate curing

**Properties:**
- Very durable; hardens by oxidation (not evaporation)
- Water-resistant (good for kitchens, bathrooms)
- Beautiful satin finish
- Slow curing (weeks to full hardness)
- Low VOC if using natural pigments and minimal additives
- More expensive than latex

**Application:**
- Thin, even coats
- Requires ventilation during curing
- Not suitable for latex primer underneath (adhesion issues)

### Modern Latex Paint

Acrylic resin emulsion in water; most common modern paint.

**Advantages:**
- Fast drying (1–3 hours)
- Low odor; low VOC options available
- Easy cleanup; water-soluble
- Durable (10–15 years)
- Wide color range
- Inexpensive

**Disadvantages:**
- Less breathable than lime or oil paint (can trap moisture)
- Plastic-based; synthetic origin
- Lower sheen (matte) becomes chalky with weathering
- Not ideal for solid wood (water-based can cause wood to swell)

### Egg Tempera (Interior Decorative)

Ancient paint using egg yolk as binder.

**Recipe:**
- 1 egg yolk
- 1 tbsp linseed oil
- 1 tbsp water
- Earth pigments (add gradually to avoid clumping)

**Uses:** Interior walls, murals, decorative finishes (not high-traffic areas)
**Durability:** Moderate; can be varnished for protection

## Floor Finishes

Floor finish choice depends on substrate, foot traffic, aesthetics, and maintenance tolerance.

### Wood Floors

**Oil finish (natural oils soak into wood):**
- Linseed oil, tung oil, or blends
- Penetrates wood; enhances grain
- Matte appearance; natural beauty
- Requires recoating every 2–3 years
- Low toxicity; pleasant smell

**Wax finish (over oil or bare wood):**
- Beeswax or synthetic wax applied on top of oil
- Adds sheen; slippery (can be hazard)
- Requires frequent buffing (every few months)
- Traditional; very beautiful if maintained

**Varnish (film-forming):**
- Polyurethane or natural varnishes (linseed-based)
- Creates protective layer over wood
- Glossy/satin sheen
- Durable (5–10 years with high-traffic); requires periodic renewal
- More plastic-looking; less natural appearance

**Shellac (traditional):**
- Natural resin dissolved in alcohol
- Applied in thin coats; built up to glossy finish
- Beautiful amber tone; enhances wood color
- Requires maintenance (recoat every few years)
- Sensitive to water, heat, alcohol

### Stone/Tile Floors

**Sealant (for porous stone):**
- Penetrating sealer: Soaks into stone, makes water-resistant without changing appearance
- Film-forming sealer: Creates surface layer; glossy appearance
- Frequency: Reapply every 1–2 years depending on traffic and type

**Wax (optional, over sealer):**
- Adds sheen and slip resistance
- Requires buffing
- Reapply every 6–12 months

### Concrete Floors

**Polished concrete:**
- Grind with progressively finer grits; seal with penetrating sealer
- Modern aesthetic; durable; minimal maintenance
- Can be slippery when wet

**Stained concrete:**
- Acid stain or water-based stain applied; sealed
- Permanent; no recoating needed
- Colors are subtle, earthy tones

**Painted concrete:**
- Epoxy or polyurethane paint applied
- Durable; colorful; slip-resistant (textured options)
- Requires recoating every 5–10 years

### Earthen/Cob Floors

**Raw earthen:**
- Compressed earth, possibly with straw reinforcement
- Soft; wears over time; needs resurfacing periodically
- Very beautiful; unfinished aesthetic
- High maintenance in high-traffic areas

**Sealed earthen:**
- Apply natural oil (linseed, tung) or penetrating sealer
- More durable; still breathable
- Requires resealing annually or every few years

## Tiling Basics

Tile provides durable, water-resistant surfaces for walls and floors.

### Tile Selection

**Materials:**
- **Ceramic:** Fired clay; porous unless glazed; economical; varied aesthetic
- **Porcelain:** Dense fired clay; highly durable; moisture-resistant; more expensive
- **Stone (slate, marble, granite):** Natural; high aesthetic value; variable durability; requires sealing
- **Glass:** Decorative; waterproof; slippery if used on floors

**Glazing (surface coating):**
- Glazed tile: Vitrified surface; impermeable; easy to clean
- Unglazed tile: Absorbent; earthy appearance; requires sealing

### Installation (Basic Process)

1. **Prepare substrate:** Must be flat, stable, moisture-resistant
   - Use cement board or waterproofing membrane in wet areas (bathrooms, kitchens, showers)
   - Avoid direct installation on drywall in wet areas (leads to mold)

2. **Apply mortar (thin-set):**
   - Cement-based mortar (cement + sand + water + additives)
   - Applied with notched trowel in ridges
   - Combed at consistent angle for consistent coverage

3. **Set tile:**
   - Press tile into mortar; slight twist and pressure
   - Use spacers for consistent gaps
   - Check for plumb/level periodically

4. **Grout:**
   - After mortar cures (24 hours), apply grout (cement + water + sand)
   - Grout fills gaps between tiles
   - Unsanded grout for gaps <1/8 inch; sanded for larger gaps
   - Seal grout after curing (grout is porous; stains easily)

5. **Cure:** Keep area dry for 48–72 hours during grout curing

### Tile Care

- Glazed tile: Simple soap and water cleaning
- Unglazed tile: Requires sealing (annually or every 2–3 years)
- Grout: Can be sealed for stain resistance
- Epoxy grout: More stain-resistant; harder to clean during installation

:::info-box
Tiling is skilled work. Even small mistakes (lippage — uneven tile surfaces, poor grout joints) are visible and difficult to repair. Practice on small areas before major projects, or hire professionals.
:::

## Trim and Molding

Trim covers gaps, transitions, and provides visual interest.

### Types

**Baseboards:** Along floor-wall junction
- Protect wall from furniture, vacuum, water damage
- Provides visual weight to room bottom
- Styles range from simple 1" board to ornate 8"+ profiles

**Crown molding:** Where wall meets ceiling
- Provides visual interest; softens ceiling line
- Complex angles at corners require precise cuts
- Ornate styles period-specific; modern styles simple

**Casing:** Trim around doors and windows
- Covers gap between frame and wall
- Provides finished appearance
- Styles vary from simple flat board to detailed profiles

**Chair rail:** Horizontal trim at chair back height
- Protects wall from chair damage (historical function)
- Divides wall color/wallpaper (aesthetic)
- Typically 32–36" above floor

### Materials

- **Wood:** Traditional; stainable; paintable; requires finishing
- **MDF (medium-density fiberboard):** Engineered product; stable; accepts paint well; cheaper than solid wood; cannot be stained
- **Polyurethane:** Pre-finished; no staining/painting needed; plastic appearance
- **Vinyl:** Moisture-resistant; limited aesthetic options; modern/industrial looks

</section>

## Wall Coverings (Wallpaper and Alternatives)

### Traditional Wallpaper

Paper or fabric backed with adhesive; applied with paste.

**Advantages:**
- Wide variety of patterns, colors, textures
- Hides wall imperfections better than paint
- Adds texture and visual interest
- Removable (if quality paste and conditions right)

**Disadvantages:**
- Installation requires skill (matching patterns, avoiding air bubbles, seams visible)
- Can trap moisture behind (mold risk in humid climates)
- Removal can be labor-intensive
- More expensive than paint

### Vinyl Wallpaper

Plastic-coated paper; more durable than traditional paper.

**Advantages:**
- Waterproof; safe for kitchens, bathrooms
- Scrubbable (can wipe clean)
- More durable; lasts 10+ years
- Easier to remove than traditional paper

**Disadvantages:**
- Not breathable; traps moisture
- Plastic appearance (can look cheap unless high-quality)
- Environmental concerns (PVC-based)

### Fabric Wall Coverings

Woven fabric (linen, cotton, wool) adhered to walls.

**Advantages:**
- Beautiful natural appearance
- Acoustically absorbent
- Elegant, high-end aesthetic
- Breathable

**Disadvantages:**
- Expensive
- Can absorb odors, dust, allergens
- Not cleanable (must avoid spills)
- Requires professional installation

### Paint and Stencil

Alternative to wallpaper; custom designs painted or stenciled onto walls.

**Advantages:**
- Custom designs; infinite variety
- Removable (can paint over)
- Natural materials possible (milk paint, lime paint)
- Artistic expression

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires artistic skill or professional artist
- Labor-intensive
- Must be sealed if high-traffic area

</section>

<section id="conclusion">

## Conclusion

Interior finishes are the final impression of a space — they affect aesthetics, durability, health, and maintenance requirements. Natural materials (lime plaster, milk paint, wood oils, clay) prioritize breathability and indoor air quality but require more maintenance. Modern materials (drywall, latex paint, porcelain tile) prioritize durability and ease of installation. The best choices balance your climate, budget, desired aesthetic, and maintenance tolerance. Consider long-term durability and health impacts, not just initial appearance.
