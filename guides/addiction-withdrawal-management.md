## Overview

Substance use disorders affect millions globally. In austere or medical-scarce environments, managing addiction and withdrawal syndromes requires understanding of pharmacology, behavioral support, and harm reduction principles. This guide covers the three most common and dangerous withdrawal syndromes—alcohol, opioids, and benzodiazepines—along with practical strategies for supporting recovery.

**Critical disclaimer:** This guide is educational and not a substitute for professional addiction medicine. In severe withdrawal or overdose, seek emergency medical care immediately. These protocols assume access to basic medications (benzodiazepines, comfort medications) but not intensive detoxification centers or medication-assisted treatment (MAT) programs.

The goal of this guide is to reduce harm, prevent life-threatening complications, and support the individual's path toward recovery.

</section>

<section id="severe-withdrawal-overdose-safety-routing">

## Severe Withdrawal and Overdose Safety Routing

This guide's emergency safety surface is narrow: severe alcohol or sedative withdrawal and opioid overdose are escalation problems, not home detox projects.

Treat alcohol withdrawal, benzodiazepine withdrawal, or other sedative withdrawal as an emergency when there is a seizure, delirium tremens, new confusion, disorientation, hallucinations, fever, severe tremor with unstable vital signs, unsafe agitation, collapse, or no reliable sober adult able to supervise continuously. Do not frame severe withdrawal as a home taper, supplement plan, or alcohol-dosing plan; arrange emergency medical care or the fastest available evacuation.

For opioid exposure, separate withdrawal discomfort from overdose danger. Opioid withdrawal can be intensely uncomfortable, but overdose is suggested by slow, absent, noisy, or abnormal breathing, blue or gray color, unresponsiveness, or inability to wake. Give naloxone if available, support airway and breathing, call emergency services, and keep monitoring because symptoms can return after naloxone wears off.

After naloxone, the person may wake in acute withdrawal. Do not re-administer opioids to relieve naloxone-triggered withdrawal; that can restart respiratory depression. Keep the focus on breathing, observation, and emergency evaluation.

<section id="alcohol-withdrawal">

## Alcohol Withdrawal Syndrome

Alcohol is a central nervous system depressant. Chronic use leads to tolerance; abrupt cessation triggers hyperexcitability of the nervous system.

### Timeline and Severity

**Minor withdrawal (6–24 hours post-last drink):**
- Tremor (shakes)
- Diaphoresis (sweating)
- Tachycardia (rapid heart rate)
- Anxiety, restlessness
- Headache, nausea

**Hallucinosis (12–24 hours):** Visual, tactile, or auditory hallucinations while the person remains oriented. Scary but not dangerous if properly managed.

**Withdrawal seizures (6–48 hours):** Generalized tonic-clonic seizures; risk is 5–15% in severe withdrawal. Seizures are often multiple and can progress to status epilepticus.

**Delirium tremens (24–72 hours):** Confusion, disorientation, paranoia, severe hallucinations, autonomic instability (fever, severe tachycardia, hypertension). Mortality is 5–10% if untreated.

### CIWA-Ar Scoring

The Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment for Alcohol scale (CIWA-Ar) quantifies withdrawal severity and guides treatment.

| Assessment Item | Points |
|---|---|
| **Nausea/vomiting:** None=0, Mild=1, Moderate=2, Severe/retching=4 | 0–4 |
| **Tremor:** None=0, Can feel but not see=1, Visible at arms extended=2, Severe=4 | 0–4 |
| **Diaphoresis (sweating):** None=0, Barely perceptible=1, Beads on brow=2, Streams off face=3, Drenches clothes=4 | 0–4 |
| **Anxiety:** None=0, Mild=1, Moderate=2, Severe (patient fears death)=4 | 0–4 |
| **Irritability:** None=0, Mild=1, Moderate=2, Severe=4 | 0–4 |
| **Tactile disturbances (itching, numbness, burning, tingling):** None=0, Mild=1, Moderate=2, Severe=4 | 0–4 |
| **Auditory disturbances (sounds loud, unpleasant, hard to describe):** None=0, Mild=1, Moderate=2, Severe=4 | 0–4 |
| **Visual disturbances (seeing flashes, shapes, animals, people, threatening images):** None=0, Mild=1, Moderate=2, Severe=4 | 0–4 |
| **Headache, fullness in head:** None=0, Not severe=1, Moderately severe=2, Very severe=4 | 0–4 |
| **Orientation and clouding of sensorium:** Oriented to person, place, time=0, Disoriented to time=1, Disoriented to place/person=2 | 0–2 |

**Scoring:**
- **5–7:** Minimal withdrawal (outpatient management possible)
- **8–15:** Mild-to-moderate withdrawal (requires close monitoring)
- **>15:** Severe withdrawal (requires hospitalization, risk of seizure/delirium tremens)

### Management by Severity

**Mild withdrawal (CIWA <8):**
- Support and education
- Thiamine 100 mg daily (prevent Wernicke encephalopathy)
- Folic acid, multivitamin
- Hydration, electrolyte replacement if needed
- Antacid for stomach protection
- Daily CIWA reassessment

**Moderate withdrawal (CIWA 8–15):**
- **Benzodiazepine:** Lorazepam 1–2 mg or diazepam 5–10 mg PO every 6 hours; increase dose or frequency if CIWA remains >10
- Thiamine, folic acid
- Hydration
- Monitor for progression to delirium tremens
- **Phenytoin is NOT effective** for alcohol withdrawal seizure prevention; only benzodiazepines prevent seizures

**Severe withdrawal (CIWA >15):**
- **Aggressive benzodiazepine:** Lorazepam 2–4 mg IV/IM every 15–30 minutes until the patient is lightly sedated (CIWA controlled)
- **Seizure precautions:** Soft restraints if available; padded rails; maintain airway
- Thiamine IV if available (100 mg)
- **IV fluids** with electrolyte replacement (magnesium, potassium, phosphate often depleted)
- ICU-level monitoring (continuous cardiac monitoring, frequent vital signs)
- **Cooling measures** if febrile (delirium tremens)
- **Antipsychotics** (haloperidol 5 mg IV/IM) if severely agitated or actively hallucinating, but **only after benzodiazepines started**

:::danger
Seizures and delirium tremens are medical emergencies. If either occurs, escalate immediately to IV benzodiazepines and prepare for airway management.
:::

### Prevention of Wernicke Encephalopathy

Chronic alcohol use depletes thiamine (vitamin B1). Abrupt carbohydrate loading (IV dextrose) without thiamine can trigger Wernicke encephalopathy (confusion, eye movement abnormalities, ataxia—inability to walk). Always give thiamine **before** IV dextrose.

- **Thiamine 100 mg daily for at least 3–5 days**
- **Folic acid 1 mg daily**
- **Magnesium replacement** if depleted (check blood levels if available; supplement 1–2 g daily)

## Opioid Withdrawal Syndrome

Opioid withdrawal is intensely uncomfortable but not life-threatening if respiratory status is maintained. Untreated withdrawal drives high relapse rates and mortality.

### Timeline

**Early withdrawal (6–12 hours post-last use):** Dysphoria (low mood), anxiety, lacrimation (tearing), rhinorrhea (runny nose), yawning, diaphoresis, insomnia

**Peak withdrawal (24–72 hours):** Severe myalgias (muscle aches), nausea, diarrhea, vomiting, abdominal cramping, mydriasis (dilated pupils), piloerection ("goose bumps"), agitation, insomnia. Vital signs may elevate (tachycardia, mild hypertension, fever).

**Timeline varies:** Withdrawal from short-acting opioids (heroin) peaks in 24–48 hours and resolves in 5–7 days. Withdrawal from long-acting opioids (methadone) peaks in 3–8 days and lasts 10–20 days.

### COWS Scoring

The Clinical Opiate Withdrawal Scale (COWS) quantifies opioid withdrawal severity.

| Assessment Item | Points |
|---|---|
| **Resting Pulse:** <80=0, 80–120=1, 120–160=2, >160=4 | 0–4 |
| **Sweating:** None=0, Barely perceptible=1, Beads on brow/visible moisture=2, Dripping/running down face=3, Drenching=4 | 0–4 |
| **Knee buckles:** Straight legs=0, Slight bend=1, Moderate bend=2, Severe (unable to walk/stand)=4 | 0–4 |
| **Bone aches:** None=0, Mild=1, Moderate=2, Severe=4 | 0–4 |
| **Runny nose or tearing:** Normal=0, Nasal stuffiness/unusually moist eyes=1, Nose running/tearing=2, Nose constantly running/tears running down face=4 | 0–4 |
| **GI upset:** None=0, Stomach cramps=1, Nausea/loose stool=2, Vomiting/diarrhea=3, Multiple episodes=5 | 0–5 |
| **Tremor observation:** None=0, Can feel but not see=1, Slight tremor visible=2, Gross tremor=4 | 0–4 |
| **Yawning:** None=0, 1–2 times=1, 3 or more times=2, 4 or more times=4 | 0–4 |
| **Anxiety:** None=0, Mild=1, Moderate=2, Severe (patient states obvious anxiety)=4 | 0–4 |
| **Irritability:** None=0, Mild=1, Moderate=2, Severe=4 | 0–4 |
| **Pupil size:** Normal=0, Possible enlargement=1, Moderately dilated=2, So dilated only rim visible=5 | 0–5 |
| **Bone or Joint aches:** None=0, Mild=1, Moderate=2, Patient reports severe pain=4 | 0–4 |
| **Gooseflesh skin:** None=0, Barely perceptible=3, Pronounced=5 | 0–5 |

**Scoring:**
- **5–12:** Mild withdrawal
- **13–24:** Moderate withdrawal
- **25–36:** Moderately severe withdrawal
- **>36:** Severe withdrawal

### Management

**Goals:** Reduce discomfort, prevent relapse, address underlying addiction.

**Mild-to-moderate withdrawal:**
- **Clonidine:** 0.1–0.2 mg PO every 6–8 hours (reduces autonomic symptoms—sweating, myalgias, anxiety; does not reduce GI symptoms)
- **Loperamide:** 4 mg initially, then 2 mg after each loose stool (max 16 mg/day) for diarrhea
- **Ondansetron or metoclopramide** for nausea
- **Ibuprofen or acetaminophen** for bone aches
- **Diphenhydramine** for sleep (25–50 mg at bedtime)
- **Behavioral support:** Counseling, peer support, distraction

**Moderate-to-severe withdrawal:**
- **Buprenorphine** (partial opioid agonist) is ideal but requires proper licensing and training:
  - Induction: 4–8 mg SL (sublingual) on day 1; titrate up to 16 mg daily in divided doses
  - Maintenance: 8–24 mg daily (typical 16 mg) in divided doses
  - Less euphoria than heroin; no withdrawal if stopped abruptly; can be transitioned to long-term treatment
- **Methadone** (long-acting full agonist) if buprenorphine unavailable:
  - Induction: 10–30 mg daily (lower for non-opioid-dependent patients)
  - Titrate by 5–10 mg every 3–5 days until withdrawal is suppressed
  - Maintenance: 40–120 mg daily (typical 80 mg)
  - Careful: overdose risk is high; requires monitoring
- **Clonidine + comfort medications** as adjuncts (clonidine does NOT treat withdrawal alone)

**Harm reduction principles:**
- Do not withhold pain relief or comfort medications because the patient uses opioids
- Offer needle exchange and education on overdose recognition
- Provide naloxone (opioid antagonist) with instructions for family members
- Encourage engagement with peer support (12-step programs, medication-assisted treatment clinics)

:::warning
Abrupt discontinuation of maintenance opioids (methadone, buprenorphine) can trigger dangerous withdrawal. If the patient is on maintenance therapy, continue it and address comorbidities (pain, mental health) separately.
:::

## Benzodiazepine Withdrawal Syndrome

Benzodiazepines (diazepam, lorazepam, alprazolam) are CNS depressants. Chronic use leads to dependence; abrupt cessation causes dangerous withdrawal.

### Timeline (Varies by Half-Life)

**Short-acting benzodiazepines (alprazolam, lorazepam):** Withdrawal onset 12–24 hours; peak 24–48 hours; duration 5–7 days

**Long-acting benzodiazepines (diazepam, clonazepam):** Withdrawal onset 3–5 days; peak 5–10 days; duration 10–20 days

### Symptoms

- Anxiety, panic, tremor
- Insomnia, nightmares
- Diaphoresis, tachycardia
- Myalgias, arthralgias (joint pain)
- Perceptual disturbances (light sensitivity, auditory sensitivity, paresthesias)
- Seizures (risk 10–30% if chronic high-dose use)
- Psychosis (rare but severe)
- Delirium

### Management: Tapering Schedule

**Never abruptly stop benzodiazepines.** Seizures and delirium can occur. Instead, use a slow taper.

**Approach 1: Switch to Long-Acting, Then Taper**

1. Convert current benzodiazepine to equivalent dose of diazepam (longer half-life makes tapering safer):
   - Lorazepam 1 mg = diazepam 10 mg
   - Alprazolam 1 mg = diazepam 20 mg
   - Clonazepam 1 mg = diazepam 20 mg

2. **Taper over 6–8 weeks minimum** (or longer for high doses):
   - **Week 1:** Reduce by 10% (10 mg daily → 9 mg daily)
   - **Weeks 2–7:** Reduce by 10% every 1–2 weeks
   - **Week 8+:** Reduce slowly (2–5 mg every week until off)

3. **Monitor for withdrawal symptoms** at each step; slow taper further if symptoms worsen

**Approach 2: Direct Taper (If Converting to Long-Acting Not Possible)**

Reduce the current benzodiazepine by 10–25% every 1–2 weeks, depending on tolerance and symptoms. Slower is safer.

### Adjunct Medications for Comfort

- **Hydroxyzine 25–50 mg** for anxiety (not a benzodiazepine; safe non-addictive alternative)
- **Melatonin 3–10 mg** at bedtime for insomnia
- **Valerian or passionflower** herbal supplements (modest evidence but well-tolerated)
- **Magnesium 400–500 mg daily** (supports relaxation)
- **Beta-blocker (propranolol 20–40 mg TID)** for tremor and tachycardia if needed

:::danger
If seizures occur during benzodiazepine withdrawal, resume the benzodiazepine at a higher dose than before and taper much more slowly. Seizures indicate undermanagement.
:::

## Harm Reduction Principles

Harm reduction acknowledges that abstinence is not always immediately achievable and focuses on minimizing risks.

### Core Principles

**1. Respect autonomy:** The individual, not the provider, decides their recovery path.

**2. Meet people where they are:** Offer services without preconditions of abstinence.

**3. Focus on reducing harms, not eliminating use:** Lower-risk drug use (oral, snorted) has fewer harms than injection.

**4. Provide evidence-based interventions:**

- **Needle exchange:** Prevent hepatitis C, HIV transmission
- **Overdose prevention:** Naloxone distribution, supervised consumption sites (if legal)
- **Medication-assisted treatment:** Methadone, buprenorphine, naltrexone
- **Testing services:** Fentanyl strips, drug checking (if available)
- **Wound care:** Wound/vein care education for people who inject
- **Mental health and pain management:** Address comorbid conditions that drive use

**5. Reduce stigma:** Substance use disorder is a medical condition, not a moral failing.

### Community-Based Recovery Supports

**Peer support groups:**
- Narcotics Anonymous (NA), Alcoholics Anonymous (AA) — 12-step programs with spiritual component
- SMART Recovery — self-empowerment focus, no higher power required
- Refuge Recovery — Buddhist-informed approach
- Online meetings available worldwide (ZOOM, etc.)

**Medication-assisted treatment (MAT):**
- Requires trained clinician; not available in all austere settings
- Combines medication (methadone, buprenorphine, or naltrexone) with counseling
- Reduces illicit use, improves treatment retention, decreases overdose risk

**Behavioral therapies:**
- Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) for substance use
- Motivational interviewing (MI) to resolve ambivalence
- Contingency management (incentivizing abstinence)
- Family therapy to repair relationships

### Naloxone (Opioid Reversal)

Every household with opioid use should have naloxone.

**Administration:**
- **Intranasal (easiest):** 2–4 mg spray into one nostril; repeat in 2–3 minutes if unresponsive
- **IM injection:** 0.4–0.8 mg into outer thigh; repeat every 2–3 minutes
- **IV (fastest onset):** 0.4–0.8 mg; repeat every 1–2 minutes

**After naloxone:** The person will likely experience acute withdrawal (very uncomfortable but not dangerous). Do not re-administer opioids to relieve withdrawal. Call emergency services and monitor airway and breathing.

## Managing Co-Occurring Mental Health and Medical Conditions

Most people with substance use disorders have concurrent mental health conditions (depression, anxiety, PTSD, bipolar disorder). Treating both improves outcomes.

### Depression

Opioid and alcohol use often mask or worsen depression. During withdrawal, depression commonly emerges.

- **SSRIs** (sertraline, fluoxetine): First-line; safe with benzodiazepines
- **Counseling:** Cognitive-behavioral therapy for depression
- **Peer support:** Shared experiences reduce isolation
- **Monitor suicide risk:** If expressed, prioritize mental health evaluation

### Anxiety Disorders

Anxiety often precedes or accompanies substance use.

- **Benzodiazepines:** Use cautiously (risk of cross-addiction); prefer shorter-term use with clear taper plan
- **SSRIs:** Longer-term management
- **Non-pharmacologic:** Mindfulness, breathing exercises, cognitive restructuring

### PTSD (Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder)

Trauma frequently drives substance use. Address PTSD directly.

- **Trauma-focused CBT** or **EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing)**
- **SSRIs:** Paroxetine and sertraline have evidence in PTSD
- **Prazosin 1–5 mg at bedtime:** Reduces nightmares in PTSD
- **Avoid alcohol and benzodiazepines:** They delay PTSD recovery

### Pain Management

Chronic pain often drives opioid use and complicates withdrawal.

- **Multimodal approach:** NSAIDs, physical therapy, topical analgesics, non-opioid medications
- **Buprenorphine:** Provides mild pain relief; appropriate for pain + opioid use disorder
- **Avoid escalating opioids** during recovery; address pain separately

## Relapse Prevention and Long-Term Management

### Triggers and Coping Strategies

Relapse is common. Identify personal triggers and develop coping skills:

| Trigger | Coping Strategy |
|---------|-----------------|
| Social pressure / friends who use | Avoid environments; find new social groups; practice refusal skills |
| Negative emotions (anger, sadness, loneliness) | Counseling; mindfulness; physical activity; peer support |
| Physical pain | Multimodal pain management; avoid opioid escalation |
| Boredom or lack of structure | Meaningful activities; employment; volunteering; hobbies |
| Drug paraphernalia or locations | Avoid using environments; change routines; move if necessary |

### Contingency Management

Positive reinforcement for abstinence (vouchers, prizes, time off) is evidence-based.

- Regular urine drug screens with rewards for negative results
- Vocational support to rebuild employment
- Housing stability (unstable housing is relapse risk)

### Long-Term Medication Management

- **Methadone or buprenorphine:** Ongoing maintenance (often years) reduces relapse
- **Naltrexone:** Opioid antagonist blocks euphoria; useful for highly motivated patients
- **Acamprosate (alcohol):** Maintains abstinence; well-tolerated

### Follow-Up and Monitoring

- **Frequent contact:** Weekly initially, then spacing based on stability
- **Urine drug screens:** Objective verification of abstinence
- **Therapy:** Regular individual and/or group therapy
- **Medical monitoring:** Liver function (alcohol, injection-related), cardiac status (stimulant use), infectious disease screening (HIV, Hep C)

</section>

<section id="summary">

## Summary

Addiction is a treatable medical condition. Withdrawal syndromes are dangerous but manageable with appropriate medications and support. Alcohol and benzodiazepine withdrawal require seizure prevention; opioid withdrawal is uncomfortable but not life-threatening if respiratory status is maintained. Harm reduction, peer support, and addressing comorbid conditions are essential to long-term recovery. In austere environments, focus on basic benzodiazepines for alcohol/benzodiazepine withdrawal, comfort medications for opioid withdrawal, and connection to community recovery resources.
