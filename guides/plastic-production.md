## Polymer Chemistry Basics

A polymer is a large molecule composed of repeating units (monomers) linked by covalent bonds. Plastics are polymers that can be melted and formed into shapes. Understanding the distinction between thermoplastics (melt and reflow repeatedly) and thermosets (cross-linked, cannot reflow) is fundamental to processing.

### Monomer Linkage Types

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Type</th><th scope="row">Mechanism</th><th scope="row">Example</th><th scope="row">Properties</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Addition polymerization</td><td>Monomers add to a growing chain via double bonds; no byproducts</td><td>Polyethylene (PE) from ethylene C2H4</td><td>Thermoplastic; low cost; simple process</td></tr><tr><td>Condensation polymerization</td><td>Monomers react by releasing small molecules (H2O, NH3, HCl); typically forms cross-links</td><td>Bakelite (phenol + formaldehyde)</td><td>Thermoset; hard; heat-resistant</td></tr><tr><td>Free-radical polymerization</td><td>Initiated by free radicals (e.g., from peroxides); rapid chain growth</td><td>PVC, polystyrene</td><td>Controlled chain length possible; thermoplastic</td></tr></tbody></table>

## Bakelite: Phenol-Formaldehyde Synthesis

Bakelite was the first fully synthetic plastic (invented 1907). It's created by condensation of phenol and formaldehyde, producing a hard, heat-resistant thermoset plastic.

### Chemistry and Reaction Mechanism

**Overall reaction:** n C6H5OH + n CH2O → (C7H8O2)n + n H2O

The reaction occurs in two stages:

-   **Resole stage (alkaline):** Phenol + formaldehyde with NaOH catalyst; forms methylol (-CH2OH) and methylene (-CH2-) bridges. Product is soluble and thermoplastic at this stage.
-   **Novolac stage (acidic):** Further heating with acid catalyst causes additional cross-linking; forms a hard, insoluble thermoset.

### Production Procedure

**Stage 1: Resole Formation (Alkaline)**

1.  Mix phenol (100 g) with formaldehyde solution (37% aqueous, 150 mL) in a round-bottom flask fitted with a thermometer and condenser
2.  Add sodium hydroxide (3-5 g) as catalyst; mixture becomes exothermic and temperature rises immediately to 40-50°C
3.  Stir constantly for 5 minutes to distribute catalyst evenly; avoid clumping
4.  Gradually increase temperature by applying external heat (hot water bath or heating mantle) to reach 80°C over 15-20 minutes
5.  Maintain 80-100°C with stirring for 2-3 hours; the mixture darkens from colorless to amber to dark brown
6.  As water evaporates, mixture becomes increasingly viscous and resinous (syrupy consistency)
7.  Product is now soluble in water and alcohol—this is "resole," a thermoplastic intermediate stage
8.  Cool mixture to 50-60°C; pour the resinous product onto a metal plate or into water to harden
9.  Once cool, cut resinous mass into small pieces (2-3 cm cubes)—easier to handle in next stage

**Stage 2: Novolac Formation (Acidic Cure)**

10.  Place resin pieces into a heated steel mold (150-180°C preheated)
11.  Alternatively, heat resin pieces in an oven at 150°C for 10-15 minutes until soft
12.  Add acid catalyst: 1-2 mL of concentrated hydrochloric acid per 100 g resin (alternative: oxalic acid 1-2 g per 100 g)
13.  Mix acid thoroughly into heated resin; the mixture should bubble slightly as heating accelerates condensation
14.  Place resin mixture into mold, close mold, and apply pressure: 1000-2000 PSI
15.  Maintain 160-180°C under pressure for 1-2 hours (longer = harder final product)
16.  Monitor for smoke or strong chemical odor (indicates overheating)—reduce temperature slightly if visible
17.  Allow mold to cool to room temperature under pressure (minimum 30 minutes) to prevent cracking
18.  Open mold and remove cured bakelite piece; it should be hard, dark brown, and crack-resistant
19.  Final product can be sanded, polished, or machined to final dimensions

:::warning
Formaldehyde is toxic and carcinogenic. Phenol is corrosive and absorbed through skin. Work in a ventilated fume hood with appropriate PPE (nitrile gloves, goggles, apron).
:::

### Bakelite Properties

-   Hardness: Mohs 4-5 (scratches with steel)
-   Heat resistance: Stable to 200°C continuous, 300°C short-term
-   Electrical insulation: Excellent; used for electrical plugs and sockets
-   Brittleness: Prone to cracking; reinforced with filler (wood flour, fabric) for toughness
-   Water absorption: Low; good moisture resistance

## Celluloid Production: Cellulose Nitrate and Camphor

Celluloid, invented in 1870, was the first commercial plastic. It's made by nitrating cellulose (from cotton or wood pulp) and plasticizing with camphor.

### Cellulose Nitration

**Reaction:** Cellulose + HNO3 → Cellulose nitrate (CN) + H2O

The degree of nitration (fraction of hydroxyl groups replaced with nitrate) determines properties: 10-12% = plastics grade; 13%+ = explosive guncotton.

### Production Procedure

**Stage 1: Cellulose Preparation**

1.  Start with high-purity cotton linters (short fibers) or purified wood pulp; remove all lignin and impurities
2.  Dry cellulose completely in a low-temperature oven (40-50°C) to <5% moisture content (moisture interferes with nitration)
3.  Weigh out cellulose needed (typically 50-100 g batch scale)

**Stage 2: Acid Mixture Preparation (Critical—Do Slowly)**

4.  In a fume hood, place concentrated H₂SO₄ (20 mL) in a glass beaker surrounded by ice bath
5.  Slowly add concentrated HNO₃ (60 mL) to the sulfuric acid dropwise while stirring (never reverse order—highly exothermic)
6.  Mixture heats to 50-60°C; allow to cool back to <20°C before proceeding
7.  This creates a mixed acid that is stable for nitration

**Stage 3: Nitration Reaction**

8.  With mold temperature at <15°C (keep in ice bath), slowly add dried cellulose to acid mixture in small portions (1-2 g at a time)
9.  Stir continuously between additions to prevent lumping
10.  Complete addition takes 30-45 minutes; maintain <20°C throughout
11.  Once all cellulose added, continue stirring for 1-2 hours at <20°C; mass becomes gelatinous and swells
12.  Temperature may rise slowly—monitor carefully; if temperature exceeds 25°C, the reaction accelerates dangerously and must be cooled with fresh ice

**Stage 4: Quenching and Washing**

13.  Pour reaction mixture very slowly into a large volume of ice water (ratio ~1:10 product to water) in a stainless steel container
14.  The cellulose nitrate precipitates as a white or light yellow fibrous mass; acid dilutes
15.  Stir gently for 5 minutes
16.  Filter through fine cheesecloth into a funnel; collect cellulose nitrate solids
17.  Wash solids repeatedly in distilled water: first wash removes most acid (20 mL water, drain; repeat 3 times)
18.  Final wash: soak in 0.5% dilute acetic acid (25 mL) for 10 minutes—neutralizes residual mineral acid
19.  Drain and squeeze (gently) to remove excess water

**Stage 5: Drying and Storage**

20.  Spread wet cellulose nitrate on a clean cloth or paper towel in a low-temperature oven set to 30-40°C (NOT higher)
21.  Dry for 4-8 hours until brittle and crumbly (moisture content ~2-3%)
22.  **Critical:** Store dried cellulose nitrate submerged in distilled water or mineral oil in sealed glass jars—prevents self-ignition

**Stage 6: Plasticization**

23.  Measure camphor and weigh out 20-30% of cellulose nitrate mass (e.g., 20-30 g camphor per 100 g cellulose nitrate)
24.  Place cellulose nitrate and camphor in a heavy-walled glass or metal vessel; heat in a water bath to 60-70°C
25.  Begin mixing with a wooden or metal spatula; the camphor gradually dissolves into the cellulose nitrate
26.  Continue heating and kneading for 20-30 minutes as mixture becomes more uniform and plastic
27.  Increase temperature gradually to 80-90°C (keep in water bath, don't allow direct heating); continue mixing for another 20-30 minutes
28.  Mixture should become uniform in color (pale yellow to light brown), smooth, and pliable like dough
29.  Further heat to 120-150°C while stirring (use heating mantle or oil bath, never open flame) for 30-60 minutes to fully plasticize
30.  Allow to cool to 80-100°C; while still warm and pliable, transfer to a mold or shape by hand-pressing
31.  Cool completely (4-8 hours) to room temperature under modest pressure; the material hardens and becomes glossy
32.  The resulting material is celluloid—moldable when reheated, hard and translucent when cooled

:::warning
Cellulose nitrate is extremely flammable and explosive. Store in water or under mineral oil. Never allow it to dry completely. Never use fire as heat source. Use only electric heating. Minimal quantities should be prepared, and only with appropriate training.
:::

## Casein Plastic from Milk

Casein, a milk protein, was used to make plastic ("galalith") beginning in 1897. It's the most accessible protein-based plastic for small-scale production.

### Casein Extraction and Processing

1.  Start with skim milk (casein is the principal protein; fat removed)
2.  Acidify the milk with dilute acetic acid (vinegar) or citric acid until casein precipitates (pH ~4.6)
3.  Filter through cheesecloth; collect the white casein curd
4.  Wash thoroughly with water to remove lactose and residual acid
5.  Press the curd to remove water
6.  Dry at <40°C for 24-48 hours until brittle
7.  Grind into fine powder

### Molding Casein Plastic

1.  Mix casein powder with water (1:1 by weight) to form a stiff paste (e.g., 50 g casein + 50 mL water); add water slowly while stirring to avoid lumps
2.  Let paste sit for 5-10 minutes to allow casein to fully hydrate
3.  Heat mixture to 50-60°C with gentle, constant stirring (use water bath or low heat); the paste softens and becomes more fluid
4.  Monitor temperature carefully; if overheated or boiled, the protein denatures and product becomes grainy and weak
5.  Once softened (mixture flows slightly but remains stiff), add formaldehyde solution: 37% aqueous formaldehyde (10-15% by weight relative to casein mass—so 5-7.5 mL for 50 g casein)
6.  Stir thoroughly for 2-3 minutes; formaldehyde cross-links casein protein molecules
7.  Increase temperature to 80-90°C while stirring continuously for 30-60 minutes; mixture gradually becomes stiffer and changes color (whitish to tan/brown depending on formaldehyde concentration)
8.  As cross-linking progresses, mixture becomes increasingly difficult to stir (may require significant force by end)
9.  At 45-minute mark, test consistency: mixture should be thick but still moldable (like stiff dough)
10.  While still warm (80-90°C), transfer the mixture into a preheated mold or press between two flat plates under light pressure (50-200 PSI)
11.  If hand-molding, shape quickly before cooling; the material hardens rapidly and becomes difficult to deform
12.  For flat sheets: press between oiled metal plates; use weights (2-5 kg) to maintain pressure as cooling occurs
13.  Allow to cool at room temperature for 30-60 minutes; residual heat can be dissipated faster by placing mold in a cool water bath (not ice—thermal shock causes cracking)
14.  Once fully cooled, remove from mold; the material should be hard, dimensionally stable, and light brown
15.  Finish by sanding (start coarse 120-grit, progress to fine 220-grit) to smooth surfaces
16.  Optional: Dye using acid dyes (casein accepts dyes well)—dissolve dye in hot water, immerse cooled casein plastic for 1-2 hours, dry thoroughly
17.  Polish with cloth and light oil or wax for glossy appearance

### Properties of Casein Plastic

-   Colors well with dyes (historically used for decorative items, buttons)
-   Moderate hardness (softer than bakelite)
-   Water-sensitive; swells if exposed to moisture long-term
-   Biodegradable (important early advantage)
-   Heat resistance: Poor; softens above 80°C

## Shellac Resin Processing

Shellac is a natural resin secreted by lac beetles (Kerria lacca) on tree branches in India. It can be processed into a plastic-like material through melting and molding.

### Raw Shellac Preparation

1.  Start with raw shellac (stick lac or seed lac from lac insect colonies)
2.  Heat to 120-140°C to soften (traditional: use sand bath)
3.  Pour through fine mesh or fabric to strain out insect debris and wood particles
4.  Cool on a metal plate; breaks into "flake shellac" when solid
5.  The flakes are now pure shellac resin

### Shellac as a Plastic

Shellac can be molded directly by heating to 150-180°C and pressing into molds. When cooled, it hardens into a glossy, hard material useful for phonograph records, buttons, and decorative items.

**Properties:**

-   Natural glossy finish
-   Hard and brittle (Mohs 3-4)
-   Soluble in alcohol (useful for coating and adhesive)
-   Heat resistance: Softens above 80°C
-   Excellent insulation properties

## Natural Rubber Vulcanization

Natural rubber (from Hevea brasiliensis latex) is soft and sticky. Vulcanization—cross-linking rubber with sulfur—creates a durable, elastic material (Charles Goodyear, 1844).

### Latex to Rubber Conversion

1.  Collect latex (milk-like sap from rubber trees)
2.  Acidify with formic acid or acetic acid to coagulate (rubber particles precipitate)
3.  Wash the coagulated rubber in water
4.  Press and dry to remove water (produces "crepe rubber" or sheet rubber)

### Vulcanization Process

**Reaction:** Natural rubber (polyisoprene) + Sulfur → Cross-linked rubber network

Sulfur atoms form bridges between rubber molecules, increasing hardness, elasticity, and heat resistance.

**Procedure:**

1.  Mix raw rubber with sulfur powder (1-10% by weight depending on desired hardness)
2.  Add accelerators (zinc oxide ~5%, stearic acid ~2%) to speed vulcanization and improve properties
3.  Heat the mixture to 140-180°C for 30-60 minutes depending on degree of vulcanization desired
4.  Higher sulfur content (10%) creates hard ebonite (>90°C still provides elasticity, very hard); lower sulfur (~2%) creates flexible rubber tire material
5.  Cool slowly to prevent cracking
6.  The vulcanized rubber can now be molded into forms or used as sheets

### Vulcanization Effects

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Sulfur Content</th><th scope="row">Product Type</th><th scope="row">Properties</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>2-3%</td><td>Soft rubber</td><td>Elastic, flexible, good tensile strength</td></tr><tr><td>5-10%</td><td>Semihard rubber</td><td>Moderate elasticity, harder</td></tr><tr><td>25-35%</td><td>Hard rubber (ebonite)</td><td>Very hard, brittle, insulating, non-elastic</td></tr></tbody></table>

## Thermodynamic Data and Safety Ranges

Plastic production involves exothermic reactions and precise temperature control. Understanding reaction thermodynamics, safe operating temperature ranges, and hazard indicators prevents runaway reactions and toxic fume generation.

### Reaction Temperatures for Common Plastic Formulations

#### Bakelite (Phenol-Formaldehyde)

**Resole stage (alkaline formation):**
- Reaction temperature range: 70-100°C
- Exothermic reaction: Yes, temperature rises 10-15°C naturally with catalyzed mixing
- Peak temperature: 100°C (maximum safe); higher temps cause premature polymerization
- Reaction time: 2-3 hours at 80-100°C
- Heat source: Hot water bath or heating mantle (never open flame)

**Novolac stage (acid-catalyzed cure):**
- Reaction temperature range: 140-180°C
- Exothermic reaction: Moderate; temperature can rise 20-30°C from initial charge temperature
- Safe upper limit: 200°C (exceeding this causes formaldehyde gas release and decomposition)
- Reaction time: 1-2 hours under pressure at 160-180°C
- Pressure requirement: 1000-2000 PSI (excessive pressure = slower cure, inadequate pressure = porous product)

**Critical temperature points:**
- Below 140°C: Cure too slow; product remains tacky
- 140-160°C: Slow but acceptable; conservative approach
- 160-180°C: Optimal; reasonable cure time with good properties
- Above 180°C: Risk of thermal decomposition; gas evolution becomes visible (smoke)

:::danger
**Runaway reaction warning:** If temperature rises >10°C in 5 minutes during novolac cure, remove from heat immediately. Place mold in cool water bath (not ice—thermal shock causes cracking) to quench reaction. Temperature rising uncontrollably indicates insufficient cooling or mold temperature measurement error.
:::

#### Celluloid (Cellulose Nitrate + Camphor)

**Nitration reaction:**
- Temperature: Keep below 20°C (ice bath required)
- Exothermic reaction: Highly exothermic; temperature rises rapidly if not cooled
- Thermal hazard: Uncontrolled rise above 25°C accelerates nitration exponentially and risks explosion
- Monitoring: Use thermometer; if temperature exceeds 25°C, immerse reaction vessel in fresh ice bath immediately

**Plasticization (camphor mixing):**
- Temperature range: 60-150°C
- Initial mixing: 60-70°C (stiff paste softens)
- Intermediate heating: 80-90°C (camphor dissolves into cellulose nitrate)
- Final heating: 120-150°C (full plasticization achieved)
- Exothermic reaction: Minimal; heating is endothermic (requires heat input)
- Heating rate: Slow (10°C per 10 minutes) to prevent local overheating

**Safety limits:**
- Minimum: 60°C (below this, camphor doesn't dissolve adequately)
- Maximum: 150°C (above this, cellulose nitrate begins to decompose)
- Never exceed 160°C (decomposition accelerates; smoke/fumes appear)

:::danger
**Cellulose nitrate decomposition:** Temperatures above 150°C cause cellulose nitrate to release nitrogen oxides (brown/reddish fumes) and become unstable. Continued heating can lead to self-ignition. If brown smoke appears, immediately remove from heat and allow to cool in air (do NOT cool with water—rapid cooling can cause violent decomposition).
:::

#### Casein Plastic

**Hydration phase:**
- Temperature: 50-60°C
- Reaction: Casein protein swells in water; endothermic
- Exothermic reaction: None; requires heat input only
- Heating rate: Slow (5-10°C per minute)

**Cross-linking with formaldehyde:**
- Temperature range: 50-90°C
- Exothermic reaction: Slightly exothermic; temperature may rise 5-10°C naturally
- Safe upper limit: 90°C (above this, protein denatures and product becomes grainy)
- Critical temperature: Exceeding 95°C causes irreversible damage to protein structure
- Reaction time: 30-60 minutes at 80-90°C

**Cooling phase:**
- Slow cool (5°C per 10 minutes) from 90°C to room temperature
- Rapid cooling causes cracking (thermal stress in protein matrix)
- Once cooled to <30°C, material can be demolded

:::warning
**Formaldehyde gas release:** Even at 90°C, casein plastic releases small amounts of formaldehyde vapor (pungent smell). Ensure adequate ventilation. Above 95°C, formaldehyde release increases dramatically (visible in fumes).
:::

#### Starch Bioplastics

**Gelatinization phase:**
- Temperature: 70-80°C
- Exothermic reaction: No; endothermic (requires heat input)
- Time: 10-15 minutes
- Heating rate: Moderate (2-5°C per minute); faster heating causes lumping

**Plasticizer addition & final heating:**
- Temperature: 80-100°C
- Exothermic reaction: Slight; temperature may rise 3-5°C naturally
- Safe upper limit: 105°C (above this, water evaporates, altering composition)
- Critical point: 100°C (at this point, most water has evaporated; further heating causes brittleness)

**Safety consideration:** Starch gelatinization is primarily thermal; no exothermic decomposition risk at these temperatures (unlike bakelite or celluloid).

### Exothermic Reaction Hazard Warnings

Exothermic reactions generate heat; uncontrolled reactions "runaway" and cause explosions, fires, or toxic gas release.

**Bakelite exothermic reaction hazard:**
- Resole stage: Moderate exothermic (naturally rises 10-15°C); manageable with cooling
- Novolac stage: Moderate exothermic; heat generation can be excessive if cure happens too fast
- Runaway risk: Low if temperature controlled; moderate risk if not monitored
- Prevention: Maintain water bath cooling; monitor temperature every 5-10 minutes; reduce heat input if temperature rises >5°C above target

**Celluloid exothermic reaction hazard:**
- Nitration stage: HIGHLY exothermic; uncontrolled reaction can explode
- Risk: Highest of all plastic production processes
- Prevention: Ice bath MANDATORY; never allow temperature above 20°C; stop adding cellulose if temperature rises >25°C
- Emergency: If temperature rises uncontrollably, immediately pour entire reaction mass into large volume of ice water to quench

**Casein and starch reactions:**
- Exothermic reactions: Minimal; heating is largely endothermic (requires input)
- Runaway risk: Very low
- Hazard: Primarily from formaldehyde vapor (casein) and steam (starch); not explosion hazard

### Safe Operating Temperature Ranges (Quick Reference)

| Plastic Type | Phase | Min Temp | Optimal Temp | Max Temp | Hazards |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Bakelite | Resole | 70°C | 80-100°C | 110°C | Premature polymerization |
| Bakelite | Novolac | 140°C | 160-180°C | 200°C | Thermal decomposition, gas release |
| Celluloid | Nitration | <5°C | 10-20°C | 25°C | Explosion risk if exceeds 25°C |
| Celluloid | Plasticization | 60°C | 100-120°C | 150°C | Decomposition & fume release >150°C |
| Casein | Hydration | 40°C | 55°C | 70°C | Incomplete hydration below 40°C |
| Casein | Cross-link | 70°C | 80-90°C | 95°C | Protein denaturation >95°C |
| Starch | Gelatinization | 60°C | 75°C | 85°C | Uneven heating causes lumps |
| Starch | Plasticization | 80°C | 90-100°C | 110°C | Excessive water loss >110°C |

### Temperature Monitoring Best Practices

**Equipment:**
- Digital thermometer (±2°C accuracy) for all processes
- Dial thermometer as backup (mechanical, reliable if calibrated)
- Temperature controller (proportional-integral-derivative system) for automated temperature maintenance
- Infrared thermometer (quick surface checks; less accurate than probe thermometers)

**Monitoring procedure:**
1. Measure temperature every 5-10 minutes during reactive phases
2. Position thermometer probe in the middle of the reaction mixture (not at edges where heat distribution is uneven)
3. Record temperature and time on a log sheet
4. If temperature deviates >5°C from target, adjust heat input and re-measure in 2 minutes
5. If temperature rises >10°C in 5 minutes, immediately remove heat and cool

**Data logging benefit:** Review temperature logs after each batch. Patterns reveal equipment behavior, heating rate, and cooling rate. Early identification of equipment drift prevents next batch failure.

## Compression and Injection Molding

![Plastic Production diagram 1](../assets/svgs/plastic-production-1.svg)

Compression molding vs. injection molding: Different methods for shaping heated plastic

### Compression Molding

Plastic is heated and pressed into a mold cavity under high pressure and temperature. Used for thermosets and some thermoplastics.

**Procedure:**

1.  Create a two-part mold (male and female dies) from steel or aluminum
2.  Heat the mold to 150-200°C depending on material
3.  Place plastic pellets or preforms into the mold cavity
4.  Close the mold with a hydraulic press, applying 1000-5000 PSI
5.  Hold pressure for 30 seconds to 5 minutes as plastic flows and fills the mold
6.  Heat causes curing (if thermoset) or softening (if thermoplastic)
7.  Open mold and remove finished part; allow to cool

### Injection Molding

Molten plastic is injected under high pressure into a mold cavity. Faster than compression molding; used for thermoplastics.

**Equipment needed:**

-   Hopper (feeds plastic pellets)
-   Heating barrel with screw auger (melts and mixes plastic)
-   Nozzle (delivers plastic to mold)
-   Mold cavity
-   Hydraulic clamping system

**Cycle:**

1.  Heat barrel to 200-300°C depending on material
2.  Screw auger rotates, heating and mixing plastic
3.  Pressure builds at nozzle; when sufficient, screw moves forward, injecting plastic into mold
4.  Mold is held at 1000-2000 PSI as plastic cools
5.  Once plastic solidifies (10-120 seconds), mold opens
6.  Ejector pins push finished part out
7.  Cycle repeats

## Plastic Extrusion

Extrusion forces heated plastic through a shaped die, creating continuous profiles (pipes, film, fibers, sheet). The shape of the die determines the output shape.

### Simple Extrusion Setup

1.  Heat plastic pellets in a barrel (200-300°C depending on material)
2.  Rotate a screw auger that mixes and pushes plastic toward the die
3.  Design a die with the desired output shape (circular for tubing, flat for sheet, custom for profiles)
4.  Plastic flows through die under pressure (~500-2000 PSI)
5.  As it exits, pull gently with rollers or wind onto a spool (controls thickness and prevents sagging)
6.  Cool in air or water bath to solidify
7.  Optional: Cut to desired length

### Die Design Considerations

-   **Wall thickness uniformity:** Even walls (within 0.5 mm variation) prevent weak points and uneven cooling. Thin walls (1-3 mm) require 260-290°C; thick walls (>5 mm) benefit from 240-260°C to prevent overheat in thin sections.
-   **Internal surface finish:** Smooth surfaces (Ra <1.6 μm) reduce friction and allow easier material flow. Rough or etched dies cause hanging up of material, pressure buildup, and poor surface finish.
-   **Temperature gradients:** Too hot → sagging, distortion, material degradation (yellowing, browning); too cool → high back-pressure, poor fill, potential for gate freeze-off (plastic hardens before filling cavity).
-   **Throughput control:** Regulated by screw speed (50-200 RPM typical), barrel temperature (varies by material type), and material viscosity (measured in melt flow index, MFI). Higher viscosity materials require higher temperatures or slower screw speeds.
-   **Draft angles:** Minimum 1-2 degrees taper on all vertical walls aids ejection and prevents sticking.

</section>

<section id="bioplastics">

## Bioplastics from Corn Starch

Starch-based bioplastics are moldable when heated with plasticizers (water, glycerin). They biodegrade in weeks to months, unlike petroleum-based plastics.

### Production Procedure

1.  Mix corn starch (100 g) with distilled water (50 mL) in a heavy-bottomed beaker or pot; stir thoroughly to break up lumps
2.  Heat slowly to 70-80°C with constant stirring using a wooden spoon; starch granules absorb water and begin to swell
3.  Monitor closely: mixture becomes increasingly viscous as heating continues; starch loses crystalline structure and becomes gelatinous
4.  At 75-80°C, starch granules reach maximum swelling; mixture is translucent to opaque white and very thick
5.  Once starch is gelatinized (80°C), add plasticizer: glycerin or polyethylene glycol (20-30 mL for 100 g starch)
6.  Alternatively, use urea (10-15 g for 100 g starch) as plasticizer, which lowers the glass transition temperature (Tg), making material more flexible
7.  Mix plasticizer thoroughly into heated starch (this step is critical—thorough mixing ensures uniform properties)
8.  Continue heating to 90°C with stirring for 10-15 minutes; plasticizer penetrates and swells starch granules further
9.  Increase temperature gradually to 100°C over 10 minutes while stirring; mixture becomes smoother and more uniform in appearance
10.  Maintain 100°C for 20-30 minutes with continuous stirring; mixture should become uniform, glossy paste without visible granules
11.  Optional enhancement: For improved strength and flexibility, add biodegradable polymer (polybutylene succinate PBS, 5-10 g) or cellulose fiber (2-5 g) at 90°C; mix thoroughly before final heating to 100°C
12.  Once mixture is uniform at 100°C, remove from heat; allow to cool to 80-90°C
13.  While still warm and pliable, transfer mixture into molds (silicone molds work well due to easy release)
14.  Press gently to fill mold completely and remove air bubbles
15.  Allow molds to cool at room temperature for 2-4 hours; cooling can be accelerated by placing in a cool water bath (not ice—thermal shock causes cracking)
16.  Once fully cooled to room temperature, the material is set and can be removed from molds
17.  The resulting material is moldable when reheated (80-100°C), becoming plastic and workable; upon cooling returns to rigid state

### Properties of Starch Bioplastics

-   Biodegradable: Microorganisms break down starch in weeks to months
-   Water-sensitive: Swells if exposed to moisture; reduces shelf life
-   Heat resistance: Limited; softens above 80°C
-   Tensile strength: Lower than petroleum plastics; improved with additives (cellulose, polyester)
-   Applications: Packaging films, utensils, injection-molded items

## Common Mistakes in Plastic Production

Understanding frequent errors helps avoid costly failures and safety hazards.

### Bakelite-Specific Errors

**Mistake:** Adding formaldehyde too quickly to phenol; or phenol to formaldehyde (wrong order).
**Result:** Violent exothermic reaction; mixture boils over, acid vapors release uncontrollably.
**Prevention:** Always add formaldehyde (liquid) to phenol (solid), slowly, with constant stirring and cooling.

**Mistake:** Skipping the resole stage; attempting to directly heat phenol-formaldehyde mixture to final cure temperature.
**Result:** Mixture polymerizes too rapidly, becomes unworkable lumps before filling mold. Final product is porous and weak.
**Prevention:** Follow two-stage process: first resole (80-100°C, 2-3 hours, still flowable), then novolac (150-180°C, 1-2 hours under pressure).

**Mistake:** Insufficient mold pressure during cure (attempt to cure in open air or with too-light pressure).
**Result:** Product is porous, weak, and warped. May crack during cooling.
**Prevention:** Always apply 1000+ PSI pressure during cure phase. Use a hydraulic press or heavy weights.

### Celluloid-Specific Errors

**Mistake:** Storing dried cellulose nitrate in air (dry storage).
**Result:** Spontaneous self-ignition after hours or days (exothermic decomposition).
**Prevention:** Always store cellulose nitrate submerged in distilled water or mineral oil in sealed containers. Never allow to dry completely.

**Mistake:** Heating cellulose nitrate mixture above 150°C without careful temperature control.
**Result:** Rapid decomposition, smoke, potential explosion.
**Prevention:** Use thermometer monitoring. Use low heat source (water bath or heating mantle), never open flame. Never exceed 150°C during final plasticization.

**Mistake:** Reversing acid addition order (HNO₃ to H₂SO₄ instead of H₂SO₄ to HNO₃).
**Result:** Violent, uncontrollable exothermic reaction; acid sprays, explosion risk.
**Prevention:** Always add HNO₃ slowly to H₂SO₄ (already in bath), never reverse.

### Casein-Specific Errors

**Mistake:** Adding formaldehyde before heating casein paste; or using temperature above 90°C before formaldehyde added.
**Result:** Premature cross-linking before mixture is fully heated; product sets before reaching mold, becomes unusable.
**Prevention:** Heat casein to 50-60°C first, ensure uniform, soft consistency, then add formaldehyde.

**Mistake:** Boiling the mixture (>100°C) or overheating above 90°C during formaldehyde cross-linking.
**Result:** Protein denatures; product becomes crumbly, weak, and grainy.
**Prevention:** Use water bath (maintains <90°C easily). Monitor temperature with thermometer. Don't exceed 90°C.

**Mistake:** Attempting to mold casein plastic after it has cooled completely.
**Result:** Material is rigid and cannot be shaped; attempting to force-shape causes cracking.
**Prevention:** Mold while still warm (above 60°C). Once cooled fully, material is locked in shape.

### Bioplastic (Starch) Errors

**Mistake:** Using insufficient plasticizer (glycerin); attempting to make bioplastic with starch and water alone.
**Result:** Final product is brittle and cracks easily; does not remain flexible after cooling.
**Prevention:** Always use 20-30% glycerin or urea by weight. Blending starch with other materials improves properties.

**Mistake:** Heating starch above 100°C before adding plasticizer.
**Result:** Starch gelatinizes incompletely; mixture becomes lumpy and unworkable.
**Prevention:** Add plasticizer after initial starch gelatinization (70-80°C), then continue heating together.

## Plastic Quality Control and Testing

Product quality depends on controlling critical variables during production. Quality assurance prevents failures and waste.

### Visual Inspection

**Color and clarity:** Consistent color throughout batch indicates proper mixing and temperature control. Discoloration (yellowing, browning) indicates thermal degradation or contamination.

**Surface quality:** Look for: sink marks (surface depression over voids), warping (deviation from design shape), flash (excess material squeezed between mold halves), weld lines (visible lines where two mold flow fronts meet).

**Dimensional accuracy:** Measure critical dimensions with calipers. Injection-molded parts typically hold ±0.1mm tolerance; compression-molded parts ±0.5mm. Deviation indicates mold temperature or pressure issues.

### Mechanical Testing

**Tensile strength:** Pull test piece until failure. Measure maximum load before breaking. Strong plastic shows high load (>30 N for 1cm width) before failure. Brittle plastic breaks suddenly at low load.

**Impact strength:** Drop weight from specified height onto test sample. Material survives impact without cracking = good impact strength. Thin plastics brittle in cold; thicker or properly annealed samples more impact-resistant.

**Hardness (Scratch test):** Attempt scratching surface with known hardness objects (fingernail ~2-3 Mohs, penny ~3, glass ~5.5). Record what scratches the material. Harder plastics resist scratching—useful for wear resistance applications.

### Thermal Testing

**Heat deflection temperature (HDT):** Heat sample under load until deflection reaches 1mm. Temperature at which deflection occurs indicates heat resistance. Bakelite HDT ~200°C (good thermal resistance). Celluloid HDT ~80°C (poor thermal resistance).

**Thermal cycling:** Rapid temperature changes (-20°C to +60°C repeatedly) test dimensional stability. Material that maintains dimension after 10 cycles = thermally stable. Cracking or permanent deformation indicates poor thermal properties.

### Chemical Resistance

**Solvent immersion test:** Immerse plastic sample in potential solvents (water, oil, alcohol, acid). Check for: swelling (material expands), discoloration, surface softening, or cracking. Resistant plastic shows no change after 24-hour immersion.

**Acid/alkali test:** Immerse in dilute acid (10% HCl) and dilute alkali (10% NaOH) for 24 hours. Check weight change (>5% weight gain indicates poor chemical resistance). Check for visible damage.

## Troubleshooting Plastic Production

### Molding Problems

**Problem:** Final part dimensions smaller than mold cavity (underfilling).

**Cause:** Insufficient pressure, inadequate packing time, cold mold, low material temperature.

**Solution:** Increase packing pressure, extend holding time, increase mold temperature to 60-80°C, increase plastic temperature if safe for material type.

**Problem:** Part has weld lines or visible flow fronts.

**Cause:** Insufficient plastic temperature or low flow rate. Flow fronts cool before merging.

**Solution:** Increase plastic temperature 10-20°C. Increase injection speed/pressure to reduce dwell time in mold. Optimize gate location (where plastic enters mold) to reduce flow fronts.

**Problem:** Sink marks (depressions) on part surface.

**Cause:** Thick sections cool slower than thin sections. Thick sections shrink more, pulling surface inward.

**Solution:** Increase packing pressure (forces plastic into cavity longer). Add gate closer to thick section (keeps plastic flowing, fills shrinkage). Redesign part to have more uniform wall thickness.

### Material Problems

**Problem:** Plastic becomes dark or blacks during processing (scorch marks).

**Cause:** Excessive temperature or residence time in heated barrel. Material begins to thermally degrade.

**Solution:** Reduce barrel temperature 10-20°C. Reduce residence time (clear barrel more frequently between batches). Check temperature sensors (may be reading incorrectly).

**Problem:** Plastic becomes brittle after cooling.

**Cause:** Excessive cooling rate (quenching) causes internal stress. Annealing (slow cooling from stress-relief temperature) relieves stress.

**Solution:** Reduce cooling rate. Remove parts from mold more slowly. If possible, anneal finished parts at 80-120°C for 1-2 hours (temperature depends on plastic type), then slow cool.

**Problem:** Plastic sticks to mold (won't eject).

**Cause:** Mold is too hot or plastic is too soft. Draft angles insufficient (mold sides should taper 0.5-1 degree).

**Solution:** Reduce mold temperature slightly. Verify draft angles adequate. Apply mold release (silicone spray, parting agent) to mold cavity before injection.

<section id="safety-plastic">

## Safety in Plastic Production

Plastic production involves hazardous chemicals and high temperatures. Proper safety precautions prevent serious injury.

:::warning
**WARNING:** Molten plastic (250-300°C) causes severe burns. Never contact hot plastic. Rapid cooling in water can cause steam explosions.
:::

:::tip
**Temperature Monitoring Strategy:** Use a reliable digital thermometer (±2°C accuracy) for all heating processes. Bakelite and celluloid production are temperature-sensitive; deviations of just 10-15°C can result in runaway reactions, product degradation, or failure to cure. For professional results, use a temperature controller (proportional-integral-derivative system) on heating mantles or hot plates. Keep a log of target vs. actual temperatures for each batch—patterns reveal equipment drift or calibration issues.
:::

### Chemical Hazards

**Formaldehyde (Bakelite production):** Toxic gas causing respiratory irritation and cancer risk. Fume hood with dedicated ventilation essential. Gloves and goggles protect from liquid.

**Phenol (Bakelite):** Corrosive, absorbed through skin. Causes chemical burns and systemic toxicity. Protective equipment (nitrile gloves, face shield, apron) required.

**Nitric acid (Celluloid production):** Highly corrosive. Causes severe burns. Fume hood required. Immediate water rinse if exposure occurs.

**Acetone and solvents:** Volatile organic compounds causing respiratory and CNS effects. Well-ventilated area or fume hood required. Avoid skin contact (can dissolve natural skin oils).

### Thermal Hazards

**Molten plastic burns:** If spilled or splashed, immediately immerse in cool water (NOT ice—temperature gradient causes cracking). Do not pull away from skin (plastic sticks). Cool gradually. Seek medical attention for burns >2 sq cm.

**Steam explosions:** Water in heated plastic creates steam rapidly. Never use water to quench hot plastic in barrel. Allow cooling or use dry methods.

**Fire from overheating:** Organic plastics ignite if heated excessively. Keep fire suppression (dry powder or CO₂) nearby. Don't use water on organic plastic fires.

### Pressure Hazards

**Injection/compression molding:** High pressures (1000-5000 PSI) can cause serious injury if hands caught in closing mold. Never reach into mold during operation. Install guards/interlocks preventing hand access during operation.

**Pressure vessel rupture:** If sealed container (barrel, mold) overpressures, explosion hazard exists. Pressure relief valves essential. Check gauges for correct operation.

### Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

- **Eyes/face:** Safety goggles for chemical work, face shield for hot plastic operations
- **Hands:** Nitrile gloves for chemical exposure, insulated gloves near heat
- **Body:** Lab coat or apron protecting from splashes
- **Respiratory:** Half-mask respirator with cartridges appropriate for fumes (formaldehyde, organic solvents)
- **Feet:** Closed-toe shoes; never wear sandals in plastic processing area

## Sustainable Plastic Production

As petrochemical plastics deplete post-collapse, sustainable alternatives become essential.

### Cellulose-Based Plastics

Cellulose (from wood pulp or cotton) can be converted to various plastics:

**Celluloid:** Historical process; less efficient than modern methods but requires minimal equipment.

**Cellulose acetate:** Modify celluloid by acetylation. More stable than cellulose nitrate (lower flammability). Production requires acetic anhydride (harder to synthesize).

**Regenerated cellulose (viscose):** Dissolve cellulose in alkali, form into fibers or sheets. Produces cellulose film (transparent, flexible) useful for packaging/windows.

### Starch Bioplastics (Reprise)

Starch-based plastics offer rapid biodegradation (weeks to months). Limitations: water-sensitive, poor thermal stability.

**Improvement:** Blend starch with other polymers (PVA, polybutylene succinate) to improve properties. Requires access to these polymers (salvage from old products).

### Protein Plastics

Casein (milk protein) and other proteins create moldable bioplastics. Limited heat resistance (soften above 80°C) but adequate for many non-structural applications.

### Lignin Plastics

Lignin (wood component) mixed with resins creates composite materials. Properties depend on source and processing. Generally brittle but useful for non-load-bearing parts.

### Recycled Plastic Blending

Mixing virgin plastic with recycled plastic (proportions: 10-50% recycled) improves sustainability. Properties degrade with each recycling cycle; limit recycling to 5-10 iterations maximum.

:::note
**Post-Collapse Note:** In true resource scarcity, recycling becomes critical. Accept lower properties (more brittle, reduced clarity) as tradeoff for material availability.
:::
