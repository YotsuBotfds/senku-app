<section id="travois">

## Travois & Sleds

The simplest form of transportation relies on dragging loads across terrain. A travois is an A-frame structure that can be pulled by humans or animals, distributing weight efficiently along two poles. This ancient design requires minimal materials and no wheels.

### Travois Construction

A basic travois consists of two long poles (8-12 feet) lashed together at one end. The wide end drags on the ground while the narrow end is attached to a pack animal or harness. Between the poles, straps or branches create a platform for cargo.

:::card
:::card
#### Materials Needed

Two sturdy poles (6-8 cm diameter), rawhide strips or rope, wooden crossbars, animal hide or canvas for platform
:::

:::card
#### Advantages

Easy to make, works on any terrain, no wheels to break, supports heavy loads, minimal maintenance
:::

:::card
#### Limitations

Slower than wheeled transport, high friction, wears poles quickly on rocky ground, difficult on slopes
:::
:::

### Travois Design Diagram

![⚙ Transportation &amp; Infrastructure diagram 1](../assets/svgs/transportation-1.svg)

### Sled Variations

For snowy or sandy terrain, sleds offer advantages over travois. A sled spreads weight across a wider surface, reducing sinking. Wooden sleds with curved runners glide smoothly and can carry heavier loads. Sleds work well with dog teams or single large animals.

Construction: Use curved hardwood runners connected by cross-supports. Apply animal fat to runners to reduce friction. Height should allow a person to walk alongside while controlling the load.

## Wheels &amp; Carts

The wheel revolutionized transportation by reducing friction and enabling much heavier loads. Creating reliable wheels requires understanding wood selection, construction techniques, and axle design. A well-made wheel can last decades.

### Solid Wheel Construction

The simplest wheel is a thick wooden disk cut from a cross-section of large log. However, solid wheels are heavy and prone to cracking. They work adequately for carts traveling short distances, but better designs distribute wood and reduce weight.

### Spoked Wheel Design

A spoked wheel consists of a hub (center), spokes (radiating support pieces), and a rim. This design is much lighter while maintaining strength. Spokes should be made from straight-grained wood and fitted into the hub with tight joints.

![⚙ Transportation &amp; Infrastructure diagram 2](../assets/svgs/transportation-2.svg)

### Axle &amp; Bearing Systems

The axle must support the wheel's weight while allowing smooth rotation. A simple wooden axle works for light loads, but friction causes overheating. Wrapping the axle with leather or canvas provides some bearing surface.

For better performance, create bearing surfaces with:

-   **Stone or ceramic cups** at each end of the axle
-   **Iron or bronze sleeves** around the axle (superior if metal is available)
-   **Animal fat or plant oil** as lubricant (replenished regularly)
-   **Wooden bushings** with smooth grain running horizontally

### Cart Design

A basic two-wheel cart consists of a rectangular frame on two wheels, with a tongue or shaft for a draft animal. A four-wheel wagon offers better stability and can carry more weight. Key measurements:

:::card
:::card
#### Two-Wheel Cart

Lighter, more maneuverable. Wheel height: 3-4 feet. Cargo box: 4-6 feet long. Good for 500-800 lbs.
:::

:::card
#### Four-Wheel Wagon

Stable, higher capacity. Front wheels smaller (2-3 ft) than rear (4-5 ft) for turning. Capacity: 1-2 tons.
:::

:::card
#### Draft Pole

Connect to animal via yoke or harness. Height: 3-4 feet from ground. Length: 8-12 feet depending on animals.
:::
:::

## Pack Saddles &amp; Harnesses

Before wheels existed—or in terrain where they cannot operate—pack animals carried goods. Horses, mules, donkeys, and oxen require proper equipment to carry loads safely and maintain health during long journeys.

### Pack Saddle Types

The sawbuck packsaddle is the most versatile design for rough terrain. It features an X-shaped frame over the animal's back, designed to balance loads on both sides. Cargo attaches directly to the frame without touching the animal's spine.

### Sawbuck Packsaddle Construction

![⚙ Transportation &amp; Infrastructure diagram 3](../assets/svgs/transportation-3.svg)

### Harness and Fitting

Proper fit is critical for animal welfare and efficiency. Ill-fitting equipment causes sores, reduces performance, and can injure the animal permanently.

:::warning
**Poorly fitted pack saddles cause permanent damage.** A saddle that shifts causes friction sores on the animal's back, leading to infection and long-term disability. Check fit daily and adjust for muscle loss during extended trips. Replace any chafed areas with padding immediately.
:::

:::card
:::card
#### Horses

Saddle sits behind withers. Harness includes shoulder straps, crupper (under tail), and breeching. Can carry 150-250 lbs comfortably.
:::

:::card
#### Mules &amp; Donkeys

Stronger than horses pound-for-pound. Less prone to panic. More sure-footed on rough terrain. Can carry 150-200 lbs.
:::

:::card
#### Oxen

Strongest draft animal, slower than horses. Use wooden yoke. Single ox: 1200 lbs pull capacity. Pair: 2500+ lbs.
:::
:::

### Weight Distribution Principles

:::warning
**Uneven loading strains animals unevenly and causes permanent injury.** Keep loads centered over the animal's strongest points (shoulders and hips) and distribute weight equally on both sides (within 5%). Avoid pressure on the spine or kidneys.
:::

-   Pack awkward items in the middle; balance on sides
-   Check fit daily; adjust for muscle loss over long trips
-   Rest animals every 15-20 miles; let them graze and drink

<section id="roads">

## Road Building

Effective transportation requires reliable roads. A good road reduces travel time, protects cargo, minimizes animal wear, and enables trade. Roads require ongoing maintenance but provide returns across generations.

### Basic Road Grading

The first step is clearing vegetation and establishing a defined path. Mark the route using stakes. The road width should accommodate two-way traffic (12-16 feet for main routes). Slope the center slightly for drainage.

### Drainage and Ditch Systems

Drainage is essential to prevent roads from becoming impassable mud. Dig shallow ditches parallel to the road, sloping away from the center. These ditches direct water into larger collection channels that feed to streams or wetlands.

![⚙ Transportation &amp; Infrastructure diagram 4](../assets/svgs/transportation-4.svg)

### Gravel Road Construction

Layer different sizes of aggregate to create a stable, draining surface:

1.  **Base layer (30cm):** Large stones (5-10cm) to prevent soil from mixing with road
2.  **Second layer (15cm):** Medium gravel (2-5cm) for interlocking stability
3.  **Surface layer (5-10cm):** Smaller gravel or crushed stone for smooth travel

### Corduroy Roads

For wet or marshy terrain, lay logs perpendicular to the road direction. These corduroy (log) roads allow passage through otherwise impassable ground. Space logs 1-2 feet apart and peg them down. The road surface is rough but functional.

### Mountain Roads and Switchbacks

On steep slopes, switchbacks prevent erosion and make climbing easier. A properly graded switchback has a gentle slope (never more than 10-15% grade). Each switchback should have a radius of at least 30-40 feet to accommodate wagons. Place turnouts every 500 yards where traffic can pass.

<section id="bridges">

## Bridges

Bridges enable transport networks to cross obstacles. Different designs suit different situations. The choice depends on span width, water current, available materials, and traffic volume.

### Log Beam Bridge

The simplest design: large logs laid parallel across a stream, supporting a deck. Select logs 1-2 feet in diameter and at least twice the span length. Secure the ends in solid banks or on stone abutments. Space logs 1-2 feet apart and cover with planks or smaller logs.

### Truss Bridge Design

For longer spans (20-50+ feet), a simple truss distributes weight more efficiently. A triangulated frame distributes forces through multiple members rather than bending a single beam.

![⚙ Transportation &amp; Infrastructure diagram 5](../assets/svgs/transportation-5.svg)

### Suspension Bridges

For deep canyons or wide rivers, hang the deck from strong cables anchored to high points on either side. Use twisted plant fiber, leather strips, or iron wire depending on materials available. The cables must be redundant (multiple parallel cables) for safety. A simple plank or wooden deck hangs from vertical suspender cables.

### Pontoon Bridges

Floating platforms (pontoons) made from sealed wooden barrels or logs support a deck. Rope anchors secure them to banks or bed anchors. Pontoon bridges work well for calm rivers and are portable for military applications.

### Ford Crossings

For shallow streams, ford crossings allow direct passage. Stone or gravel fords improve traction and reduce erosion. Mark the shallowest, safest route with poles or cairns. Survey annually to maintain the crossing.

<section id="boats">

## Boat Building

Waterways provide natural highways. Building reliable boats enables cargo transport, trade, and exploration. Different designs suit different waters and purposes.

### Dugout Canoe

The simplest boat is a hollowed log. Select a large, straight tree (2-3 feet diameter minimum). Burn and scrape out the interior, or use tools to hollow it gradually. Seal cracks with pitch or plant resin. A crude dugout is stable and lasts for years.

### Bark Canoe

Layer birch (or similar) bark over a wooden frame. This design is lighter than dugout canoes and easier to repair. Sew the bark with roots and seal seams with pitch. Bark canoes are excellent for long journeys as they can be hauled across land.

### Plank Boat Construction

Using sawed or split planks, build a hull with multiple layers. This design requires more skill but produces faster, stronger vessels.

![⚙ Transportation &amp; Infrastructure diagram 6](../assets/svgs/transportation-6.svg)

### Coracle

A round boat made by stretching animal hide or plant-fiber fabric over a wooden frame. Coracles are lightweight, portable, and surprisingly stable. They work well in rocky rivers and coastal areas.

### Raft Construction

Bundle logs or sealed barrels together to create a floating platform. Lash them securely with rope. A simple raft can transport heavy cargo and is ideal for river transport. Add a pole for steering or a simple oar.

:::tip
**Boat maintenance is critical for long-term reliability.** Caulk seams regularly, apply pitch to prevent rot, check structural integrity, and beach boats annually to clean and repair the hull. A well-maintained boat lasts 20-30 years or more.
:::

## Sailing Basics

Harnessing wind power multiplies the cargo that can be transported. Understanding sail trim, boat handling, and wind direction is essential for effective navigation.

### Points of Sail

A boat's course relative to the wind determines sail efficiency:

![⚙ Transportation &amp; Infrastructure diagram 7](../assets/svgs/transportation-7.svg)

### Basic Sail Types

:::card
:::card
#### Square Sail

Traditional design, hangs perpendicular to mast. Excellent downwind. Harder to adjust, less efficient upwind. Common on large trading ships.
:::

:::card
#### Lateen Sail

Triangular sail, hangs at angle. Better upwind performance. Allows tacking. More efficient for exploring and trade routes with variable wind.
:::
:::

### Sail Rigging and Trim

-   Leech and luff: The trailing and leading edges of sail. Tension controls shape and efficiency.
-   Halyards: Ropes pulling the sail up. Multiple halyards allow rapid sail adjustment.
-   Sheets: Ropes controlling boom angle. Trim sheets to maximize wind capture without stalling.
-   Boom: Horizontal pole supporting sail bottom. Angle to wind determines boat direction.

### Tacking and Jibing

Tacking (coming about) means turning toward the wind, crossing the wind line, and shifting sails to the other side. Tacking allows a boat to sail into the wind by zigzagging. Each tack loses speed, so minimize tacking on long voyages.

:::danger
**Jibing (wearing) is dangerous when not executed carefully.** Turning away from the wind means the boom swings across the boat. If crew is not clear or the boom strikes someone, serious injury or death can result. Always shout "Jibe ho!" to clear crew before executing a jibe. Secure loose equipment so it doesn't swing with the boom.
:::

### Basic Seamanship

:::tip
**Proper seamanship practices prevent accidents and extend vessel life.** Know your exit route before entering narrow waters. Keep essential gear tied down; heavy items belong low in the boat. Rotate crew regularly to prevent fatigue on long passages. Monitor weather constantly and understand local wind patterns.
:::

-   Anchor in calm water to rest; use proper anchor technique
-   Maintain the boat: check ropes for fraying, repair sails, caulk hull regularly

## Canals &amp; Waterways

Artificial canals extend transport networks into regions without natural rivers. Canals require significant labor but provide returns across centuries through trade and resource movement.

### Canal Routing

A successful canal connects two water sources (rivers, lakes) via the flattest possible route. Survey carefully to minimize elevation changes. Even small drops require locks or spillways, which are expensive to build.

### Lock Construction

Locks allow boats to pass elevation changes. A simple lock is a wooden or stone chamber with gates on both ends. To raise a boat, fill the lock with water, raising the boat to the higher level. To lower a boat, drain water through sluice gates. A single lock can serve both directions.

### Canal Cross-Section

A typical barge canal has:

-   **Depth:** 4-6 feet for full-sized barges (smaller canals: 2-3 feet)
-   **Bottom width:** 30-50 feet to accommodate barges
-   **Side slopes:** 1:2 (for every foot high, two feet wide) to prevent collapse
-   **Towpath:** 12-15 feet wide alongside the canal for draft animals
-   **Berms:** Flat areas on outer banks for loading/unloading

### Barge Design

Barges are flat-bottomed cargo vessels designed for canals and rivers. Typical dimensions:

-   **Length:** 50-100 feet
-   **Beam (width):** 12-18 feet
-   **Draft (depth in water):** 3-4 feet when loaded
-   **Cargo capacity:** 30-100 tons depending on size

Barges are towed by draft animals walking on the towpath, using a rope 50-100 feet long. One horse can pull a loaded barge at 2-3 miles per hour on a calm canal.

### Maintenance

Canals require constant upkeep: clearing vegetation, repairing embankments, dredging silt accumulation, and maintaining locks. A well-organized community can maintain a canal indefinitely with regular labor contributions.

## Vehicle &amp; Fuel Scavenging

In post-collapse scenarios, vehicles become precious transport resources. Understanding fuel salvage, storage, and basic vehicle maintenance extends vehicle operational life. Fuel drives are one of the first year's critical priorities for transport and equipment operation.

### Fuel Siphoning Safely

:::danger
**Never use mouth suction to siphon gasoline.** Gasoline is toxic. Swallowing even small amounts causes serious poisoning, including respiratory damage, organ failure, and potential death. This is an absolute rule—no exceptions.
:::

**Tube siphon method (safest):** Use clear tubing (clear allows monitoring for fuel entry). Insert one end into tank, place other end in collection container. Create siphon by filling tube with fuel using hand pump or by dipping below tank level. Gravity does the work once siphon primed. Stop by lifting collection end above tank level or pinching tube.

**Emergency method:** If no pump available, fill tube below tank level, insert one end in tank, place other end in lower container. Gravity pull starts siphon when you release air from your mouth into the tube (never suck).

### Fuel Shelf Life and Storage

| Fuel Type | Shelf Life | Special Considerations |
|-----------|------------|------------------------|
| Gasoline | 3-6 months | Volatile components evaporate; fuel becomes gummy. Add fuel stabilizer to extend to 1-2 years. |
| Diesel | 6-12 months | More stable than gasoline. Water absorption is risk—condenses in tank. Keep sealed. |
| Ethanol-free fuel | 12+ months | Longer lasting (ethanol absorbs water). Seek non-ethanol blends if available. |

:::tip
**Proper fuel storage maximizes shelf life.** Use food-grade plastic containers (not metal unless coated). Keep tightly sealed, cool, away from sunlight. Rotate stock (oldest first). Store fuel outside building in ventilated area. Gasoline vapors are heavier than air and accumulate at ground level—never store indoors.
:::

### Basic Vehicle Troubleshooting

#### Dead Battery

-   **Push start (manual transmission only):** Vehicle must be on incline or have helpers push. Press clutch in, shift to second gear. Release clutch while vehicle rolling = engine fires. If you do not have a manual transmission, this option is not available.
-   **Jumper cables &amp; donor battery:** Use proper automotive jumper cables and a donor battery. Connect positive cable to dead battery positive terminal, then to donor battery positive. Connect negative to donor negative, then to unpainted metal on dead vehicle (ground). Wait a few minutes, start dead vehicle. Reverse disconnection order.
-   **No jumper cables or donor battery:** Do not improvise with USB cords, consumer electronics chargers, or direct-battery wire splices. Treat the vehicle as a repair/abandonment problem and switch transport plans.
-   **Battery replacement:** Old batteries can be recycled. Lead can be recovered. Worn-out batteries won't hold charge.

#### Flat Tire

-   Use jack to raise vehicle safely (place jack on level ground, under frame, not on plastic trim).
-   Loosen lug nuts before raising vehicle (easier to break loose when weight on tire).
-   Remove tire, inspect for damage (puncture, sidewall tear, bead damage).
-   Small puncture can be plugged with kit (temporary repair). Larger damage requires tire replacement.
-   Spare tire (if available) is temporary. Repair or replace permanent tire ASAP.

#### Belt Replacement

:::warning
**A broken serpentine belt leaves you stranded.** This belt drives the alternator, water pump, and AC compressor. A worn or cracked belt will slip or break, causing loss of charging and engine cooling. Monitor belt condition regularly and replace at first sign of fraying or cracks.
:::

-   Remove old belt by loosening tensioner. Note routing before removal (diagram usually under hood).
-   Install new belt same routing. Tighten tensioner so belt has ~0.5" deflection at midpoint under moderate pressure.
-   Test by running engine briefly. Listen for squealing (indicates slipping belt).

### Alternative Fuels for Diesel Engines

**Critical advantage of diesel:** Can run on alternatives. Provides flexibility in fuel-scarce situations.

-   **Vegetable oil (straight):** Will work in warm climates (viscosity is issue in cold). Fuel filters clog faster. Clean/filter vegetable oil before use.
-   **Biodiesel:** Vegetable oil converted through chemical process (methylation). Superior to straight oil. Can blend with diesel for better properties.
-   **Waste motor oil (filtered):** Used engine oil can fuel diesel if extremely well-filtered (remove water and particles). Use 50/50 blend with diesel for reliability.

:::warning
**Alternative fuels reduce engine lifespan.** Use only if necessity demands. Proper filtration prevents damage. Plan to return to standard diesel when fuel situation improves.
:::

### Wood Gas Conversion (Gasifier Technology)

A gasifier converts wood into combustible gas that fuels internal combustion engines. This allows wood-fueled vehicles. Complex technology; cross-reference energy-systems guide for detailed construction. Requires wood supply, proper design, and operator knowledge. Can extend vehicle life indefinitely with fuel self-sufficiency.

<section id="fuel-variations">

## Fuel Variations and Alternative Fuels for Engines

When conventional fuel is unavailable, several alternative fuels can sustain engine operation. Each has distinct properties, preparation requirements, and effects on engine longevity.

### Wood Gas (Producer Gas) - Highest Priority for Sustainability

Wood gas production converts solid wood into combustible gas through controlled pyrolysis (decomposition in low-oxygen environment). The resulting gas mixture (CO, hydrogen, methane) fuels modified gasoline or diesel engines.

**Gasifier System Components:**
1. **Fuel hopper:** Holds wood chips or charcoal (1-2 inch diameter pieces optimal)
2. **Combustion chamber:** Hot zone where fuel partially oxidizes, generating heat
3. **Reduction zone:** Below combustion chamber; heat converts remaining material to gas
4. **Cooling system:** Cools gas before entering engine; removes moisture
5. **Filtration:** Removes tar and particulates that damage engine
6. **Gas mixer:** Blends producer gas with air in proper proportion for engine

**Wood Fuel Preparation:**
- Chip size: 1-2 inches (smaller fuel produces gas faster; larger fuel sustains longer)
- Moisture: <20% (dry wood produces better gas; wet wood requires excessive heat)
- Species: Hardwoods preferred (oak, maple, birch); avoid softwoods (pine, fir) with high resin
- Charcoal: Can be used; higher energy density than wood; produces superior gas

**Power Output:**
- 1 kg of dry wood produces ~3-4 kWh of usable engine power
- Typical vehicle consumption: 1.5-2.5 kg wood per hour under load
- 10-day supply for frequent vehicle user: 25-30 kg wood (requires preparation time)

**Efficiency and Performance:**
- Engine power: Reduced ~30% compared to gasoline (insufficient power for steep grades or rapid acceleration)
- Thermal efficiency: 25-35% (lower than pure fuel engines due to cooling losses)
- Cold starting: Difficult; typically requires preheating chamber before starting
- Scaling: Can power from small (10hp motorcycle) to large (50hp truck) engines with appropriate design

**Maintenance and Challenges:**
- Tar accumulation in coolers and filters; requires weekly cleaning
- Occasional engine fouling from tarry deposits; requires more frequent oil changes
- Corrosion from wood gas moisture (sulfurous compounds); use stainless or coated pipes
- Operator skill: Starting, adjustment, maintenance requires training and attention

:::warning
**Wood Gas Safety:** Improperly designed gasifiers produce carbon monoxide, an odorless, lethal gas. Ensure proper ventilation during operation and storage. Never operate gasifiers indoors without external exhaust venting.
:::

### Biodiesel (Vegetable Oil Derivatives) - High Energy, Renewable

Biodiesel is vegetable or animal oil converted through methylation (reaction with methanol and catalyst) into fuel chemically similar to petroleum diesel. Diesel engines can run on pure biodiesel or blends with conventional diesel.

**Feedstock Sources:**
- Vegetable oils: Soy, canola, sunflower, peanut, coconut
- Animal fats: Tallow (cattle fat), lard (pork fat), fish oil
- Waste oil: Used cooking oil from restaurants/food processing
- Oil yield: Typical crop produces 150-300 gallons per acre annually

**Biodiesel Conversion (Transesterification):**
1. **Collection:** Collect and filter oil to remove food particles
2. **Heating:** Warm oil to 100-120°F
3. **Methanol addition:** Add methanol (10-20% of oil weight) to oil
4. **Catalyst:** Potassium hydroxide (lye) at ~1% of oil weight catalyzes reaction
5. **Mixing:** Stir for 30-60 minutes while maintaining temperature
6. **Settling:** Allow mixture to separate overnight (biodiesel settles below glycerin byproduct)
7. **Separation:** Drain biodiesel from bottom; save glycerin for soap-making
8. **Washing:** Rinse biodiesel with water to remove residual catalyst/glycerin
9. **Drying:** Heat gently to remove water (critical for engine performance)

**Diesel Engine Compatibility:**
- Pure biodiesel (B100): Works in modern diesel engines; older engines may have seal/gasket degradation
- Blends (B5-B20): 5-20% biodiesel + 80-95% conventional diesel; works in virtually all diesels
- Advantages: Excellent fuel economy (~98% of diesel); less particulate pollution; renewable
- Disadvantages: Slight reduction in power; higher cloud point (gels in cold weather) unless winterized

**Yield and Economics:**
- 100 lbs vegetable oil → ~100 lbs biodiesel (+ 13 lbs glycerin byproduct)
- Typical vehicle: 25-30 mpg on biodiesel (equivalent to conventional diesel)
- Annual demand for typical vehicle: ~500 lbs biodiesel (requires ~500 lbs feedstock oil)
- For community self-sufficiency: ~1 acre oil crop per vehicle (sustainable)

**Maintenance:**
- Oil filter changes more frequent (biodiesel is solvent; cleans injectors and fuel tanks, initially releasing deposits)
- Engine life: Slightly extended (reduced particulates, better lubricity)
- Storage: Biodiesel oxidizes slowly; store in cool, dark conditions; 6-12 month shelf life

### Ethanol (Alcohol Fuel) - Limited but Possible

Ethanol (ethyl alcohol) is produced by fermentation of sugars. Can power modified gasoline engines as pure ethanol (E100) or blended fuel (E10-E85).

**Fermentation Production:**
1. **Sugar source:** Grain (corn, wheat), fruit, sugarcane, potatoes, honey
2. **Fermentation:** Mix sugar source with yeast in anaerobic container (no air access)
3. **Duration:** 5-10 days at room temperature
4. **Distillation:** Heat fermented liquid to boiling; ethanol vapor rises and is captured, condensed
5. **Concentration:** Process yields 5-15% ethanol in water; repeated distillation produces >90% ethanol

**Gasoline Engine Modifications:**
- Pure ethanol (E100) requires fuel system modifications (ethanol corrodes some seals/gaskets)
- Modern engines: Accept up to E10-E15 blends without modification
- Higher ethanol blends (E85): Require fuel injector adjustment, engine timing changes
- Cold starting: Ethanol evaporates more readily; engines start easier in winter but harder in extreme cold

**Efficiency and Performance:**
- Energy content: Ethanol ~67% of gasoline (by volume); reduces fuel economy ~30%
- Engine power: Ethanol's high octane allows advanced ignition timing; can produce equal power if properly tuned
- Fuel consumption: Vehicle using E85 (85% ethanol) consumes ~50% more fuel than gasoline for same distance

**Yield and Sustainability:**
- Grain ethanol: 2.8-3.2 gallons per bushel corn (1 bushel = 56 lbs)
- Annual yield: 1 acre corn → 300-350 gallons ethanol (sufficient for one vehicle annually)
- Competition: Food crops for fuel creates ethical concerns; consider non-food crops (cellulose, industrial waste)

### Charcoal (Solid Fuel) - Limited Application, High Energy Density

Charcoal is partially-carbonized wood (pyrolyzed); high energy density and clean burn. Cannot directly fuel gasoline engines but can be converted to gas in gasifier or burned for stationary power.

**Production:**
- Burn wood with limited oxygen (~50%) to partial completion
- Result: Charcoal remains with most of wood's energy retained
- Energy density: ~2x that of wood (by weight)

**Vehicle Application:**
- Not directly useful for gasoline/diesel engines
- Charcoal can fuel gasifiers (superior to wood gas)
- Charcoal briquettes can provide heat for warming fuel tanks in winter

### Effects of Alternative Fuels on Engine Maintenance

**Wood Gas:**
- Oil changes: Every 25-50 hours (vs. 200+ hours conventional fuel) due to tar contamination
- Filter replacement: Weekly cleaning or replacement
- Spark plugs: More frequent fouling; cleaning/replacement every 50 hours

**Biodiesel:**
- Oil changes: Normal intervals (biodiesel maintains oil quality)
- Fuel filter: More frequent replacement initially (cleaning deposits); stabilizes after break-in
- Engine life: Potentially extended (cleaner combustion)

**Ethanol:**
- Oil changes: Normal intervals
- Fuel system: Corrosion potential; protect with rust inhibitors
- Engine tuning: Periodic adjustment for optimal performance

**Charcoal/Gasifier:**
- Maintenance: Similar to wood gas (see above)
- Advantage: Less tar production than wood gas

:::warning
**Emissions and Legality:** Alternative fuels often produce different emissions; local regulations may restrict use in populated areas. Document fuel type clearly to avoid confusion with theft/contamination. Some jurisdictions require engine modification permits; check local regulations before implementation.
:::

### Fuel Storage and Shelf Life

| Fuel Type | Shelf Life | Storage Conditions | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Gasoline** | 3-6 months | Cool, dark, sealed | Degrades; add stabilizer for extension |
| **Diesel** | 6-12 months | Cool, dry, sealed | More stable than gas; water absorption risk |
| **Biodiesel** | 6-12 months | Cool, dark, sealed, nitrogen purge if available | Oxidizes; sealed storage extends life |
| **Ethanol** | 12+ months | Sealed, away from water | Absorbs water; store with desiccant |
| **Wood/Charcoal** | Years if dry | Dry shelter, protected from weather | Moisture reduces heating value |
| **Producer Gas** | Used immediately | N/A (gas-only fuel) | Cannot be stored; produced on-demand |

### Transition Strategy for Fuel Scarcity

In scenario of progressive fuel shortage:

1. **Phase 1 (Gasoline available, expensive):** Blend conventional fuel with biodiesel (B5-B10); reduces consumption rate without major modifications
2. **Phase 2 (Gasoline scarce):** Switch to biodiesel/ethanol blends; modify engine for E85 or higher biodiesel blend
3. **Phase 3 (All liquid fuels scarce):** Convert to wood gas if engine modification feasible; otherwise transition to animal/human-powered transport
4. **Phase 4 (Complete transition):** Establish stable renewable fuel production (biodiesel from oil crops, ethanol from grain) or abandon motorized transport for community-scale work

## Bicycle Mechanics

The bicycle is the most efficient human-powered transport. A cyclist can travel 40-50 miles per day and carry 50-100 lbs on cargo racks. Salvaging and repairing bicycles is essential knowledge for off-grid communities.

### Bicycle Efficiency

A bicycle converts about 95% of human effort into motion—far more efficient than walking (25%) or running (40%). A single cyclist uses less energy than a person on foot covering the same distance. Cargo bikes extend this advantage, enabling one person to transport loads that otherwise require draft animals. Over a year, one bike replaces one draft animal for light hauling work.

### Detailed Tire Repair

**Patching inner tube (temporary fix):**

-   Locate puncture (listen for hissing, feel for air, submerge in water to see bubbles).
-   Mark hole with pen. Remove any foreign object.
-   Roughen area around hole with sandpaper (included in patch kit). Smoothing improves patch adhesion.
-   Apply thin, even layer of adhesive. Wait specified time (usually 30-60 seconds).
-   Press patch firmly over hole. Hold for 1-2 minutes. Allow adhesive to cure per instructions before inflating.
-   Reinflate tube. Test patch by listening for air leak.
-   **Note:** Patches are temporary. Plan for permanent repair or tube replacement.

**Replacing tube and tire:**

1.  Remove wheel from bike frame (loosen quick-release or axle nuts).
2.  Remove tire levers (or two spoons in emergency). Insert carefully between rim and tire bead.
3.  Pry tire away from rim gradually around wheel (don't puncture tube further).
4.  Remove old tube. Inspect tire interior for thorns, glass, or damage.
5.  Remove rim tape (protective barrier between tube and spoke ends). Inspect for spoke protrusions.
6.  Install new rim tape (align with spoke holes). Smooth carefully to prevent wrinkles.
7.  Insert new tube. Start by inflating slightly to give tube shape and proper positioning.
8.  Reinstall tire bead. Press tire back onto rim around wheel. Requires patience (tires are tight when new).
9.  Inflate gradually, checking that bead seats properly (sidewall bead line should align with rim edge).
10. Inflate to recommended pressure (sidewall of tire shows range).
11. Reinstall wheel on bike. Tighten axle nuts or close quick-release securely. Spin wheel to verify it doesn't rub frame.

### Chain Maintenance in Depth

-   **Cleaning:** Wipe chain with cloth while rotating pedals. Alternatively, soak chain overnight in degreaser, scrub with brush, rinse thoroughly, dry.
-   **Lubrication:** Apply light chain lube (not grease). Too much attracts dirt; too little causes wear. Spin pedals to work lube into links. Wipe excess.
-   **Chain removal (for deep cleaning):** Use chain tool to push out a pin (or break chain on master link). Remove old pin/link. Chain can now be cleaned thoroughly off-bike. Reinstall by pushing pin back in or reconnecting master link.
-   **Broken link:** Chain links can break (bent or cracked). Remove broken link with chain tool. Reinstall chain shortening by one link. Works if link broken (connects correctly). Major wear causes multiple link breaks (chain worn—replacement needed).
-   **Chain stretch/wear:** Over time, chains stretch (distance between pins increases). Worn chain slips on sprockets. Use chain checker tool (cheap, essential). Replace when chain stretches 0.5% (usually ~2000 miles). New chains on worn sprockets will slip until chain stretches to match sprocket wear—both need replacement together eventually.

### Brake Service Details

:::warning
**Brake failure is life-threatening.** Test brakes regularly on descents. Brake pads must have adequate thickness; worn pads reduce stopping power dramatically. Brake cables can fray and bind, losing stopping power abruptly. Replace any questionable cables immediately—your life depends on reliable brakes.
:::

-   **Rim brakes (caliper):** Brake pads press against wheel rim. Adjust brake by tightening cable (barrel adjuster) until pads are ~2mm from rim. Equal pressure on both sides (adjust with brake mounting bolts). Replace pads when worn (minimal pad thickness).
-   **Disc brakes (modern bikes):** Brake pads press against rotor. Adjust by centering caliper (rotor should be centered between pads). Replace pads when worn. Bleeding brake fluid (hydraulic brakes) requires specialized technique/equipment.
-   **Testing:** Brake should stop bike firmly within 10-15 feet from moderate speed. No rubbing sounds. Both brakes should work equally.

### Wheel Truing (Fixing Wobbles)

-   Place bike upside-down or use wheel stand. Spin wheel slowly; observe if it rubs on brake pads or frame.
-   Identify which direction spoke(s) pull wheel. Use spoke wrench to adjust tension.
-   Tighten spokes on side wheel is pulling away from (pulls wheel back toward center). Loosen spokes on opposite side if needed.
-   Small adjustments (quarter-turn on spoke wrench). Check after each adjustment.
-   Goal: wheel spins true, no rubbing, even tension on all spokes.
-   **Skill note:** Truing requires practice. Poor truing causes uneven brake wear and handling issues.

### Spoke Replacement

-   Broken spoke weakens wheel structure. Replace immediately.
-   Remove tire and tube to access spokes.
-   Remove broken spoke. Note length and gauge (thickness)—spokes are specific to wheel size/pattern.
-   Salvage spokes from old/damaged wheels if available (community resource).
-   Thread new spoke through wheel hub. Route properly (follows pattern of other spokes—over/under pattern).
-   Thread into rim nipple. Tighten with spoke wrench until tension matches adjacent spokes (use spoke tension meter if available, or listen for equal tone when plucking spokes).
-   Reinstall tire/tube. May need to true wheel after new spoke (tension adjustment).

### Cargo Bike Conversion for Heavy Loads

-   **Rear racks:** Heavy-duty racks rated 50+ lbs. Mount securely to frame (not seatpost—will bend). Use rack with pannier bags for balanced loading.
-   **Front racks:** Lighter loads in front. Affects steering. Use low-rider racks (mount low on fork) for better handling than high baskets.
-   **Trailer option:** Single-wheel trailer (BOB or similar) tows from seatpost. Capacity 50-100 lbs. Handles weight better than loaded bike. Easier to park/maneuver loaded.
-   **Gearing:** Low gears (small chainring, large rear sprocket) essential for hills with load. Single-speed cargo bikes should have ~1.5 ratio (chainring size / sprocket size) for typical terrain.
-   **Wheels:** Cargo load stresses wheels/spokes. Thicker spokes (12-gauge), stronger rims handle loads better. Replace standard 13-gauge spokes with 12-gauge for cargo duty.
-   **Tires:** Wider, more puncture-resistant tires reduce flats under load. Thicker rubber layer protects from sharp objects.

### Bicycle as Force Multiplier

-   **Energy efficiency:** Bicycle requires 1/3 the energy of walking to cover same distance (3-4x more efficient).
-   **Cargo capacity:** One cyclist with cargo trailer = equivalent to person walking with 4-5 days pack capacity. Extends range dramatically.
-   **Speed:** Typical cycling speed 12-15 mph (vs. 3-4 mph walking). Cuts travel time to 1/4 of walking journey.
-   **Reliability:** No fuel required. Human body powered by food (more efficient than animals for converted energy). Maintenance simpler than vehicles.
-   **Stealth:** Bicycles are quiet. Less conspicuous than vehicles in security-conscious scenarios.

### Maintenance Checklist (Monthly)

-   Check tire pressure (low pressure increases rolling resistance and puncture risk)
-   Inspect tires for cuts, embedded objects, wear indicators
-   Oil the chain (cleanliness prevents wear)
-   Test brakes for stopping power (both should be equally effective)
-   Check all bolts/fasteners are tight (vibration loosens components)
-   Spin wheels—verify no rubs or wobbles
-   Inspect spokes for cracks or bent condition
-   Test derailleur shifting (front and rear, smooth through all gears)
-   Inspect frame for cracks or bends
-   Check seatpost, handlebars, stem for movement

:::tip
**A well-maintained bicycle lasts decades.** Salvage parts from abandoned bikes to keep community fleet operational. Bicycles manufactured 20-30 years ago are still serviceable with replacement wear parts. Build community resource library of spare parts.
:::

## Animal Power

Before machines, animals provided the primary motive force for transportation and work. Understanding animal physiology, design of equipment, and safe handling enables efficient use of this renewable power source.

### Ox Yoke Design

An ox yoke is a wooden collar connecting two oxen for draft work. The yoke distributes force evenly across both animals' shoulders.

![⚙ Transportation &amp; Infrastructure diagram 8](../assets/svgs/transportation-8.svg)

### Horse Collar

Unlike oxen, horses pull from the chest using a padded collar. The collar distributes force across the shoulders and chest. A proper horse collar multiplies pulling power by allowing force through the full body, not just the neck.

### Draft Calculations

Understanding an animal's pulling capacity helps plan work and transport. General guidelines:

| Animal | Pulling Power | Speed | Daily Endurance | Load Capacity |
|--------|---------------|-------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Horse | 500-800 lbs | 3-4 mph | 8-10 hours | 1-1.5 tons in cart |
| Ox (single) | 1000-1500 lbs | 2 mph | 8 hours | 1.5-2 tons |
| Donkey/Mule | 300-500 lbs | 3 mph | 8-10 hours | 600 lbs-1 ton |

### Plowing with Animals

A plow cuts soil and turns it for planting. Proper hitch (the connection between animal and plow) is critical. The plow should be pulled from the lowest draft point to avoid tipping. Mark fields into sections; plow in straight lines for efficiency.

### Hauling Loads

When hauling cargo, load the cart carefully:

:::warning
**Overloaded carts injure animals and cause permanent damage.** Distribute weight evenly side-to-side and center on the cart. Imbalanced loads cause stress on animals' joints and spines. Never exceed the animal's rated capacity—an exhausted or injured animal takes months to recover and may never regain full strength.
:::

-   Heavy items toward the center and low
-   Secure cargo to prevent shifting
-   Rest animals every 4-5 hours on long trips
-   Provide water and grazing opportunities

### Animal-Powered Mills

Animals walking in circles drive mills for grinding grain, sawing wood, and pumping water. A gear system converts rotational motion into useful work. One horse or ox can power a mill continuously, replacing multiple human workers.

## Draft Animal Training and Management

Productive use of draft animals requires training, proper equipment, and understanding of each species' capabilities and limitations. Untrained animals are unpredictable and dangerous; proper training creates safe, reliable work partners.

### Training Methodology by Animal Type

**Horses (Most Responsive to Training):**
- **Initial halter training:** Start young (yearlings); teach to lead on halter with gentle but consistent pressure
- **Desensitization:** Expose to noise, equipment, unfamiliar objects gradually. Calm, confident demeanor from trainer is essential
- **Harness introduction:** First fit harness without work; let animal adjust to weight and feel
- **Single-animal work:** Drive single horse pulling light loads (empty cart) before pairing with other animals
- **Team training:** Pair with experienced horse; novice horse learns from behavior of trained partner
- **Work introduction:** Progress from empty cart → light loads → progressively heavier work over weeks

**Oxen (Slower but More Powerful):**
- **Yoke training:** Young oxen (18-24 months) accept yoke more readily than older animals
- **Yoke fit:** Proper fit is critical—poor fit causes sores and resistance. Yoke should rest comfortably on shoulders without pressure on windpipe
- **Voice commands:** Oxen respond very well to consistent voice commands ("gee" for right, "haw" for left, "whoa" for stop)
- **Slow progression:** Oxen are methodical; rushing training causes permanent behavioral problems
- **Teamwork:** Oxen are naturally herd animals; pair with experienced ox for best results

**Mules and Donkeys (Independent But Trainable):**
- **Respect-based training:** Mules are intelligent and require clear, consistent rules; punishment is ineffective
- **Patience:** Mules learn more slowly than horses but retain knowledge longer
- **Positive reinforcement:** Reward good behavior immediately; ignore bad behavior (attention can reinforce misbehavior)
- **Work adaptation:** Mules excel on steep, rough terrain; use for mountain and forest work where horses struggle

### Harness Types and Fitting

**Horse Collar (for Horses):**
- **Design:** Padded horseshoe-shaped collar distributing force across chest and shoulders
- **Proper fit:** Should be snug enough to prevent slipping (2-finger space between horse and collar at top) but not restricting breathing
- **Hames:** Metal or wooden pieces attached to collar; traces (pulling straps) connect hames to cart/plow
- **Adjustment:** Collar should be positioned so draft force pulls parallel to spine (slightly forward), not upward or downward

**Ox Yoke (for Oxen):**
- **Design:** Wooden crosspiece connecting two oxen at the neck; distributes pulling force equally
- **Yoke dimensions:** Typically 4-6 inches wide, 6-8 inches tall, length depends on ox size (allow 1 inch gap between oxen's necks)
- **Bows:** Metal U-shaped pieces under oxen's necks; prevent yoke from rotating
- **Tongue connection:** Trace or chain from yoke center attaches to cart/plow tongue
- **Balance:** Yoke must be perfectly balanced (hang from center point; should balance level) for even work distribution

**Pack Saddle (for Pack Animals):**
- **Sawbuck type:** X-shaped frame distributes load over horse's back; prevents pressure on spine
- **Load distribution:** Hang loads in panniers (bags) on both sides, balanced within 5% of each other
- **Cinches:** Multiple straps secure saddle and prevent shifting; loose cinches cause saddle migration and sores

### Feeding Requirements

**Daily Feed Intake (by animal and weight):**

| Animal | Weight | Daily Feed (Dry Matter) | Grazing Time | Water |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Horse | 1000 lbs | 20-25 lbs hay + 5-10 lbs grain | 12-16 hours | 10-20 gallons |
| Ox | 1500 lbs | 25-30 lbs hay + 5-8 lbs grain | 14-18 hours | 15-25 gallons |
| Mule | 900 lbs | 18-22 lbs hay + 4-6 lbs grain | 12-14 hours | 8-15 gallons |
| Donkey | 500 lbs | 10-12 lbs hay + 2-3 lbs grain | 10-12 hours | 5-10 gallons |

**Forage Quality:**
- Good grass hay: 10-12% protein, 35-40% fiber (adequate for maintenance without work)
- Legume hay (alfalfa, clover): 15-20% protein (better for working animals)
- Pasture grass: Superior nutrition; 6+ hours grazing daily reduces hay requirement

**Grain Supplementation (for Working Animals):**
- Light work: 2-3 lbs grain daily
- Moderate work (6-8 hours/day): 5-8 lbs grain daily
- Heavy work (hauling loads all day): 10-15 lbs grain daily
- Grain types: Oats (traditional, safe), barley, corn (high energy), mix (often best)

**Water Requirements:**
- Continuous access to clean water when possible
- Working animals drink more (increase 50% on hot days)
- Offer water frequently during work; restrict only before extreme exertion (prevent colic)
- In winter, animals will drink less cold water; warming water increases intake

**Seasonal Adjustments:**
- **Summer:** Reduce hay, increase grazing; provide salt (animals lose electrolytes sweating)
- **Winter:** Increase grain and hay; ensure shelter from severe cold
- **Transition periods (spring/fall):** Monitor animal condition; adjust feed as weather changes

### Rest Schedules and Work Limits

**Daily Work Hours by Animal:**

| Animal | Light Work | Moderate Work | Heavy Work |
|---|---|---|---|
| Horse | 10-12 hours | 6-8 hours | 4-5 hours |
| Ox | 8-10 hours | 5-7 hours | 3-4 hours |
| Mule | 10-12 hours | 7-8 hours | 4-6 hours |
| Donkey | 6-8 hours | 4-5 hours | 2-3 hours |

**Rest Protocol:**
- **Daily:** Animals should rest 1-2 hours at midday during work (allow grazing, water, cooling)
- **Weekly:** At least one full rest day per week (no work, full grazing)
- **Seasonal:** Consider reducing work load in extreme heat or cold
- **After travel:** Multi-day journeys require full rest days (ratio of 1 rest day per 3 travel days minimum)

**Signs of Exhaustion (Requires Immediate Rest):**
- Labored breathing that doesn't recover after work stops
- Refusal to eat or drink
- Reluctance to move or swelling of legs
- Excessive sweating or high heart rate (>80 bpm for horse)
- Any lameness or limping

**Conditioning for Endurance:**
- Gradually increase work duration over weeks
- Well-conditioned animals can work longer and harder than poorly-conditioned animals
- Young animals (<3 years) should not do heavy work until mature
- Old animals (>15-20 years) require lighter work loads

### Health Maintenance

**Regular Inspection Checklist:**
- **Hooves:** Clean daily, check for cracks or stones; trim every 6-8 weeks
- **Teeth:** Age animals by teeth; old animals may need soft feed if teeth wear
- **Parasites:** Minimize with grazing management; remove manure from feeding areas
- **Vaccinations:** If available, vaccinate for common diseases (tetanus, equine influenza)
- **Dental care:** Float (rasp) teeth annually to prevent uneven wear

**Common Problems and Prevention:**
- **Colic (intestinal gas/impaction):** Provide water frequently, avoid sudden feed changes, exercise regularly
- **Lameness:** Maintain farrier schedule, avoid excessive work on hard ground
- **Sores/Harness rubs:** Check fit daily, use saddle pads, allow skin to dry between work days
- **Dehydration:** Provide water access; monitor intake in hot weather

## Winter Transport

Snow and ice present unique challenges and opportunities. Winter transport requires specialized equipment but enables movement that is impossible in warmer seasons when roads turn to mud.

### Snowshoe Construction

Snowshoes distribute body weight across snow, preventing sinking. A basic snowshoe consists of a wooden frame with a webbed surface. Ash or hickory provide strong, flexible frames. Rawhide strips (untreated leather) create the webbing.

Teardrop design is most efficient: rounded front, pointed back. Typical dimensions: 10-14 inches wide, 30-36 inches long. Weight: 2-3 lbs per pair. Proper fitting allows efficient walking across deep snow.

### Skis

Longer and narrower than snowshoes, skis enable faster travel on packed snow. Straight-grained wood works best; slightly curved (cambered) skis handle better. Attach leather bindings for the foot, but allow heel lift for efficiency. Traditional skis (no metal edge) work adequately on packed snow and ice.

### Dog Sleds

In regions with sufficient snow, dog sleds provide reliable transport. A team of 6-12 dogs can haul 500-1000 lbs across snow. Sled design: lightweight wooden frame, curved runners, cargo platform. Dogs require training, food (500+ lbs per season), and care. In communities without other winter transport, dog sleds are invaluable.

### Ice Roads

Frozen rivers and lakes provide natural highways for winter. Sleds move freely on ice, enabling transport of heavy loads. Mark safe routes with stakes or branches. Check ice thickness (minimum 4 inches for pedestrians, 8 inches for sleds, 12 inches for horses). Some communities maintain ice roads for the entire winter with seasonal labor.

### Seasonal Planning

Maximize winter transport windows:

:::tip
**Plan winter transport strategically to maximize efficiency.** Build sleds and repair equipment in fall before snow arrives. Stock supplies for winter animal care. Schedule long-distance trips when snow is deep and stable. Use ice roads for heaviest cargo (easier than summer roads). Maintain routes by marking them and removing obstacles. Plan spring transport before mud season makes roads impassable.
:::

</section>

## Infrastructure Planning

Effective transportation networks don't happen by accident. Long-term planning, community cooperation, and strategic placement of infrastructure enable regional trade and resilience.

### Route Selection

Choose routes that minimize:

-   **Distance:** Fewer miles saves time and animal feed
-   **Elevation change:** Steep grades are slow and wear animals
-   **Water crossings:** Each crossing requires infrastructure and increases time
-   **Forests:** Dense trees require clearing and maintenance
-   **Wetlands:** Swamps and bogs need extensive work (corduroy roads)

Survey alternative routes carefully. A route that is 10% longer but 50% easier to maintain might be superior long-term.

### Trade Routes

Historic trade routes connected regions with different resources. A successful trade route:

-   Connects resource-rich regions (forests, mines, agricultural areas)
-   Passes through reasonable terrain
-   Has water access for canal/boat transport where possible
-   Avoids hostile regions or has defensive positions
-   Has adequate stopping points for rest and resupply

### Way Stations

Establish rest stops every 15-25 miles along major routes. A way station should include:

-   **Water source:** Well or reliable spring
-   **Grazing:** Pasture for animals
-   **Shelter:** Basic building for travelers
-   **Storage:** Warehouse for goods in transit
-   **Supplies:** Basic food, tools, repair materials

Way stations become trading hubs and grow into towns over time. Wise communities invest in infrastructure at strategic way stations.

### Bridge and Ferry Network

Plan water crossings strategically. Each bridge or ferry location should handle multiple routes. Coordinate placement to:

-   Minimize total water crossings on route network
-   Place bridges where rivers are narrowest/shallowest
-   Use fords where bridges aren't yet justified
-   Establish ferries for deep rivers
-   Upgrade ferries to bridges when traffic justifies the investment

### Community Connectivity

Transportation networks serve multiple purposes:

-   **Trade:** Exchange goods between communities
-   **Cultural exchange:** Movement of people strengthens bonds
-   **Defense:** Shared roads enable mutual aid
-   **Specialization:** Communities can specialize when trade is reliable
-   **Knowledge:** Travelers spread techniques and ideas

Invest in transportation infrastructure as a strategic priority. The returns—in trade, cultural exchange, and mutual aid—justify the labor investment.

### Maintenance Organizations

Transportation infrastructure requires ongoing maintenance:

-   Form a road committee responsible for annual maintenance
-   Assess communities based on road usage (percentage of trade)
-   Schedule maintenance before seasonal impacts (spring flooding, winter snow)
-   Maintain tool stocks and trained crews
-   Document route conditions and needed repairs
-   Plan expansions based on traffic growth

Communities that organize maintenance cooperatively can maintain hundreds of miles of road with modest per-capita labor contributions.

## Wagons &amp; Carts

Two-wheeled carts and four-wheeled wagons form the backbone of land transport for most civilizations. Understanding the tradeoffs between cart and wagon designs, construction techniques, and load capacity helps communities choose appropriate transport for their needs.

### Cart vs. Wagon: Design Tradeoffs

The choice between a two-wheel cart and a four-wheel wagon depends on cargo, terrain, and available animals.

:::card
:::card
#### Two-Wheel Cart

**Advantages:** Lighter (simpler construction), requires only one animal, more maneuverable in narrow spaces, easier to repair, lower material cost. Ideal for market gardeners, small farms, local transport.
:::

:::card
#### Four-Wheel Wagon

**Advantages:** Greater cargo capacity (2-3x cart), more stable on rough terrain, distributes weight better, easier loading/unloading, better for long distance. Requires two animals but superior for heavy freight and trade routes.
:::
:::

### Basic Cart Construction

A functional cart requires four main components: axle, wheels, bed, and tongue/shafts. For simplicity, focus on solid construction and proper materials.

-   **Axle (hardwood or iron):** Hardwood axles use lignum vitae or ash (naturally hard, durable). Iron axles (if available) never need replacement. Axle must be straight and properly sized—too small, it bends; too large, unnecessary weight.
-   **Wheels (solid or spoked):** Solid disk wheels (5-8 inches thick, carved from large log) work for light carts (50-100 feet travel). Spoked wheels are lighter, more durable for longer use. Wheel diameter affects pulling effort: larger wheels (3-4 feet) roll easier over obstacles.
-   **Bed (plank deck):** Wooden planks (2-3 inches thick) form cargo platform. Extend sides 2-3 feet above axle. For grain/loose cargo, add tailgate and sideboards to contain load.
-   **Tongue/shafts:** Two wooden poles (8-12 feet long) extend forward. Shafts fit around draft animal (horse/ox) and attach to harness. Design allows easy harnessing and quick removal when not in use.

### Wheel Construction in Detail

A well-made wheel is the foundation of cart reliability. Wheel construction involves careful material selection and assembly technique:

-   **Hub (elm preferred):** Center rotating piece (4-8 inches diameter). Elm resists splitting and handles impact loads. Must be straight-grained. Axle hole bored through center, lined with iron or bronze sleeve for smooth rotation.
-   **Spokes (oak, ash, or hickory):** Radiating support pieces (typically 8-12 per wheel). Straight-grained wood critical—any curves cause imbalance. Spokes are tapered (thicker at hub, thinner at rim). Mortise and tenon joints connect spokes to hub with tight friction fit.
-   **Felloes/rim (ash preferred):** Curved wooden segments forming outer ring. Multiple pieces (4-8 felloes) are steam-bent and bolted together. Joints staggered for strength. Felloe must be perfectly round and even.
-   **Iron tire (shrink-fit):** Steel band wraps the rim (1.5-3 inches wide, 0.25 inches thick). Application requires forge: heat tire until red-hot (expands ~0.5%), place on wheel, press on quickly, then quench with water. As tire cools and contracts, it grips the rim with tremendous force. This is permanent—tire won't slip or loosen.

![⚙ Transportation &amp; Infrastructure diagram 9](../assets/svgs/transportation-9.svg)

### Wheel Sizing and Specifications

Wheel diameter affects pulling effort, speed, and rough terrain performance:

:::card
:::card
#### Farm Work Cart (36-42" diameter)

Smaller diameter reduces pulling effort for field work. Adequate for short distances. Higher ground clearance needed to avoid crop damage when working through fields.
:::

:::card
#### General Wagon (48-56" diameter)

Larger diameter rolls over obstacles more easily. Better for rough roads and long distances. Slightly harder to pull initially but maintains momentum better.
:::

:::card
#### Tire Width Impact

Wider iron tire (3-5 inches) spreads weight better, cuts less rut, and damages roads less. Narrow tire (1-1.5 inches) sinks deeper into soft ground. Trade weight vs. benefit.
:::
:::

### Wagon Springs and Suspension

Rough roads damage cargo and animal joints. Leaf springs absorb shock and improve ride quality:

-   **Leaf spring basics:** Multiple curved wood or metal leaves stacked together, attached at center and ends to frame. As wheels hit bump, leaves flex upward, absorbing impact energy. Springs then straighten, pushing wagon back down gently.
-   **Spring placement:** Mount under wagon frame between front and rear axles. Should compress 2-4 inches under full load.
-   **Materials:** Hardwood leaves (oak, hickory) work adequately. Iron or steel leaves (if available) are superior and last longer.
-   **Maintenance:** Springs loosen over time. Check bolts regularly. Replace cracked/broken leaves to restore suspension function.

### Brake Systems

:::danger
**Brake failure on a loaded wagon going downhill is life-threatening.** A 3000-pound wagon at full speed (5 mph) needs 30-50 feet to stop with proper braking. Without brakes, the wagon becomes a runaway death trap. Test brakes before every steep descent. On very steep hills, position team member behind wagon with brake pole for additional mechanical advantage.
:::

Brakes prevent runaway wagons on downhill stretches and allow controlled stops:

-   **Lever-activated shoe brake:** Wooden shoe (brake pad) presses against rear wheel. Operator pulls lever (usually with foot or hand) to engage. Friction between shoe and tire slows wagon.
-   **Placement:** Brake shoe contacts outside of tire (iron band provides friction surface). Should be easily adjustable (slack in dry season, tight in muddy conditions).
-   **Safety:** Always engage brake before descending. On very steep hills, use team member on foot behind wagon with brake pole (additional mechanical advantage).

### Load Capacity Guidelines

Understanding maximum safe loads prevents equipment failure and animal injury:

:::card
:::card
#### Light Cart (500 lbs)

Two-wheel cart, single horse or ox. Market use, local hauling. Suitable for vegetables, small goods, personal transport.
:::

:::card
#### Farm Wagon (2000-3000 lbs)

Four-wheel wagon, pair of oxen or horses. Harvest transport, fodder hauling. Covers 10-15 miles per day with comfort.
:::

:::card
#### Heavy Freight Wagon (5000+ lbs)

Large four-wheel wagon, strong team of oxen. Trade routes, bulk goods. Specialized construction with heavy springs and reinforced frame.
:::
:::

Note: Load capacity assumes good roads. Soft ground, mud, and rough terrain reduce safe loads by 30-50%. Always assess route conditions before loading.

## Road Building

Building and maintaining roads is one of the highest-return infrastructure investments. A good road enables trade, facilitates troop movement, and reduces transport costs by 70-80%. Roads require ongoing maintenance but create wealth across generations.

### Roman Road Method (Multi-Layer Construction)

Roman roads lasted centuries because they employed proper layering and drainage. The method remains superior:

-   **Foundation layer (large stones, 30cm):** Largest stones (6-12 inches) laid to prevent mud and soil from mixing with upper layers. Creates stable base. Stones need not be fitted perfectly; they provide drainage pathways.
-   **Sub-base layer (gravel, 15cm):** Medium gravel (2-5cm pieces) interlocks and provides secondary drainage. Keeps foundation stones from shifting.
-   **Surface layer (fitted stones or gravel, 5-10cm):** Smaller stones (1-3cm) fitted for smooth travel. Can use smaller gravel or crushed stone. Surface stones should slope slightly toward sides for water runoff.
-   **Crown and drainage:** Road center is 2-5% higher than edges. This gentle slope directs water toward side ditches. Ditches must be deep enough to drain quickly (3-5 feet below road surface).

### Corduroy Roads (Last Resort for Swamps)

For wet terrain or swamps where drainage is impossible, lay logs perpendicular to road direction. Log surfaces wear quickly and ride is rough, but enables passage through otherwise impassable ground:

-   **Construction:** Select logs 6-12 inches diameter, 12-18 feet long. Lay parallel, touching or slightly spaced. Peg down with wooden stakes through drilled holes.
-   **Spacing:** 1-2 feet apart allows some water drainage while supporting wheels. Closer spacing is rougher ride but more stable.
-   **Maintenance:** Corduroy roads require frequent repair. Logs rot within 5-10 years in wet conditions. Replace sections as needed.
-   **Limitation:** Use only when alternatives impossible. Rough surface damages cargo and wears animals.

### Gravel Road Maintenance Program

A well-maintained gravel road requires regular seasonal work:

-   **Grading (spring):** Use road grader or drag to redistribute gravel, filling ruts and divots. Compact with roller if available.
-   **Ditching (spring/summer):** Clear side ditches of debris. Deepen ditches if water pools during rain. Ensure water flows toward main channels.
-   **Culverts (as needed):** Install stone or wooden culverts under road where water naturally flows. Prevents pooling and erosion under road surface.
-   **Dust control (summer):** Water road lightly during dry spells (reduces dust and stabilizes surface). Alternative: spread calcium chloride (if available) which attracts moisture from air.
-   **Crown maintenance (continuous):** Monitor center crown; repair areas that flatten or become lower than edges.

### Bridge Basics

Different bridge types suit different situations. Choose based on span distance, water current, and materials available:

:::card
:::card
#### Beam Bridge (log span)

Simplest design: large logs laid parallel. Suitable for spans up to 20 feet. Logs must be selected carefully (straight, large diameter). Fast to build, adequate for local traffic.
:::

:::card
#### Simple Truss (King Post, Queen Post)

Triangulated wooden frame distributes weight efficiently. King post for 20-40 foot spans. Queen post for 40-60+ feet. Requires skilled carpentry but enables longer spans than beam bridges.
:::

:::card
#### Arch Bridge (stone, for permanence)

Curved stone arch transfers weight outward to abutments. Suitable for permanent crossings over significant water. Requires stone quarry and skilled masons. Lasts centuries with minimal maintenance.
:::
:::

### Ford Improvements

For shallow streams, improved fords allow passage without bridges:

-   **Gravel bottom:** Improve traction by laying gravel (2-3 inches deep) in the shallowest section. River current gradually moves gravel; maintain seasonally.
-   **Guide posts:** Mark safe passage with stakes on both banks. Shows travelers the shallowest, safest route.
-   **Depth markers:** Paint or carve depth measurements on guide posts (e.g., "2 feet", "3 feet"). Helps wagons assess water depth safely.
-   **Annual survey:** Check ford condition each spring. Clear debris, add gravel, repair guide posts. Some fords become unusable after heavy floods (may need temporary bridge until water recedes).
-   **Seasonal variation:** Spring flooding may make fords impassable. Communities often build temporary bridges or wait for water to drop before crossing.

## Bicycle Mechanics Deep Dive

Beyond basic repairs, comprehensive bicycle understanding allows complete restoration of salvaged bikes and construction of specialized versions for cargo transport and power generation. Well-maintained bicycles last 30+ years, making them invaluable community assets.

### Complete Bicycle Repair Kit Essentials

A community bicycle workshop should include:

-   **Chain tools:** Chain breaker (removes pins), master link pliers (connects/disconnects modern chains). Allows full chain removal for cleaning.
-   **Tire repair:** Patch kit (rubber cement, multiple patch sizes), tire levers (3-4), hand pump with gauge. Allows roadside repairs and complete tire rebuilds.
-   **Spoke replacement:** Spoke wrench (specific to spoke size), spoke tool, replacement spokes in common sizes. Wheels require occasional spoke replacement.
-   **Bearing maintenance:** Bearing cones and cups (for hub overhaul), grease (lithium or waterproof), bearing race puller if available. Bearings require periodic repacking.
-   **Brake service:** Brake cable, brake pads (rim and disc types), barrel adjusters. Enables complete brake system rebuild.
-   **Derailleur adjustment:** Limit screw adjustment tools, cable tension barrel adjusters, replacement cables. Allows fine-tuning of shifting performance.

### Chain Repair Techniques

Chain maintenance is critical for drivetrain longevity:

-   **Chain breaking:** Use chain tool to push out a pin (or break on master link). Allows removal of entire chain for thorough cleaning. Soak in degreaser, scrub with brush, rinse until water runs clear. Dry thoroughly.
-   **Link repair:** Broken or damaged link (cracked pin or bent link plate) can be removed. Disconnect one side, remove bad link, reconnect with remaining chain shortened by one link. Use chain tool to reinstall pin securely.
-   **Master link:** Modern chains use master link (removable connecting link). Pliers or hands can remove/install. Simpler than pushing out pins. Spare master links should be carried.
-   **Lubrication:** After repair, apply chain-specific lube (not grease). Spin pedals forward while lubing, coating inner and outer plates. Wipe excess—too much attracts dirt.

### Bearing Overhaul (Hub, Bottom Bracket, Headset)

Smooth bearings enable efficient pedaling. Three critical bearing sets require periodic maintenance:

-   **Hub bearings (wheel rotation):** Cup and cone design (traditional) or sealed cartridge (modern). Cone tightens/loosens with adjustable cup; ball bearings roll between. Overhaul: remove wheel, loosen cone, remove bearings, clean in degreaser, repack with fresh grease, reassemble. No play when tightened; spins freely.
-   **Bottom bracket (crank rotation):** Sealed or open design. Sealed cartridge lasts longer but must be replaced when worn. Open design allows regreasing. Access requires removing crank arms (usually with puller tool).
-   **Headset (steering):** Two bearing sets (top and bottom of fork steering tube). Similar cup/cone design. Overhaul requires removing fork and handlebars. Critical for handling—loose headset causes wobbling; overtight causes stiff turning.
-   **Grease specification:** Use waterproof lithium grease. General-purpose grease washes out in wet conditions. Adequate grease coverage is critical—too little causes noise and wear; too much attracts dirt.

### Brake Adjustment and Maintenance

Brakes are safety-critical and require precise adjustment:

-   **Rim brakes (caliper):** Pads press against wheel rim. Adjustment: loosen caliper mounting bolts, center brake arms, tighten bolts. Cable tension (barrel adjuster) controls pad distance from rim (target: 2mm gap). Replace pads when worn to minimal thickness (wear indicator lines visible on pads).
-   **Disc brakes (modern bikes):** Hydraulic or mechanical. Hydraulic requires brake fluid and sealed system (difficult to service without tools). Mechanical disc brakes use cable, easier to adjust. Rotor alignment critical—brake should not rub when spinning freely.
-   **Coaster brakes (pedal-backward to stop):** Internal brake in rear hub. Minimal adjustment needed—rare failure if properly lubricated. Replace if coasting resistance develops.
-   **Cable service:** Brake cables fray and may bind. Replacement: remove old cable, thread new cable through frame stops and housing, attach to brake lever and brake mechanism. Tension cable so brakes engage smoothly.

### Drivetrain: Freewheel vs. Freehub, Derailleur Adjustment

Understanding drivetrain components helps with diagnosis and repair:

-   **Freewheel (older bikes):** Sprockets and freewheel mechanism combined in one cassette. Thread onto hub. Removal requires freewheel puller tool. Coasting happens when chain slows faster than wheel.
-   **Freehub (modern bikes):** Freewheel mechanism built into hub; sprockets slide onto freehub body. Easier to remove individual sprockets. Coasting cleaner and more efficient.
-   **Derailleur limit screws:** Two limit screws control maximum and minimum gear position. Adjust so chain doesn't fall off sprockets. Low limit (smallest sprocket) and high limit (largest sprocket) prevent damage.
-   **Cable tension:** Derailleur cable pulls derailleur sideways, moving chain between sprockets. Barrel adjuster fine-tunes cable tension. Loose cable: slow upshift to larger sprockets. Tight cable: chain wants to drop off.
-   **B-screw adjustment:** Controls clearance between derailleur cage and sprockets. Proper clearance (2-3mm) prevents rubbing while allowing close positioning for accurate shifts.

### Cargo Bikes and Trailers

Converting bikes for heavy hauling extends their utility dramatically:

-   **Cargo rack conversion:** Mount heavy-duty rack (rated 50-100 lbs) to seat tube and rear stays. Pannier bags hang on both sides for balanced loading. Distribute weight center-low for better handling.
-   **Front racks (low-rider style):** Load capacity 20-30 lbs. Mount low on fork (not on handlebars) to improve steering. Front loading reduces rear wheel lift when pedaling hard.
-   **BOB trailer (single-wheel towing):** Tows from seatpost. Capacity 50-100 lbs. Superior handling compared to loaded bike—cargo weight doesn't affect steering or balance as much. Easier to park/maneuver.
-   **Flatbed trailer construction:** Build simple wooden platform on two wheels. Pivot connection at bike seat tube. Larger capacity (100+ lbs) than commercial trailers. Requires stronger frame and proper bearing.
-   **Child trailer:** Small enclosed platform carries child. Safety features: protective cage, secure harness, reflectors/lights. Tows behind seat tube. Allows parent to transport child while cycling.

### Bicycle-Powered Machines

Bicycles can power stationary machines, multiplying utility:

-   **Grain mill:** Connect rear wheel to mill via belt or gears. Pedaling spins mill stones. Single person can grind grain into flour. Requires 30-45 minute effort per pound of grain depending on fineness.
-   **Water pump:** Pedaling drives pump mechanism. Supplies water from well to elevated tank. One cyclist can pump 5-20 gallons per minute depending on depth and pump design.
-   **Electrical generator:** Dynamic hub generator (dynamo) produces 3-6 watts while cycling. Sufficient for lights and small electronics. Bottle dynamos (friction-driven) simpler but less efficient. Belt-drive alternator (12V automotive alternator on rear wheel) produces more power but adds friction.
-   **Power output:** Average cyclist generates 75-100 watts sustained. Peak output: 300-500 watts (short bursts). Design machines to use average power; peak power burns out unsustainable rapidly.

## Railroad Basics

Rails dramatically reduce friction between wheels and track, allowing massive cargo transport with minimal animal effort. A single horse pulls 1 ton on roads but 8-10 tons on rails. This efficiency gain justifies the labor and material cost of track construction for high-traffic routes.

### Track Gauge (Width Standards)

Track gauge (distance between inner rail edges) affects compatibility and construction cost:

:::card
:::card
#### Standard Gauge (4ft 8.5in)

International standard. Compatible with most equipment. Suitable for main routes and inter-regional trade. Requires more resources to build initially.
:::

:::card
#### Narrow Gauge (2-3 ft)

Easier to build (curves tighter, less earthwork). Suitable for regional lines and mining operations. Lower weight capacity per car. Common gauge: 2-3 feet for steep terrain.
:::
:::

### Rail Materials and Construction

Rail development shows material evolution:

-   **Wooden rail with iron strap (earliest, cost-effective):** Wooden beam (6x6 inches, hardwood) laid on timber sleepers. Iron strap (3-4 inches wide, 0.5 inches thick) nailed to wooden rail top. Reduces friction and prolongs wood life. Requires frequent maintenance; splinters and strap separation are common.
-   **Iron T-rail (standard):** T-shaped cross-section: top flange (wheel running surface), vertical stem (holds wheel), base (spreads load). Iron T-rail lasts 30-50 years. Steel rail lasts longer. Bolted to wooden or concrete sleepers (ties).
-   **Sleeper spacing:** 20-24 inch centers typical. Closer spacing (18 inches) improves ride; wider spacing (30 inches) reduces cost. Wood sleepers last 10-20 years; replace as they decay.

### Handcar and Pump Trolley

Manual rail transport precedes locomotives and suits off-grid scenarios:

-   **Handcar (pump trolley):** Four-wheeled car with up-and-down pump lever. Two people pump levers in alternating rhythm. Moves at 5-8 mph on level track. Capacity: 500-1000 lbs cargo plus operators.
-   **Construction:** Wooden frame on iron axles, flanged wheels grip rail, pump mechanism connected to axles via ratchet gears. Simple mechanics, easy to maintain.
-   **Use cases:** Maintenance inspection, light cargo transport, personnel movement on established track. Easier than walking heavy supplies.

### Horse-Drawn Tramway (Most Practical Pre-Industrial Option)

Animals pulling carts on rails is the simplest way to capture rail efficiency:

:::tip
**Horse-drawn tramways are the sweet spot for animal-powered rail transport.** One horse pulls 1 ton on road; same horse pulls 8-10 tons on rail. This multiplier justifies track construction for high-traffic routes. Start with short routes between high-traffic locations (mine to port, farm to market). Expand as community resources permit.
:::

-   **Design:** Horses pull regular carts but wheels run in grooved rail (or on flat rail). Eliminates wagon wheel friction on road. Massive reduction in pulling effort.
-   **Track construction:** Simpler than standard railroad—use iron T-rail or strap rail, wooden sleepers. Can be built gradually by labor-intensive work without heavy machinery.
-   **Scalability:** Expand as community resources permit. Existing tracks continue to serve even if newer tracks bypass them.

### Advantages of Rails

-   Friction reduction of 80-90% compared to roads
-   Predictable routes enable rapid transit
-   Capacity scales with car design (heavier cars carry more)
-   Animal-drawn or human-powered (no fuel required)
-   Infrastructure amortizes over decades/centuries
-   Once built, extremely low per-ton-mile cost

## Waterway Transport

Natural and artificial waterways provide the lowest-friction transport available. A single barge carrying bulk goods moves cheaper and faster via water than any land transport. Communities with access to navigable waterways have decisive economic advantages.

### River Barge and Raft Transport

Large-scale bulk goods movement uses water routes where possible:

-   **River barge:** Flat-bottomed boat designed to float in shallow rivers (3-4 foot draft). Can carry 30-100 tons depending on size. Towed by team of horses on towpath, or drifts downstream in current (slow upstream return). See *shipbuilding-boats.html* for construction details.
-   **Log raft:** Bundle logs together (50-200 logs per raft). Lash securely with rope. Float downstream with minimal effort (current does work). Poling (pushing with long pole against bottom) steers and controls speed. Raft can be broken apart at destination, logs sold as cargo.
-   **Load examples:** Grain (weight 50+ tons), ore (weight 100+ tons), timber (volume measured in logs), coal (bulk commodity). Water transport reduces cost 70-80% vs. land hauling.

### Canal Construction Basics

Artificial canals extend transport networks into regions without natural rivers:

-   **Lock principle:** Two gates (upstream and downstream) enclose a chamber. Water level inside lock raised or lowered to match upstream/downstream level, allowing boat passage across elevation changes. Single lock serves both directions.
-   **Lock cycle time:** 15-30 minutes per lock. Water enters/exits through sluice gates (controllable openings). Operator controls gate positions to raise/lower water level smoothly.
-   **Lock dimensions:** Must accommodate barges intended to use canal. Typical cargo barge: 60x15 feet, 3-4 foot draft. Lock must be 10 feet longer/wider than largest barge.
-   **Canal dimensions for barge traffic:** 4-6 foot depth, 30-50 foot bottom width, 1:2 side slopes (1 foot vertical per 2 feet horizontal, prevents collapse). Towpath: 12-15 feet wide alongside canal.

### Portage

Carrying boats and cargo around obstacles (waterfalls, rapids, portage trails) connects waterway segments:

-   **Wooden rollers:** Large logs placed perpendicular under boat. Boat slides on rollers with teams pulling. Reduces friction enormously. Portable—can be repositioned for next segment.
-   **Skids:** Wooden paths (logs placed flat). Boat slides on skids with teams pulling. Less effective than rollers but simpler.
-   **Portage path:** Clear and maintain dedicated path around obstacles. Mark regularly traveled routes to reduce labor. Some historic portages became roads and eventually settlements.
-   **Labor:** Large boats require 20-50 people and day of effort to portage. Small canoes: 2-4 people per canoe. Portage costs increase with distance—limits practical portage to 1-2 miles max.

## Trail/Path Construction &amp; Bridge Load Rating

Developing a network of reliable pathways and bridge infrastructure is essential for moving goods and people efficiently across diverse terrain. Well-engineered trails reduce travel time by 40-60% compared to unmarked cross-country travel and can support heavy pack animal and cart traffic. Bridge load capacity must match intended use and be regularly maintained to prevent catastrophic failures.

### Foot Traffic Trail Design

Pedestrian paths form the foundation of community mobility and should be optimized for both ease of travel and drainage:

-   **Trail width:** Minimum 0.75 meters (2.5 feet) for single-file foot traffic. 1.2-1.5 meters (4-5 feet) for two-way pedestrian use without congestion. Wider paths reduce meeting-place friction and enable emergency stretcher passage.
-   **Surface grading:** Maintain 2-4% cross-slope for drainage (perpendicular to path direction). Avoid flat surfaces that accumulate water and create mud. Crowned center (slightly higher in middle) sheds water to both sides. Maximum slope along path: 10-12% for comfortable foot traffic; anything steeper becomes difficult for children and elderly.
-   **Drainage management:** Install shallow drainage channels (10-15 cm deep) on downslope side of trails on hillsides. Space water bars (small berms that divert water off-trail) every 10-15 meters on slopes to prevent erosion gullies. Direct all drainage to existing waterways or designated fields; never let trail water erode adjacent land.
-   **Surface materials:** Compacted earth/clay: cheapest, maintains well in dry climates, becomes impassable when wet. Gravel (2-3 cm layer): improved drainage, comfortable walking, requires annual maintenance to refill wear zones. Cobblestone: excellent drainage, long-lasting (50+ years), high initial labor cost (15-20 person-days per 100 meters). Wood chips/bark: comfortable, moderately durable (3-5 year lifespan), good for wet climates.
-   **Switchbacks on slopes:** For slopes over 8%, use zigzag switchbacks instead of straight climbing paths. Reduces effective grade and prevents erosion. Typical switchback: 50-100 meter legs at 6-7% grade, with 180-degree turns. Mark outer edges with rocks or logs to prevent shortcutting, which creates erosion.

### Pack Animal Trail Requirements

Trails designed for horses, mules, or llamas require wider paths with specific engineering to handle concentrated animal weight and hooves:

-   **Minimum width:** 1.5 meters (5 feet) to prevent pack loads scraping on vegetation. 2 meters (6.5 feet) preferred for loaded animals and handlers walking beside. Narrower trails force pack animals into dangerous single-file arrangements on steep terrain.
-   **Load capacity per animal:** Horses: 50-90 kg (110-200 lbs) sustained over long distance. Mules: 60-100 kg (130-220 lbs) are more durable than horses. Llamas: 25-40 kg (55-90 lbs) but excellent on steep terrain. Donkeys: 25-50 kg (55-110 lbs). Distribute loads evenly in panniers or packframes; asymmetrical loads damage animals' spines.
-   **Grade limitations:** Maximum sustainable grade for loaded pack animals: 12-15%. Grades over 15% cause fatigue and increase injury risk. Use switchbacks liberally on steep slopes; a 20% slope should become 10% via switchbacks.
-   **Hoof wear and surface:** Paved or rocky trails wear hoof material quickly; soft paths (earth, gravel) distribute load better. Extremely rocky paths cause foot soreness; clear loose rocks (>5 cm) from trail beds. Muddy paths cause slips; maintain drainage meticulously on animal trails.
-   **Rest zones:** Every 3-5 km (2-3 miles) of travel, provide a flat spot 5+ meters wide where animals can stand safely and rest. Pack animals work for 4-6 hours maximum before needing 2-hour rest. Mark rest zones clearly with rocks or cleared spaces.

### Cart &amp; Wagon Road Construction

Wheeled vehicle infrastructure requires different engineering than foot or pack animal trails, focusing on surface bearing capacity and drainage:

-   **Road width:** Single cart: 2.5-3 meters (8-10 feet). Two-way cart traffic: 4-5 meters (13-16 feet). Wider roads reduce congestion and allow passing without one vehicle leaving the surface.
-   **Bearing surface preparation:** Remove topsoil (0-30 cm) along entire path. Compact subgrade with animals trampling or water saturation method. Layer construction (bottom to top): 1) Subgrade (compacted earth), 2) 15 cm large stones (5-10 cm diameter) for drainage, 3) 10 cm gravel/crushed rock for binding, 4) 5 cm fine gravel or paving surface. Total: 30-40 cm of prepared layers.
-   **Crown and slope:** Maintain 3-5% crown (center higher) for water shedding. This prevents puddles and mud accumulation. Cross-slope must be consistent across entire width.
-   **Longitudinal grade:** Maximum 6-8% sustained grade for practical cart and wagon use (steeper requires animals to rest frequently). For steeper terrain, use switchbacks. 15% grade becomes impractical for loaded vehicles—switchbacks become necessary.
-   **Drainage infrastructure:** Install ditches 30-50 cm deep on both sides of road to remove runoff. In wet climates, ditches must be 1+ meter away from road edge to prevent undercutting. Ford streams at designated shallow crossings (30-60 cm deep) rather than attempting to bridge every stream.
-   **Surface maintenance:** After rain, roads with poor drainage become impassable for 2-3 days. Regular maintenance (raking, re-crowning, filling ruts) every 2-4 weeks during wet season prolongs road life by 200%+. Major resurfacing required every 5-10 years depending on traffic volume and climate.

### Bridge Types and Load Ratings

Bridges enable road networks to cross water barriers. Bridge failure is catastrophic; construction and design must prioritize safety margins:

-   **Simple log bridge (foot traffic):** Two parallel logs (15-20 cm diameter) lashed together with cross-planks. Load capacity: 100 kg individual foot traffic maximum. Failure mode: side rotation if logs not lashed properly. Lifespan: 5-8 years before rot compromises logs. Cost: 1-2 person-days. Use for streams up to 3 meters wide.
-   **Beam bridge (pack animals and light carts):** Single or doubled beams (20-30 cm diameter logs) spanning water, supported on abutments on each bank. Cross-beams distribute load from loads on top. Design load capacity: 500-1000 kg total (single loaded horse or light cart). Calculates as: beam diameter in cm × span in meters = approximate safe load in kg (rough rule). Proper abutments prevent wash-out: bury supports 1+ meter into bank, use stone foundation. Lifespan: 10-20 years with maintenance. Cost: 3-5 person-days.
-   **Arch bridge (heavy traffic):** Stone or timber arch transfers load outward and downward to abutments. Much stronger than beam bridges; can span longer distances. Load capacity: 2000-5000 kg or more depending on materials. Construction complexity: high. Requires centering (temporary support) during construction, skilled stonework if masonry. Lifespan: 50+ years (stone lasts centuries). Cost: 20-50 person-days.
-   **Suspension bridge (deep gorges):** Cable-supported design for very wide spans (20+ meters). Load capacity: 500-2000 kg depending on cable. Highly visible but lower capacity than arch bridges of similar size. Used to cross deep gorges where arch/beam would be impractical. Lifespan: 20-40 years (cables require replacement). Cost: 30-100 person-days for proper construction.
-   **Load rating certification:** Paint load capacity clearly on bridge entrance: "Max 1000 kg" for horse traffic, "Foot traffic only" for pedestrian-only bridges. Post guards to enforce limits. Inspect bridges quarterly and after heavy flooding. Replace any cracked or rotting timbers immediately. Test new bridges before use with dummy loads 20% above rated capacity.
-   **Design calculations:** For a simple beam bridge: load per animal (horse + rider: 600 kg) = maximum safe load. Distribute across two logs to halve stress per log. For width spans: 5 meters maximum for unbraced log beam (diameter 20+ cm); longer spans require intermediate supports. Always over-design rather than under-design; failure kills people and livestock.

### Trail and Bridge Maintenance Schedules

Preventive maintenance extends infrastructure life and prevents failures:

-   **Weekly (during high-use seasons):** Walk critical bridges and paths. Check for loose planks, broken railings, new erosion gullies. Remove debris (fallen branches, stones) from surfaces.
-   **Monthly:** Re-crown roads to maintain drainage. Refill ruts and low spots. Clear water bars of accumulated soil. Check bridge posts for rot (use knife to test—resistance = healthy wood).
-   **Seasonal (after heavy rain/winter):** Inspect for washouts and erosion. Rebuild damaged water bars. Replace failed surface gravel. Check abutments for undercutting or settlement.
-   **Annual:** Major trail resurfacing as needed. Full bridge inspection with load testing (if load-bearing). Replacement of significantly rotted logs (>5 cm deep rot). Re-lashing or re-nailing of bridge components.
-   **Every 5-10 years:** Full trail re-grading and surface renewal. Replacement of severely compromised bridge beams. Abutment stabilization or rebuilding. Cost: significant but prevents catastrophic failures.

Well-maintained trails and bridges become community assets that reduce travel time, enable commerce, and increase safety. A 50-kilometer road network supporting pack animal and cart traffic can be maintained by a community of 100-200 people with 2-3 person-days per month of collective labor.
