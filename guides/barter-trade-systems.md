**Scope:** This guide covers barter and informal trade in austere conditions. It assumes currency has collapsed, failed, or is inaccessible. The systems described work for communities of 20–10,000+ people, though mechanisms scale differently. This is not financial advice; each community must adapt based on available goods, local values, and cultural norms.

<section id="establishing-value">

## 1. Establish Shared Values to Enable Fair Trade

![Trade goods exchange and barter network flow diagram](../assets/svgs/barter-trade-systems.svg)

**Establish community agreement on value immediately.** When currency fails, value becomes negotiable only if you fail to act. Humans naturally develop shared understandings of worth based on utility, scarcity, and cultural importance—but only if leaders guide this process intentionally.

### Take Action: Why Establishing Value Prevents Crisis

Do this now to prevent conflict later:
- [ ] Establish clear, agreed-upon values → Reduce conflict over "fair" trades
- [ ] Create transparent price lists → Enable faster transactions, less haggling
- [ ] Define baseline labor rates → Allow planning and cost predictability
- [ ] Post standards publicly → Prevent exploitation of those unfamiliar with local markets
- [ ] Review and adjust quarterly → Maintain community trust in fair trade

Without action, communities will:
- Spend hours negotiating every single trade
- Become vulnerable to exploitation by experienced traders
- Experience repeated disputes and resentment escalation
- Risk violence when trades are perceived as unfair
- Lose confidence in their economic system entirely

### Core Principle: Value Derives from Scarcity and Utility

**Utility** = how much people need the good
**Scarcity** = how hard it is to obtain

**High utility + high scarcity = very valuable** (fresh meat when starving, water in drought, medical supplies)
**High utility + low scarcity = moderately valuable** (salt, grain, basic tools)
**Low utility + high scarcity = low-moderate value** (decorative items, rare books)
**Low utility + low scarcity = minimal value** (abundant wood in forest region, straw)

### Use Labor as Your Baseline for All Value

**Set labor as your first unit of measurement.** This works because everyone understands the value of their own work time.

**Choose baseline time-units (pick one):**
- [ ] 1 day of skilled labor (carpentry, weaving, herbal medicine) = 1 unit
- [ ] 1 day of unskilled labor (field work, hauling, digging) = 1 unit
- [ ] 1 hour of skilled labor = 1 unit
- [ ] 1 day of unskilled labor = 1 unit

**Then establish skill multipliers** (compare all other labor to your baseline):
- [ ] Skilled labor: 2–5x unskilled baseline (depends on skill scarcity)
- [ ] Teaching/apprenticeship: 10–50x unskilled baseline (reflects knowledge transfer importance)
- [ ] Healthcare labor: 5–10x unskilled baseline (depends on need and provider scarcity)

**Example:** If 1 day of unskilled labor = 1 unit, then:
- 1 day of carpentry = 5 units (skilled multiplier 5x)
- 1 hour of herbal medicine instruction = 1 unit (reflects knowledge value)

**Then value all goods relative to your baseline labor:**
- 1 bushel of grain = 2 units (worth 2 days of unskilled labor)
- 1 tool (axe) = 10 units (worth 10 days of unskilled labor)

### Create Community Price Lists (Step-by-Step)

<div class="trade-box">

**Who:** Bring together 3–5 trusted traders/leaders plus community scribe.

**When:** Establish immediately after collapse. Reassess quarterly when scarcity changes.

**Process:**
1. Choose baseline: "1 day of unskilled labor = 1 unit" (or your chosen baseline)
2. For each essential good, decide: "How many units of labor is this worth?"
3. Write down each value in permanent record (ledger, posted board, or both)
4. Publicly post the final list at market, meeting place, or both
5. Allow challenges and adjustment period (3-day dispute window before enforcement)
6. Update list every 3 months or after major scarcity event

**Example baseline values (assuming 1 day unskilled labor = 1 "labor unit"):**

| Good | Quantity | Value | Notes |
|------|----------|-------|-------|
| Salt | 1 lb | 0.5 labor units | Preserved 6+ months; essential for food preservation |
| Grain (wheat) | 1 bushel | 2 labor units | Provides 3 meals for 1 person; stable storage |
| Fresh meat | 1 lb | 1.5 labor units | Highly desirable; spoils in 3 days; seasonal |
| Preserved meat | 1 lb | 3 labor units | Lasts 6+ months; labor-intensive to produce |
| Tool (axe) | 1 | 10 labor units | Lasts years; enables work; hard to replace |
| Cloth (linen) | 1 yard | 2 labor units | Labor-intensive to produce; essential for clothing |
| Honey | 1 lb | 1 labor unit | Long-term storage; food and medicine; bees are work |
| Oil (cooking) | 1 liter | 2 labor units | Preserves food; light source; hard to produce |
| Candle | 1 lb | 1 labor unit | Light; burnable storage of plant energy |
| Nails (iron) | 1 lb | 2 labor units | Reusable; durable; require smithing skill |
| Brick | 10 | 3 labor units | Durable; enables building; labor-intensive |
| Copper sheet | 1 | 4 labor units | Rare; conducts heat; corrosion-resistant |
| Herbal medicine | 1 dose | 1 labor unit | Prevents illness; extends life; rare knowledge |
| Fresh vegetable | 1 lb | 0.5 labor units | Seasonal; spoils quickly; nutritious |
| Preserved vegetable | 1 lb | 1.5 labor units | Lasts months; labor-intensive to preserve |
| Wood (building) | 1 board-foot | 0.25 labor units | Abundant in forest; heavy to transport |
| Rope | 1 lb | 2 labor units | Essential for binding, hauling; labor-intensive to produce |
| Leather | 1 hide | 5 labor units | Durable; multi-use; complex preservation process |
| Paper (writing) | 1 sheet | 0.25 labor units | Requires skill; limited utility; valuable for records |
| Books (existing) | 1 | 10+ labor units | Rare; knowledge-dense; irreplaceable if lost |

**Adjustments as conditions change:**
- **Seasonal goods:** Fresh fruit worth MORE in winter, LESS during harvest
- **Emergency shortages:** Salt worth MORE during disease outbreak; medicine worth MUCH MORE
- **New production:** Once beekeeping established, honey drops in value
- **Surplus:** After grain harvest, grain drops 40–50% in value; restabilizes by spring

</div>

### Value vs. Desire

Important distinction: Some goods have high cultural/emotional value but low practical value.

**Example:** A pre-collapse photograph, book, or clothing item may have enormous emotional value to someone but minimal survival utility. A community that honors this (trading fairly for sentimental goods) builds social cohesion. But in starvation, emotional value cannot feed families.

**Strategy:** Acknowledge sentimental value but price it separately from utility value. "This photo is priceless to you; I'll trade it fairly because it matters."

</section>

<section id="trade-goods">

## 2. Identifying and Valuing Trade Goods

Not every good is equally valuable in trade. The best trade goods are those that:
- Last without spoiling (durable or preserved)
- Are portable (easy to carry long distances)
- Are in consistent demand
- Can be divided into smaller units (fractional trading)
- Cannot be easily produced locally (scarcity)

### Essential Trade Goods (Universal, High Demand)

<div class="trade-box">

**Salt**
- Why: Food preservation is survival; salt is irreplaceable for this
- Scarcity: Depends on access to salt mines or ocean. Extremely scarce if neither available.
- Portability: High (compact, dense)
- Storage: Indefinite (does not spoil)
- Divisibility: Excellent (can be measured in precise amounts)
- Trade networks: Salt historically drives long-distance trade

**Preserved/Dried Goods (grain, vegetables, meat, fruit)**
- Why: Provide calories and nutrition for months
- Scarcity: Moderate (seasonal; all communities can produce but good preservation is skilled work)
- Portability: Moderate (heavy but compact in dried form)
- Storage: 6–24 months depending on method
- Divisibility: Good (can sell individual units)
- Trade networks: Staple of all grain-based economies

**Tools and Metal Goods**
- Why: Enable all other work; cannot be easily made without smithing
- Scarcity: High (require specialized skill)
- Portability: Low (heavy)
- Storage: Indefinite (rust-resistant if treated)
- Divisibility: Poor (difficult to trade "half an axe")
- Trade networks: Major trade goods in pre-industrial economies; worth long-distance transport

**Cloth, Rope, Leather**
- Why: Essential for clothing, binding, containers
- Scarcity: Moderate to high (labor-intensive; requires animal husbandry or plant fiber)
- Portability: High (light, compressible)
- Storage: Long-term (rot-resistant if dry)
- Divisibility: Excellent (yard by yard, piece by piece)
- Trade networks: High-value in all pre-industrial trade

**Medicines, Herbal Preparations**
- Why: Health is essential; skilled knowledge is scarce
- Scarcity: High (require specialized knowledge and rare ingredients)
- Portability: High (concentrated, light)
- Storage: Variable (6 months to several years depending on preservation)
- Divisibility: Excellent (can be portioned into doses)
- Trade networks: Herbal medicines are ancient trade goods; valued across all cultures

**Oils, Fats, Honey**
- Why: Calorie-dense, light source, food preservation, medicine
- Scarcity: Moderate (production requires animals/bees or plant pressing)
- Portability: Moderate (liquid; needs container)
- Storage: 1–3 years depending on type
- Divisibility: Excellent (can be measured out precisely)
- Trade networks: Fat/oil trade is ancient and valuable

**Books, Maps, Knowledge**
- Why: Information is valuable; literacy is scarce
- Scarcity: Very high (limited pre-collapse books; new ones require skill)
- Portability: Moderate (depends on size)
- Storage: Long-term if protected from moisture
- Divisibility: Poor (books are indivisible; may trade for specific sections)
- Trade networks: Knowledge goods trade at premium in austere conditions

</div>

### Local vs. Exotic Goods

<div class="trade-box">

**Local goods** (produced in or near your community):
- Examples: Local grain, local timber, goods made by local artisans
- Trade value: Low to moderate (everyone has access to same goods)
- Strategic value: May be important for evening out seasonal surpluses

**Exotic goods** (from distant regions):
- Examples: Salt from coast to inland, tropical spices, metals from mining regions
- Trade value: Very high (scarcity drives value)
- Strategic value: Enables specialization (one region produces salt, another grain; both trade for the other)

**Strategy for communities:** Develop trade networks with DIFFERENT ecological zones to ensure mutual benefit. Coastal communities trade salt to inland communities; highland communities trade honey and herbs to lowland communities. This creates interdependence and reduces conflict.

</div>

</section>

<section id="trade-post-setup">

## 3. Trade Post Setup and Operation

A trade post is a designated location where multiple traders meet regularly to exchange goods. It's simpler than a market (no buildings required) but more organized than ad-hoc barter.

### Location and Infrastructure

**Location selection:**
- Neutral ground (not favoring any faction)
- Accessible (central to major population centers if serving multiple communities)
- Safe (defendable if needed; watch posts if trade routes are dangerous)
- Has water and space for animals
- Regular (same location, same day/time each week or month)

**Infrastructure (minimal):**
- Open area or large structure (barn, field) for goods display
- Covered space for perishables and valuable items (tarp, roof)
- Scales or measuring tools (for weight, volume)
- Table or bench for negotiation and recording
- Storage for goods held in trust (between trades or payment delays)

**Records area:**
- Ledger or tablet for recording trades (see accounting guide)
- Witness for signature/mark (ensures accountability)
- Public posting of agreed values

### Trade Post Schedule and Rules

<div class="trade-box">

**Schedule (example):**
- Weekly: Every 5th day, 10am–4pm
- Monthly: Major trade fair on first day of month
- Emergency: Can be called by council in event of shortage or crisis

**Posting rules (prevent monopoly and fraud):**
1. All goods must be declared honestly (no hiding spoiled items, mixing good with bad)
2. All traders pay small fee (e.g., 2% of trade value) to community (funds maintenance, records)
3. No violence or threats (enforced by security)
4. Disputes resolved by neutral party (see conflict de-escalation guide)
5. Prices must align with posted community values (to prevent exploitation)

**Access:**
- Open to all community members and visitors
- Some communities restrict large quantity purchases by outsiders (protects food security)
- Traveling traders welcome if they follow rules

**Security:**
- Trade post manager (neutral community person)
- 2–4 security people (prevent theft, enforce rules)
- Separate area for violent disputes resolution (do not allow fighting in market)

</div>

### Trade Post Manager Responsibilities

- Maintain ledger of trades
- Enforce community trade values (prevent exploitation)
- Collect fees and manage funds
- Resolve disputes between traders
- Schedule space for different goods categories
- Ensure all goods are honestly represented
- Report significant shortages to community leader (may indicate crisis)
- Facilitate payment when goods must be held in trust

### Types of Goods Storage

**At trade post (perishable goods held in trust):**
- Cold room for meat, fish, dairy
- Dry storage for grain, vegetables
- Container for oil, honey, other liquids
- Duration: 3–7 days maximum (after that, goods spoil or storage becomes expensive)

**Community granary (long-term storage):**
- Managed by community for emergency food
- Filled during harvest season
- Released during shortages or winter
- Separate from trade post (see agriculture guide)

</section>

<section id="negotiation">

## 4. Negotiation Basics and Fair Exchange

Not all trades follow posted prices exactly. Negotiation is normal and healthy. However, unbalanced negotiation power leads to exploitation.

### Fair Negotiation Framework

<div class="negotiation-step">

**Step 1: Agree on the Baseline Value**
- "This goat is worth 3 labor units, based on our posted values. Do you agree?"
- If disagreement: "Let's discuss why the values differ."
- Adjustment: "This goat is lean; normally worth 3, but I'd value this one at 2.5 labor units."

**Step 2: Identify What Each Person Has to Offer**
- Trader A: "I have salt. Salt is worth 0.5 labor units per pound. I have 20 pounds. Total: 10 labor units."
- Trader B: "I need 3 goats, worth 9 labor units. I have salt, cloth, and can do labor. What would you accept?"

**Step 3: Find the Gap**
- Trader A needs: 9 labor units
- Trader B has offered: Salt (10 labor units) + cloth (3 labor units) = 13 labor units
- Gap: B is offering 4 extra labor units
- Solution: B can reduce quantity (trade 20 lb salt + 1 yard cloth, not 3), or A can adjust the price, or B can take goods back as "payment" for transport labor

**Step 4: Negotiate the Difference**
- "I can give you 20 pounds of salt (10 labor units) for your 3 goats (9 labor units). You're getting the better deal; I need goat meat for winter."
- "Fair point. What if I add 1 hour of my carpentry work? That's worth 1/8 of a day labor unit. That brings it to 9.125, closer to even."
- "I'll accept that."

**Step 5: Finalize and Record**
- Both parties agree
- Trade post manager records: "Trader A gave 20 lb salt + Trader B gave 3 goats + 1 hour carpentry. Recorded [date]."
- Both parties sign/mark if able

</div>

### Preventing Exploitation in Negotiation

<div class="fraud-warning">

**Power imbalances that enable exploitation:**
1. **Desperation** - someone needs to trade immediately (hunger, emergency)
2. **Ignorance** - someone doesn't know fair values
3. **Illiteracy** - someone can't verify contracts or ledger entries
4. **Weak social position** - someone has no allies to advocate for them
5. **Unfamiliar traders** - out-of-town traders may not know local values

**Protections:**
- **Post all values visibly** (prevents false claims of different prices)
- **Trade post manager present** (ensures both parties are treated equally)
- **Verbal agreement witnessed** (both parties and witness confirm deal)
- **Protect vulnerable people:** Assign advocate if someone is in desperate situation (ensure they're not cheated)
- **Educational access:** Teach new traders or young people the value system
- **Dispute resolution:** Anyone can challenge a trade within 3 days if they believe it was unfair (go to community leader)

**Red flags for fraudulent traders:**
- Offering dramatically different prices than posted values
- Refusing to have agreements witnessed
- Pressuring quick decisions ("You must decide NOW")
- Goods that are mislabeled or partially hidden
- Weighing goods when community standard is volume (or vice versa, to enable cheating)
- Claiming prices are "secret" or "different for different people"

</div>

</section>

<section id="preventing-fraud">

## 5. Fraud Prevention and Quality Assurance

Fraud destroys trust in the trade system faster than anything else. Communities must establish clear fraud detection and response.

### Common Fraud Methods

<div class="fraud-warning">

**Product fraud:**
- Hiding spoiled/inferior goods under good ones in container
- Mixing sand or rocks with grain to increase weight
- Selling watered-down oil or honey
- Selling used/broken tools as new
- Mislabeling goods ("calling cloth 'wool' when it's lower-quality plant fiber")

**Quantity fraud:**
- Short-weighing (scales that favor the seller)
- Damp goods that dry out after sale (buyer gets less weight)
- Compressed goods that expand after sale
- Counting goods incorrectly

**Value fraud:**
- False claims about origin or quality
- Refusing to accept return of spoiled goods
- Agreeing to trade value, then claiming different value at settlement

</div>

### Quality Control at Trade Post

**Buyer protections:**
1. **Inspect goods before completing trade** - open containers, check quality, verify quantity
2. **Weigh/measure at trade post** (using community scales, not trader's scales)
3. **Test perishables** (grain for moisture, oil for rancidity, meat for smell)
4. **Rights to return:** Within 24 hours, any good found to be fraudulent can be returned for refund

**Seller protections:**
1. **Dispute resolution** - if buyer claims fraud after 24 hours, matter goes to arbitrator (not automatic refund)
2. **Witness to transaction** - both parties and manager sign/mark, confirming goods condition at time of trade
3. **Reputation protection** - false fraud accusations can damage trader's name; council may enforce penalty for false claims

**Repeated fraud:**
- First offense: Public apology, refund, compensation to injured party
- Second offense: Banned from trade post for 3 months
- Third offense: Permanent ban, public record of dishonesty

</section>

<section id="networks-credit">

## 6. Barter Networks and Inter-Community Trade

As communities establish trade posts, a network emerges: multiple communities trading with each other over distance. This enables specialization and reduces conflict.

### Network Structure

**Hub-and-spoke model (common):**
- Central trade post (larger community or neutral location)
- Smaller communities send representatives weekly/monthly with goods
- All communities benefit from access to diverse goods
- One central ledger tracks all trades

**Ring model:**
- Communities form a circle; each community trades with neighbors
- Example: A trades with B, B trades with C, C trades with D, D trades back with A
- No central hub; more resilient if one link breaks
- More complex record-keeping

**Open network:**
- Multiple independent trade posts; traders move between them
- Most flexible; also most prone to fraud and inconsistent values
- Works if communities trust each other and maintain regular communication

### Trade Route Safety

<div class="trade-box">

**Dangers on routes:**
- Bandits (organized or opportunistic)
- Dangerous wildlife
- Harsh weather or terrain
- Getting lost
- Inadequate supplies for journey

**Protections:**
1. **Traders travel in groups** (minimum 3 people; armed if possible)
2. **Posted routes** - use known, marked paths
3. **Scheduled movements** - regular traders on same days (allows people to watch for them; predictability discourages bandits)
4. **Safe houses** - communities spaced 1 day's travel apart along major routes; offer shelter, water, food
5. **Trade truce** - neighboring communities agree "no violence against traders on routes"; violators face trade embargo
6. **Protection fee:** Communities may pay security to escort traders, especially valuable cargo

**Managing bandit risk:**
- Traders post bond with community (refunded if they return)
- If a trader is robbed, community leader may pursue bandits
- Trading communities agree on bandit response (shared security)
- Last resort: Reroute trade to avoid dangerous areas

</div>

</section>

<section id="ious-credit">

## 7. IOUs, Credit Systems, and Tracking Debt

In pure barter, every trade is immediate and complete. But sometimes someone needs goods they cannot pay for now. Credit systems enable this while protecting against fraud.

### Simple IOU System

<div class="trade-box">

**IOU (I Owe You) format:**

```
Date: [date]
Debtor: [Name of person who owes]
Creditor: [Name of person owed]
Item/Service: [what is owed]
Quantity/Amount: [how much]
Agreed Value: [value in labor units or goods]
Due Date: [when payment is due]
Collateral (optional): [what is pledged if payment fails]
Witness: [signature or mark of neutral party]
```

**Example:**
```
Date: 3rd month, 22nd day
Debtor: John (farmer)
Creditor: Mary (healer)
Item: Herbal medicine for fever
Quantity: 5 doses
Agreed Value: 5 labor units
Due Date: After autumn harvest (2 months)
Collateral: 1 goat (if payment fails)
Witness: Tom (trade post manager)
```

**Legal force:**
- IOU is binding contract (see contract guide)
- Witnessed IOU can be enforced by community leader if debtor defaults
- If debtor refuses to pay, they forfeit collateral or face community sanctions
- Unpaid debts damage credit (future traders refuse to extend credit)

</div>

### Credit System for Communities

Larger communities may develop community credit:

**Model:**
- Central fund held by trusted person (treasurer)
- Individuals can borrow from fund at interest (e.g., borrow 10 labor units, repay 11 at harvest)
- Interest is 10–20% (covers risk of default and provides small incentive to lenders)
- Default has social consequences (exclusion from future borrowing, community pressure)

**Dangers:**
- High interest can trap borrowers in debt cycles
- Poor record-keeping enables fraud
- Debt can be weaponized against vulnerable people

**Safeguards:**
- Community council approves loan terms (prevents usury)
- Ledger is public (everyone can see who owes what)
- Statute of limitations (debt forgiven after 5 years of good-faith payment attempts)
- Debt forgiveness (jubilee) every 7 years (prevents intergenerational debt slavery)

### Tracking Debt and Repayment

See basic accounting guide for ledger systems. Key principles:
- Every IOU is recorded with unique identifier
- Payments are recorded with date and amount
- Remaining balance is always visible
- Collateral is explicitly stated
- Creditor and debtor both have copy of agreement

</section>

## 8. Transitioning to Representative Currency

If barter becomes too complex (many traders, frequent trades), communities may create representative currency: tokens that represent agreed-upon value.

### When to Introduce Currency

**Signs barter is becoming insufficient:**
- Negotiations taking too long (>1 hour per trade)
- Frequent disputes about relative values
- Some goods cannot be divided into necessary units ("How do I trade half a goat?")
- Trade is so frequent that tracking IOUs becomes overwhelming

### Simple Currency System

<div class="trade-box">

**Token design:**
- Physical token (carved wood, stamped metal, marked bone)
- Each token represents 1 labor unit (or other agreed baseline)
- Different denominations (1, 5, 10 labor units; marked clearly)
- Authority mark (seal of community leader or council)

**Issuing currency:**
1. Community decides how much currency to issue (based on anticipated trading volume)
2. Tokens are created and authority-marked
3. Initial distribution: Tokens are given to people in exchange for goods/services
4. Circulation: Tokens move through economy as people trade

**Valuation:**
- 1 token = 1 labor unit (unchanging, reinforced by community commitment)
- All posted prices convert to token equivalent
- Goods and tokens are freely exchanged at posted rates

**Dangers:**
- If too much currency issued: Inflation (tokens become worthless)
- If too little: Deflation (tokens become hoarded, trade slows)
- If authority tries to devalue: Traders refuse tokens, revert to barter
- Counterfeiting: Fake tokens undermine entire system

**Safeguards:**
- Only authorized person creates tokens
- Tokens are difficult to counterfeit (require special materials or skill)
- Regular inventory of circulating tokens (compare to amount issued)
- Any counterfeits are publicly identified (counterfeiter faces justice)
- Community maintains discipline about not over-issuing

</div>

### Record-Keeping with Currency

Once currency is introduced, all trades must be recorded to prevent fraud and monitor inflation. See accounting guide for ledger systems.

</section>

<section id="seasonal-patterns">

## 9. Seasonal Trade Patterns

Trade does not occur evenly throughout the year. Seasonal abundance and scarcity drive major fluctuations.

### Seasonal Cycles

<div class="trade-box">

**Spring (planting season):**
- Supply: Seeds, seedlings, early greens
- Demand: High (people need food from winter stores depleted)
- Prices: Volatile (desperation increases if winter stores ran out)
- Opportunity: Communities with preserved winter goods can sell at premium

**Summer (growth season):**
- Supply: Fresh foods, increased labor availability
- Demand: Moderate (some foods are abundant, less need for preserved goods)
- Prices: Declining (fresh food is cheap compared to spring)
- Opportunity: Festivals, celebrations, and trade fairs are profitable

**Autumn (harvest):**
- Supply: Peak abundance of grains, preserved goods being made
- Demand: Moderate (food abundant)
- Prices: Lowest (supply exceeds demand)
- Opportunity: Communities that did well in harvest can trade grain/goods at volume discounts; buy cheap to resell in winter

**Winter (scarcity season):**
- Supply: Limited (only preserved/stored goods)
- Demand: Very high (food is critical; fresh goods extremely scarce)
- Prices: Highest (scarcity drives value)
- Opportunity: Communities that stored goods can profit; those who didn't face hardship

</div>

### Strategic Trading Over Seasons

**Smart traders:**
- Buy cheap in autumn (harvest), store carefully
- Sell at premium in spring (desperate demand)
- Profit margin covers storage costs and provides income for off-season

**Community strategies:**
- Use harvest surplus to fill community granary (reduces need for expensive spring purchases)
- Plan for winter shortage (don't trade all surplus away in autumn)
- Build relationships with other communities (enable borrowing/trading in emergency)

</section>
