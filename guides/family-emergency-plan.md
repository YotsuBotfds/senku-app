## Introduction: Why This Matters

A family emergency plan is not a luxury item—it's insurance. When cell networks jam, when roads close, when you're separated from your family by 30 miles and no one can reach anyone else, this plan is the difference between panic and coordinated action.

The good news: a solid family emergency plan costs nothing. It requires only a few hours of conversation now, a phone call to each household member every 6 months to update it, and a one-evening walk around your neighborhood with a notebook. That's it.

This guide walks you through building a plan that works for your specific household, your kids' schools, your workplace, and your Minnesota-specific disaster risks. By the end, everyone in your family will know:

- Who to call first (the one person everyone tries to reach)
- Where to go if you can't go home
- How to get to school pickup if roads are impassable
- Which neighbor to ask for help and what kind
- What disaster is most likely where you live, and what to do

:::warning
**This guide assumes your family has a working phone and can add a contact to your phone's memory or write it on paper.** If internet is down or cell networks are jammed, you will rely on the communication tree, not group chats or social media. Test this plan in writing, not just in conversation.
:::

## Section 1: The Communication Tree – The Spine of Your Plan

When disaster strikes, here's what happens to phone networks:

1. **First 15 minutes:** Everyone tries to call 911, a loved one, or their workplace. Lines overflow.
2. **Next 30 minutes:** Cell towers prioritize emergency services and roaming traffic. Local calls fail, long-distance calls succeed (they route through different infrastructure).
3. **After 1 hour:** Texting works before calling. A text message is a small data packet that queues; a voice call demands real-time bandwidth. Texts will send even if calls fail.

A communication tree is a checklist of people, in order, that every family member contacts when separated or unable to reach the primary family group.

### Step 1: Designate an Out-of-State Contact

This person is the anchor of your tree. Choose someone who:
- Lives at least 200 miles away (so they're unaffected by the same local disaster)
- Answers calls 24/7 or checks messages frequently
- Understands the plan and has agreed to be the hub

Typically this is an adult relative: a parent, sibling, or trusted friend in a different state.

**Examples:**
- Sister in Chicago
- Cousin in Iowa
- Best friend's parent in Colorado
- Trusted family friend in another time zone

**Your out-of-state contact:**
```
Name: _______________________________
Relationship: _______________________________
Primary phone: _______________________________
Backup phone: _______________________________
Email (if landline): _______________________________
```

:::tip
**Test the contact monthly.** Send them a text or call them on the 19th of each month. "Hey, just testing our emergency plan — all clear here." This takes 30 seconds and tells you whether their number is still active.
:::

### Step 2: Everyone Memorizes This Number

This is non-negotiable. Do not rely on your phone's contacts list. In an actual emergency, your phone might be dead, lost, or in a different room. Every family member from age 8 onward should memorize this number:

**Out-of-state contact's phone number to memorize:**
```
[___] [___] - [____]
```

Practice this weekly. When you're in the car, ask kids "what's Aunt Sarah's number?" Make it as automatic as knowing your own name.

:::info-box
**Why memorize instead of write down?** Because your phone could be dead, wet, or at the bottom of a car. If your kids are separated from you in a flood or after a car accident, they need to call your out-of-state hub from any available phone — a neighbor's landline, a fire truck radio, a stranger's phone. They can't depend on having paper.
:::

### Step 3: Text, Don't Call – The Rule That Saves Your Plan

When networks jam, calls fail but texts queue. Train your household:

**When you're separated or worried:**
1. DON'T try to call anyone (the network is overloaded)
2. DO send one text to the out-of-state contact with: your location, any injuries, what you're doing next

**Text format (keep it short):**
```
At [location]. [status: safe/injured/trapped/OK]. Heading to [next location]. Will check in at [time].
```

**Example:**
```
At work downtown. Safe. Heading to pick up kids at school. Will check in at 4pm.
```

**Example:**
```
Safe but at shelter. Car won't start. Generator on my block. Will update at 6pm.
```

The out-of-state contact then forwards this to the rest of the family tree, and by the time you're reunited, everyone knows everyone is alive.

:::warning
**Never assume the network is fast.** Your text might take 30 minutes to send during a disaster. Your received texts might be hours old by the time they arrive. Do not send time-critical information via text. Send updates at the checkpoint times you agree on (see next section).
:::

### Step 4: Agree on Check-In Times

Set a recurring time when everyone will attempt to contact the hub, whether they have news or not. Even if you have no new information, you check in: "Still safe, no change."

**Good check-in intervals:**
- Every hour (if immediate danger or active evacuation)
- Every 6 hours (if safe but separated, e.g. power out but no immediate danger)
- Once daily (if shelter is stable)

**Your family's agreed check-in times:**

During a crisis, we text the out-of-state contact at: **[__]:00** every **[__]** hours until reunited.

:::tip
**Make it automatic.** If you pick "top of each hour," it's easy to remember: 4:00, 5:00, 6:00, etc. Set a phone alarm if it helps. The message only needs two sentences: "Still OK, no updates" or "Still OK, still en route to [place]."
:::

### Step 5: Secondary Contacts

In case the out-of-state contact can't be reached, have a backup list. This includes nearby family, close friends, and colleagues who can relay information:

```
Secondary contacts (in order of preference):

1. Name: ____________________  Phone: [___] ___ - ____
2. Name: ____________________  Phone: [___] ___ - ____
3. Name: ____________________  Phone: [___] ___ - ____
```

Everyone in the family should have this list written down (or memorized, if possible).

## Section 2: Meeting Points – The Physical Anchors

When you're separated from family, knowing where to go is as important as knowing who to call. A good meeting point system has three tiers, each activated depending on the scale of the emergency.

### Primary Meeting Point: Home

Assume home is accessible and safe. If your house is intact and power is on, you return home and wait.

**Your home address (everyone should know this):**
```
_________________________________________________________________
```

:::info-box
**Kids should memorize your home address** the same way they memorize your phone number. If they're young (under 10), write it on a card in their backpack. If they walk or bike to school, make sure they can navigate home alone or with a sibling.
:::

### Secondary Meeting Point: Nearby Landmark

Home might be inaccessible if:
- Your street is blocked by a tree or debris
- Your house is damaged or on fire
- Evacuation order is in effect for your area
- Power is out but not dangerous (still safe to walk to meeting point, but why?)

Your secondary point should be:
- Within 1–2 blocks of home
- A recognizable, permanent landmark
- Accessible on foot (doesn't require crossing major roads)
- Outdoors (so it's accessible even if a building is locked)

**Good examples:**
- South entrance of [School Name], near flagpole
- East side of [Park Name], at the big oak tree
- Parking lot of [Familiar Store], north corner
- Community center entrance, under the green awning
- Bridge over [Creek/River], north side

**Your secondary meeting point:**
```
Location: _________________________________________________________________
Landmark/entrance: _________________________________________________________________
How long to walk from home: _____________ minutes
```

**For kids:** Walk to this spot together now. If they'll walk there independently in an emergency, practice once more.

### Tertiary Meeting Point: Out-of-Area Evacuation Point

If your neighborhood is being evacuated (due to wildfire, major flooding, or industrial accident), you need a pre-arranged place to go that's at least 10 miles away but familiar.

This could be:
- A relative's or close friend's house in a different town
- A public location that's far enough away to avoid the disaster (e.g. if evacuation is for a fire west of you, the meeting point is east)
- A motel or shelter (pre-planned, so you know the address)

**Important:** If you name a relative's house, confirm with them NOW that you can use their house as a meetup. Get their address and phone number. If there are multiple families, set a timeframe: "We'll arrive within 2 hours, or we'll check in by 8pm."

**Your tertiary meeting point:**
```
Location: _________________________________________________________________
Address: _________________________________________________________________
Contact (if a person): ______________________  Phone: [___] ___ - ____
How to get there: _________________________________________________________________
Distance: _____________ miles
Estimated drive time: _____________ minutes (in normal traffic)
Estimated walk time: _____________ hours (if no vehicle available)
```

:::warning
**Tertiary points are only for evacuation.** Do not use them for routine separation. If your power is out but your house is fine, you don't evacuate to the tertiary point — you go to the secondary point for a short time, then return home.
:::

### The Meeting Point Card

Write all three points on a card, laminate it, and keep one copy:
- In your car
- In each child's backpack
- On the fridge
- In your wallet
- Shared with your out-of-state contact

## Section 3: School Pickup Protocols

When a disaster strikes during school hours, schools do not release kids immediately. They shelter in place, account for all students, and release only to authorized adults on file. You need a plan that survives impassable roads and failed communications.

### Step 1: Build Your Authorized Pickup List

Contact the school and ask for the "emergency contact" or "student release" form. Update it every August (start of school year) and again in January (mid-year check).

List adults in this order:

1. **Primary:** You (parent/guardian)
2. **Secondary:** Other parent or co-guardian
3. **Tertiary:** Relative or trusted adult who's available to pick up on short notice
4. **Quaternary:** Neighbor or backup adult

Include:
- Full legal name
- Relationship to child
- Two phone numbers
- Driver's license number (for ID at pickup)

**For each child, update:**

```
CHILD: _______________________________

1. Name: ___________________________  Phone: [___] ___ - ____
   ID #: _____________
2. Name: ___________________________  Phone: [___] ___ - ____
   ID #: _____________
3. Name: ___________________________  Phone: [___] ___ - ____
   ID #: _____________
4. Name: ___________________________  Phone: [___] ___ - ____
   ID #: _____________
```

:::tip
**Include a neighbor.** If you and all listed family members are unreachable, schools will release to neighbors. Choose someone you trust and who lives close.
:::

### Step 2: Give Kids the Pickup Plan

Children should know:
- Who can pick them up (show them photos if they haven't met everyone)
- That they should never leave school with anyone not on the list
- That they should never leave the school building without adult permission, even if asked by someone they know

**Age-appropriate conversation:**

**Ages 5–8:** "If there's an emergency and you're at school, you wait there. Mrs. Johnson or Uncle Tim or Neighbor Sarah will come get you. You only go with people I show you pictures of. If a stranger asks, you say 'I have to ask a teacher.'"

**Ages 9–12:** "If something happens and I can't pick you up, [List names] are authorized to pick you up. The school has their phone numbers. You stay at school and wait. A teacher will call them. Only leave with someone on the authorized list. If you're not sure, ask the teacher."

**Ages 13+:** "Here's the authorized pickup list. If there's a disaster, I'll text you an update using our family hub [name]. If I can't reach you, I'll have [person] pick you up or meet you at [place]. Check your phone every 30 minutes."

### Step 3: Account for Impassable Roads

Minnesota blizzards, ice storms, and flooding can make roads impassable for days.

**Ask the school:**
- What is their snow day threshold? (Usually: 1/2" of snow + wind, or ice-covered roads)
- If roads are impassable but school is open, can you pick up early? (Answer: usually no, schools won't release early unless official conditions trigger dismissal)
- Do they have a "reverse 911" system to notify parents? (Most do; sign up for it)
- Where can you find official snow day announcements? (Website, local news, text alert)

**Your plan for impassable roads:**

During winter, if roads are bad but school is open:
1. Kids stay at school. School has heat, water, food, and supervision.
2. You do not attempt to pick up early (this clogs the system and takes teachers away from other kids).
3. You stay updated via school alerts.
4. If roads become fully impassable (e.g., "all roads to [town] closed"), school will announce closure or dismissal.
5. You pick up as soon as your vehicle can move safely.

:::warning
**Do not be a hero.** The worst traffic accidents happen when parents try to drive in blizzards to pick up kids early. Schools are prepared to shelter kids overnight if needed. Let them.
:::

### Step 4: Kids Walking Home Safely

If your children are old enough to walk home from school (typically 3rd grade and up), and roads are impassable to vehicles:

**Prepare now:**
1. Walk the route home together, twice. Once in good weather, once in winter.
2. Identify safe checkpoints: houses of trusted neighbors, a business they can duck into, a shelter (park gazebo, covered bus stop).
3. Teach them the route in both directions (so they can re-route if a street is blocked).
4. Agree on a time: "If you don't hear from me by [time], you walk home."

**The plan:**
```
WALKING ROUTE HOME

School: _________________________________________________________________

Route landmarks (in order):
1. _________________________________________________________________
2. _________________________________________________________________
3. _________________________________________________________________

Safe checkpoints to rest/shelter:
1. _________________________________________________________________  (neighbor/friend/business)
2. _________________________________________________________________  (neighbor/friend/business)
3. _________________________________________________________________  (shelter: park gazebo, etc.)

Expected walk time: _____________ minutes (normal pace)
Expected walk time in snow: _____________ minutes

Worst-case conditions (ice, deep snow): stay at school, wait for pickup
```

:::info-box
**Test the route.** In winter, walk it together once when there's snow on the ground. Your child will learn what it feels like, how long it takes, and whether the planned checkpoints are realistic. A route that feels safe in September might be terrifying in a whiteout.
:::

## Section 4: Neighbor Network – Your Local Lifeline

"Knowing your neighbors" is vague and useless. A neighbor network means you know specific people, their skills, and how to reach them when networks fail. This section turns abstract safety into concrete relationships.

### Why Neighbors Matter in a Disaster

When infrastructure fails:
- Cell networks are jammed (you can't call 911, family, or friends)
- Roads are blocked (emergency services can't reach you quickly)
- Power is out (no online services, navigation, or communication)

In the first 24–48 hours, your neighbors are the only resource you can reliably access. One neighbor might have a generator. Another might be a nurse. A third might have tools to clear a downed tree. Knowing who has what, and how to reach them when your phone is dead, can mean the difference between a minor inconvenience and a critical situation.

### Step 1: Knock on Four Doors This Month

Start small. This month, visit four neighbors within one block of your house. Bring nothing. The conversation should take 3–5 minutes. Here's a script:

**What to say:**

"Hi, I'm [your name] from [your address]. I'm talking to neighbors about emergency preparedness. If a disaster happens—power out, trees down, roads blocked—do you have any useful skills or equipment? For example, do you have a generator, medical training, chainsaw, or other resources? And can I have your phone number so we can help each other if needed?"

**Listen for:**
- Medical background (nurse, doctor, EMT, first aid training)
- Equipment (generator, chainsaw, tools, 4WD vehicle)
- Skills (electrical, plumbing, carpentry, mechanics)
- Availability (works from home, retired, often home)
- Family status (elderly, mobility issues, young kids, pets)

**Write it down immediately after:**

```
NEIGHBOR 1
Name: _________________________________
Address: _________________________________
Phone: [___] ___ - ____
Skills/resources: _________________________________
Medical training? Yes / No
Generator? Yes / No
Chainsaw? Yes / No
Mobility/care concerns? _________________________________
```

Repeat for neighbors 2, 3, and 4.

:::tip
**Timing matters.** Knock on the door between 6–7pm on a weekday (people are home but not eating dinner yet). Avoid weekends and midday. If no one answers, leave a friendly note with your name and number: "Hi, I'm talking to neighbors about emergency prep. Would love to chat. Call [number]."
:::

### Step 2: Identify Specific Skill Resources

After you've talked to 4–8 neighbors, map their resources:

```
NEIGHBOR RESOURCE MAP

Medical resources (first aid, nursing, pharmacy knowledge):
_________________________________________________________________

Equipment resources (generator, chainsaw, tools):
_________________________________________________________________

Vehicle resources (4WD, truck, heavy machinery):
_________________________________________________________________

Isolated neighbors (elderly, mobility issues, lives alone):
_________________________________________________________________

Home with large family/kids (good shelter for nearby kids):
_________________________________________________________________
```

### Step 3: Offer Specific Help

Once you've identified resources, make an offer tied to their skill:

- To the nurse neighbor: "If someone's injured and we can't reach 911, can I call you?"
- To the generator neighbor: "If power's out, can we come charge phones or run a space heater for 30 minutes?"
- To the chainsaw neighbor: "If a tree blocks the road, can we borrow the chainsaw or ask you to help clear it?"
- To the elderly neighbor: "Can I check on you daily if the power's out? Do you need to keep a spare key somewhere?"

This transforms a vague "know your neighbors" into concrete, mutual help.

### Step 4: Create a Neighborhood Contact List

After visiting 8–12 neighbors, make a simple list and share it:

```
NEIGHBORHOOD EMERGENCY CONTACTS

[My name] [My address] [My phone]
[Neighbor 1 name] [Their address] [Their phone] — Generator, medical trained
[Neighbor 2 name] [Their address] [Their phone] — Chainsaw, 4WD truck
[Neighbor 3 name] [Their address] [Their phone] — Lives alone, needs daily check-in
...
```

Print copies and give them to neighbors who want them. This takes 30 minutes and transforms isolation into a mutual aid network.

:::warning
**Physical paper copies matter.** During a power outage or network failure, your neighbor can't look up who has a generator by searching a group chat. A printed list on their fridge is gold.
:::

### Step 5: The Neighborhood Walk – Find Critical Resources

Visit your neighborhood in person, without being weird about it. Identify:

**Nearest house with a wood stove:**
```
Address: _________________________________________________________________
Why this matters: In a multi-day power outage, a wood stove is a heat source that doesn't depend on electricity. Know where it is.
```

**Nearest house with a generator:**
```
Address: _________________________________________________________________
Type of generator (if known): _________________________________________________________________
Why this matters: A neighbor's generator can charge phones, run a refrigerator, or provide light.
```

**Nearest medical professional:**
```
Address: _________________________________________________________________
Profession (nurse, doctor, EMT, etc.): _________________________________________________________________
Why this matters: If 911 is overwhelmed and someone is injured, a trained neighbor can help triage or provide first aid.
```

**Nearest house that's occupied during the day:**
```
Address: _________________________________________________________________
Why this matters: This is a shelter if you get trapped somewhere near your home and need to call for help or warm up.
```

## Section 5: Know Your Local Risks – Minnesota-Specific Threats

Every region has disasters it's prone to. Minnesota's are specific, predictable, and manageable if you know what to expect. This section walks you through the most likely threats and what you do about each.

### Risk 1: Blizzard / Winter Storm (Most Likely)

**Probability:** Very high — Minnesota gets 1–3 significant blizzards per winter
**Duration:** 12–48 hours (sometimes longer)
**Impact:** Impassable roads, power outages, extreme cold, stranded vehicles, school closures

**What a blizzard looks like:**
- Rapid snowfall (6"+ in under 6 hours)
- Wind (20+ mph gusts, reducing visibility to near-zero)
- Temperature drop (into negatives, wind chill to -30°F or lower)
- Road closures (highways impassable, some back roads too)
- School closures (usually called by 6am)

**Your plan:**

```
BLIZZARD PROTOCOL

If blizzard is forecast:
1. Stock 3 days of shelf-stable food (what you eat anyway: pasta, canned goods, frozen meals)
2. Fill bathtubs with water (in case pipes freeze)
3. Charge all devices (phones, laptops, tablets)
4. Fill car with gas (gas stations may close if power is out)

If you're at work when blizzard hits:
1. Check school closure list — kids are already home or at school shelter
2. DO NOT attempt to drive home if roads are impassable
3. Stay at work or find shelter nearby (workplace, friend's house, hotel)
4. Text family hub: "Blizzard, staying at [location], roads impassable. Will update at [time]."
5. Wait for roads to be plowed (usually 4–12 hours after snow stops)

If you're home:
1. Stay home (heat, water, shelter are all there)
2. Close off unused rooms (concentrate warmth in small space)
3. Use fireplace or wood stove if available
4. Check on neighbors, especially elderly or isolated
5. Keep phone charged (conserve battery for emergency calls)

If power goes out:
1. Leave refrigerator and freezer closed (food stays cold for days)
2. Use candles or flashlights (not a stove or oven for heat — carbon monoxide risk)
3. Wear layers; close off unused rooms
4. Open fireplace damper if you have one; use wood stove if available
5. If extremely cold (below -20°F), consider staying with a neighbor with heat/generator
```

**Your family's blizzard supplies location:**
```
Bottled water stored at: _________________________________________________________________
Shelf-stable food stored at: _________________________________________________________________
Flashlights/candles stored at: _________________________________________________________________
```

:::warning
**Never run a car, generator, stove, or grill for heat indoors.** Carbon monoxide builds up in minutes and kills silently. If you're cold, wear layers, close off rooms, and if absolutely necessary, stay with a neighbor who has working heat.
:::

### Risk 2: Extended Power Outage (1–14 Days)

**Probability:** High in winter; moderate in summer
**Causes:** Ice storm, downed trees, transformer failure, grid failure
**Duration:** Typically 1–7 days; rarely up to 14 days
**Impact:** No heating/cooling, no water pressure, no refrigeration, no internet, no charging

**What happens during a power outage:**
- Hour 1–2: You turn on flashlights, charge phones
- Hour 4–8: You realize heat isn't working (winter) or A/C isn't working (summer), or water pressure is gone
- Hour 12+: Food is starting to spoil; you're running out of ice
- Day 2+: Boredom, anxiety, kids are restless; stores are closing or running out of supplies

**Your plan — Preparation (do this now):**

```
POWER OUTAGE PREPARATION

Heat source (winter):
☐ Fireplace/wood stove functional
☐ Generator or portable heater (with fuel)
☐ Sleeping bags suitable for -10°F (if no backup heat)
☐ Know how to operate wood stove/fireplace
Location: _________________________________________________________________

Water:
☐ Bathtub can be filled (for flushing toilets, not drinking)
☐ Stored drinking water: _____________ gallons
☐ Location: _________________________________________________________________
☐ Water purification method if stored runs out: _________________________________

Food:
☐ Cooler and ice in freezer
☐ Shelf-stable food for 7 days (canned, pasta, peanut butter, crackers, nuts)
☐ Location: _________________________________________________________________
☐ Non-perishable food for kids (familiar snacks, not survival bars)
☐ Location: _________________________________________________________________

Charging:
☐ Power banks (battery packs) for phones
☐ Car charger for phones
☐ Laptop fully charged before outage (can power other devices via USB)
☐ Candles and flashlights (not matches or lighters; less reliable)

Information:
☐ Portable radio (battery or hand-crank)
☐ Local utility company's outage number written down
☐ Know which neighbors have generators and how to ask to borrow them
```

**During a power outage:**

```
HOUR-BY-HOUR CHECKLIST (First 24 hours)

Hour 0–2:
☐ Turn off major appliances (so when power returns, inrush doesn't overload)
☐ Charge all phones and power banks
☐ Fill bathtub with water
☐ Find flashlights and candles
☐ Check on elderly/isolated neighbors

Hour 2–8:
☐ Listen to radio for outage updates and restoration time
☐ If outage will be >12 hours, don't open refrigerator (cold lasts 48 hours if sealed)
☐ Eat perishable food first (meat, dairy) before it spoils
☐ If winter, close off unused rooms and seal air leaks with towels
☐ Text family hub with status (if you haven't already)

Hour 8–24:
☐ If winter, light fireplace/wood stove if available
☐ Keep kids entertained (books, games, puzzles — no screen time, preserve battery)
☐ Boil water if you need drinking water and stored supply is low
☐ Use cooler with ice to preserve high-value perishables
☐ Check on neighbors again (daily check-ins)
```

**If outage extends 3+ days:**
- Move to a friend/family's house with power (don't tough it out in a cold house)
- Buy gas for generator (stations may have supplies or power from backup generators)
- Stay hydrated; boil water if needed
- Check on elderly neighbors daily

:::info-box
**The "open the fridge" mistake:** Every time you open a refrigerator, cold escapes. In a power outage, a sealed fridge keeps food cold for 48+ hours. Every time you open it, you reduce that by hours. Decide what you'll need in advance and get it all at once.
:::

### Risk 3: Spring Flooding (Seasonal, Late March–May)

**Probability:** Moderate to high in areas near rivers or low-lying zones
**Duration:** 1–7 days (water recedes slowly)
**Impact:** Evacuations, impassable roads, basement flooding, contaminated water

**Before spring (in March):**

```
FLOOD PREPAREDNESS

Do I live in a flood zone?
☐ Check FEMA Flood Map: floodsmart.gov
☐ Check local county flood plain maps (county assessor's office)
☐ Ask neighbors if area has flooded historically

If I live in a flood zone:
☐ Know evacuation route (which roads lead uphill/away from water)
☐ Know alternate routes (in case primary is flooded)
☐ Sump pump functional? ☐ Yes ☐ No ☐ Have backup power
☐ Basement waterproofing adequate?
☐ Storm drains and gutters clear?

Evacuation plan:
☐ Know where I'd go (secondary/tertiary meeting point, or relative's house)
☐ Know how long it takes to drive there
☐ Have supplies packed (go-bag, important documents, medications)

If I don't live in a flood zone:
☐ Still know evacuation routes (flooding can spread to unexpected areas)
☐ Be prepared to help neighbors who do live in flood zones
```

**During flood watches:**
- Monitor river levels and weather alerts
- Prepare go-bag (important documents, photos, medications)
- Fill car with gas (may need to evacuate on short notice)
- If evacuation order issued, GO. Don't wait, don't assume it won't reach you

:::warning
**Never drive through flooded roads.** Water 1 foot deep can float a car. Water 2 feet deep can sweep away an SUV. "Turn around, don't drown" is the official flood safety rule. If a road is flooded, it's not passable — full stop.
:::

### Risk 4: Tornado (Seasonal, Spring–Summer)

**Probability:** Low to moderate (Minnesota averages 20–30 tornadoes per year, affecting small areas)
**Duration:** Minutes (but warning time is usually 15–30 minutes)
**Impact:** Extreme destruction (in affected areas), high casualty risk

**Tornado safety is simple: have a shelter plan and practice it.**

```
TORNADO SHELTER PLAN

Safest shelter (in order of preference):
1. Basement (if I have one): _________________________________________________________________
   Location in basement: _____________ (interior corner, away from windows)
   
2. Interior room with no windows (if no basement):
   Location: _____________ (bathroom, closet, or interior hallway)
   
3. Safe room (if constructed):
   Location: _________________________________________________________________

Shelter contents:
☐ Flashlight and batteries
☐ First aid kit
☐ Shoes (to protect from broken glass)
☐ Mattress or blankets (to crouch under)

Practice plan:
☐ Walk to shelter once per spring (show kids where to go)
☐ Practice getting there in under 3 minutes (the time you might have)
☐ Practice staying there for 15 minutes (how long a tornado warning lasts)
```

**During tornado warning:**
1. Go to shelter immediately (don't watch the sky, don't take photos)
2. Crouch low, protect your head and neck
3. Stay until warning expires
4. Check for injuries and damage before exiting

**During tornado watch (conditions are favorable):**
- Stay inside or very close to shelter
- Monitor weather alerts
- Have go-bag ready (in case you need to evacuate after tornado)

:::tip
**Your phone will alert you.** Most phones receive emergency weather alerts automatically (Wireless Emergency Alerts). Make sure this is turned ON in your phone settings. You'll get a loud alert and a text saying "TORNADO WARNING" or "TORNADO WATCH" — that's your cue to act.
:::

### Risk 5: Wildfire (Low Probability in Minnesota, But Growing)

**Probability:** Very low currently; higher in western Minnesota and during drought years
**Duration:** Hours to days (if evacuation is ordered)
**Impact:** Evacuations, air quality, visibility

**If you live in a wildfire-prone area:**
- Clear brush and dead trees from around your house (defensible space)
- Have evacuation supplies ready (ID, photos, medications, valuables)
- Know your evacuation route and destination
- Monitor air quality alerts (if smoke is nearby)

**If wildfire evacuation is ordered:**
- Leave immediately (don't wait for all-clear)
- Drive to secondary or tertiary meeting point
- Take important documents and irreplaceables
- Lock windows/doors (fire departments may search for people)
- Use evacuation text alerts from your county

For most Minnesotans, this is low-priority compared to blizzard and flooding prep. But if you live in a fire-prone area (northwest Minnesota, during drought), know your route and keep supplies ready.

## Section 8: Teaching Kids About the Plan

Kids learn better by doing than by being told. Here's how to teach the plan by age:

### Ages 5–8: The Basics
- Memorize: parents' full names, grandparent's phone number
- Know: primary meeting point (home) and secondary meeting point (landmark)
- Know: they stay at school if something happens; an authorized adult will pick them up
- Do: walk to secondary meeting point together (practice)
- Practice: "What's grandma's phone number?" (ask weekly)

### Ages 9–12: The Detail Level
- Memorize: out-of-state contact's phone number
- Know: all three meeting points and why each one exists
- Know: authorized pickup list and that they only leave with those people
- Know: how to walk home from school if roads are impassable
- Do: help create neighborhood contact list; deliver it to neighbors
- Do: help stock food/supplies for power outage
- Practice: "When would you go to the secondary meeting point?" / "What do you do if you're at school and power is out?"

### Ages 13+: Full Participation
- Know: the entire plan (communication tree, meeting points, school protocols, neighbor resources)
- Know: how to navigate to secondary/tertiary meeting points using landmarks (not GPS)
- Know: what to do if separated from family (text the hub, go to meeting point, check in hourly)
- Responsibility: help maintain neighborhood contact list; follow up with out-of-state contact monthly
- Practice: family meetings twice per year to review and update the plan

:::info-box
**Kids take plans seriously if adults model seriousness.** If you test your out-of-state contact monthly, kids will take it seriously. If you practice meeting points, kids will remember them. If you check on the elderly neighbor, kids will too.
:::

## Conclusion: The Plan is the Insurance

A family emergency plan is perhaps the most asymmetrical investment you can make in preparedness. It takes 10–15 hours total to build a solid one. It costs nothing. And it works whether disaster is a power outage, a blizzard, a school lockdown, or something you never imagined.

The worst time to figure out how to reach your family is while you're separated and panicked. The worst time to learn that your neighbor has a generator is the night the power goes out.

Do the work now. Walk four doors over this month. Memorize a phone number. Identify three meeting points. In a crisis, you and your family will have done the hard thing when there was no urgency, and that preparation will become clarity when panic might have taken over.

**Start this week. One phone call, one conversation, one walk. That's how you build a plan that actually works.**.
