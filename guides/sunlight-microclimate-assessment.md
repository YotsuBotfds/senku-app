## Overview

Sun exposure is the single largest variable determining where plants thrive. A site that receives 8 hours of sun in one location receives 2 hours 100 feet away due to shadows from trees, buildings, or terrain. Microclimates—small areas with distinct conditions—exist within a property. A north-facing slope is cooler and wetter than a south-facing slope 50 yards away. Understanding light availability and microclimate conditions is essential for garden placement and crop selection.

This guide covers methods to map sun exposure through the growing season, identify microclimates (frost pockets, wind-exposed areas, heat sinks), assess slope and aspect, and estimate growing zones using phenological indicators (plant and animal behavior) rather than relying on maps or internet data.

## Sun Path Through Seasons

The sun's path across the sky changes throughout the year. In temperate zones, the sun traces a low arc in winter and a high arc in summer.

### Understanding Sun Position

**Key angles and times:**

| **Season** | **Solar Noon Height** | **Sunrise Direction** | **Sunset Direction** | **Day Length** |
|-----------|----------------------|----------------------|----------------------|----------------|
| Summer solstice (Jun 21) | Highest (~70° at 40°N) | NE (~60°) | NW (~300°) | ~15 hours |
| Spring/Fall equinox (Mar/Sep 21) | Medium (~50° at 40°N) | E (90°) | W (270°) | ~12 hours |
| Winter solstice (Dec 21) | Lowest (~25° at 40°N) | SE (~120°) | SW (~240°) | ~9 hours |

**Implications:**
- **South-facing areas** receive full sun year-round (best for cold-sensitive crops in temperate zones).
- **North-facing areas** are shaded in winter by trees and buildings; marginal for most crops but cool in summer (good for shade-loving plants or lettuce in peak summer).
- **East-facing areas** receive morning sun (good for frost protection; frost melts slowly).
- **West-facing areas** receive afternoon sun (hottest; can scorch tender plants).

### Determine Your Latitude

Without internet, estimate latitude by observing the sun's angle or using a map:

**Shadow stick method** (works any date):
1. Place a vertical stick (1 meter tall) on level ground on a clear day.
2. Measure the stick's shadow length at solar noon (when the sun is highest; watch for the shadow pointing due north in the Northern Hemisphere, due south in the Southern Hemisphere).
3. **Calculation**: Latitude ≈ 90° – arctan(shadow length / stick height)
   - Example: stick height 1 m, shadow 1 m → arctan(1) = 45°, so latitude = 90° – 45° = 45°N.

**Rule of thumb (without calculation):**
- Shadow is much longer than stick height → you are far north (high latitude, ~50°N+).
- Shadow length equals stick height → latitude ~45°N.
- Shadow is much shorter than stick height → you are near the equator (low latitude, <30°N).

:::info-box
Once you know approximate latitude, use the sun path angles above to estimate where shadows will fall. In summer, shadows are short; in winter, shadows are long. South-facing slopes get sun year-round in temperate zones.
:::

## Direct Sun Measurement

Sun exposure varies by location on your property. Measure the number of hours each area receives direct sun.

### Solar Path Finder (DIY Transparent Disk)

**Materials:**
- Clear plastic sheet (or clear glass).
- Protractor or printed compass rose.
- Ruler.

**Method:**
1. Place the transparent disk on a tripod or holder in the location you want to assess.
2. Mark the disk with cardinal directions (N, S, E, W) using a compass.
3. At multiple times throughout the day (8 AM, 10 AM, 12 PM, 2 PM, 4 PM, 6 PM), trace the shadow of nearby trees/buildings onto the disk with a marker. Record the time and direction of each shadow.
4. Over several days, build a map of shadow patterns.

**Interpretation:**
- Mark areas on the disk that have shadows throughout the day → heavily shaded (full shade).
- Mark areas that have shadows only in morning → afternoon sun (6+ hours).
- Mark areas with little shadow → full sun (8+ hours).

### Simple Hourly Observation

Observe and record hourly:

**Observation template** (fill daily for 3 days across seasons—spring, summer, winter):

| **Time** | **Spring** | **Summer** | **Fall** | **Winter** |
|---------|-----------|-----------|---------|-----------|
| 8 AM | Sun/shade | Sun/shade | Sun/shade | Sun/shade |
| 10 AM | Sun/shade | Sun/shade | Sun/shade | Sun/shade |
| 12 PM | Sun/shade | Sun/shade | Sun/shade | Sun/shade |
| 2 PM | Sun/shade | Sun/shade | Sun/shade | Sun/shade |
| 4 PM | Sun/shade | Sun/shade | Sun/shade | Sun/shade |
| 6 PM | Sun/shade | Sun/shade | Sun/shade | Sun/shade |

**Sum total hours of direct sun per season:**
- Add up the "Sun" entries for each season. This tells you seasonal variation.
- Example result: "Spring: 10 hours, Summer: 14 hours, Winter: 4 hours" → good for spring/summer crops; poor for winter production.

### Smartphone App Alternative

If you have a smartphone with a solar angle app (downloaded while you have internet, then used offline), apps like "Sun Surveyor" or "Sun Path" show sun position and shadow projection for your location and date. Mark shadow outlines on your property map using the app's prediction.

## Microclimate Identification

Within a few acres, multiple distinct microclimates exist. Identifying them is critical for crop placement.

### Frost Pocket Identification

Frost pockets are low-lying areas where cold air settles on clear nights, creating dangerous late-spring or early-fall frosts.

**Visual identification:**
- Low-lying areas (valley bottoms, depressions).
- Areas downhill of higher terrain (cold air drains downhill like water).
- Near bodies of water in depressions (water at the bottom of the slope condenses moisture and radiates cold).

**Field test** (requires multiple frost-prone nights):
1. On a clear night with ground frost expected, place a thermometer at ground level in a potential frost pocket.
2. Place another thermometer at the same time on higher ground nearby.
3. Compare morning readings.
4. If the low area reads 5°F or more colder than the high area, it is a frost pocket.

**Indicator plants:**
- Frost pockets have a shorter growing season and hardier species naturally.
- Look for native hardy shrubs, late-leafing deciduous trees, or evergreens in pockets.
- High areas have tender species and earlier leaf-out.

:::warning
Avoid planting frost-sensitive crops (tomatoes, peppers, stone fruits) in frost pockets. Use pockets for hardy crops (potatoes, root vegetables, late-blooming apples) or for cold storage (natural cooler in summer).
:::

### Heat Sink Areas

Heat sinks are areas that absorb and radiate heat, creating warmer microclimates.

**Identification:**
- South-facing slopes (slope angle 20–40° south of horizontal).
- Areas next to dark surfaces (dark rock, dark mulch, pavement, dark buildings).
- Wind-protected areas (sun exposure without cooling wind).

**Observation:**
- Snow melts earlier in spring in heat sinks.
- Vegetation leafs out earlier (2–3 weeks earlier than north-facing areas).
- Frost occurs later in fall.

**Use:** Ideal for early spring planting, tender crops (tomatoes, peppers), and cold-sensitive perennials.

### Wind-Exposed Areas

Strong wind desiccates plants and reduces growth.

**Identification:**
- Ridgetops and exposed slopes (wind funnels over them).
- Areas without tree or building shelter.
- Bent or stunted vegetation (weather-shaped trees).

**Wind observation:**
- Watch trees, grass, and standing water on a breezy day.
- Identify the direction of strongest and most consistent wind.
- Note which plants bow or show one-sided growth (wind-pruned).

**Management:**
- Avoid wind-exposed areas for delicate crops.
- Use wind-exposed areas for hardy crops and wind-break establishment.
- Plant shelter belts (trees/shrubs in rows) perpendicular to prevailing wind.

### Wet/Waterlogged Areas

Low areas with poor drainage become waterlogged, unsuitable for most crops.

**Identification:**
- Standing water after rain.
- Soggy soil that doesn't dry quickly (even weeks after rain).
- Species adapted to wet conditions (sedges, rushes, willows).

**Test:**
1. Dig a hole 12 inches deep.
2. Fill with water.
3. If water drains within a few hours, drainage is adequate.
4. If water remains for days, area is too wet for most crops.

**Use:** Wet areas are ideal for water-loving crops (rice paddies, aquatic plants), wildlife habitat, or water catchment features (ponds).

### Dry/Sandy Areas

Areas with coarse soil and low organic matter drain quickly and dry out rapidly.

**Identification:**
- Light-colored, sandy soil.
- Sparse vegetation or stress (wilting plants in summer despite rain).
- Rapid drainage (water disappears within minutes of heavy rain).

**Test:**
1. Squeeze wet soil in your hand.
2. Sandy soil falls apart immediately; clay-rich soil holds its shape.

**Use:** Dry areas suit drought-tolerant crops (potatoes, root vegetables, hardy herbs) once amended with compost/mulch. Without amendment, they are marginal.

## Aspect and Slope Assessment

**Aspect** is the compass direction a slope faces. **Slope** is the angle of the terrain.

### Aspect Determination

**Compass method:**
1. Use a compass to face downhill from the site in question.
2. Read the compass bearing (direction).
3. The aspect is that bearing.
   - 0° = North, 90° = East, 180° = South, 270° = West.

**Observation method** (without compass):
- Identify where the sun rises (east) and sets (west).
- Face the sun at noon; the direction you face is south (Northern Hemisphere).
- If you are on a slope that faces the noonday sun, it is a south-facing slope.

**Aspect effects** (Northern Hemisphere; reverse for Southern Hemisphere):

| **Aspect** | **Sun Exposure** | **Moisture** | **Temperature** | **Growing Season** |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| South | Full sun year-round | Driest | Warmest | Longest |
| Southwest | Very high | Dry | Very hot | Long (may be too hot) |
| West | High afternoon sun | Moderate | Hot | Long |
| North | Low (full shade) | Wettest | Coolest | Shortest |
| Northeast | Morning sun only | Wet | Cool | Short |
| East | Morning sun | Moderate | Cool-warm | Moderate |

:::info-box
South-facing slopes are the most versatile for crops in temperate zones. North-facing slopes are cool and slow to warm in spring, making them poor for most crops but excellent for shade-loving plants and summer heat-sensitive species.
:::

### Slope Measurement

**Percentage slope:**
1. Measure a horizontal distance D (e.g., 100 feet down the slope).
2. Measure the vertical rise R (e.g., 10 feet of elevation change).
3. Percentage slope = (R / D) × 100.
   - 10 feet rise / 100 feet distance = 10% slope.

**Interpretation:**
- **0–5% slope**: Flat to gentle; good for most crops; water may pond.
- **5–15% slope**: Moderate; adequate for most crops; some runoff management needed.
- **15–30% slope**: Steep; erosion risk; best for perennials (trees, shrubs) or terraced crops.
- **>30% slope**: Very steep; erosion risk is high; forests or terraces only.

**Angle method** (using a level and ruler):
1. Lay a straightedge (ruler, board) horizontally across the slope.
2. Measure vertical distance from the end of the ruler to the ground (rise).
3. Measure horizontal distance of the ruler (run).
4. Slope angle = arctan(rise / run).
   - Rise 1 foot, run 5 feet → angle = arctan(1/5) = ~11°.

## Phenological Indicators (Plant/Animal Behavior)

Without temperature records or internet, observe when plants and animals respond to seasonal changes. These indicators suggest whether your site is early-spring, normal, or late-spring—helping you estimate your growing zone and frost risk.

### Spring Indicators

**Early bloom:**
- First flowers to appear: Bloodroot, hepatica, wood anemone (late winter/early spring). If these bloom unusually early, spring is coming early; frost risk window is shifting earlier.
- Skunk cabbage emergence: Early March in temperate zones; later in colder areas.

**Bud break:**
- Observe trees and shrubs: Forsythia, pussy willow, oak, maple.
- Record date of first bud swell and first leaves unfurled.
- In temperate zones: Early bud break (March) = warm, early spring. Late bud break (May) = cold, late spring.

**Animal activity:**
- Frog/toad call start: Indicates soil warming; ~50°F soil temperature.
- Bird arrival: Migratory birds return when food (insects, buds) become available. Early arrival = early spring.
- Butterfly/insect emergence: Monarchs, mourning cloaks, and other overwintering butterflies emerge when daylength and temperature permit.

### Summer Indicators

- Maximum daylight hours (June 21 solstice): Length varies by latitude.
- Peak bloom of summer wildflowers (black-eyed Susans, daisies, coneflowers).
- Insect peak activity (dragonflies, bees, grasshoppers abundant).

### Fall Indicators

- First frost date: Observable as a light frost on grass and leaves. Record the date.
- Leaf color change: Tree species change color sequentially; record dates.
- Seed maturation: Observe when seeds on native plants mature and fall.

**Example phenological record:**

| **Event** | **Date (Year 1)** | **Date (Year 2)** | **Difference** | **Notes** |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|----------------|----------|
| Bloodroot bloom | March 18 | March 10 | 8 days earlier | Year 2 was warmer, earlier spring |
| Oak bud break | April 22 | May 2 | 10 days later | Year 2 was colder, later spring |
| Frog calls | April 5 | March 28 | 8 days earlier | Soil warmed earlier in Year 2 |
| First frost | October 12 | October 5 | 7 days earlier | Year 2 had earlier fall |
| Frost-free days | ~175 | ~160 | — | Year 2 was shorter season |

:::info-box
Track phenological events for 2–3 years to establish local patterns. This local knowledge is more useful than any USDA hardiness zone map because it captures your exact site conditions.
:::

## Creating or Modifying Microclimates

Once you identify microclimates, you can modify them to extend growing seasons and improve crop resilience.

### Cold Frame / Row Cover Extension

**Early spring warmth:**
- Use transparent plastic covers (row covers, cloches, cold frames) over beds in early spring.
- Temperature under plastic rises 5–15°F compared to open air.
- Extend planting date by 2–4 weeks.

**Construction:**
- Simple: Lay clear plastic over soil on a cold frame (wooden box with transparent top).
- Dynamic: Vent (open) the frame on warm days to prevent overheating; close at night to retain heat.

### Mulch for Heat Retention

- Apply dark mulch (compost, aged bark) to soil in spring.
- Dark mulch absorbs and radiates heat, raising soil temperature.
- Effect: Soil warms 5°F faster in spring; frost risk window shortens.

### Windbreak Establishment

- Windbreaks reduce wind speed 40–50% at distance 3× windbreak height.
- Plant trees/shrubs perpendicular to prevailing wind.
- Effect: Reduced water loss, warmer microclimate in lee, less plant damage.

### Water Feature for Temperature Moderation

- Ponds or large water containers moderate temperature: water cools slowly in fall, warms slowly in spring.
- Areas near water experience fewer extreme cold/heat events.
- Effect: Extends growing season by ~2 weeks near large water bodies.

### Wall/Reflective Surface for Heat

- South-facing walls absorb heat and radiate it to adjacent areas.
- Reflective surfaces (white plastic, aluminum) reflect sunlight onto tender plants.
- Effect: Tender perennials and late-season crops thrive against south-facing walls.

## Estimating Growing Zone Without Maps

Hardiness zones are standardized geographic regions based on average minimum winter temperature. Without internet access, estimate your zone locally.

### Average Minimum Temperature Method

**Record minimum temperatures** (using a thermometer placed outdoors):
1. Place a min/max thermometer outside (shaded, in a shelter).
2. Each morning, note the minimum temperature reached overnight.
3. Repeat daily for all of winter (November–March in temperate zones).
4. Calculate the average of the minimum temperatures recorded.

**Conversion to hardiness zone** (USDA Hardiness Zone):
- **Zone 1:** avg min <–50°F (–46°C)
- **Zone 2:** –40 to –50°F (–40 to –46°C)
- **Zone 3:** –30 to –40°F (–34 to –40°C)
- **Zone 4:** –20 to –30°F (–29 to –34°C)
- **Zone 5:** –10 to –20°F (–23 to –29°C)
- **Zone 6:** 0 to –10°F (–18 to –23°C)
- **Zone 7:** 10 to 0°F (–12 to –18°C)
- **Zone 8:** 20 to 10°F (–7 to –12°C)

Example: If your winter minimum averages –18°F, you are in Zone 5 (–10 to –20°F).

### Plant-Based Zone Estimation

Observe which perennials survive winter without protection:

| **If This Survives Winter Unprotected** | **Likely Zone** |
|----------------------------------------|-----------------|
| Tropical plants (banana, hibiscus) | 9–11 |
| Evergreen ferns, tender perennials | 8–9 |
| Many ornamental grasses, butterfly bush | 7–8 |
| Coneflowers, black-eyed Susans, lavender | 5–7 |
| Native trees (oak, maple, birch), lilac | 4–5 |
| Only the hardiest shrubs and very hardy perennials | 3–4 |

Plant your yard with species from your estimated zone; they will thrive without special winter protection.

## Creating a Microclimate Map

Integrate sun exposure, frost pockets, wind, aspect, and slope into a single property map.

**Steps:**
1. Sketch your property (to scale, e.g., 1 inch = 10 feet).
2. Mark north with an arrow.
3. Shade areas receiving full sun in summer (8+ hours) in yellow.
4. Shade areas receiving partial sun (4–8 hours) in orange.
5. Shade areas receiving shade (2–4 hours) in light blue.
6. Mark frost pockets with an "F."
7. Mark heat sinks with an "H."
8. Mark wind-exposed areas with arrow(s) showing wind direction.
9. Mark slopes: write percentage or angle.
10. Mark aspect: write compass direction (N, S, etc.).
11. Mark wet areas with "W."
12. Mark dry areas with "D."

**Example map labels:**
```
┌─────────────────────┐
│ PROPERTY MAP        │
│ ↑ = North          │
├─────────────────────┤
│  F (Frost pocket)  │
│ ┌─────────────────┐ │ FULL SUN (S-facing)
│ │ 15% slope,     │ │ → Wind
│ │ South-facing  │ │ PARTIAL SHADE
│ │ H (Heat sink) │ │ (N-facing, tree shade)
│ │ yellow shading│ │
│ └─────────────────┘ │
│  W                  │
│ (Wet area,         │
│  poor drainage)    │
└─────────────────────┘
```

**Use the map for:**
- Placing sun-loving crops (tomatoes, peppers, squash) in full-sun areas.
- Placing shade-tolerant crops (lettuce, spinach, herbs) in partial-shade areas.
- Placing frost-hardy crops in frost pockets.
- Placing tender perennials and late-season crops near heat sinks or south-facing walls.
- Placing windbreaks perpendicular to prevailing wind direction.

## Summary Checklist

1. **Map sun exposure** using hourly observation or a solar path finder.
2. **Identify frost pockets** by observing low areas and comparing spring bloom dates.
3. **Locate heat sinks** on south-facing slopes and dark surfaces.
4. **Mark wind-exposed areas** by observing tree shape and wind direction.
5. **Determine aspect** using a compass or solar observation.
6. **Measure slope** using rise and run, or angle with a level.
7. **Record phenological events** (bud break, first frost) for 2+ years to establish local timing.
8. **Estimate hardiness zone** from winter minimum temperatures or surviving plant species.
9. **Create a map** integrating all observations.
10. **Plan crop placement** based on microclimate conditions.

A simple property map with sun exposure, frost pockets, and wind patterns is one of the most valuable planning tools available, requiring no equipment beyond observation and a pencil.
