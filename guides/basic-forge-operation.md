:::danger
**Carbon Monoxide (CO) Poisoning Hazard:** Carbon monoxide (CO) is produced by incomplete combustion in forges. CO is odorless and colorless. Symptoms include headache, dizziness, nausea, confusion, and loss of consciousness. ALWAYS ensure adequate ventilation. Never operate a forge in an enclosed space. Install CO detectors if available. If anyone shows symptoms, move to fresh air immediately.
:::

A forge is simply a controlled fire hot enough to soften metal for working. You don't need expensive equipment to get started. This guide covers how to build a simple forge from available materials, manage the fire, and make your first pieces safely.

<section id="forge-safety-quickstart">

## Forge Safety Quickstart

Before lighting the forge, confirm five things:

1. **Ventilation:** Outdoor setup or forced draft that keeps carbon monoxide from building around the operator.
2. **Eye and face protection:** At minimum safety glasses; ideally goggles plus face shield during aggressive fire tending, grinding, or scale-shedding work.
3. **Clothing:** Natural fibers, leather gloves, closed boots, and no synthetic sleeves that can melt into burns.
4. **Fire perimeter:** Clear a ring around the forge so hot fuel, sparks, and dropped stock do not land in clutter.
5. **Hot-metal control:** Establish one place for hot stock, one place for tools, and one escape path if fuel or clinker spills.

If you cannot control those five items, delay the forging session. Most beginner forge injuries come from CO exposure, eye injury, clothing catching sparks, or stepping on dropped hot metal.

</section>

<section id="forge-basics">

## What a Forge Does

A forge heats metal to workable temperatures:
- **Red heat** (~500°C): Metal barely glows red, still very hard
- **Cherry red** (~750°C): Glowing red, becoming workable
- **Orange heat** (~900°C): Bright orange, much softer, easy to shape
- **Yellow heat** (~1000°C+): Yellow to white glow, metal is very plastic, almost flows

Different metals need different temperatures. Steel is workable from cherry red upward. Copper and brass work at lower temperatures. Cast iron is best worked from orange heat and higher.

**The forge's job:** Reach these temperatures and maintain them while you work.

<section id="simple-forge-designs">

## Simple Forge Designs (No Special Equipment)

### The Dirt Hole Forge

**Most basic option for outdoor work.**

**Materials needed:**
- Dirt/clay (for containment)
- Scrap metal (flat piece for hearth)
- Air source (hand pump, hair dryer, or leaf blower)
- Pipe (3–4 inches diameter) to deliver air from blower

**Construction:**
1. Dig a shallow hole in the ground (12 inches diameter, 6 inches deep)
2. Line the sides with clay or wet soil, packing firmly
3. Place flat scrap metal on the bottom (anvil or hearth)
4. Create or position a chimney: Build a frame around and above the hole using rocks or bricks, leaving a hole at the top for smoke
5. Run air pipe to the bottom of the hole, or insert a blower nozzle from the side
6. Feed fuel (charcoal or wood) into the hole around the air source

**Advantages:** Free or very cheap; easy to move or rebuild; works well

**Disadvantages:** Dirt can contaminate metal; takes practice to control temperature; smoke and ash everywhere

### The Brake Drum Forge

**Uses the drum from a car brake as the forge body.**

**Materials needed:**
- Brake drum (salvaged from car parts)
- Grating or metal mesh (for coal bed)
- Support frame (metal stand or bricks)
- Air source (hand pump or blower)
- Pipe to deliver air

**Construction:**
1. Place brake drum on its side, supported on a metal frame or bricks
2. Create an air inlet: Drill or cut a hole near the bottom of the drum
3. Insert pipe into the hole; attach blower to pipe
4. Place metal grating or mesh inside the drum above the air inlet (to support coal)
5. Build up or position a chimney extension at the top

**Advantages:** Creates better-contained heat; more controllable than dirt hole; good for small to medium work

**Disadvantages:** Requires some fabrication; brake drum can deteriorate with repeated heating

### The Blacksmith Forge (Coal-Powered)

**More serious option for dedicated metalworking.**

**Materials needed:**
- Metal box or basin (12 x 12 x 8 inches, roughly)
- Grating for coal bed
- Air supply system (foot bellows or hand pump)
- Pipe for air delivery
- Chimney/hood to vent smoke

**Construction:**
1. Fabricate or source a metal forge body (steel box or cast iron basin)
2. Create an air chamber below the grating
3. Position grating above the air chamber
4. Install bellows or pump, connected via pipe to the air chamber
5. Build chimney or hood to draw smoke upward and away
6. Insulate the box if possible (loose bricks, fireclay)

**Advantages:** Better heat control; more durable; professional setup; better draft

**Disadvantages:** More complex to build; requires bellows or pump; slower to heat up initially

<section id="fuels-and-ignition">

## Fuels and Fire Management

### Charcoal

**Best fuel for metalworking:** Burns hotter than wood, cleaner, and produces less ash.

**Where to get it:**
- Hardware stores (barbecue charcoal)
- Scrap dealers
- Make it yourself by heating wood in a low-oxygen environment (advanced)

**How to use:**
1. Start a small fire with kindling (dry wood shavings, paper)
2. Add chunks of charcoal gradually, building the fire
3. Use the air blower to increase heat and draft
4. Bank charcoal around the metal as it heats

**Properties:**
- Burns at ~700°C in open air; hotter in an enclosed forge with air supply
- Takes 10–15 minutes to reach working temperature for small pieces
- Produces carbon monoxide; always work outdoors or in well-ventilated spaces

### Coal

**Alternative to charcoal; requires access and processing.**

**Types:**
- Bituminous coal (soft coal): Burns hotter, produces smoke and clinker (ash)
- Anthracite (hard coal): Burns cleaner, hotter, less ash

**How to use:**
1. Break coal into fist-sized chunks
2. Start with charcoal fire underneath
3. Add coal gradually
4. Coal burns dirtier than charcoal; requires good ventilation and chimney draft

**Note:** Anthracite is better for metalworking but harder to find. Bituminous coal works but produces more clinker (hardened ash) that must be removed.

### Wood

**Less ideal but workable if charcoal unavailable.**

**Issues:**
- Burns cooler than charcoal
- Produces more ash and creosote (tarry residue)
- Requires larger fires to reach working temperatures
- Wood ash can contaminate metal

**Method:**
1. Use seasoned hardwood (oak, maple, ash)
2. Burn in quantities larger than charcoal for same heat
3. Keep fire clean of ash buildup

:::tip
Hardwoods burn hotter than softwoods. Pine and fir produce too much tar. Oak, maple, and hickory are better choices.
:::

<section id="temperature-control">

## Controlling Heat and Temperature

### Reading Color

Your eyes are the primary tool for judging temperature:

| Color | Temperature (°C) | Workability |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| Black body (no glow) | 400–500 | Very hard, barely workable |
| Dull red | 500–650 | Hard, requires force |
| Cherry red | 650–750 | Workable, good for bending |
| Bright cherry red | 750–850 | Very workable, good for shaping |
| Orange | 850–950 | Excellent workability, plastic-like |
| Yellow-orange | 950–1050 | Very plastic, risk of grain damage |
| Yellow-white | 1050°C+ | Danger of burning; grain damage certain |

**Practice:** Before you heat metal, learn to recognize these colors in a dim light. Step away from the forge opening to see the glowing metal better. Bright sunlight makes color judgment harder.

### Managing the Fire

**To increase heat:**
1. Increase blower speed (more air feeds the fire)
2. Add more fuel (charcoal)
3. Create a better air seal (less air escapes; more heats the metal)

**To decrease heat:**
1. Reduce blower speed
2. Remove fuel
3. Pull the metal away from the hottest part of the fire

**Common mistake:** Overheating the metal beyond orange. This burns the grain structure and weakens the metal. Yellow-white heat is dangerous; white heat risks permanent damage.

### The Drafting Effect

Heat rises. Good forge design creates draft—hot air and smoke are pulled up and out through a chimney or hood. This:
- Draws in fresh air at the coal bed (supplies oxygen)
- Creates efficient burning
- Removes smoke and fumes from your breathing area

**Improve draft:**
- Build the chimney higher
- Reduce leaks in the forge body (seal gaps)
- Clear ash and clinker buildup regularly

:::warning
Always work outdoors or in a space with excellent ventilation. Carbon monoxide from the fire is invisible and deadly. Never run a forge in a closed building without proper exhaust.
:::

<section id="working-technique">

## Basic Working Technique

### Setting Up for Work

1. **Start the fire:** Light kindling, add charcoal gradually, build to good heat
2. **Position the metal:** Place work in the hottest part of the fire, supported in the coal
3. **Don't bury completely:** Leave part of the metal exposed so you can see the color
4. **Take your time:** Small pieces take 5–10 minutes; larger pieces may take 20+ minutes

### Heating the Metal

1. Use the blower to increase heat when the metal is in the fire
2. Check color frequently by briefly lifting the metal out of the coal (use tongs)
3. Return to fire and continue heating until the desired color is reached
4. The metal is ready when it glows the target color throughout

**Common problem:** The surface heats while the interior is still cold. Patience is essential; better to overheat slightly than to work cold metal, which breaks or cracks.

### The First Strike

1. Remove the hot metal from the fire with tongs
2. Place it on the anvil or work surface
3. Strike decisively with the hammer
4. The metal should deform noticeably with each blow
5. If it resists, it's too cold; return to the forge

**Timing:** You have roughly 30–60 seconds of usable heat from the forge to the anvil. Work quickly but not recklessly.

### Building a Simple Piece: The S-Hook

**Your first project:** An S-shaped hook for hanging things.

**Steps:**
1. Start with a rod about 6 inches long and 1/4 inch diameter (scrap rebar or wrought iron)
2. Heat to cherry-red
3. Clamp one end in a vise
4. Bend the free end into a loop using a bending fork or by hand (slow curves)
5. Heat again to cherry-red
6. Flip and bend the other end into an opposing loop
7. Reheat to orange and shape the curves more precisely
8. Cool by air (let it sit); or quench in water if you want it hard

**What you learn:** How heat makes metal workable, how metal cools and hardens, how to make controlled bends.

<section id="annealing">

## Annealing (Restoring Workability)

As you hammer and bend metal at room temperature, it work-hardens and becomes brittle. Annealing resets the structure.

### Basic Annealing Process

**For steel:**
1. Heat the metal to a bright cherry red or just past
2. Remove from fire
3. Cool slowly (bury in ash, sand, or just air-cool)
4. Do NOT quench in water (causes cracking)
5. Once cool, the metal is soft and workable again

**Time:** Takes 30 minutes to an hour for the metal to cool naturally.

### Temperature Control for Annealing

**Steel:** Heat to bright cherry-red or just into orange, then cool slowly.

**Copper and brass:** Heat to a dull red, then cool slowly.

**Aluminum:** Heat until barely glowing, cool slowly. (Aluminum is difficult; safer to work cold if possible.)

### Signs of Successful Annealing

- Metal is easily scratched with a file
- Hammer blows deform the metal significantly
- No cracking when bent or shaped

### Signs of Failed Annealing

- Metal still resists deformation
- Reheated unevenly (some parts soft, some hard)
- Cooled too quickly (quenched)

Solution: Heat again, and cool more slowly.

:::tip
Annealing is not a perfect process when done simply. Some unevenness is normal. The goal is to make the metal workable again, not perfectly uniform.
:::

<section id="safety">

## Forge Safety

:::warning
**Hot Metal Rule:** Assume all metal in or near the forge is dangerously hot. Use tongs or long-handled tools. Never touch hot metal with bare hands.
:::

:::warning
**Protective Gear Required:**
- **Eye protection:** Dark glasses or a face shield (bright forge light is harsh)
- **Leather apron:** Protects from sparks and hot metal particles
- **Long sleeves and pants:** Denim or leather
- **Closed-toe shoes:** Never open sandals or bare feet
- **Work gloves:** Heat-resistant leather
- **Long hair:** Tie back securely; never let hair hang loose near the forge
:::

:::warning
**Ventilation:** Work outdoors or in spaces with good ventilation. Never run a forge in a sealed building. Carbon monoxide (CO) is produced by the fire and is invisible and deadly.
:::

:::warning
**Fire Safety:**
- Keep flammable materials (wood, cloth, fuel containers) away from the forge
- Have water nearby (bucket or hose) in case of accident
- Don't carry hot metal over flammable materials
- Clear the area around the forge of debris
:::

:::warning
**Quenching:** If you quench hot metal in water, the steam is extremely hot and can cause burns. Keep your face and hands away from the quench bucket. Quench slowly or carefully.
:::

<section id="troubleshooting">

## Common Problems and Solutions

| Problem | Likely Cause | Solution |
|---------|--------|----------|
| Metal not getting hot enough | Insufficient fuel; poor air supply; heat escaping | Add more charcoal; increase blower speed; check for leaks in forge body |
| Metal overheating (white glow) | Too much heat | Reduce blower speed; pull metal away from hottest part of fire |
| Fire goes out or won't light | Wet fuel; too much fuel choking fire; air blocked | Use dry charcoal; start small; clear air paths |
| Metal cracks when struck | Cold metal; too much force; brittle alloy | Ensure metal is at orange heat; use lighter blows; anneal frequently |
| Can't see metal color clearly | Sunlight too bright; forge opening blocked | Step away from bright sun; position forge with better light angle |
| Metal sticks to the forge floor | Burning; metal oxidizing and fusing | Don't leave metal in fire too long; rotate periodically; work with fresher fuel |

</section>

<section id="next-steps">

## Next Steps

After your first few projects:

1. **Practice annealing:** Learn to judge when to anneal and when you can continue working cold
2. **Try different metals:** Copper is more forgiving than steel; brass is stiffer
3. **Invest in tools:** An anvil, hammer, and tongs specifically designed for metalworking improve results significantly
4. **Study heat colors more:** Each metal has slightly different color ranges; mastery comes with experience
5. **Expand your repertoire:** Move from simple bends to curves, twists, and more complex shapes

The basics you've learned—heat control, timing, and the feel of workable metal—are the foundation of all metalworking. Everything else builds on these fundamentals.

## Salvaged Metal Identification

When working with scrap and salvaged materials, identifying the metal type is essential for determining workability, hardness, and appropriate temperatures. You often won't know what metal you're working with until you test it.

### Visual Inspection

**Color:**
- **Reddish/copperish:** Likely copper (very soft, easily worked), brass (harder, more yellow), or bronze (brown, containing tin)
- **Silver/shiny gray:** Could be aluminum (very light), steel (heavier), stainless steel (very shiny), or zinc-coated steel (powdery white coating)
- **Black/dark gray:** Likely steel or cast iron (both dark, but cast iron is heavier and more brittle)
- **Rough, pitted surface:** Cast iron (porous structure); steel (can be smooth or rough depending on age)

**Weight (for same-sized piece):**
- Very light: Aluminum
- Light: Copper or brass
- Heavy: Steel, cast iron, or lead (extremely heavy)
- Lead is recognizable—it's unusually heavy, soft, and toxic when heated/sanded

**Magnetic response:**
- Strong attraction to magnet: Iron or steel (ferrous metals)
- Weak or no attraction: Copper, brass, bronze, aluminum (non-ferrous metals)
- Stainless steel varies—some stainless types are slightly magnetic

### Spark Testing

Strike the metal against a grinding wheel or rough surface and observe the sparks. This is the most useful field test for distinguishing ferrous metals.

**Bright yellow sparks with lots of branching explosions:** High carbon steel or cast iron. Cast iron produces shorter, more prolific sparks; steel produces longer lines with distinct bursts.

**Fewer, duller sparks:** Low carbon steel or mild steel (good for forging).

**No sparks or very faint sparks:** Non-ferrous metals (copper, brass, aluminum). These don't spark because they're not iron-based.

**Red/orange sparks:** May indicate manganese steel or alloy steels—useful for tool-making but harder to forge.

**Practice:** Collect sample scraps of known metals and spark-test them to learn the pattern before working with unknown salvage.

### Color Test Under Heat

Heat a small scrap in your forge and observe how color progresses:

**Steel:** Progresses through distinct color stages—black, dull red, cherry red, orange, yellow, white. The progression is smooth and predictable.

**Cast iron:** Heats similarly to steel but is more brittle when hot. It shatters or breaks rather than bending.

**Copper:** Turns black when hot (oxidation), not bright red. It remains malleable at lower temperatures than steel.

**Brass:** Similar to copper—oxidizes to black or dark brown. Less workable than pure copper.

**Aluminum:** Oxidizes to white/gray when hot. Very difficult to work with forging; tends to melt unpredictably.

### Magnet Test Details

Carry a small magnet with you when salvaging. Test suspicious pieces:

**Ferrous metals (magnetic):** Steel, cast iron, wrought iron, magnetic stainless
**Non-ferrous (non-magnetic):** Copper, brass, bronze, aluminum, lead, most stainless steels

If a metal is magnetic and spark-tests show lots of sparks, it's likely cast iron. If it's magnetic with fewer, duller sparks, it's likely mild steel—excellent for forgework.

:::tip
When in doubt, test small scraps first. Heat a scrap in your forge, try to bend or shape it, and observe how it behaves. This real-world test beats guessing.
:::

## Hardening and Tempering Steel

Raw steel (as-forged) is relatively soft and won't hold a sharp edge or resist wear well. Hardening and tempering transform steel into a tool or blade that's both hard and resistant to breaking.

### The Hardening Process

**Hardening** makes steel extremely hard by rapid cooling (quenching) from a high temperature.

**Steps:**

1. **Heat the steel to critical temperature:** For carbon steel, this is bright cherry-red to orange heat (typically 750–900°C depending on carbon content). This is the "hardening temperature."

2. **Recognize the critical point:** The metal should glow uniformly this color throughout. Unevenness means uneven hardening.

3. **Quench immediately:** Remove from fire and plunge into quenching medium (see below). Speed is essential—delay allows cooling in air, which defeats hardening.

4. **Cool completely:** Leave in quenching medium until cool. Do NOT pull out early.

5. **Result:** The steel is now very hard but extremely brittle and prone to cracking.

:::warning
**Hardening Safety:**
- Quenching produces violent steam. Never put your hands near the quenching medium when immersing hot metal.
- Use long tongs and stand back.
- Very hot steam can cause severe burns.
- Never quench in sealed containers—pressure buildup is dangerous.
:::

### Quenching Media (Cooling Mediums)

**Water:** Fastest cooling, produces maximum hardness. Best for carbon steel and most tools. Cools so rapidly it can cause cracking in thicker pieces or high-carbon steel.

**Oil (mineral oil or specialized quench oil):** Slower than water, safer for delicate pieces and high-carbon steels. Produces slightly less hardness than water quench but better toughness.

**Saltwater:** Faster than oil, slower than fresh water. Used when water quench is too harsh.

**Air:** Slowest, used only for low-carbon steels or when other options aren't available. Produces soft steel—not true hardening.

**Recommendation for beginners:** Start with oil. It's more forgiving than water and reduces cracking risk.

### Understanding Heat Colors and Hardness

After quenching, the steel is hard but brittle. To make it less brittle, you must "temper" it by reheating to a lower temperature and cooling again.

**Color-based tempering:** The color of the steel as it reheats indicates temperature. These colors correspond to specific hardness levels:

| Color | Temperature (°C) | Hardness | Best Use |
|-------|---------|----------|----------|
| Pale yellow | 200–230 | Extremely hard | Engraving tools, scribers |
| Light yellow | 230–250 | Very hard | Chisels, punches, files |
| Straw yellow | 250–280 | Hard | Knives, shears, cutting edges |
| Deep yellow/brown | 280–300 | Hard-tough balance | General purpose tools, springs (light) |
| Purplish-brown | 300–330 | Tough | Springs, fine blades |
| Light purple | 330–370 | Very tough | Large springs, blades needing flexibility |
| Dark blue | 370–450 | Tough | Heavy springs, hinges |
| Light blue | 450–500 | Soft | Screw-drivers, low-stress parts |

**Temper by color:**

1. After hardening, the steel is black from oxidation and quenching
2. Polish off the black scale using rough cloth or a file to reveal shiny metal
3. Heat slowly using a small flame or hot surface
4. Watch the color progress from pale yellow upward
5. When the desired color appears (match against the table above), immediately quench to stop the tempering process
6. The steel is now hardened AND tempered—hard but not brittle

**Example:** A knife blade should temper to straw yellow or light brown for a good cutting edge. Heavy springs temper to dark blue for flexibility.

:::warning
**Tempering Mistakes:**
- **Over-tempering (too high temperature):** Steel becomes soft and won't hold an edge
- **Under-tempering (too low temperature):** Steel remains brittle and cracks easily
- **Uneven tempering:** Part of the blade is hard; part is soft—unpredictable performance
:::

### Case Hardening Techniques

Case hardening is a process where you harden only the outer "case" of the steel while keeping the core softer and tougher. This combines the advantages of both—a hard edge that resists wear and a tough interior that resists impact.

**Why case harden?**
- Mild steel (low carbon) cannot be hardened effectively by quenching alone
- You can turn cheap, easy-to-work mild steel into a tool with a hard surface
- The resulting tool is tougher than fully hardened steel—less likely to shatter

### Pack Hardening (Carburization)

The simplest case-hardening method for small-scale work.

**Materials:**
- Charcoal (activated charcoal is better, but barbecue charcoal works)
- Animal bone (optional but improves results)
- A sealed container (steel box, old brake drum, crucible)
- Mild steel part to harden

**Process:**

1. **Pack the work:** Surround the steel part completely with charcoal in the container. Mix activated charcoal with ground animal bone (2:1 charcoal to bone) for better results.

2. **Seal:** Close the container (lid, clay, or bricks). The goal is to prevent oxygen from burning away the charcoal.

3. **Heat:** Place the entire container in the forge at high temperature (bright orange heat, ~900°C). Maintain this temperature for 1–2 hours for thin pieces, 4–8 hours for thicker work.

4. **Soak:** The hotter the temperature and longer the soak, the deeper the carbon penetration into the steel. A thin case forms in 1 hour; a thick case requires several hours.

5. **Cool:** Remove the container and allow it to cool completely (many hours or overnight).

6. **Extract:** Open container and remove the hardened part. The surface is now carbonized (contains carbon).

7. **Quench and temper:** Heat the piece to orange heat and quench in oil or water. Then temper as described above.

**Result:** The surface is hard and holds an edge; the interior remains tougher and less brittle.

### Cementation (Historical Method)

An older, more elaborate case-hardening method used historically and still practiced by purists:

1. Layer mild steel pieces with bone dust and charcoal in a sealed steel box
2. Heat in the forge at high temperature for many hours (6–12 hours)
3. Carbon penetrates the surface layers
4. Cool in the container
5. Quench and temper

This produces extremely good case hardening but requires commitment and precise temperature control. It's slower than pack hardening and uses more fuel.

### Practical Limits

- **Maximum depth:** Case hardening typically produces a hard layer 0.5–2 mm thick, depending on time and temperature
- **Depth vs. time:** Longer soaking at high temperature produces deeper hardening
- **Mild steel only:** This works best on low-carbon steel (mild steel). Higher-carbon steel doesn't benefit as much

:::tip
For beginners, practice simple quench-and-temper hardening on carbon steel before attempting case hardening. The principles are the same, but case hardening demands longer cooking times and more control.
:::
