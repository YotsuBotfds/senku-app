<section id="overview">

## Overview

Fever is elevation of core body temperature above the normal set point (typically >38°C / 100.4°F), usually in response to infection or inflammation. Hydrotherapy—the therapeutic use of water—is a safe, low-cost, and effective method for temperature regulation in austere settings. This guide covers fever assessment, management, and water-based cooling techniques for both fever reduction and burn treatment.

**Important note:** Fever itself is usually a beneficial response that aids immune function. The goal of fever management is not always to eliminate fever, but to reduce suffering and prevent dangerous elevations (>41°C / 105.8°F). In some infections, aggressive fever reduction may prolong illness.

</section>

<section id="fever-physiology">

## Fever Physiology and Staging

### Normal Body Temperature

**Core temperature** (measured rectally, via esophageal probe, or tympanic membrane): 36.5–37.5°C (97.7–99.5°F)

**Oral temperature:** Usually 0.5°C lower than core
**Axillary (armpit) temperature:** Usually 0.5°C lower than oral
**Temporal artery (forehead):** Usually accurate to ±0.5°C

### Fever Staging

| Stage | Temperature | Description | Duration |
|-------|-------------|-------------|----------|
| **Prodrome** | Normal→38°C | Chill, shivering, vasoconstriction; patient feels cold despite rising temperature | 30 min–1 hour |
| **Plateau** | 38–40°C (or higher) | Temperature stable; patient feels hot; sweating begins | Hours to days |
| **Defervescence (crisis)** | 40°C→normal | Profuse sweating, vasodilation; patient feels hot and uncomfortable; temperature falls rapidly | 1–4 hours |

### Fever Patterns

**Continuous fever:** Temperature remains elevated without returning to normal (seen in pneumonia, typhoid)

**Remittent fever:** Temperature elevated but fluctuates >2°C daily (seen in TB, abscess)

**Intermittent fever:** Temperature normal at times, elevated at others (seen in malaria—cyclical, or sepsis)

**Hectic fever:** Wide swings (>2°C) within 24 hours (seen in severe infections, disseminated TB)

<section id="fever-assessment">

## Fever Assessment and Management Decisions

### When to Treat Fever

**Treat fever when:**
- Temperature >40.5°C (104.9°F) in adults or children (risk of febrile seizure in young children, risk of delirium in elderly)
- Patient is uncomfortable or distressed
- Underlying condition worsens with high fever (cardiac disease, heat sensitivity)
- Fever prevents sleep or rest

**Do NOT aggressively treat fever when:**
- Mild-to-moderate fever (<39.5°C) in immunocompetent host (may aid healing)
- Fever is part of normal immune response (e.g., first 24–48 hours of infection)
- Patient is otherwise stable and comfortable

### Fever Assessment Protocol

**History:**
- Onset: Acute (hours) vs insidious (days)?
- Associated symptoms: Cough, sore throat, rash, abdominal pain, diarrhea?
- Recent travel, sick contacts, immunization status?
- Current medications (some cause drug fever)?
- Pre-existing conditions (immunosuppression, chronic infection)?

**Exam:**
- **Vital signs:** Temperature (core if possible), heart rate, respiratory rate, blood pressure
- **General appearance:** Alert vs lethargic; well vs toxic
- **Skin:** Rash (viral exanthem, meningococcemia), warmth, diaphoresis
- **Lymph nodes:** Swollen?
- **Throat:** Erythema, exudate?
- **Chest:** Breath sounds, pneumonia signs?
- **Abdomen:** Tenderness, distension?
- **Meninges:** Neck stiffness (meningitis)?
- **Extremities:** Petechiae (meningococcemia, disseminated infection)?

### Danger Signs Warranting Urgent Evaluation

- Petechial or purpuric rash
- Altered mental status or delirium
- Severe headache with neck stiffness (meningitis)
- Difficulty breathing
- Severe abdominal pain
- Hypotension or signs of shock
- Focal neurologic signs
- Fever >41°C in any patient (heat stroke risk)

:::danger
If danger signs are present, focus on evacuation rather than fever-reduction measures. Treat the underlying cause, not just the fever.
:::

</section>

<section id="tepid-sponging">

## Tepid Sponging Protocol

Tepid sponging uses lukewarm water (27–32°C / 80–90°F) to promote evaporative cooling without causing shivering.

### Preparation

**Materials:**
- Basin or bucket of tepid water (test with elbow; should feel slightly cool but not cold)
- Soft cloths or sponges
- Large towel for wrapping
- Additional water for moistening cloths
- Thermometer for monitoring

**Patient preparation:**
- Undress completely or minimize clothing
- Position in warm room (avoid chilling)
- Ensure comfort and privacy

### Procedure

1. **Explain to patient:** Warn that initial contact will feel cold; sensation will improve as body adjusts.

2. **Start with extremities:** Soak cloth in tepid water; wring out excess; apply to forearms and legs using long, gentle strokes. This cools large blood vessels near the skin surface without causing shivering.

3. **Progress to trunk:** Apply tepid sponging to chest, back, and abdomen using same gentle strokes.

4. **Avoid rapid cooling:** Do NOT use ice water or ice packs initially (causes vasoconstriction, reduces cooling efficacy, triggers shivering, generates heat).

5. **Continue for 20–30 minutes:** Monitor core temperature; recheck every 5–10 minutes. Stop when temperature drops 1–2°C or patient begins to shiver.

6. **Dry gently:** Pat skin dry with soft towel; do not rub vigorously.

7. **Reposition:** Keep patient partially covered with light blanket to maintain comfort.

### Frequency

Repeat tepid sponging every 4–6 hours as needed, or when temperature rises again above the target threshold.

:::tip
Tepid sponging is most effective during the defervescence phase (when fever is "breaking" and patient is sweating). During the prodrome (when patient feels cold and shivering), focus on comfort measures instead.
:::

</section>

<section id="fever-management-ladder">

## Fever Management Ladder

### Step 1: Environmental Measures (All Fever)

- Maintain cool room temperature (20–22°C / 68–72°F if possible)
- Use light clothing and bedding
- Ensure adequate hydration (water, oral rehydration solution, electrolyte drinks)
- Small, frequent meals
- Rest

### Step 2: Oral Antipyretics (Fever 38–40°C)

**Acetaminophen (paracetamol):**
- Adults: 500–1000 mg every 4–6 hours (max 4000 mg/day)
- Children: 15 mg/kg every 4–6 hours (max 5 doses/day)
- Safe, effective; gentle GI profile

**Ibuprofen (NSAID):**
- Adults: 200–400 mg every 6–8 hours (max 3200 mg/day)
- Children: 10 mg/kg every 6–8 hours (max 40 mg/kg/day)
- Effective; slightly more anti-inflammatory than acetaminophen
- Caution: Avoid in dehydration, renal disease, or GI ulcers

**Aspirin:**
- Adults: 325–650 mg every 4–6 hours
- Effective; avoid in children <18 years (Reye syndrome risk) and in dengue (bleeding risk)

### Step 3: Tepid Sponging (Fever 39–40.5°C)

Combine with oral antipyretics for better effect.

### Step 4: Enhanced Cooling (Fever >40.5°C)

If fever does not respond to oral antipyretics + tepid sponging:

**Ice packs to groin and axillae:**
- Apply after initial tepid sponging lowers temperature somewhat
- Wrap ice in cloth (do not apply directly to skin)
- Apply for 20–30 minutes; remove if patient is shivering
- Groin and axillae have large blood vessels; cooling here is more efficient

**Fans and air circulation:** Enhance evaporative cooling

**Cool compresses:** To forehead and neck

**Immersion in cooler water (32–37°C):** If patient tolerates; cooler than tepid sponging but warmer than ice bath

:::warning
Avoid extreme cooling measures (ice baths, ice packs directly on skin) unless temperature is >41°C or unresponsive to moderate measures. Overly aggressive cooling causes discomfort, vasoconstriction (paradoxically reducing cooling), and shivering (heat generation).
:::

</section>

<section id="alternative-cooling-methods">

## Alternative Cooling Methods (Austere Conditions)

When antipyretics or water are limited, alternative cooling techniques can effectively reduce fever. These methods work through different mechanisms: evaporative cooling, convection, and conduction.

### Evaporative Cooling Techniques

Evaporation is nature's most efficient cooling mechanism. Sweat that evaporates cools the body dramatically; several techniques enhance this process without requiring ice or flowing water.

**Fan-assisted cooling:**
- Fanning the patient increases air circulation around the body, accelerating evaporation from skin and sweat
- Manual fanning (using cloth, palm leaves, cardboard): Fan the patient for 15-30 minutes, focusing on face, neck, and trunk
- Improvised fans: Create from cloth, branches, or cardboard
- Effectiveness: Can lower temperature 0.5-1°C in 30 minutes without water consumption
- Timing: Most effective during defervescence phase (when patient is actively sweating)

**Wet cloth + fan combination:**
- Drape damp cloth over patient (arms, legs, trunk—not covering face)
- Fan the wet cloth to accelerate evaporation
- Wring out cloth as it dries and re-dampen
- Effectiveness: Combines direct evaporation + fanning; very efficient
- Requires: Small amount of water (just enough to dampen cloths repeatedly)

**Air circulation:**
- Position patient in open air if safe (not in direct sun)
- Create natural breeze by opening shelters, positioning patient near ventilation
- Allow air to circulate around patient without direct wind chill (which causes discomfort)

### Cool Compress Protocols with Timing

Cool compresses work through direct contact cooling (conduction) and evaporative cooling. Specific timing optimizes effectiveness.

**Protocol 1: Rotating cool compresses (2 cloths)**
1. Soak two cloths in available water (temperature 20-30°C; cool but not cold)
2. Apply one cloth to patient's forehead, neck, or armpits for 10-15 minutes
3. While first cloth is applied, wring out and re-cool the second cloth
4. Swap cloths every 10-15 minutes
5. Continue for 30-60 minutes
6. Frequency: Every 4-6 hours as needed

**Protocol 2: Strategic high-vascularity areas**
- Apply compresses specifically to areas with large blood vessels near skin surface:
  - Forehead (temporal artery)
  - Neck (carotid artery)
  - Armpits (axillary artery)
  - Groin (femoral artery)
  - Inner wrists (radial artery)
- Cooling these areas cools circulating blood, rapidly lowering core temperature
- Duration: 5-10 minutes per area; rotate areas to avoid local cold injury
- Effectiveness: 0.5-1°C temperature reduction per 30 minutes of strategic cooling

**Protocol 3: Ongoing compress replacement**
- For moderate fevers (38-39.5°C): Use one cool compress, replace as it warms (typically every 15-20 minutes)
- For higher fevers (>40°C): Use multiple compresses simultaneously to different areas, rotating continuously
- Combine with other methods (fanning, hydration) for additive effect

### Tepid Sponge Bath Procedures (Review & Expansion)

The tepid sponge bath is one of the most effective non-medication cooling techniques. Specific timing and technique optimize results.

**Preparation (critical):**
- Room temperature: Keep room cool (20-22°C if possible) to allow evaporation
- Water temperature: 27-32°C (80-90°F) — test on your inner elbow; should feel slightly cool but not cold
- Duration: Allow 20-40 minutes total for significant cooling

**Systematic sponging sequence:**
1. **Explain to patient:** Warn that initial contact will feel cool; sensation normalizes
2. **Start distally (extremities first):** Apply wet cloth to forearms and hands, using long gentle strokes (not vigorous rubbing)
3. **Progress to legs:** Sponge the lower legs and feet, again using long gentle strokes
4. **Move to trunk:** Once extremities are warmed to cool (patient adjusts), sponge chest, back, and abdomen
5. **Avoid rapid transitions:** Do not move quickly from extremity to trunk; gradual progression prevents shivering
6. **Allow evaporation time:** Between applications, let wet skin air-dry slightly; this evaporation cools more effectively than leaving skin dripping
7. **Monitor patient response:** If shivering develops, slow the process; shivering generates heat, defeating the purpose

**Frequency of application:**
- Repeat every 4-6 hours if fever remains above target threshold
- Most effective during fever "breaking" (defervescence phase) when patient is naturally sweating

**Enhancement techniques:**
- Combine sponging with fanning for additive evaporative effect
- Apply cool compresses to high-vascularity areas (neck, armpits) while sponging extremities
- Sponge while patient drinks cool water (if available) for additional cooling

### Internal Cooling (Cool Fluid Intake Rates)

Drinking cool water or fluids lowers core temperature from inside. Specific protocols optimize this effect without causing digestive upset.

**Cool water intake guidelines:**
- Temperature: 10-20°C (cool but not ice-cold; ice water can cause cramping)
- Quantity: Frequent small sips (50-100mL / ~2 ounces per sip) rather than large gulps
- Frequency: Every 15-30 minutes for fever >39.5°C; every 30-60 minutes for moderate fever
- Total goal: 200-300mL per hour during active cooling attempt
- Duration: Continue for 2-4 hours during peak fever reduction window

**Fluid options (in order of cooling effectiveness):**
1. **Plain cool water** — Most available, effective
2. **Cool electrolyte solution** (water + salt + sugar; WHO ORS) — Rehydrates while cooling
3. **Cool herbal tea** (cooled chamomile, peppermint) — Provides medicinal benefit while cooling
4. **Cool broths** (salt-containing) — Provides minerals and calories, supports hydration
5. **Avoid:** Hot or warm fluids (increase fever), sugar-only drinks (can increase thirst paradoxically)

**Timing relative to other methods:**
- Combine cool fluid intake with external cooling (sponging, fanning) for additive effect
- Space cool water intake 15-30 minutes before/after eating to avoid digestive upset
- If patient nauseous or vomiting: offer sips of cooler fluid (reduces nausea better than warm)

### Environmental Cooling (Shade, Ventilation, Positioning)

Passive environmental modifications cool the patient without active intervention.

**Shade and sun protection:**
- Position patient out of direct sunlight (reduces radiant heat load)
- If outdoors, create shade using cloth, branches, or natural shelter
- Effect: Reduces ambient temperature sensation by 5-10°C equivalent

**Ventilation optimization:**
- Position patient in naturally ventilated area (open to breeze if safe)
- Create cross-ventilation by opening opposite sides of shelter
- Use natural air movement to enhance evaporative cooling
- Avoid enclosed, stagnant areas (which trap heat and humidity)

**Elevation and positioning:**
- Elevate patient's head (15-30° angle) to improve air circulation around trunk
- Position on back or side (not face-down) for heat dissipation
- Allow air to circulate under patient (avoid lying directly on impermeable surface)
- Use minimal, loose bedding (cotton preferred; breathable)

**Water proximity:**
- If near water source (river, lake, stream): Position patient nearby
- Proximity to water (even if not in water) reduces ambient temperature perception and provides psychological comfort
- Occasional wading or immersion in cool water is very effective if patient tolerates

### Cooling Method Selection Based on Fever Severity

**Mild fever (38-38.5°C):**
- Environmental cooling (shade, ventilation)
- Hydration (cool fluids)
- Minimal intervention; fever aids immune response

**Moderate fever (38.5-40°C):**
- Cool compresses to high-vascularity areas
- Tepid sponging (20-minute session)
- Hydration with electrolyte solution
- Fan-assisted evaporative cooling
- Frequency: Repeat every 4-6 hours as needed

**High fever (40-40.5°C):**
- Aggressive tepid sponging (30-40 minutes)
- Cool compresses to multiple areas simultaneously
- Cool fluid intake (frequent small sips)
- Fanning + wet cloth combination
- Strategic immersion (cool bath, 10-20 minutes) if available
- Frequency: Repeat every 2-4 hours; monitor closely

**Very high fever (>40.5°C; heat stroke risk):**
- Immersion cooling (cool bath/stream if available; 32-37°C water)
- Ice packs or cold compresses to groin, armpits, neck (after initial tepid cooling)
- Aggressive hydration (cool fluids, frequent intake)
- Fan with wet compresses on all high-vascularity areas
- Seek urgent medical care if available
- Frequency: Continuous cooling; monitor vital signs closely; watch for shivering (stop if shivering develops; indicates excessive cooling)

:::info-box
**Key principle:** Multiple cooling methods combine for additive effect. A patient with fever >40°C benefits more from tepid sponging + fanning + cool compresses + hydration (all simultaneously) than from any single method alone. Layer methods and rotate between them to maintain patient comfort while achieving temperature reduction.
:::

## Burn Cooling with Water Temperature Control

Burns require rapid cooling to stop ongoing tissue damage. The cooling method depends on burn size and depth.

### Cooling Principles

- **Water temperature:** 15–25°C (59–77°F) is ideal. Cold water is more effective but causes more pain; lukewarm is tolerable.
- **Duration:** Cool for 10–20 minutes, or until pain is controlled
- **Method:** Immersion or running water preferred over ice packs (more even cooling, less pain)

### Procedure: Partial Immersion or Running Water

1. **Submerge or irrigate the burned area** with cool running water (tap water is acceptable if clean; filtered or boiled if contaminated water).

2. **Continue for 10–20 minutes,** or until pain decreases significantly.

3. **Monitor for hypothermia:** If burn is extensive (>15% body surface), prolonged cooling can cause body temperature to drop. Stop if patient develops shivering or feels very cold.

4. **Pain management:** Oral analgesic (ibuprofen, acetaminophen) or opioid if available.

5. **Cover with clean cloth** after cooling to prevent contamination.

### Procedure: Ice Pack Cooling (For Small, Superficial Burns)

- Apply ice pack (wrapped in cloth) to burned area for 10–15 minutes
- Effective for small burns (fingertips, small areas)
- Less pain than water immersion for small burns
- Risk of ice burn if applied directly to skin

### Procedure: Immersion Cooling (For Hands, Feet)

If feasible, submerse burned hand or foot in cool water for 10–20 minutes.

:::warning
Do NOT use excessive cooling (ice baths, very cold water) for large burns—risk of hypothermia is significant.
:::

### What NOT to Do After Cooling

- Do not apply ice directly to skin (causes ice burn)
- Do not continue cooling after pain is controlled (risk of hypothermia)
- Do not apply butter, oil, or ointments (traps heat; increases infection risk)
- Do not break blisters (barrier to infection)

<section id="contrast-therapy">

## Contrast Therapy for Inflammation

Alternating hot and cold water (contrast therapy) may reduce inflammation and promote healing in non-acute injuries.

### Principle

- Cold causes vasoconstriction (reduces swelling)
- Heat causes vasodilation (increases blood flow and healing)
- Alternating creates a "pumping" effect, removing inflammatory mediators

### Procedure: Contrast Bath for Extremities

1. Prepare two tubs or basins:
   - **Hot water:** 40–45°C (104–113°F) (test with elbow; should feel pleasantly warm)
   - **Cold water:** 12–18°C (54–64°F) (cold but not painful)

2. **Immerse affected extremity:**
   - Hot water: 3–4 minutes
   - Cold water: 1 minute
   - Repeat cycle 4–5 times, ending with cold

3. **Duration:** Total 15–20 minutes

4. **Frequency:** Once daily, or 2–3 times weekly

5. **Dry and rest:** After contrast bath, rest the extremity

### Indications

- Chronic inflammation (joint pain, not acute)
- Poorly healing wounds (after acute phase)
- Muscle soreness
- Circulation improvement in diabetic neuropathy

### Contraindications

- Acute swelling (first 24–48 hours; use cold only)
- Open wounds or infections
- Decreased sensation (risk of burn from hot water)
- Severe peripheral vascular disease

## Delirium Prevention in High Fever

Fever >40.5°C can cause delirium (acute confusion, hallucinations, disorientation), especially in elderly, very young, or immunocompromised patients.

### Signs of Fever Delirium

- Confusion or disorientation
- Visual or auditory hallucinations
- Agitation or combativeness
- Incoherence
- Loss of consciousness

### Prevention and Management

**Cooling measures (primary):** Aggressive cooling (tepid sponging, ice packs, immersion) to reduce fever quickly is the most effective prevention.

**Environmental measures:**
- Quiet, calm environment
- Soft lighting (avoid bright lights)
- Familiar faces nearby
- Avoid unnecessary stimulation
- Consistent caregiver(s)

**Fluid and electrolyte management:**
- Ensure adequate hydration (fever increases fluid loss via sweating)
- Monitor for dehydration (dry mucous membranes, decreased urine output)
- Oral rehydration solution or IV fluids if necessary

**Medications (adjunctive):**
- **Benzodiazepines:** Lorazepam 1–2 mg every 4–6 hours for severe agitation (helps protect patient from self-harm)
- **Antipsychotics (if available):** Haloperidol 5 mg IM/IV for severe hallucinations (use with caution; can lower seizure threshold)

**Seizure precautions:**
- Padded side rails or cushions
- Keep airway adjuncts nearby
- Benzodiazepines at bedside for rapid administration if seizure occurs
- Monitor closely during peak fever

### Febrile Seizure in Children

Febrile seizures occur in young children (6 months – 5 years) during high fever, especially if fever rises rapidly. Most are brief (<5 minutes), single episodes with good prognosis.

**Management:**
1. Protect from injury (soft surface, move away from hard objects)
2. Time the seizure; do not restrain
3. Turn to side to prevent aspiration
4. Ensure airway is open
5. **Lorazepam or diazepam IV/IM** if seizure lasts >5 minutes
6. **Aggressive fever reduction** to prevent recurrence

See Pediatric Emergency Medicine guide for detailed febrile seizure management.

## Hydration and Nutritional Support During Fever

Fever increases metabolic rate; combined with fluid loss via sweating, dehydration develops rapidly.

### Fluid Requirements

**Increased needs during fever:**
- Baseline + 10–15% increase per °C above 37°C
- For example, at 40°C (3°C elevation), increase fluids by 30–45%

**Oral rehydration solution (ORS):**
- WHO standard: Sodium chloride 75 mmol/L, glucose 75 mmol/L (simplification: 1 liter water + 6 teaspoons sugar + ½ teaspoon salt)
- Offers both hydration and electrolytes
- Small, frequent sips (prevents vomiting)

**Other fluids:**
- Water
- Broths
- Herbal teas (cooled or lukewarm)
- Coconut water (if available; electrolyte-rich)
- Diluted fruit juices (not concentrated; high sugar can worsen diarrhea)

### Nutrition

- **Soft, easily digestible foods:** Rice, bread, soup, boiled vegetables
- **Protein:** Eggs, yogurt, lean meat (supports immune function)
- **Avoid:** Spicy, greasy, or heavy foods (may worsen nausea)
- **Vitamin C:** Citrus, leafy greens (supports immune system)
- **Small, frequent meals:** Easier to tolerate than large meals

<section id="monitoring">

## Monitoring and When to Escalate

### Daily Monitoring Parameters

**Temperature:**
- Record every 2–6 hours
- Note time of peak temperature
- Track trend (improving vs worsening)

**Vital signs:**
- Heart rate (tachycardia common with fever; expect ~10 bpm increase per °C)
- Respiratory rate (tachypnea also expected)
- Blood pressure (may be low if dehydrated)

**Hydration status:**
- Urine color (dark = dehydrated)
- Mucous membranes (moist vs dry)
- Capillary refill (normal <2 seconds)
- Skin turgor

**Mental status:**
- Alertness, orientation
- Any delirium, confusion, or behavioral change

**Associated symptoms:**
- Cough, sputum (respiratory infection)
- Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea (GI infection or dehydration)
- Rash (viral exanthem, meningitis)
- Joint pain, muscle pain (viral syndrome)

### Escalation Criteria (Seek Professional Help)

- Fever >41°C refractory to cooling
- Delirium, confusion, or severe agitation
- Loss of consciousness
- Severe headache with neck stiffness (meningitis)
- Petechial or purpuric rash
- Difficulty breathing
- Severe abdominal pain
- Persistent vomiting (risk of dehydration)
- Fever lasting >7 days without improvement
- Very young infant (< 3 months) with any fever

<section id="answer-card-safe-boundary">

## Reviewed Safety Boundary for Fever Support

This guide supports stable fever comfort and supportive care only after emergency signs are excluded.

For routine fever care, use light clothing, a cooler room, small frequent fluids, rest in a calm setting, tepid sponging/cool compresses, and regular temperature checks.

Avoid routine-use escalation: do not add medication dose schedules, do not use aggressive cooling, and do not apply this guide as the primary route for newborn fever, confusion or neurologic deterioration, suspected meningitis/sepsis, heat stroke, poisoning suspicion, or major-burn management.

Stop active cooling if shivering, new confusion, worsening breathing trouble, persistent vomiting, or inability to keep fluids occurs.

Hand off to newborn fever and pediatric emergency care, sepsis/meningitis pathways, thermal-injury/heat illness care, poisoning guidance, burn-treatment support, or emergency care when those concerns are present.

</section>

<section id="summary">

## Summary

Fever is usually a beneficial immune response. Water-based cooling through tepid sponging, adequate hydration, and oral antipyretics manages fever effectively in austere settings. Aggressive cooling is reserved for temperatures >40.5°C, delirium, or febrile seizure risk. Burn cooling with appropriate water temperature prevents further tissue damage. Monitor for danger signs (delirium, meningitis, shock) and escalate as needed. Education and reassurance reduce fear; most fevers resolve with supportive care alone.
