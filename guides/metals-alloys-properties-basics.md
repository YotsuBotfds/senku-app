Metals are the foundation of tools, structures, and machines. Understanding their properties—how they behave under heat, stress, and corrosion—allows you to choose the right material for the job and work with it safely and effectively. This guide covers the most common metals you'll encounter in survival and practical skills contexts.

![Metal properties comparison chart](../assets/svgs/metals-alloys-properties-basics-1.svg)

<section id="what-are-metals">

## What Makes Metals Special

Metals differ fundamentally from wood, stone, and ceramics because of their atomic structure. Metal atoms are loosely bonded, creating free electrons that move through the material. This electron mobility explains why metals:

- **Conduct heat and electricity** — electrons carry thermal and electrical energy
- **Are malleable** — atoms can shift positions without breaking bonds
- **Are ductile** — can be drawn into wires without fracturing
- **Reflect light** — free electrons absorb and re-emit light, creating shine
- **Are strong and dense** — atoms pack tightly, creating weight and structural strength

:::info-box
**Malleability** = ability to be hammered, pressed, or rolled into shape
**Ductility** = ability to be stretched or drawn into thin wires
**Tensile strength** = resistance to being pulled apart
**Hardness** = resistance to scratching or deforming under pressure
:::

</section>

<section id="key-properties">

## Key Properties to Understand

When choosing a metal for a project, consider these characteristics:

### Melting Point

The temperature at which a metal becomes liquid. Affects both forging and joining:

- **High melting point (hard to melt):** Iron (2,800°F / 1,540°C), steel (2,400–2,800°F), copper (1,980°F / 1,083°C)
- **Medium melting point:** Aluminum (1,220°F / 660°C), brass (1,650°F / 900°C)
- **Low melting point (easy to melt):** Lead (621°F / 327°C), tin (450°F / 232°C), zinc (787°F / 420°C)

**Practical implication:** Higher melting point metals require hotter fires and more fuel to work. Lead and tin can be melted in a simple charcoal fire; iron requires a proper forge.

### Hardness vs Brittleness

Hard materials resist deformation but may crack under impact.

| Metal | Hardness | Brittleness | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| Iron (pure) | Low | Low | Forging, deformation |
| Steel (medium-carbon) | Medium-High | Medium | Tools, balanced strength |
| Cast iron | High | High | Heat-resistant, brittle if shocked |
| Copper | Low | Low | Shaping, electrical work |
| Aluminum | Medium | Low | Lightweight structures |
| Lead | Very low | Very low | Radiation shielding, weight |

:::warning
**Hard ≠ Strong.** A hard material (like cast iron) can shatter. A strong material (like steel) can deform without breaking. The best materials balance both.
:::

### Corrosion Resistance

How well metals resist rust and chemical attack:

- **Excellent:** Copper, tin, lead (stable in air)
- **Good:** Stainless steel (chromium content prevents rust)
- **Poor:** Iron and mild steel (rust readily without protection)
- **Very poor:** Aluminum (oxidizes, but oxide layer slows further corrosion)

**Practical tip:** In wet environments, copper or stainless steel are worth the expense. Carbon steel needs regular maintenance or protective coatings.

### Thermal Conductivity

How quickly metal conducts heat:

- **Excellent:** Copper, aluminum (used for pots, heat distribution)
- **Good:** Iron, steel (adequate for cooking, forging)
- **Poor:** Lead (insulator, used to shield heat/radiation)

### Electrical Conductivity

- **Excellent:** Copper (best conductor; primary for electrical work), aluminum (lighter alternative)
- **Good:** Iron, steel (adequate, but not ideal for fine electrical work)
- **Poor:** Lead, tin (insulators, not suitable for electrical paths)

<section id="ferrous-metals">

## Ferrous Metals (Iron-Based)

The most common and versatile metals for tools and structures.

### Pure Iron

**Properties:**
- Soft and malleable (easy to shape by forging)
- Low carbon content (<0.1%)
- Rusts readily (must be protected or finished)
- Weak compared to steel

**Uses:**
- Wrought iron — historically worked by blacksmiths (forge-welded, forged into shapes)
- Interior decorative work (gates, hardware) — relies on traditional forging technique

**Working:** Iron forges at moderate heat (1,500–1,800°F); can be hammered, bent, and welded. Doesn't harden with quenching.

### Wrought Iron (Historical Iron)

Hand-forged iron, impure but with low carbon content. Often contains slag (impurities), making it fibrous and tough.

**Modern availability:** Largely replaced by mild steel. True wrought iron is rare and expensive.

**Why it was valued:** Better corrosion resistance than mild steel (due to slag composition); strong, forgivable in bending.

### Cast Iron

**Production:** Iron melted, poured into molds. High carbon content (2–4%), other impurities present.

**Properties:**
- Hard and brittle (resists wear, shatters under shock)
- Excellent heat retention (retains temperature evenly)
- Cannot be forged (too brittle when hot)
- Rusts if wet; seasoning creates protective layer

**Uses:**
- Cookware (cast iron skillets, Dutch ovens)
- Machinery parts (not subject to shock)
- Engine blocks, decorative items

**Working:** Cast iron cannot be hammered hot. Machining, grinding, and filing are the only shaping methods. Seasoning (oil layer) protects from rust.

### Steel

Iron with carbon content 0.1–2%. Adding carbon dramatically changes properties.

**Properties:**
- Stronger than iron or cast iron
- Hardenable by quenching and tempering
- More brittle than pure iron but tougher than cast iron
- Can be forged, welded, and machined
- Rusts without protection

**Carbon content classification:**

| Type | Carbon % | Properties | Uses |
|---|---|---|---|
| Low (mild) | 0.1–0.3% | Soft, easy to forge, weldable | General structures, nails, wire |
| Medium | 0.3–0.6% | Balanced strength and forgework | Tools, springs, hand tools |
| High | 0.6–1.4% | Hard, less forgiving, slower to forge | Chisels, saw blades, files |
| Very High | 1.4–2% | Very hard, brittle, difficult to forge | Specialized tools, wear surfaces |

**Hardening:** Steel can be hardened by heating to color (depends on carbon content, typically 750–900°F for medium carbon) and quenching in water or oil. Creates a hard but brittle edge. Tempering (reheating to lower temp) restores some toughness.

### Stainless Steel

Steel with 10%+ chromium (and sometimes nickel). Chromium prevents rust by forming a protective oxide layer.

**Properties:**
- Excellent corrosion resistance
- Harder than mild steel, harder to forge and sharpen
- Expensive
- Stays bright without maintenance

**Uses:**
- Knives, utensils, cookware
- Marine and wet-environment hardware
- Medical and food-prep equipment

**Forging:** Stainless is harder to forge than mild steel; requires hotter fire and more careful handling. Can be brittle if quenched incorrectly.

:::tip
For survival tools, mild to medium-carbon steel is the practical choice: it's easy to work, inexpensive, and forgeable. Stainless is better if corrosion resistance is critical, but it's harder to sharpen and repair in field conditions.
:::

<section id="non-ferrous-metals">

## Non-Ferrous Metals (No Iron)

### Copper

**Properties:**
- Excellent electrical and thermal conductor
- Soft, very malleable (easy to hammer, shape, bend)
- Beautiful red/orange color (oxidizes to green patina)
- Excellent corrosion resistance
- Expensive compared to steel

**Uses:**
- Electrical wiring and connections
- Cookware (bottom of pots for heat distribution)
- Roofing (long-lasting, turns green)
- Plumbing (water lines, fittings)

**Working:** Copper can be annealed (heated until red-hot, then cooled) to soften it after hammering. Very forgeable; no hardening needed for structural strength.

### Brass

**Composition:** Copper + zinc (typically 60% copper, 40% zinc)

**Properties:**
- Harder than copper alone, but still workable
- More wear-resistant than copper
- Gold color
- Good corrosion resistance
- Machines well
- Not magnetic

**Uses:**
- Hardware (hinges, locks, fittings)
- Decorative items
- Bearings (low friction with steel shafts)
- Bullets (brass cartridge cases)

**Working:** Brass is more difficult to forge than copper but easier than steel. Can be annealed like copper. Machines and files well.

### Bronze

**Composition:** Copper + tin (typically 88% copper, 12% tin)

**Properties:**
- Harder and stronger than copper or brass
- Excellent corrosion resistance
- Beautiful gold-brown color
- Expensive (tin adds cost)
- Lower melting point than steel (easier to cast)

**Uses:**
- Bearings and bushings (low friction, strong)
- Statues and art
- Bells (sound quality)
- Structural components in marine environments

**Working:** Bronze is cast, not forged. Too brittle when hot for forging.

### Aluminum

**Properties:**
- Very light (1/3 density of steel)
- Good electrical and thermal conductor
- Weak compared to steel (aluminum must be thicker for equivalent strength)
- Excellent corrosion resistance (oxide layer forms instantly)
- Easy to machine
- Not magnetic
- Cannot be hardened by heat treatment (only by alloying and working)

**Uses:**
- Aircraft and vehicle parts
- Cookware and food containers
- Architectural cladding and roofing
- Electrical transmission lines (lighter than copper)

**Working:** Aluminum cannot be forged effectively (too weak when hot). Machining, casting, and extrusion are standard methods. Welding is possible but requires inert gas shielding.

### Lead

**Properties:**
- Very dense (heavy, despite soft appearance)
- Extremely soft and malleable
- Excellent corrosion resistance (white oxide coating)
- Very low melting point (easy to cast)
- **Toxic** (see safety section)

**Uses:**
- Radiation shielding (hospitals, nuclear facilities)
- Weights (fishing, counterweights)
- Solder (lead-tin mixture for joining)
- Historical paint and pottery glazes (now known to be toxic)

:::danger
**Lead is toxic.** Inhaling lead dust or fumes causes permanent neurological damage. Never:
- Melt or cut lead without respiratory protection
- Use lead-based paint
- Allow children to handle lead objects
- Dispose of lead in water supplies

See safety section below.
:::

### Tin

**Properties:**
- Soft and malleable
- Excellent corrosion resistance (doesn't rust)
- Low melting point
- Expensive

**Uses:**
- Solder (tin-lead alloy for joining metals)
- Coating steel (tin-plated steel cans)
- Alloy component (bronze, pewter, bearing metals)

**Working:** Tin is cast or melted; rarely forged due to low strength.

### Zinc

**Properties:**
- Brittle at room temperature
- Excellent corrosion resistance (sacrificial; oxidizes instead of underlying metal)
- Low melting point
- Used as coating on steel (galvanizing)

**Uses:**
- Galvanized steel (zinc-coated steel for corrosion resistance)
- Brass alloying
- Die-casting (combines zinc with aluminum, copper)

**Working:** Zinc is cast or used as a coating. Not forged or machined extensively.

:::warning
**Zinc fume fever:** Heating galvanized steel (zinc-coated steel) or zinc dust produces zinc oxide fumes. Inhaling causes flu-like symptoms (body aches, fever, nausea) lasting 24–48 hours. Not fatal, but disabling. Always avoid heating galvanized materials, especially indoors. See safety section.
:::

<section id="alloys">

## What Alloys Are and Why They Matter

An alloy is a metal combined with one or more other elements (metals or non-metals) to improve specific properties.

**Examples:**

- **Steel:** Iron + carbon (improved hardness, strength)
- **Brass:** Copper + zinc (harder, more wear-resistant than copper)
- **Bronze:** Copper + tin (strong, corrosion-resistant)
- **Solder:** Lead + tin (lower melting point for joining)
- **Stainless steel:** Iron + chromium + nickel (corrosion resistance)
- **Aluminum alloy:** Aluminum + copper, manganese, magnesium (higher strength)

**Why alloy?** Pure metals often lack the right balance of properties. Adding small amounts of other elements can:
- Increase strength without losing workability
- Improve corrosion resistance
- Lower melting point (for easier casting/joining)
- Improve hardness or wear resistance
- Adjust color or appearance

**Practical principle:** You rarely work with truly "pure" metals. Steel, brass, bronze, and stainless are all alloys. Understanding what's mixed in tells you how to work the material.

<section id="identifying-metals">

## Identifying Metals by Appearance and Testing

When salvaging or working with unknown metal, identification helps you choose the right technique.

### Visual Inspection

| Metal | Color | Surface | Magnetic |
|---|---|---|---|
| Iron / Steel | Gray-black | Dull, rusts easily | **Yes** |
| Cast iron | Gray-black | Rough, dark | **Yes** |
| Copper | Red-orange | Bright, oxidizes green | No |
| Brass | Yellow-gold | Bright, polishes well | No |
| Bronze | Gold-brown | Warm tone, duller than brass | No |
| Aluminum | Silver-gray | Light, thin oxide layer | No |
| Lead | Silver-gray, dull | Soft, scratches easily | No |
| Tin | Silver-white | Bright, easily scratched | No |
| Stainless steel | Gray-silver | Shiny, smooth | **Weakly** (some types) |

### Spark Test (Iron Content)

Strike the metal with a file or grinder in a dark space. Iron and steel produce bright sparks; non-ferrous metals do not.

- **Steel/iron:** Bright sparks, often branching
- **Stainless:** Few or no sparks
- **Copper/brass/aluminum:** No sparks
- **Lead/tin:** No sparks

### Weight and Density

Heft the object. Metals feel surprisingly heavy:

- **Very heavy:** Lead, cast iron
- **Heavy:** Steel, iron
- **Medium:** Brass, bronze, copper
- **Light:** Aluminum

### Magnet Test

Use a magnet (neodymium magnets work best):

- **Attracted:** Iron, steel, cast iron
- **Not attracted:** Copper, brass, bronze, aluminum, lead, tin, stainless steel (most)

### Hardness Test

Scratch with a file or knife:

- **Hard (resists scratching):** Steel, stainless, bronze, cast iron
- **Soft (scratches easily):** Copper, aluminum, lead, tin
- **Medium:** Brass

### Cutting Test

A file or hacksaw reveals:

- **Hard to cut:** Steel, stainless, cast iron (brittle; breaks)
- **Easy to cut, shiny chips:** Aluminum, copper, brass
- **Very easy to cut, soft:** Lead, tin

<section id="selection-guide">

## Practical Selection Guide: What Metal for What Job?

### For Hand Tools (Hammers, Chisels, Files, Saws)

**Choose:** Medium-carbon or high-carbon steel, hardened and tempered

**Why:** Strength + hardness + durability. Steel can be quenched and tempered to create a hard edge and strong body.

**Avoid:** Copper (too soft), cast iron (too brittle), aluminum (too weak)

### For Structural Components (Beams, Columns, Frames)

**Choose:** Mild steel or wrought iron

**Why:** High tensile strength, forgiving in bending, weldable. Can carry loads safely.

**Avoid:** Cast iron (brittle, unpredictable failure), aluminum (less strong, lighter but less ideal for heavy loads)

### For Cookware and Heat Transfer

**Choose:** Cast iron or copper-bottomed steel

**Why:** Cast iron retains heat evenly; copper distributes heat fast. Both resist corrosion from food.

**Avoid:** Lead (toxic in food), pure aluminum (reacts with acidic food; must be anodized)

### For Corrosion Resistance (Wet Environments, Marine)

**Choose:** Copper, brass, stainless steel, bronze

**Why:** Don't rust. Excellent for outdoor, wet, or saltwater use.

**Cost:** Higher, but justified by longevity.

### For Electrical Conductivity

**Choose:** Copper (best), aluminum (lighter, acceptable), brass (adequate)

**Why:** Free electrons in copper and aluminum conduct electricity with minimal resistance.

**Avoid:** Lead, tin, steel (poor conductors)

### For Low Cost and Availability

**Choose:** Mild steel, cast iron, copper (if not electronic use)

**Why:** Common, salvageable, inexpensive.

**Trade-off:** Requires maintenance (rust protection, seasoning)

### For Lightweight Structures

**Choose:** Aluminum

**Why:** Strength-to-weight ratio is excellent. Less load to support.

**Trade-off:** Requires more thickness for equivalent strength; machining required (no forging)

<section id="salvage-recycling">

## Salvaging and Recycling Metals

Scrap metal is a valuable resource. Learning to recognize, separate, and reuse metals extends material availability.

### Common Sources

- **Appliances:** Copper wiring, aluminum frames, steel cases
- **Old tools:** Steel blades, chisels, saws
- **Vehicle parts:** Steel, aluminum, brass, copper
- **Plumbing:** Copper pipes, brass fittings, lead solder (see safety)
- **Roofing:** Copper, zinc, aluminum flashing
- **Industrial waste:** Bearing metals, tool steel, specialty alloys

### Separation by Type

Use magnet + visual inspection:

1. **Ferrous (magnetic):** Steel, iron, cast iron — keep separate for later identification if needed
2. **Non-ferrous (no magnet):** Copper, brass, aluminum, lead, tin — separate by weight, color, spark test

### Storage and Safety

- **Keep dry:** Steel rusts if wet; store indoors or under cover
- **Keep organized:** Sort by type (copper, aluminum, steel) for easy identification later
- **Avoid mixing:** Especially keep lead separate (avoid contamination)
- **Protect from weather:** Cover piles to slow rust

<section id="safety">

## Safety When Handling Metals

### Cuts and Puncture Wounds

Metal edges are sharp and metal splinters are irritating.

**Prevention:**
- Use proper tools (files, saws, shears); don't improvise
- Wear gloves when handling sharp or jagged metal
- Smooth burrs after cutting with a file
- Never use metal scraps as cutting tools (unpredictable)

**Treatment:**
- Wash minor cuts and embedded-splinter skin injuries thoroughly; deep puncture wounds or retained metal need medical evaluation
- Soak in warm soapy water, remove visible debris with tweezers
- Apply antibiotic ointment and cover
- Watch for signs of infection (increasing pain, redness, swelling)

### Toxic Metal Exposures

#### Lead Poisoning

Lead exposure causes permanent neurological damage, especially in children.

**Sources:**
- Lead solder (joining pipes, electronics)
- Lead-based paint (especially old buildings)
- Lead fishing weights, shot, ammunition
- Lead shielding in old equipment
- Some ceramics and glazes

**Dangers of melting lead:**
- Burning lead produces lead oxide fumes
- Chronic exposure: cognitive damage, kidney problems, anemia
- Even acute exposure (single incident of melting indoors) can cause harm

**Prevention:**
- Never melt lead indoors; outdoor ventilation only
- Never inhale fumes from melting lead
- Wear dust mask or respirator if handling lead dust
- Wash hands before eating or drinking
- Assume old paint is lead-based; don't sand, burn, or heat it

**Disposal:** Lead is hazardous waste. Never discard in regular trash. Check local hazardous waste collection sites.

#### Zinc Fume Fever

Heating galvanized steel (zinc-coated) or zinc dust causes temporary but disabling illness.

**Symptoms (onset 4–12 hours after exposure):**
- Fever, chills, body aches
- Nausea, loss of appetite
- Cough, shortness of breath
- Headache, fatigue

**Duration:** Typically 24–48 hours; not fatal but very unpleasant

**Prevention:**
- Never weld, braze, or heat galvanized steel indoors (gases accumulate)
- Outdoor welding of galvanized steel is safer but still avoid prolonged exposure
- Remove zinc coating (grind, wire brush, or chemical stripping) before high-heat work
- Ventilation must be continuous and strong

**Treatment:** Rest, hydration; symptoms resolve without medical intervention

#### Cadmium (Rare but Serious)

Found in some plating, old batteries, some alloys.

**Dangers:** Acute exposure causes lung damage; chronic exposure damages kidneys

**Prevention:** Avoid heating or melting unknown metals that may contain cadmium. If unsure, don't heat it.

### Fume Protection

**For any heated metalwork:**
- Work outdoors or in well-ventilated areas
- If indoors, use powerful extraction fans that exhaust completely outside (not recirculating)
- Wear NIOSH-approved respiratory protection if fumes are visible or suspected
- P100 or R100 cartridges for most metal fumes

### Burn Prevention

Heated metal retains temperature long after removal from heat source.

**Prevention:**
- Always assume metal is hot unless you know otherwise
- Use tongs, pliers, or heat-resistant gloves
- Place hot metal on heat-resistant surface clearly marked as hot
- Allow cooling time; don't rush
- Never quench hot metal with water if you're holding it (steam burns)

### Eye and Skin Protection

**Grinding or cutting metal:**
- Wear safety glasses (metal splinters)
- Wear face shield for high-speed grinding
- Wear work apron or leather to protect clothing and skin

**Welding or brazing:** Use appropriate eye protection (covered in those guides)
