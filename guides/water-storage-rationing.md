## Overview

Water storage is foundational to post-collapse survival. Unlike food, which can be preserved for years, stored water degrades through microbial growth and chemical changes. This guide covers storage sizing, quality monitoring, rationing protocols, and minimum intake calculation for group sustainability. Tank cleaning and upkeep belong in Tank Maintenance.

Use this guide when the question starts with how long stored water will last, how much reserve to keep, how many liters or gallons per person per day, or how to cut use during drought or disruption. If the task is about collecting rain from a roof, routing gutters into barrels, first-flush devices, or overflow from active rain capture, go to [Rainwater Harvesting Systems](rainwater-harvesting-systems.html).

### Per-Person Per-Day Water Rationing Ladder

| Condition | Liters/Person/Day | Drinking | Cooking | Hygiene | Sustainable |
|-----------|-------------------|----------|---------|---------|-------------|
| Emergency (Level 4) | 1-2 L | 0.5 L min | 0.5 L | 0-0.5 L | 4-8 weeks max |
| Moderate (Level 3) | 3-4 L | 1-1.5 L | 1 L | 1-1.5 L | Months |
| Normal (Level 1-2) | 8-15 L | 2-3 L | 2-3 L | 4-9 L | Indefinite |

All figures are **per person per day**. Storage sizing uses separate calculations (see Water Storage System Design). Tank maintenance and sanitation procedures belong in [Tank Maintenance](water-storage-tank-maintenance.html), not rationing.

A household or small group should target **2-4 weeks of stored water** as baseline (during drought or supply disruption). Larger communities require monthly assessment and rotation systems.

:::warning
Stored water is vulnerable to contamination. Regular testing and rotation are essential. Contaminated water sources cause dysentery, typhoid, and cholera—leading causes of post-collapse mortality. Invest heavily in storage systems and monitoring.
:::

## Container Selection & Preparation

### Container Types & Suitability

| Container Type | Lifespan | Pros | Cons | Cost |
|----------------|----------|------|------|------|
| Food-grade plastic (5-55 gal) | 5-7 years | Durable, portable, UV protection | Leaching possible; odor | Low-Medium |
| Glass (bottles, carboys) | 20+ years | No leaching, inert | Heavy, fragile, limited size | Medium |
| Ceramic/earthenware | 10+ years | Traditional, inert | Porous (needs coating), fragile | Medium |
| Metal (sealed barrels) | 5-10 years | Durable, portable | Rust interior; coating fails | Low |
| Salvaged containers | 2-3 years | Free/cheap | Unknown history; risky | Minimal |

**Recommended:** Food-grade plastic containers (clearly marked as water-safe) or salvaged bottles (if sourced carefully)

### New Container Preparation

**For food-grade plastic:**
1. Rinse thoroughly with clean water (minimum 3 times)
2. Fill with water, add 1 tablespoon bleach per gallon, let sit 24 hours
3. Drain, rinse thoroughly with clean water
4. Air dry completely before filling
5. Store in cool, dark location (prevents UV degradation and algae growth)

**For salvaged containers:**
1. Scrub interior with brush and soapy water
2. Rinse thoroughly (multiple times)
3. Test by filling with small amount of water—assess smell and color
4. If odor persists or color is off, do not use (unknown contamination)
5. Fill and store as above

### Food-Grade Plastic Integrity Monitoring

- **Inspect monthly:** Look for cracks, clouding, or discoloration
- **Smell test:** Odor indicates bacterial growth or chemical breakdown
- **Taste test (after testing safety):** Off-taste indicates contamination or leaching
- **Weight check:** Significant weight loss indicates evaporation or leakage
- **Replace when:** Any structural failure, persistent odor, or discoloration >1 year of storage

:::info-box
Food-grade plastic degrades in sunlight (UV breaks down polymer chains). Storage in complete darkness extends lifespan 2-3 years. Bury containers partially or store in basements/cellars if possible.
:::

## Water Degradation Mechanisms

### Microbial Contamination During Storage

**Bacteria** (Bacillus, Clostridium, Pseudomonas):
- Thrive in stored water if oxygen present
- Initially slow growth (days-weeks), then exponential (months-years)
- Produce biofilm (slimy layer on container interior) protecting bacteria from disinfectants
- Prevention: Sealed containers, regular disinfection, rotation

**Algae:**
- Only grow with light exposure (require photosynthesis)
- Turn water green/brown; not directly harmful but indicate light exposure
- Prevention: Complete darkness, sealed containers

**Viruses:**
- Persist in stored water but do not multiply without host cells
- Generally decline over weeks-months
- Some (like poliovirus) remain viable for extended periods (>6 months)
- Prevention: Regular disinfection, source water treatment before storage

**Protozoa** (Giardia, Cryptosporidium):
- Very resistant to chlorine disinfection
- Cysts can survive months in cool, dark water
- Prevention: Initial filtration of source water before storage, UV treatment or boiling

### Chemical Degradation

**Dissolved minerals:**
- Hard water (high calcium/magnesium) does not degrade; stable indefinitely
- Soft water may absorb CO₂ from air, becoming slightly acidic

**Chlorine dissipation:**
- If chlorine was added for preservation, it decays over time (1-2% per month)
- After 6-12 months, chlorine protection may be insufficient
- Replenish by re-treating stored water periodically

**Plastic leaching:**
- BPA and phthalates (plasticizers) leach into water over years
- More rapid in warm conditions
- Risk is chronic exposure, not acute; not a primary emergency concern
- Minimize by storing in cool, dark location

## Water Storage System Design

### Household/Small Group System (2-4 weeks, 8-12 people)

**Storage requirement:** 1-2 gallons per person per day minimum
- For 10 people × 21 days × 1.5 gallons = 315 gallons (~1200 liters)

**Configuration:**
- 6-8 food-grade barrels (55 gallons each), stored in cool basement or buried partially in yard
- Plus 4-6 portable containers (5 gallons) for kitchen access and rapid refill capability
- Rotation schedule: Replace one barrel monthly (every 30 days)

**Installation:**
- Store on stable surface, not directly on concrete (promotes staining but not failure)
- Create labeled inventory (date filled, contents, rotation schedule)
- Position near rainwater collection point if possible (allows refill from precipitation)

### Community/Large Group System (1-3 months, 30-100+ people)

**Storage requirement:** 30-75 gallons per person (accounting for crops, livestock, sanitation)

**Configuration:**
- 10-20 large barrels (200+ gallons) or constructed cistern
- Gravity-fed distribution system (water flows from elevated storage to taps, no pumps needed)
- Regular testing schedule (weekly or bi-weekly for microbial contamination)

**Installation:**
- Elevated storage (4-6 m above distribution point) provides pressure without pumps
- Protective shelter (roof, but not fully enclosed, allowing inspections)
- Multiple input sources (rainwater, spring, well) reducing single-point failure
- Overflow drain to prevent overfilling and mosquito breeding

### Tank Construction from Available Materials

**Earthenware or clay:**
- Dig large pit (1-2 m deep, 2-3 m diameter)
- Line with clay (10-15 cm thickness), pack firmly
- Seal surface with bentonite clay (reduces permeability)
- Cover with wood frame + tarp to prevent contamination and algae
- Capacity: 10,000+ liters; minimal cost but labor-intensive

**Buried plastic:**
- Obtain large plastic drum or tank (from salvage or new)
- Dig pit slightly larger than tank
- Set tank in pit, backfill around edges
- Install inflow and outflow pipes with valves
- Cap with secure top
- Capacity: 100-5000 liters depending on tank size

**Masonry cistern:**
- Build walls from brick or stone, 1 m+ thick (structural integrity)
- Line interior with waterproof coating (clay, bentonite, or salvaged sealant)
- Construct roof from concrete or salvaged materials
- Install drainage, overflow, and inspection ports
- Capacity: 5000+ liters; durable, long-term storage

:::warning
Buried cisterns require careful maintenance (regular inspection for leaks, checking for contamination). If underground water is present (high water table), cistern may need special sealing or additional reinforcement against flotation.
:::

## Minimum Viable Water Intake by Conditions

### Baseline Calculation

**Daily baseline (per person):**
```
Drinking + cooking:    2-3 liters
Hygiene allocation:    2-4 liters
Washing allocation:    2-4 liters
TOTAL RATIONING BUDGET:6-8 liters per person per day
```

Reduce this if necessary, but understand the trade-offs:

### Austere Conditions (Emergency Rationing)

**Intake per person per day: 1-2 liters**
- Drinking: 0.5 liters minimum (prevents severe dehydration over weeks)
- Cooking: 0.5 liters (soups, grains require minimal water)
- Sanitation: 0-0.5 liters (dry cleaning, minimal hygiene)

**Consequences:**
- Dehydration develops slowly (weeks) but is serious once started
- Disease risk from poor hygiene increases dramatically
- Physical performance declines significantly
- Psychological impact on group morale (major hardship indicator)

**Timeframe:** Sustainable 4-8 weeks maximum before health consequences escalate

### Moderate Conditions (Mild Rationing)

**Intake per person per day: 3-4 liters**
- Drinking: 1-1.5 liters
- Cooking: 1 liter
- Sanitation: 1-1.5 liters

**Consequences:**
- Dehydration risk minimal if intake is consistent
- Hygiene remains adequate; disease risk acceptable
- Physical performance maintained
- Quality of life acceptable for extended periods (months-years)

**Timeframe:** Sustainable indefinitely if water supply is reliable

### Comfortable Conditions (Normal Usage)

**Intake per person per day: 8-15 liters**
- Drinking: 2-3 liters
- Cooking: 2-3 liters
- Sanitation: 4-9 liters (includes washing clothes, bathing)

**Requirements:**
- Abundant water source (spring, well, rainwater system)
- Capacity for storage of excess
- Ongoing purification system

## Rationing Schedules

### Alert Levels & Response

**Level 1 (Abundant - >4 weeks stored):**
- Full usage (bathing, washing clothes, cooling)
- No restrictions; normal consumption protocols

**Level 2 (Adequate - 2-4 weeks stored):**
- Begin rationing monitoring
- Reduce non-essential use (limit bathing to once weekly, minimal laundry)
- Maintain drinking + cooking at baseline
- Activate rainwater collection if not already operational

**Level 3 (Low - <2 weeks stored):**
- Implement formal rationing: 3-4 liters per person per day
- Allocate by role (heavy work, pregnant/nursing, sick get slightly more)
- Restrict bathing to necessary sanitation only
- All laundry becomes hand-washing in minimal water
- Increase frequency of source water collection efforts

**Level 4 (Critical - <1 week stored):**
- Emergency rationing: 1-2 liters per person per day
- Prioritize water for cooking and drinking; minimal hygiene
- All personnel mobilized to source water from emergency reserves
- Activate contingency water sources (rainwater harvesting, well drilling, stream collection)

### Daily Rationing Protocol (Level 3)

**Morning allocation (per person):**
- 0.5 liters drinking water (stored in personal container)
- 0.25 liters for food preparation (breakfast/lunch)
- 0.25 liters for sanitation (wash hands, face)

**Afternoon allocation:**
- 0.5 liters drinking water
- 0.25 liters for food preparation (dinner)
- 0.25 liters for sanitation

**Evening allocation:**
- 0.5 liters drinking water (or as needed for medical purposes)
- 0.5 liters for livestock/gardens if applicable

**Total: ~3.5 liters per person per day (adjusted for group priorities)**

### Enforcement & Fairness

- **Distribute by queue:** Daily allocation at central point (prevents hiding/hoarding)
- **Visible measurement:** Use calibrated containers (prevents disputes over quantity)
- **Designated distributor:** Trusted group member with no authority to modify allocations
- **Exception protocol:** Medical need, heavy labor, nursing mothers can petition for increased allocation (reviewed daily)
- **Accountability:** Track daily consumption against forecast (alerts if someone is using excess)

:::info-box
Perceived fairness is critical to group morale. Even if water is abundant, if rationing system seems unfair, conflict escalates. Be transparent about remaining storage, collection rates, and rationing calculations.
:::

## Water Quality Monitoring

### Visual & Odor Assessment (Daily)

- **Clarity:** Should be clear (not cloudy, green, brown, or yellowed)
- **Odor:** Should be odorless (any smell indicates contamination)
- **Taste:** Slight mineral taste acceptable; no off-tastes
- **Sediment:** No visible particles or sludge

If any concern: Do not drink. Treat by boiling or disinfection before use.

### Simple Testing Methods

**Chlorine test (if chlorine was added for preservation):**
- Test strips (if available): Should show 0.5-1.0 ppm chlorine
- If none detected: Water has lost disinfection; treat before drinking

**Turbidity test:**
- Fill clear glass with water
- Hold up to light
- Should be clear enough to read text through water
- If opaque: Contamination likely; filter and/or boil before use

**Microbial contamination (no-equipment method):**
- Visual biofilm (slimy coating on container interior)
- Unusual odor (sour, sulfurous)
- If suspected: Empty container, clean thoroughly, refill with fresh water

### Advanced Testing (if equipment available)

- **pH test strips:** Water should be 6.5-8.5 pH (neutral to slightly alkaline)
- **Bacterial culture (improvised):** Pour water sample into bowl, cover loosely, let sit 24 hours at room temp. Cloudiness or smell indicates bacterial growth.
- **TDS meter (Total Dissolved Solids):** If available, indicates mineral content (useful for identifying source water quality)

## Emergency Water Sourcing

### Rainwater Harvesting (Immediate - Hours)

**Collection area:**
- Roof (most efficient); area × annual rainfall = total collection
- Example: 100 m² roof × 0.5 m annual rainfall = 50,000 liters/year
- Gutters routed to cistern or barrel collection

**First-flush diverter:**
- Directs first 10-20 mm of rainfall away from storage (rinses roof contaminants)
- Can be as simple as a diverting hose manually moved when rain is clean
- Prevents initial dirt/debris from contaminating storage

**Pre-filtration:**
- Roof debris catches in simple mesh screen
- Water flows through cloth or sand filter before entering storage
- Removes leaves, insects, and large particles

### Spring or Stream Collection (Hours to Days)

**Source evaluation:**
- Clear, cold water indicates recent emergence (less microbial growth)
- Discolored water indicates suspended sediment or contamination
- Test for smell and taste before declaring safe

**Collection protocol:**
- Upstream of any settlement or agricultural runoff
- Collection at flowing water (not stagnant pools)
- Filter through cloth immediately after collection
- Boil or chemically treat before drinking

### Well Drilling (Days to Weeks)

**Shallow well (3-10 m deep):**
- Hand-dug or hand-augered in soft soil
- Most vulnerable to contamination (groundwater near surface)
- Requires careful casing and sealing

**Deep well (10-50 m):**
- Requires specialized drilling equipment (post-collapse may not be available)
- More resistant to surface contamination
- Higher likelihood of safe groundwater

**Minimal contamination risk:** Wells >10 m deep in areas without upstream agriculture/industry

### Fog/Mist Harvesting (Climate-Dependent)

- Mesh screen positioned to catch water-laden fog
- Gravity feed to collection basin below
- Useful in coastal or high-elevation areas with frequent fog
- Typically yields 1-10 liters per m² of mesh per day (highly variable)

## Group Water Management Protocols

### Communal Water Hygiene

**Rule 1: No shared cups**
- Each person has individual drinking vessel
- Reduces disease transmission (throat/mouth pathogens)
- Container must be washed daily

**Rule 2: No water stored in living areas**
- Reduces spoilage and contamination risk
- Central storage prevents individual hoarding
- Easier to monitor usage and quality

**Rule 3: Water handling responsibility**
- Designated water manager (2-3 people minimum) oversees storage, testing, distribution
- Trained in basic disinfection and monitoring
- Daily inspection of storage containers

**Rule 4: Boiling for uncertain sources**
- Any water collected from untested source is boiled before drinking/cooking
- Boiling for 1 minute at rolling boil kills most pathogens
- Extended boiling (10+ minutes) reduces some chemicals
