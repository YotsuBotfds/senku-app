## Overview

Settlements fail slowly when the layout is wrong. The first houses may look fine, but over time the same mistakes keep compounding: muddy paths, smoke blowing into sleeping areas, latrines too close to water, kitchens placed where carts cannot reach, workshops starting fires next to homes, and new families crowding into whatever space is left.

This guide is for the question behind all of those problems:

- how do we lay out a new settlement
- where should roads, water, and common buildings go
- how do we leave room for growth without rebuilding everything later

If the real complaint is about the water itself, such as cloudy, stale, smelly, oily, or otherwise suspicious water, use `questionable-water-assessment-clarification.md` instead.

It is not a deep surveying manual or a full construction manual. It is the planning guide that sits between them.

## Core Rule

Do not place buildings first and hope the infrastructure works itself out later.

Lay out the settlement in this order:

1. water and drainage
2. access routes
3. hazard buffers
4. common-use and dirty-use zones
5. household clusters
6. expansion space

If you reverse that order, the settlement will spend years correcting avoidable mistakes.

## 1. Start With Site Reality

Before choosing the final layout, walk the site in rain if possible and check:

- where water already flows
- where water stands after rain
- where winter wind hits hardest
- where smoke naturally drifts
- where carts, animals, and hand-carried loads can move without constant struggle
- where steep ground, unstable soil, flood channels, and falling-rock zones make building unsafe

The best-looking flat ground is not always the best ground. A slightly higher, drier site with reliable drainage is usually better than a low, convenient basin that stays wet.

### Do not build in these first-choice danger zones

- flood channels, stream banks, or obvious wash paths
- steep undercut slopes
- the bottom of a drainage bowl
- directly downhill from unstable rock or eroding banks
- immediately beside busy workshop, forge, kiln, or fuel-storage space
- directly upwind of latrine, refuse, or livestock concentration areas

## 2. Sketch the Settlement Skeleton First

Before assigning individual house spots, mark the settlement skeleton:

- main access road
- footpaths and cart turn areas
- water source, storage, and collection points
- sanitation and waste direction
- common work areas
- public trade space or market square, if the settlement will have one
- food storage and shared cooking areas if applicable
- protected future expansion area

If the question is phrased as "where should we put houses" or "where should the market go," this is the step to answer first. If it is "where should latrines go" or "where should animals stay," decide those dirty-use zones before placing homes.

When the market is a planned public space, reserve its zone here first, then hand off the stall spacing, circulation, notices, and loading details to `marketplace-trade-space-basics.md`.

When the clinic is a planned public space, reserve its zone here first, then hand off the patient flow, triage, waiting, and isolation details to `clinic-facility-basics.md`.

Think of this as drawing the bones before adding flesh.

### Minimum layout questions

Ask these before the first permanent build:

- where does clean water come from
- how does dirty water leave
- how do people move in bad weather
- where do carts turn around
- where do night soil, trash, and animal waste go
- what burns, smokes, smells, or attracts pests
- where can the next 10-20 households go without blocking roads or water

## 3. Separate Clean, Dirty, Quiet, and Hazardous Uses

One of the simplest ways to avoid long-term chaos is to separate incompatible uses early.

### Clean-use zone

Put these in the cleaner, drier, better-drained part of the settlement:

- homes
- sleeping shelters
- child spaces
- clinic or sick-care space
- school or teaching space
- protected food preparation and storage

### Dirty-use zone

Place these downwind and downhill when practical, while still allowing access:

- latrines and waste pits
- slaughter or butchering zones
- tannery, dye, or heavy chemical work
- refuse sorting
- manure concentration
- animal holding areas that stay muddy

### Heat and fire zone

Do not scatter high-heat work randomly among houses. Cluster and buffer:

- forge
- kiln
- charcoal or fuel processing
- large communal bake or cook areas
- ash disposal

Leave clear working space and simple firebreaks between these areas and sleeping structures.

## 4. Put Water and Sanitation Up Front

Water, drainage, and waste should shape the layout from day one.

### Practical ordering

- protect the water source first
- keep latrines and dirty runoff away from the source and away from the route that brings water into the settlement
- place washing, bathing, and kitchen wastewater where it can drain without turning main paths into mud
- avoid routing graywater through child play space or house entrances

### Simple settlement water rule

If clean water must travel through the dirty part of the settlement to reach homes, the plan is wrong.

### Simple sanitation rule

If rain can wash waste toward homes, wells, storage, or cooking space, the plan is wrong.

For detailed sanitation design, cross-check `sanitation-waste-management.md` and `latrine-sanitation-design.md`.

## 5. Plan Roads and Footpaths for Bad Weather, Not Good Weather

People usually plan with dry-season eyes. They imagine the easiest sunny-day path, then spend the wet season wading through mud.

Lay out access using the route that still works when:

- it is raining
- someone is carrying water
- someone is sick or injured
- a cart is loaded
- animals need to pass
- it is dark

### At minimum, provide

- one main route that stays passable in bad weather
- short secondary footpaths that do not cut through work hazards
- a turning area near storage, clinic, or common buildings
- drainage beside important paths if the ground holds water

If the main route becomes a stream every rain, fix the route before adding more buildings.

## 6. Build Household Clusters, Not Random Scatter

Randomly scattered homes waste walking time and complicate water, sanitation, and mutual aid.

Use loose clusters with:

- enough space for light, airflow, and fire separation
- easy path access
- room for a cooking area, small work area, and storage
- shared access to water without forcing long lines through private sleeping space

Do not pack the first shelters so tightly that later growth can only happen by filling every remaining gap.

## 7. Reserve Space for Common Functions Early

Even small settlements need shared-use areas.

Reserve space for:

- meeting or notice area
- storage or warehousing
- clinic or first-aid space
- teaching area
- market square or public trade edge, if regular trading is part of the settlement plan
- shared kitchen or food distribution if used
- repair yard or material staging

If these spaces are not reserved early, they get pushed into leftover corners where access, drainage, and safety are all worse.

For the internal market layout itself, use `marketplace-trade-space-basics.md` once the public trade zone is set.

## 8. Leave Expansion Corridors

Most settlements do not fail because they start too small. They fail because they grow without leaving space for roads, drainage, and shared services.

Leave obvious expansion corridors for:

- additional homes
- larger storage
- future workshops
- animal separation if herd size grows
- wider road access if trade volume grows

### Practical expansion rule

Do not use your best future road corridor as your temporary dump, garden fence line, or random shack strip.

Protect expansion lines on the map and on the ground.

## 9. Use a Simple Layout Checklist Before Building

Before approving a permanent structure, check:

| Question | Yes / No |
|---|---|
| Can water drain away from this spot naturally? |  |
| Can a person reach it at night or in mud? |  |
| Is it far enough from fire, waste, and heavy animal traffic? |  |
| Does it block future road or path widening? |  |
| Does it leave room for maintenance around the building? |  |
| Will smoke, noise, or smell create constant conflict nearby? |  |
| Does it crowd a future public-use area we will regret losing? |  |

If two or more answers are "no," stop and re-site the build unless there is a very strong reason not to.

## 10. First 30-Day Layout Sequence

If a new community is starting from raw ground, a practical early sequence is:

1. identify water source, drainage direction, and obvious hazard zones
2. mark one main access path or road line
3. mark clean-use zone and dirty-use zone
4. reserve common area for storage, meetings, and first aid
5. reserve public trade and clinic zones before filling leftover land with houses or sheds
6. place the first household cluster on the driest practical ground
7. reserve future expansion strips before they get occupied
8. document the layout in writing and on a simple map

Even a rough map is far better than relying on memory and verbal description after the site gets busy.

## 11. Common Mistakes

### Mistake: Building on the flattest ground without checking drainage

Consequence:
- standing water, rot, mud, mosquitoes, and repeated rework

### Mistake: Putting latrines or dirty runoff too close to where water is collected

Consequence:
- contamination risk and constant relocation work

### Mistake: Letting roads, footpaths, and animal movement emerge "naturally"

Consequence:
- the busiest routes cut through the wrong places and become hard to fix later

### Mistake: Using every open gap immediately

Consequence:
- no room for expansion, no fire separation, no access for carts or repairs

### Mistake: Mixing noisy, dirty, and hazardous work directly into sleeping zones

Consequence:
- conflict, fire risk, smell, injury exposure, and constant disruption

## 12. What Good Layout Looks Like

A good settlement layout usually feels boring:

- water moves where it should
- waste moves away from living space
- roads stay useful in bad weather
- homes are close enough for mutual help but not packed dangerously tight
- noisy or dirty work is kept where it belongs
- public trade space is easy to reach without clogging homes, drainage, or the main access road
- the next wave of growth already has somewhere to go

Boring is good. Boring means the settlement is not fighting itself.
