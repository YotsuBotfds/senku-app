## Document Recovery from Water Damage

Water damage is the leading cause of document loss. Fast intervention prevents permanent loss.

### Immediate Assessment (First 24 Hours)

**Wet or sodden papers:**
- Remove from wet environment immediately
- Do NOT wring or squeeze documents
- Do NOT attempt to separate stuck pages while wet
- Sort by material type (paper, leather, cloth, parchment)

**Priority hierarchy:**
1. **Tier 1 (highest priority):** Maps, technical diagrams, unique manuscripts, genealogical records, deeds
2. **Tier 2 (medium priority):** Published books (replaceable), journals, correspondence
3. **Tier 3 (lowest priority):** Newspapers, magazines, duplicates

Salvage Tier 1 items before Tier 2; Tier 2 before Tier 3.

### Drying Methods

**Air drying (simplest, safest):**
- Lay documents flat on absorbent material (cloth, blotting paper, newspaper)
- Change absorbent layer every 4–6 hours
- Ensure good air circulation (fan, open windows, warm dry environment)
- Do not use heat above 25°C (heat can warp paper and set stains)
- Drying typically takes 7–14 days for thick documents

**Freeze-drying (if available, most effective):**
- Pack wet documents in waterproof containers
- Freeze to -15°C or colder
- Transport to a facility with freeze-drying equipment
- Allows later thawing and drying without mold growth during storage

:::warning
If you lack freeze-drying capability, freeze wet documents if possible and defer treatment to a time when proper drying is feasible. Frozen documents won't mold or deteriorate, buying time for recovery options.
:::

### Mold Remediation During Drying

Mold begins growing within 24–48 hours in humid conditions:

- **Monitor smell:** Musty odor indicates mold growth
- **Inspect for fuzzy growth:** Small spots indicate early mold
- **Ventilate aggressively:** Increase air flow; open windows in dry conditions
- **Remove moldy pages:** If mold is widespread, isolate affected documents
- **Gentle brushing:** Once dry, gently brush off mold spores with soft brush outdoors (wear mask)

Do NOT use bleach on paper — it causes deterioration. Mold is cosmetic damage; document is still readable underneath.

<section id="fire-smoke-damage">

## Fire and Smoke Damage Recovery

Fire damage assessment:

| Damage Level | Paper Condition | Recovery Outlook | Effort |
|---|---|---|---|
| Smoke only | Discolored, brittle | Excellent | Cleaning |
| Charred edges | Outer cm burned, interior intact | Good | Careful handling |
| Deeply charred | Fragile, crumbly | Fair | Extremely delicate |
| Ash | Unidentifiable fragments | Poor | Documentation only |

### Smoke-Damaged Documents

**Deacidification:**
- Smoke makes paper acidic, accelerating deterioration
- Gentle exposure to alkaline environment neutralizes acid
- Option 1: Store with acid-free paper interleaved (slower, safe)
- Option 2: If possible, expose to calcium hydroxide gas in sealed chamber (faster, requires equipment)

**Odor removal:**
- Airing outdoors in dry, sunny conditions (UV light helps)
- Interleaving with activated charcoal or crumpled newspaper
- Patience — odor fades over months of airing

:::tip
Handle charred documents with extreme care. Use cotton gloves. Support entire document when moving. Do not bend or fold. Charred material is fragile and will disintegrate if stressed.
:::

### Partially Charred Documents

1. **Isolation:** Do not stack or fold; lay flat
2. **Gentle removal:** Use spatula or stiff board to support entire document
3. **Light cleaning:** Allow to cool completely; gently brush away loose debris outdoors
4. **Drying:** Lay flat on acid-free paper in warm, dry location
5. **Stabilization:** Once dry, encapsulate between archival plastic sheets to prevent further disintegration

<section id="mold-remediation">

## Mold Remediation and Prevention

Mold destroys documents through enzymatic degradation and cannot be reversed.

### Signs of Mold

- Fuzzy growth (white, black, green, or yellow) on surface or between pages
- Musty, earthy smell
- Staining (mold leaves permanent discoloration)
- Softening and weakening of paper

### Treatment

**Early stage (surface mold, small area):**
1. Isolate document in sealed container
2. Place silica gel or calcium chloride desiccant in container (absorbs moisture)
3. After 1–2 weeks of drying, gently brush off mold spores outdoors
4. Store in dry location with continuous desiccant monitoring

**Advanced stage (deep mold, significant staining):**
- Accept that damage is permanent
- Document (photograph) the content before preservation
- Focus on transcription rather than physical preservation
- Consider digitization if equipment available

### Prevention

**Humidity control:**
- Store documents at 30–40% relative humidity (RH)
- Use desiccants (silica gel, calcium chloride) in storage containers
- Replace desiccants monthly
- Monitor RH with inexpensive hygrometers

**Air circulation:**
- Avoid sealed containers without ventilation
- Ensure storage areas have air flow
- Avoid basements (high moisture) unless actively dehumidified

**Temperature stability:**
- Keep storage between 10–20°C
- Avoid fluctuation (causes condensation)
- Do not store documents near heat sources, sun, or outside walls

:::danger
Mold is irreversible. Prevention through moisture control is far easier than remediation. If mold appears, accept some loss but prevent spread to other documents.
:::

<section id="paper-drying">

## Paper Drying Techniques

Different paper types require different approaches.

### Standard Paper (Books, Documents)

| Condition | Method | Duration | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Wet, undamaged | Air dry flat on absorbers | 7-14 days | Change absorbers every 6h |
| Damp, pliable | Air dry standing (spine down) | 10-20 days | Allows spine to flex |
| Partially dry, warped | Weight lightly; air dry | 14-30 days | Flatten during drying |
| Fragile/old | Lay flat; very slow air dry | 30+ days | Never speed with heat |

### Leather-Bound Books

Leather becomes brittle when dried too rapidly:

1. **Initial soak:** If dry, soak spine area gently in water for 30 minutes
2. **Squeeze gently:** Remove excess water with soft cloth (no wringing)
3. **Open carefully:** Slowly open pages; do not force
4. **Dry standing:** Store upright or at angle, allowing gravity to help
5. **Condition:** Once dry, apply leather conditioner (oil, lanolin) to prevent cracking

### Parchment/Vellum

Parchment is animal skin and shrinks when wet:

1. **Do NOT soak:** Use minimal water exposure
2. **Blot only:** Press gently with cloth to remove surface water
3. **Dry flat:** Lay on acid-free paper; do NOT use heat
4. **Weight lightly:** Place flat board with light weight on top to prevent curling
5. **Very slow:** Allow weeks for complete drying

Expect permanent buckling with wet parchment — accept this as part of water damage cost.

## Microfilm, Microfiche, and Microcard Readers

Microfilm is a preservation format from the mid-20th century. Many archives still rely on it.

### Microfilm Basics

- **Format:** 35mm film, rolled onto reels
- **Microfiche:** 105mm film sheets, typically 4×6 inch
- **Microcard:** Opaque card format (less common)
- **Lifespan:** 100+ years if stored at cool, dry temperature

### Reader Operation

A microfilm reader uses:

1. **Light source** — Projects light through film
2. **Lens** — Magnifies image (10×, 20×, 40×)
3. **Screen** — Displays enlarged image for reading
4. **Film advance mechanism** — Manual (crank) or motorized

**Basic operation:**
1. Insert film reel onto supply spindle
2. Thread film through aperture (opening)
3. Wind film onto take-up reel
4. Turn on light
5. Focus lens until image is sharp
6. Use crank or motor to advance film frame-by-frame
7. Photograph or transcribe content as needed

### Preservation of Microfilm Stock

- **Storage temperature:** 10–13°C (cold, not freezing)
- **Humidity:** 20–30% RH (very dry)
- **Light:** Store in sealed, opaque containers
- **Inspection:** Check reels annually for brittleness or mold
- **Handling:** Wear cotton gloves; avoid touching film surface

:::tip
Microfilm requires minimal maintenance and is stable for centuries. If your community has microfilm archives, preserve the films and readers carefully — they are extremely valuable historical records.
:::

<section id="maps-charts">

## Map and Chart Recovery

Maps and technical drawings are irreplaceable and warrant special preservation effort.

### Vulnerability

Maps deteriorate faster than books due to:

- **Larger surface area:** More exposed to air pollution and moisture
- **Folding:** Creases weaken paper; fold lines eventually tear
- **Ink types:** Some inks (iron-based) acidify the paper
- **Heavy use:** Maps handled frequently degrade faster

### Stabilization

1. **Assess condition:** Check for tears, creases, foxing (brown stains), insect damage
2. **Gentle cleaning:** Soft eraser can remove surface dirt; avoid water initially
3. **Tape removal:** If old tape present, carefully remove with solvent (if available) — do not pull directly
4. **Flattening:** Place under light weight between acid-free paper for weeks; replace paper if it absorbs moisture
5. **Mounting:** Once stable, mount on acid-free backing using reversible adhesive (or no adhesive, just support)

### Storage

- **Flat, never rolled:** Rolling creates permanent creases
- **Drawers or boxes:** Acid-free archival boxes, laid flat
- **Interleaving:** Place acid-free paper between maps to prevent ink transfer
- **Environmental control:** Cool (10–18°C), dry (30–40% RH), dark storage

### Transcription Priority

For heavily damaged maps:

1. Photograph the map in good light
2. Trace major features (coastlines, rivers, roads, settlements) onto acid-free overlay
3. Record scale, compass direction, date, source
4. File with archive for future reference

## Photographic Record Preservation

Photographs are particularly vulnerable: film and emulsions degrade through chemical reactions.

### Photograph Types and Stability

| Type | Lifespan | Risk | Notes |
|------|----------|------|-------|
| Black/white silver print | 80-100 years | Moderate | Stable if well-stored |
| Color print | 20-50 years | High | Dyes fade rapidly |
| Slide (Kodachrome) | 50-100 years | Moderate | More stable than prints |
| Slide (other colors) | 10-30 years | Very high | Dyes fade quickly |
| Instant film (Polaroid) | 10-20 years | Very high | Extremely unstable |

### Storage Best Practices

- **Temperature:** 10–15°C (cool slows degradation)
- **Humidity:** 30–40% RH (not dry, not humid)
- **Light:** Complete darkness (store in sealed, opaque containers)
- **Materials:** Acid-free sleeves, boxes, mounting supplies only
- **Separation:** Store different photo types separately (color away from B/W to prevent cross-contamination)

### Digitization Priority

Given limited lifespan of originals:

1. **Highest priority:** Unique color photographs, family records, historical events
2. **Medium priority:** Duplicate B/W prints, common subjects
3. **Lowest priority:** Damaged or indecipherable images

Digitize by photographing with a good camera (or scanner if available) in consistent light. Document source and date.

:::warning
Never "restore" old photographs using water, chemicals, or tape. Any attempt at cleaning can cause permanent damage. Accept deterioration; digitize instead.
:::

## Hard Drive Data Recovery Basics

Modern hard drives store vast amounts of information but are vulnerable to failure.

### Types of Failures

**Logical failure (data intact, drive inaccessible):**
- Corrupted file system
- Accidental deletion
- Water/minor damage to connectors

**Physical failure (mechanical damage):**
- Head crash (reading mechanism strikes spinning disk)
- Motor failure (drive won't spin)
- Water immersion, fire exposure

### Preliminary Assessment

1. **Power on cautiously:** If no clicking/grinding sound, drive may still be functional
2. **Listen:** Clicking = head problems; grinding = motor/bearing problems; silence = power problem
3. **Do not force:** Repeated power-on attempts with physical damage can worsen failure
4. **Isolate:** Remove from power immediately if sounds indicate damage

### Data Recovery Options

**Option 1: DIY Logical Recovery (no damage, file corruption)**

If a drive still spins and is recognized by computers but file system is corrupted:

1. Do NOT attempt repair (repairing can worsen corruption)
2. Connect drive as external storage to another computer
3. Use disk recovery software (if available: GETDATABACK, PhotoRec, or similar open-source tools)
4. Copy recoverable files to safe storage
5. Success rate: 30–80% depending on corruption extent

**Option 2: Physical Failure (requires specialist)**

Drives with mechanical damage require:

- Clean room facility (to prevent dust from entering)
- Replacement parts (identical drive model for donor parts)
- Specialized tools and expertise

Recovery is possible but expensive and time-consuming. Prioritize:

1. Unique data with no other copy (family photos, genealogy, irreplaceable records)
2. Skip data that exists on other media or is replaceable

### Prevention (Best Strategy)

Rather than recovery, prevent loss:

- **Multiple copies:** Critical data stored on at least 2–3 separate drives or media
- **Offline storage:** Keep archival copies without power, in cool dry location
- **Rotation:** Periodically (yearly) copy data from old drives to new ones
- **Testing:** Monthly power-on test of archived drives; replace failing ones early

:::tip
In post-collapse scenarios, digital storage is irreplaceable. Prioritize data conservation: multiple backups, offline archival copies, and careful handling of all drives.
:::

## Library Salvage Priorities

When saving books during disasters, prioritize ruthlessly.

### Tier 1 (Save First)

- **Unique originals:** Manuscripts, first editions, handwritten records
- **Technical references:** Engineering, agriculture, medicine, crafts
- **Genealogy/local history:** Community records, maps, photographs
- **Skill guides:** Practical handbooks on survival, crafts, agriculture

### Tier 2 (Save If Possible)

- **Completed book series:** Full runs of reference materials
- **Multiple copies of essential books:** Can be used to ensure at least one survives
- **Literature with cultural significance:** Poetry, philosophy, history
- **Children's books:** Rebuilding society requires educating next generation

### Tier 3 (Lowest Priority)

- **Duplicate copies of common books:** Multiple encyclopedias, replaceable references
- **Magazines and ephemera:** Newspapers, journals with short shelf life
- **Damaged beyond recovery:** Books with mold throughout, fire-destroyed covers

### Practical Salvage Rules

- **Water damage:** Dry immediately; book is salvageable if pages are not completely disintegrated
- **Mold damage:** If mold is early-stage (surface only), treat with desiccants and gentle brushing. If throughout, assess if content is critical before spending effort
- **Structural damage:** Torn pages, missing covers. If content is critical, prioritize; if replaceable, save space
- **Handling:** Never stack wet books. Support spine. Allow drying before moving.

## Transcription Priorities

When documents are too damaged to preserve physically, transcription preserves content.

### Transcription Decision Matrix

| Condition | Unique Content | Common Content | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Intact, readable | Critical | Low | Preserve physically; optional transcription |
| Damaged but readable | Critical | Low | Transcribe + dry carefully |
| Badly damaged, hard to read | Critical | Low | Prioritize careful transcription |
| Barely readable, damaged | Low | Low | Skip; focus on other materials |

### Transcription Methods

**Hand transcription:**
- Slow but requires no equipment
- Suitable for smaller documents (50–500 pages)
- One person reads; one writes (verification step)
- Organize into chapters/sections with dates and sources

**Photocopying/photography:**
- If copy machine available: scan all pages to preserve image
- If camera available: photograph pages in consistent light
- Store copies in archival box with identifying label

**Digital text entry:**
- If community has working computer: manually type text for searchability
- Verify accuracy (proofread against original)
- Store as plain text (.txt) or formatted document (.pdf, .odt)

### Organization of Transcriptions

Create simple metadata for each transcribed document:

```
SOURCE: [Original title, author if known]
DATE: [Original date if known; transcription date]
LOCATION: [Where original is archived]
PAGES: [Page count]
CONDITION: [State of original: damaged, stained, partial, etc.]
TRANSCRIBER: [Name of person who did transcription]
NOTES: [Illegible passages, missing sections, interpretive choices]
```

This allows future users to understand what they have and assess reliability.
