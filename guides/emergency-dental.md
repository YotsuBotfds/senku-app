**Medical Disclaimer:** This guide is for educational reference in emergency situations where professional medical care is unavailable. These procedures carry risks of serious harm or death. Performing medical procedures without proper training and licensing may be illegal in your jurisdiction. Always seek professional medical care when available.
:::

:::warning
**SAFETY WARNING:** Dental procedures including tooth extraction and nerve blocks carry risks of hemorrhage, infection, nerve damage, and anesthetic toxicity. Local anesthetic overdose (lidocaine maximum: 7 mg/kg) can cause seizures, cardiac arrhythmias, and death. Only attempt emergency dental procedures when professional dental care is unavailable and the condition poses immediate health risk.
:::

<section id="overview">

## Overview

Emergency dental procedures address acute pain, infection, and trauma to teeth and supporting structures. In low-resource or austere environments without professional dental care, proper technique can prevent tooth loss, manage life-threatening infections, and alleviate severe pain. This guide covers recognition, assessment, safe field procedures, and preventive measures using improvised materials when necessary.

Start here when the complaint is urgent first: throbbing tooth pain, fever with a tooth problem, pain that is constant or worsening, facial or jaw swelling, pus or foul drainage, a loose or knocked-out tooth after trauma, uncontrolled bleeding after dental injury, or pain severe enough to stop sleep, eating, or mouth opening. This is the right guide for complaint-first prompts like `I feel sick with a fever and my tooth is throbbing and waking me at night`.

Do not route routine hygiene complaints here just because they mention gums or teeth. If the real question is `how do I clean my teeth`, `my gums bleed when I brush`, `my gums bleed every time I brush but there is no swelling or pain`, `my mouth feels dirty`, `my tooth is only a little sensitive`, `my tooth aches mildly and I can still eat but it is sensitive to cold`, `my denture rubs my gums`, or `my breath smells bad unless I clean better`, use `preventive-dental-hygiene` or `dental-prosthetics` first unless swelling, pus, fever, trauma, or escalating pain is also present.

Do not route cleaner ingestion, unknown pill ingestion, or mixed-cleaner fume complaints here just because they mention the mouth, drooling, or burning. If someone swallowed drain cleaner, swallowed unknown pills, or has chest tightness after mixing bleach and vinegar, use `unknown-ingestion-child-poisoning-triage` or `chemical-safety` instead of dental procedures.

If the complaint is an appliance sore or fit problem with swelling, pus, fever, or rapidly worsening pain, treat it here as an urgent infection or trauma problem instead of sending it only to `dental-prosthetics`.

### Visual Assessment Checklist

- **Check tooth color:** Gray/black indicates pulp death or staining; white chalky spots indicate decay
- **Look for visible fracture:** Check all surfaces (buccal, lingual, occlusal) for chips or cracks
- **Assess swelling:** Palpate cheek and jaw for symmetry; note location and hardness (soft = fluid, hard = bone)
- **Test mobility:** Grasp tooth gently with gloved hands; excessive movement (>2 mm) suggests bone loss
- **Examine gum appearance:** Red/purple with suppuration (pus) = infection; blanching with pressure = vascular compromise
- **Check lymph nodes:** Palpate under jaw and neck; enlargement suggests systemic infection

:::warning
Fever >101°F (38.3°C) with spreading facial swelling indicates possible systemic infection. Do not delay antibiotics or professional referral. Risk of spread to brain (meningitis) or airway obstruction is real.
:::

## Common Dental Emergencies and Initial Assessment

### Broken or Chipped Tooth

If the break is small, there is no pain, and no sharp edges, the situation may still be watch-and-wait. See `preventive-dental-hygiene` for monitoring guidance. Come here when the complaint becomes urgent: sharp edges cutting soft tissue, a large piece missing, the tooth loose after injury, active bleeding from trauma, or pain present.

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Emergency</th><th scope="row">Symptoms</th><th scope="row">Immediate Action</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Toothache (pain)</td><td>Sharp/dull ache, worsens with heat/cold</td><td>If mild and no swelling, start with `preventive-dental-hygiene`; if constant, worsening, or night-waking, use NSAIDs (ibuprofen 400-600 mg), cold compress on cheek, avoid hot foods, and escalate if swelling or fever appears</td></tr><tr><td>Abscess (infection)</td><td>Swelling, pus, fever, severe pain</td><td>Antibiotics if available, warm salt rinses, pain relief; drain if safe</td></tr><tr><td>Avulsion (knocked out)</td><td>Tooth completely out of socket</td><td>Reimplant within 30 min if possible; store in milk or saliva if not</td></tr><tr><td>Fracture/crack</td><td>Chip or crack visible, sharp edges, pain on biting</td><td>Cover sharp edge with temporary cement or wax; avoid that side when chewing. If no pain and no sharp edges, see `preventive-dental-hygiene` for monitoring</td></tr><tr><td>Loose/extruded tooth</td><td>Tooth pushed partway out, visible displacement</td><td>Gently push back into socket; stabilize with splint or bite guard</td></tr></tbody></table>
If pain is still mild, there is no swelling, and the cause is unclear, route back to `preventive-dental-hygiene` rather than treating it as an emergency.

<section id="extraction">

## Tooth Extraction Technique

:::warning
Tooth extraction is invasive and carries risks of nerve/blood vessel damage, excessive bleeding, jaw fracture, and infection. Only attempt if the tooth cannot be saved and professional dental care is unavailable for extended period. Proper anesthesia and sterile technique are essential.
:::

### Extraction Instruments Diagram

![Emergency Dental Care diagram 2](../assets/svgs/emergency-dental-2.svg)

### Pre-Extraction Examination

1.  Use mouth mirror or flashlight to visualize the tooth
2.  Check for bone loss (X-ray ideal but not always available); loose teeth with severe bone loss are easier to extract
3.  Assess mobility by grasping with fingers/gloved hands; healthy teeth are firmly anchored
4.  Take tooth radiographs if possible to identify root anatomy (one vs. multiple roots affects technique)

:::info-box
**Mobility Assessment:** Grasp tooth gently between gloved thumb and forefinger. Movement >2 mm (tooth-width) in any direction suggests advanced bone loss. Tooth with severe bone loss may loosen more easily but leaves painful socket and requires prolonged healing.
:::

:::warning
Do not extract if patient has uncontrolled bleeding disorder (hemophilia, on warfarin anticoagulation), thrombocytopenia (<50,000 platelets), or recent chemotherapy. Risk of uncontrollable hemorrhage. Consult or delay if possible.
:::

### Extraction Instruments

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Instrument</th><th scope="row">Use</th><th scope="row">DIY Alternative</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Forceps (universal or root-tip)</td><td>Grasp and luxate tooth</td><td>Pliers (smooth-jaw surgical pliers preferred)</td></tr><tr><td>Elevators</td><td>Separate tooth from ligament</td><td>Flat-blade screwdriver or chisel-tipped metal pick</td></tr><tr><td>Scalpel</td><td>Incise and reflect gum tissue</td><td>Sharp blade or scalpel knife</td></tr><tr><td>Periosteal elevator</td><td>Lift periosteum from bone</td><td>Smooth metal spatula</td></tr></tbody></table>

### Extraction Procedure

1.  Administer local anesthesia (inferior alveolar nerve block for lower teeth; infraorbital block for upper front)
2.  Test anesthesia: gently probe gum with explorer; patient should not feel pain
3.  If gum is loose/inflamed, make small incision to reflect soft tissues slightly
4.  Place elevator under the tooth at the gum line; apply gentle rocking motion to loosen periodontal ligament
5.  Grasp tooth firmly with forceps at the crown (just below gum line)
6.  Use controlled rocking/rotational motion—avoid jerking; apply steady pressure
7.  Upper teeth: rotate and elevate upward; lower teeth: elevate and rotate
8.  Once tooth is mobile, remove with steady traction
9.  Apply gauze and direct pressure to control bleeding; patient bites down for 15-30 minutes
10.  Inspect socket; remove any root fragments or debris

## Local Anesthesia Administration

### Inferior Alveolar Nerve Block (Lower Teeth)

Anesthetizes lower jaw and teeth on one side (most common for lower extractions)

1.  Position patient supine or semi-reclining; head tilted back slightly
2.  Identify the anterior border of mandibular ramus (rough vertical edge of jaw)
3.  Find the lingula (small bony landmark on inner surface of ramus, at midheight)
4.  Insert needle at lingula level, on inner surface of ramus, at approximately 90° angle to jaw
5.  Inject 1.0-1.5 mL of 2% lidocaine; needle should contact bone at correct depth
6.  Withdraw 0.5 mm and inject remaining solution
7.  Patient may feel slight tingling in lower lip and tongue
8.  Wait 3-5 minutes for full effect before proceeding

:::tip
**Landmark confirmation:** Ask patient to palpate their own jaw to help you locate the angle. The lingula is roughly at the level of the patient's eye when looking straight ahead. In very thin/emaciated patients, landmarks may be less prominent; palpate more carefully.
:::

### Infraorbital Nerve Block (Upper Front Teeth)

For upper front teeth (incisors, canines, and sometimes premolars)

1. Locate infraorbital foramen: below orbit, roughly at midpoint of line from nose to ear
2. Insert needle just above upper lip, angling upward and inward toward the foramen
3. Inject 0.5-1.0 mL of 2% lidocaine
4. Anesthetizes upper front teeth and upper lip
5. Onset: 2-3 minutes; duration: 30-60 minutes

### Anesthetic Agents

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Agent</th><th scope="row">Concentration</th><th scope="row">Duration</th><th scope="row">Notes</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Lidocaine</td><td>2% solution</td><td>30-60 min</td><td>Most common; fast onset</td></tr><tr><td>Procaine</td><td>1-2% solution</td><td>15-30 min</td><td>Shorter duration; less effective</td></tr><tr><td>Bupivacaine</td><td>0.5% solution</td><td>90-120 min</td><td>Long duration; slower onset</td></tr></tbody></table>

### Dosage Calculations

Maximum safe dose of lidocaine: 7 mg/kg body weight (up to 500 mg total)

-   2% lidocaine = 20 mg/mL
-   For 70 kg adult: maximum 490 mg = 24.5 mL of 2% solution (typically only 1-2 mL needed per block)
-   For 50 kg individual: maximum 350 mg = 17.5 mL (use smaller volumes in small-framed patients)

:::danger
**Anesthetic Toxicity Signs:** Tremor, dizziness, tinnitus (ringing ears), metallic taste, numbness of lips, confusion, seizures, loss of consciousness, cardiac dysrhythmias (slow/fast heart rate, chest pain). Toxicity occurs when anesthetic is injected into blood vessel (intravascular) or total dose exceeds safe limit. **IMMEDIATE ACTION:** Stop injection, position patient on side (recovery position), monitor airway and breathing, call emergency services. If seizure occurs, do NOT restrain; protect head from injury; clear airway; administer oxygen if available. If cardiac arrest, begin CPR.
:::

:::warning
**Aspiration technique:** Before injecting, pull back slightly on syringe plunger. If blood enters syringe, needle is in blood vessel. Withdraw and reposition. This simple step prevents most intravascular injections.
:::

## Temporary Fillings

### Zinc Oxide + Eugenol (ZOE) Cement

Simple, biocompatible temporary restoration. Eugenol (clove oil) is the active ingredient.

**Preparation:** Mix zinc oxide powder with eugenol liquid (3:1 powder to liquid by weight) until putty-like consistency. Apply to cavity with small instrument; fill completely to prevent food trapping.

**Working time:** 10-15 minutes before hardening

**Removal:** Hardens in 24-48 hours; can be removed with explorer

### IRM (Intermediate Restorative Material)

Reinforced zinc oxide-eugenol; stronger and longer-lasting than simple ZOE

**Preparation:** Mix powder and liquid according to package; fill cavity

**Durability:** 6-12 months if properly sealed

### Temporary DIY Fillings

-   **Calcium hydroxide (slaked lime) mixed with water:** Paste form; biostimulative (promotes healing). Mix until thick consistency; apply to cavity.
-   **Oil of cloves (eugenol) + zinc oxide powder (from calamine lotion):** DIY ZOE substitute; mix to putty consistency
-   **Gutta-percha (natural rubber) softened in warm water:** Moldable temporary sealant; place in cavity while warm
-   **Beeswax mixed with resin:** Temporary sealant; mold when warm

## Abscess Drainage and Management

A dental abscess is a localized infection (usually from untreated decay or trauma). Symptoms: pain, swelling, pus, fever, difficulty swallowing. This section is for complaints like `my face is swelling`, `I have pus on my gum`, or `my tooth pain comes with fever`. If gums are bleeding with brushing but there is no swelling or pus, start with `preventive-dental-hygiene` instead.

### Safe Drainage Procedure

1.  Examine visually to confirm pus-filled swelling (yellow/white point on gum)
2.  Sterilize area and instrument (scalpel or needle) with alcohol or boiled water
3.  Make small incision (3-5 mm) at the white point where abscess is closest to surface
4.  Apply gentle pressure around abscess to encourage pus drainage
5.  Allow pus to drain completely (may take 5-10 minutes); this is therapeutic and not a sign of damage
6.  Insert small rubber drain (tubing) or leave incision open to continue drainage
7.  Instruct patient to rinse with warm salt water (1/2 teaspoon salt in 8 oz water) 3-4 times daily post-drainage
8.  Prescribe antibiotics if available (amoxicillin 500 mg every 8 hours, or clindamycin 300 mg every 6 hours if penicillin allergy)
9.  Follow up in 2-3 days; if abscess has not improved or is worsening, extract the causative tooth

:::danger
**Do NOT attempt drainage if:**
- Swelling extends to floor of mouth or neck (Ludwig's angina—life-threatening airway emergency)
- Patient has fever >101°F with spreading swelling beyond jaw margins (possible cellulitis/sepsis)
- Swelling extends toward eye or affecting vision (orbital cellulitis—risk of blindness)
- Difficulty swallowing (dysphagia) or drooling (suggests airway compromise)
- Stridor or voice changes (airway obstruction)
These conditions require hospitalization, IV antibiotics (ceftriaxone 1-2 g IV q12h), and possibly airway protection. Drainage at home will not resolve systemic infection.
:::

:::warning
After drainage, monitor daily: Is swelling decreasing? Is patient afebrile? If swelling returns within 24-48 hours or fever spikes, causative tooth likely needs extraction. Drainage alone may not cure infection if tooth pulp is dead and infected.
:::

## Infection Management

### Antibiotics for Dental Infection

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Antibiotic</th><th scope="row">Dose</th><th scope="row">Frequency</th><th scope="row">Notes</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Amoxicillin</td><td>500 mg</td><td>Every 8 hours x 7 days</td><td>First-line for dental infections</td></tr><tr><td>Clindamycin</td><td>300-450 mg</td><td>Every 6-8 hours x 7 days</td><td>For penicillin allergy; good tissue penetration</td></tr><tr><td>Azithromycin (Z-pack)</td><td>500 mg day 1, then 250 mg daily</td><td>5 days total</td><td>Alternative; shorter course</td></tr></tbody></table>

### Supportive Care

-   NSAIDs: Ibuprofen 400-600 mg every 6 hours (reduces inflammation and pain)
-   Warm salt rinses: 4-6 times daily (promotes drainage, reduces pain)
-   Soft diet: Avoid hot, hard, crunchy foods
-   Hydration: Drink plenty of water to support immune function
-   Rest: Allow adequate sleep for healing

:::tip
**Warm salt rinse preparation:** 1/2 to 1 teaspoon of salt in 8 ounces of warm (not hot) water. Gentle rinse (do not vigorously swish); let solution bathe area for 30 seconds. Repeat after meals and before bed. This reduces bacteria load and promotes drainage.
:::

:::warning
If fever, swelling, or pain worsens after 48 hours on antibiotics, infection may be resistant or source not treated. Consider switching antibiotics or extracting the causative tooth if not already done. Delay risks spreading infection.
:::

</section>

## Herbal & Natural Pain Management for Dental Emergencies

When pharmaceutical anesthetics are unavailable, traditional herbal remedies provide both anesthetic and analgesic properties. These agents are not replacements for proper local anesthesia but are valuable adjuncts in resource-limited settings.

### Clove Oil (Eugenol) — Primary Natural Anesthetic

**Active compound:** Eugenol (a phenolic compound) has been used in dentistry for centuries and remains in commercial dental materials.

**Mechanism:** Eugenol acts as a local anesthetic by blocking nerve transmission and reducing inflammation via inhibition of pro-inflammatory cytokines.

**Preparation and Use:**
- Obtain pure clove oil (if available, extract by steaming dried cloves or crush cloves in cloth)
- Soak a cotton ball or gauze pad in clove oil
- Apply directly to the affected tooth and surrounding gum
- Leave in place for 2-5 minutes; repeat as needed
- Effects begin within 2-5 minutes; duration 15-30 minutes (shorter than pharmaceutical anesthetics)

**Dosage and Safety:**
- Topical application in small amounts is safe
- Do not swallow large quantities (can cause upset stomach, burning sensation)
- Pregnant women should use cautiously (eugenol has uterotonic properties in high doses)
- Allergic reactions are rare but possible (itching, swelling of lips/mouth)

**Effectiveness Profile:**
- Excellent for surface/topical anesthesia of gums and root surfaces
- Insufficient for deep pulpal anesthesia (cannot replace nerve blocks for major extraction)
- Works best on inflamed, infected tissue (paradoxically, infection-related inflammation may reduce effectiveness slightly)
- Synergistic with NSAIDs: give ibuprofen 600 mg 30 minutes before clove oil application for additive pain relief

**Field Sourcing:**
- Dried whole cloves (ground to powder or steeped)
- Ground clove spice (if available in community supplies)
- Fresh clove leaves or buds (less potent than dried)
- Oil extraction: Simmer crushed dried cloves in coconut oil or rendered fat for 2-3 hours, strain through cloth

### Willow Bark Tea — Systemic Analgesia

**Active compounds:** Salicin and salicylate (precursors to salicylic acid/aspirin)

**Mechanism:** Inhibits COX-1 and COX-2 enzymes (same mechanism as ibuprofen/aspirin), reducing prostaglandins and thereby pain and inflammation. Also has antipyretic (fever-reducing) and mild anxiolytic (anxiety-reducing) properties.

**Preparation:**
1. Harvest inner bark from willow tree (preferably white or crack willow; avoid toxic varieties)
2. Dry bark thoroughly or use fresh
3. Steep 2-3 teaspoons dried bark (or 3-4 tablespoons fresh bark) in 8 oz hot water for 10-15 minutes
4. Strain and drink, warm or cool
5. Bitter taste; can add honey or other herbs (ginger, mint) for palatability

**Dosage:**
- One cup every 4-6 hours (similar frequency to ibuprofen)
- Do not exceed 3-4 cups per day
- Slower onset than pharmaceutical ibuprofen (30-60 minutes vs. 15-30 minutes) but similar duration

**Limitations:**
- Mild salicylate allergy possible (rare; includes aspirin sensitivity)
- Avoid in patients with bleeding disorders or on anticoagulants
- Pregnancy: traditionally considered safe but consult if available
- Stomach irritation possible in sensitive individuals (consume with food)
- Slower acting than NSAIDs for acute severe pain (but still effective for moderate pain)

**Sourcing and Alternatives:**
- Willow bark is widespread in temperate regions (northern hemisphere and southern regions)
- Alternatives with similar mechanisms: meadowsweet (salicylates), white pine bark (antioxidant; weak analgesic), birch bark

### Other Traditional Herbal Adjuncts

**Ginger Root (Anti-inflammatory & Warming)**
- Steep thin slices of fresh ginger in hot water; drink warm
- Reduces nausea (common with severe dental pain or anesthesia)
- Mild analgesic via inhibition of inflammatory mediators
- Warming effect may soothe chronic toothaches
- Safe, no major interactions
- Use: 1-2 cups daily; chew small piece (1/2 inch) for localized pain

**Turmeric (Curcumin — Potent Anti-inflammatory)**
- Curcumin is one of the most researched anti-inflammatory compounds
- Inhibits NF-κB transcription factor (reduces inflammatory gene expression)
- Preparation: Mix 1/4 to 1/2 teaspoon turmeric powder with water to form paste; apply topically to gum or tooth
- Or: steep 1/2 teaspoon in 8 oz warm milk or water; drink (absorption improved with black pepper and fat)
- Onset slower than other agents (6-8 hours for systemic effect) but sustained reduction in inflammation
- Safe; very low toxicity even at high doses
- Staining: turmeric will temporarily stain teeth and lips yellow (washes off with water)
- Use: 2-3 times daily for 3-5 days for best effect

**Peppermint or Spearmint (Cooling & Mild Anesthetic)**
- Menthol has mild topical anesthetic and cooling properties
- Reduces anxiety and nausea (psychological benefit in acute pain)
- Preparation: Steep fresh or dried leaves in hot water; drink warm or use as mouthwash (rinse gently, do not swish aggressively)
- Also: Apply crushed fresh leaves directly to gum for cooling sensation
- Safe; no contraindications
- Use: 2-4 cups daily as needed

**Raw Honey (Antimicrobial & Wound Healing)**
- Hydrogen peroxide production and osmotic effect kill bacteria
- Promotes epithelialization (healing of wound surfaces)
- Preparation: Apply raw, unheated honey directly to extraction site post-procedure or to abscess
- Do NOT use with injectable medications (honey can interfere with absorption)
- Safety: Avoid in infants <1 year (botulism risk)
- Use: Small amount (1 teaspoon) applied directly; repeat 2-3 times daily

### Combination Approach: Herbal Pain Management Protocol

For severe dental pain without pharmaceutical options:

1. **0 minutes:** Give willow bark tea (steep 10 minutes; drink 30-60 minute lag to onset)
2. **5 minutes:** Apply clove oil-soaked cotton to tooth and gum (topical relief begins in 2-5 min)
3. **10 minutes:** If available, give ibuprofen 600 mg with ginger tea (synergistic anti-inflammatory effect)
4. **15-30 minutes:** Begin deep breathing and distraction techniques; onset of willow bark approaching
5. **30+ minutes:** Maximum pain relief should be achieved by 30-60 minutes; window lasts 15-30 minutes for topical and 4-6 hours for systemic agents
6. **Repeat cycle:** If pain persists beyond pain relief window, repeat topical clove application every 30 minutes as needed
7. **Post-extraction:** Switch to turmeric paste and honey for wound healing; continue willow bark or ginger tea every 4-6 hours for 3-5 days

:::warning
This herbal approach reduces pain significantly but is not equivalent to pharmaceutical local anesthesia with nerve blocks. Acceptable for minor procedures (temporary filling placement, minor suturing) or as a bridge until antibiotics control infection. For major extraction in severe pain without adequate anesthesia, consider delaying 24-48 hours while antibiotics reduce inflammation (infection + inflammation interfere with anesthetic action anyway).
:::

:::tip
**Synergy with ibuprofen:** Herbal analgesics (willow bark, ginger, turmeric) work through different mechanisms than ibuprofen. Combining ibuprofen with one herbal agent is more effective than either alone, without exceeding ibuprofen's daily dose limit (2400 mg). Example: ibuprofen 600 mg + willow bark tea + ginger = three-pronged attack on pain and inflammation.
:::

## Dental Infection Identification & Types

Distinguishing between different types of dental infections is critical because management varies significantly. Some infections remain localized (manageable with drainage and antibiotics), while others spread systemically and become life-threatening within hours.

### Periapical Abscess (Most Common Dental Infection)

**Anatomy:** Infection at the tip of the tooth root, in the bone surrounding the root apex.

**Cause:** Dead (necrotic) tooth pulp; usually from untreated deep decay, trauma, or failed root canal.

**Recognition signs:**
- Localized swelling of gum directly above root tip (often appears as white/yellow "pimple")
- Pain specifically when biting down on affected tooth (percussion pain)
- Tooth may be discolored (gray, black, or translucent)
- Lymph nodes under jaw and neck may be enlarged
- Drainage of pus (tastes foul)
- Typically unilateral (one side) unless multiple teeth involved

**Progression:** Begins as localized abscess; if untreated, drains into bone, forms sinus tract (pathway of drainage through jaw), and may drain through skin of face/neck or into oral cavity.

**Risk level:** Low to moderate if treated quickly; high if allows spread to deeper tissues.

**Management:**
- Drain abscess if accessible (gently incise white point on gum; allow drainage)
- Start antibiotics immediately (amoxicillin 500 mg every 8 hours or clindamycin 300 mg every 6 hours)
- Warm salt rinses 4-6 times daily
- Extraction of causative tooth if not already done
- Prognosis: Excellent with drainage and antibiotics; most resolve within 5-7 days

### Periostitis (Bone Inflammation)

**Anatomy:** Inflammation of periosteum (membrane covering bone surface around tooth).

**Cause:** Bacterial toxins irritating the periosteum without full abscess formation; often precedes frank abscess.

**Recognition signs:**
- Swelling of bone and overlying gum (firm, not fluctuant/squishy)
- Pain worse with chewing and percussion
- Sometimes visible bone prominence/bulging
- May NOT have visible pus drainage point
- Fever possible but not always

**Distinction from periapical abscess:**
- Periostitis: swelling of bone surface itself, usually firm
- Abscess: fluid collection (feels squishy or "points" with white head)

**Management:**
- NSAIDs to reduce inflammation (ibuprofen 600 mg every 4 hours)
- Antibiotics (clindamycin preferred for bone penetration)
- Warm moist heat to promote drainage and blood flow
- Extraction of causative tooth
- Ice first 24-48 hours, then warm compresses after

### Cellulitis (Spreading Soft Tissue Infection)

**Anatomy:** Spreading bacterial infection through soft tissues (gums, cheek, floor of mouth, neck) WITHOUT pus localization.

**Cause:** Highly virulent bacteria or severe host immune suppression; often follows extraction, trauma, or spread from untreated abscess.

**Recognition signs (these are RED FLAGS—see Ludwig's Angina below for most severe form):**
- Spreading swelling: starts at tooth site, extends to cheek, jaw, or neck
- Swelling is diffuse (not localized like abscess); borders not well-demarcated
- Skin may be red and warm to touch
- Pitting edema possible (pressing leaves indent mark)
- Fever (often >101.5°F)
- Lymph node swelling
- **Critically:** Difficulty swallowing (dysphagia), drooling, voice changes, or stridor = cellulitis spreading to airway; **EMERGENCY**

**Risk level:** Very high; cellulitis can progress to sepsis and death if untreated.

**Management:**
- **Do NOT attempt to drain.** Cellulitis is not localized pus; incision worsens spread.
- **Antibiotics immediately:** IV preferred (ceftriaxone 1-2 g IV every 12 hours) or high-dose oral clindamycin (600 mg every 6 hours)
- Swelling management: elevation, ice (not heat—promotes spread)
- Extract causative tooth if not already done (only AFTER antibiotics started and after 24-48 hours to reduce trauma-induced spread)
- **Airway monitoring:** Difficulty swallowing, voice changes, or stridor = prepare for intubation or airway obstruction management
- **Escalate immediately:** This requires hospitalization in any resourced setting

### Ludwig's Angina (Bilateral Floor-of-Mouth Infection) — LIFE-THREATENING EMERGENCY

**Anatomy:** Cellulitis of sublingual and submandibular spaces (the area beneath the tongue and in front of the neck); **BILATERAL** (both sides).

**Cause:** Usually from infected lower premolars or molars; spreads rapidly along fascial planes (low-resistance pathways).

**Recognition signs:**
- Severe swelling of floor of mouth (area under tongue)
- "Brawny" swelling: feels woody/hard, not squishy (cellulitis, not abscess)
- Swelling extends to BOTH sides of neck (bilateral—this is the key distinguishing feature)
- Difficulty swallowing (dysphagia); may drool
- Tongue pushed upward and back (toward airway)
- Voice changes, "hot potato voice" (sounds like talking with food in mouth)
- **Stridor or respiratory difficulty = imminent airway obstruction**
- Fever (often high, >103°F)
- Severe pain and anxiety
- May have rigidity/stiffness of neck

**Why it's life-threatening:**
- Rapid airway obstruction: Swelling can completely obstruct airway within hours
- Spread to mediastinum (chest): Bacteria follow fascial planes down into chest, causing mediastinitis (infection of tissue around heart) and sepsis
- Difficulty intubating (swelling blocks access)
- Swallowing impairment leads to aspiration of saliva/pus into lungs

**Management — EMERGENCY PROTOCOL:**
1. **Position patient upright** (not lying down; gravity helps prevent airway obstruction)
2. **Prepare for airway emergency:**
   - Have suction available
   - If possible, prepare equipment for cricothyrotomy or needle airway if intubation fails
   - Do NOT sedate unless you can manage airway
3. **Start antibiotics immediately** (do NOT wait for culture):
   - **IV first choice:** Ceftriaxone 2 g IV every 12 hours PLUS metronidazole 500 mg IV every 6-8 hours (covers anaerobes)
   - **Oral only if IV unavailable:** Clindamycin 600 mg every 6 hours PLUS amoxicillin 875 mg every 8 hours (broad spectrum)
4. **Extract causative tooth** ONLY after 24-48 hours of antibiotics (early extraction increases spread; delayed extraction after some improvement safer)
5. **Incision and drainage** may be needed if pus collection develops (but cellulitis itself should NOT be incised)
6. **Hospitalization** is non-negotiable; mortality is 5-40% even with hospitalization if intubation/surgery delayed

**Field scenario (no hospital available):**
- Aggressive IV antibiotics (or maximum-dose oral if IV impossible)
- Keep patient upright at all times
- Monitor airway continuously; if stridor develops or patient cannot swallow saliva, prepare for emergency airway
- Hydration IV if available (patient cannot swallow)
- If airway begins to fail and no hospital available: cricothyrotomy (emergency surgical airway) may be lifesaving

:::danger
**Ludwig's Angina is a TRUE MEDICAL EMERGENCY.** If you recognize bilateral floor-of-mouth swelling, stridor, dysphagia, or voice change in a patient with dental infection, this is one of the few dental conditions that rivals cardiac arrest in urgency. EVERY SECOND counts. Begin antibiotics immediately, position for airway, and escalate to emergency services/hospitalization without delay. Waiting even 1-2 hours increases mortality risk exponentially.
:::

### Acute Necrotizing Ulcerative Gingivitis (ANUG/Trench Mouth)

**Anatomy:** Infection affecting gums (gingiva) and underlying tissues; **gums ulcerate (break down), bleed, and slough off**.

**Cause:** Bacterial overgrowth (anaerobic bacteria) in setting of poor oral hygiene, immunosuppression, stress, or malnutrition; more common in post-collapse scenarios.

**Recognition signs:**
- Severe gum pain (out of proportion to appearance)
- Spontaneous gum bleeding or bleeding with light touch
- Ulceration: gums appear punched out (crater-like defects); tissue death (necrosis)
- Foul odor (putrid smell from dead tissue and bacteria)
- Gray pseudomembrane (dead tissue coating) on gums
- Difficulty eating or swallowing
- Lymph node swelling and fever possible
- Rapid progression over days

**Distinction from simple gingivitis:**
- Gingivitis: red, swollen gums but intact; often painless
- ANUG: painful, bleeding ulcers with gum tissue loss; foul smell; rapid progression

**Risk level:** Moderate if treated; high if ignored (can progress to osteomyelitis or spread infection).

**Management:**
- Gentle mechanical debridement: Remove dead tissue and plaque with soft brush or gauze (gently; painful but necessary)
- Antibiotics (metronidazole 500 mg every 6-8 hours is most specific; or clindamycin 300 mg every 6 hours)
- Mouth rinses: Dilute hydrogen peroxide (3% solution) rinse 3-4 times daily; gently (not vigorously)
- Salt water rinses after meals
- Soft diet; avoid spicy/acidic foods
- Improve overall health: nutrition, hydration, rest, stress reduction
- Smoking cessation (if applicable; smoking markedly worsens ANUG)
- Prognosis: Good with antibiotics and oral hygiene; responds faster than other infections

### Osteomyelitis (Bone Infection)

**Anatomy:** Bacterial infection within bone itself; spreads through bone marrow and haversian canals.

**Cause:** Usually from untreated dental abscess; risk factors include immunosuppression, poor blood flow to jaw, or trauma.

**Recognition signs:**
- Severe, unrelenting jaw pain (often diffuse, not localized to one tooth)
- Swelling of jaw; may be subtle initially
- Fever and malaise
- **Pathognomonic sign:** Numbness or "paresthesia" of lower lip and chin (inferior alveolar nerve involvement = advanced osteomyelitis)
- Difficulty opening mouth or eating
- Drainage from skin of face/neck (pus tracking through tissue)
- Slow onset: unlike acute abscess, may develop over weeks

**Risk level:** Very high; chronic osteomyelitis is difficult to treat and can persist for years.

**Management:**
- **Antibiotics:** Prolonged course (4-6 weeks minimum) needed; clindamycin preferred for bone penetration
- Extract causative tooth and infected areas of gum/bone if identified
- Imaging critical (X-ray shows bone loss; MRI shows marrow involvement)
- Hyperbaric oxygen (if available) accelerates healing
- Surgical debridement of necrotic bone may be necessary
- **This often requires professional care and is difficult to manage in field settings**

:::warning
Osteomyelitis is a chronic infection that often requires months of antibiotics and may need surgical bone removal. Prevention is critical: treat dental infections early (within 48-72 hours) with antibiotics and source control (extraction or drainage). Do not delay treatment hoping infection resolves on its own—bone infections are much harder to eliminate than soft-tissue infections.
:::

### Sinusitis from Dental Infection

**Anatomy:** Infection of maxillary sinus (upper jaw), usually from upper molars.

**Cause:** Bacteria from upper molar abscess or failed root canal spread into sinus above roots.

**Recognition signs:**
- Facial pain, usually unilateral (under eye, cheek, forehead)
- Nasal congestion; drainage of pus from nose
- Sinus pressure/fullness
- Pain worse when bending over (gravity increases sinus pressure)
- Toothache in upper molars area
- May have fever and general malaise

**Distinction:** Difficult because sinus pain mimics upper toothache; key is that dental sinusitis is tied to a specific infected tooth.

**Management:**
- Treat underlying dental infection (antibiotics, extract tooth if necessary)
- Nasal saline irrigation (helps drain sinus)
- Decongestants if available
- Usually resolves as dental infection clears
- Prognosis: Good if underlying dental problem treated

<section id="infection-without-antibiotics">

## Dental Infection Management Without Modern Antibiotics

In true resource-scarcity scenarios, pharmaceutical antibiotics may be unavailable or in limited supply. This section covers strategies to manage and reduce bacterial load without pharmaceutical agents—recognizing that outcomes are less certain and mortality risk increases.

### Prevention-First Approach: Sterile Technique is Critical

When antibiotics are unavailable, prevention becomes paramount:

**Field sterilization of instruments:**
1. Boil all dental instruments (forceps, elevators, syringes) for 20-30 minutes before use
2. Allow to cool on sterile cloth (not touching unsterile surfaces)
3. Between procedures, store in sealed container with alcohol-soaked cloth inside (container keeps cloth moist; alcohol acts as preservative)
4. Single-use where possible: use each syringe needle once; discard; use new needle for each injection

**Hand hygiene:**
- Wash hands thoroughly with soap and water before any procedure
- Wear clean gloves if available; if not, ensure hands are clean and dry
- Change gloves between patients

**Field asepsis:**
- Clean extraction site with alcohol or iodine before procedure
- If neither available: salt water rinse (1 teaspoon salt in 8 oz boiled water)
- Use clean (boiled) instruments; wipe blood with clean cloth

### Natural Antimicrobial Measures

**Honey (Manuka if available, otherwise any raw honey):**
- Osmotic action: dehydrates bacteria and draws out fluid/pus
- Hydrogen peroxide production (from glucose oxidase enzyme in raw honey)
- Polyphenol compounds have direct antimicrobial effect
- Protocol: Pack extraction socket with raw honey; change daily for 5-7 days; honey absorbs and can be gently flushed out with salt water
- Effectiveness: Moderate; useful for minor infections and wound healing; insufficient for severe/spreading infection
- Advantages: Ubiquitous (most communities have honey); safe; promotes healing
- Limitations: Does NOT cure systemic infection (fever, spreading swelling)

**Salt water irrigation and rinses:**
- Osmotic effect kills bacteria; mechanical removal of debris
- Protocol: 1 teaspoon salt in 8 oz warm water; gentle rinse (not vigorous) 4-6 times daily
- Best used post-drainage or extraction; less effective for intact abscess (cannot penetrate)
- Advantages: Extremely simple; salt universally available; very safe
- Limitations: Surface effect only; cannot treat deep or spreading infection

**Garlic (raw, fresh):**
- Allicin (produced when garlic is crushed) has broad antimicrobial spectrum
- Protocol: Crush 2-3 cloves; apply paste directly to abscess or extraction site; wrap with gauze or bind with cloth; change 2-3 times daily
- Taste and odor intense; patient acceptance variable
- Effectiveness: Moderate antimicrobial action; better for prevention than treatment of established infection
- **Caution:** Can irritate delicate oral mucosa; use cautiously in gums
- Safe: No significant toxicity

**Turmeric (curcumin — strongest herbal antimicrobial):**
- Curcumin has broad antimicrobial spectrum (bacteria, fungi, some viruses)
- Mechanism: Inhibits bacterial biofilm formation; disrupts cell membranes
- Protocol: Mix 1/4 to 1/2 teaspoon turmeric powder with small amount of water or honey to form paste; apply topically to abscess, extraction site, or gum infection; cover with gauze; change 2-3 times daily
- Can also consume internally: 1/2 teaspoon in warm milk or water; absorption improved by adding black pepper and fat
- Onset: Slower than other methods (6-8 hours) but sustained effect over days
- Effectiveness: Moderate to strong antimicrobial; strong anti-inflammatory
- Staining: Will temporarily color mouth/teeth yellow; washes off
- Safe: Extremely low toxicity; even high doses cause no harm

**Clove oil and cloves (as antimicrobial, not just anesthetic):**
- Eugenol has antimicrobial properties (not just anesthetic)
- Protocol: Pack extraction socket or abscess with whole dried cloves (crushed slightly) or soak gauze in clove oil; apply and hold in place; change daily
- Also effective as rinse: steep crushed cloves in hot water; allow to cool to warm; rinse gently 3-4 times daily
- Effectiveness: Moderate antimicrobial; combines pain relief with infection control
- Safe; well-tolerated

**Onion or leek:**
- Organosulfur compounds (allicin precursors) have antimicrobial properties
- Less potent than garlic but available in most gardens
- Protocol: Crush bulb; apply paste to affected area; change 2-3 times daily
- Effectiveness: Mild to moderate
- Safe

### Drainage Over Antibiotics Principle

When antibiotics are unavailable, **prioritize drainage of abscess** over hoping infection will resolve:

1. **Identify abscess:** Look for white/yellow "pimple" on gum near infected tooth
2. **Prepare:** Sterilize needle or small blade with boiling water; allow to cool
3. **Incise gently:** Make small (3-5 mm) incision at the white point where abscess is closest to surface
4. **Express pus:** Apply gentle pressure around abscess; allow pus to drain completely (5-10 minutes)
5. **Leave open:** Do NOT suture or seal incision; allow continued drainage over next 24-48 hours
6. **Irrigation:** Rinse with warm salt water every 2-3 hours; this keeps drainage path open and continues pus removal
7. **Packing (optional):** Insert small gauze drain or leave open; if packing used, change daily with fresh sterile gauze
8. **Monitor:** Check daily for continued improvement (swelling decreasing, fever dropping)

**Expected outcome:** With proper drainage, most localized abscesses improve within 5-7 days even without antibiotics, provided:
- Drainage path remains open
- Causative tooth is extracted or root canal performed (if patient can tolerate without anesthesia)
- Nutritional and immune support maintained

### Immune Support & Nutrition

Without antibiotics, immune function becomes the primary defense:

**Nutritional priorities:**
- Protein: Support immune cell production (eggs, legumes, meat if available)
- Vitamin C: Supports immune function and collagen healing (citrus, leafy greens, organ meats)
- Zinc: Essential for immune response (seeds, nuts, shellfish if available)
- Avoid refined sugars: Glucose impairs neutrophil function (immune cells); limit simple carbs

**Rest and sleep:** Healing is prioritized during sleep; infection management will be slower with sleep deprivation

**Fever management:** Fever is beneficial (kills bacteria); do not suppress with antipyretics unless fever >103°F (39.4°C) or causing distress. High fevers risk febrile seizure (rare in adults) and further immune suppression.

### When to Accept Defeat: Extraction as Last Resort

If infection persists despite drainage and herbal measures for 7-10 days:

1. **Causative tooth likely needs extraction:** Abscess will not resolve while infected pulp remains
2. **Extraction procedure:**
   - Give maximum herbal pain relief (willow bark + clove oil + ibuprofen if available)
   - Drain abscess fully first
   - Attempt extraction; use techniques described in main extraction section
   - Even partial extraction (crown removal leaving root fragments) may relieve systemic infection if root fragments are small
3. **Post-extraction care without antibiotics:**
   - Honey packing (honey has antimicrobial effect and promotes healing)
   - Salt water rinses every 2-3 hours
   - Herbal support: turmeric paste, garlic if tolerated
   - Avoid drying socket: keep moist with frequent rinses; avoid smoking/spitting/straws
   - Nutritional support as above

### Prognosis Without Antibiotics

- **Localized abscess (non-spreading):** 60-80% resolve with drainage + tooth extraction + good hygiene
- **Mild cellulitis (spreading swelling, no airway compromise):** 40-60% respond to aggressive drainage + herbal measures + extraction; remainder progress to systemic infection
- **Severe spreading infection (fever >102°F, airway compromise, spreading swelling):** Mortality 5-20% without antibiotics; <1% with antibiotics in hospitalized patients. Field fatality rate higher.

**Key principle:** Without antibiotics, the goal is damage control. Prevent systemic spread through early drainage, extraction, and immune support. Fatalities will be higher; prepare for this reality and focus on salvageable cases.

:::danger
This section is not an endorsement of managing severe dental infections without antibiotics if antibiotics are available. The goal is survival when pharmaceutical options are exhausted. Outcomes are significantly worse. If ANY access to antibiotics exists (trade, scavenging, triage distribution), use them. Reserve herbal-only approach for true resource collapse scenarios where even antibiotics have been completely depleted.
:::

</section>

<section id="life-threatening-dental">

## When Dental Issues Become Life-Threatening Emergencies

Most dental infections progress slowly and can be managed with extraction and antibiotics over days. However, certain presentations are TRUE EMERGENCIES that threaten life within hours. Recognizing these patterns is critical.

### Immediate Life Threat: Airway Obstruction from Swelling

**Signs of impending airway compromise:**
- Stridor (high-pitched breathing sound)
- Inability to swallow saliva (drooling)
- "Hot potato voice" (sounds like talking with mouth full)
- Tongue pushed back or swelling visible in back of throat
- Restlessness, anxiety, sensation of throat closing
- Difficulty breathing even when calm and upright
- Cyanosis or color changes (bluish lips/tongue)

**Causes:**
- Ludwig's angina (bilateral floor-of-mouth swelling—see Dental Infection Identification section)
- Pharyngeal abscess spreading from tooth
- Epiglottitis from oral infection (rare but rapidly fatal)

**Immediate action:**
1. **Position upright** (sitting forward, not lying down)
2. **Prepare airway management:**
   - If possible, arrange emergency transport immediately
   - If field setting, prepare for emergency airway (cricothyrotomy) if transport impossible
3. **Do NOT sedate or restrain** (risks complete airway loss)
4. **Cool humidified air** (steam from warm water in bowl held near mouth may reduce swelling temporarily)
5. **Maximum antibiotics** (IV if available; otherwise oral high-dose)
6. **Do NOT attempt drainage** (airway obstruction is angioedema/swelling, not localized pus; incision won't help and wastes time)

**Survival depends on rapid airway management. This is equivalent to anaphylaxis in urgency.**

### Sepsis from Dental Source

**Signs of sepsis (systemic infection):**
- High fever (>103°F / 39.4°C) with rigors (shaking chills)
- Tachycardia (heart rate >120 at rest)
- Tachypnea (respiratory rate >20, fast shallow breathing)
- Hypotension (blood pressure dropping; patient feels faint)
- Altered mental status (confusion, difficulty concentrating, agitation)
- Skin mottling or cyanosis
- Oliguria (urine output <0.5 mL/kg/hour; patient urinating very little despite drinking)

**Progression from local infection to sepsis:**
- Local abscess → spreading cellulitis → blood-borne bacteria → sepsis
- Timeline: Can progress from stable to septic shock within 24-48 hours with severe infection

**Management:**
1. **IV antibiotics immediately** (do not wait for confirmation):
   - Ceftriaxone 2 g IV every 12 hours PLUS metronidazole 500 mg IV every 8 hours (if available)
   - Or: Clindamycin 600 mg IV/IM every 6 hours
   - Goal: Blood levels high enough to kill bacteria rapidly
2. **Source control:** Extract infected tooth, drain accessible abscess
3. **Supportive care:**
   - IV fluids (aggressive; sepsis causes fluid loss through capillary leak)
   - Monitoring: Temperature, heart rate, blood pressure every 30-60 minutes
   - Oxygen if available (sepsis reduces oxygen delivery; supplementation helps)
4. **Transport to hospital** if any possibility; septic shock mortality 30-50% even in ICU

**Field prognosis without IV antibiotics/fluids:** Very poor; mortality likely without intervention.

### Acute Severe Swelling Affecting Vision or Causing Proptosis

**Signs:**
- Swelling around eye (edema of eyelid/cheek)
- Difficulty opening eye
- Vision changes or double vision
- **Proptosis:** Eye bulging outward from orbit (sign of orbital cellulitis—infection in eye socket tissues)
- Pain on eye movement

**Cause:** Infection from upper molars spreading along facial fasciae to orbit.

**Why urgent:** Orbital cellulitis can cause blindness and can spread to meninges (brain infection).

**Management:**
- IV antibiotics (same as sepsis protocol above)
- Do NOT attempt drainage (risk of spreading to brain)
- Elevate head aggressively (reduces swelling)
- Ice on eye area
- Urgent transport to hospital (ophthalmology consultation needed)

### Acute Difficulty Swallowing or Breathing After Extraction

**Scenario:** Patient undergoes extraction; initially stable; then develops difficulty swallowing or breathing within 1-6 hours.

**Possible causes:**
- Post-extraction swelling (normal) worsening unexpectedly
- Airway angioedema from anesthetic allergy
- Aspiration of blood/fluids into airway
- Hemorrhage into tissues compressing airway

**Management:**
1. Position upright
2. Assess breathing: If stridor or severe difficulty, prepare emergency airway
3. If post-extraction swelling: Ice + elevation; may resolve with time
4. If allergic reaction suspected (angioedema, itching, hives): Epinephrine if available (0.3 mg IM); antihistamines
5. Monitor continuously; if worsening, prepare for emergency airway
6. Do NOT re-suture or pack socket (risks blocking airway further)

### Uncontrolled Hemorrhage >1 Hour

**Signs:**
- Bright red bleeding that doesn't slow with 20 minutes of continuous pressure
- Patient becoming pale, dizzy (hemorrhage shock)
- Blood loss >500 mL estimated (soaked gauze saturates multiple pads)

**Causes:**
- Vessel laceration (inferior alveolar artery or other branches)
- Coagulopathy (hemophilia, warfarin use, thrombocytopenia)
- Anesthesia with epinephrine causing vasoconstriction, then rebound vasodilation and bleeding

**Management:**
1. **Direct pressure:** 20-30 minutes continuous (do not remove to check)
2. **Pack tightly:** Use gauze soaked in epinephrine (1:10,000) if available, or hemostatic agent
3. **Elevation + cold:** Head of bed elevated; ice pack on jaw
4. **Avoid anticoagulants:** No aspirin; ibuprofen okay
5. **Coagulation support:** If available, give vitamin K (if warfarin use) or FFP (fresh frozen plasma)
6. **Transfusion:** If blood loss >1 liter or signs of shock, transfusion needed
7. **Surgical control:** If bleeding persists, vessel may need ligation (suturing); requires professional care

**Field scenario:** Without access to surgery, prolonged pressure and packing are sole options; mortality increases significantly if hemorrhage uncontrollable.

### Anesthetic Toxicity (Local Anesthetic Overdose)

**Signs (appear within minutes of injection):**
- Tremor, shaking
- Dizziness or lightheadedness
- Tinnitus (ringing in ears)
- Metallic taste
- Numbness/tingling of lips and tongue
- Confusion, altered speech
- **Progression to severe:** Seizure, loss of consciousness, cardiac dysrhythmias, cardiac arrest

**Cause:**
- Excessive anesthetic volume (>7 mg/kg lidocaine)
- Accidental intravascular injection (needle hits blood vessel)
- Rapid absorption from inflamed/infected area

**Immediate management:**
1. **STOP injection immediately**
2. **Position patient supine** on left side (recovery position if altered consciousness)
3. **Establish airway:** Clear airway; be ready to provide rescue breathing
4. **Seizure if occurring:** Do NOT restrain; protect head; place rolled cloth in mouth side (not between teeth); clear airway when seizing stops
5. **Monitor breathing and heart rate** continuously
6. **Oxygen if available:** Supplemental oxygen helps during anesthesia recovery
7. **Do NOT attempt further dental procedure** (focus entirely on life support)
8. **Emergency services:** Call for transport immediately

**Prevention:** Aspirate before injection (pull back on plunger; if blood appears, needle is intravascular—reposition). Calculate correct dose for body weight. Inject slowly.

:::danger
Anesthetic toxicity can progress to cardiac arrest. If available, ACLS (Advanced Cardiac Life Support) knowledge or CPR capability essential. In field settings, oxygen and manual rescue breathing are lifesaving. Modern toxicity treatment (lipid emulsion infusion) unlikely available in austere setting; survival depends on preventing overdose and supportive care during acute toxicity.
:::

### Necrotizing Fasciitis from Oral Source (Rare but Rapidly Fatal)

**Signs:**
- Extreme pain out of proportion to appearance (pain much worse than would expect from visible swelling)
- Swelling progressing rapidly (over hours, not days)
- Skin discoloration: purple, gray, or bullae (fluid-filled blisters)
- Systemic toxicity: high fever, confusion, hypotension
- Crepitus (crackling sensation under skin from gas-producing bacteria)

**Cause:** Highly virulent anaerobic bacteria (Group A Strep, mixed anaerobes) spreading along fascial planes

**Why rapidly fatal:** Spreads along planes of least resistance; can go from dental area to chest in hours; toxin production causes shock

**Management:**
1. **Aggressive IV antibiotics immediately** (clindamycin 600 mg IV q6h PLUS high-dose penicillin)
2. **Emergency surgical debridement:** Large area of dead tissue must be surgically removed; multiple debridements often needed
3. **Aggressive fluid resuscitation:** Septic shock from toxin production
4. **Amputation may be necessary** if spread to limb (sacrificing limb to save life)

**Field prognosis:** Extremely poor without emergency surgical facility. Mortality >50% even with treatment.

:::warning
Necrotizing fasciitis is so rare from dental source that you may never see it. But it's included because missing it costs lives. Any patient with extreme pain, rapid swelling, and systemic toxicity from dental source should be treated as necrotizing fasciitis until proven otherwise. Transport to hospital is non-negotiable.
:::

### Mortality Checkpoints: When to Switch to Comfort Care

In austere environments without advanced medical support, certain presentations indicate imminent mortality:

1. **Airway obstruction + no airway management capability:** Intubation or cricothyrotomy impossible/unavailable
2. **Septic shock + no IV antibiotics/fluids:** Hypotension dropping despite maximum fluid resuscitation attempt
3. **Necrotizing fasciitis + no surgical facility:** Spreading despite antibiotics; toxin-induced shock progressing
4. **Anesthetic/hemorrhage cardiac arrest + no ACLS capability:** CPR alone insufficient for recovery; time window for reversibility past

In these cases, goal shifts to comfort care: pain relief, dignity, allowing family presence, rituals around dying.

## Jaw Fracture Stabilization

Jaw fractures cause severe pain, malocclusion (teeth don't meet properly), difficulty swallowing, and risk of airway obstruction.

### Recognition

-   Swelling and pain at the fracture site
-   Teeth do not occlude normally (bite is off)
-   Difficulty opening mouth fully
-   Numbness of lower lip (inferior alveolar nerve compression)

### Barton Bandage (Simple Stabilization)

A figure-8 bandage that supports the jaw:

1.  Use elastic bandage or strips of cloth (3-4 inches wide)
2.  Start at lower jaw, pass under chin
3.  Cross diagonally up one side of face, over the head
4.  Return diagonally down the opposite side, under chin again
5.  Repeat 2-3 times to create figure-8 pattern
6.  Secure with tape or ties; tension should support jaw without cutting off circulation
7.  Ensure teeth can still be slightly apart (allows swallowing, prevents aspiration if vomiting)

### Care Instructions

-   Soft diet only; liquid nutrition for severely restricted opening
-   Pain relief: NSAIDs or acetaminophen
-   Ice on swelling: 15 minutes on, 15 minutes off for first 48 hours
-   Maintain oral hygiene: Gentle rinses only, no vigorous brushing
-   Seek professional care as soon as possible for X-rays and proper fixation

<section id="complications">

## Complications & When to Stop

### Bleeding Complications

**Normal:** Bleeding for 15-30 minutes post-extraction; controlled by biting gauze
**Abnormal:** Bleeding continues beyond 45 minutes despite pressure; indicates vessel damage or coagulopathy

**Management of Excessive Bleeding:**
1. Apply direct pressure with gauze for full 20 minutes continuously (do not remove to check)
2. If still bleeding, pack socket with gauze soaked in 1:10,000 epinephrine or thrombin if available
3. If epinephrine unavailable, use pressure dressing: tie gauze firmly across jaw with elastic bandage
4. Patient should remain upright (not lying down); avoid talking/rinsing for 24 hours
5. Ice pack on cheek: 15 minutes on, 15 minutes off for first 6 hours
6. Avoid aspirin (increases bleeding); use ibuprofen (reduces inflammation, supports clotting)

:::danger
If bleeding does not stop after 1 hour of continuous pressure, patient has uncontrolled hemorrhage. Seek professional care immediately. Risk of anemia, airway compromise if swallowed blood triggers vomiting.
:::

### Infection Signs Requiring Immediate Antibiotic Escalation

- Temperature >101.5°F (38.6°C) within 48 hours of procedure
- Swelling spreading beyond local site (cheek to ear, jaw to neck, or crossing midline)
- Difficulty swallowing or breathing (stridor, voice changes)
- Trismus (inability to open mouth >2 fingers between front teeth)
- Pus discharge with foul odor

**Action:** Start IV antibiotics if available (ceftriaxone 1-2 g IV q12h). If oral only, switch to clindamycin 600 mg PO q6h for better tissue penetration in spreading infection.

### Nerve Damage Indicators

**Temporary (expected):** Mild paresthesia (tingling) of lower lip/chin lasting 2-4 hours after inferior alveolar block (anesthetic wearing off)

**Concerning (abnormal):** Numbness/tingling persisting beyond 24 hours; weakness or loss of sensation in lip, chin, or tongue

**Mechanism:** Needle trauma to inferior alveolar nerve (lower extractions) or infraorbital nerve (upper front extractions)

**Management:** Document baseline sensation. Most resolve within 2-8 weeks. If persisting beyond 3 months, permanent nerve injury likely. No emergency treatment; wound care/pain management only.

### When to Stop and Seek Professional Care

**Stop extraction immediately if:**
- Tooth splinters and large root fragment breaks off (risk of leaving root remnant; requires surgical removal)
- Excessive bone loss requires flap reflection and bone removal (risk of improper healing)
- Heavy bleeding despite elevation and pressure (vessel damage; needs sutures/ligation)
- Patient experiences sudden sharp pain during procedure (may indicate nerve/vessel hit)
- Patient has signs of systemic reaction: chest pain, severe dizziness, loss of consciousness (anesthetic overdose or cardiac event)

:::danger
**STOP and emergency referral if:**
- Anesthetic reaction: tremor, seizures, loss of consciousness, cardiac dysrhythmias. Treat: airway management, oxygen, monitor heart rhythm. Call emergency services immediately.
- Airway compromise: stridor, inability to swallow saliva, panic. Position upright, clear airway, prepare for intubation or cricothyrotomy if available.
- Uncontrolled hemorrhage: >100 mL blood loss in <5 minutes or continuing bright red bleeding >1 hour. Apply direct pressure and elevate head.
:::

</section>

<section id="aftercare">

## Recovery & Aftercare

### Immediate Post-Procedure (0-24 hours)

**Bleeding control:**
- Bite on gauze for 20-30 minutes continuously after extraction
- Do not rinse, spit, or use straw (dislodges clot)
- Do not smoke (nicotine delays healing)
- Sleep with head elevated on 2-3 pillows (reduces swelling)

**Pain management:**
- Ibuprofen 400-600 mg every 6 hours (preferred; anti-inflammatory)
- Acetaminophen 650 mg every 6 hours if ibuprofen contraindicated
- Do not exceed 3000 mg acetaminophen per day; 2400 mg ibuprofen per day
- Avoid alcohol (increases bleeding, interferes with antibiotics)

**Swelling reduction:**
- Ice pack: 15 minutes on, 15 minutes off for first 24 hours
- After 48 hours, switch to warm compresses (increases blood flow, promotes healing)
- Elevate head, avoid vigorous activity

**Dietary:**
- Soft foods only (soup, yogurt, mashed potatoes, smoothies)
- Avoid hot foods/drinks (can trigger bleeding)
- Avoid hard, crunchy, sticky foods (can dislodge clot or damage temporary filling)

:::tip
Drink plenty of water (2-3 liters daily) to support immune function and promote healing. Avoid carbonated beverages (can irritate extraction site).
:::

### Days 2-7: Socket Healing Phase

**Socket appearance:** May appear white/gray (fibrin clot formation) or yellow (granulation tissue); this is normal

**Infection monitoring:** Check daily for signs of infection (fever, increased pain after day 2, pus drainage, spreading swelling)

**Rinsing protocol:** After 24 hours, start gentle salt-water rinses (1/2 teaspoon salt in 8 oz warm water) after meals and before bed to keep area clean

**Activity restrictions:**
- No vigorous rinsing or use of water pik (dislodges protective clot)
- No smoking for at least 7 days (impairs healing)
- Avoid strenuous exercise for 5-7 days (increases bleeding risk)

### Weeks 2-4: Bone Remodeling Phase

**Socket changes:** Gradually fills in with bone; may feel tender when chewing nearby
**Temporary filling:** Should remain in place; monitor for loss (if lost, clean with salt water and replace)
**Return to normal diet:** Slowly introduce slightly harder foods as comfort permits; protect extraction site from hot/cold irritation

**Duration of complete healing:** Surface heals in 2-3 weeks; bone remodeling continues 6-8 months. Final implant or bridge placement typically waits 3-6 months to allow full bone healing.

:::info-box
**Dry Socket Risk:** Occurs in 2-5% of extractions (higher in smokers, oral contraceptive users, difficult extractions). Symptoms: severe throbbing pain starting days 3-4, foul odor, visible bone in socket. Management: gentle saline irrigation, temporary iodoform gauze packing changed daily, pain relief, antibiotics if infection present.
:::

<section id="troubleshooting">

## Troubleshooting & Field Adaptation

### Problem: Tooth Breaks During Extraction

**Cause:** Excessive force, curved roots, long extraction time, or weak tooth structure

**Field fix:**
1. Stop pulling; assess remaining tooth portion visible
2. If crown is removed but root remains, root must be extracted or will become infected
3. Use elevator to gently loosen remaining root; often mobilizes separately
4. If root is deeply seated and cannot be removed, leave it in place and pack socket. Start antibiotics immediately (penicillin allergy = clindamycin).
5. Infection risk: High. Rinse daily with salt water; watch for abscess formation. Professional removal will be needed when available.

**Prevention:** Rock tooth side-to-side gently, never straight pulling. Take extra time with multi-rooted teeth (molars). Extract during peak anesthesia (5-15 minutes post-injection).

### Problem: Severe Swelling Despite Antibiotics

**Cause:** Either inadequate antibiotic penetration or wrong antibiotic (resistant organism)

**Field response:**
1. Increase ibuprofen frequency to 600 mg every 4 hours (max 3000 mg/day) to reduce inflammation
2. Ice-pack aggressively: 20 minutes on, 10 minutes off during waking hours
3. Elevate head above heart at all times
4. Encourage drainage if abscess present (warm compress encourages pointing; drain if accessible)
5. Switch antibiotic class: If started on amoxicillin, switch to clindamycin 300-450 mg every 6 hours (better anaerobe coverage)
6. Check for spread: If swelling extends to other side of face, below jaw, or toward eye/ear, infection is spreading and professional care is critical

**When to consider extraction:** If swelling does not improve within 72 hours of antibiotics and drainage, the causative tooth may need extraction to stop infection source

### Problem: Temporary Filling Falls Out

**Recognition:** Loss of seal; patient reports piece came out while eating; visible cavity again

**Immediate management:**
1. Clean the cavity gently with water and let dry completely
2. Remove any remaining old filling material with explorer/pick
3. Pack dry if wet; use gauze wad to absorb fluid, discard, repeat
4. Prepare new filling material:
   - **ZOE:** Mix zinc oxide powder + eugenol (3:1 ratio) to thick putty
   - **DIY:** Beeswax + pine resin softened in warm water; mold into cavity shape
5. Fill cavity completely; smooth surface flush with tooth
6. Instruct patient: avoid that side when chewing for 24-48 hours until set

**Prevention:** Pack filling generously above cavity level; it will compress as it sets. Use material that hardens (not stays soft like beeswax alone). Instruct patient on diet restrictions.

### Problem: Numbness After Anesthetic Injection

**Normal:** Tingling in lower lip, tongue, or chin for 2-4 hours (anesthetic effect)

**Prolonged (concerning):** Numbness persists beyond 24 hours

**Possible causes:**
- Needle hit nerve during injection (transient paresthesia common)
- Hematoma (bruising) compressing nerve
- Allergic reaction to anesthetic (rare)

**Management:**
- Do not attempt repeat injection
- Document baseline sensation (can patient feel touch? pinprick?)
- Reassure patient: Most resolve within weeks
- Monitor for progression (spreading numbness = worse injury)
- If numbness worsens or new symptoms develop (facial droop, eye closure weakness), seek professional care

**Prevention:** Use proper injection technique (lingula landmark for inferior alveolar block). Inject slowly. Do not inject if patient reports sharp pain traveling to ear/temple (near nerve).

### Problem: Unable to Achieve Adequate Anesthesia

**Signs:** Patient still reports pain during extraction despite anesthetic injection

**Causes:**
- Needle missed target (incorrect anatomy landmark)
- Insufficient anesthetic volume or too dilute
- Patient anxiety creating pain perception
- Infection/inflammation preventing anesthetic diffusion

**Field alternatives:**
1. **Topical anesthesia escalation:** Apply 20% benzocaine spray (if available) directly to surgical site; wait 2-3 minutes; repeat
2. **Clove oil (eugenol):** Soak cotton ball in clove oil, apply to tooth + gum for 3-5 minutes. Provides 15-30 minute window of topical numbing
3. **Systemic pain relief:** Give ibuprofen 600 mg + acetaminophen 650 mg 1 hour before procedure (synergistic effect)
4. **Anxiety reduction:** Slow breathing, reassurance. If available, oral sedation (diazepam 5-10 mg) may help
5. **Delay extraction:** If patient cannot tolerate, postpone 24 hours. Provide antibiotics for infection; pain control. Some teeth loosen as infection progresses, making extraction easier later.

:::warning
Do not attempt forced extraction without adequate anesthesia. Patient trauma, incomplete extraction, excessive bleeding, and nerve damage more likely. Trust patient's pain report.
:::

<section id="common-mistakes">

## Common Mistakes & How to Avoid Them

| Mistake | Why It's Bad | Prevention |
|---------|-------------|-----------|
| Using excessive force to extract | Fractures tooth, damages jaw, excessive bleeding, nerve damage | Rock tooth gently side-to-side. Use elevator FIRST to loosen. Let anesthetic work (wait 5-10 min). |
| Extracting without anesthesia | Extreme patient pain, thrashing, airway risk, incomplete extraction | Always test anesthesia. Have alternatives (topical, systemic) ready. Nerve blocks require landmarks. |
| Leaving root fragments behind | Root becomes infected; abscess forms later; requires surgical removal | After extraction, visually inspect socket. Palpate with gloved finger for fragments. Remove all pieces. |
| Injecting anesthetic intravascularly (into blood vessel) | Rapid systemic toxicity: tremor, seizures, cardiac dysrhythmia | Aspirate before injecting (pull back on syringe plunger). If blood returns, withdraw and reposition. |
| Packing socket too loosely | Blood clot dislodges; dry socket develops; delayed healing, pain | Pack gauze firmly. Patient bites hard for 20-30 min. If bleeding continues, pack tighter or use hemostatic agent. |
| Not recognizing spreading infection | Infection spreads to neck/mediastinum; airway obstruction; sepsis | Monitor temperature and swelling daily. Swelling crossing midline or extending to ear/neck = emergency. Start antibiotics immediately. |
| Prescribing wrong antibiotic dose | Infection not cured; bacteria develop resistance | Use standard dosing: amoxicillin 500 mg every 8 hours × 7 days. Document each dose given. |
| Ignoring dry socket warning signs | Painful healing; bone exposure; infection risk | Screen days 3-4 post-extraction. Severe throbbing pain + foul odor = dry socket. Treat with saline irrigation. |
| Allowing patient to resume hard diet too early | Dislodges clot; reopens socket; bleeding | Soft diet for 1 week minimum. No chewing near extraction site for 2 weeks. |
| Not documenting procedure or complications | No record for handoff to professional; repeat procedures; poor follow-up | Document: tooth number, reason for extraction, anesthetic used, bleeding control, complications, antibiotics given, date. |

<section id="field-adaptations">

## Field Adaptations for Low-Resource Settings

### Anesthesia Without Injectable Lidocaine

**Topical alternative: Clove Oil Method (Eugenol)**
- Soak cotton ball or pad in clove oil
- Hold against affected tooth and gum for 2-3 minutes
- Onset: 2-5 minutes; duration: 15-30 minutes
- Numbs surface only; insufficient for deep anesthesia but helpful for pain reduction pre-extraction
- Repeat application as needed
- Safe if not swallowed in large quantities; mild bitter taste

**Systemic support (without oral sedation):**
- Ibuprofen 600 mg + acetaminophen 500-650 mg together (wait 60 minutes before procedure)
- Willow bark tea: steep 2-3 teaspoons dried bark in 8 oz hot water for 10 minutes; drink (contains salicin, precursor to salicylic acid; analgesic and anti-inflammatory)
- Deep breathing exercises: slow, controlled breathing reduces anxiety and pain perception
- Distraction: conversation, counting, focusing on distant object

### Extraction Tools Without Surgical Instruments

**Improvised elevator (extraction):**
- Flat-head screwdriver (1/4" width); file smooth to remove sharp edges
- Old chisel or surgical chisel (if available); smooth all edges
- Butter knife (wider, less precise; use only if no alternative)
- Technique: Insert under tooth at gumline; lever gently side-to-side (rock, not pry). Never force; tooth loosens gradually.

**Improvised forceps:**
- Locking pliers (vise-grips); tighten grip screw just enough to hold without crushing
- Standard smooth-jaw pliers; wrap jaw with cloth to prevent slipping if tooth wet
- Technique: Grasp at crown (visible part above gum), not root. Rock gently before pulling. Apply steady pressure; do not twist.

**Improvised mouth mirror:**
- Polished stainless steel sheet or spoon bowl
- Position in mouth angled to reflect light source (flashlight, sunlight)
- Alternative: Use flashlight alone to illuminate; work by direct vision

### Temporary Filling Without Commercial Cement

**Zinc oxide + eugenol (ZOE) DIY:**
1. Zinc oxide powder: Available in calamine lotion or zinc ointment (pharmacies, first aid kits). Scrape/dissolve in small amount of water or alcohol to isolate powder.
2. Eugenol: Clove oil (many home kitchens) or dried ground cloves
3. Mix zinc oxide powder + clove oil (or extract) to thick putty consistency (3 parts zinc oxide : 1 part eugenol by weight)
4. Fill cavity completely; let harden 24-48 hours

**Beeswax + plant resin:**
1. Beeswax: Candles, natural sources
2. Pine resin or tree sap (if available): Collect sticky resin from pine/spruce bark
3. Soften in warm water or near heat; mold into cavity shape while warm
4. Hardens as it cools; remove by warming and picking out gently

**Calcium hydroxide (slaked lime):**
1. Obtain quicklime (CaO) or slaked lime (Ca(OH)2 — calcium hydroxide)
2. Mix with water to form thick paste
3. Apply to cavity; acts as antimicrobial and biostimulant
4. Set: 1-2 hours; can be removed with explorer

### Antibiotics from Field Sources

**If pharmaceutical antibiotics unavailable:**

**Herbal antimicrobials (not substitutes, but supporting care):**
- **Garlic:** Raw or mashed garlic directly on abscess; antimicrobial compounds (allicin). 2-3 cloves 3-4 times daily, or apply paste to gum
- **Turmeric:** Curcumin has antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties. Mix with water to paste; apply to gum or use as rinse (1/2 teaspoon in warm water)
- **Honey (raw):** Antimicrobial and wound-healing properties. Apply directly to extraction site or abscess
- **Salt water rinse:** 1 teaspoon salt in 8 oz warm water; kills surface bacteria; use 4-6 times daily
- **Willow bark tea:** Salicin provides analgesia and reduces inflammation; brew 2-3 teaspoons in 8 oz hot water; drink or use as rinse

**Effectiveness caveat:** Herbal measures reduce inflammation and support healing but do NOT cure systemic infection. If fever, spreading swelling, or severe symptoms present, escalate care or use pharmaceutical antibiotics if any available.

:::tip
In true resource-scarcity settings, prioritize: clean technique, pain control, swelling management (ice), nutritious diet (supports immune response), and rest. Infection prevention through proper sterile technique is as important as treatment.
:::

<section id="prevention">

## Preventive Dental Care

### Daily Oral Hygiene

-   **Brushing:** 2-3 times daily with fluoride toothpaste (1450 ppm fluoride); use soft bristles
-   **Flossing:** Daily to remove food and plaque from between teeth
-   **Rinsing:** Warm salt water after meals helps remove debris
-   **Avoid sugary foods:** Bacteria ferment sugars, producing acid that demineralizes enamel

### Fluoride Application

Fluoride strengthens enamel by forming fluorapatite, which is more acid-resistant than normal hydroxyapatite.

-   **Topical:** Fluoride toothpaste (1000-1450 ppm) or rinse (0.05% daily or 0.2% weekly)
-   **Professional:** Sodium fluoride gel (1.1%) or acidulated phosphate fluoride (1.23%) applied annually
-   **Systemic (children):** Fluoridated water (1 ppm) during tooth development

### Professional Cleaning

Tartar (calcified plaque) can only be removed professionally. Buildup leads to gum disease and tooth loss. Professional cleanings recommended every 6-12 months.

## Improvised Dental Tools

### Dental Elevators (Extraction Tools)

-   **Material:** Flat-head screwdriver (1/4" to 1/2" width)
-   **Preparation:** File smooth all edges; remove sharp burrs that would cut gums. Edges should be rounded, not sharp.
-   **Technique:** Insert under tooth at gumline, gently lever to break periodontal ligament. Rock side-to-side, not back-and-forth, to avoid crushing tooth.
-   **Alternative:** Chisel (old or broken, filed smooth); butter knife (though wider and less precise).

### Improvised Forceps

-   **Material:** Locking pliers (vise-grips) or standard pliers with smooth jaws
-   **Modification:** If teeth rough, file smooth or wrap jaws with cloth to avoid slipping and crushing tooth.
-   **Grip:** Grasp tooth crown (visible part), not root. Root is not accessible and is fragile.
-   **Extraction:** Rock gently side-to-side to expand socket, then steady upward pull. Do NOT twist or pull at angles—risk of breaking tooth and leaving root.

### Anesthesia: Clove Oil (Eugenol)

-   **Agent:** Eugenol, a natural compound in clove oil, is a local anesthetic that numbs tissue on contact.
-   **Application:** Soak cotton ball in clove oil, apply directly to affected tooth and surrounding gum. Repeat as needed.
-   **Onset:** 2-5 minutes for effect; lasts 15-30 minutes.
-   **Limitation:** Numbs surface only; does not penetrate deep tooth pulp. Helpful for pre-extraction anesthesia but not sufficient for major procedures.
-   **Side effects:** Rare if applied topically; excessive swallowing of clove oil can cause upset stomach.

### Tooth Powder Recipe

**Basic Tooth Powder:**  
Baking soda: 2 parts (sodium bicarbonate—mild abrasive, slightly alkaline, deodorizing)  
Fine salt: 1 part (abrasive, antimicrobial)  
Optional: Crushed charcoal: 1/4 to 1/2 part (absorbs staining compounds, slightly more abrasive)  
  
Mix thoroughly. Store in sealed container.  
Usage: Damp toothbrush, apply 1/4 teaspoon powder, brush gently.  
Effectiveness: Removes surface staining and plaque. Less fluoride benefit than modern toothpaste, but adequate for mechanical cleaning.

## Chew Sticks for Tooth Cleaning

### Miswak (Salvadora persica)

-   **Traditional use:** Used for 7,000+ years in Middle East and Africa for tooth cleaning.
-   **Properties:** Contains natural antimicrobial compounds and mild abrasive fibers.
-   **Preparation:** Cut fresh twig (pencil-thick), chew one end until fibers fray into brush-like form.
-   **Usage:** Rub fray-end against teeth in vertical motions. Can be softened by soaking in water.
-   **Effectiveness:** Removes plaque and debris; naturally fights bacteria. Clinical studies show effectiveness similar to modern toothbrushes.

### Willow Twigs

-   **Alternative:** If miswak not available, fresh willow twigs work similarly.
-   **Selection:** Young, tender twigs; avoid old, brittle wood.
-   **Preparation:** Cut 6-inch sections, chew one end until fibers separate into brush.
-   **Properties:** Willow contains salicin (salicylic acid precursor), which provides slight analgesic and antimicrobial benefit.

### General Chew Stick Technique

-   **Chewing motion:** Gently work one end with teeth to fray and soften fibers; do not bite hard enough to splinter.
-   **Brushing:** Once fray-end is prepared, rub against each tooth surface: outer surfaces, inner surfaces, and biting surfaces.
-   **Vertical strokes:** Brush downward from gum toward tooth tip; this motion helps clean under gumline.
-   **Frequency:** Use after meals; takes 5-10 minutes per session.
-   **Lifespan:** Single chew stick lasts 2-3 days with regular use; then discard and prepare new stick.
