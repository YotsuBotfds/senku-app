## Overview

Fermentation is the metabolic process by which microorganisms (primarily bacteria and yeasts) break down sugars and other organic compounds in the absence of oxygen, producing acid, alcohol, or both as byproducts. This process preserves food, enhances nutritional value, improves digestibility, and creates distinctive flavors. Understanding fermentation microbiology, environmental requirements, and troubleshooting transforms food preservation from an unpredictable art into a reliable, scientifically grounded practice.

![Fermentation timeline showing bacterial succession and pH changes over time](../assets/svgs/fermentation-science.svg)

This guide covers the primary fermentation pathways (lacto-fermentation, alcohol fermentation, acetic acid fermentation), microbial ecology, optimal temperature and salt levels, starter culture development and maintenance, common batch failures and remedies, pH testing and safety, and applications across fermented foods and beverages.

**Scope note:** This guide focuses on the *microbiology, ecology, and theory* behind fermentation — organism identification, succession dynamics, salt/temperature science, starter culture maintenance, pH safety, and troubleshooting diagnosis. For hands-on step-by-step recipes, fermentation kinetics, yeast biology deep-dives, vessel design, and industrial applications, see the companion guide **Fermentation Science (Applied)** (GD-424).

## Primary Fermentation Pathways

### Lacto-Fermentation (Lactic Acid Fermentation)

Lactic acid bacteria (LAB) convert sugars into lactic acid, creating a sour product that inhibits spoilage organisms.

**Dominant organisms:** Lactobacillus species (L. plantarum, L. brevis, L. pentosus), Leuconostoc mesenteroides, Pediococcus acidilactici.

**Chemical pathway:**
```
Glucose (sugar) → Pyruvate → Lactic Acid
C6H12O6 → 2 C3H6O3
```

**Conditions:**
- **Salt concentration:** 2–5% by weight of vegetables (or 2–10 g/L brine).
- **Temperature:** 55–72°F (13–22°C) optimal for L. plantarum; higher temperatures favor Leuconostoc (faster fermentation, less complex flavor).
- **Anaerobic environment:** Essential; submerged vegetables prevent mold and oxidation.
- **pH final:** Typically pH 3.5–4.0 (sufficiently acidic to prevent botulism and spoilage).

**Timeline:**
- **Days 1–3:** Leuconostoc and other heterofermentative bacteria dominate; produce CO2 bubbles (visible fermentation).
- **Days 3–7:** Transition; homofermentative Lactobacillus becomes dominant.
- **Days 7–14:** Lactobacillus dominates; acid accumulation continues; flavor develops.
- **Days 14–28:** Maturation; flavor complex; fermentation slows as pH drops.
- **Storage:** Continues slowly at cool temperature (40–50°F) for months or years.

**Products:** Sauerkraut, kimchi, naturally fermented pickles, lacto-fermented vegetables, fermented beverages (kvass).

### Alcohol Fermentation

Yeasts convert sugars into ethanol (alcohol) and CO2, creating beverages.

**Dominant organisms:** Saccharomyces cerevisiae (beer, wine, bread), Saccharomyces pastorianus (lager beer), wild yeasts.

**Chemical pathway:**
```
Glucose → Pyruvate → Ethanol + CO2 + Energy
C6H12O6 → 2 C2H5OH + 2 CO2
```

**Conditions:**
- **Sugar concentration:** 10–20% (must) for wine; 4–8% for beer; high sugar concentrations inhibit yeast (osmotic stress).
- **Temperature:** 50–80°F (10–27°C) depending on yeast strain; ale yeasts prefer 60–70°F; lager yeasts prefer 50–60°F.
- **Anaerobic environment:** Fermentation occurs anaerobically; aerobic conditions favor yeast reproduction but inhibit alcohol production.
- **Nitrogen availability:** Yeast needs amino acids (from malt proteins, nutrient additions, or from fruits).
- **pH:** 3.0–4.5 optimal; naturally occurs in most fruits and grains.

**Timeline:**
- **Hours 0–24:** Yeast activation; respiration phase (some aerobic activity if present).
- **Days 1–7:** Peak fermentation; vigorous CO2 production (visible bubbling).
- **Days 7–21:** Fermentation slowing; sediment (dead yeast) accumulating at bottom.
- **Days 21–60:** Maturation and aging; flavor development; sediment settling.
- **Post-fermentation:** Clarification occurs over weeks/months; flavor continues evolving.

**Final alcohol:** Typically 5–20% ABV depending on initial sugar; excess sugar results in residual sweetness or yeast stress at high alcohol.

**Products:** Wine, beer, cider, mead, distilled spirits (requires equipment and knowledge beyond basic fermentation).

### Acetic Acid Fermentation

Acetic acid bacteria convert ethanol into acetic acid, creating vinegar.

**Dominant organisms:** Acetobacter aceti, Acetobacter pasteurianus, Gluconobacter species.

**Chemical pathway:**
```
Ethanol → Acetaldehyde → Acetic Acid
C2H5OH → CH3CHO → CH3COOH
```

**Conditions:**
- **Oxygen requirement:** Requires aerobic conditions (acetic acid bacteria are obligate aerobes).
- **Starting substrate:** Dilute alcohol (4–10% ABV) is optimal; too strong or too weak results in slow fermentation.
- **Temperature:** 68–86°F (20–30°C) optimal; slow at lower temperatures.
- **pH:** Tolerates highly acidic environments (pH 2.5–3.5); acetic acid itself inhibits competing microbes.

**Timeline:**
- **Weeks 1–2:** Slow initial activity (acetic acid bacteria establish).
- **Weeks 2–8:** Peak fermentation; clear liquid becomes cloudy as bacteria multiply; strong vinegar smell develops.
- **Weeks 8–12:** Maturation; mother (cellulose and bacteria biofilm) forms on surface; fermentation slowing.
- **Months 3–12:** Aging improves flavor complexity; sediment settling.

**Products:** Vinegar from wine, cider, or diluted alcohol; also produced from fruits and honey.

## Microbial Ecology in Fermentation

### Microbial Competition and Succession

Fermentation involves predictable microbial succession:

**Initial phase:** Many microorganisms present (natural microbiota on vegetables, in brine, in starter cultures).

**Selective pressures:**
- **Salt:** Inhibits salt-sensitive pathogens (E. coli, Salmonella) while favoring halotolerant LAB.
- **Acid:** Drops as fermentation proceeds (from pH ~5–6 to pH 3–4); acid-tolerant organisms thrive; acid-sensitive organisms die off.
- **Anaerobic environment:** Favors obligate anaerobes (Lactobacillus) and facultative anaerobes; inhibits strict aerobes (spoilage molds, acetic acid bacteria in anaerobic conditions).
- **Temperature:** Favors specific species; cool favors complex flavor development; warm favors rapid fermentation but less complexity.

**Result:** Within days, LAB dominate (producing acid faster than other organisms), and by week 2, the ferment is so acidic that few other microorganisms can survive.

### Spontaneous vs. Inoculated Fermentation

**Spontaneous fermentation (no added starter):**
- Relies on natural LAB present on vegetable surfaces and in the environment.
- Less predictable; fermentation may be slow or fail if initial LAB load is low.
- May include unwanted organisms (mold, film-forming yeasts) if sanitation is poor.
- Flavor develops from mixed microbial communities; often complex and regionally distinctive.

**Inoculated fermentation (added starter culture):**
- Starter culture (e.g., salt, LAB suspension, or whey) provides high initial load of desired organisms.
- Fermentation begins faster; outcome more predictable.
- Reduces risk of mold or spoilage organisms becoming dominant before LAB acidify the environment.
- Flavor more uniform but potentially less complex than wild fermentation.

### Biofilm Formation (Mother/Pellicle)

Many fermented products form a biofilm—a visible layer of yeast or bacteria on the surface.

**Characteristics:**
- **Kombucha SCOBY (Symbiotic Culture of Bacteria and Yeast):** Primarily Acetobacter and Zygosaccharomyces; cellulose-rich biofilm; regenerates with each fermentation.
- **Vinegar mother:** Cellulose layer formed by acetic acid bacteria; acts as a seed culture for future batches.
- **Kefir grains:** SCOBY containing Lactobacillus and yeasts; used as a continuous starter.
- **Sourdough starter:** Mixture of Lactobacillus and wild yeasts; maintains through regular feeding.

**Function:** Biofilms create an oxygen barrier, maintain pH stability, and produce beneficial secondary metabolites (vitamins, antimicrobial compounds).

## Starter Culture Creation and Maintenance

### Creating a Lacto-Fermentation Starter

**From vegetable-only fermentation (spontaneous):**

1. Ferment cabbage (high natural LAB) with 2% salt at 65–70°F for 7–14 days.
2. By day 7, fermentation is vigorous (LAB-dominated); culture is ready.
3. Store in cool, dark place; remains viable for months.
4. Use 1–3 tablespoons per liter of new ferment.

**From whey (if available):**

1. Collect whey from yogurt or cheese (contains abundant LAB and some yeasts).
2. Mix 1/4 cup whey per liter of brine (2–5% salt, water).
3. Add desired vegetables; ferment at 65–72°F for 5–10 days.
4. Results in faster, more reliable fermentation than spontaneous.

**From purchased culture (most reliable):**

1. Use commercial powdered LAB culture (available from fermentation suppliers).
2. Rehydrate according to instructions; often 1 packet for 1–2 gallons of brine.
3. Add to vegetables; ferment at specified temperature.
4. After fermentation, save liquid to use as starter for next batch.

### Maintaining a Continuous Starter

**Lactobacillus starter maintenance:**

1. **Regular feeding:** If not fermented regularly, feed starter every 2–4 weeks with sugar and salt to keep bacteria active.
   - Mix: 1 tablespoon sugar, 1 tablespoon salt, 1 cup water per cup of starter.
   - Ferment 24–48 hours at room temperature.
   - Discard half, replace with fresh fed brine, or use in new ferment.

2. **Storage:** Cool (40–50°F) slows activity but maintains viability for months. Room temperature requires more frequent feeding (every 2 weeks).

3. **Signs of health:**
   - Pleasant sour smell (vinegar-like or lactic acid smell).
   - Clear, tan, or slightly cloudy liquid.
   - Possible sediment at bottom.

4. **Signs of problems:**
   - Pink or orange hue (spoilage bacteria, Serratia marcescens).
   - Foul smell (rotten, sulfurous).
   - Visible mold.
   - Discard and restart if any of these appear.

**Yeast starter (for brewing):**

1. Keep in sterile, sealed container.
2. Feed weekly: 1 cup starter, add 1 cup sugar-water solution, aerate by swirling, seal loosely.
3. Store cool (50–60°F).
4. After 30 days, if not used, discard half and feed again.

**Sourdough starter:**

1. Mix 50g flour, 50g water daily.
2. After 24 hours, discard half (or use in baking).
3. Feed with fresh 50g flour, 50g water.
4. Repeat for 5–7 days until vigorous bubbling occurs (ready to use).
5. Storage: Room temperature requires daily feeding; cool storage (50–60°F) requires feeding every 5–7 days.

## Temperature and Salt Requirements

### Salt Concentration

**Why salt is critical:**

Salt preserves by:
- Creating osmotic stress (water leaves microbial cells).
- Slowing fermentation (allows flavor development).
- Selecting for salt-tolerant LAB while inhibiting salt-sensitive pathogens.
- Preventing mold growth on the surface.

**Concentration guidelines (by product type):**

| Product | Salt % by weight | Brine concentration | Purpose |
|---------|------------------|-------------------|---------|
| Sauerkraut | 2–3% | 2–3 g per 100g cabbage | Retains crispness |
| Kimchi | 2–3% | 2–3% brine | Traditional, balanced |
| Pickles (cucumber) | 3–5% | 3–5% brine | Maintains crunchiness |
| Fermented vegetable medley | 2–4% | 2–4% brine | Balanced preservation/flavor |
| Miso (soy ferment) | 5–10% | High salt paste | Extended aging, prevents mold |
| Fish sauce | 20–30% | High salt | Long preservation, unique flavor |

**Temperature effects on fermentation speed and salt tolerance:**

| Temperature | Effect | Recommended salt |
|-------------|--------|------------------|
| 50–55°F | Very slow; complex flavor | 2–3% (less inhibition needed) |
| 55–65°F | Slow; balanced | 2–3% (standard) |
| 65–72°F | Moderate; good flavor development | 2–4% |
| 72–80°F | Fast; less complex | 3–5% (more inhibition needed) |
| Above 80°F | Very fast; risk of mushy texture | 4–5% (prevent spoilage) |

### Temperature Optimization

**For sauerkraut and vegetable ferments:**
- Optimal: 60–65°F for 3–4 weeks (complex, layered flavor).
- Acceptable: 65–72°F for 2–3 weeks (faster but less complex).
- Avoid: Above 75°F (mushy texture, simple flavor).

**For miso and bean pastes:**
- Optimal: 60–72°F for months to years (slow flavor development).
- Faster: 72–80°F for weeks (adequate but less complex).

**For alcohol fermentation:**
- Ale yeasts: 60–70°F optimal.
- Lager yeasts: 50–60°F optimal.
- Temperature fluctuation during fermentation alters yeast performance and flavor profile.

<section id="troubleshooting">

## Troubleshooting Failed Batches

:::warning
**Safety note:** If you observe signs of pathogenic contamination (unusual colors, foul odors, slime not typical of healthy ferments), discard the batch immediately.
:::

### Mold Growth

**Appearance:** White, green, or black fuzzy growth on surface or submerged vegetables.

**Cause:** Oxygen exposure; vegetables not fully submerged; contaminated starter.

**Prevention:**
- Use weight (glass weight, plastic bag filled with brine, plate weighted down) to keep vegetables 1+ inch submerged.
- Use tight lid that allows CO2 escape (airlock, or burping daily).
- Sanitize all equipment before fermentation.

**Remedy:**
- If mold is only on top layer (surface), carefully skim off mold and discard. If vegetables below are unaffected and smell normal, continue fermentation.
- If mold penetrates deeply or ferment smells off, discard entire batch.

### Off-Flavors and Smells

**Sulfurous or rotten smell:**
- **Cause:** Anaerobic bacteria (Clostridium) or Leuconostoc producing compound sulfides.
- **Prevention:** Use adequate salt (3%+); ensure starter culture is healthy; avoid bruised vegetables.
- **Remedy:** Early in fermentation (days 1–3), continue fermentation (smell often dissipates as LAB dominates and acid rises). If smell persists past day 5, discard.

**Vinegary smell (excess acetic acid):**
- **Cause:** Extended aerobic exposure (mold formed then removed, or container leaked) or acetic acid bacteria contamination.
- **Prevention:** Ensure anaerobic conditions; use airlock or tight lid.
- **Remedy:** If vinegary but pH is safe (below 3.5), ferment is still edible; use in cooking or as a vinegar.

**Slimy or mucoid texture without visible mold:**
- **Cause:** Excessive Leuconostoc mesenteroides (produces dextran, a polysaccharide slime).
- **Prevention:** Use higher salt (4–5%); ferment at cooler temperatures (55–65°F) favoring Lactobacillus over Leuconostoc.
- **Remedy:** If texture is unpleasant but smell is normal and pH is safe, ferment is safe; texture may firm after extended cool storage.

**Flat, boring flavor (no fermentation activity):**
- **Cause:** Insufficient LAB (low salt, contaminated starter, insufficient time at adequate temperature).
- **Prevention:** Use robust starter culture; ensure salt is adequate; maintain temperature above 55°F.
- **Remedy:** If no signs of mold or off-odors, continue fermentation at 70°F for additional 1–2 weeks. If no improvement, discard and restart.

### Mushy Vegetables

**Cause:** Temperature above 72°F (rapid enzyme activity breaks down cell walls); high salt (osmotic breakdown); extended fermentation at high temperature.

**Prevention:**
- Ferment at 60–68°F.
- Use 2–3% salt only.
- Use firm vegetables (avoid overripe tomatoes, soft cucumbers).
- Harvest cucumbers young (firmer cell structure).

**Remedy:**
- Accept the texture (still edible and safe); use in soups or blended applications rather than as whole pickles.
- Reduce salt and temperature for next batch.

### Carbonation Issues

**Insufficient fizz (flat ferment):**
- **Cause:** Slow fermentation (low temperature, high salt, weak starter); CO2 escaping past lid.
- **Remedy:** Check lid seal; ensure temperature is 60–70°F; reopen to allow re-fermentation if stored in airtight container.

**Excessive carbonation (over-pressurization):**
- **Cause:** Bottled too early in fermentation; fermentation continuing in sealed bottle.
- **Remedy:** Open bottle slowly (point away from face); burp container daily during active fermentation to release CO2 before bottling.

## pH Testing and Safety

### pH Basics

**pH scale:** 0 (most acidic) to 14 (most alkaline); 7 is neutral.
- Lactic acid ferments: pH 3.5–4.0 (acidic).
- Alcohol ferments: pH 3.0–4.5 (acidic).
- Acetic acid ferments: pH 2.0–3.5 (very acidic).
- **Food safety threshold:** Below pH 4.6, Clostridium botulinum (botulism pathogen) cannot grow or produce toxin.

### pH Testing Methods

**pH strips (simplest, adequate for fermentation):**
- Dip into ferment; compare color to chart.
- Cost: $5–15 for 100 strips.
- Accuracy: ±0.5 pH units (sufficient for fermentation).
- Procedure: Test at days 3, 7, 14, and when storing.

**pH meter (most accurate):**
- Electronic meter with electrode; calibrated to known pH buffers (7.0, 4.0, 10.0).
- Cost: $20–100 for basic; $200+ for laboratory-grade.
- Accuracy: ±0.1 pH units.
- Procedure: Calibrate before use; rinse electrode between tests; store electrode in storage solution.

**Taste and smell (traditional, imperfect but useful):**
- Healthy ferment tastes sour (acidic) and pleasant.
- Unsafe ferment tastes rotten or off.
- Not reliable alone; combine with pH testing.

### Safety Implications

**pH < 4.0:** Safe from botulism; storage at cool temperature (50–70°F) prevents mold and spoilage organisms.

**pH 4.0–4.6:** Borderline; botulism risk is minimal but not zero. Store cool and monitor for signs of spoilage.

**pH > 4.6:** Potentially unsafe; botulism risk exists for anaerobic ferments. Must use additional preservation (pressure canning if shelf-stable storage is desired).

:::warning
**Botulism warning:** Fermented foods stored in anaerobic conditions (sealed jars, buried in brine) at pH above 4.6 carry botulism risk. If uncertain of pH, assume unsafe for long-term storage without pressure canning.
:::

### Sauerkraut (Cabbage Ferment)

**Basic recipe:**
1. Shred cabbage; mix with 2% salt by weight (e.g., 10 lbs cabbage + 0.2 lbs salt).
2. Pack into jar, pressing down; liquid rises to cover.
3. Weight down to keep submerged; cover with cloth or loose lid.
4. Ferment at 60–70°F for 2–4 weeks.
5. Taste daily after day 7; store cool when desired sourness is reached.

**Variations:**
- Add caraway, dill, or juniper seeds for flavor.
- Add ginger, turmeric, or other spices.
- Mix with other vegetables (beets, turnips, cauliflower).

**Yield:** 1 lb cabbage ≈ 0.5 lb finished sauerkraut (water loss and fermentation byproducts).

### Kimchi (Korean Spiced Ferment)

**Basic recipe:**
1. Cut Napa cabbage into 2-inch pieces.
2. Brine in 3% salt solution overnight (draws moisture out).
3. Drain; mix with paste of gochugaru (Korean red chili powder), garlic, ginger, fish sauce (or salt for vegetarian), and sugar.
4. Pack tightly into jar; ferment at 60–72°F for 1–2 weeks.
5. Refrigerate when desired fermentation is reached.

**Characteristics:**
- Spicy, complex ferment.
- Fish sauce adds umami; vegetarian versions omit this.
- Continues slowly fermenting in refrigerator (pH drops over weeks).

### Naturally Fermented Pickles

**Basic recipe:**
1. Pack small, firm cucumbers into jar with dill, garlic, peppercorns.
2. Cover with 3–5% salt brine.
3. Weight down; ferment at 60–70°F for 2–6 weeks.
4. Taste after week 2; store cool.

**Differences from vinegar pickles:**
- No vinegar added; fermentation creates the acid.
- Probiotics remain alive (more digestive benefit).
- Flavor is fresher, less sharp than vinegar pickles.

### Yogurt and Dairy Ferments

**Basic yogurt:**
1. Heat milk to 180°F (pasteurization); cool to 110–115°F.
2. Add 2–3 tablespoons live-culture yogurt (starter) per quart; stir gently.
3. Maintain 110–115°F for 4–12 hours (longer = tangier).
4. Refrigerate; sets fully after cooling.

**Kefir (grain ferment):**
1. Add kefir grains to milk (1 tablespoon grains per cup milk).
2. Ferment at 68–75°F for 12–24 hours (until grains rise or ferment separates).
3. Strain grains into fresh milk; repeat.
4. Second ferment (optional): Store finished kefir in sealed jar for 24 hours for more carbonation and tartness.

### Miso and Fermented Pastes

**Basic miso:**
1. Cook soybeans; inoculate with koji (aspergillus oryzae mold culture, available as inoculant).
2. Incubate at 77°F for 2–3 days until white mold covers soybeans.
3. Mix inoculated soybeans with salt (high salt, 5–10% by weight).
4. Pack into jar; ferment in cool, dark place for 6 months to 3 years.
5. Stir occasionally; thicker crust on top is normal (protective).

**Characteristics:**
- Complex umami flavor (glutamate and nucleotides accumulate over time).
- Longer fermentation = deeper, more complex flavor.
- High salt prevents spoilage during extended aging.

### Fermented Beverages

**Kvass (fermented grain and rye drink):**
1. Bake rye bread; soak in boiling water overnight.
2. Strain; add herbs (beet, dill), starter culture or whey, sugar.
3. Ferment 2–3 days at room temperature.
4. Bottle; finish fermentation creates slight carbonation.

**Ginger bug (ginger starter for naturally carbonated beverages):**
1. Combine ginger, sugar, water; ferment 5–7 days with daily feeding (add ginger and sugar daily).
2. Strain; use liquid to ferment fruit juice or herbal tea.
3. 2–3 tablespoons ginger bug per liter of juice; ferment 1–3 days in sealed bottle.
4. Results in naturally carbonated beverage.

**Vinegar-based shrubs (drinking vinegars):**
1. Infuse vinegar with fruit, herbs, and sugar.
2. Age 2–4 weeks; strain.
3. Dilute with water and ice for refreshing drink.

### Fermented Fish and Meat Products

**Fish sauce (umami-rich ferment):**
1. Layer fish and salt (30% salt by weight) in jar.
2. Weight down; ferment at room temperature for 6–12 months.
3. Liquid (fish sauce) separates from solids.
4. Strain; use as seasoning.

**Fermented sausages (cured and fermented):**
1. Mix ground meat with salt, cure (nitrate/nitrite), and starter culture.
2. Stuff into casings.
3. Ferment at 55–65°F and 85–90% humidity for 2–4 weeks.
4. Mold develops on surface (Penicillium); this is protective and desired.
5. Dry-age further (weeks to months) for shelf-stable product.

## Preservation Through Fermentation

Fermentation preserves through multiple mechanisms:

**Acid production:** LAB produce lactic acid, lowering pH to 3.5–4.0, inhibiting spoilage organisms and pathogens (except botulism pathogen below pH 4.6).

**Alcohol production:** Yeast ferments produce ethanol (5–20% ABV), inhibiting microbial growth directly.

**Protective biofilms:** Kombucha SCOBY, vinegar mother, and other biofilms create oxygen barriers and produce antimicrobial compounds (acetic acid, ethanol, secondary metabolites).

**Competitive exclusion:** Established ferments are so acidic or alcoholic that few organisms can survive; subsequent contamination is rare.

**Cold storage:** Fermented foods stored cool (40–60°F) slow residual fermentation and spoilage. Cool storage of wine or sauerkraut preserves for months or years.

**Shelf-stable fermentation:** Some ferments (vinegars, high-salt pastes like miso, alcohols over 15% ABV) are shelf-stable at room temperature for years due to high acid, high alcohol, or high salt content.

## Health Considerations

### Probiotics and Digestive Health

Fermented foods contain live microorganisms and beneficial metabolites:
- **Live cultures:** LAB and yeasts can pass through the digestive tract, providing temporary prebiotic and probiotic effects.
- **Short-chain fatty acids:** Produced during fermentation; beneficial for gut lining health.
- **Vitamins:** Fermentation increases B vitamins (particularly B12 in some ferments).
- **Enzymes:** Fermentation produces enzymes aiding digestion.

### Anti-Nutrient Reduction

Fermentation reduces anti-nutrients (phytates, tannins) that inhibit mineral absorption:
- **Phytates:** Fermentation reduces phytic acid; improves calcium, iron, and zinc absorption.
- **Tannins:** Fermentation reduces astringent compounds in grains and legumes.

### Histamine Content

Some fermented foods (especially longer fermentation) contain elevated histamine:
- Sauerkraut: 20–100 mg/100g (higher with long fermentation).
- Kimchi: 50–250 mg/100g (higher with longer fermentation).
- Wine: 1–25 mg/L (red wine higher than white).
- Individuals with histamine intolerance should ferment shorter (1–2 weeks) and consume fresh, not aged.
