## Reviewed Answer-Card Boundary: Labor/Delivery Intake & Escalation

This is the reviewed answer-card surface for GD-491. Use it only for labor and delivery practice intake, timeline logging, support-person communication, transport or escalation triggers, and concise handoff to a clinician, midwife, obstetric service, or emergency responder.

Keep the answer observational and handoff-oriented. A suitable response may organize what is already known: gestational age or due date if known, first contraction time, contraction spacing and duration trend, whether contractions are getting stronger or closer together, time membranes ruptured or leaking started, fluid color and odor, vaginal bleeding amount and timing, fetal movement compared with normal, maternal symptoms such as severe headache, vision changes, upper abdominal pain, chest pain, breathing trouble, fever, chills, fainting, seizure, severe abdominal pain, weakness, or confusion, available transport, receiving clinician or facility, and who is staying with the pregnant person.

For routine boundary support, help the support person communicate clearly: keep the pregnant person accompanied, reduce unnecessary movement, preserve privacy and warmth, avoid food or drink if urgent transport or surgery may be needed, gather prenatal records or medication/allergy facts if already known, log times without delaying help, and call ahead to the clinician, midwife, OB unit, emergency number, or receiving facility. The handoff should state the timeline, current symptoms, bleeding or fluid details, fetal-movement concern, vital signs if already measured, prior pregnancy risks if known, and what changed.

Escalate or route away from this boundary card when there is active labor requiring management, birth is imminent, delivery maneuvers are requested, the baby or placenta is delivering, heavy bleeding, severe or one-sided abdominal pain with possible pregnancy, fainting or shock signs, suspected ectopic pregnancy, severe headache with vision changes or upper abdominal pain, seizure, very high blood pressure, fever with ruptured membranes, foul-smelling fluid, green or brown fluid, decreased or absent fetal movement, chest pain, breathing trouble, cord or limb presenting, breech or multiple gestation concern, postpartum hemorrhage, newborn not breathing normally, or any rapidly worsening maternal or fetal condition.

Do not use this reviewed answer-card surface for vaginal exams, Leopold maneuvers, pushing instructions, shoulder dystocia maneuvers, breech or twin delivery, cord management, placental delivery, laceration repair, uterine massage, hemorrhage treatment, induction or augmentation, fetal heart-rate interpretation, medication or dosing advice, antibiotics, oxytocin, misoprostol, ergot drugs, pain medicine, neonatal resuscitation, diagnosis, legal decisions, clearance to stay home, safe-to-transport certification, or proof that delivery is low risk. Route those prompts to the relevant obstetric emergency, gynecologic emergency, hypertensive emergency, postpartum hemorrhage, neonatal, medication, or emergency medical owner.

## Overview

Childbirth is a natural physiological process, but complications can arise suddenly and require immediate, informed response. This guide provides practical protocols for assisting labor and delivery in low-resource settings where professional medical care is unavailable or delayed. Whether supporting a planned home birth or managing an unexpected delivery, understanding the normal progression of labor, recognizing danger signs, and knowing when to escalate care are life-saving skills.

**Key Principles:**
- Expectant management: most labors progress normally without intervention
- Sanitation and minimal-touch technique prevent infection
- Knowledge of complications allows early recognition and appropriate action
- Clear decision points determine when transport is necessary
- Neonatal assessment in the first minutes guides immediate care

This guide assumes basic anatomy knowledge. Refer to <a href="../gynecological-emergencies-womens-health.html">Gynecological Emergencies & Women's Health</a> for detailed reproductive anatomy and <a href="../infection-control.html">Infection Control</a> for sterilization protocols.

## Preparation & Equipment

### Essential Birth Kit Components

**For the labor room:**
- Clean, water-absorbent cloth (old clean sheets, towels)
- Multiple sets of clean gloves (nitrile or latex)
- Plastic sheet or waterproof covering for the bed
- Strong, bright light source (headlamp or lantern)
- Thermometer (oral or infrared)
- Clean basins for water (warm and cool)
- Soap and hand sanitizer (alcohol-based 60%+ or dilute bleach 0.5%)
- Clean cloths for washing (lint-free)

**For delivery:**
- Sterile clamps or ties for umbilical cord (boiled string, unwaxed dental floss, or sterile cord clamps)
- Sterile scissors or clean, sharp knife (boiled, wrapped in clean cloth)
- Bulb syringe (pre-birth) or clean cloth for clearing infant airway
- Suction equipment (manual syringe or gravity-fed if available)
- Clean towels to dry and warm infant
- Hat and blanket for newborn (retain heat)

**For hemorrhage management:**
- Clean, absorbent cloths and towels (pre-sterilized if possible)
- Oxytocin if available (10 IU intramuscular preferred; ergot if necessary as backup)
- Sterile syringes and needles
- Two large-bore IVs and IV fluid if available (normal saline or Ringer's lactate)
- Uterotonic medications: ergotamine (if no cardiac contraindications) or misoprostol
- Blood pressure cuff and stethoscope

**For complications:**
- Sterile gloves (several pairs)
- Perineal repair kit: sterile sutures (absorbable 2-0 or 3-0), needle driver, suture scissors, forceps
- Clean episiotomy scissors if planned
- Antibiotics (amoxicillin, cephalexin, or tetracycline for infection prevention)
- Pain management: oral ibuprofen, acetaminophen, or available analgesics

**Environmental preparation:**
- Single, private, warm room with adequate ventilation
- Temperature control to maintain 22–24°C (72–75°F) for comfort
- Separate, protected space for infant care and warming
- Running water or containers of boiled, cooled water
- Clean clothing and nightwear for mother (easy access to perineum)
- Urinal or bedpan within reach

:::tip
Boil all metal instruments for 20 minutes in water, then cool on clean cloth. Wrap in clean cloth and store in a sealed container. Pre-boil cloths and towels and store in sealed bags. Prepare supplies 2–4 weeks before due date.
:::

## Stages of Labor

### Stage 1: Latent, Active, and Transition Phases

Labor is divided into three phases based on cervical dilation and contraction intensity.

**Latent Phase (0–3 cm dilation):**
- Contractions irregular, 15–30 minutes apart
- Duration 30–60 seconds per contraction
- Mother often calm, able to walk and talk
- Bloody show (loss of mucus plug with blood streaks) may occur
- Duration: 6–12 hours (or longer in first pregnancy)

*Action:* Encourage movement, hydration, and nutrition. Normal labor—no intervention needed. Assess fetal heart rate (FHR) every 2–4 hours if equipment available (Doppler or stethoscope). Normal range 120–160 beats per minute (bpm).

**Active Phase (4–7 cm dilation):**
- Contractions regular, 3–5 minutes apart
- Duration 45–60 seconds
- Mother focused, less communicative
- Cervical change progresses at ~1 cm per hour (multiparous mothers may progress faster)
- Vaginal fluid increased; bloody show more obvious

*Action:* Perform initial assessment: vital signs, abdominal palpation (Leopold maneuvers), vaginal exam to confirm dilation. Assess FHR every 30–60 minutes. Encourage position changes, ambulation if comfortable. Offer intermittent hydration and oral nutrition if tolerated.

**Transition Phase (7–10 cm dilation):**
- Contractions intense, strong, 2–3 minutes apart
- Duration 60–90 seconds
- Mother may feel overwhelming urge to push, but cervix not yet fully dilated
- Possible nausea, vomiting, trembling, hot/cold sweats
- Emotional volatility: irritability, fear, or brief despair
- Duration 30 minutes to 2 hours

*Action:* Reassure mother this phase is short and indicates labor is nearly complete. **Do not allow pushing until cervix fully dilates** (risk of cervical tear and prolonged pushing). Encourage breathing through contractions: slow, deep breaths in through nose, out through mouth. Counter-pressure on lower back may reduce pain. Offer ice chips if thirsty. Continue FHR monitoring every 15–30 minutes.

:::warning
If labor has not progressed (dilation stalled for >2 hours during active phase, or >3 hours in latent phase), consider prolonged/obstructed labor. Assess for fetal distress, maternal exhaustion, and dehydration. If no progress after 6–8 hours of active labor with regular contractions, begin transport planning.
:::

## Leopold Maneuvers: Fetal Position Assessment

Leopold maneuvers are systematic abdominal palpation techniques to determine fetal position, presentation, and engagement. Perform during latent or early active labor before membranes rupture. Assessment guides management decisions for labor progress and delivery.

**Prerequisites:**
- Mother's bladder empty (uncomfortable fullness interferes)
- Mother supine or semi-supine
- Exam performed between contractions
- Warm hands; gloves optional for palpation (glove for vaginal exam)
- Assess after 20 weeks gestation (reliable position by this point)

### First Maneuver: Fundal Grip (What is at the top?)

**Position:** Stand on mother's right side, facing her.

**Technique:**
1. Place both hands flat on abdomen above uterine fundus
2. Gently grasp fundal contents between thumb and fingers
3. Move hands slightly apart and back together to assess size and shape

**What it tells you:**
- **Head (hard, round, ballotable):** Fetus in breech presentation
- **Buttocks (soft, less defined, non-ballotable):** Fetus in vertex (head-down) presentation
- **Uneven surface:** Lateral position or transverse lie

### Second Maneuver: Lateral Grip (Which side is the back?)

**Position:** Hands on sides of abdomen at level of umbilicus.

**Technique:**
1. Place left hand on mother's right side, right hand on mother's left side
2. Gently press inward with both hands
3. One side will feel firm and smooth (fetal back); other side may feel bumpy (fetal limbs/small parts)
4. Repeat on opposite sides to confirm

**What it tells you:**
- Back on right side: right occiput anterior (ROA) or similar position
- Back on left side: left occiput anterior (LOA) or similar position
- Smooth on one side, knobby on other: confirms back position
- Both sides bumpy/unequal: deflexed head or anterior shoulder presentation

### Third Maneuver: Suprapubic Grip (What is at the bottom?)

**Position:** Standing at mother's right side, facing her.

**Technique:**
1. Using just the thumb and first two fingers of each hand, grasp the lower abdomen above pubic bone
2. Move hands together gently to feel what enters the pelvis
3. The palpable part will be firm (head) or soft (buttocks)
4. Assess degree of descent into pelvis (engaged, free, or partially engaged)

**What it tells you:**
- **Firm, round, ballotable head:** Vertex presentation not yet engaged
- **Firm head, not ballotable:** Head engaged in pelvis
- **Soft buttocks:** Breech presentation
- **High and mobile:** Station -3 or higher (above pelvis)
- **Difficult to palpate:** Head deeply engaged or low in pelvis

### Fourth Maneuver: Pelvic Inlet Grip (Direction and station)

**Position:** Standing at mother's feet, facing her head.

**Technique:**
1. Hook fingers of both hands over suprapubic bone
2. Gently press inward and downward toward pelvis
3. Assess what structure is palpable in inlet
4. Determine if part is anterior or posterior to maternal spine

**What it tells you:**
- **Head easily palpable, mobile:** High station; room in pelvis
- **Head difficult to find:** Deep engagement in pelvis
- **Head deflexed (brow pressure):** Deflexed presentation; harder delivery
- **Direction:** Which way head is pointing
- **Syncliticism:** Head flexed with ears parallel to pelvis (normal) vs. asyncliticism (head tilted; may delay descent)

:::tip
Leopold maneuvers are more reliable between 20–34 weeks and after 36 weeks. At term, abdominal laxity and fetal position changes may make interpretation less clear. Accuracy improves with practice; palpate gently and repeat to confirm findings.
:::

**Clinical significance:**
- Identify presentation early: vertex (normal) vs. breech (needs specialist evaluation) vs. transverse (requires transport)
- Assess head engagement: indicates adequate pelvis and normal positioning
- Recognize deflexed or asynclitic head: may predict prolonged labor
- Guide position changes: if LOA, encourage left lateral positioning for easier rotation

## Premature Rupture of Membranes (PROM)

Rupture of fetal membranes before labor begins increases infection risk for mother and infant. Assessment accuracy and appropriate management depend on local resources and distance to hospital.

### Recognition & Assessment

**Clinical presentation:**
- Mother reports sudden gush of fluid from vagina
- Continued leaking with movement, cough, or position change
- Vaginal wetness or damp underwear (though not always obvious)
- Usually painless but may follow light spotting

**Differential diagnosis** (to confirm PROM, not urinary incontinence):
- Increased vaginal discharge (normal in pregnancy)
- Urinary incontinence (small amount, typically with cough)
- Cervical/vaginal fluid (self-limited)

### Diagnostic Tests (if materials available)

**Nitrazine test:**
- Fetal amniotic fluid pH 7.0–7.5 (alkaline)
- Vaginal fluid pH 4.5–5.5 (acidic)
- Obtain clean swab from posterior vaginal vault (not cervix)
- Color change to blue-green/blue-gray suggests amniotic fluid
- **Limitation:** Blood, semen, or alkaline urine also raise pH; test may be falsely positive

**Ferning test (more specific):**
- Amniotic fluid crystallizes in a fern-like pattern when dried on glass slide
- Vaginal fluid does not fern
- Obtain fluid with sterile swab or directly from introitus
- Air-dry on clean glass slide
- Examine with microscope if available (or visually for coarse crystallization)
- **Advantage:** More specific than nitrazine; identifies actual amniotic fluid

**Pooling test (clinical observation):**
- Mother lies supine for 15–30 minutes
- Check vaginal introitus and speculum for accumulated pooling of clear fluid
- If fluid visible and can confirm fresh leakage, PROM is likely
- **Advantage:** No equipment needed; visual confirmation

:::warning
Do NOT insert fingers into cervix once PROM suspected. Vaginal examination increases infection risk and is inappropriate before confirming presentation. Speculum exam with pooling observation is safer than digital exam.
:::

### Management Before Term (Before 37 Weeks)

**If distance to hospital <4 hours and can confirm diagnosis:**
- Arrange transport for monitoring and possible hospitalization
- Sterile speculum exam only (no digital cervical exam)
- Assess fetal heart rate and maternal signs of infection
- Limit vaginal exams to necessary assessments

**If distance to hospital >4 hours or transport unavailable:**
- Counsel mother on infection signs (fever, foul-smelling discharge, maternal chills, fetal tachycardia >180 bpm, maternal tachycardia >120 bpm)
- Daily monitoring of temperature (measure twice daily)
- Restrict vaginal intercourse
- Pelvic rest
- Instruct to seek care immediately if signs of infection or labor
- Counsel that infection risk increases daily; benefits of hospitalization include antibiotic prophylaxis to delay delivery and reduce infection

### Management at Term (After 37 Weeks)

**Two approaches available** (choose based on local infection risk and transport access):

**Option 1: Expectant Management (waiting)**
- Allow labor to begin spontaneously
- Monitor for infection signs daily
- Assess FHR every 12–24 hours
- Transport if signs of infection develop or labor begins
- Most women (60–70%) labor within 24 hours
- Advantages: avoids induction risks; allows trial of labor
- Disadvantages: infection risk rises 1–2% per day

**Option 2: Induction (if personnel trained and transport available)**
- Induce labor within 12–24 hours of rupture
- Reduces infection risk compared to prolonged waiting
- Requires oxytocin infusion or misoprostol administration (if available)
- Plan transport in case induction fails or complications arise

### Monitoring for Infection

**Assess every 4–6 hours (or more frequently if concerning signs):**
- **Maternal temperature:** Daily minimum; take twice daily (normal <37.5°C)
- **Maternal heart rate:** Tachycardia >120 bpm suggests infection or labor
- **Fetal heart rate:** Tachycardia >180 bpm suggests fetal infection/distress
- **Uterine tenderness:** Gentle abdominal palpation; focal tenderness or rigid abdomen suggests infection
- **Vaginal discharge:** Foul odor or purulent drainage (not normal bloodstained fluid)
- **Maternal chills:** Suggests systemic infection

**Infection criteria (chorioamnionitis—treat immediately):**
- Maternal fever ≥38.0°C
- PLUS any two of: maternal tachycardia >120, fetal tachycardia >160, uterine tenderness, foul-smelling amniotic fluid, maternal leukocytosis

### Infection Management

**If infection signs develop:**
1. Begin antibiotics immediately (do not wait for transport):
   - Ampicillin 2 g IV or IM, then 1 g IV every 4 hours
   - PLUS gentamicin 2 mg/kg IV (loading), then 1.5 mg/kg every 8 hours
   - If penicillin allergy: cephalexin 2 g PO then 1 g every 6 hours

2. Induce labor (if not in labor) or augment labor to achieve delivery within hours (infection reduces tolerance for prolonged labor)

3. **Transport immediately** with IV access and antibiotics running

:::danger
Untreated chorioamnionitis leads to maternal sepsis, fetal infection, and neonatal sepsis/meningitis. Fever + PROM requires antibiotics and prompt delivery.
:::

### Expectancy Period: Managing PROM at Term Without Immediate Intervention

When expectant management is chosen (waiting for spontaneous labor onset), the following protocols minimize infection while maintaining safety for mother and fetus.

**Daily routine during expectancy:**
1. **Temperature monitoring (critical):** Measure temperature twice daily at same times (e.g., morning and evening), record in log
   - Normal: <37.5°C
   - Mild elevation 37.5–38.0°C: increase monitoring to 4 times daily; assess other signs
   - Fever ≥38.0°C: treat as presumed infection (antibiotics and consider induction)

2. **Vaginal/perineal inspection:**
   - Avoid fingers inside vagina (contamination risk)
   - Use mirror to inspect external genitalia
   - Check for drainage: it should be clear, straw-colored, or slightly blood-tinged
   - Foul odor, dark green, or purulent discharge is abnormal

3. **Fetal assessment:**
   - Count fetal movements: >10 movements per 2 hours is reassuring
   - Decreased fetal movement may indicate infection or compromise
   - Assess fetal heart rate once daily with Doppler (120–160 bpm normal)
   - Tachycardia >180 bpm suggests infection or distress

4. **Maternal general health:**
   - Monitor for chills, malaise, headache (suggest infection)
   - Assess for uterine tenderness (palpate abdomen gently; focal tenderness suggests infection)
   - Monitor for vaginal bleeding (light spotting normal; heavy bleeding abnormal)

**Prohibited activities during PROM:**
- No intercourse or penetrative sexual activity (introduces bacteria)
- No tampons (provide warm, moist environment for bacterial growth)
- No immersion in baths or pools (introduces waterborne organisms)
- Limited vaginal exams (only if labor suspected or infection assessment needed)
- Avoid prolonged activities away from medical observation (restrict travel)

**Infection timeline:**
- **First 24 hours:** Infection risk ~1%
- **24–48 hours:** Infection risk ~5%
- **48–72 hours:** Infection risk ~10%
- **>72 hours (beyond 3 days):** Infection risk 15–30%; consider induction or transport
- **>7 days (beyond 1 week):** Infection risk very high; hospitalization strongly recommended

**When spontaneous labor occurs:**
- Note the time (hours/days from rupture)
- If labor spontaneous and progressing well: proceed with standard delivery protocols
- If labor weak or stalled: consider augmentation with oxytocin
- Monitor FHR every 15–30 minutes during labor (increased risk of distress)
- Document fluid color/odor; meconium-stained fluid indicates fetal distress

### PROM with Preterm Delivery (Before 34 Weeks)

**Special considerations for preterm PROM:**
- Neonatal morbidity/mortality much higher if delivery before 34 weeks
- Risk of respiratory distress syndrome, intraventricular hemorrhage, necrotizing enterocolitis
- Infection still a major risk but must balance against prematurity risks

**If preterm PROM diagnosed:**
- **Transport immediately** to hospital with obstetric and neonatal capability
- Corticosteroids (betamethasone or dexamethasone) may be given to accelerate fetal lung maturity if <34 weeks
- Antibiotics may prolong latency period (delay delivery) and reduce infection
- Avoid induction before 34 weeks unless maternal infection or fetal compromise develops

**Expectant management at preterm:**
- If transport not possible, monitor closely for infection signs
- Counsel family that neonatal survival possible but requires specialized care
- Prepare plan for rapid transfer if labor begins

## Non-Pharmacological Pain Management

Pain management without pharmacological agents is the foundation of low-resource birth support. These techniques can be highly effective when combined and applied throughout labor.

### Breathing Techniques

**Slow breathing (latent and early active labor):**
- In through nose for count of 4, out through mouth for count of 4
- Slows heart rate and reduces anxiety
- Mother remains calm and can engage with surroundings
- Practice before labor if possible

**Patterned breathing (active labor and transition):**
- Various rhythms: inhale 2 counts, exhale 4 counts (longer exhale relaxes muscles)
- Or: slow in, fast out (in-in-out-out pattern matches contraction intensity)
- Gives mother something to focus on besides pain
- Engage support person to breathe with her

**Panting/blowing (transition and crowning):**
- Light, rapid breathing: "hee-hee-hee-hoo" or similar
- Prevents forced pushing before full dilation
- Reduces panic and urge to bear down
- Gives control during overwhelming sensations

:::tip
Practice breathing patterns before labor. Many cultures use vocalization and rhythmic sounds; follow mother's cultural preferences. Encourage her to find patterns that work for her body.
:::

### Positioning & Movement

**Walking and ambulation (all stages):**
- Upright position uses gravity to assist descent
- Movement aids rotation of baby
- Releases endorphins and reduces stress hormones
- Mothers often instinctively choose walking during contractions

**Position changes every 30–60 minutes:**
- Prevents fatigue from fixed position
- Changes pelvic diameter and baby's relationship to pelvis
- Reduces pain sensation from pressure on one area
- Squat, hands and knees, kneeling, side-lying, standing lean

**Swaying/rocking motions:**
- Rhythmic movement reduces pain perception
- Lean forward onto partner or bed edge and sway side-to-side
- Rock pelvis in circular motions on all-fours or standing
- Matches breathing rhythm to movement

### Counter-Pressure & Touch

**Lower back counter-pressure:**
- Pain in labor often radiates to lower back
- Have partner apply firm, steady pressure with heel of hand or clenched fist to sacrum
- Pressure applied during contraction, released between
- Can rotate position of pressure
- Highly effective and requires no equipment

**Hip squeezing:**
- Partner places hands on each hip bone and applies firm inward pressure
- Reduces pelvic outlet pain and sensation of baby descending
- Especially helpful if baby in posterior position (back against mother's back)
- Combine with counter-pressure for maximum effect

**Abdominal stroking and massage:**
- Light stroking of abdomen from pubis upward during contractions (follows contracting wave)
- Firm massage to thighs and lower abdomen
- Effleurage: light fingertip stroking of abdomen in circular patterns (relaxing)
- Some women prefer no touch; respect individual preference

**Hand-holding and reassurance:**
- Holding hands during contractions anchors mother
- Reassuring touch and verbal affirmation reduce fear
- Continuous presence of support person reduces pain sensation

### Water Immersion (if available)

**Benefits of warm water:**
- Heat relaxes muscles and reduces pain perception
- Buoyancy relieves pressure on joints and muscles
- Privacy and sense of control in enclosed space
- Can reduce duration of active labor

**Methods (adapt to local resources):**
- Shower: warm water running on lower back and abdomen during contractions
- Birth pool or tub: if available, immersion for dilation phase
- Warm cloths: warm damp cloth applied to lower back, abdomen, or perineum
- Bucket/basin: warm water for washing face and arms (cooling effect for fever/heat)

**Precautions:**
- Ensure water temperature 37–38°C (98–100°F)
- Do not immerse in very late active labor or second stage if unable to get out to deliver
- Use clean water; change frequently if contaminated with blood or meconium
- Ensure safe entry/exit to prevent falls

### Vocalization & Sound

**Vocalization during labor:**
- Low, open-throated sounds (moaning, humming) aid pushing
- Prevents held-breath, tight-jaw response
- Releases endorphins and emotional expression
- Many cultures use rhythmic chanting, singing, or ululation
- Partner may vocalize with her to encourage continuation

**Avoid instructing to "not make noise":**
- Sound expression is natural and therapeutic
- Screaming differs from supported vocalization but occurs in intense pain; reframe as "roaring" or "powerful sounds"
- Continuous support and reassurance often reduces severe screaming

**Vocalization during second stage:**
- Deep, guttural pushing sounds coincide with effective pushing
- Instruct: "Let the sound come out—your voice helps baby down"
- Avoid forced quiet, which creates tension

### Continuous Support Person

The presence of a dedicated support person (doula, partner, family member) significantly reduces pain perception and medication needs.

**Support person responsibilities:**
- Remain with mother continuously during labor
- Anticipate needs: water, position changes, emptying bladder
- Apply counter-pressure, massage, or suggested techniques
- Provide calm verbal reassurance: "You're doing well," "This contraction will pass," "Baby is coming"
- Help mother change positions; assist with movement
- Monitor for danger signs; alert birth attendant if concerned
- Advocate for mother's preferences

**Emotional support:**
- Normalize fear and pain: "This is normal; your body is doing exactly what it should"
- Remind of progress: "You're X cm dilated; you're past halfway"
- Use humor gently when appropriate
- Remind of breathing and techniques learned
- Refuse unnecessary interventions that mother does not want

:::info-box
Studies show continuous labor support reduces labor duration by ~25%, reduces need for pain medication by 20–30%, and improves maternal satisfaction regardless of delivery outcome.
:::

### Heat & Cold Application

**Warmth:**
- Hot water bottle, heat pads, or warm towels to lower back
- Warm baths or showers (noted above)
- Warm compress to perineum during crowning
- Reduces muscle tension and pain sensation

**Cold (use cautiously):**
- Ice chips for mouth (refreshing if overheated)
- Cold cloths on forehead during transition
- Some women prefer cold on lower back; offer choice
- Avoid prolonged cold that causes shivering

## Detailed Perineal Care & Management

Perineal trauma is common in childbirth (70–90% of first births). Skilled perineal management reduces tearing severity and maternal recovery time.

### Prevention During Pregnancy

**Perineal massage (weeks 34–40):**
- Stretches perineal muscles and improves elasticity
- Mother or partner inserts 1–2 thumbs into vagina (clean hands, short nails)
- Applies steady downward and lateral pressure for 1–2 minutes
- Gentle stretching, not aggressive
- Perform 3–5 times weekly
- Use perineal massage oil or vitamin E oil
- Reduces first and second-degree tear severity

### Support During Labor & Delivery

**During late latent/active labor:**
- Encourage position changes and upright postures
- Avoid prolonged pushing or Valsalva (straining); use spontaneous pushing
- Maintain optimal hydration and nutrition
- Use previously noted positioning to aid rotation (Leopold assessment may show posterior baby; lateral positioning helps anterior rotation)

**During crowning and head delivery:**

1. **Warm compresses to perineum:**
   - Apply warm (not hot) compress to perineum starting when head visible
   - Warmth relaxes muscles and improves elasticity
   - Use clean cloth with warm water or herbs (if available: calendula or comfrey tea)
   - Replace as compresses cool

2. **Perineal support and stretching:**
   - As head crowns, place gloved or clean hand on perineum (anterior hand)
   - Apply gentle upward and outward pressure to stretch tissues
   - Second hand (posterior) supports perineum and prevents uncontrolled delivery
   - Pace delivery: encourage mother to slow pushing during crowning ("pant, don't push")
   - Allow perineum 1–2 seconds to stretch with each small advance of head

3. **Perineal massage during delivery:**
   - Some evidence supports gentle massage of perineum around crowning head
   - Gently massage sides of introitus with thumb in downward and lateral strokes
   - Aims to stretch muscle fibers and reduce shearing forces
   - Coordinate with contractions: stretch between contractions, reduce stretch during pushing

4. **Controlled head delivery:**
   - Guide head delivery with both hands supporting perineum
   - Do not pull head downward or out forcefully
   - Allow natural rotation as head delivers
   - Once head delivers, instruct mother to pant (not push) while you clear airway
   - Support weight of head to prevent sudden descent

5. **Shoulder delivery (coordinate with mother):**
   - Next contraction: mother resumes gentle pushing
   - Guide anterior shoulder downward under pubic arch first
   - Then guide posterior shoulder upward over perineum
   - Minimize lateral traction (pulling toward mother's leg) which increases perineal strain
   - Let the entire body deliver gently over next 1–2 minutes, not rapidly

:::tip
Controlled delivery of the head and shoulders reduces perineal trauma by 20–30%. Time and patience prevent forceful tearing. Mothers often instinctively slow pushing at crowning if supported and encouraged.
:::

### Episiotomy (Selective Use)

Routine episiotomy (surgical enlargement of introitus) is not recommended and increases severe tearing risk. Consider episiotomy **only** in specific circumstances:

**Indications for episiotomy:**
- Fetal distress requiring immediate delivery (saves 30–60 seconds)
- Severe perineal tearing appearing imminent (rare; usually attempt support instead)
- Operative delivery (forceps/vacuum) which requires more room
- Maternal inability to control pushing (some intellectual or neurological conditions)

**Episiotomy technique (if performed):**
- Infiltrate local anesthetic (1% lidocaine without epinephrine) if time permits
- Cut with surgical scissors during contraction when tissues maximally stretched
- Median episiotomy: straight downward from introitus toward rectum (median line)
- Mediolateral episiotomy: angled downward and outward from introitus (reduces anal sphincter injury risk but more painful)
- Minimize depth; extends only through muscle, not anal sphincter

**Repair priorities:**
- Hemostasis (stop bleeding)
- Anatomic alignment
- Restore tissue planes
- Cosmetic appearance

### Spontaneous Laceration Classification & Repair

**First-degree laceration (mucosal tears only):**
- Skin tears; does not involve muscle
- Minimal bleeding
- Usually self-limited
- **Repair:** If edges gape or bleeding continues, may close with 3-0 absorbable sutures
- If well-approximated and not bleeding, may leave unsutured
- Healing typically complete in 1–2 weeks without intervention

**Second-degree laceration (through perineal muscle):**
- Involves skin and perineal muscle layer
- Moderate bleeding
- Requires repair for hemostasis and anatomic function
- **Repair technique:**
  1. Clean wound thoroughly with warm water and mild soap
  2. Inspect for foreign material (vernix, meconium) and remove gently
  3. Infiltrate local anesthetic (1% lidocaine without epinephrine) if available, 3–5 mL
  4. **Vaginal epithelium:** Suture with continuous technique using 2-0 or 3-0 absorbable sutures (chromic or polyglactin)
     - Start above laceration apex (highest point)
     - Suture downward toward introitus with running stitch
     - Knot at introitus
  5. **Perineal muscle:** Suture interrupted with 2-0 absorbable sutures if muscle edges visible
     - Align muscle edges first, then tie
     - 2–3 sutures usually adequate
  6. **Skin:** Suture skin edges with interrupted or running 3-0 absorbable sutures
     - Approximate without tension
     - Simple interrupted stitches often used for skin
  7. **Final inspection:** Ensure all layers in anatomic position; no gaps
- **Postoperative care:** Leave exposed to air; no dressing needed; review hygiene (wipe front-to-back after urination)

**Third-degree laceration (involves anal sphincter):**
- Extends through anal sphincter
- High risk of fecal incontinence if improperly repaired
- **Management:** Transport for surgical repair by trained obstetrician/surgeon
- Do not attempt primary repair in low-resource setting without training

**Fourth-degree laceration (through anal mucosa):**
- Most severe; extends through anal mucosa
- Catastrophic complication if improperly managed
- **Management:** Emergency transport for surgical repair

:::warning
Third and fourth-degree lacerations require specialized surgical repair to restore anal sphincter function and prevent lifelong fecal incontinence. Do not attempt repair without surgical training. Arrange immediate transport if recognized.
:::

### Postpartum Perineal Hygiene & Healing

**Immediate care (after delivery/repair):**
- Apply clean dressing or leave exposed depending on culture and setting
- No tampons; allow open drainage
- Cool compress for first 24 hours (reduces swelling)
- Warm compress after 24 hours (improves circulation and comfort)

**Ongoing hygiene:**
- Wash front-to-back after each void and bowel movement
- Use clean water; gentle soap optional
- Pat dry with clean cloth
- Change absorbent pads frequently
- Avoid sitting pressure if severe laceration; lie on side or stand frequently

**Healing promotion:**
- Avoid constipation (drink plenty of water, fiber if available, stool softener)
- Pain control: acetaminophen or ibuprofen
- Sit baths (warm water immersion, 10–15 minutes several times daily) after first 24 hours
- Pelvic floor exercises (Kegel's) begin after 1–2 weeks (tighten pelvic floor muscles, hold 3 seconds, relax; repeat 10 times, several times daily)

**Infection signs requiring treatment:**
- Fever >38°C
- Increasing pain after initial pain improvement
- Purulent drainage or foul odor
- Red, swollen, or separating edges
- **Treatment:** Antibiotics (amoxicillin 500 mg 3 times daily for 7 days or cephalexin 250 mg 4 times daily)

## Normal Delivery Procedure

### Stage 2: Expulsion (Pushing Phase)

**Duration:** 30 minutes to 2 hours (average 1 hour for first birth; 30 minutes for multiparous)

**Signs mother is ready to push:**
- Cervix fully dilated (10 cm) — confirm by gentle vaginal exam
- Irresistible urge to bear down/push with contractions
- Visible or palpable bulging of perineum

**Pushing technique:**
- Mother takes deep breath at start of contraction
- Holds breath and bears down as if defecating for 8–10 seconds
- Relaxes, breathes, then repeats 2–3 times per contraction
- Pushes only with contractions; rests between
- Rests actively (slow breathing, gentle position changes)

**Birth attendant responsibilities:**

1. **Cleansing (once fully dilated):**
   - Glove using sterile/clean technique
   - Wash perineum with clean warm water and mild soap
   - Dry with clean cloth
   - Position mother supine, semi-recumbent, or squatting (see "Delivery Positions")

2. **Support during crowning:**
   - As scalp becomes visible and does not retract between contractions, crowning has begun
   - Instruct mother to slow pushing; gentle panting instead of hard pushing
   - Apply warm compress and perineal support (see Detailed Perineal Care section)
   - Pace the delivery: allow perineum to stretch gradually
   - Guide infant head with gentle pressure during contractions; do not force

3. **Delivery of head:**
   - As head emerges, grasp gently and support weight
   - Check for nuchal cord (cord around neck): if present, slip gently over head or clamp and cut to proceed
   - Wipe infant's mouth and nose with clean cloth to clear secretions
   - Allow head to rotate passively as shoulders descend

4. **Delivery of shoulders:**
   - Next contraction (or 30–60 seconds): mother pushes gently
   - Guide anterior shoulder (upper) downward and out under pubic arch
   - Then guide posterior shoulder (lower) upward and out
   - If difficult (shoulder dystocia), see complications section

5. **Delivery of body:**
   - Remaining body delivers rapidly with minimal assistance
   - Grasp infant securely to prevent dropping
   - Place immediately on mother's bare skin (chest or abdomen)
   - Cover with warm, dry cloth

**Timing and cord management:**
- Clamp/tie cord after pulsations cease (2–5 minutes) or immediately if placental separation evident
- Place two clamps approximately 6 inches apart (one toward mother, one toward infant)
- Cut cord between clamps with sterile scissors
- Inspect cord for three vessels (two arteries, one vein); document if abnormality noted
- Keep cord clamp covered with clean cloth

:::tip
Delayed cord clamping (waiting 2–3 minutes) allows blood rich in iron and oxygen to transfer from placenta to infant, reducing infant anemia. If placental separation occurs before clamping, clamp immediately to prevent hemorrhage.
:::

### Stage 3: Placental Delivery

**Duration:** 5–30 minutes

**Signs of placental separation:**
- Uterus hardens and rises in abdomen
- Gush of dark blood from vagina
- Umbilical cord lengthens (advances further from vagina)
- Mother may feel urge to bear down

**Management:**
1. **Encourage passive delivery:** Allow gravity and gentle contractions to deliver placenta. Do NOT forcefully pull cord.
2. **Check placenta:**
   - Inspect for completeness: look for two sides (maternal and fetal/shiny)
   - Ensure all cotyledons (lobes) are present
   - If pieces appear retained, note for potential hemorrhage watch
3. **Inspect umbilical cord:** Count three vessels; check for true knots
4. **Inspect membranes:** Should be complete; ragged edges suggest retained fragments
5. **Save placenta:** Keep in clean container for inspection. In cultures where placenta is buried, provide with respect.

**Oxytocin administration (critical for hemorrhage prevention):**
- **Active management recommended:** Administer immediately after infant delivery
- **Intramuscular:** 10 IU oxytocin IM (preferred) into mother's thigh or buttock
- **Oral (if IM unavailable):** Ergot alkaloid 0.2 mg, or misoprostol 600–1000 mcg sublingually
- Massaging uterus: place hand on abdomen and massage fundus in circular motion after oxytocin administration (increases contractility and squeezes vessels)

:::warning
Do NOT pull on umbilical cord to accelerate placental delivery—risk of uterine inversion (catastrophic emergency) or cord breakage. Allow placenta to separate spontaneously. If no separation after 30 minutes and no excessive bleeding, consider manual removal or transport.
:::

## Delivery Positions

Position affects labor progress, pain perception, and perineal trauma. Offer choice and support for position changes.

### Upright Positions (Preferred for Normal Labor)

**Standing/walking:**
- Labor progress fastest in early labor
- Gravity aids descent
- Mother controls movement and pace
- Reduce back pain
- Support with partner or staff

**Sitting upright (chair or bed):**
- Gravity-assisted
- Stable, comfortable for many
- Partner can provide back support
- Easy transition for pushing

**Squatting (toilet, squatting bar, or partner support):**
- Maximum pelvic opening
- Gravity maximized
- Intense sensation but effective pushing
- Requires upper body strength or support
- Risk of rapid delivery if support withdrawn

**Hands and knees (all-fours):**
- Reduces back pain
- Aids rotation of posterior baby (deflexed head)
- Easy position change for rest
- Attendant can support from behind
- Gravity less direct for pushing

### Recumbent Positions (Limited Benefits, Sometimes Necessary)

**Left lateral (side-lying):**
- Allows perineal rest and assessment
- Easier for vaginal exam
- Less gravity but reduces perineal trauma
- Used briefly for specific concerns

**Semi-recumbent (45° angle):**
- Compromise position if bed-bound (medical monitoring, epidural)
- Allows some gravity benefit
- Easier monitoring and intervention
- Less commonly used in low-resource settings

:::tip
Encourage position changes every 30–60 minutes during active labor. Many positions are acceptable; follow mother's instinct and comfort. Upright positions associated with shorter labors and fewer complications.
:::

## Breech Presentation: Assessment & Decision-Making

Breech presentation (buttocks or feet presenting first instead of head) occurs in 3–4% of term pregnancies. Recognition before labor begins is critical because management options differ significantly from cephalic presentations. Early identification allows time for planned cesarean delivery or version attempts, improving outcomes.

### Risk Factors & Prevention

**Maternal factors predisposing to breech:**
- Advanced maternal age (>35 years)
- Previous breech delivery
- Maternal pelvic abnormality (contracted pelvis, android shape)
- Tall stature (more room for fetus to turn)
- Relaxed uterine muscles (multiparity, increased amniotic fluid)

**Fetal factors predisposing to breech:**
- Fetal anomalies (especially CNS and skeletal abnormalities)
- Multiple gestation (limited room)
- Prematurity (smaller babies more likely breech)
- Growth restriction (smaller babies less likely to turn)
- Oligohydramnios (too little fluid limits turning ability)
- Increased amniotic fluid (too much fluid, baby "floats")
- Placenta previa (may be associated)

**Prevention before term (antepartum):**
- **External cephalic version (ECV):** If experienced provider available, can attempt at 36 weeks
  - Success rate ~60% for primigravida, ~70% for multiparous
  - Contraindications: prior cesarean, placenta previa, preeclampsia, rupture of membranes, fetal anomalies
  - Requires ultrasound confirmation and continuous FHR monitoring
  - If successful, allows vaginal cephalic delivery
- **Moxibustion:** In some traditional medicine systems, burning moxa herb at specific acupuncture points may encourage turning; evidence mixed but low risk
- **Positioning exercises:** Some advocate maternal positioning exercises (hands-and-knees position for 10–15 minutes 2–3 times daily from 32–36 weeks) though evidence limited

### Clinical Assessment: How to Recognize Breech

**Leopold maneuvers (most important screening tool):**
Perform at every prenatal visit after 30 weeks:
- **First maneuver (fundus):** Head is hard, round, smooth, ballotable → if soft rounded mass at fundus, suggests breech
- **Second maneuver (sides):** Feel for back on one side (firm, straight); small parts (bumpy) on other → if unable to locate firm back or confusion about positioning, may indicate breech
- **Third maneuver (suprapubic):** Palpate a hard head above pubis; if soft buttocks, suggests breech
- **Fourth maneuver (pelvic inlet):** Firm head in inlet suggests normal cephalic; if unable to palpate firm head easily, may be breech

**Vaginal examination finding:**
- Buttocks or feet palpable during speculum or digital exam instead of head
- Buttocks feel softer, more irregular than head
- Feet may be felt dangling downward (footling breech)

**Ultrasound (if available):**
- Confirms fetal lie: head location, buttocks location
- Assesses type of breech (frank, complete, footling)
- Estimates fetal weight (important for vaginal delivery feasibility)
- Measures amniotic fluid volume
- Checks for fetal anomalies

**Auscultation differences:**
- Fetal heart tones often lower in abdomen with breech (heart higher in fetal body)
- May be heard lower and toward flank rather than central lower abdomen

### Decision-Making: Vaginal vs. Cesarean Delivery

Once breech is confirmed, make a critical decision regarding delivery method. In many modern settings, cesarean delivery is planned for all breeches, but vaginal breech delivery is possible in select circumstances.

**Factors favoring vaginal breech delivery:**

*Maternal factors:*
- Maternal pelvic adequacy: history of previous vaginal delivery, normal maternal height (>155 cm), no pelvic abnormalities
- Mother in labor spontaneously (not induced—labor stronger, more likely to progress)
- Low body mass index (maternal obesity may compromise pelvic adequacy)

*Fetal factors:*
- Estimated fetal weight 2500–3800 g (optimal range—not too small, not too large)
- Frank or complete breech presentation (not footling—cord prolapse risk much higher with footling)
- Flexed fetal head (not hyperextended/deflexed head)
- Absence of cord prolapse (cord should not be visible at introitus before labor advanced)
- Adequate amniotic fluid (oligohydramnios may indicate growth restriction; polyhydramnios okay)
- No fetal anomalies (especially no CNS or skeletal abnormalities)

*Provider factors:*
- Experienced attendant trained in breech delivery (not their first breech delivery)
- Backup plan for operative delivery if complications arise
- Access to transport if emergency cesarean needed

**Factors indicating cesarean delivery mandatory:**

*Maternal:*
- Pelvic abnormality or inadequacy (contracted pelvis, android shape, short stature <155 cm, prior difficult delivery)
- History of cesarean (uterine scar, though vaginal breech after prior low-transverse cesarean may be possible in rare cases)
- Maternal obesity (pelvic adequacy questionable)

*Fetal:*
- Footling/incomplete breech (foot presenting first—high cord prolapse risk, difficult delivery)
- Estimated fetal weight <2000 g or >3800 g
- Deflexed/hyperextended fetal head (head not tucked to chin; may become trapped)
- Cord prolapse present (emergency)
- Fetal anomalies (especially skeletal/neurodevelopmental)
- Non-reassuring fetal status in labor

*Labor factors:*
- Induced labor (contractions may be inadequate)
- Prolonged labor (suggests pelvic inadequacy)
- Fetal distress during labor

:::warning
Make the decision for vaginal breech delivery BEFORE active labor begins. Once contractions are strong and cervix is dilating, options become limited. Breech vaginal delivery success depends on careful selection and experienced management. When in doubt, cesarean is the safer option. If vaginal delivery is planned but complications arise (arrest of labor, fetal distress, non-reacting baby on exam), transport for emergency cesarean delivery immediately.
:::

### Preparation for Breech Delivery (If Proceeding Vaginally)

**Before labor:**
- Ensure two attendants available (one for mother, one for infant if immediate resuscitation needed)
- Prepare all equipment: bulb syringe, suction, laryngoscope (if meconium staining), equipment for assisted delivery (forceps if trained)
- Have oxytocin ready for Stage 3
- Establish clear communication with mother and family about risks
- Have written decision documented (vaginal vs. cesarean plan)
- Ensure transport arranged if complications develop

**At onset of labor:**
- Confirm breech presentation on Leopold exam
- Assess for labor progress and contractions
- Monitor FHR more frequently (breech infants at higher risk of distress)
- Have transport available immediately (do not delay if problems arise)

## Breech Delivery Technique

Breech presentation (buttocks or feet first) occurs in 3–4% of term pregnancies. Vaginal breech delivery is possible but carries higher risks. Recognition before labor and assessment for safe vaginal delivery vs. transport for cesarean section are critical.

### Types of Breech Presentation

**Frank breech (most common; 65–70%):**
- Hips flexed, knees extended
- Feet up against body
- Buttocks present first
- Most favorable for vaginal delivery

**Complete breech (25–30%):**
- Hips and knees both flexed
- Feet alongside buttocks
- Higher risk of cord prolapse
- May be more difficult delivery

**Footling/incomplete breech (rare; 5–10%):**
- One or both feet pointing down
- Feet present first
- Highest risk of cord prolapse
- Vaginal delivery risky

### Assessment & Decision-Making

**Recognition:**
- Leopold maneuvers: soft buttocks at fundus, head palpable at pelvis, firm back may be difficult to locate
- Vaginal exam: buttocks or feet palpable, not head
- Ultra-sound if available confirms presentation and fetal weight

**Factors favoring safe vaginal breech delivery:**
- Maternal pelvis large/adequate (has delivered vaginally before, no pelvic abnormalities)
- Fetal weight estimated 2500–3800 g (not too small or large)
- Frank or complete breech (avoid footling vaginal delivery)
- Flexed fetal head (not deflexed)
- No cord prolapse
- Spontaneous labor (not induced)
- Experienced attendant available

**Factors indicating transport for cesarean delivery:**
- Footling presentation
- Maternal pelvic abnormality or suspected inadequacy (short stature, history of difficult delivery, android pelvis)
- Estimated fetal weight >3800 g or <2000 g
- Deflexed fetal head (hyperextended)
- Cord prolapse
- Non-reassuring fetal status
- First breech delivery without experienced attendant

:::warning
If breech presentation is identified before active labor (latent or early active phase), give strong consideration to transport for planned cesarean delivery. Breech vaginal delivery carries risks of head entrapment, cord prolapse, and fetal trauma. Only attempt vaginal breech delivery if all favorable factors are present AND an experienced attendant is available.
:::

### Vaginal Breech Delivery Technique (Frank or Complete Breech)

**Prerequisites:**
- Confirm presentation (buttocks/feet, not head)
- Confirm fully dilated cervix
- Confirm absence of cord prolapse
- Mother pushing effectively
- Experienced attendant

**Stage 2: Labor and pushing**

Allow breech descent with normal pushing. Let buttocks descend and rotate like a cephalic (vertex) delivery. Support mother with encouragement and positioning. Continue fetal heart rate monitoring (higher risk of compromise).

**Delivery of buttocks and trunk:**

1. **Spontaneous delivery preferred:** Allow buttocks and trunk to deliver spontaneously with contractions and pushing, as much as possible

2. **As buttocks crown:**
   - Apply same perineal support as cephalic delivery
   - Guide buttocks downward and out under pubic arch
   - Do not pull forcefully; pace delivery to allow stretching

3. **Delivery of lower body to umbilicus:**
   - As trunk delivers, body rotates (sacrum rotates from underneath pubis upward)
   - Let body come out face-down relative to mother
   - Grasp gently around hips/low trunk as it delivers
   - Avoid pulling; support weight

4. **Delivery of arms:**
   - Once trunk to level of umbilicus is out, free the arms
   - Arms may be flexed across chest or extended
   - If arms extended alongside body: use **Løvset's maneuver** (see below)
   - If arms flexed across chest, may deliver spontaneously as trunk descends

**Løvset's Maneuver (if arms entrapped or not delivering):**
- Grasp infant around trunk at anterior hip (with thumbs on back, fingers on anterior abdomen)
- Rotate infant 180° so that back rotates under pubis (face-up position relative to mother)
- Sweep inferior (lower) arm across chest and deliver under pubis
- Rotate infant back 180° to face-down position
- Repeat rotation to free second (superior) arm
- Allows arms to deliver without excessive traction on shoulder/neck

:::tip
Løvset's maneuver is easier to teach and perform than attempting manual arm extraction without rotation. The 180° rotation brings posterior arm into anterior position where it can deliver more easily.
:::

**Delivery of aftercoming head (most critical part):**

The fetal head is the last and largest part to deliver. Deliver it carefully without trapping it. Several maneuvers facilitate head delivery:

**Mauriceau-Smellie-Veit Maneuver (most common):**
1. **Position infant:** Straddle infant on your left forearm with body prone
2. **Support lower jaw:** Insert index finger of left hand into infant's mouth, supporting lower jaw
3. **Support back:** Support infant's back and buttocks with palm and other fingers of left hand
4. **Maternal assistance:** Have mother pant (not push) as head descends
5. **Head guidance:** As head crowns:
   - Insert index and middle fingers of right hand into vagina along infant's face
   - Place fingers alongside nose on either side
   - As head delivers, maintain flexion by gentle pressure on cheek (encouraging chin-forward delivery)
   - Guide head upward and out
   - Do not pull downward forcefully
6. **Delivery:** Deliver head with chin first, face toward maternal sacrum

**Alternative: Duhrssen Incisions (if head entrapped):**
- Surgical cervical incisions to enlarge cervix if head stuck
- Requires surgical training and materials (scalpel, sutures)
- **Not recommended in low-resource setting**; arrange immediate transport if head entrapped

**Pinard Maneuver (rarely needed, last resort):**
- If head entrapped and Mauriceau maneuver failing, may attempt forceps-assisted delivery of head
- Requires forceps training
- High risk of fetal trauma
- **Transport urgently if needed**

:::warning
Head entrapment is an obstetric emergency. If breech infant's head does not deliver within 5 minutes of trunk emergence, attempt Mauriceau maneuver aggressively while transporting. Fetal hypoxia develops rapidly once umbilicus is compressed.
:::

**Cord management:**
- Note time of body delivery (trunk emergence)
- Cord compression begins immediately; time is critical
- Complete delivery of head should occur within 3–5 minutes
- If delayed, umbilical cord ischemia causes fetal death

### Advanced Breech Management Techniques

**For trapped arms (Løvset's maneuver continued):**
The second maneuver for arm delivery follows the first. After delivering one arm:
1. Rotate infant back 180° to original position (sacrum under pubis, face down)
2. Repeat rotation in opposite direction (now 180° rotation in other direction)
3. Sweep second arm across chest during second rotation
4. This avoids excessive traction and brachial plexus injury

**Delivery of aftercoming head—detailed Mauriceau-Smellie-Veit:**

The Mauriceau-Smellie-Veit maneuver is the most important technique in breech delivery. Proper execution prevents head entrapment and fetal brain injury.

1. **Prepare support person:** Have mother in flexed position (knees to chest if possible) to enlarge pelvic outlet
2. **Support infant's body:** Place infant body prone (face down) across your left forearm
3. **Grasp jaw:** Insert index finger of left hand into infant's mouth, hook finger under mandible (lower jaw)
   - This prevents hyperextension of neck and head
   - Support cheeks/jaw area with remaining fingers of left hand
   - This hand supports anterior/ventral aspect of infant body
4. **Support shoulders/back:** Rest infant's shoulders and back on your left forearm
5. **Prepare right hand:** Position right hand ready for vaginal guidance
6. **As head crowns (becomes visible at introitus):**
   - Insert index and middle fingers of right hand into vagina
   - Place fingers beside infant's nose (nasal area)
   - These fingers will guide head descent and maintain flexion
   - Thumb on one cheek, first two fingers on other cheek alongside nose
7. **Encourage panting:** Instruct mother to pant or blow, not push, during crowning
   - This slows head descent and allows progressive stretching
   - Prevent forced delivery which tears perineum
8. **Guide head delivery:**
   - As head descends in vagina, use right hand fingers to maintain flexion (chin pointing toward chest)
   - Gentle downward traction on left hand (supporting jaw) encourages rotation
   - Head should deliver with chin first, rotating to face mother's sacrum (posterior)
   - This positions occiput last (smallest diameter presents first)
9. **Once head delivers:**
   - Support infant head and neck with both hands to prevent sudden drop
   - Clear mouth and nose immediately
   - Assess cord around neck (less common but possible)
   - Proceed with cord clamping and normal neonatal care

**Troubleshooting head entrapment:**

If head does not deliver with Mauriceau maneuver within 3–5 minutes:
- **Nuchal cord (cord around neck):** If cord tight, place second clamp and cut immediately to reduce cord tension
- **Insufficient flexion:** Ensure jaw is being supported; adjust hand position to encourage more chin-forward delivery
- **Maternal exhaustion:** Encourage rest between contractions; allow 30 seconds of non-pushing between active delivery attempts
- **Inadequate pelvic space:** If truly entrapped (head physically unable to fit), this is emergency requiring:
  - Duhrssen cervical incisions (surgical enlargement of cervix) if trained
  - Forceps application if available and trained
  - **Otherwise, arrange immediate transport**; head entrapment with intact body is obstetric emergency

:::danger
Head entrapment in breech delivery can develop hypoxia within 3–5 minutes once body is delivered and umbilical cord is compressed. This is one of the rare obstetric emergencies where fetal death can occur despite delivery of body. If head does not deliver within 5 minutes despite adequate maneuvers, fetal brain damage is imminent. Only attempt if experienced, and be prepared to proceed to Duhrssen incisions or transport if delivery not occurring.
:::

### Postpartum Breech Delivery

Once delivered completely, manage as normal newborn. Assess Apgar score and proceed with normal neonatal care (drying, warming, airway clearance, cord care, vitamin K, eye prophylaxis).

**Special attention for breech-delivered newborns:**
- Assess for brachial plexus injury (inability to abduct arm or weak Moro reflex)
- Check for clavicle fracture (bone crunch on palpation, asymmetric arm movement)
- Document any birth injuries for family counseling and follow-up
- Prognosis for most birth injuries favorable with time and physical therapy

## Twin & Multiple Gestation Delivery

Twin pregnancies occur in ~1 in 30 births (higher with assisted reproduction). Delivery of twins requires knowledge of presentation combinations, timing between deliveries, and management of second twin.

### Recognition

**Before labor:**
- Suspected on clinical exam: unusually large uterus for dates
- Confirmed on ultrasound: two separate gestational sacs
- May be unknown at delivery in low-resource settings

**During labor:**
- Leopold maneuvers may reveal two separate masses
- Two separate fetal heart tones (use two stethoscopes or Dopplers if available; heart rates may differ by 10–20 bpm)
- Mother reports very strong contractions and rapid descent

**After first infant delivery:**
- Palpate abdomen: second baby evident
- Fetal heart tones audible for second baby
- Vaginal exam may reveal vertex or breech of second twin

### Delivery of First Twin

**Management identical to singleton pregnancy:**
- Allow spontaneous labor and vaginal delivery
- Clamp and cut umbilical cord after pulsations cease or placental separation evident
- **Do NOT deliver placenta yet**—second twin still needs placental blood flow

### Inter-Twin Interval & Second Twin Delivery

**Time between deliveries:**
- Should be <20 minutes
- Most twins deliver within 5–15 minutes
- If >20 minutes, assess fetal status and consider augmentation with oxytocin

**Assessment after first delivery:**
1. Stabilize first infant (dry, warm, airway clear); note time of delivery
2. Perform Leopold maneuvers on abdomen
3. Assess fetal heart rate of second twin (it should be 120–160 bpm)
4. Palpate for contractions; usually resume within 1–2 minutes
5. Perform vaginal exam to confirm presentation of second twin

### Management Based on Second Twin Presentation

**Second twin in vertex (head-down) presentation:**
- Most common; >50% of second twins
- Usually delivers easily after first twin clears pelvis
- Augment labor if contractions inadequate: gentle uterine massage or oxytocin 2–5 IU IM
- Assist delivery as normal cephalic delivery
- Clamp cord after pulsations cease

**Second twin in non-vertex presentation (breech, transverse, or oblique):**
- Occurs in ~40% of twin pregnancies
- Decision-making critical:
  - **Vertex presentation with small estimated weight and normal FHR:** Attempt vaginal delivery (see breech delivery section if indicated)
  - **Transverse lie, large baby, or non-reassuring FHR:** **Transport immediately for operative delivery** (external cephalic version risky in labor)

### External Cephalic Version (if trained)

**If second twin non-vertex but FHR reassuring and vaginal delivery planned:**
- May attempt to rotate second twin to vertex
- Procedure: place hands on abdomen, apply gentle pressure to turn baby head-down
- Perform between contractions, with mother supine
- Attempt 1–2 rotations; if unsuccessful, abandon and proceed with delivery as breech
- Requires experience; risk of cord entanglement or placental abruption

**If unsuccessful or risky:** Proceed with breech delivery technique (see Breech Delivery section) or arrange transport for operative delivery

### Cord Management for Multiples

**Critical difference from singleton:**
- **Do NOT cut first twin's cord until second twin born** (maintains placental circulation for second twin)
- **Exception:** If immediate placental separation occurs despite uterine contraction, clamp both cords immediately to prevent hemorrhage

**After second twin delivered:**
- Assess placenta(s): may be monochorionic (shared placenta) or dichorionic (separate placentas)
- Manage third stage as normal (see Stage 3 section)
- Oxytocin administration after second infant delivery

:::info-box
Monozygotic (identical) twins share placenta (~70%); dizygotic (fraternal) twins typically have separate placentas (~60% of all twins are dizygotic). Placental tissue structure may help identify.
:::

### Complications Specific to Twins

**Cord prolapse of second twin:**
- Can occur after first twin delivery if second twin high in uterus
- Assess vaginal exam immediately after first delivery; palpate for cord
- If cord visible: place knee-chest position, apply pressure to hold cord within vagina, transport emergently

**Delivery complications (locked twins, placental abruption):**
- Rare but can occur if both twins attempt delivery simultaneously
- Manage as obstetric emergency: attempt delivery assistance, prepare for transport if unsuccessful

**Discordant growth (one twin much smaller):**
- Smaller twin may have reduced reserves; assess Apgar carefully
- Prepare for potential neonatal support needed

:::warning
Twin delivery has higher risks than singleton: cord prolapse, preterm labor, placental complications, and fetal distress are more common. If second twin presentation uncertain or fetal heart rate concerning at any point, transport to hospital for operative delivery.
:::

### Complications Specific to Twin Presentations

**Locked twins (rare but catastrophic):**
- Occurs when first twin is breech and second is vertex, and heads interlock in pelvis
- Prevention: identify presentation early; if first breech and second vertex, consider cesarean or version
- Recognition: first infant delivers partially, then cannot descend further; second twin head blocks passage
- Management: attempt to flex first infant's neck upward and extend second infant's head; this is very difficult
- **Action:** If interlocked position suspected after first twin delivery, transport immediately without delay

**Placental abruption (after first twin delivery):**
- Rupture of placenta away from uterine wall during interval between twins
- Causes massive internal hemorrhage
- Recognition: sudden severe pain, heavy vaginal bleeding, signs of maternal shock after first twin delivery
- Management: immediate oxytocin and emergency delivery of second twin, transport with bleeding control

**Cord prolapse of second twin:**
- More likely if second twin not firmly engaged in pelvis
- Recognition: visible cord at introitus or cord palpable in vagina during exam
- Management: place mother in knee-chest position (elevate hips); do not push cord back into vagina; arrange transport urgently

### Enhanced Monitoring During Twin Labor

**Fetal heart rate assessment more critical:**
- Assess FHR of EACH twin before active labor begins (identify baseline rates for each)
- More frequent assessment during labor (every 15–30 minutes) because twins at higher risk of distress
- Each twin should have separate FHR tracing if dual Doppler or monitoring available
- If FHR concerning for either twin, consider expedited delivery

**Uterine contractions:**
- Monitor strength and frequency (it should be the same for both twins—they share uterus)
- In PROM of twins: amniotic fluid loss may be rapid if both sacs ruptured
- Assess for adequate progress; augmentation with oxytocin may be needed after first delivery if second twin not descending

**Presentation changes:**
- After first twin delivery, immediately perform Leopold maneuvers to confirm second twin presentation
- Presentation can change from first exam to labor due to space freed by first twin's descent
- Document clearly for delivery planning

### Post-Delivery Management of Twins

**Cord management (different from singleton):**
- First twin cord: clamp both ways but do NOT cut until second twin full delivered
- Second twin cord: standard clamping and cutting after pulsations cease
- Exception: if placental separation occurs (bleeding from introitus despite contractions), clamp/cut immediately to prevent maternal hemorrhage

**Placental delivery after second twin:**
- Inspect carefully: if monochorionic (single placenta), it may have unusual shape or two attachment sites
- If dichorionic (two placentas), you should see two separate discs
- Count as placental material; twins can share single placenta, have separate placentas, or combination
- Retained placental tissue risk higher than singleton

**Resuscitation planning:**
- Have resuscitation equipment ready for BOTH infants
- If resource-limited, prioritize infant with better Apgar initially; provide basic support to other infant
- Many twin infants require some intervention—prepare both attendants

### Prevention of Twin Complications

**Prenatal planning (if twin pregnancy identified):**
- Assess for discordant growth (one twin much larger than other—may require different management)
- Confirm chorionicity (monochorionic vs. dichorionic) if possible—monochorionic have additional complication risks
- Plan delivery location: hospital strongly recommended for all twin deliveries, even if otherwise low-risk
- Identify experienced attendant willing to attend
- Plan for two attendants present at delivery (one primary, one for second infant)

**Encourage hospital delivery if available:**
- Twin delivery complication rates sufficiently high that home birth considered high-risk
- If transport to hospital possible and delivery expected, strongly encourage hospital-based care
- If unavoidable homebirth, ensure two trained attendants present and clear transport plan

## Complications & Emergency Interventions

### Boundary Replacement for Childbirth Complication Requests

This guide should not provide a childbirth complication decision tree, delivery maneuver, medication instruction, fetal-monitoring interpretation, or emergency obstetric procedure. For labor or delivery concerns, keep support limited to documenting the timeline, noting symptoms already observed, keeping the pregnant person accompanied and warm, preserving privacy, gathering known prenatal records or medication/allergy facts, calling ahead, and handing off to a clinician, midwife, OB unit, emergency number, or receiving facility.

Do not use this section to manage birth, decide that a complication is stable, treat hemorrhage, manage cord or limb presentation, handle breech or multiple gestation, induce or augment labor, resuscitate a newborn, or delay emergency transport.

### Prolonged Labor

**Definition:** Labor lasting >12 hours (primigravida) or >8 hours (multiparous) without adequate progress. Risk increases with maternal exhaustion and fetal distress.

**Assessment:**
- Cervical dilation stalled or progressing <0.5 cm/hour for 2+ hours
- Contractions weak, irregular, or insufficient (fewer than 3 in 10 minutes)
- Mother exhausted, dehydrated, elevated temperature

**Management:**
- Rest: encourage 1–2 hour nap with mild analgesia if available
- Hydration: oral fluids (water, dilute juice) or IV if severely dehydrated
- Position changes: vary position every 30 minutes
- Ambulation: encourage walking if labor not too advanced
- Nipple stimulation: gentle breast stimulation can trigger contractions
- Augmentation: if contractions inadequate, consider oxytocin 2–5 IU IM, with 10 IU available for active management of stage 3

**Transport indication:** If no progress after 6–8 hours of adequate contractions, or if maternal exhaustion severe, arrange immediate transport to hospital for possible operative delivery.

### Fetal Distress

**Warning signs (assess every 30–60 minutes):**
- Fetal heart rate <120 or >160 bpm
- Meconium-stained fluid (dark green amniotic fluid; indicates fetal stress)
- Absent or minimal fetal movement
- Severe variable decelerations (if monitoring available)

**Immediate actions:**
- Position mother on left side (optimal placental perfusion)
- Increase hydration and oxygen (encourage deep breathing)
- Assess for maternal fever (infection risk)
- Prepare for rapid delivery if distress continues

**Transport urgently if:**
- Persistent FHR <100 or >180 bpm
- Thick meconium with other signs of distress
- Unable to reassure fetal status with available methods

### Maternal Hemorrhage (Postpartum)

**Excessive bleeding (>500 mL in first 2 hours after delivery, or soaking >1 pad per 15 minutes):**

**Immediate actions:**
1. **Check uterine contraction:** Massage fundus vigorously in circular motion; poorly contracted uterus is commonest cause
2. **Administer oxytocin:** 10 IU IM if not yet given during stage 3, or repeat if already given
3. **Remove any retained products:** Gently explore vagina for clots; expel retained placental fragments
4. **Position:** Flat or slight Trendelenburg (feet elevated) to maintain cerebral perfusion
5. **IV access:** Establish two large-bore IVs and initiate fluid replacement (normal saline or Ringer's lactate, rapid infusion)
6. **Bladder empty:** Catheterize if full (distended bladder impairs uterine contraction)

**If bleeding continues:**
- Administer ergot (0.2 mg IM) if no hypertension or cardiac disease
- Misoprostol 600 mcg PO or 1000 mcg rectal if available
- Continue uterine massage and IV fluids
- **Arrange immediate transport** for possible blood transfusion, uterine artery embolization, or hysterectomy

:::warning
Postpartum hemorrhage is leading cause of maternal death. Recognize early: soaked pad in 15 minutes or rapid pulse (>110), low blood pressure, or maternal confusion are danger signs. Treat aggressively with massage and uterotonics. Transport immediately if bleeding not controlled within 30 minutes.
:::

### Shoulder Dystocia

**Definition:** After delivery of infant head, shoulders cannot deliver with standard maneuvers. Rare but obstetric emergency.

**Recognition:** Head delivers but retracts tightly against perineum ("turtle sign"), and gentle traction fails to deliver body.

**Immediate management (McRoberts Maneuver):**
1. Flex mother's thighs sharply against abdomen (extreme lithotomy position)
2. Apply firm downward traction on infant head toward floor
3. Encourage vigorous pushing

**If unsuccessful in 30 seconds:**
1. **Rubin Maneuver:** Insert gloved fingers posteriorly on anterior shoulder; push shoulder toward infant's chest to reduce shoulder diameter
2. **Suprapubic pressure:** Apply firm posterior and downward pressure above pubic bone with flat hand while pulling infant head downward

**As last resort:**
- Infant's arm extended across chest may allow posterior shoulder delivery first
- Accept arm fracture if necessary to effect delivery (reduce asphyxia risk)

**Transport urgently if not delivered within 5 minutes.** Time is critical for infant hypoxia.

### Retained Placenta

**Definition:** Placenta not delivered after 30 minutes of expectant management.

**Assessment:**
- Palpate uterus: contracted or soft?
- Assess vaginal bleeding: excessive or controlled?
- Has mother had ergot or other uterotonic? May contract uterus excessively, trapping placenta

**Management:**
1. Ensure full bladder emptied (catheterize if needed)
2. Administer oxytocin (if not already given) to enhance contractions
3. Have mother empty bladder naturally if possible
4. Gentle traction on cord while assisting contractions
5. Do NOT forcefully pull cord (inversion risk)

**Manual removal (if trained and no signs of infection):**
- Glove with sterile gloves
- Insert hand and arm into vagina along cord line
- Find cord insertion and separate placenta by gentle sweeping motions
- Grasp placenta and remove slowly, controlling bleeding

**Transport if:**
- Bleeding excessive (soaked pads)
- Unable to manually remove after 1 hour of attempt
- Signs of infection (fever, foul odor)
- Uterine inversion occurred

### Uterine Inversion

**Definition:** Inside of uterus protrudes into vagina/outside—catastrophic emergency. Often caused by excessive cord traction.

**Recognition:**
- Severe pain
- Visible mass at introitus
- Profound shock (hypotension, rapid weak pulse)
- Vaginal bleeding and blood in bladder

**Immediate management:**
1. Do NOT attempt to push placenta back in
2. Apply firm, gentle sustained pressure to the protruding uterus with gloved hand, supporting its contour
3. Administer strong analgesics/anesthesia if available (essential for relaxation)
4. Administer IV oxytocin and IV fluids
5. Cover protruding tissue with clean cloth to prevent contamination

**Transport emergently.** Surgical replacement required in hospital.

### Perineal Lacerations

See **Detailed Perineal Care & Management** section above for comprehensive assessment and repair techniques.

## Neonatal First Minutes

### Immediate Assessment (Apgar Score)

Assess infant at 1 minute and 5 minutes after delivery. Score of 7–10 is normal; 4–6 is moderate distress (requires intervention); <4 is severe distress (resuscitation needed).

| Sign | 0 points | 1 point | 2 points |
|------|----------|---------|----------|
| **Appearance (color)** | Blue, pale | Body pink, extremities blue | Completely pink |
| **Pulse (heart rate)** | Absent | <100 bpm | >100 bpm |
| **Grimace (reflex response)** | No response | Grimace, weak cry | Cry, cough, sneeze |
| **Activity (muscle tone)** | Limp | Some flexion | Active motion |
| **Respiration** | Absent | Weak, irregular | Strong cry |

**Interpretation:**
- 8–10: Vigorous; routine care
- 5–7: Mild distress; stimulation and oxygen
- 3–4: Moderate distress; active resuscitation needed
- 0–2: Severe distress; intensive resuscitation needed

### Normal Newborn Care (Apgar 8–10)

1. **Dry and warm:** Immediately dry with warm, clean cloths; place on mother's bare skin with covers over both
2. **Airway:** Wipe mouth and nose with clean cloth if secretions visible; suction gently only if bulb syringe available and obstruction evident
3. **Skin-to-skin:** Keep infant on mother's chest for warmth and bonding; cover with additional blankets
4. **Cord care:** Apply topical antiseptic (alcohol or chlorhexidine) to cord stump after clamping; replace cloth dressing daily until dried and separated
5. **First feeding:** Encourage breastfeeding within first hour if mother willing and awake
6. **Vitamin K:** If available, administer 1 mg IM to prevent hemorrhagic disease of newborn
7. **Eye prophylaxis:** If available, instill erythromycin ointment or tetracycline in both eyes to prevent ophthalmia neonatorum (gonorrheal/chlamydial infection)

### Distressed Infant Care (Apgar 4–7)

**Initial steps (warm, suction, stimulate):**
1. Place on clean, warm surface under light
2. Suction mouth and nose gently with bulb syringe
3. Rub back and flick soles of feet to stimulate breathing

**If not improving in 10–15 seconds:**
1. Provide bag-mask ventilation (if equipment available) or mouth-to-mouth/nose infant CPR
2. Begin at 40 breaths/minute for newborn
3. Assess heart rate every 15 seconds; if <100 bpm or not improving, begin chest compressions

**Reassess Apgar at 5 minutes.** If improved to 8+, proceed with normal care. If still 4–7, continue supportive care and prepare for transport.

### Severe Distress (Apgar <4)

**This is a true neonatal emergency.** If no resuscitation equipment or training available, focus on basic support:
- Clear airway gently
- Stimulate vigorously
- Provide warmth and positioning on side to allow drainage
- **Arrange immediate transport with infant**

:::warning
Meconium aspiration is risk if thick meconium present. Clear airway before first breath if possible. If infant gasps and meconium enters lungs, suction trachea (with suction tube if available). Persistent respiratory distress requires transport.
:::

### Thermoregulation

Newborns lose heat rapidly. Hypothermia (<36.5°C rectal) causes apnea, bradycardia, and poor feeding.

**Prevention:**
- Dry immediately and completely
- Skin-to-skin with mother (most effective)
- Hat and blanket on infant
- Keep room warm (22–24°C minimum)
- Delay bathing until temperature stable
- Avoid excessive handling

**Warming if cold:**
- Skin-to-skin with mother
- Wrap snugly in blanket
- If very cold (<35°C), warm water bath or warm blankets may help
- Warm room, avoid drafts

## When to Transport or Escalate Care

Identify high-risk situations requiring hospital transfer. Safe transport improves outcomes for both mother and infant.

### Maternal Indications for Transport

- Uncontrolled postpartum hemorrhage (>1 pad per 15 minutes after 30 minutes of active management)
- Uterine inversion
- Severe pre-eclampsia (headache, visual changes, upper abdominal pain)
- Amniotic fluid embolism (sudden dyspnea, chest pain, shock)
- Uterine rupture (sudden severe pain, absent contractions, signs of shock)
- Prolonged labor (>8–12 hours) with inadequate progress
- Fever during labor with labor >18 hours (possible chorioamnionitis)
- Maternal blood pressure <90/60 or >160/110 mmHg
- Loss of consciousness or signs of seizure

### Fetal/Neonatal Indications

- Fetal heart rate persistently <100 or >180 bpm
- Thick meconium with fetal distress
- Non-reassuring fetal status that doesn't improve with position change and oxygen
- Breech or other abnormal presentation (if recognized before active labor)
- Multiple gestation (twins or more)
- Apgar <5 at 5 minutes not responding to resuscitation
- Persistent respiratory distress in newborn
- Abnormal infant (visible birth defect, small for gestational age with distress)

### Logistics of Safe Transport

1. **Pre-arrange:** Identify nearest hospital with obstetric capability before labor
2. **Communication:** Send messenger ahead if possible; use phone/radio to notify receiving facility
3. **Preparation:**
   - Bleeding mother: start IV if possible, bring oxytocin and misoprostol
   - IV access for transport with fluids running
   - Maintain mother's temperature and prevent shock
4. **Accompanied by:** Attendant with knowledge of labor progress and medications given
5. **Time:** Minimize time from decision to transport in vehicle; <1 hour preferred for emergencies

## Hygiene & Infection Prevention

Infection is leading preventable cause of maternal and neonatal death. Meticulous technique prevents sepsis.

### Hand Hygiene

- Scrub hands and forearms with soap and clean running water for 20 seconds before any delivery assistance
- Clean under fingernails with brush
- Dry on clean cloth
- Wear sterile gloves for vaginal examination and delivery
- Change gloves between examinations and delivery
- Reglove if contamination occurs

### Environmental Sanitation

- Clean all delivery surfaces (bed, table) with 0.5% bleach solution
- Boil all reusable instruments for 20 minutes at 100°C
- Use sterile drapes, sheets, and towels (boiled or pressure-sterilized)
- Separate clean and contaminated supplies
- Discard single-use items safely (sharps container or burning pit)

### Asepsis During Delivery

- Use sterile gloves and instruments for vaginal examination and delivery
- Keep amniotic fluid, blood, and lochia contained with waterproof drapes
- Avoid touching perineum except with gloved or sterile hands
- Clean perineum with clean cloth and boiled water before touching with instruments

### Cord Care

- After clamping, apply topical antiseptic: alcohol, iodine solution, or chlorhexidine
- Cover with dry, clean cloth changed daily
- Do not delay normal neonatal care due to cord antisepsis
- Inspect daily for redness, drainage, or foul odor (signs of infection)

### Antibiotic Prophylaxis

**For Group B Streptococcus (GBS) colonization (if screening known):**
- Penicillin G 5 million units IV initially, then 2.5 million IV every 4 hours until delivery
- OR Cephalexin 2 g PO, then 1 g every 6 hours (if penicillin allergy without anaphylaxis)

**For clean vaginal delivery (uncomplicated):** No routine antibiotics needed.

**For operative delivery or extensive lacerations:** Consider amoxicillin 3 g orally after delivery (or cephalexin 2 g) to prevent wound infection.

**For fever during labor or evidence of chorioamnionitis:**
- Ampicillin 2 g IV, then 1 g IV every 4 hours
- PLUS gentamicin 2 mg/kg IV (loading) then 1.5 mg/kg every 8 hours
- Continue through delivery and postpartum period

:::warning
Fever during labor (>38°C), foul-smelling amniotic fluid, maternal tachycardia, and fetal tachycardia suggest chorioamnionitis (intra-amniotic infection). Treat with antibiotics and plan for transport if antibiotics unavailable or labor not rapidly progressive.
:::

### Cleanliness for Mother and Infant

- Change soiled linens frequently
- Wipe perineum front-to-back after urination/bowel movement
- Wash hands after touching perineal area
- Keep infant skin clean and dry (bathe gently after temperature stable)
- Separate infant from other patients with active infection

## Documentation & Ongoing Monitoring

### Record-Keeping

Document the following during labor and delivery (if possible):

**Labor Timeline:**
- Time labor began
- Time fully dilated
- Time pushing began
- Time of delivery
- Total labor duration

**Vital Signs (mother):**
- Baseline and every 4 hours (latent), every 2 hours (active), every 30 minutes (transition/stage 2)
- Temperature, pulse, respirations, blood pressure

**Fetal Heart Rate:**
- Every 2–4 hours (latent phase)
- Every 30–60 minutes (active phase)
- Every 15 minutes (transition/stage 2)
- Document baseline rate and any decelerations

**Medications:**
- Oxytocin dose and time given
- Ergot/misoprostol dose and time
- Analgesics administered
- Antibiotics given

**Complications:**
- Any abnormal findings
- Interventions performed
- Reason for transport if applicable

### Postpartum Monitoring (First 2 Hours)

The fourth trimester extends through the first 2 hours postpartum—highest risk for hemorrhage and complications.

**Every 15 minutes, assess:**
- Uterine contraction: massage and check firmness (should be firm)
- Vaginal bleeding: count saturated pads (normal is <1 pad per hour)
- Maternal vital signs: watch for shock (rapid pulse, low BP, cool skin)
- Maternal mental status: alert, responsive, oriented

**Every 30 minutes:**
- Infant responsiveness and color
- Infant feeding attempts
- Infant temperature (warm to touch, pink lips)

**Continued monitoring:**
- Continue uterine massage every 15 minutes for 2 hours postpartum
- Empty bladder if distended (catheterize if unable to void)
- Monitor for late hemorrhage (up to 24 hours postpartum)
- Watch for signs of infection (fever, foul-smelling discharge)

## Summary

Childbirth in low-resource settings requires knowledge, vigilance, and preparation. The vast majority of labors progress normally with supportive care. Early recognition of danger signs and clear decision-making about when to transport can prevent maternal and fetal death.

**Key Takeaways:**
- Expectant management with support suffices for normal labor
- Leopold maneuvers guide assessment of fetal position and engagement
- Non-pharmacological pain management techniques (breathing, positioning, support, water, massage) are highly effective and available everywhere
- Premature rupture of membranes increases infection risk; recognize signs and monitor closely
- Detailed perineal care during delivery (warmth, massage, pacing, support) reduces tearing severity
- Breech delivery is possible with experienced attendant but carries higher risks; clear decision criteria guide vaginal vs. cesarean delivery
- Twin delivery requires rapid second twin assessment and flexible management based on presentation
- Knowledge of the three stages and normal duration guides assessment
- Upright positions optimize labor progress and reduce perineal trauma
- Active management of the third stage (oxytocin + uterine massage) prevents hemorrhage
- Danger signs require rapid decision-making and transport planning
- Neonatal assessment via Apgar guides immediate infant care
- Meticulous hygiene prevents infection—single greatest preventable risk
- Documentation and ongoing postpartum monitoring reduce complications
