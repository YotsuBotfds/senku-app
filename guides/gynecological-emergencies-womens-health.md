:::danger
Gynecological emergencies (ectopic pregnancy, ovarian torsion, severe hemorrhage) are life-threatening and require rapid assessment and intervention. Many conditions mimic appendicitis or other acute abdomen emergencies. High suspicion and systematic examination are essential.
:::

## Complaint-First Red-Zone Ownership: Pregnancy, Pelvic Pain, Heavy Bleeding

Use this guide first for **missed or late period plus one-sided pelvic pain**, **possible pregnancy plus shoulder-tip or belly pain**, **early pregnancy bleeding with dizziness or fainting**, and **heavy vaginal bleeding with severe pelvic pain**. Treat these as possible **ectopic pregnancy, ovarian torsion, miscarriage complications, or hemorrhage** until proven otherwise.

First actions are emergency actions: arrange urgent medical care or evacuation now, assess airway/breathing/circulation, lay the person flat if faint or dizzy, keep them warm, do not give food or drink if surgery may be needed, and check a pregnancy test if available without delaying escalation.

For vaginal bleeding with pelvic pain or possible pregnancy, use pads or cloth only to observe bleeding amount during transport. Do **not** apply direct pressure inside the vagina, perform internal inspection, or use uterine massage unless this is clearly postpartum bleeding after a recent delivery. Uterine massage belongs to postpartum hemorrhage, not early pregnancy bleeding, missed-period pain, or undifferentiated heavy pelvic bleeding.

Do **not** route these red-zone prompts to routine menstrual-cramp care, STI-only advice, hemorrhoid/direct-pressure care, nosebleed cleanup, cough/cold care, or general survival basics just because the user says "period," "bleeding," "cramps," or "not sure if pregnant."

## Menstrual Disorders

### Amenorrhea (Absence of Menses)

**Primary amenorrhea:** Never menstruated by age 15–16.
**Secondary amenorrhea:** Previously regular menses; now absent >3 months.

| Cause | Mechanism | Management |
|-------|-----------|------------|
| **Pregnancy** | Hormonal suppression of menses | Urine/serum pregnancy test; prenatal care if confirmed |
| **Extreme thinness/malnutrition** | Fat stores critical for estrogen production | Nutritional rehabilitation |
| **Excessive exercise** | Depletes body fat and triggers hypothalamic shutdown | Reduced exercise intensity |
| **Stress/trauma** | Hypothalamic-pituitary suppression | Stress reduction; may spontaneously resolve |
| **Thyroid dysfunction** | Hyper- or hypothyroidism disrupts cycle | TSH testing; thyroid medication if available |
| **Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)** | Androgen excess, insulin resistance | Metformin if available; weight loss; dietary modification |

**Management in austere settings:**
- Rule out pregnancy first (test if available).
- Assess nutritional status; increase caloric/protein intake if undernourished.
- Reduce physical exertion.
- Reassess in 3 months.

### Menorrhagia (Heavy Menstrual Bleeding)

**Definition:** Menstrual bleeding >80 mL per cycle (typically soaks >5 pads per day).

**Red-zone boundary:** Heavy bleeding with severe pelvic pain, dizziness, fainting, possible pregnancy, missed or late period, shoulder-tip pain, or belly pain is not routine menorrhagia care. Treat it as possible ectopic pregnancy, ovarian torsion, miscarriage complication, or gynecologic hemorrhage: arrange urgent medical care or evacuation first. Use pads or cloth only to observe bleeding amount during transport. Do not apply vaginal direct pressure, do not perform internal inspection, and do not use uterine massage unless this is clearly postpartum bleeding after delivery or a visible external wound is the bleeding source.

**Causes:**
- Fibroid uterus (benign smooth muscle tumor)
- Polyps (benign growths in endometrium)
- Coagulopathy (bleeding disorder)
- Intrauterine device (IUD) side effect
- Pelvic inflammatory disease (infection)

**Management:**
1. **Check for anemia:** Assess pale conjunctivae, fatigue, shortness of breath (blood loss effects).
2. **Give iron supplementation:** Ferrous sulfate 325 mg daily to rebuild hemoglobin.
3. **Give hemostatic medications (if available):**
   - Give tranexamic acid (antifibrinolytic): 1–1.5 g 3–4× daily during menses; reduces bleeding 30–50%
   - Give NSAIDs (ibuprofen): 400–600 mg 3–4× daily during menses; modest reduction in bleeding
4. **Ensure hydration/nutrition:** Provide adequate fluid and protein intake.
5. **Evacuate if:** Hemoglobin <7 g/dL (symptomatic anemia) or bleeding uncontrollable despite above measures.

### Dysmenorrhea (Painful Menses)

**Primary dysmenorrhea:** Painful periods from menarche (often no structural pathology).
**Secondary dysmenorrhea:** Pain from identifiable pathology (endometriosis, fibroids, PID).

**Management:**
1. **Give NSAIDs:** Ibuprofen 400–600 mg or naproxen 250 mg, 3–4× daily starting day 1 of menses. Most effective if given preemptively.
2. **Apply heat:** Use warm compress or heating pad to lower abdomen; reduces pain intensity 30–40%.
3. **Encourage exercise:** Light activity (walking, stretching) reduces cramping.
4. **If severe:** Consider underlying pathology; seek ultrasound evaluation if available.

## Ectopic Pregnancy: Recognition and Management

Ectopic pregnancy is fertilized egg implanted outside uterus (usually fallopian tube). Life-threatening emergency with high mortality if untreated.

**Emergency-first aliases:** early pregnancy bleeding with dizziness, bleeding in early pregnancy with faintness, missed or late period with one-sided pelvic pain, possible pregnancy with shoulder-tip pain or belly pain, and heavy bleeding with severe pelvic pain all route here first. In these presentations, do not apply vaginal direct pressure, do not inspect internally, and do not use uterine massage unless the person has clearly just delivered a baby or there is a visible external wound.

### Risk Factors

- **Prior pelvic inflammatory disease (PID):** Scarring narrows tubes
- **Endometriosis:** Abnormal uterine tissue
- **IUD use:** Increases relative risk (though absolute risk low)
- **Prior ectopic:** Recurrence risk 25%

### Diagnosis

| Finding | Ectopic Pregnancy | Normal Pregnancy |
|---------|-------------------|------------------|
| **Urine hCG test** | Positive | Positive |
| **Serum hCG level** | Present but rises slowly (doubles every 48–72 hr; normal doubles every 48 hr) | Rapid rise |
| **Ultrasound** | No gestational sac in uterus; possible mass in tube or pelvis | Gestational sac in uterus; yolk sac visible by 5–6 weeks |
| **Symptoms** | Vaginal bleeding, pelvic pain (often one-sided) | Vaginal bleeding, pelvic pain (usually bilateral or midline); less severe |
| **Hemodynamic** | Shock possible (ruptured tube) | Stable unless hemorrhage from other cause |

### Clinical Presentation

**Early (before rupture):**
- Positive pregnancy test
- Vaginal bleeding (usually lighter than menstrual bleeding)
- Pelvic pain (often one-sided, corresponding to tube location)
- Symptoms may be minimal initially

**Rupture (emergency):**
- Sudden severe abdominal pain (often described as worst pain ever)
- Vaginal bleeding
- Shoulder-tip pain, belly pain, fainting, or severe dizziness can mean internal bleeding
- Hypotension, tachycardia, shock (pale, clammy, lightheaded)
- Peritoneal signs (tenderness throughout abdomen)

### Management

**Bleeding in early pregnancy with dizziness or fainting:**
- Arrange urgent medical care or evacuation immediately; treat as possible ectopic rupture or serious hemorrhage until proven otherwise.
- Lay the person flat if dizzy or faint, keep warm, monitor airway/breathing/circulation, and avoid food or drink if surgery may be needed.
- Use pads or cloth only to observe bleeding amount during transport. Do not apply direct pressure to vaginal bleeding, do not perform internal inspection, and do not use uterine massage.

**Suspected ectopic (before rupture):**
- Confirm with ultrasound if available
- If confirmed and stable (no rupture signs): Give methotrexate injection (stops pregnancy growth). Requires close hCG monitoring over weeks.
- If methotrexate unavailable: Arrange surgical intervention (salpingectomy, removal of tube)
- Restrict strenuous activity; bleeding risk increases with exertion

**Ruptured ectopic (emergency):**
1. **Get IV access:** Insert large-bore IV; begin rapid fluid resuscitation
2. **Type and cross:** Blood typing and crossmatch for transfusion
3. **Arrange surgical intervention:** Surgical exploration and removal of ruptured tube
4. **Give transfusion if needed:** Goal hemoglobin >7 g/dL
5. **Provide ICU-level care:** Post-operative monitoring for complications

**Prognosis:** Without surgery, ruptured ectopic is often fatal (mortality 50% if untreated). With rapid surgery, survival >99%.

## Ovarian Torsion

Ovarian torsion is twisting of ovarian ligament, cutting off blood supply. Surgical emergency.

### Risk Factors

- **Large ovarian cyst:** Increases weight, torsion risk
- **Pregnancy:** Physiologic changes increase risk
- **Prior torsion:** Recurrence possible
- **Young age:** More common in reproductive years

### Presentation

**Classic findings:**
- Sudden-onset severe abdominal pain (often colicky, intermittent)
- One-sided (ipsilateral to affected ovary)
- Nausea/vomiting common
- Hemodynamic stability usually maintained (unlike ectopic rupture)
- Peritoneal signs variable

**Timing:** Pain may come and go (intermittent torsion with spontaneous detorsion).

### Diagnosis

**Clinical:** Sudden severe unilateral pelvic pain in young woman with pelvic ultrasound findings (see below).

**Ultrasound (if available):**
- Enlarged ovary (>4 cm)
- Absent or reduced blood flow on Doppler
- Cyst or mass often visible
- Free fluid in pelvis possible

**Differential diagnosis (see similar presentation):**
- Appendicitis (pain right side, nausea/vomiting)
- Pelvic inflammatory disease (fever, bilateral tenderness)
- Ectopic pregnancy (vaginal bleeding, positive pregnancy test)
- Kidney stone (hematuria, flank pain radiating to groin)

### Management

**Immediate:**
1. **IV access:** Establish large-bore IV
2. **Analgesics:** Pain control important (often severe)
3. **NPO (nothing by mouth):** Surgery likely needed
4. **Surgical consultation:** Definitive diagnosis + treatment is emergency surgery

**Surgical intervention:**
- **Laparoscopy (minimally invasive):** Allows visualization and detorsion; ovarian preservation possible if viable
- **Laparotomy (open surgery):** If ovary necrotic (dead tissue), removal necessary
- **Timing critical:** Ischemia time <6 hours: ovary salvageable 90% of cases. >12 hours: ovary usually necrotic.

**Prognosis:** Excellent if operated <6 hours (ovary salvage). If delayed, fertility impact depends on whether ovary salvageable.

</section>

## Postpartum Hemorrhage: Recognition and Management

Postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) is leading cause of maternal death globally. Defined as bleeding >500 mL within 24 hours of delivery (>1000 mL = severe).

This section applies only after delivery. Do not borrow uterine massage, manual uterine exploration, or postpartum laceration inspection for early pregnancy bleeding, missed-period pelvic pain, or undifferentiated heavy vaginal bleeding with severe pelvic pain.

### Primary Causes

| Cause | Mechanism | Risk Factors | Management |
|-------|-----------|--------------|------------|
| **Uterine atony (most common, ~50%)** | Uterus fails to contract after placenta delivery | Prolonged labor, multiple prior pregnancies | Uterotonic drugs (oxytocin, ergot), uterine massage |
| **Placental abnormality (~20%)** | Retained placenta or abnormal implantation | Prior cesarean, placental disease | Manual removal, curettage |
| **Genital tract laceration (~20%)** | Tears to cervix, vagina, or perineum | Instrumental delivery, rapid labor | Direct visualization and repair (sutures) |
| **Coagulopathy (~10%)** | Blood clotting failure | Sepsis, DIC, massive transfusion | Fresh frozen plasma, platelets, correction of underlying cause |

### Recognition and Initial Management

**Early signs (before hemodynamic instability):**
- Vaginal bleeding soaking >1 pad per 15 minutes
- Boggy uterus (uterine atony) on abdominal palpation
- Tachycardia (heart rate >110 bpm)
- Tachypnea (breathing >20 breaths/min)

**Immediate actions:**
1. **Call for help** — Do not manage alone
2. **Large-bore IV access:** ×2 if possible
3. **Uterine massage:** Place hand on lower abdomen, massage uterine fundus in circular motion. Stimulates contraction.
4. **Empty bladder:** Full bladder impedes uterine contraction; catheterize if needed
5. **Uterotonic medication:**
   - **Oxytocin (Pitocin):** 10 units IM or IV. First-line; causes sustained contraction
   - **Ergot alkaloid (ergotamine):** 0.2 mg IM (if oxytocin unavailable). Causes sustained contraction (but contraindicated in hypertension)
   - **Misoprostol:** 800–1000 mcg rectally (if oxytocin/ergot unavailable). Slower action but effective

**Advanced hemorrhage (if bleeding continues despite above):**
1. **Cross-matched blood:** Transfuse if available
2. **Fluid resuscitation:** Rapid normal saline/lactated Ringer's bolus
3. **Manual exploration:** Check for retained placenta, lacerations
4. **Uterine tamponade:** Pack uterus with gauze or apply Foley catheter balloon (inflated to 30–50 mL in uterine cavity) to apply pressure
5. **Surgical intervention:** If above fails, may require surgery (hysterectomy is last resort but life-saving in uncontrollable PPH)

**Prognosis:** Modern management with prompt uterotonic + transfusion has 98–99% survival. Delayed recognition worsens outcome.

## Breast Infections: Mastitis and Abscess

### Mastitis (Breast Inflammation/Infection)

**Cause:** Usually bacterial (Staphylococcus aureus, Streptococcus) entering through cracked nipple.

**Symptoms:**
- Breast tenderness, swelling, redness
- Fever (often >38.5°C / 101.3°F)
- Flu-like symptoms (myalgia, malaise)
- Usually unilateral

**Risk factors:**
- Breastfeeding (milk stasis, cracked nipple)
- Immunosuppression

**Management:**
1. **Continue breastfeeding** (if nursing): Empties breast, prevents milk stasis. Infection not transmitted to infant.
2. **Antibiotics:** Dicloxacillin or cephalexin 500 mg 4× daily for 10 days (covers Staph)
3. **Analgesics:** Ibuprofen or acetaminophen
4. **Local measures:** Warm compresses, supportive bra
5. **Avoid nipple trauma:** Ensure correct latch; express milk if direct breastfeeding painful

**Prognosis:** Mastitis responds well to antibiotics and continued feeding. Untreated, can progress to abscess.

### Breast Abscess

**Cause:** Untreated or progressive mastitis; localized collection of pus.

**Symptoms:**
- Persistent fever despite antibiotics
- Focal fluctuance (movable mass indicating pus collection)
- Increasing pain
- Erythema and induration localizing to one area

**Management:**
1. **Antibiotics:** Continue or upgrade if initial regimen failed
2. **Drainage:** Needle aspiration (can often be done bedside) or surgical incision and drainage
3. **Culture:** Send pus for bacterial culture + sensitivity (ensures optimal antibiotic selection)
4. **Imaging:** Ultrasound confirms abscess and guides drainage

**Prognosis:** Drainage essential; abscess will not resolve with antibiotics alone. Good outcome with prompt drainage.

## Urinary Tract Infection and Pelvic Inflammatory Disease

### Acute Cystitis (Bladder Infection/UTI)

**Symptoms:** Dysuria (painful urination), frequency, urgency, suprapubic tenderness, hematuria possible.

**Diagnosis:** Urinalysis showing pyuria (white blood cells), nitrites, leukocyte esterase.

**Management:**
- **Antibiotics:** Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole (TMP-SMX) 1 double-strength tablet 2× daily for 3 days (uncomplicated UTI), or nitrofurantoin 100 mg 2× daily for 5 days
- **Analgesics:** Phenazopyridine (Pyridium) relieves dysuria (colors urine orange; temporary)
- **Hydration:** Increased fluid intake flushes bacteria

### Acute Pyelonephritis (Kidney Infection)

**Symptoms:** Fever, flank pain (CVA tenderness), nausea/vomiting, dysuria (less prominent than cystitis).

**Diagnosis:** Urinalysis as above, plus fever and systemic symptoms. Blood culture if septic.

**Management:**
- **Hospitalization if:** Sepsis, pregnancy, inability to tolerate oral medications
- **Antibiotics:** IV ceftriaxone 1–2 g daily (if hospitalized) or oral fluoroquinolone (ciprofloxacin 500 mg 2× daily) for 7–14 days
- **Fluids:** IV hydration if hospitalized; oral if outpatient
- **Analgesics:** Ibuprofen or acetaminophen

**Prognosis:** Good with prompt antibiotics. Untreated, can progress to sepsis and renal scarring.

### Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)

**Cause:** Bacterial infection (usually sexually transmitted: chlamydia, gonorrhea) ascending from cervix to uterus, fallopian tubes, ovaries.

**Symptoms:**
- Pelvic pain (often bilateral)
- Cervical motion tenderness (pain with movement of cervix on exam)
- Fever
- Vaginal discharge (purulent if gonorrhea)
- Dysuria

**Diagnosis:** Clinical (above findings) + cultures if available.

**Management:**
1. **Antibiotics (empiric, covering both chlamydia and gonorrhea):**
   - Ceftriaxone 250 mg IM + azithromycin 1 g oral (single dose), then doxycycline 100 mg 2× daily for 7 days
   - Or: Fluoroquinolone (ciprofloxacin 500 mg 2× daily for 7 days) + metronidazole (for anaerobic coverage)
2. **Analgesics and rest**
3. **Follow-up:** If not improving in 48–72 hours, consider hospitalization, IV antibiotics, or imaging (ultrasound/CT) to rule out abscess

**Complications:** Ectopic pregnancy (scarred tubes), infertility, chronic pelvic pain, tubo-ovarian abscess.

**Prognosis:** Early treatment prevents complications. Untreated, can progress to sepsis and infertility.

## Menstrual Management and Reusable Menstrual Products

In austere settings, menstrual products may be unavailable. Alternatives sustain dignity and prevent infection.

### Reusable Pads

**Cloth pads (DIY):**
1. Cut 4–6 layers of soft absorbent fabric (old cloth diapers, cotton T-shirts, flannel sheets)
2. Fold into pad shape (7×15 cm roughly)
3. Wash daily in cold water (hot water sets blood stains), then hot wash with soap
4. Air-dry or sun-dry (sunlight is natural disinfectant)
5. Rotate through 5–6 pads so always have clean pads available

**Insertion:** Fold edges, place in underwear; may need elastic strip to hold in place.

### Menstrual Cup

If available commercially:
- Reusable silicone cup inserted into vagina; collects blood
- Changed every 6–8 hours
- Wash with soap and water; boil between cycles
- Requires learning curved insertion technique
- Very sustainable; lasts 5–10 years

### Alternative Methods

**Period panties (reusable period underwear):**
- Underwear with absorbent gusset; washed like cloth pads
- High absorbency; can be worn alone on light days

**Sponges (natural):**
- Natural sea sponges or crafted cotton sponges
- Insert like tampon; absorb blood
- Wash thoroughly after each use; allow to dry
- Reusable indefinitely with care
- Risk: May harbor bacteria if not dried completely

**Patience (menstrual extraction/suppression):**
- In austere conditions, some women learn to empty menstrual fluid by hand (similar to menstrual extraction technique)
- Requires practice and clean hands; infection risk if technique poor
- Not standard practice; only in survival situations

### Hygiene in Austere Settings

- **Change product frequently:** Every 2–4 hours at minimum (reduces infection risk)
- **Wash hands before/after:** Prevent introducing bacteria
- **Genital washing:** Use clean water and cloth; avoid soaps (disrupt normal flora)
- **Dry thoroughly:** Moisture promotes fungal growth
- **Sunlight:** Hang used products in sunlight to dry (kills bacteria and removes stains)

## Cervical Cancer Screening Basics

Cervical cancer is preventable with screening. HPV (human papillomavirus) is primary risk factor.

### Pap Smear Test

**How it works:**
1. Speculum inserted into vagina
2. Brush or spatula scrapes cells from cervix
3. Cells spread on slide or placed in liquid medium
4. Sent to lab for microscopy

**Interpretation:**
- **Normal (negative):** No abnormalities
- **ASC-US (atypical squamous cells):** Possibly caused by HPV; repeat screening
- **LSIL (low-grade SIL):** Mild dysplasia; often regresses; repeat screening
- **HSIL (high-grade SIL):** Moderate-to-severe dysplasia; risk of cancer; colposcopy needed
- **Cancer:** Invasive cancer; immediate referral to oncology

**Screening interval:**
- Age 21–29: Every 3 years
- Age 30–65: Every 3 years (Pap) or 5 years (HPV testing)
- Age >65: Discontinue if adequate prior screening

### Field-Expedient Cervical Inspection

In austere settings without Pap smear capability:

1. **Visual inspection with acetic acid (VIA):**
   - Apply 5% acetic acid (white vinegar) to cervix
   - Observe for 1 minute
   - White area = positive (suggests dysplasia)
   - Has ~60–70% sensitivity for HSIL
   - Can guide biopsy if resources available

2. **Cervical biopsy:**
   - If visible lesion or positive VIA: Take small tissue sample with biopsy forceps
   - Send to pathology (if available)
   - Confirms dysplasia grade

### HPV Vaccination (Prevention)

- **Gardasil (recombinant vaccine):** Covers 9 HPV types
- **Cervarix (bivalent vaccine):** Covers 2 HPV types
- **Age:** Most effective <25 years old; efficacy decreases with age
- **Dose:** 2–3 injections over 6–12 months
- **Efficacy:** 95%+ in preventing vaccine-type HPV infections if given before sexual activity

Prevention is most cost-effective strategy; vaccination in adolescence prevents most cervical cancers.
