## Overview

Dead reckoning (also called "deduced reckoning") is navigation using distance and direction from a known point, without external reference (compass, stars, GPS). In post-collapse scenarios where sky is obscured (cloud, smoke, urban canyons) for extended periods or compasses fail, dead reckoning enables controlled movement and prevents catastrophic disorientation.

This guide covers field-tested techniques for measuring distance, maintaining direction without magnetic reference, and using natural and terrain features as navigation anchors.

:::tip
**Boundary:** If you only need to face the right direction, recover north/south/east/west, or get a compass or sky fix before you start moving, use [Navigation & Timekeeping](navigation) or [Map Reading & Compass Basics](map-reading-compass-basics). Use this guide when you already have a known start point and need to keep a route on course leg by leg using paces, turns, landmarks, handrails, or catch features.
:::

:::warning
Dead reckoning accuracy degrades with distance and time. After 5+ km, error accumulates significantly (±5-15% deviation is typical). Combine dead reckoning with terrain association and natural features for best results.
:::

## Distance Measurement: Pace Counting

### Individual Pace Calibration

**Baseline (walking at normal speed on level terrain):**

Most adults cover approximately 1 meter per stride (from toe of one foot to toe of the same foot on next step). To establish personal calibration:

1. **Measure reference distance:** Mark a 100-meter distance on level ground (use rope, mark with stones)
2. **Walk naturally:** Walk the distance at normal pace (not rushing, not dragging)
3. **Count steps:** Note number of steps taken (left foot step = 1, right foot = 1, or count every other step and multiply by 2)
4. **Calculate pace:** Divide 100 by number of steps

**Example:** 100 meters = 68 steps → 100/68 = 1.47 meters per step

**Repeat 3-5 times** to establish average. This is your **individual pace length.**

### Pace Counting in the Field

**Technique:**
1. **Count every double step** (one pace = left step + right step = 2 meters for typical adult)
2. **Reset counter every 10 paces** (10 paces = ~20 meters typical)
3. **Keep tally:** Use pebbles in pocket (move one pebble per 10 paces to a different pocket), or simple tally marks

**Example tracking system:**
- 1 pebble moved = 10 paces = ~20 meters
- 5 pebbles = 50 paces = ~100 meters
- After 50 paces, clear the pebbles and restart

**Calculation example:**
- 50 paces moved = 5 groups × 10 paces = 50 paces
- At 1.47 m/step × 2 (double step) = 2.94 m per 10-pace group
- 50 paces = 5 groups × 2.94 m = 147 meters traveled

### Pace Correction Factors

Individual pace varies based on conditions:

| Condition | Adjustment | Reason |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| Uphill (moderate) | +15-25% steps needed | Fighting gravity |
| Downhill (moderate) | -10-15% steps needed | Less effort per distance |
| Soft ground (mud, sand) | +20-30% steps needed | Sinking, less stride length |
| Dense brush/forest | +25-40% steps needed | Zigzagging, obstacles |
| Heavy load (pack) | +10-20% steps needed | Weight increases effort |
| Fatigue | +10-30% steps needed | Stride shortens |
| Night/darkness | +15-25% steps needed | Cautious, shorter stride |

**Application:** If traveling uphill with pack and mud, expect 50-70% more steps than flat ground baseline. Adjust pace count accordingly or accept higher error margin.

## Direction Maintenance Without Compass

### Natural Direction Indicators (Northern Hemisphere)

**Sun position (primary method if sky is partially visible):**
- **Sunrise:** East (roughly 90° from north)
- **Noon:** South (roughly 180° from north)
- **Sunset:** West (roughly 270° from north)
- **Midpoint between sunrise and sunset:** South

**Accuracy:** ±30 degrees possible with practice; useful for confirming general direction

**Shadow stick method (if sun briefly visible):**
1. Drive straight stick vertically into ground
2. Mark shadow tip with stone/marker
3. Wait 15-20 minutes (shadow moves perceptibly)
4. Mark new shadow tip
5. Line from old marker to new marker points approximately east-west
6. Perpendicular line points north-south

**Moon phase (if sky clears at night):**
- New moon: Not useful (invisible)
- Crescent (early): Points west in evening
- Half moon (first quarter): South at sunset
- Full moon: East at sunset, south at midnight
- Half moon (last quarter): South at sunrise

**Star position (if multiple stars visible):**
- Northern Hemisphere: Find Polaris (North Star) using Big Dipper pointer stars
- If Polaris obscured: Use Cassiopeia (W-shaped constellation) for north reference
- Accuracy: ±5 degrees with training

**Wind patterns (slow but reliable):**
- Seasonal winds often follow consistent directions
- Prevailing wind in temperate regions: West (varies by latitude and season)
- Morning/evening breezes often follow terrain valley directions
- Useful for gradual course correction over hours/days

**Vegetation indicators (long-term, less reliable):**
- Snow on north-facing slopes lasts longer (indicates north)
- Tree growth often denser/greener on north-facing slopes in some climates
- Lichen tends to be denser on north-facing tree trunks (unreliable; avoid relying solely on this)
- Moss growth is unreliable and climate-dependent

:::info-box
No single natural indicator is perfectly reliable. Combine multiple methods (sun position + wind + terrain features) to triangulate direction and reduce error.
:::

## Terrain Association

### Terrain Features as Navigation Anchors

Terrain features provide structure and memory aids for navigation when dead reckoning alone is risky.

**Linear features (excellent anchors):**
- **Rivers/streams:** Flow generally downhill; follow downstream for lower elevation/population areas
- **Ridgelines:** Consistent bearing; visible from distance
- **Valley directions:** Often aligned with ancient travel routes; consistent bearing
- **Roads/trails:** May be overgrown but visible on terrain; follow or parallel for consistent direction

**Point features (landmarks):**
- **Hill/mountain peak:** Visible from distance; use for back-bearing confirmation
- **Building or structure:** Visible from distance; useful reference
- **Rock formation/cliff:** Permanent, visible reference for correction

**Catch features (stopping points):**
- **Road or highway:** East-west or north-south running; signals when you've gone too far
- **River/major stream:** Stops travel; indicates direction (by flow direction)
- **Impassable terrain (cliff, ravine):** Stops travel; forces direction correction
- **Elevation change (significant hill):** Forces awareness of location

### Terrain Map Memory (Without Written Map)

If no map is available, commit terrain to memory verbally/narratively:

**Describe route step-by-step:**
1. "Start at white pine, head toward ridge for 2 km"
2. "Cross stream at rocky ford (stream running north-south)"
3. "Climb to ridge top (should see radio tower to left/west)"
4. "Follow ridge southeast for 3 km"
5. "Descend into valley toward farmhouse"

**Repeat verbally** 2-3 times to commit to memory. Describe landmarks, directions, approximate distances.

**In the field:** Review sequence every 2-3 landmarks to stay oriented within the narrative route.

## Escape Route Planning & Back-Bearing

### Deliberate Offset Strategy

When traveling an unknown distance in potentially hostile terrain, use **deliberate offset** to ensure you don't overshoot a target:

**Concept:** Aim deliberately left or right of actual target; when you hit a catching feature, you know which direction to turn.

**Example:**
- Target: Settlement directly north, approximately 10 km away
- Plan: Aim 15 degrees west of north (aim for point west of settlement)
- Route: Travel with 15-degree west offset for full distance
- Expected outcome: Hit a north-south road or river that is west of settlement
- Action: Turn right (east) and follow road/river to find settlement

**Advantages:**
- Prevents overshooting (disastrous in survival scenarios)
- Provides clear catching feature (road, river) for final navigation
- Reduces final search area significantly

**When to use:**
- Approaching urban areas (want to find perimeter, not get lost in middle)
- Navigating to linear feature (road, river) that is difficult to hit exactly
- When final approach in low-visibility conditions

### Back-Bearing Without Compass

If you need to return to a previous location but lack a compass:

**Method 1: Reverse pace-count**
1. Count paces to destination point
2. Turn 180 degrees (face opposite direction from arrival)
3. Count return paces (same number should bring you back)
4. Adjustment: Add 10-15% more paces if terrain is more difficult

**Accuracy:** ±10-20 meters for distances under 1 km (manageable for camp recovery)

**Method 2: Terrain-based return**
1. Memorize 2-3 prominent terrain features passed on outbound route
2. Return route: Reverse sequence of features (last feature becomes first on return)
3. Example outbound: "Passed white rock, then stream, then ridge"
   Return: "Follow ridge back, cross stream, pass white rock"

**Accuracy:** ±50-100 meters depending on feature recognition (adequate for finding camp)

**Method 3: Handrail feature**
1. During outbound travel, note consistent terrain feature parallel to your route (ridge, valley, stream)
2. Return route: Use same feature as handrail back
3. Example: If you traveled north with a river on your right (east), follow that same river on your left (west) going south

**Accuracy:** ±5-15 meters if handrail is close enough to see; much more reliable than other back-bearing methods

## Handrail & Catching Features

### Handrail Features (Linear Navigation Aids)

**Definition:** A linear terrain feature that runs parallel to your travel direction, keeping you on course.

**Examples:**
- Stream or river flowing in your direction of travel
- Ridge or valley edge
- Road or trail
- Power line or utility corridor
- Forest edge or vegetation boundary

**Usage:**
1. Identify handrail feature before starting route
2. Travel with feature on consistent side (e.g., river on your left)
3. Check frequently that feature is still adjacent
4. If feature crosses your path, reassess direction

**Advantage:** Reduces navigation workload; you only need to go forward; handrail keeps you aligned.

**Limitation:** Handrail may not exist in your direction of travel; must plan route through terrain with suitable handrail.

### Catching Features (Stopping Points)

**Definition:** A linear feature that crosses your expected travel direction; tells you "stop here, you've gone too far."

**Examples:**
- Major road
- River or large stream
- Cliff or escarpment
- Dense vegetation barrier
- Urban area or settlement

**Usage:**
1. Identify catching feature before starting route
2. Aim deliberately toward one side of target (deliberate offset)
3. When you reach catching feature, stop
4. Determine which side you are on (left or right of target)
5. Travel along feature to find target

**Example:**
- Target: Camp on north bank of river
- Plan: Aim 20° west of north, travel until hitting river
- Execution: Hit river after 8 km of travel
- Resolution: Turn right (east) along river until finding camp

**Advantage:** Ensures you don't miss destination in the wilderness; catching feature is impossible to miss.

## Distance Estimation at Different Paces

### Rapid Movement (Running, Crisis Evacuation)

- Normal running pace: 2-3 meters per step
- Pace counting less reliable (breathing, concentration affected)
- Alternative: Count time (10 seconds = ~30-50 meters for typical adult running)
- Accuracy: ±30% (poor, but useful for rapid evacuation)

### Slow Movement (Dense Forest, Night, Hostile Territory)

- Careful walking pace: 0.5-1 meter per step
- Pace counting more reliable (concentration maintained)
- Count every single step, not double-steps
- Accuracy: ±10% (good for stealth movement)

### Exhausted/Injured Movement

- Severely reduced pace: 0.3-0.5 meters per step
- Frequent rest breaks (10-15 minutes per hour)
- Pace counting essential (only way to track distance without landmarks)
- Accuracy: ±15-20% (challenging due to inconsistent stride)

## Route Planning Without Map

### Mental Route Construction

**Step 1: Establish reference points**
- Starting location
- Destination location
- Known locations between (settlements, natural features)

**Step 2: Identify terrain between points**
- Elevation changes (uphill means longer distance, slower movement)
- Obstacle terrain (forest, swamp, mountains)
- Linear features (roads, rivers) that can serve as handrails

**Step 3: Estimate time**
- Flat terrain: 4-5 km per hour walking
- Uphill: 2-3 km per hour
- Difficult terrain (forest, swamp): 2-3 km per hour
- Very difficult terrain: 1-2 km per hour
- Night or low-visibility: 2-3 km per hour

**Step 4: Plan for contingency**
- Identify alternative routes (if primary is blocked)
- Identify sheltering locations (if travel must be interrupted)
- Identify water sources (critical for survival)
- Identify danger zones (active conflict areas, wildlife, environmental hazards)

**Step 5: Establish checkpoints**
- Waypoints every 5-10 km where direction or strategy changes
- Notable landmarks for verification of progress
- Prepared contingency decisions at each checkpoint (continue, shelter, return?)

:::tip
Discuss route with group before departure. Different people may suggest hazards or landmarks you missed. Shared mental map increases safety and group confidence.
:::

## Group Navigation Without Compass

### Maintaining Group Cohesion While Dead Reckoning

**Formation:**
- Single file (when space is limited or stealth is needed)
- Loose group (when space allows and mutual visibility is important)
- Pair system (when high danger; pairs maintain contact)

**Communication:**
- Establish hand signals (point ahead for direction, hand up for stop, etc.)
- Noise discipline (whisper, no unnecessary sound)
- Regular check-in (every 10 minutes: "Everyone here? Everyone okay?")

**Pace maintenance:**
- Leader sets pace; group follows (no one ahead, no one drastically behind)
- Slower members move toward front of formation (to set sustainable pace)
- Rest breaks every 1-2 hours (essential for group cohesion and morale)

### Preventing Separation

**If someone becomes separated:**
1. Halt immediately (don't keep walking away)
2. Establish current location (note landmarks, terrain)
3. Send 2-person search team back along route (other pairs hold position)
4. Search team calls periodically (if noise discipline allows); looks for separated person
5. If not found after 30 minutes: Establish temporary camp, plan next-day search or return to known meeting point

**Prevention—establish meeting points:**
- Before starting journey: "If we get separated, meet at the large oak tree" (or other specific landmark)
- Check understanding verbally before travel
- Ensure all members know meeting point description

## Challenges & Troubleshooting

### Disorientation Recovery

**If you become disoriented (uncertain of location/direction):**

1. **STOP immediately.** Do not continue walking in wrong direction.
2. **Assess pace count.** Are you certain of how far you've traveled? Verify by reviewing landmarks.
3. **Check direction.** Use any available method (sun, wind, terrain, natural indicators) to verify compass direction.
4. **Review route.** Can you backtrack to last known location? Use back-bearing or return along terrain features.
5. **If completely lost:** Establish temporary shelter; wait for improved visibility (dawn, clearing weather); reassess with fresh mind.

:::warning
Lost people often become panicked and make worse decisions (rushing, ignoring landmarks, going in circles). Taking time to sit, calm down, and think clearly is often the best survival strategy.
:::

### Dead Reckoning Error Accumulation

**Over 5+ km, errors compound:**
- ±5 degrees direction error per km = ±87 meters lateral displacement per 1 km
- ±10% distance error per 5 km = ±500 meters total distance error for 5 km

**Total potential error for 5 km:** ±500-1000 meters (requires catching feature or handrail to recover)

**Mitigation:**
- Use terrain association (landmarks) to verify progress every 2-3 km
- Cross-check direction with multiple natural methods frequently
- Plan routes with catching features to prevent major overshooting
- Accept higher uncertainty for longer distances; plan accordingly (larger search area, establishing meeting points with wider margins)
