## Overview

Survival is not primarily a physical challenge—it is psychological. Groups fail not because of starvation or cold, but because social structures collapse, communication breaks down, and conflicts escalate unchecked. This guide addresses the predictable psychological patterns that emerge in survival groups and provides practical protocols for maintaining cohesion.

:::warning
High-stress environments amplify personality conflicts and increase irrational decision-making. Groups without conscious protocols for decision-making and conflict resolution frequently fragment within weeks, reducing overall survival capability.
:::

## Group Dynamics Across Crisis Phases

### Phase 1: Acute Crisis (Days 1-7)

**Psychological state:**
- Heightened adrenaline; most individuals function normally or above baseline
- Denial is common; many believe "this will be resolved in a few days"
- Roles often emerge rapidly (natural leaders, helpers, skeptics)
- Temporary unity driven by immediate threat

**Group characteristics:**
- High cohesion but unstable
- Decision-making rapid and often unilateral (no time for consensus)
- Few conflicts (adrenaline suppresses petty disputes)
- High morale and sense of purpose

**Manager focus:**
- Establish basic authority structure
- Create daily briefing routines
- Ensure basic needs are met (water, shelter, some food)
- Avoid making permanent decisions; frame everything as "temporary measures"

### Phase 2: Establishing Routines (Weeks 2-4)

**Psychological state:**
- Adrenaline crashes; fatigue becomes real
- People begin processing loss and uncertainty
- Doubt emerges ("will this actually resolve?")
- Personality conflicts suppressed in Phase 1 now surface

**Group characteristics:**
- Natural hierarchy consolidates (leaders become clearer)
- Cliques form (natural allies group together)
- First serious conflicts emerge over resource allocation or decision-making
- Morale drops as "new normal" becomes apparent
- Some depression and withdrawal common

**Manager focus:**
- Formalize decision-making and authority structure
- Address first conflicts directly (prevents escalation)
- Establish clear roles and responsibilities
- Create communication channels (regular meetings, feedback mechanisms)
- Monitor for depression; engage withdrawn individuals

### Phase 3: Long-Term Adaptation (Months 2-6)

**Psychological state:**
- Grief becomes prominent (losses from collapse, possibly group members)
- Identity shifts from "surviving collapse" to "living in new reality"
- Some individuals thrive; others become listless
- Boredom and routine can be as challenging as early crisis

**Group characteristics:**
- Stable authority structure established
- Well-defined subgroups with specialized roles
- Conflicts endemic but manageable if protocols exist
- Some members may abandon group (if possible) seeking better conditions
- Institutional memory forms (traditions, inside jokes, shared narratives)

**Manager focus:**
- Maintain morale through meaningful work and small celebrations
- Continue grief processing (acknowledge losses)
- Adjust authority structure if not working (avoid permanent structural changes, but correct obvious failures)
- Build institutional knowledge and systems
- Begin planning for future, not just survival

## Authority Structure & Decision-Making

### Hierarchical Models

**Single leader model:**
- One person holds decision-making authority
- Fast decisions; clear chain of command
- Risk: Authoritarian drift, abuse of power, group fractures if leader becomes incapacitated
- Best for: Early crisis (first weeks), small groups (<10), or groups with strong trust in leader

**Council model:**
- 3-5 leaders share authority; decisions made by majority vote
- Slower decisions but more consensus
- Risk: Gridlock on important decisions; internal council conflicts visible to group
- Best for: Medium/larger groups, stable conditions, groups valuing equity

**Specialist delegation model:**
- Central authority delegates specific domains to specialists (water, food, security, health, etc.)
- Specialists make decisions within domain; major decisions go to central authority
- Fast, efficient decisions; clear accountability
- Risk: Domain conflicts (e.g., water manager wants storage investment, security wants walls)
- Best for: Stable long-term communities, specialized knowledge required

**Rotating leadership model:**
- Leadership rotates weekly or monthly; all members share authority
- High equity and engagement
- Risk: Inconsistent decisions, learning curve for each new leader, group instability
- Best for: Small, highly-cohesive groups; not recommended for larger groups or crisis phases

:::info-box
**Recommendation:** Begin with clear single or council leadership during acute crisis. Transition to specialist delegation or shared authority as conditions stabilize and group size grows.
:::

### Formal Decision-Making Process

**Establish at first group meeting:**

1. **Decision type classification:**
   - **Urgent (life/death, immediate threat):** Leader decides unilaterally, reports afterward
   - **Important (major resource allocation, group safety policy):** Discussion, then vote or leader decision with feedback
   - **Routine (daily tasks, schedule):** Specialist decides, reports to group if needed

2. **Discussion format:**
   - Time limit (30 minutes for discussion, prevents endless rehashing)
   - Everyone speaks once before anyone speaks twice (ensures hearing all voices)
   - Leader summarizes consensus before vote

3. **Vote procedure (if used):**
   - Majority vote (>50%) for most decisions
   - Supermajority (2/3 or 3/4) for major changes
   - Unanimous consent required for admission of new members (prevents bad fits)

4. **Dissent protocol:**
   - Minority is heard; decisions explained to whole group
   - Dissent is allowed; active undermining of decision is not
   - Decisions can be revisited if conditions change or new information emerges

## Conflict Recognition & De-Escalation

### Conflict Escalation Patterns

**Stage 1: Disagreement (Manageable)**
- Two parties have different preferences or views
- Example: "I think we should plant crops; you think we should focus on hunting"
- Resolution: Respectful discussion, compromise, or agreement to disagree

**Stage 2: Conflict (Serious, Needs Intervention)**
- Parties assume bad intent ("You don't care about group survival")
- Emotional intensity rises; rational discussion becomes difficult
- Sides form (others take positions)
- Resolution: Neutral third party mediates; focus on shared values

**Stage 3: Crisis (Dangerous)**
- Threats emerge ("If you keep promoting that idea, I'll leave/hurt you")
- Authority is questioned or directly violated
- Group morale drops; some members become anxious or withdrawn
- Resolution: Leader intervention with clear authority; possible temporary separation of parties

**Stage 4: Collapse (Emergency)**
- Violence occurs or group fragments
- Authority structure fails; chaos spreads
- Some members flee; others become aggressive toward each other
- Resolution: Requires immediate external intervention or system reset (new authority, new structure)

### Early Intervention Protocol (Stage 1-2)

**Warning signs of escalating conflict:**
- People avoiding direct conversation (going through intermediaries)
- Group splitting into conversation camps (one person dominates discussion)
- Resource disputes becoming personal ("She's selfish")
- Increased alcohol/drug use in specific individuals (self-medicating stress)

**Intervention steps:**

1. **Private conversation with each party** (separately):
   - Listen without judgment
   - Identify underlying concern (often not the stated dispute)
   - "What would resolution look like for you?"
   - Do NOT take sides

2. **Mediated discussion** (if both agree):
   - Neutral third party (respected group member, not authority figure)
   - Each party states position and underlying need
   - Mediator identifies shared values or interests
   - Generate options that address both parties' needs

3. **Agreement and follow-up:**
   - Written or verbal agreement on solution
   - Check-in after 1 week (is solution holding?)
   - Publicize resolution (reduces rumors)

:::warning
Do NOT allow conflict to fester. Unaddressed Stage 1 conflicts almost always escalate to Stage 2 or 3. Early intervention prevents 80% of serious conflicts.
:::

### Crisis Intervention (Stage 3-4)

If conflict reaches Stage 3 or 4:

**Immediate response:**
1. Separate parties (physically, into different areas if necessary)
2. Authority figure addresses group (reasserts control, reduces anxiety)
3. Ban on further discussion (48-72 hour cooling-off period)
4. Individual check-ins to assess mental state

**Resolution approach:**
- Acknowledge that conflict is serious but manageable
- Restate group mission and shared values
- Offer mediated discussion after cooling-off period
- If parties cannot resolve: Establish separate roles/responsibilities (minimize contact)
- If violence occurred: Remove person temporarily; discuss reinstatement conditions

**Follow-up:**
- Process conflict with group (after resolution): "Here's what happened and how we fixed it"
- Adjust systems if conflict reveals structural problems (e.g., unclear authority, unfair resource allocation)

## Grief Management & Loss Processing

### Grief Stages in Survival Groups

Groups experience predictable grief patterns after member death:

**Denial (hours to days):**
- "This isn't really happening"
- Shock protects emotional system
- Individuals may act as if person is still present

**Anger (days to weeks):**
- "Why did this happen? It's not fair"
- Anger directed at deceased, at group, at circumstances
- Individuals may criticize deceased's choices or group decisions

**Bargaining (weeks):**
- "If only we had done X differently..."
- Regret and self-blame
- Individuals review what they could have changed

**Depression (weeks to months):**
- Acceptance that person is gone
- Sadness and reduced energy
- Withdrawal from group activities common

**Integration (months):**
- Memories of deceased become easier to recall
- Loss becomes part of group narrative
- Individual functions return to near-baseline

:::info-box
Grief is not linear. Individuals may cycle through stages, and different members progress at different rates. The goal is NOT to "move on" quickly, but to integrate loss while maintaining group function.
:::

### Group Grief Rituals

**Within 24 hours of death:**
- Gather group
- Allow time for people to share memories (10-20 minutes per person)
- Acknowledge contribution of deceased to group
- Plan simple ceremony or marker (can be minimal—important is acknowledgment)

**1-week memorial:**
- Formal gathering
- Share stories of deceased (funny, meaningful, ordinary moments)
- Discuss what group learned from their presence
- Optional: Create marker, monument, or dedicatory action in their name

**Monthly remembrance:**
- Brief acknowledgment in group meeting
- Name spoken aloud (prevents erasure)
- Share how the loss changed group or individual

**Integration into group narrative:**
- Reference deceased's ideas in discussions ("Remember when Jane suggested...?")
- Mention them in casual conversations (normalizes their memory)
- Use their methods or tools in ongoing work ("She always did it this way...")

### Children Witnessing Death

**Immediate response:**
- Involve child in ritual (if they wish; never force)
- Simple, honest explanation (age-appropriate)
- Reassurance that death is part of life; their own mortality is not immediate
- Avoid euphemisms ("passed" vs. "died"); use direct language

**Follow-up:**
- Check in regularly on emotional state
- Allow questions; answer honestly
- Watch for behavioral changes (aggression, withdrawal, regression)
- Assign trusted adult for additional support if child shows distress

## Role Assignment & Specialization

### Natural Role Emergence

In early crisis, roles often emerge organically:
- **Doers:** Task-focused, action-oriented, implement decisions
- **Talkers:** Communication-focused, handle group discussion, mediation
- **Thinkers:** Planning-focused, strategic, anticipate problems
- **Nurturers:** Care-focused, support individuals, maintain morale

Most people have primary and secondary roles. Balanced groups have all four roles represented.

### Deliberate Role Assignment

**After first week, formalize roles:**

1. **Identify person's skills and preferences**
2. **Assign primary responsibility** (one major area)
3. **Assign secondary responsibility** (backup or complementary)
4. **Create role description** (10-point checklist of responsibilities)
5. **Set performance expectations** (what "success" looks like)

**Critical specialized roles (larger groups):**
- **Water manager:** Collects, stores, monitors water quality, oversees rationing
- **Food/agriculture:** Grows/gathers food, manages storage, plans meals
- **Security:** Assesses threats, manages watches, handles perimeter
- **Health:** Tends to sick/injured, maintains medical supplies
- **Tools/resources:** Maintains tools, equipment, salvage items, coordinates repairs
- **Education/morale:** Teaches children, organizes group events, maintains institutional knowledge

**Rotation policy:**
- Annual rotation recommended (prevents stagnation, builds broad skills)
- Some roles (health, security) require training before rotation
- Allow individuals to stay in role if they excel and wish to remain

### Role Conflict & Adjustment

If role assignment is failing:

**Signs of failure:**
- Individual repeatedly not completing role responsibilities
- Other group members complaining about role performance
- Role-holder expressing stress or inadequacy
- Clear mismatch between person's skills and role demands

**Intervention:**
1. Private discussion with role-holder (identify barriers)
2. Adjust expectations or role scope (maybe role is too large)
3. Provide training or support if skill is lacking
4. If fundamentally wrong fit: Reassign to better-suited role

Do NOT publicly humiliate person; reframe as "realigning for better group success."

## Morale Maintenance

### Morale Drivers (High Priority)

**Meaningful work:**
- People need sense of purpose beyond survival
- Assign tasks that connect to future (building, crafting, teaching)
- Celebrate completed projects visibly

**Predictability and routine:**
- Regular meal times, sleep times, work times
- Daily briefing (15 minutes, same time each day) builds anticipation and structure
- Weekly group meeting for decisions and celebrations

**Small celebrations:**
- Birthdays (even if minimal, acknowledge the day)
- Harvests or successful hunts (communal meal, recognition)
- Holidays (maintain traditions or create new ones)
- Personal achievements (successful craft, learned skill, solved problem)

**Autonomy and choice:**
- Offer choices where possible ("Would you prefer to work on shelter or water collection today?")
- Allow personal decision-making in non-critical areas
- Respect individual space and time

**Fairness in resource distribution:**
- If rationing is necessary, be transparent about why and when
- Ensure allocation is consistent and equal
- Acknowledge that fairness sometimes requires unequal distribution (pregnant, injured, nursing)

### Morale Red Flags

**Withdrawal:**
- Individual stops participating in group activities
- Contributes minimally to assigned tasks
- Stops eating with group or engaging in conversation

**Hopelessness:**
- Statements like "We won't survive" or "It doesn't matter what I do"
- Apathy about future planning
- Neglecting self-care

**Anger/irritability:**
- Person becomes short-tempered with others
- Escalates minor disputes into major conflicts
- Criticizes group decisions frequently

**Substance use:**
- Increased alcohol or drug use (if available)
- Using substances to escape/self-medicate

**Response protocol:**
- Private check-in conversation
- Listen for underlying causes (grief, exhaustion, medical issue, relationship conflict)
- Offer concrete support (additional rest, role change, mediation)
- Connect with trusted group member for ongoing support (buddy system)

## Sleep Deprivation & Fatigue Management

### Sleep Deprivation Effects

**After 24 hours without sleep:**
- Decision-making quality drops 20%
- Reaction time slows
- Irritability increases
- Appearance of intoxication (slurred speech, lack of coordination)

**After 48 hours:**
- Cognitive impairment severe (decisions should not be made)
- Hallucinations possible
- Physical coordination severely compromised
- Individual is danger to themselves and others

**After 72+ hours:**
- Severe cognitive dysfunction
- High accident/injury risk
- Health consequences (immune suppression, blood pressure elevation)

### Watch Schedule Design

For security watches or critical night tasks:

**Two-person watch (most common):**
- 2 hours awake, 6 hours off-duty (allows 4 hours sleep + 2 hours buffer)
- Rotate partners every 3-4 nights (prevents relationship strain)
- Ensure watches are during least-dangerous times (early night, before dawn)

**Three-person watch:**
- 2 hours on, 4 hours off-duty
- Allows more sleep time
- More personnel capacity

**Never use:**
- Watches longer than 3 hours (attention drops sharply)
- More than 2 consecutive nights on watches (debt accumulates)
- Watches during hours of natural sleep (unless emergency)

### Sleep Recovery Protocol

If someone has been severely sleep-deprived:
- Day 1: Minimize responsibility, full sleep opportunity
- Day 2: Light duties, monitor for safety
- Day 3: Return to normal with observation

## Communication Protocols

### Daily Briefing (15 minutes)

**Time:** Same time each day (breakfast or morning)
**Format:**
1. Weather forecast (if possible)
2. Day's work assignments
3. Resource status (food, water, fuel)
4. Health updates (anyone sick, injured, needing support)
5. Group announcements (birthdays, celebrations, reminders)
6. Open questions (15 minutes for group to raise concerns)

**Why:** Reduces rumors, builds routine, allows early conflict identification

### Weekly Meeting (1 hour)

**Time:** Same day and time (e.g., Sunday evening)
**Format:**
1. Work review (accomplishments, challenges from past week)
2. Resource assessment (current inventory, forecasts)
3. Decisions needed (raised by any group member)
4. Celebration (acknowledge accomplishments or personal wins)
5. Planning (next week's priorities)

**Why:** Ensures all voices heard, prevents small issues from festering, maintains transparency

### Private Consultation (As Needed)

- **Authority figure available:** 30 minutes per day for individual concerns
- **Scheduled appointments:** Reduces feeling of being too busy for people
- **Confidentiality:** What is said is kept private unless it affects group safety

## Children in Survival Groups

### Age-Appropriate Roles

**Ages 4-7:**
- Simple, supervised tasks (gathering small items, sorting)
- Play integrated with work (learning through play)
- Primary need: Safety, routine, parental closeness

**Ages 8-12:**
- Real responsibility (gathering water, weeding, watching younger children)
- Skill teaching (cooking, tool use, basic first aid)
- Primary need: Competence, peer group, structure

**Ages 13-18:**
- Adult-equivalent responsibilities (security, food production, skilled work)
- Leadership opportunity (teaching younger kids, mentoring)
- Primary need: Autonomy, sense of purpose, peer acceptance

### Child Morale Maintenance

**Maintain education:**
- Daily 1-2 hours learning (literacy, math, practical skills)
- Keeps normalcy, prepares for future
- Critical for long-term community stability

**Maintain play:**
- Unstructured play time (critical for psychological health)
- Games and sports (group bonding, physical activity)
- Creative activities (art, music, storytelling)

**Maintain family structure:**
- Protect parent-child time
- Family decisions involve children (age-appropriately)
- Privacy for families

## Integration of Strangers

### Vetting & Decision Process

**Before admitting new member:**
1. **Extended observation** (1-2 weeks outside/near camp)
2. **Formal interview** by authority figure (assess skills, intentions, compatibility)
3. **Group vote** (unanimous consent requirement recommended)
4. **Trial period** (2-4 weeks; revocable membership)

**Assessment questions:**
- What skills do you bring?
- Why do you want to join us?
- What are you expecting from the group?
- Are you willing to accept our authority structure and decisions?

### Integration Protocol

**Week 1:**
- Assign buddy (experienced member) for orientation
- Clear explanation of rules, responsibilities, expectations
- Supervised in all activities

**Week 2-3:**
- Assigned to role/work area
- Integrated into daily meetings and group activities
- Monitored for conflict with existing members

**Week 4:**
- Full evaluation: Skills assessment, compatibility, performance
- Group decision: Accept permanently, extend trial, or decline

### Special Considerations

**Family groups:**
- Accept as unit (rarely beneficial to separate)
- Faster integration if family is self-sufficient

**Diverse backgrounds:**
- Language barriers: Assign multi-lingual buddy, patience required
- Cultural differences: Acknowledge explicitly, adapt rules where possible
- Trust issues: Build slowly; don't force integration
