## Overview

Healthcare without electricity, resupply, or expert physicians means **knowing what you *can* handle and what you cannot**. This guide is a systematic assessment framework—not a training program, but a diagnostic tool to measure your community's medical preparedness gap.

The bridge connects **diagnostic capability** (can you identify what's wrong?) → **treatment capacity** (can you actually help?) → **supply sufficiency** (do you have enough?) → **surgical readiness** (can you handle trauma?) → **infection control** (can you prevent cascade failure?).

## What You Need Before Starting

- Access to at least one trained first-aider or EMT in your community
- Reference: anatomy-fundamentals, infection-control-manual, herbal-medicine, wound-treatment
- Inventory of all existing medical supplies
- List of community members with medical training, nursing experience, or relevant knowledge
- Access to reference books (anatomy, drug interactions, herbal properties)—NOT reliant on internet
- A quiet space to conduct honest assessment (this often reveals uncomfortable gaps)

## Layer 1: Diagnostic Capability—Can You Identify What's Wrong?

Medicine without diagnosis is guessing. Your first layer of resilience is *knowing what you're dealing with*.

**Diagnostic tools you should have (non-instrument-dependent):**

| Tool | Cost | Utility | Limitation |
|------|------|---------|-----------|
| **Visual inspection** | Free | Rashes, wounds, paleness, jaundice | Misses internal pathology |
| **Palpation** | Free | Organ swelling, tenderness, bumps | Requires training to interpret |
| **Auscultation** (stethoscope) | $15–50 | Lung/heart sounds, GI motility | Requires training, needs quiet |
| **Thermometer** (non-electric) | $2–10 | Fever detection | Only shows temperature, not cause |
| **Urine dipstick** | $0.50/stick | UTI, glucose, protein, ketones, blood | Doesn't explain *why* |
| **Blood glucose meter** | $15–30 | Diabetes screening/management | Requires testing strips (limited shelf life) |
| **Otoscope/ophthalmoscope** | $30–150 | Ear/eye infection diagnosis | Requires training, fragile |
| **Peak flow meter** | $20–30 | Asthma/COPD monitoring | Only asthma/lung-specific |

**Diagnostic questions you *must* be able to answer:**

:::info-box
**Is this life-threatening right now?**
- Unconscious? No response to pain? Call emergency.
- Not breathing or severe difficulty? Call emergency.
- Severe bleeding you can't stop? Call emergency.
- Chest pain radiating to arm? Call emergency.

(If you truly cannot call emergency, you're in full-offline survival mode—see trauma-surgery guide.)
:::

**Is this an infection?**
- Signs: fever, pus, warmth, redness, swelling, foul odor
- Severity: localized vs. spreading vs. system-wide (sepsis signs: confusion, extreme chills, organ dysfunction)
- Action: can you clean and drain it? Do you have antibiotics?

**Is this dehydration?**
- Signs: dry mucous membranes, poor skin turgor, dark urine, weakness
- Severity: mild (thirst, dry mouth) vs. moderate (weakness, rapid heartbeat) vs. severe (confusion, collapsed veins)
- Action: can you provide safe drinking water + oral rehydration salts?

**Is this a chronic condition flare?**
- Signs: patient has known diabetes, asthma, heart disease, epilepsy—is it acting up?
- Severity: manageable with current medication vs. emergency-level
- Action: do you have their medications? Can you stabilize them?

**Is this poisoning or overdose?**
- Signs: unknown substance ingestion, overdose of known medication, environmental exposure
- Severity: asymptomatic vs. vomiting vs. unconscious vs. seizures
- Action: do you have charcoal, naloxone, or other antidotes? Can you support breathing?

---

**Diagnostic capability tier:**

**Tier 0 (no capability):** No equipment, no training. You guess and hope.

**Tier 1 (basic):** Stethoscope, thermometer, visual/palpation skills. You can identify fever, infection signs, gross internal bleeding.

**Tier 2 (functional):** Tier 1 + dipsticks, glucose meter, otoscope, peak flow. You can narrow down UTI, diabetes, ear infection, some lung issues.

**Tier 3 (advanced):** Tier 2 + access to reference library and trained diagnostician. You can rule in/out many conditions with reasonable accuracy.

:::warning
**Critical limitation:** Even Tier 3 cannot replace imaging (X-ray, ultrasound, CT). Broken bones, internal bleeding, pneumonia—you'll suspect but not definitively confirm.
:::

### Physical Exam Sequence for Untrained Personnel

If your community doesn't have a trained provider, this systematic head-to-toe approach helps untrained people conduct basic exams without missing obvious signs of trouble.

**Before you start:**
- Wash hands with soap and water (20 seconds minimum)
- Ask the patient where they hurt or feel worst first
- Keep them warm and comfortable
- Explain what you're doing before you touch them

**Head and face:**
- Look: asymmetry, facial drooping, rash on face?
- Feel: lumps, swelling, scalp tenderness
- Eyes: pupils equal and reactive to light? Yellowing of whites (jaundice)?
- Ears: discharge (pus, blood, CSF)? Redness? Ask if hearing is normal
- Nose: clear nasal passages or blocked? Discharge color?
- Mouth: white patches (thrush)? Gums red/swollen? Teeth loose? Foul breath?
- Throat: use flashlight + tongue depressor; red, swollen, pus spots?

**Neck:**
- Look for swelling, rash
- Feel gently for lymph nodes (bumps on sides, under jaw)
- Can they turn head side-to-side, up-and-down? (stiff neck = bad sign)

**Chest and lungs:**
- Look: ribs visible? Asymmetry? Labored breathing?
- Listen (with stethoscope if available): crackles, wheezes, clear air entry?
- If no stethoscope: listen with ear to chest—any rattling, wheezing, silence?
- Cough productive (bringing up sputum)? What color is it?

**Heart:**
- Listen (stethoscope) at left side under armpit: regular rhythm? Racing? Skipping?
- Take pulse at wrist: count for 60 seconds or 15 seconds × 4. Normal = 60–100 bpm. Over 100 = elevated (infection, pain, dehydration, fear)

**Abdomen:**
- Ask: where does it hurt?
- Visual: distension, bruising, surgical scars
- Gently press each quadrant; ask if it hurts
- Rebound tenderness: press gently then release quickly—does it hurt MORE on release? (suggests peritonitis = serious)
- Feel for lumps, hard areas
- Listen for gut sounds (gurgling, silence?)

**Extremities:**
- Look for swelling, redness, asymmetry
- Feel for warmth, swelling, tenderness
- Check capillary refill: press fingernail bed, release—does color return in under 2 seconds? (slow = bad circulation)
- Check strength: ask them to squeeze your hands, push/pull against your resistance
- Skin: rashes, wounds, bruises? Pale or flushed?

**Skin overall:**
- Color: pale, flushed, dusky, yellow?
- Turgor: pinch skin on forearm; does it snap back or stay folded? (slow = dehydration)
- Rashes: where, what does it look like, itchy or painful?
- Wounds: clean or infected (pus, spreading redness, foul smell)?

**Neurological (if you suspect altered mental status):**
- Alert and oriented? Know name, date, location?
- Speech clear, slurred, confused?
- Can they follow commands? ("Squeeze my hand," "Look up")
- Any tremors, seizure-like activity?

**Temperature and vital signs (if tools available):**
- Oral temperature (most accurate)
- Pulse (wrist, 60 sec)
- Breathing rate (count chest rises for 60 sec; normal = 12–20/min)
- Blood pressure (if cuff available; normal = ~120/80)
- Urine: color, smell, frequency, urgency?

:::tip
**Document what you find.** Even if you can't interpret it perfectly, write down temperatures, where it hurts, what you see and feel. If a trained person arrives later, they need this baseline.
:::

## Layer 2: Treatment Capacity—What Can You Actually Fix?

Knowing what's wrong is step one. Fixing it is step two—and most communities are limited here.

**Conditions most communities *can* treat offline:**

- Acute wounds (cuts, scrapes, lacerations) — clean, suture/glue if needed
- Minor infections (surface wounds, simple UTIs) — topical treatment, herbal/antibiotic if available
- Dehydration — oral rehydration salts + clean water
- Pain (various causes) — aspirin, ibuprofen, herbal analgesics, heat/ice
- Diarrhea — electrolyte replacement, antimotility agents if not dysentery
- Fever (non-serious) — hydration, antipyretics, addressing cause if possible
- Minor fractures/sprains — immobilization, ice, elevation, pain management
- Allergic reactions (non-anaphylaxis) — antihistamines, rest, monitoring
- Anxiety, insomnia — herbal support, talk therapy, environmental control

**Conditions most communities *cannot* treat offline (or only barely):**

- Severe internal bleeding — needs surgery + transfusion
- Pneumonia (bacterial) — needs IV antibiotics or high-dose oral + oxygen support
- Sepsis — needs IV fluids, broad-spectrum antibiotics, supportive care
- Acute appendicitis — needs surgery; without it, rupture and death
- Compound fractures (bone through skin) — needs surgery + antibiotics
- Severe allergic reaction (anaphylaxis) — needs epinephrine immediately; without it, airway closes
- Myocardial infarction (heart attack) — needs specialized care + drugs; offline = likely fatal
- Diabetic ketoacidosis — needs insulin + IV therapy
- Uncontrolled hemorrhage — needs blood products + surgery
- Severe infections beyond surface wound — needs IV antibiotics, possibly surgery

:::tip
**Honest assessment:** Most offline communities will handle minor-to-moderate issues and provide comfort care for serious conditions they cannot cure. Accept this limit; it informs everything else.
:::

## Layer 3: Supply Sufficiency—Do You Have Enough?

Even if you can treat something, you need supplies. Run this inventory against your diagnostic/treatment capability.

**Core medical kit (assuming 50-person community, 1 year):**

| Category | Items | Annual Qty | Cost |
|----------|-------|-----------|------|
| **Wound care** | Sterile gauze, tape, elastic wrap, antibiotic ointment | 500 units | $50 |
| **Sutures/closure** | Suture material, sterile needles, steri-strips | 100 packs | $75 |
| **Pain/fever** | Aspirin, ibuprofen, acetaminophen | 10,000+ tablets | $30 |
| **Diarrhea** | ORS (oral rehydration salts), bismuth subsalicylate | 200 packets | $20 |
| **Infection** | Broad-spec antibiotics, antifungals | *limited; see below* | $100+ |
| **Allergies** | Antihistamines, hydrocortisone cream | 2,000 tablets | $25 |
| **Respiratory** | Inhalers (if people need them), expectorant | 100+ doses | $60 |
| **GI** | Antacids, anti-nausea, fiber | 1,000 tablets | $20 |
| **Topicals** | Burn cream, insect repellent, hemorrhoid cream | 50+ oz | $30 |
| **Environmental** | Thermometers, blood pressure cuff, stethoscope | 5–10 sets | $100 |
| **Preservation** | Rubbing alcohol, hydrogen peroxide, salt (for preservation) | 50+ liters | $30 |

**Total: ~$540 for basic kit serving 50 people, 1 year**

**Medication warning: Antibiotics**

Antibiotics are the hardest to obtain offline. Options:

1. **Prescription stockpile** — If your community has people on chronic antibiotics, you might stockpile spares (with physician approval). Limited.
2. **Fish/veterinary antibiotics** — Online-legal for aquaculture, used off-label. Quality/potency varies. Controversial ethically but practical.
3. **Herbal antimicrobials** — Garlic, honey, thyme, elderberry, colloidal silver. Less predictable than drugs but complementary.
4. **Home fermentation** — Some molds produce penicillin-like compounds (moldy cheese corners, etc.). Very unreliable.

:::warning
**Realistic constraint:** Offline, serious bacterial infections you cannot treat with available antibiotics may be fatal. This is a major gap—plan for it in your resilience strategy (prevention focus, infection control emphasis).
:::

### Supply Degradation Timeline: Medication Shelf Life & Rotation

Most medications degrade in potency over time, especially in heat and humidity. This table shows realistic shelf lives for common stockpile items and when you need to start rotation. Store in cool, dry, dark places to maximize lifespan.

| Medication | Form | Shelf Life (unopened) | Degradation Pattern | Rotation Action |
|-----------|------|---------------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **Aspirin** | Tablet | 5–10 years | Slow; may smell vinegary when expired | Replace at 8 years |
| **Ibuprofen** | Tablet | 3–5 years | Moderate; potency drops ~10%/year after 3 yr | Replace at 5 years |
| **Acetaminophen** | Tablet | 3–5 years | Similar to ibuprofen | Replace at 5 years |
| **Antihistamines** (diphenhydramine) | Tablet | 3–5 years | Gradual potency loss | Replace at 5 years |
| **Antacids** (calcium carbonate) | Tablet | Indefinite | Stable if dry | Check and replace if wet/caked |
| **Hydrocortisone cream** | Topical | 2–3 years | Potency loss in heat | Replace at 2 years |
| **Antibiotic ointment** (neomycin) | Topical | 2–3 years | Oil separation, potency drop | Replace at 2 years or if liquid |
| **Antibiotics (amoxicillin)** | Capsule/powder | 1–3 years | Rapid degradation; don't use if changed color/smell | Replace yearly; discard if any discoloration |
| **Oral rehydration salts (ORS)** | Powder packet | 3–5 years (unopened) | Hygroscopic; absorbs moisture | Store with desiccant packets; replace at 3 years |
| **Bismuth subsalicylate** | Tablet | 3–5 years | Slow potency loss | Replace at 5 years |
| **Loperamide** | Tablet | 3–5 years | Moderate stability | Replace at 5 years |
| **Cough syrup** | Liquid | 2–3 years | Separation, mold risk | Keep sealed; discard if moldy or separated |
| **Insulin** (if community diabetic) | Injectable | 1 year (room temp) | Rapid loss of potency above 30°C | Requires rotation, cool storage critical |
| **Epinephrine auto-injector** | Injectable | 1–2 years | Loses potency; discoloration = unusable | Check expiration monthly; replace annually |
| **Topical burn cream** | Topical | 2–3 years | Separates in heat | Replace at 2 years; store cool |
| **Hydrogen peroxide** | Liquid (3%) | 1–2 years (opened) | Volatile; loses effectiveness when exposed to light | Keep dark, sealed; discard if flat |
| **Isopropyl alcohol** (70%) | Liquid | Indefinite | Very stable if sealed | Check seal; replace if evaporated |

**Rotation protocol:**
- Label all supplies with purchase date and expiration estimate
- Monthly: check insulin, epinephrine auto-injectors, liquid medications
- Quarterly: spot-check sealed packages for damage, moisture
- Annually: replace antibiotics, ORS, hydrocortisone; rotate ibuprofen/aspirin stock
- Every 2 years: overhaul all topical creams; discard discolored items
- Create a **medication log:** list what you have, when acquired, when to replace

**Storage optimization:**
- Cool (under 25°C / 77°F preferred)
- Dry (silica gel packets in sealed containers)
- Dark (opaque containers, away from windows)
- Sealed (remove from original packaging if it degrades, but keep labels)
- Separate humidity-sensitive items (antibiotics, ORS) from topicals

:::info-box
**Failing medications are worse than none:** Expired antibiotics may not kill infection (selecting resistant bacteria). Expired epinephrine won't reverse anaphylaxis. Use fresh supplies when lives depend on efficacy.
:::

## Layer 4: Surgical Readiness—Can You Handle Trauma?

Surgery in a home setting with no anesthesia, sterility, or blood supply is horrifying. But it happens in true collapse scenarios.

**Tier 0 (none):** No surgical training, no instruments, no hope.

**Tier 1 (emergency only):** One trained person, basic instruments (scalpel, forceps, scissors, clamps), local anesthetic. Can stop bleeding, remove obviously embedded objects, clean severe wounds. Cannot handle internal surgery safely.

**Tier 2 (field medicine):** Tier 1 + suture sets, bone-setting supplies, chest decompression needle. Can stabilize trauma and reduce infection risk.

**Tier 3 (minimal surgical):** Tier 2 + someone with actual surgical training (vet, retired surgeon), surgical gloves, drapes, sterilization (pressure cooker autoclave). Can attempt appendectomy, C-section under extreme duress. Still high mortality.

:::info-box
**Reality check:** Military surgeons with perfect conditions lose patients. You in a kitchen without blood replacement will lose more. Document who *wants* this attempt and who doesn't—informed consent is critical.
:::

**Essential surgical items (if pursuing Tier 2+):**

- Sterile scalpels, forceps, scissors, clamps (order online now)
- Suture material (various gauges, absorbable + non-absorbable)
- Local anesthetic (procaine, lidocaine—if you can source)
- Sterile gloves, gowns, drapes
- Disinfectants (isopropyl alcohol, hydrogen peroxide, iodine)
- Pressure cooker or boiling pot (sterilization)
- Clotting agents (gauze, hemostatic powder, tourniquets)
- Pain management (aspirin, herbal analgesics; no opioids assumed)

## Layer 5: Infection Control & Quarantine

Infectious disease scales—one infection becomes many. Your last layer of resilience is *stopping spread*.

**Infection control practices (implemented NOW):**

1. **Hand hygiene:** Soap + water (no sanitizer needed if soap is used correctly—20 seconds). Most critical single practice.
2. **Respiratory protection:** Cloth masks for respiratory illness; upgrade to paper masks if available. Keep separate from others.
3. **Waste disposal:** Contaminated items burned or buried downwind of water/crops.
4. **Water safety:** Boil, filter, or bleach-treat all water during outbreak. No exceptions.
5. **Food prep separation:** One person for sick, different surfaces/utensils for healthy food prep.

**Quarantine space:**

- Dedicate one building (or section of building) as quarantine
- Separate air if possible; accept higher risk if not
- Outside waste disposal
- Single caregiver if possible (reduces exposure)
- 2-week minimum isolation post-symptom resolution

**Diseases you will face (offline context):**

| Disease | R₀ (contagiousness) | Offline survival | Control strategy |
|---------|-------------------|------------------|------------------|
| **Flu** | 1–2 | High (mild–moderate) | Masks, isolation, time |
| **COVID-19** | 2–3 | Moderate (vulnerable die) | Same as flu; prioritize oxygen |
| **Measles** | 12–18 | Moderate (vaccine immunity matters) | Isolate, supportive care |
| **Dysentery/cholera** | Water-borne | Moderate (treatable with hydration) | Water safety priority #1 |
| **Tuberculosis** | Low, slow | Moderate (treatable if you catch early) | Isolation, nutrition, time |

:::warning
**Outbreak planning:** If your community reaches "everyone sick at once," non-emergency services stop. Plan for 1–2 weeks where essential care only (food, water, critical meds) happens. Non-essential people recover on their own.
:::

## Layer 6: Mental Health & Chronic Disease Management

Collapse scenarios produce two hidden medical crises: psychological breakdown and decompensation of chronic conditions. Many deaths offline aren't from acute infection—they're from diabetics without insulin, depressed people who stop eating, or uncontrolled seizures. Your sixth layer addresses these.

### Psychiatric First Aid Capacity

**Mental health conditions you will face:**

1. **Acute grief/loss** — death of family, loss of home, perceived end of world
2. **Anxiety attacks** — hyperventilation, chest pain, feeling of imminent death (often mistaken for heart attack)
3. **Depression** — withdrawal, anhedonia, suicidal ideation, poor self-care
4. **Panic disorder** — acute terror episodes triggered by minor stress
5. **PTSD** — intrusive memories, hypervigilance, avoidance of triggers
6. **Psychosis** — delusions, hallucinations (rare but devastating when it happens)
7. **Sleep disturbance** — insomnia from stress, hypervigilance, noise/environmental chaos

**What untrained people can do (Tier 1 - Basic Support):**

- **Listen without judgment.** Let people talk; don't minimize their fear.
- **Normalize reactions.** "It's normal to feel afraid right now. You're not crazy."
- **Maintain routine.** Keep meal times, work times, sleep times consistent. Chaos feeds anxiety.
- **Physical contact.** Hand-holding, shoulder touch, sitting close (when appropriate) calms the nervous system.
- **Breathing exercises.** Slow breathing (4-count in, 6-count out) interrupts panic spirals.
- **Distraction tasks.** Assign meaningful work (gardening, cooking, repairs). Idle minds spiral.
- **Reduce stimuli.** Dim lights, reduce noise, create quiet spaces during high-stress periods.
- **Herbal support.** Chamomile tea, valerian root, passionflower for anxiety; St. John's Wort for mild depression (if available).
- **Peer support groups.** Regular community gatherings where people can express fear/loss in presence of others.

**What requires trained mental health person (Tier 2 - Active Intervention):**

- **Suicide risk assessment and safety planning.** If someone is expressing suicidal thoughts, trained person identifies risk level and removes access to lethal means.
- **Medication management.** If community has someone on antidepressants, anti-anxiety meds, antipsychotics, trained person monitors dose, side effects, early warning signs of decompensation.
- **De-escalation of acute crises.** If someone becomes aggressive, delusional, or non-responsive to basic support, trained person uses verbal techniques to prevent violence.
- **Referral and documentation.** Even offline, mental health crises need basic records: who has persistent suicidal ideation, who's on meds, what triggered crisis.

**Psychiatric first aid toolkit:**

- Reference book on mental health (e.g., *The Noonday Demon* or basic psychiatric manual)
- Journal for tracking mental health concerns community-wide
- Herbal supplies (chamomile, valerian, passionflower)
- List of anyone taking psychiatric medications (dosage, indication)
- Quiet space for crisis conversations
- One trained facilitator (can be layperson with first-aid training)

:::warning
**Psychosis and severe mental illness:** If someone becomes acutely psychotic (hallucinating, delusional, non-responsive), offline care is supportive only—keep them safe, prevent harm, maintain basic function. No medication = slow resolution. Suicide risk during depressive crises is real; identify and monitor high-risk individuals.
:::

### Chronic Disease Management Assessment

**Critical chronic conditions in offline scenarios:**

**Diabetes (Type 1 & Type 2):**
- **Type 1 (insulin-dependent):** Without insulin, patient dies in weeks (diabetic ketoacidosis). This is non-negotiable.
  - Assessment: Do you have anyone Type 1 diabetic? How much insulin stockpiled? How is it stored (needs refrigeration)?
  - Offline action: Strict carb rationing, high-dose oral medications if available, hope for slow/no progression. Mortality high without insulin.
- **Type 2 (often medication-controlled):** May survive on oral meds (metformin, sulfonylureas) + diet.
  - Assessment: Oral medication stockpile? Can community enforce low-carb diet?

**Asthma/COPD:**
- **Inhalers critical.** Without bronchodilators, severe exacerbation = respiratory failure.
- Assessment: Which community members need inhalers? How many spares? Do you have backup oral medications (theophylline)?
- Offline action: Minimize triggers (smoke, dust, exertion), reserve inhalers for crises, consider herbal bronchodilators (ephedra if available, though restricted).

**Hypertension:**
- **Usually survives offline** if managed with diet (low sodium), stress reduction, herbal support (hawthorne berry, garlic).
- Assessment: Is anyone on critical blood pressure meds (ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers)? Can they function without them?
- Offline action: Stockpile common BP meds if possible; emphasize low-sodium food production.

**Cardiovascular disease (post-MI, stents, arrhythmias):**
- **High mortality without medications.** Aspirin may help, but nitroglycerin and other cardiac meds are critical.
- Assessment: Any community members with known CAD? Medication stockpile? Do they know their warning signs?
- Offline action: Stress reduction, medication rationing, prepare for likely death if severe exacerbation occurs.

**Epilepsy:**
- **Seizures controlled by anticonvulsants.** Without meds, status epilepticus (prolonged seizures) is fatal.
- Assessment: Any epileptic community members? Medication stockpile adequate? Do caregivers know seizure first aid (side positioning, airway, timing)?
- Offline action: Strict medication adherence, identify seizure triggers, ensure caregivers can handle breakthrough seizure.

**Thyroid disease:**
- **Hypothyroidism manageable** with levothyroxine stockpile; can be supported with iodine-rich diet if meds run out (slow compensation).
- **Hyperthyroidism harder.** Methimazole/PTU are hard to stockpile; herbal support (lemon balm, bugleweed) weak.
- Assessment: Who is hypothyroid? Levothyroxine stockpile adequate for 2+ years?

**Chronic pain conditions (arthritis, back pain, neuropathy):**
- **Usually survive but with reduced function.** Offline = herbal support + physical therapy.
- Assessment: Heavy reliance on opioids? If so, addiction risk during collapse; plan tapering + herbal alternatives (turmeric, ginger, willow bark).

### Medication Stockpiling for Chronic Conditions

**Tier 1 (basic):** 3-month supply of critical meds (insulin, anticonvulsants, inhalers)

**Tier 2 (moderate):** 6-month supply of all chronic meds for all community members

**Tier 3 (resilient):** 1-2 year supply of critical meds + documentation of how to taper/substitute if supply runs out

**Procurement strategy:**
1. Consult with each community member's physician (explain scenario); request larger prescriptions
2. Fill prescriptions early each month if insurance allows accumulation
3. Store excess in cool, dry place with desiccant packets
4. Maintain written log: person, condition, medication, dose, quantity on hand, expiration date
5. For oral medications, split into monthly containers in case of loss

:::info-box
**Insurance red flag:** Doctors may refuse to prescribe excess medication citing "drug-seeking behavior." Frame as disaster preparedness; if they refuse, consider finding sympathetic provider or switching to veterinary/online sources (higher risk but sometimes necessary).
:::

### Mental Health & Chronic Disease Tier Assessment

**Tier 0 (no capability):**
- No awareness of psychiatric needs or chronic disease burden in community
- No medication stockpiles
- No trained mental health support

**Tier 1 (basic):**
- Inventory of all chronic conditions and current medications
- 3+ month stockpile of critical meds (insulin, anticonvulsants, inhalers)
- One community member trained in basic mental health first aid
- Herbal supplies for anxiety/depression support
- Quiet space identified for mental health crises

**Tier 2 (moderate):**
- 6+ month stockpile of all chronic meds
- Written care plans for each person with chronic condition (warning signs, med adjustment, end-of-life wishes)
- Mental health support person + backup
- Herbal garden (chamomile, valerian, St. John's Wort, lemon balm)
- Documentation of psychiatric medications + baseline mental health status of community members

**Tier 3 (advanced):**
- 1+ year medication stockpile for all chronic conditions
- Licensed mental health provider on-site or on-call (even part-time)
- Mental health crisis protocol with designated quiet space, de-escalation training for all staff
- Peer support groups already meeting pre-collapse (habit formation helps)
- Taper plans for every psychiatric med (if supply runs out, how do you slowly discontinue?)

## Capability Gap Prioritization: Which Gaps Kill First?

You cannot fill every gap. Communities must prioritize. This framework helps you rank which deficits pose the highest mortality risk and should be addressed first.

**Rank mortality impact 1–5 for your community:**

1. **Uncontrolled infection (sepsis):** Any community. Treatable infections becoming fatal fast.
   - **Priority if:** No antibiotic stockpile, poor water quality, minimal infection control training
   - **First fix:** Water safety + hand-washing protocols + antibiotic sourcing (script, vet, fish meds)

2. **Acute trauma (uncontrolled bleeding, crush injury):** Depends on hazard level (mining, unstable infrastructure, vehicle trauma).
   - **Priority if:** No one trained in trauma first aid, no tourniquets/hemostatic supplies, no surgical capability
   - **First fix:** Trauma kit assembly + train one person in emergency bleeding control

3. **Chronic condition decompensation (diabetic with no insulin, epileptic with no seizure meds):** Depends on who's in your community.
   - **Priority if:** Type 1 diabetic, severe asthmatic, or person on critical cardiovascular meds with minimal stockpile
   - **First fix:** Inventory chronic conditions + begin medication stockpiling immediately

4. **Respiratory failure (pneumonia, severe asthma, drowning):** Common in offline scenarios.
   - **Priority if:** No oxygen delivery capability, no one trained in rescue breathing, immunocompromised people in group
   - **First fix:** Train BLS (basic life support), secure any available oxygen supplies, herbal bronchodilators

5. **Severe dehydration/electrolyte collapse:** Highly treatable with ORS but deadly if missed.
   - **Priority if:** No ORS stockpile, contaminated water, high diarrheal disease risk, no clean water source
   - **First fix:** Water safety plan + ORS production (can be made from salt, sugar, clean water)

6. **Undiagnosed/mismanaged acute illness (missing meningitis, appendicitis, MI):** High but variable risk.
   - **Priority if:** No trained person to do systematic exam, no reference books, decision-making paralysis
   - **First fix:** Train physical exam skills + buy reference manuals (hardcopy)

7. **Mental health crisis/suicide:** Real but often deprioritized. Psychological breakdown cascades.
   - **Priority if:** History of depression/anxiety in community, isolated settlement, high stress (conflict, climate threat)
   - **First fix:** Identify one mental health advocate, establish peer support meetings, secure herbal support supplies

**Decision matrix:**

Ask: **"If we have zero warning and collapse happens tomorrow, what kills the most people in first 6 months?"**

| Scenario | Highest Priority Gap |
|----------|---------------------|
| Diabetic Type 1 in community | Insulin stockpile #1 |

### Overall Community Readiness Tier Calculation

**Calculate average score across all layers:**

```
Tier = (Layer 1 + Layer 2 + Layer 3 + Layer 4 + Layer 5 + Layer 6) ÷ 6
```

| Score | Tier | Interpretation | Recommended Action |
|-------|------|-----------------|-------------------|
| 0–20% | **Tier 0: Survival (Critical)** | Minimal capacity; high mortality from treatable conditions | Prioritize water safety + trauma first aid + pain meds |
| 21–40% | **Tier 1: Basic Readiness** | Can handle minor to moderate cases; serious conditions likely fatal | Build systematic training, establish supply chains |
| 41–60% | **Tier 2: Functional** | Can manage routine cases + minor trauma; prepared for common diseases | Strengthen weak layers, extend supply stockpiles |
| 61–80% | **Tier 3: Resilient** | Can handle most acute issues + some surgical capability; strong prevention | Focus on chronic disease + mental health; prepare for rare events |
| 81–100% | **Tier 4: Advanced** | Comprehensive medical capacity; minimal preventable mortality | Mentor other communities; focus on knowledge preservation |
