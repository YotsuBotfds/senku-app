## Overview

A loom is a force multiplier for textile production. Without a loom, weaving a cloth is a tedious 1-to-1 process (one thread at a time). With a loom, the weaver interlaces two perpendicular yarn systems — warp (lengthwise) and weft (crosswise) — at rates of 100+ picks per minute. A single weaver produces meters of cloth in a day that would take weeks by hand.

This guide covers four designs suited to low-tech production: the frame loom (simplest, most portable), the warp-weighted loom (historical, vertical), the rigid heddle loom (most practical for production), and the inkle loom (specialized for bands and straps). Each design is explained with build materials, heddle/reed construction, and a warping walkthrough.

:::info-box
**Loom terminology:**
- **Warp:** Lengthwise threads, under tension
- **Weft:** Crosswise threads, interlaced with warp
- **Shed:** The gap between raised and lowered warp threads (where the shuttle passes)
- **Pick:** One pass of the weft thread across the warp (one row of weave)
- **Heddle:** A device that raises/lowers alternating warp threads to create the shed
:::

For producing the spun yarn a loom requires, see <a href="../spinning-fiber-production.html">Spinning and Fiber Production</a>. For building a spinning wheel to spin yarn efficiently, see <a href="../spinning-wheel-construction.html">Spinning Wheel Construction</a>.

</section>

<section id="simple-frame-loom">

## Simple Frame Loom

The frame loom is the easiest to build and requires only four boards and some nails. It's ideal for learning weaving and creating small tapestries.

**Materials:**

- **Four boards:** 1 × 2 lumber (or similar), lengths = 18, 18, 12, 12 inches (adjust for desired width/height)
- **Nails:** 1½–2 inch finish nails (20–30 nails for a 12-inch-wide loom)
- **Yarn:** Warp yarn (4-ply worsted wool, 2–4 oz weight). Weft yarn can be any thickness (thinner for tabby, thicker for tapestry effects)
- **Dowel or stick:** ½-inch dowel for the bottom roller (helps manage tension)

**Construction:**

1. **Assemble the frame:**
   - Lay the two 18-inch boards flat on a table, parallel, 12 inches apart
   - Butt the two 12-inch boards between them, one at each end, forming a rectangle
   - Screw or nail the corners firmly (use 3–4 fasteners per corner for rigidity)

2. **Install warp pegs:**
   - Drive nails along the top edge of the frame, spaced ½ inch apart (for a 12-inch-wide loom, that's 24 nails)
   - Drive another row of matching nails along the bottom edge, directly below the top row
   - Nails should protrude ½–¾ inches

3. **Attach the tension dowel:**
   - Fit a wooden dowel (½ inch diameter, 12 inches long) into notches or holes drilled on the inside of the two side boards, about 2 inches from the bottom
   - The dowel holds the warp stable and can be rotated to increase tension as weaving progresses

**Warping the frame loom:**

1. **Tie the yarn onto the first peg** at the top left corner
2. **Stretch warp yarn down** to the matching peg at the bottom
3. **Wrap around the bottom peg** and bring the yarn back up to the top, side by side with the first wrap (creating a U-shape for each wrap)
4. **Continue this figure-8 pattern**, spacing wraps ½ inch apart, until you reach the right edge of the frame
5. **Tie off** at the top-right peg; weave the tail through the last few warps for security

**Tension:** The warp should be taut enough to pluck like a guitar string, but not so tight that the frame warps or twists.

:::tip
For a beginner's first project, a 12-inch-wide, 18-inch-tall frame with a simple 2/2 plain weave (over 2 threads, under 2 threads) is ideal. It can be completed in 2–3 hours.
:::

**Weaving on the frame loom:**

- Use a shed stick (a flat dowel or piece of card) pushed behind the warp to separate the warp threads into two layers
- Pass the weft shuttle (a simple inch-wide card wrapped with yarn works fine) through the shed, lay the yarn across, and beat it snug with the shed stick
- Flip the shed (rotate the stick 90°) and pass the shuttle back through the opposite shed
- Continue until the loom is full

**Removal:** Cut the warp ties at the top and bottom; slide the finished cloth off and tie the warp ends to prevent unraveling.

## Warp-Weighted Loom

The warp-weighted loom is the most historically significant design — used for thousands of years to weave linen and wool on a large scale. The warp hangs vertically, with heavy weights (stones or clay) hanging from the bottom of each warp thread to maintain tension.

**Materials:**

- **Frame:** Two upright posts (4 × 4 or two 2 × 4s together), 5–6 feet tall, set 2–3 feet apart
- **Top beam:** A horizontal log or board (8–12 inches diameter, 2–3 feet long) spanning the uprights at the top, secured with bolts or rope
- **Shed bar:** A wooden dowel or rod (1 inch diameter, 2 feet long), hung at about the top beam level
- **Heddle rod:** A second bar below the shed bar, to control the alternate warp threads
- **Weights:** Clay loom weights (shaped like a disk with a hole, 2–4 lbs each), or stones tied to warp bundles. For a 24-inch-wide loom, you'll need 40–50 weights.
- **Yarn:** 2–4-ply wool (the warp can be fairly coarse; the weft finer)

**Construction:**

1. **Set up the frame:**
   - Dig post holes 2 feet deep and set the two uprights in concrete or packed earth
   - Space them 3 feet apart
   - Attach the top beam between the uprights with bolts and an angled support beam underneath (to prevent sag)

2. **Hang the shed bar:**
   - Secure it near the top beam (1–2 feet down from the top) using rope or leather straps so it can pivot slightly
   - This bar will be used to separate the warp into a shed (front and back layers)

3. **Hang the heddle rod:**
   - Position it about 12 inches below the shed bar
   - Attach heddle loops (short lengths of yarn) to every other warp thread; these threads will be controlled by the heddle rod

4. **Warp preparation:**
   - Wrap the warp yarn around the top beam in a continuous length, creating the warp for the full width of the loom
   - The warp should hang straight down, creating a vertical "V" shape at the back

5. **Attach loom weights:**
   - Tie a small loop at the bottom of each warp thread (bundle every 2–3 threads together)
   - Slip a loom weight (disk with a hole) onto each loop, or tie a stone to the bundle
   - The weights hang 1–2 feet below the final woven edge, maintaining constant tension

**Weaving on a warp-weighted loom:**

- **Create the shed:** Push the shed bar forward to separate the front and back warp layers
- **Pass the shuttle:** Throw the weft shuttle through the shed from one side to the other
- **Change sheds:** Pull the heddle rod toward you (raising the heddle-controlled warp threads) to reverse the shed
- **Beat the weft:** Use a wooden sword (a flat board the width of the loom) to beat the weft threads snug against the developing cloth edge
- Continue alternating sheds and passing the shuttle; the loom weights keep the warp vertical and tensioned as the cloth grows downward

**Yield:** A warp-weighted loom can produce 3–6 feet of cloth in a sustained weaving session (6–8 hours of work).

:::warning
Warp-weighted looms are freestanding and tall — set them up in a sheltered location. Wind gusts can shift the weight of the hanging warp and cause the entire structure to tip. Secure the base to prevent tipping.
:::

## Rigid Heddle Loom

The rigid heddle loom is the most practical design for serious textile production. A "rigid heddle" is a flat frame (traditionally wood, now often composite or plastic) with a series of holes and slots. As the heddle is raised and lowered, warp threads (in the holes) and warp threads (in the slots) move in opposite directions, creating the shed.

**Materials:**

- **Heddle:** A wooden board (12–36 inches wide, 1½ inches tall, 1 inch thick) with evenly spaced holes (¼ inch diameter) and slots (¼–½ inch wide) in alternating pattern, spaced about ¼ inch apart. A 24-inch heddle might have 100+ holes and slots.
- **Frame:** Two wooden uprights (36–48 inches tall), fixed 24–30 inches apart. The heddle hangs between them on rope or leather straps.
- **Beater/sword:** A flat wooden blade (24 inches long, 2–3 inches tall, ½ inch thick) that swings in front of the heddle on rope or leather arms. This beats the weft into place.
- **Cloth beam/roller:** A wooden dowel or rod (24 inches long, 1½–2 inches diameter) at the front of the frame, rotated by a handle. The woven cloth is wound onto this roller.
- **Warp beam/roller:** A similar beam at the back, on which the warp is wrapped.
- **Yarn:** Same as above — 2–4-ply yarn for warp and weft

**Heddle construction (DIY):**

If a commercial heddle is unavailable, you can construct one:

1. **Drill the holes:** Mark a line down the center of a wooden board at ¼-inch intervals. Drill ¼-inch holes through the board, skipping every other mark (so holes are spaced ½ inch apart, center-to-center).

2. **Cut the slots:** Between each pair of holes, saw a slot ¼ inch wide and ½ inch tall (or slightly wider/taller, depending on yarn thickness). The slot must be symmetric — centered between two holes horizontally and positioned so that it's level with the holes when viewed from the front.

3. **Sand smooth:** All holes and slots must have smooth edges (rough edges snag yarn).

**Frame assembly:**

1. **Build uprights:** Two boards, 36–48 inches tall, spaced 24–30 inches apart, secured at the base with cross-bracing
2. **Mount the heddle:** Hang the heddle between the uprights using rope or leather straps that allow it to pivot up and down
3. **Install beater arms:** Attach two ropes or leather straps at the top of the frame, extending downward to the left and right of the heddle, and secure the beater/sword to these arms. The beater should hang about 6 inches in front of the heddle when at rest.
4. **Install the cloth beam:** Position a wooden roller at the front bottom of the frame, with a hand crank on one end
5. **Install the warp beam:** A matching roller at the back

**Warping the rigid heddle loom:**

1. **Thread the warp:** Starting from the left, insert each warp thread alternately through a **hole** and a **slot** in the heddle, working toward the right. A 24-inch heddle with ¼-inch spacing holds 96–120 warp threads (about 100 for typical cloth).

2. **Wrap the warp:** Wind the warp around the warp beam (rear) several times, creating multiple parallel lengths. Use spacers (cards or sticks) between wraps to keep the warp from tangling.

3. **Tension the warp:** Pull the warp beam toward you, creating 2–3 pounds of tension. Tie it off to the frame.

4. **Thread the cloth beam:** At the front, wrap the warp around the cloth beam (same warp ends that are wrapped on the warp beam) and tie off.

**Weaving on a rigid heddle loom:**

1. **Raise the heddle:** Pull the heddle upward (the warp threads in the holes move up; threads in the slots move down). This creates a **shed** — a gap between the two sets of warp threads.

2. **Pass the shuttle:** Throw the weft shuttle through the shed from left to right.

3. **Beat:** Pull the beater/sword toward you, striking the weft against the developing cloth edge.

4. **Lower the heddle:** Push the heddle down (reversing which threads are up and down).

5. **Pass the shuttle:** Throw the weft from right to left through the new shed.

6. **Beat:** Strike the beater forward again.

7. **Advance the cloth:** Turn the cloth beam handle, winding the finished cloth onto the beam. Simultaneously, turn the warp beam handle backward, releasing more warp.

**Speed:** A practiced weaver can throw 80–120 picks per minute on a rigid heddle loom — far faster than hand-weaving or frame looms.

:::tip
The rhythm becomes automatic: **raise-throw-beat-lower-throw-beat**. It's almost musical. After 30 minutes of practice, most people find a comfortable pace.
:::

## Inkle Loom

An inkle loom is a specialized frame for weaving narrow bands (1–6 inches wide). It uses a figure-8 warp arrangement around a series of pegs, making it quick to set up and ideal for tablet weaving, decorative bands, and trim.

**Materials:**

- **Frame:** Four boards (24, 24, 12, 12 inches) assembled into a rectangle, or a commercial inkle frame
- **Pegs:** Wooden dowels (½ inch diameter) inserted into drilled holes around the frame perimeter, spaced to create the warp path

**Peg arrangement:**

The pegs are arranged in a specific pattern so that winding the warp around them creates a continuous figure-8 path. For a typical inkle loom:

- **Top-left peg (A)** at position (1, 10)
- **Top-right peg (B)** at position (11, 10)
- **Bottom pegs (C, D, E, F...)** arranged in a series at positions (1, 2), (2, 2), (3, 2), ... (11, 2) (adjust spacing as needed)

**Warping an inkle loom:**

1. **Tie the warp** to peg A (top-left)
2. **Wrap the warp** in a figure-8 pattern: down to peg C, across to peg D, back up to peg B, across to peg A, and repeat
3. **Each loop** around the peg sequence creates a new warp thread
4. **Continue** until the band is the desired width (typically 10–30 threads for a 2–4-inch band)
5. **Tie off** at the starting peg

**Weaving on an inkle loom:**

- Simple over-under weaving (plain weave) creates a basic band
- Or use **tablet weaving** (threading warp through cards) for patterned bands
- Throw the shuttle through the shed, beat, and advance

**Advantages:**

- Fast setup (5–10 minutes vs. 30+ minutes for other looms)
- Portable (can be a small desk-size frame)
- Great for producing trim, belts, and decorative bands in bulk
- Warp lengths of 10–20 feet are achievable, making long bands without re-warping

## Heddle Construction (Advanced)

For rigid heddle looms, heddles can be purchased or constructed by hand.

**Wooden heddle (carved from a single board):**

1. Select a straight-grained board (maple or ash, ¾–1 inch thick, ¼ inch taller than desired finished heddle)
2. Mark the heddle grid: evenly spaced lines (¼ inch apart) representing the holes and slots
3. Drill all holes first (½-inch bit, slow speed to avoid splintering)
4. Saw the slots using a thin kerf saw, stopping at the pencil lines so slots don't extend to the edges (which would weaken the heddle)
5. Sand the entire heddle until all holes and slots are smooth (no snagging)
6. Test-fit warp yarn — it should slide freely

**Heddle spacing (EPI = ends per inch):**

Common spacings for different fabrics:

| Yarn Weight | Spacing | EPI | Heddle Holes/Inch |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Thin (2-ply)** | 1/16 in | 16 | 16 |
| **Medium (4-ply)** | 1/8 in | 8 | 8 |
| **Thick (8-ply)** | 1/4 in | 4 | 4 |

**Alternative heddles:**

- **Heddle bar with loops:** A dowel with small yarn loops tied at regular intervals (loops serve as the "holes"; warp threads pass through)
- **Multi-harness heddles:** A complex loom might have 4+ heddles, each controlling a different set of warp threads, allowing for complex weaves (twill, damask, etc.)

## Reed/Beater Making

A reed (or beater) is the instrument that compacts the weft into place after each pass.

**Wooden beater (simple):**

1. Select a board (maple, ash, or pine), 24 inches long, 3 inches tall, ½ inch thick
2. Sand smooth, rounding the edges
3. Drill a hole near each end (½ inch) for attaching to the beater arms via rope or leather straps
4. The flat face should be smooth and slightly curved (to match the natural arc of the warp)

**Reed with teeth (more efficient):**

For finer fabrics, a reed with teeth (cut slots) can be more efficient:

1. Cut a series of 1-inch-tall slots into the bottom edge of the beater, spaced about ½ inch apart
2. Each slot spans 3–4 warp threads, and the reed teeth (the wood between slots) compact the weft more uniformly
3. This is particularly useful for plain weave (tabby) where consistent beat is important for a smooth fabric

## Warping Procedures

The most common mistake beginners make is improper warping — crossed threads, tangled warp, or uneven tension. Here's the systematic approach:

**Calculate warp length:**

For a finished cloth, you need:

- **Loom waste:** 2–3 feet (the beginning and end of warp that cannot be woven)
- **Desired cloth length:** The actual length of fabric you want
- **Shrinkage:** Typically 5–10% during finishing
- **Total warp length needed:** (loom waste) + (cloth length) + (cloth length × shrinkage)

Example: 2 feet loom waste + 4 feet cloth + 0.4 feet shrinkage = **6.4 feet warp length per thread**

**Warping a rigid heddle loom (step-by-step):**

1. **Measure and mark:** Determine how many warp threads you need. For a 24-inch cloth at 8 EPI, you need 192 threads.

2. **Use a warping board or pegs:** Wind all 192 warp threads in a continuous figure-8 around two pegs set the appropriate distance apart (to create the 6.4-foot length). This takes 30–45 minutes for most weavers.

3. **Bundle the warp:** Tie the bundle at three points (start, middle, end) to prevent tangling.

4. **Transfer to the loom:** Carefully unwind one thread at a time, inserting it through the heddle (alternating holes and slots) and wrapping it around the front and back beams.

5. **Tension:** Crank both beams to create even tension across all threads (2–3 pounds per thread, roughly).

**Avoid common warping errors:**

- **Crossed threads:** Every thread must follow a clear path through the heddle; intersecting threads cause shed problems.
- **Uneven tension:** Tight threads vs. loose threads create an uneven cloth surface.
- **Twisted warp:** If the warp bundle was twisted during transfer, individual threads may be twisted. Untwist before tensioning.

:::warning
Improper warping is the #1 cause of weaving failures. If you encounter shed problems, dropped threads, or lumpy cloth, the issue is almost always in the warping step.
:::

## Maintenance

**Loom care:**

- **Keep dry:** Wood looms absorb moisture and can warp or swell. Store in a dry location. If stored long-term, check for wood rot before using.
- **Oil wooden parts:** Periodically oil the frame and beams with linseed oil to prevent cracking.
- **Check beam alignment:** Ensure the front and back beams are parallel and level. Misaligned beams cause uneven cloth.

**Heddle maintenance:**

- **Clean slots and holes:** Fluff and lint accumulate. Use a small brush or cloth to clean periodically.
- **Check for cracks:** Wooden heddles can split if struck hard or exposed to moisture. Replace if cracked.

**Warp management:**

- **Remove finished cloth carefully:** Don't jerk or pull sharply — this can break warp threads.
- **Tie off warp ends:** Use overhand knots to prevent unraveling while you're finishing the cloth.

## First Project Guide

**Recommended first weave: A simple 4-yard scarf on a rigid heddle loom**

**Materials:**
- 4-ply worsted wool, 4 oz (for warp), 2 oz (for weft)
- Rigid heddle loom, 20-inch width
- Beater and shuttle (can be hand-made)

**Steps:**

1. **Warp:** 160 threads at 8 EPI, total length 5 feet (allows for loom waste + 4 yards cloth)
2. **First 12 inches:** Plain weave (over 1, under 1). This is the easiest weave and builds rhythm.
3. **Beat with consistent force:** Aim for a fabric density of 10–12 picks per inch (match the warp density for balanced plain weave).
4. **Weave for 4 yards:** Total time = 4–6 hours of actual weaving (excluding setup).
5. **Finish:** Remove from loom, tie off warp ends, wash in warm water, and lay flat to dry.

**Result:** A warm, handmade wool scarf that cost $15 in materials and 6 hours of labor. Many handweavers find this deeply satisfying and continue to weaving as a serious craft.
