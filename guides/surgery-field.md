## Overview

Field surgery in low-resource or no-resource environments is among the most challenging medical procedures. Success depends entirely on understanding principles: infection prevention above all, speed when time is critical, and knowing when NOT to operate. The single greatest cause of death from field surgery is not bleeding or technical failure—it is infection leading to sepsis. Before operating, ensure you have mastered <a href="../trauma-hemorrhage-control.html">Trauma Hemorrhage Control</a> techniques, as blood loss can occur from inadvertent vessel injury. Understanding <a href="../shock-recognition-resuscitation.html">Hemorrhagic Shock & Emergency Resuscitation</a> is essential for managing intraoperative and postoperative complications.

:::danger
Field surgery without proper training or equipment carries extreme risk of mortality. The infection rate from improvised procedures exceeds 40%. Only attempt surgery when patient will certainly die without intervention. Ensure adequate pain control before any procedure.
:::

:::warning
The most critical factor determining survival is sterilization. A single non-sterile instrument can introduce lethal bacteria (Clostridium tetani, Staphylococcus aureus, E. coli) that cause gangrene, sepsis, and death. Boiling for 20+ minutes is non-negotiable. Do not skip this step. See <a href="../infection-control.html">Infection Control</a> for detailed sterilization protocols and hand hygiene best practices.
:::

## 1\. Sterilization Techniques

The single most common cause of death from improvised surgery is infection. Sterilization is not optional—it is the difference between healing and death from sepsis.

:::danger
Never reuse sterilization water or instruments touched by non-sterile surfaces. If you are uncertain whether something is sterile, assume it is contaminated. Better to sterilize twice than to use questionable instruments.
:::

### Boiling Sterilization (Most Accessible)

Boiling is the most reliable method available without autoclave equipment:

-   **Duration:** 20 minutes at a rolling boil (212°F / 100°C)
-   **Container:** Stainless steel pot with lid
-   **Water depth:** Ensure all instruments are fully submerged; add more water as it boils away
-   **Instruments:** Place on clean cloth at bottom of pot; avoid touching bottom or sides
-   **Cooling:** Remove with sterile forceps onto sterile cloth; allow to air dry
-   **Storage:** Keep sterile instruments on sterile field until use

### Pressure Cooker Sterilization (Faster Alternative)

Higher temperature and pressure kills spores more effectively:

-   **Pressure:** 15 PSI (pounds per square inch)
-   **Duration:** 15 minutes at full pressure
-   **Temperature:** Approximately 250°F (121°C)
-   **Preparation:** Place instruments on cloth or wire rack at bottom, cover with cloth
-   **Loading:** Add water per pressure cooker manual; do not overcrowd
-   **Cooling:** Allow slow natural pressure release (5-10 minutes) before opening
-   **Advantage:** More effective against spore-forming bacteria (Clostridium)

### Flame Sterilization (Minimal Equipment)

Used for metal instruments when liquid sterilization unavailable:

-   **Method:** Pass metal instruments through open flame (candle, torch, or stove) until red-hot
-   **Duration:** 2-3 seconds at visible red heat minimum
-   **Timing:** Repeat flame passage until no visible discoloration
-   **Cooling:** Place on sterile cloth; allow to cool without touching
-   **Limitation:** Does not work well for fragile items; not suitable for some materials
-   **Warning:** Metal becomes dangerously hot—allow to cool before use

### Chemical Sterilization (Alcohol/Betadine Soak)

Less effective than heat but useful as supplementary method:

-   **70% Isopropyl Alcohol:** 15 minutes minimum soak for instruments
-   **Betadine Solution (Iodine):** 10-15 minutes soak; better than alcohol alone
-   **Combined approach:** Boil first, then 10-minute alcohol soak for critical instruments
-   **Limitation:** Does not reliably kill spores; use only when heat unavailable
-   **Preparation:** Clean instruments thoroughly of organic material first
-   **Drying:** Use sterile gauze or allow air dry on sterile field

### Creating and Maintaining a Sterile Field

A sterile field is the clean area where sterilized instruments remain uncontaminated before and during surgery:

#### Sterile Field Protocol

1.  **Base layer:** Use clean, recently boiled cotton cloth or disposable white paper as field surface
2.  **Environment:** Minimize air movement; avoid fanning or strong drafts
3.  **Boundaries:** Keep field as small as practical; consider everything beyond edge as non-sterile
4.  **Contact rule:** Nothing non-sterile contacts sterile field—no casual touching
5.  **Height:** Maintain field at waist level or above; never below waist
6.  **Placement:** Never place items at field edges—risk of contamination
7.  **Covering:** If field must sit for extended time, cover with sterile cloth
8.  **Visibility:** Keep operator at position where entire field is visible

### Surgical Hand Scrub Technique

The operator's hands are the most likely source of contamination during surgery.

:::info-box
The surgical scrub is the single most important infection-prevention step performed by the surgeon. Hands will be in direct contact with open surgical sites and must be as clean as humanly possible. A thorough surgical scrub removes >99% of surface bacteria.
:::

#### Step-by-Step Surgical Scrub

1.  **Pre-wash:** Remove watches, rings, bracelets; trim nails short
2.  **Water:** Use warm running water; keep water temperature comfortably hot
3.  **Soap selection:** Plain soap is acceptable; antiseptic soap (iodine, chlorhexidine) is better if available
4.  **Timing:** Scrub for 3-5 minutes minimum (some protocols recommend 10 minutes)
5.  **Technique:** Scrub systematically:
    -   Wet hands and forearms to 2 inches above elbow
    -   Apply soap; work into lather
    -   Scrub nails and nail beds with brush (1 minute)
    -   Scrub palms, backs of hands, and between fingers (1 minute)
    -   Scrub forearms in circular motions up to 2 inches above elbow (1-2 minutes)
    -   Rinse thoroughly from fingers to elbows; keep hands higher than elbows
    -   Repeat entire process
6.  **Final rinse:** Dry with sterile cloth in single direction (hand to elbow)
7.  **Don gloves:** Use sterile gloves; handle only by cuff, not exterior surface
8.  **Timing:** Complete scrub immediately before surgical procedure; do not touch anything after scrub

### Protective Barriers

-   **Hair coverage:** Cover all hair (head and facial) with clean cloth or hat
-   **Mouth/nose:** Sterile cloth or improvised mask covering mouth and nose
-   **Hands:** Sterile gloves; double-glove if available (outer pair can be removed if contaminated)
-   **Body:** Clean protective clothing; avoid loose sleeves that could dangle into field
-   **Eye protection:** Goggles or shield to protect from blood spray

:::tip
If sterile gloves unavailable, wash hands thoroughly, dry on sterile cloth, then wear clean cloth gloves secured at wrists with tape or string. Not ideal but far better than bare hands.
:::

## 2\. Improvised Surgical Instruments

Professional surgical instruments have precise tolerances and materials. Improvised instruments are imperfect but functional. Sterilize all instruments thoroughly before use.

:::warning
Do not substitute non-metal instruments for cutting or penetrating instruments. Plastic, bone, or wooden cutting tools are unreliable and can splinter. Metal instruments (boiled steel) are the minimum standard for any cutting, retraction, or probing procedure.
:::

### Scalpel (Cutting Blade)

For initial incisions and precise cutting:

-   **Option A - Razor Blade Scalpel:**
    -   Use new, single-edge razor blade (steel, not plastic)
    -   Create handle: Tape blade to wooden dowel, pencil, or stick with 1 inch of blade exposed
    -   Alternative: Wrap blade edge with tape, leaving 3-5mm sharp edge exposed at blade end
    -   Sterilize blade by boiling before taping
    -   Sharper and more controllable than kitchen knives
-   **Option B - Obsidian Blade:**
    -   Obsidian volcanic glass flakes sharper than steel surgical instruments
    -   Knap obsidian against hammer stone to create sharp edge
    -   Wrap blade in cloth for safety; leave cutting edge exposed
    -   Sterilize by boiling; does not degrade under heat
    -   Historical use: Aztec surgeons used obsidian for cesarean sections
    -   Limitation: Brittle; may break and leave fragments in wound
-   **Option C - Sharp Knife:**
    -   Use cleanest, sharpest kitchen knife available
    -   Sterilize by boiling; sharpen before sterilization
    -   Less precise than blade, but functional

### Hemostats (Bleeding Vessel Control)

For clamping blood vessels:

-   **Improvised Hemostat from Pliers:**
    -   Use small needle-nose or locking pliers
    -   Place gauze padding on jaws to prevent tissue crushing
    -   Clamp vessel and keep clamped until ties are applied or cauterized
    -   Sterilize entire tool by boiling
-   **Bent Wire Hemoclip:**
    -   Bend stainless steel wire (coat hanger, wire) into C-shape with pliers
    -   Splay ends slightly and bend back slightly for tension
    -   Position over bleeding vessel; tension keeps vessel compressed
    -   Requires practice; less reliable than pliers

### Retractors (Tissue Pulling/Exposure)

For holding tissues back to expose surgical area:

-   **Spoon Handle Retractor:**
    -   Bend handle of metal spoon at angle using pliers
    -   Smooth any sharp edges that would cut tissue
    -   Use to gently pull back tissue and expose deeper structures
    -   Sterilize by boiling
-   **Self-Retaining Retractor:**
    -   Fashion from wire or thin metal; create loop with tension
    -   Alternatively: Use clean cloth strips tied to opposite edges of incision
    -   Less effective than professional instruments but provides gentle traction
-   **Hand-Held Retraction:**
    -   Assistant uses clean gauze to gently hold tissues back
    -   Most reliable when assistant remains still throughout procedure

### Suture Needles (Curved Needles)

For passing suture material through tissue:

-   **Sewing Needle Conversion:**
    -   Use medium-sized sewing needle (not too thick, not too fine)
    -   Curve needle: Use pliers to carefully bend into gentle curve (1/4 to 1/3 circle)
    -   Curved needle is much easier to use than straight needle in tight spaces
    -   Create handle: Wrap small section of needle with cloth for better grip
    -   Sterilize by boiling and alcohol soak
    -   Thread with suture material; ensure material is adequately secured
-   **Fish Hook Needle:**
    -   Carefully sharpen fish hook point with file or whetstone
    -   File down barb if too aggressive
    -   Fashion handle from stick and cord
    -   Less ideal but functional in emergency

Multiple options available with varying properties:

### Wound Irrigation Syringe

For cleaning wounds with antiseptic solution:

-   **Squeeze Bottle Syringe:**
    -   Use plastic squeeze bottle (empty dish soap, water bottle, or balloon bulb)
    -   If possible, attach plastic tubing to create nozzle
    -   Drill or punch small hole (1-2mm) in attachment point for focused stream
    -   Fill with boiled water or antiseptic solution
    -   Create pressure by squeezing to irrigate wound
-   **Improvised Syringe:**
    -   Use actual syringe if available (sterilize by boiling)
    -   Attach catheter or IV tubing to needle hub
    -   Sterilize entire assembly
-   **Irrigation Solution:**
    -   Boiled water (cooled) is acceptable
    -   Dilute iodine (Betadine) solution if available: 1 part iodine to 10 parts water
    -   Saline solution if salt and water available: 1 teaspoon salt per cup water, boil and cool

### Forceps and Clamps

-   **Tweezers/Forceps:**
    -   Use fine-tipped tweezers for handling delicate tissues and removing foreign material
    -   Sterilize by boiling
-   **Needle Holder/Clamp:**
    -   Small C-clamp or locking pliers can substitute
    -   Holds needle securely during suturing
    -   Sterilize by boiling

### Gauze and Dressing Materials

-   **Sterile Gauze:**
    -   Boil clean cotton cloth to create sterile gauze
    -   Cut into working sizes before procedure
    -   For packing: can use clean cloth strips boiled and dried
-   **Absorbent Materials:**
    -   Clean cotton cloth or boiled cloth strips
    -   Avoid lint-producing materials (terry cloth worse than plain cotton)

## 2A. Recognition & Assessment Before Surgery

**DO NOT operate unless you have correctly identified the problem.** Operating on the wrong site or for the wrong reason causes preventable death and disability. Spend time assessing before committing to surgery.

### Assessment Protocol

1. **Patient history:** Ask patient or witnesses what happened. When did pain start? What makes it better/worse?
2. **Physical examination:** Inspect, palpate (feel), percuss (tap), listen. Compare both sides of body.
3. **Vital signs:** Temperature, heart rate, blood pressure, respiration rate. Baseline vitals matter for detecting deterioration.
4. **Differential diagnosis:** What else could this be? Appendicitis mimics gastroenteritis. Pneumothorax mimics heart attack.
5. **Imaging if possible:** X-ray would change management—if available, use it.
6. **Time urgency:** Is this life-threatening right now, or can you defer surgery? Many surgical emergencies have brief windows of opportunity that close quickly.

:::info-box
The diagnostic decision—whether to operate—is more important than surgical technique. An excellent surgery for the wrong diagnosis kills the patient. When uncertain, ask: "Will this patient die in the next 24-48 hours without surgery?" If the answer is not definitively "yes," defer surgery and manage medically if possible.
:::

## 3\. Anesthesia & Pain Management

General anesthesia (deep sleep) is extremely dangerous in improvised settings. Local anesthesia (numbing the surgical area) is vastly preferable. Many historical anesthesia methods are cardiotoxic or hepatotoxic and carry high mortality. When possible, use local anesthesia with supportive pain management.

:::danger
NEVER attempt general anesthesia (putting patient to sleep) without training and monitoring equipment. Unconscious patients cannot tell you if they cannot breathe. Aspiration of vomit is nearly always fatal in field settings. Local anesthesia is superior in every way—patient remains conscious, can warn you of problems, and can hold still.
:::

### Local Anesthesia (Preferred)

Numbing only the surgical area while patient remains conscious and able to communicate:

#### Ice Numbing (Safest, Most Accessible)

-   **Method:** Apply ice directly to wound area or skin for 5-10 minutes before surgery
-   **Depth:** Effective for 2-3mm surface depth only; deeper tissues remain sensitive
-   **Risk:** Ice burn if applied too long
-   **Advantage:** No toxicity; completely safe; readily available
-   **Limitation:** Minimal effect for deep structures

#### Topical Alcohol Application

-   **Agent:** 70% isopropyl alcohol or high-proof spirits (whiskey, brandy)
-   **Application:** Soak cloth with alcohol; hold against skin for 3-5 minutes
-   **Depth:** Effective for superficial numbing only (1-2mm)
-   **Effect:** Rapid evaporative cooling plus topical numbing
-   **Limitation:** Does not penetrate deeply; burns if applied to open wounds

#### Infiltration Anesthesia (Tissue Injection)

-   **Concept:** Inject dilute alcohol or salt solution into tissue around surgical area to create local numbness
-   **Solution:** Dilute alcohol (50% ethanol + 50% water) or saline solution
-   **Important:** Very dilute only—alcohol injection causes significant pain and tissue damage if concentrated
-   **Injection:**
    -   Draw solution into syringe
    -   Use sterilized needle (sewing needle if necessary)
    -   Inject slowly at surgical margins and around wound edges
    -   Use small volumes (0.5-1ml per injection point)
    -   Space injections 1cm apart around perimeter
-   **Disadvantage:** Initial injection is painful; local tissue swelling may obscure anatomy
-   **Advantage:** More effective for deeper structures than topical methods

### Herbal & Plant-Based Pain Management

Safer adjuncts to local anesthesia; do not replace anesthesia:

#### Willow Bark (Salicylate Pain Relief)

-   **Source:** Bark of willow tree (white willow preferred)
-   **Active compound:** Salicin (converts to salicylic acid, similar to aspirin)
-   **Preparation:**
    -   Harvest fresh bark or use dried bark
    -   Simmer 1-2 teaspoons bark in 1 cup water for 10 minutes
    -   Strain; cool to drinking temperature
    -   Drink 1 cup 1-2 hours before surgery
-   **Effect:** Mild to moderate pain relief; reduces inflammation
-   **Timeline:** 30-60 minutes to peak effect
-   **Contraindications:** Avoid if history of aspirin allergy; can increase bleeding
-   **Dosage:** Single 1-cup dose; do not repeat within 6 hours

#### Valerian Root (Sedation/Anxiety)

-   **Source:** Dried root of valerian plant
-   **Action:** CNS depressant; reduces anxiety, aids sleep
-   **Preparation:**
    -   Simmer 1 teaspoon dried root in 1 cup water for 10 minutes
    -   Strain and drink
    -   Alternatively: soak dried root in alcohol for 2 weeks, use 1 tablespoon tincture
-   **Effect:** Calming, sedating; patient more relaxed during procedure
-   **Timing:** Give 30-60 minutes before surgery; peak effect 60-90 minutes
-   **Dosage:** Single cup of tea or 1 tablespoon tincture
-   **Precaution:** May impair judgment; ensure proper supervision

#### Clove Oil (Dental Anesthesia)

-   **Active compound:** Eugenol (topical anesthetic)
-   **Source:** Whole cloves or pure clove essential oil
-   **Preparation:**
    -   Crush whole cloves and soak in oil (coconut or olive oil) for 30 minutes, or
    -   Use pure clove essential oil directly (test for skin sensitivity first)
-   **Application:**
    -   Soak small cloth with clove oil preparation
    -   Apply directly to painful tooth or oral area
    -   Hold for 5-10 minutes
-   **Effect:** Strong local numbing of tissue; smell and taste distinctive
-   **Advantage:** Excellent for dental extraction pain
-   **Limitation:** Topical only; minimal penetration beyond surface

### Historical General Anesthesia Methods (Reference Only - NOT Recommended)

The following methods are provided for historical knowledge only. All are dangerous, unpredictable, and associated with significant mortality. STRONGLY AVOID. Modern local anesthesia is far safer.

#### Diethyl Ether Inhalation

-   **Historical use:** First widely used surgical anesthetic (1846)
-   **Method:** Inhale vapors from cloth soaked in diethyl ether
-   **Extreme Dangers:**
    -   Highly flammable—explosive risk in presence of open flame or spark
    -   Respiratory depression—can cause death if overdose
    -   Unpredictable—no dosing measurement; depth of anesthesia uncontrollable
    -   Gastric irritation—vomiting during surgery risks aspiration pneumonia
    -   Cardiac arrhythmias—sudden death from unknown cause
    -   Delayed metabolism—prolonged unconsciousness after procedure
-   **Bottom line:** Do not use under any circumstance

#### Chloroform (Trichloromethane)

-   **Historical use:** Used from 1850s onward
-   **Method:** Inhale vapors, similar to ether
-   **Extreme Dangers:**
    -   Hepatotoxic—causes liver damage and liver failure
    -   Carcinogenic—known human carcinogen
    -   Cardiac toxicity—sudden fatal arrhythmias with minimal warning
    -   Renal damage—kidney failure from metabolites
    -   No safe dosage established
    -   Idiosyncratic reactions—same dose causes death in some individuals for unknown reasons
-   **Bottom line:** Do not use under any circumstance

#### Opium Tincture / Laudanum (Morphine Derivative)

-   **Historical use:** Common surgical pain management before modern anesthesia
-   **Source:** Opium poppy (Papaver somniferum) alkaloids
-   **Preparation:** Opium dissolved in alcohol (laudanum); or raw opium juice
-   **Administration:**
    -   Oral: 10-20 drops of laudanum in water, 1 hour before surgery
    -   Or: Small amount of prepared opium paste on tongue
-   **Effect:** Significant pain relief; sedation; CNS depression
-   **Serious Problems:**
    -   Respiratory depression—can cause death from stopped breathing
    -   Addiction potential—high risk of physical dependence after use
    -   Highly variable potency—opium content varies wildly; no way to dose accurately
    -   Constipation—severe, prolonged intestinal obstruction
    -   Paradoxical sensitivity—some individuals extremely sensitive; overdose common
    -   No reliable reversal if overdose occurs
-   **Consideration:** May be last resort if patient will die of pain-related shock; otherwise avoid

#### Alcohol Intoxication (Least Preferred)

-   **Concept:** Get patient drunk to reduce pain perception and inhibit muscle movement
-   **Why this is terrible:**
    -   Increases bleeding—alcohol is anticoagulant
    -   Impairs judgment—patient unable to hold still or warn surgeon
    -   Aspiration risk—vomiting during surgery risks fatal aspiration pneumonia
    -   Hypotension—alcohol drops blood pressure; risky during surgery
    -   Unpredictable response—individual tolerance varies enormously
    -   No anesthesia—patient still feels pain, just doesn't care as much
    -   Minimal analgesia—does not actually block pain sensation
-   **Bottom line:** Avoid; use only if absolutely no other option and patient explicitly consents

### Ketamine (Dissociative Anesthetic) - Reference for Understanding

Included for educational reference only; not recommended for improvised surgery:

-   **Class:** Dissociative anesthetic (patient appears awake but disconnected from pain)
-   **Advantage:** Maintains respiratory drive better than other general anesthetics
-   **Disadvantage:** Requires precise dosing; hallucinations and dysphoria common; user population (veterinary, military) not typical field surgery setting
-   **Practical reality:** Unlikely to be available in improvised scenario

### Pain Management Philosophy

-   **Always prefer local over general:** Local anesthesia allows patient to remain conscious, alert, and communicate
-   **Layer approaches:** Combine multiple methods: ice + topical alcohol + herbal aids creates tolerable procedure
-   **Ongoing reassurance:** Patient psychology matters; calm communication reduces pain perception
-   **Speed matters:** Fast surgery minimizes total pain exposure
-   **Postoperative pain:** After local anesthesia wears off, patient needs pain management: willow bark tea, rest, elevation

:::tip
Tell patient what you are about to do BEFORE you do it. "I am about to make an incision now—this will feel like a sharp cut but should not hurt from the local anesthesia. You may feel pressure but not pain." Patients tolerate surgery better when they know what to expect.
:::

## 4\. Wound Closure Techniques

Proper closure determines infection risk and cosmetic outcome. Infection is most common complication of field surgery. Clean closure with minimal gaping is goal.

### Adhesive Strips / Butterfly Bandages (Best for Shallow Cuts)

Most effective for surface wounds that do not require deep closure:

-   **When to use:** Clean cuts less than 5mm deep with well-approximated edges
-   **Advantage:** No needle required; minimal trauma; allows wound to breathe; easy to apply
-   **Material:** Sterile adhesive tape strips (commercial butterfly bandages) or create from medical tape and cloth
-   **Technique:**
    -   Clean wound thoroughly; dry completely
    -   Approximate (bring together) wound edges with hands or tweezers
    -   Apply tape strips perpendicular to wound, starting at center
    -   Press firmly; work outward toward edges
    -   Overlap each strip slightly with previous one
    -   Cover with gauze dressing if necessary
-   **Aftercare:** Keep dry; change dressing daily or if soaked; remove strips after 7-10 days
-   **Limitation:** Only effective for shallow wounds; gaping wounds require stitching

### Simple Interrupted Suture (Standard Wound Closure)

The most common and reliable suturing technique:

![Field Surgery &amp; Advanced Wound Care diagram 1](../assets/svgs/surgery-field-1.svg)

Simple Interrupted Suture Technique: Step-by-step visual guide for proper wound closure

#### Simple Interrupted Suture - Step-by-Step

1.  **Needle positioning:** Hold curved needle with holder/pliers at position where it will enter skin
2.  **Entry point:** Penetrate skin 3-5mm from wound edge at 90° angle (perpendicular to skin)
3.  **Path through tissue:** Guide needle in gentle curve through tissue depth; exit on opposite side of wound at same depth and distance from edge (typically 3-5mm)
4.  **Spacing:** Space successive stitches 5-7mm apart (not too close, not too far)
5.  **Tying:**
    -   After needle passes through, remove needle from thread
    -   Tie suture material with square knot : right thread over left, pull tight; then left thread over right, pull tight
    -   Trim thread ends to 3-5mm from knot
6.  **Repeat:** Continue stitch spacing along entire wound length
7.  **Final appearance:** Approximate wound edges well with no gaping; use minimal tension on knots

### Mattress Suture (For Deeper Wounds)

Provides additional strength for wounds under tension or with deeper layers:

-   **When to use:** Gaping wounds that open under movement; wounds where skin edges separate
-   **Technique:**
    -   Enter 5mm from wound edge at 90°
    -   Exit 5mm from wound edge on opposite side
    -   Re-enter at same exit point
    -   Exit at point approximately 3mm from original entry point, parallel to first stitch
    -   Tie square knot as with simple interrupted stitch
-   **Advantage:** Each stitch covers larger surface area; distributes tension more evenly
-   **Spacing:** Space 8-10mm apart due to larger coverage
-   **Appearance:** Creates distinctive parallel line pattern

### Figure-of-Eight Suture (For Bleeding Vessels)

Used specifically for ligating bleeding blood vessels:

-   **Indication:** Visible bleeding vessel that requires closure without removal
-   **Technique:**
    -   Pass suture under vessel (like threading under a loop)
    -   Create loop on one side of vessel
    -   Cross over vessel
    -   Create loop on opposite side
    -   Cross back over
    -   Tie with square knot, compressing vessel between crossing points
-   **Effect:** Vessel compression stops bleeding; tissue remains in place

### Deep Layered Closure (For Large Wounds)

Large or deep wounds require closure of multiple tissue layers:

1.  **Deep fascia (if visible):** Suture fascia layer first with deep stitches to reduce tension on skin
2.  **Fat layer (if deep enough):** Close with absorbable suture if available
3.  **Skin layer:** Close skin last with visible stitches
4.  **Advantage:** Reduces tension on skin closure; more secure overall
5.  **Requirement:** Must be able to clearly identify tissue layers

### Staple Closure (Alternative to Sutures)

If suture materials not available:

-   **Improvised staple:** Bent wire staples made from coat hanger or wire
-   **Creation:** Use pliers to bend wire into inverted U shape (approximately 1cm wide)
-   **Technique:**
    -   Approximate wound edges carefully
    -   Position staple across wound perpendicular to wound line
    -   Press staple down firmly through both skin edges
    -   Space staples 5-7mm apart, similar to sutures
-   **Advantage:** Faster than suturing; minimal training required
-   **Disadvantage:** Difficult to create uniform staples; may perforate through tissue
-   **Removal:** Cut staple with pliers at midpoint; pull straight up and out

### Wound Packing (For Wounds That Cannot Be Closed)

Some wounds too contaminated or deep to close primarily:

-   **Indications:** Heavily contaminated wounds, bite wounds, wounds with significant dead tissue, very deep wounds
-   **Packing material:** Sterile cloth strips (boiled cotton cloth torn into 2cm strips), gauze
-   **Technique:**
    -   Gently pack wound cavity with sterile strips, layer by layer
    -   Fill wound loosely; do not compress excessively
    -   Leave 1-2cm extending from wound surface (for removal and drainage)
    -   Cover with gauze dressing
-   **Daily care:**
    -   Once daily: wet gauze with saline or boiled water, remove packing gently
    -   Irrigate wound thoroughly
    -   Replace packing with fresh sterile strips
    -   Wound must heal from inside out; this takes longer than closed wounds
-   **Duration:** Continue packing until wound is clean and debris-free, then allow to close by granulation

### Suture Removal Timing

-   **Face:** 3-5 days (rapid healing; early removal reduces scar)
-   **Scalp:** 7-10 days
-   **Trunk:** 10-14 days
-   **Extremities:** 14-21 days (slower healing, higher tension)
-   **Joints:** 14-21 days (continue using steri-strips after removal)
-   **Technique:**
    -   Clean area with alcohol
    -   Grasp knot with tweezers and lift slightly
    -   Cut one strand of suture at skin level with scissors
    -   Pull knot away; entire stitch slides out
    -   Repeat for each suture

## 5\. Abscess Drainage

Abscesses are localized collections of pus (infection). Drain them to achieve resolution. Untreated abscesses spread systemically and cause sepsis and death.

### Recognizing an Abscess

-   **Appearance:** Localized swelling, usually round or oval
-   **Color:** Red or deep red; may be purplish at center
-   **Warmth:** Hot to touch compared to surrounding skin
-   **Fluctuance:** "Boggy" or spongy feel when pressed; may slosh slightly under pressure (indicates liquid pus inside, not solid infection)
-   **Pain:** Tender to touch; increases with pressure
-   **Surrounding cellulitis:** Redness spreading beyond obvious abscess indicates systemic spread
-   **Systemic signs:** Fever, general malaise, lymph node swelling suggests body fighting infection

### Abscess Drainage Technique

#### Step-by-Step Drainage

1.  **Preparation:**
    -   Sterile field: clean cloth, all equipment boiled and sterilized
    -   Local anesthesia: ice numbing or dilute alcohol infiltration 5-10 minutes before incision
    -   Skin prep: scrub surrounding skin with alcohol or iodine solution
2.  **Incision site:**
    -   Locate point of maximum fluctuation (softest spot where pus is closest to surface)
    -   Incise along skin tension lines (Langer's lines) when possible—wounds along tension lines heal better
    -   If location allows, incise in dependent position (lowest point) to allow gravity-assisted drainage
3.  **Incision:**
    -   Use sterilized scalpel (razor blade or knife)
    -   Make decisive, single stroke through skin and into abscess cavity
    -   Length: 1-2cm, sufficient to allow pus drainage and instrument insertion
    -   Do not make very large opening; just sufficient for drainage
4.  **Drainage:**
    -   Pus will leak out; catch with sterile gauze
    -   If pus doesn't flow: gently probe with sterile instrument to break septations (internal divisions)
    -   May need to open cavity slightly wider if loculations exist
    -   Allow complete drainage; let pus flow freely
5.  **Cavity exploration:**
    -   Once drainage complete, gently probe abscess cavity with gloved finger or sterile instrument
    -   Break apart loculations (pockets of pus) by gentle probing
    -   Remove debris, foreign material, necrotic tissue
    -   Do not explore too aggressively—risk of bleeding or perforation into adjacent structures
6.  **Irrigation:**
    -   Irrigate cavity thoroughly with sterile solution (boiled water, saline, or dilute iodine)
    -   Use irrigation syringe to flush out remaining debris
    -   Continue until irrigation fluid runs clear
7.  **Packing:**
    -   Pack abscess cavity loosely with sterile cloth strips (boiled cotton cloth)
    -   Packing prevents edges from sealing while cavity drains internally
    -   Leave packing strip end extending from wound for easy removal
    -   Cover with sterile dressing

### Daily Abscess Care

-   **Packing changes:** Once daily minimum, more often if drainage heavy
-   **Removal:** Gently pull packing material out; old packing may have new drainage stuck to it
-   **Irrigation:** Rinse cavity with sterile solution using syringe
-   **New packing:** Pack with fresh sterile cloth strips
-   **Dressing:** Cover with fresh sterile gauze
-   **Observation:** Watch for signs that infection is improving:
    -   Less discharge volume
    -   Clearer (less purulent) drainage
    -   Reduced surrounding redness
    -   Patient feels better, fever improves

### Healing from Inside Out

-   **Abscess cavities must close gradually:** Surface closes too quickly, abscess reforms if cavity still infected internally
-   **Timeline:** Typically 7-14 days for abscess to drain completely
-   **Granulation:** Eventually cavity fills with granulation tissue (healthy new tissue)
-   **Final closure:** Once cavity is small and clean, can allow to close without packing

### When to Worry About Cellulitis Spreading

-   **Signs of systemic spread:**
    -   Red streaks extending from abscess toward heart (lymphangitis)
    -   Swollen lymph nodes near abscess (groin, armpit, neck)
    -   Increasing fever despite drainage
    -   Spreading redness beyond original abscess borders
    -   Systemic illness: chills, malaise, headache
-   **Action:** If cellulitis spreading and antibiotics available, start course immediately; continue drainage; consider higher incision if spreading upward toward trunk

## 6A. Recovery & Aftercare

Recovery from field surgery is critical—more patients die from complications in the week after surgery than die on the operating table. Infection prevention must continue post-operatively. Refer to <a href="../wound-hygiene-infection-prevention.html">Wound Hygiene & Infection Prevention</a> for detailed monitoring and early sepsis recognition protocols during healing.

### Immediate Post-Operative Care (First 24-48 Hours)

**Wound monitoring:**
- Change dressings every 6-12 hours initially; more frequently if saturated with blood or drainage
- Observe for signs of infection: increased redness, warmth, foul odor, pus
- Mild seepage of clear or straw-colored fluid is normal; excessive bleeding or purulent (pus) drainage indicates problem
- Infection typically appears within 24-72 hours; monitor closely

**Pain management:**
- Willow bark tea for mild-to-moderate pain
- Immobilize surgical site—movement increases pain and risk of re-opening
- Elevation helps reduce swelling

**Systemic support:**
- Prevent the patient from eating or drinking for first 6-8 hours post-op (risk of vomiting and aspiration)
- Once awake and nausea-free, offer small amounts of water, then light food
- Keep patient warm; monitor for shock (pale, cold, rapid pulse)

:::warning
If patient develops fever over 101°F (38.3°C), increasing redness, or purulent drainage within 48-72 hours of surgery, infection is likely developing. Start antibiotics immediately if available. Do not wait for catastrophic sepsis to develop.
:::

### Long-Term Post-Operative Care (Days 3-14)

**Dressing changes:**
- Once daily until wound is dry and sutures can come out
- Use sterile technique each time—post-op infection is preventable through good hygiene
- Clean surrounding skin gently with alcohol or dilute iodine if available

**Suture removal timing (see section 4):**
- Facial wounds: 3-5 days
- Scalp: 7-10 days
- Trunk: 10-14 days
- Extremities: 14-21 days (more tension, slower healing)

**Activity restriction:**
- Ensure the patient rests; no strenuous activity for 7-14 days minimum
- Protect wound from contamination and trauma
- Gradually increase activity as healing progresses

**Complications to watch for:**
- Infection: fever, increasing redness, drainage, foul smell
- Re-opening: wound edges separate; if >5mm gap, may need re-suturing
- Hematoma: collection of blood under skin; usually reabsorbs but watch for signs of infection
- Keloid/excessive scar: normal healing—minor cosmetic issue compared to infection

:::info-box
The weeks after surgery are when your patient is most vulnerable to dying from infection. Vigilant wound care during this period prevents most post-operative deaths. Daily inspection is not excessive—it is standard of care.
:::

## 6\. Wound Debridement

Debridement (removal of dead tissue and foreign material) is essential for wound healing. Dead tissue becomes infected and sloughs; removal speeds healing and prevents infection.

### What Needs Debridement

-   **Dead tissue (necrotic tissue):** Tissue that has lost blood supply and died; appears gray, black, or brown; feels leathery or sloughs easily
-   **Foreign material:** Dirt, glass, metal fragments, cloth, any non-biological material in wound
-   **Devitalized tissue:** Tissue that is severely damaged but not yet dead; appears crushed, bruised
-   **Contaminants:** Heavily colonized (infected) tissue that is beyond salvage

### Sharp Debridement (Surgical Removal)

Using instruments to actively remove dead tissue:

-   **Tools:** Sterilized scalpel (razor blade), scissors, or knife
-   **Technique:**
    -   Use sharp blade to trim away clearly dead tissue
    -   Cut at angle to tissue, following contours of dead-tissue boundary
    -   Cut back until reaching bleeding tissue (indicates viable tissue)
    -   Goal: remove all devitalized tissue without removing viable tissue
    -   Expect bleeding when reaching viable tissue; this is normal and desired
-   **Timing:** Can be repeated daily until all necrotic tissue removed
-   **Anesthesia:** Local anesthesia important; sharp debridement is painful

### Wet-to-Dry Dressing Changes (Autolytic Debridement)

Mechanical removal of dead tissue through repeated dressing changes:

-   **Principle:** Wet gauze applied to wound; as gauze dries, it adheres to dead tissue; removal pulls away devitalized material
-   **Technique:**
    -   Soak sterile gauze in normal saline (or boiled water mixed with salt)
    -   Wring out excess—keep gauze damp but not dripping
    -   Pack wound with moist gauze, contact all areas of wound
    -   Cover outer layer with dry gauze to wick moisture
    -   Allow to dry completely (typically 4-8 hours)
    -   Remove dry layer; wet gauze will have adhered to dead tissue
    -   Pull away old gauze firmly; dead tissue comes with it
    -   Repeat 2-3 times daily
-   **Advantage:** Gentler than sharp debridement; dead tissue gradually removed without bleeding
-   **Disadvantage:** Slower than sharp debridement; also removes some viable tissue
-   **Timeline:** Typically 5-14 days for significant debridement

### Maggot Therapy (Larval Therapy)

Sterile fly larvae (maggots) eat only dead tissue, leaving viable tissue untouched:

Maggot therapy is not disgusting—it's rational emergency medicine. Historically proven effective. Modern sterile maggot therapy uses special strains that only consume necrotic tissue. Use this technique only as a last resort when other debridement methods are unavailable or have failed.

-   **Source:** Sterile larvae of Lucilia sericata (green bottle fly) or Chrysomya bezziana if available
-   **Historical evidence:** Used extensively in military medicine (WWI) and remains effective today
-   **How they work:**
    -   Maggots secrete enzymes that dissolve dead tissue
    -   They ingest only liquefied necrotic tissue
    -   They preferentially avoid living tissue
    -   Secondary benefit: maggot secretions have antimicrobial properties
-   **Application:**
    -   If sterile larvae available: place directly in wound cavity
    -   Typically 150-300 larvae per treatment
    -   Cover with permeable dressing (allows oxygen, contains larvae)
    -   Leave 3-5 days; maggots actively feed
    -   Remove larvae (they will be much larger) and dead tissue together
-   **Advantage:** Extremely effective; selective for dead tissue only; antimicrobial; dramatically accelerates healing
-   **Limitation:** Requires access to sterile larvae; not typically available except in specialized settings
-   **Obtaining sterile larvae:** If infection in remote location and maggots naturally arriving in wound, ensure they are from non-wound-infecting species; uncertain species is risky

### Enzymatic Debridement

Use of topical enzymes (if available) to dissolve dead tissue:

-   **Enzymes:** Collagenase, papain, or bromelain
-   **Application:** Spread enzyme paste directly on necrotic tissue
-   **Timing:** Leave 12-24 hours before changing dressing
-   **Limitation:** Unlikely to have enzymatic debridement agents in field settings

### Signs of Adequate Debridement

-   All gray, black, or obviously dead tissue removed
-   Wound edges are red and bleeding or have pink appearance
-   No foul smell (infected dead tissue smells bad)
-   Wound base is clean and moist

## 7\. Fracture Management

Fracture management prevents bleeding from bone, reduces pain, and prevents further injury. Improper fracture care leads to permanent disability or amputation.

### Closed Reduction (For Simple Fractures)

Realigning a broken bone without surgery:

#### Closed Reduction Steps

1.  **Assess fracture:**
    -   Identify fracture type: simple (straight break) vs. comminuted (multiple fragments)
    -   Simple fractures are candidates for closed reduction
    -   Comminuted fractures are extremely difficult; professional orthopedic surgery needed if possible
2.  **Pain control:**
    -   Anesthesia is essential for reduction; pain causes muscle spasm that opposes realignment
    -   Local anesthesia if fracture is in extremity
    -   General anesthesia (if available and safe) if multiple fractures or trunk fracture
3.  **Traction:**
    -   Apply gentle, steady traction along length of limb to restore alignment
    -   For arm: pull arm along its long axis
    -   For leg: pull leg along its long axis; may need assistant to stabilize body
    -   Traction reduces muscle spasm and helps align fracture ends
4.  **Realignment:**
    -   While maintaining traction, carefully manipulate bone ends back into alignment
    -   Feel for "clunk" or sense that bone ends have engaged
    -   Confirm alignment through palpation (feel) and comparison with opposite side (other arm/leg)
    -   Do not force realignment—stop if excessive resistance
5.  **Immobilization:**
    -   Immediately splint fracture to prevent re-displacement
    -   Do not release traction until fully immobilized

### Splinting Materials and Techniques

#### Padded Board Splint (Simple, Effective)

-   **Materials:** Wooden board or stick, cloth padding, tape or cloth strips for securing
-   **Padding:** Wrap cloth around board to prevent pressure injuries (important!)
-   **Length:** Extend the board from above the joint above fracture to below the joint below fracture
-   **Positioning:** Place against fracture; secure with strips of cloth or tape across joints, not directly over fracture
-   **Immobility:** Ensure zero movement at the fracture site

#### SAM Splint (Improvised Aluminum/Foam)

-   **Concept:** Rollable foam-backed aluminum sheet; can be molded to any shape
-   **Improvisation:** Laminate aluminum foil to foam insulation sheet with adhesive; roll and shape as needed
-   **Advantage:** Molds to irregular shapes better than flat board
-   **Application:** Roll around fracture site; secure with tape or cloth strips

#### Plaster or Fiberglass Casting

-   **Plaster of Paris (if available):**
    -   Mix plaster powder with water to paste consistency
    -   Soak fabric strips or cloth in plaster mix
    -   Wrap around splint in overlapping layers
    -   Allow to harden (typically 30-60 minutes)
    -   Once hard, creates rigid mold of fracture
-   **Cloth and Plaster Wrap:**
    -   Simpler version: wrap cloth around padded splint, soak with plaster, allow to dry
    -   Creates less-rigid but functional immobilization

### Traction Splint for Femur (Thighbone) Fractures

Femur fractures cause life-threatening bleeding into thigh; traction pulls muscle and reduces bleeding:

-   **Indication:** Mid-shaft femur fracture (fracture of thigh bone)
-   **Goal:** Apply longitudinal traction to realign bone and reduce muscle tension that causes bleeding
-   **Technique:**
    -   Position patient supine (on back)
    -   Apply traction to injured leg: assistant pulls foot/ankle distally (downward) with steady force
    -   Create traction device: improvised frame from branches or pipe with sling for foot
    -   Maintain traction continuously (critical—cannot be released)
    -   Once traction achieved, immobilize frame and leg together
-   **Improvised traction splint:**
    -   Create frame from branches or pipe forming triangle or ladder
    -   Attach foot sling (cloth sling suspending foot)
    -   Sling positioned to apply steady downward pull (weight of foot provides some traction)
    -   Add additional weight (improvised counterweight) if available to increase traction

### Open Fractures (Bone Breaks Through Skin)

Open fractures are surgical emergencies—bone exposed to contamination:

-   **Priorities:**
    1.  Control bleeding (tourniquet if necessary)
    2.  Irrigate wound thoroughly with sterile solution—remove debris, dirt, contamination
    3.  Reduce fracture if possible (realign bone, attempt to pull bone back under skin)
    4.  Splint and immobilize immediately
    5.  Antibiotics critical—start immediately if available
    6.  Prevention of infection is key—infection in bone (osteomyelitis) is devastating
-   **Infection risk:** Open fractures have very high infection risk; aggressive antibiotic therapy essential

### Complications to Watch For

-   **Bleeding:** Fracture edges can lacerate vessels; apply pressure and tourniquet if necessary
-   **Nerve injury:** Loss of sensation or motor function below fracture suggests nerve damage; note baseline function
-   **Compartment syndrome:** Swelling in muscle compartment compresses blood vessels; causes pain, pallor, pulselessness—serious; may require fasciotomy (surgical opening of compartment)
-   **Fat embolism:** Fat from bone marrow enters bloodstream; causes shortness of breath, confusion; difficult to treat

## 8\. Emergency Amputation

Amputation is ABSOLUTELY the last resort. Only consider amputation when the limb is beyond salvage, cannot possibly heal, and retention of the limb threatens patient's life. Amputation is traumatic, has high infection risk, and leaves permanent disability. Attempt all other interventions first.

### When Amputation Becomes Necessary

Only these three scenarios justify emergency amputation:

1.  **Crush injury with no remaining blood flow:**
    -   Limb completely crushed; no sensory or motor function; no capillary refill (color return after pressing on skin)
    -   Complete arterial and venous disruption
    -   Irreversible ischemia (tissue death from loss of blood)
    -   Keeping limb would lead to massive infection, sepsis, death
2.  **Gas gangrene progressing toward core:**
    -   Clostridium perfringens infection spreading upward
    -   Visible progression (line of demarcation moving proximally toward trunk)
    -   Crepitus (gas in tissue) moving above fracture or amputation line
    -   Severe systemic toxicity despite antibiotics
    -   Amputation above line of visible infection is only chance
3.  **Completely mangled, unsalvageable limb:**
    -   Multiple fractures, massive soft tissue loss, vascular disruption
    -   Cannot physically be repaired; no functional recovery possible
    -   Remaining limb becomes gangrenous
    -   Infections from necrotic tissue threaten life

**DO NOT amputate for:** Bleeding alone (apply tourniquet), bone fracture (splint and reduce), nerve injury, simple gangrene of distal toe/finger (may demarcate and auto-amputate), pain alone

### Pre-Amputation Preparation

-   **Anesthesia:** As much pain control as possible; general if safe; local infiltration minimum
-   **Tourniquet:** Apply proximal (above) amputation site; tight, occludes blood flow completely
-   **Sterile field:** Minimal contamination is critical
-   **Instruments:** Sharp scalpel, bone saw (or sharpened knife), forceps, clamps, sutures, needle holder
-   **Blood loss management:** Have cloth to absorb blood; may need additional pressure and hemostats

### Amputation Technique - Flap Method (Preferred)

#### Steps for Lower Amputation (Leg or Foot)

1.  **Incision planning:**
    -   Plan to create anterior and posterior skin flaps that will cover stump
    -   Make flaps equal length (typically slightly longer than stump diameter)
    -   Make flaps generous enough to provide padding over stump end
2.  **Anterior flap incision:**
    -   Start incision at medial (inner) side of limb at chosen amputation level
    -   Cut diagonally across anterior (front) of limb to lateral (outer) side
    -   Length of flap extends beyond amputation level (typically 1.5x stump diameter)
3.  **Posterior flap incision:**
    -   Return to medial side, continue incision posteriorly (toward back)
    -   Cut around back of limb to lateral side
    -   Make posterior flap approximately the same length as anterior flap
4.  **Soft tissue dissection:**
    -   Retract skin and flaps back with clamps
    -   Cut muscle, fascia, and other soft tissue at amputation level
    -   Use sharp strokes with scalpel; avoid sawing or crushing tissue
5.  **Vessel ligation:**
    -   As major vessels are exposed, clamp with hemostats
    -   Tie off vessels with suture to prevent post-operative bleeding
    -   Major vessels (artery and vein) must be ligated
    -   Leave suture ends long for easy identification if bleeding resumes
6.  **Nerve handling:**
    -   Major nerves will be cut (motor and sensory)
    -   Ideal to cut nerves above amputation level so they retract (shortens phantom limb pain)
    -   Crush nerve slightly with clamp before cutting to dull it (reduces nerve signaling)
    -   Tie suture around nerve, then cut below
7.  **Bone amputation:**
    -   Use saw (bone saw, hacksaw, or sharpened knife) to cut bone
    -   Saw perpendicular (90°) to bone long axis
    -   Multiple passes with steady pressure is more effective than trying to cut through in one motion
    -   Cut bone slightly shorter than soft tissue (so soft tissue flaps can cover bone end)
8.  **Bone end preparation:**
    -   File or grind bone end smooth; no sharp edges or fracture spikes
    -   Sharp bone will erode skin and cause stump ulceration
    -   Use file, rasp, or sandstone; smooth until no sharp edges remain
9.  **Stump closure:**
    -   Bring anterior and posterior flaps together over bone end
    -   Suture flaps with interrupted stitches; technique same as wound closure
    -   No gaping; bring edges together snugly
    -   Stitches spaced 5-7mm apart

### Upper Extremity Amputation (Arm or Hand)

-   **Differences from lower extremity:**
    -   Upper extremity has more soft tissue (less bone); flaps easier to create
    -   Major blood vessels (brachial artery, basilic and cephalic veins) are easily identifiable
    -   Median and ulnar nerves must be identified and ligated
-   **General technique:** Same flap principle applies; technique adjusted for anatomy

### Post-Amputation Stump Care

-   **Dressing:** Sterile gauze covered with clean cloth; secure with tape
-   **Positioning:** Elevate stump to reduce swelling; avoid dependent position initially
-   **Pain management:** Opium or herbal pain relief as available
-   **Infection watch:** Fever, drainage, foul smell, or spreading redness indicates infection
-   **Healing timeline:** Stitches out at 14-21 days; stump takes 4-8 weeks for initial healing
-   **Phantom pain:** Patient will experience pain in amputated limb (normal); use distraction, pain management
-   **Future prosthetics:** Well-formed stump with adequate soft tissue coverage is critical for successful prosthetic fitting

## 8A. Complications & When to Stop Surgery

**Intraoperative complications** (problems during surgery) can be life-threatening. Knowing when to stop and when to continue separates experienced surgeons from dangerous ones.

### Major Intraoperative Complications

**Uncontrolled bleeding:**
- If you cannot identify the bleeding source within 2-3 minutes, stop trying
- Pack wound firmly with gauze; maintain pressure for 5+ minutes
- Apply tourniquet proximal to bleeding site if on extremity
- Do not continue surgery if bleeding prevents visualization
- Excessive blood loss causes shock and death

**Inadvertent organ perforation:**
- If you puncture bowel, bladder, or other organ, do not panic
- Stop current procedure
- Irrigate area thoroughly to remove fecal or urine contamination
- Attempt primary closure if you can safely do so
- Extensive antibiotic coverage essential post-op
- Patient at extreme risk for peritonitis and sepsis

**Discovery of unexpected massive pathology:**
- Abdominal case where you find widespread cancer, massive infection, or complex pathology beyond your ability to handle
- STOP. Do not attempt definitive treatment
- Close abdomen, manage supportively, defer to professional surgeon if transport possible
- Operating beyond your skill level kills the patient

:::danger
Know your limits. If you encounter bleeding you cannot control, pathology you don't recognize, or anatomic disruption beyond simple closure, STOP OPERATING. Close what you can safely close and get patient to professional care or manage supportively. Proceeding blindly causes death.
:::

### When NOT to Operate (Medical Management Instead)

Some surgical-appearing conditions improve with medical management and don't need surgery:

| Condition | Why It May Improve Without Surgery | Red Flag for Surgery Needed |
|-----------|-----------------------------------|-----------------------------|
| **Appendicitis** | Early cases may resolve with bowel rest, fluids, antibiotics | Perforated appendix (acute abdomen, severe sepsis) |
| **Bowel obstruction** | Partial obstructions may resolve with nasogastric tube, fluids | Complete obstruction with peritonitis |
| **Pneumothorax** | Small pneumothorax may reabsorb without drainage | Tension pneumothorax (respiratory distress, hemodynamic collapse) |
| **Abscess** | May rarely drain spontaneously or respond to antibiotics alone | Rapidly enlarging abscess or spread despite antibiotics |
| **Laceration** | Small lacerations may heal with glue or bandaging | Deep lacerations with gaping or neurovascular involvement |

## 9\. Chest Procedures

Chest emergencies require rapid intervention. Tension pneumothorax can cause death in minutes if untreated. Attempt these procedures only after specific training or when the patient is clearly dying.

### Tension Pneumothorax (Air in Chest Cavity)

Air in the pleural space (between lung and chest wall) collapses lung and compresses heart:

#### Recognition of Tension Pneumothorax

-   **Presentation:**
    -   Severe shortness of breath, unable to speak
    -   Chest pain, especially on side of pneumothorax
    -   Tachycardia (rapid heart rate)
    -   Hypotension (very low blood pressure) - this is late finding
    -   Unilateral (one-sided) decreased breath sounds or silence on exam
    -   JVD (jugular vein distension in neck)
    -   Tracheal deviation (airway pushed to opposite side)
-   **Cause:**
    -   Penetrating chest trauma (stab wound, gunshot)
    -   Blunt chest trauma with rib fractures
    -   Spontaneous pneumothorax (rare in field setting)

![Field Surgery &amp; Advanced Wound Care diagram 2](../assets/svgs/surgery-field-2.svg)

Tension Pneumothorax: Recognition and needle decompression landmarks showing 2nd intercostal space at midclavicular line

#### Needle Decompression Technique

1.  **Identify side:** Decreased breath sounds indicate pneumothorax side; needle goes in THIS side
2.  **Position:** Patient sitting upright or semi-recumbent if possible; expose chest
3.  **Landmarks:**
    -   **Intercostal space:** 2nd intercostal space (space between 2nd and 3rd ribs)
    -   **Anterior-posterior line:** Midclavicular line (imaginary vertical line through center of clavicle/collarbone)
    -   **Point of insertion:** Where 2nd intercostal space crosses midclavicular line
4.  **Needle selection:**
    -   **Preferred:** 14-gauge catheter needle (large bore) for rapid decompression
    -   **Alternative:** 18-gauge needle if 14G not available
    -   **Length:** Needle must be long enough to pass through chest wall into pleural space (typically 3-5cm for thin patient, deeper for obese)
5.  **Preparation:**
    -   Skin preparation: clean with alcohol if available; minimize time
    -   Local anesthesia: ice or topical alcohol if time allows
6.  **Needle insertion:**
    -   Hold needle perpendicular to chest wall
    -   Insert directly through skin into 2nd intercostal space at midclavicular line
    -   Aim slightly upward (toward superior rib) to avoid vessels in inferior rib groove
    -   Push needle with steady pressure until you feel "pop" as needle enters pleural space
    -   Immediate rush of air confirms diagnosis; air will hiss out loudly
7.  **Needle advancement:**
    -   Advance catheter while withdrawing needle (if using catheter needle set)
    -   Leave catheter in place; can tape to chest if needed for continued drainage
    -   Tape catheter hub; can leave uncapped for continued air escape (acts as one-way valve)
8.  **Confirmation:**
    -   Patient will feel immediate relief of shortness of breath
    -   Breath sounds will become audible on previously silent side
    -   Heart rate and blood pressure will improve

### Chest Seal for Open Pneumothorax

If patient has open wound to chest ("sucking" chest wound):

-   **Occlusive dressing:**
    -   Use plastic wrap, foil, or waterproof material
    -   Tape dressing on 3 sides, leaving one side untaped as "flutter valve"
    -   Air can escape around untaped edge on exhalation; prevents air from entering on inhalation
    -   Create flutter valve: lift edge of dressing away from chest if patient in respiratory distress
-   **Alternative:** Tape plastic completely if no tension pneumothorax develops; remove after several hours to reassess

### Chest Tube Concepts (Drainage of Fluid/Air)

For reference; rarely possible in field setting:

-   **Indication:** Hemothorax (blood in chest cavity) or large pneumothorax requiring ongoing drainage
-   **Concept:** Large catheter inserted between ribs into pleural space; connected to drainage system that allows fluid/air to escape but prevents air from re-entering
-   **Improvised drainage:**
    -   Use large needle or catheter with tubing attached
    -   Place tubing into container of water (water-seal); air bubbles out, but water blocks re-entry
-   **Practical reality:** Very difficult without proper equipment; needle decompression is simpler and sufficient in most emergencies

## 10\. Abdominal Emergencies

Abdominal surgery without proper training and sterile environment is extremely dangerous. Organ perforation, inadequate closure, massive infection are common results. Only consider abdominal surgery if patient will certainly die without it.

### Acute Appendicitis (Appendix Inflammation)

#### Recognition of Appendicitis

-   **Classic presentation:**
    -   Abdominal pain starting at umbilicus (belly button), later localizing to right lower quadrant
    -   Nausea and vomiting, appetite loss
    -   Low-grade fever (99-101°F / 37.2-38.3°C)
    -   Diarrhea or constipation
-   **Physical examination:**
    -   **McBurney's point:** Tenderness at point located 1/3 distance from anterior superior iliac spine (hip bone) to umbilicus, on right side
    -   **Rebound tenderness:** Pain is worse when examiner presses on abdomen then suddenly releases (rebound causes pain from peritoneal irritation)
    -   **Guarding:** Patient's abdominal muscles involuntarily tighten when area is touched (defense against peritonitis)
    -   **Positive Rovsing's sign:** Pain in right lower abdomen when pressure applied to left lower abdomen

![Field Surgery &amp; Advanced Wound Care diagram 3](../assets/svgs/surgery-field-3.svg)

McBurney's Point location: 1/3 distance from ASIS to umbilicus on right side; site of maximal tenderness in appendicitis

#### Appendectomy - Concept Only (Extremely Dangerous Without Training)

Appendectomy is ONLY reference information. Abdominal surgery without proper training is almost certainly fatal from septic peritonitis. Consider appendectomy only if patient will absolutely certainly die from appendicitis and no transport to professional care is possible.

-   **Decision point:**
    -   Patient has clear appendicitis signs
    -   No professional surgical care available
    -   Appendix will rupture (acute abdomen, worsening fever, rebound tenderness)
    -   Rupture will cause peritonitis and death
    -   Patient and any medical advisor agree risk of surgery less than risk of rupture
-   **Basic concept (not instructions):**
    -   Incision in right lower abdomen over McBurney's point
    -   Identify appendix and caecum (beginning of large intestine)
    -   Ligate (tie) blood vessels supplying appendix
    -   Remove appendix while minimizing spillage of appendiceal contents
    -   Close abdomen in layers: peritoneum, fascia, skin
    -   Massive infection risk; aggressive antibiotics essential post-op
-   **Reality:** This is extremely difficult procedure; contamination and inadequate closure are almost inevitable without training

### Evisceration (Intestines Protruding Through Wound)

Emergency situation where abdominal organs extrude through open wound:

#### Immediate Management

-   **DO NOT:** Try to push organs back in; will contaminate peritoneal cavity and cause infection
-   **DO:** Keep organs moist and covered
    -   Cover exposed organs with moist sterile cloth or gauze
    -   Soak cloth with sterile solution (boiled water, saline, or iodine solution)
    -   Keep organs moist; drying causes tissue damage
-   **Position:** Patient semi-recumbent (head and torso elevated 30-45°) or on side; this reduces further extrusion
-   **Splinting:** Keep the patient still; immobilize entire torso
-   **Transport:** Urgent transport to surgeon is only appropriate treatment; patient will die if organs not replaced in sterile surgical environment
-   **Prevention of further contamination:**
    -   Do not touch exposed organs except trained surgical team
    -   Keep area clean; minimize air exposure
    -   If organs must be touched, hands must be as sterile as possible

### Abdominal Wound Closure Principles

If abdominal wound must be closed in field setting:

-   **Critical layers (must close in order):**
    1.  Peritoneum (innermost lining) - if visible, use absorbable suture if available
    2.  Fascia (tough connective tissue) - must be closed to prevent hernia; use strong suture
    3.  Skin - final layer, visible stitches
-   **Tension:** Fascial closure must not be under excessive tension; may require drainage
-   **Antibiotics:** Critical; begin immediately and continue for extended course

### Other Abdominal Emergencies (Reference)

-   **Perforation (hole) in GI tract:** Contamination of peritoneal cavity; almost invariably fatal without surgery; keep NPO (nothing by mouth), antibiotics, fluid support
-   **Acute pancreatitis:** Inflammation of pancreas; causes severe epigastric pain; treatment is supportive (NPO, fluids, pain management)
-   **Bowel obstruction:** Blockage of intestines; causes pain, vomiting, abdominal distension; may resolve with nasogastric tube and fluids, or require surgery
-   **Ruptured aneurysm:** Tear in abdominal blood vessel; massive bleeding; almost certainly fatal; keep patient calm, warm, lying down, await death or hope for transport

## 12. Field Adaptations

Field conditions are never ideal. These adaptations help you succeed with limited resources.

### When You Don't Have Ideal Equipment

**No sterile gloves:**
- Wash hands surgically, dry on sterile cloth
- Wear clean cloth gloves; tie at wrist with tape or string
- Better than bare hands, though not ideal

**No professional surgical instruments:**
- Use items listed in Section 2 (improvised instruments)
- Boil everything for 20 minutes minimum
- Keep instruments organized on sterile cloth by function (cutting, clamping, retracting)

**No IV fluids for shock:**
- Place patient supine (lying flat) with legs elevated
- Keep warm; prevent heat loss
- Oral hydration if patient conscious and able to swallow
- If available, oral rehydration salts mixed in boiled water

**No antibiotics:**
- Infection prevention becomes even more critical
- Aggressive sterilization, gentle tissue handling, meticulous closure
- Herbal antimicrobials if available (garlic, honey, plantain)
- High mortality expected if major surgery and infection develops

**No suture material:**
- Use silk thread, fishing line, or other non-absorbable materials boiled 20+ minutes
- Smaller wounds can be closed with butterfly strips or staples
- Deep wounds may need packing instead of primary closure

### Extreme Environments

**High altitude (>8000 feet):**
- Increased risk of infection (lower immune response)
- Slower healing (decreased oxygen)
- Anesthesia may behave unpredictably
- Keep patient warm and well-oxygenated

**Extreme cold:**
- Hypothermia impairs immunity and healing
- Circulation to extremities diminished; risk of compartment syndrome increases
- Wound may freeze; cover with insulation
- Infection risk increases dramatically

**High heat/humidity:**
- Infection risk increases
- Wound may become saturated with sweat; change dressings more frequently
- Hydration critical—patient loses fluids rapidly

:::tip
Improvisation is not failure—it's adaptation. Experienced field surgeons in remote locations routinely succeed with improvised equipment. The most important factors are sterility, good technique, and vigilant post-operative care—not equipment sophistication.
:::

</section>

## Regional Anesthesia: Nerve Block Techniques

Nerve blocks provide localized anesthesia for procedures without requiring general anesthesia or IV medications. Invaluable in field settings where intravenous access or airway management difficult.

### Digital Block (Finger/Toe Procedures)

**Indications:** Laceration repair, abscess drainage, nail removal from fingers or toes

**Anatomy:** Two digital nerves run along the sides of each digit; local anesthetic blocks pain.

**Technique:**
1. **Position:** Patient's hand/foot supine, fingers/toes extended
2. **Landmark:** Identify the web space between digits (proximal junction where two digits meet)
3. **Injection site:** Two injections per digit:
   - First injection: Insert needle at web space on one side of digit, advance perpendicular to digit approximately 0.5-1 cm, inject 2-3 mL 1% lidocaine or mepivacaine
   - Second injection: Repeat on opposite side of digit, same depth and volume
4. **Onset:** 5-10 minutes; test by gentle pinching (should be painless)
5. **Duration:** 30-60 minutes depending on anesthetic

**Precautions:**
- Do NOT use epinephrine with local anesthetic in digits (causes vasoconstriction, tissue necrosis risk)
- Do NOT use >10 mL total volume per digit (toxicity risk)
- Avoid nerve damage by gentle needle insertion and no paresthesias (tingling) during injection—if paresthesia felt, withdraw slightly and redirect

### Wrist Block (Hand/Forearm Procedures)

**Indications:** Hand/wrist laceration repair, fracture reduction, small surgical procedures on hand/forearm

**Anatomy:** Three main nerves: radial (thumb/index side of forearm), median (palm/fingers), ulnar (pinky side); multiple branches allow comprehensive coverage.

**Simplified Three-Injection Approach:**

1. **Median Nerve Block:**
   - Landmark: Wrist crease; identify palmaris longus tendon (visible when patient flexes fingers, stands out on palm side of wrist)
   - Inject: Just medial to palmaris longus, 0.5-1 cm deep, inject 5 mL lidocaine
   - Area blocked: Thumb, index, middle, lateral half of ring finger

2. **Ulnar Nerve Block:**
   - Landmark: Wrist crease; identify ulnar artery/nerve bundle on medial (pinky) side of wrist
   - Inject: Just medial to ulnar artery (feel pulse), inject 5 mL lidocaine at same wrist level
   - Area blocked: Pinky, medial half of ring finger

3. **Radial Nerve Block:**
   - Landmark: Wrist crease; lateral (thumb) side
   - Inject: Subcutaneous infiltration along radial side of wrist (1-2 cm above wrist crease, lateral forearm), inject 5-10 mL lidocaine in continuous line
   - Area blocked: Dorsal (back) surface of thumb and hand

**Total volume:** 15-20 mL 1% lidocaine total
**Onset:** 10-15 minutes

### Ankle Block (Foot/Ankle Procedures)

**Indications:** Foot laceration repair, toe/foot procedures, ankle injury manipulation

**Anatomy:** Five nerves supply foot; practical approach blocks main nerves at ankle level.

**Five-Injection Ankle Block:**

1. **Posterior Tibial Nerve:**
   - Behind medial malleolus (bump on inner ankle)
   - Inject: Just behind medial malleolus, 1-2 cm deep, 5 mL lidocaine
   - Area: Sole of foot

2. **Sural Nerve:**
   - Behind lateral malleolus (bump on outer ankle)
   - Inject: Just behind lateral malleolus, 1-2 cm deep, 5 mL lidocaine
   - Area: Outer/lateral foot surface

3. **Deep Peroneal Nerve:**
   - Between tibia and fibula on front (anterior) ankle
   - Inject: Anterior ankle crease, between tibia/fibula bones, 5 mL lidocaine
   - Area: Dorsal (top) foot, space between big toe and second toe

4. **Superficial Peroneal Nerve:**
   - Subcutaneous on front/lateral lower leg
   - Inject: Subcutaneous infiltration from anterior to lateral ankle (1-2 cm above ankle crease), 5-10 mL lidocaine in continuous line
   - Area: Dorsal surface of foot

5. **Saphenous Nerve:**
   - Medial (inner) lower leg
   - Inject: Subcutaneous infiltration along medial ankle/lower leg, 5 mL lidocaine
   - Area: Inner ankle/foot

**Total volume:** 25-35 mL 1% lidocaine
**Onset:** 15-20 minutes

### General Nerve Block Safety

**Complications to Avoid:**
- **Intravascular injection (injecting into blood vessel):** Causes seizures, cardiac arrhythmias from local anesthetic toxicity. Aspirate (withdraw plunger slightly) before injecting to confirm no blood return.
- **Nerve damage:** Excessive pressure, large needle bore, or repeated needle passes damage nerves. Use gentle technique; if paresthesia felt, withdraw and redirect.
- **Local anesthetic toxicity:** Excessive volume or concentration. Calculate total dose: volume (mL) × concentration (%) = total grams. Lidocaine max 300 mg (30 mL of 1%), mepivacaine similar limit.

**Signs of Toxicity:**
- Tinnitus (ringing in ears), perioral numbness (early signs)
- Seizures, loss of consciousness (severe)
- Cardiovascular collapse (rare, usually with higher concentrations)

**Management:** Stop injecting, monitor airway, manage seizures with airway support and benzodiazepines if available, ensure IV access for fluid resuscitation.

## Escharotomy: Emergency Relief of Circumferential Burns

Circumferential burns (burns going all the way around a limb or torso) cause tissue swelling that restricts blood flow, leading to tissue death if not relieved. Escharotomy (surgical incision through burned tissue to relieve pressure) restores circulation.

### Recognition of Circumferential Burns

**Visual Findings:**
- Burn extends completely around limb, digit, or torso
- Often deep (3rd or 4th degree)—charred, leathery appearance
- If not relieved: limb becomes cold, cyanotic (blue), then gangrenous (black)

**Clinical Signs of Compartment Syndrome (Tissue Pressure Crisis):**
- Severe pain
- Cool/cold limb (despite surrounding tissues warm)
- Cyanosis/blue coloration of distal limb
- Loss of distal pulses (wrist or foot pulse disappears)
- Weakness or paralysis of distal muscles
- Sensory loss (inability to feel touch)

**Timeline:** Tissue death begins after 6-8 hours of reduced blood flow; permanent damage likely after 12-24 hours.

:::danger
Circumferential burns causing limb ischemia are true surgical emergencies. Tissue loss becomes inevitable without escharotomy. Once signs of ischemia apparent, escharotomy must be done within 1-2 hours.
:::

### Escharotomy Technique

**Setup:**
- Clean burned area as much as possible
- Use sterile technique (gloves, instruments boiled 20 minutes)
- No anesthesia possible in 3rd/4th degree burns (nerve destruction); no pain felt
- For distal limbs: anesthesia may help; consider nerve block if time permits

**Incision Placement:**

**For Limb Escharotomy (Arm or Leg):**
1. Make longitudinal (lengthwise) incision through burned tissue along midaxial line (center line running lengthwise through limb)
2. Incision depth: Cut through burned tissue only; stop at layer of viable (non-burned) tissue beneath
3. Length: Run full length of circumferential burn (from joint above to joint below)
4. Second incision optional: If circumferential and deep, make second incision 180 degrees opposite first (both lateral and medial, or both anterior and posterior) to relieve pressure completely

**For Torso Escharotomy:**
1. Make bilateral (both sides) longitudinal incisions along midaxillary lines (sides of chest)
2. Extend from axilla (armpit) to lower ribs
3. Additional incision: May extend escharotomy onto abdomen if circumferential burn extends inferiorly

**Postoperatively:**
- Expect significant bleeding (viable tissue below burn will bleed); apply pressure with cloth
- Charred tissue from escharotomy will separate over days/weeks as body sheds dead tissue
- Monitor distal pulses after escharotomy (should return)
- Aggressive fluid resuscitation continues (burn injuries require massive IV fluids)
- Infection prevention critical (open wounds post-escharotomy)

**Success Indicator:** Return of distal pulse (wrist/foot) within 30 minutes of escharotomy indicates relief of compartment syndrome. If pulse does not return, additional incisions may be needed or deeper compartment syndrome exists.

## Emergency Cricothyrotomy (Emergency Airway Bypass)

Cricothyrotomy is emergency surgical airway creation when standard airway management impossible (patient choking, facial trauma obscuring airway, severe swelling). This is true last-resort procedure—only when patient will die without it.

### Indications

- Airway completely obstructed (foreign body, swelling, trauma) preventing bag-mask ventilation
- Unable to intubate due to facial trauma or anatomic obstruction
- Patient dying from airway emergency and no other option available

### Anatomy Review

**Cricothyroid Membrane:** Thin membrane between cricoid cartilage (below) and thyroid cartilage (above). Highly vascular but relatively avascular midline. This is the target for needle/surgical entry.

**Palpation Landmarks:**
1. Locate thyroid cartilage (Adam's apple in males, smaller in females)
2. Locate cricoid cartilage (ring-shaped, below thyroid cartilage)
3. Feel space between them (approximately 1-2 cm)—this is cricothyroid membrane

### Needle Cricothyrotomy (Temporary Bridge)

Provides emergency airway for minutes to hours, enough time to get patient to definitive care.

**Technique:**
1. Position patient supine; extend neck (hyperextend if no cervical spine injury concern)
2. Identify cricothyroid membrane by palpation
3. Sterilize skin if time permits
4. Insert large-bore needle (14 gauge ideal, use anything available) through skin, directly through cricothyroid membrane into trachea
5. Advance needle until air bubbles felt when withdrawing plunger
6. Thread catheter over needle; withdraw needle leaving catheter in place
7. Attach to any oxygen source if available; provide high-flow oxygen or hand-ventilate with syringe/bag

**Complications:** Risk of blood vessel puncture (causes hemorrhage into airway), esophageal placement (ventilation ineffective, stomach inflates), infection if not sterile.

**Duration:** Provides temporary ventilation for 20-30 minutes, sufficient time to either resolve airway obstruction or perform surgical airway.

### Surgical Cricothyrotomy (Definitive Airway)

Provides secure, definitive airway; intended to remain in place longer than needle method.

**Technique:**
1. Position supine; hyperextend neck
2. Identify cricothyroid membrane landmarks
3. Sterilize skin
4. Make small (0.5-1 cm) horizontal incision through skin at cricothyroid membrane level
5. Deepen incision through skin and subcutaneous tissue down to cricothyroid membrane
6. Make horizontal incision through cricothyroid membrane (1-1.5 cm)
7. Insert endotracheal tube (if available) or improvised airway (tracheostomy tube or large-bore hollow tube) through opening into trachea
8. Secure tube with tape around neck
9. Listen to lungs bilaterally to confirm position

**Complications:**
- Subglottic stenosis (narrowing below vocal cords) if tube too large or left in too long
- Infection if not sterile
- Hemorrhage from vessels in area
- Damage to thyroid cartilage or vocal cords

**Expected Outcome:** If successful, patient can breathe and ventilate through tube; life saved if airway obstruction was only problem.

:::warning
**Perform surgical cricothyrotomy only if you have been trained. Poor technique causes hemorrhage, esophageal damage, or misplacement.** When performed by untrained hands in a true emergency, survival rate is 30-50%—but this is still better than 0% (certain death) from complete airway obstruction.
:::

</section>

## Compartment Syndrome: Recognition and Fasciotomy

Compartment syndrome occurs when pressure inside a tissue compartment (surrounded by fascia, a tough fibrous layer) rises above capillary pressure, cutting off blood flow. Results in muscle death (rhabdomyolysis), kidney failure, and potential limb loss if untreated.

### Recognition: The "5 P's" Plus Pain

**Classic "5 P's" of Compartment Syndrome:**
1. **Pain:** Severe, out of proportion to injury; pain with passive stretch (stretching muscles in affected compartment causes severe pain)
2. **Pressure:** Compartment feels tense/hard on palpation
3. **Pallor:** Pale skin over affected compartment
4. **Pulselessness:** Loss of distal pulse (late finding—indicates advanced ischemia)
5. **Paresthesias:** Tingling, numbness in distribution of nerves passing through compartment

**Critical Findings:**
- **Pain with passive stretch is the most sensitive finding** (strongest indicator of early compartment syndrome)
- Pulses may be intact even with significant compartment syndrome (late finding)
- Paresthesias and paralysis indicate advanced compartment syndrome (permanent damage likely)

### Causes in Survival Medicine

**Trauma (fractures):** Tibial (shin) fractures most common; femoral fractures, crush injuries
**Burns:** Circumferential burns cause compartment syndrome in underlying tissues (separate from escharotomy)
**Rhabdomyolysis:** Crush injury causing muscle cell breakdown (myoglobin released damages kidneys)
**Hemorrhage:** Large hematomas (blood collections) within compartments increase pressure

### Monitoring for Compartment Syndrome

**Serial Assessments (every 2-4 hours if compartment syndrome suspected):**
- Stretch muscles in affected compartment gently; compare pain response to opposite limb
- Palpate compartment for firmness/tense appearance
- Test distal pulses (radial for forearm, dorsalis pedis for foot, femoral for thigh)
- Assess sensation (normally sharp/dull discrimination possible; loss indicates nerve damage from ischemia)
- Assess motor function (strength of muscles distal to compartment)

**Escalation Triggers (Fasciotomy Needed):**
- Severe pain with passive stretch
- Tense compartment on palpation
- Progressive sensory loss or weakness
- Any combination of above despite conservative measures (elevation, ice, pain control)

### Fasciotomy Technique

Fasciotomy is emergency surgical decompression opening the fascia to relieve pressure.

**Leg Compartments (Most Common Site):**

The leg has 4 compartments: anterior, lateral, superficial posterior, deep posterior. All may require opening depending on compartment syndrome extent.

**Anterior Compartment Fasciotomy:**
1. Position supine; expose lower leg
2. Identify anterior tibia bone; mark a line 1-2 cm medial (inward) to tibia
3. Make longitudinal incision from knee to ankle, 1-2 cm medial to tibia
4. Deepen incision through skin and fascia
5. Open fascia along full length (make fasciotomy opening 8-10 cm long minimum)
6. Leave incision open (do NOT close); cover with clean dressing
7. Monitor for bleeding; apply pressure as needed

**Lateral Compartment Fasciotomy:**
1. Make incision between fibula and tibia
2. Open compartment between these bones
3. Make fasciotomy opening the full length of the compartment

**Posterior Compartments (Superficial and Deep):**
1. Make medial (inner) incision along leg
2. Separate muscle layers to access both posterior compartments
3. Open fascia of both layers

**Postoperative Management:**
- Leave wounds open (no primary closure; compartments need to decompress)
- Frequent dressing changes (daily if possible) to monitor for infection
- Aggressive fluid resuscitation (compartment syndrome associated with severe muscle breakdown—myoglobin damages kidneys; IV fluids essential to flush kidneys)
- Monitor urine color (dark brown/red urine indicates myoglobinuria—requires aggressive hydration)
- Plan for delayed closure or skin grafting once swelling subsides (typically 5-7 days)

**Expected Outcome:** If fasciotomy performed early (within 6-8 hours of compartment syndrome onset), muscle salvage likely and permanent disability avoided. If delayed >24 hours, permanent damage from muscle necrosis likely despite fasciotomy.

:::danger
**Compartment syndrome is a surgical emergency.** More than 6-8 hours of ischemia results in permanent muscle and nerve damage. If compartment syndrome suspected, perform fasciotomy emergently or the patient will lose limb function permanently.
:::

</section>

## 11. Peripheral Nerve Block Techniques (Local Anesthesia Without IV Access)

Peripheral nerve blocks provide regional anesthesia by infiltrating local anesthetic around nerves, numbing entire regions without requiring systemic anesthesia. Essential for field surgery when IV access limited or patient cannot tolerate general anesthesia.

### Digital Nerve Block (Finger/Thumb)

**Anatomy:** Each finger has four digital nerves (two on volar/palm side, two on dorsal/back side). Blocking both volar nerves anesthetizes entire finger.

**Technique:**
1. **Identify landmarks:** Palpate finger base where it joins hand. Feel for tender cord-like structures on either side of digit (these are the digital nerves)
2. **Needle position:** Insert 25-27 gauge needle perpendicular to skin on lateral (side) surface of finger base
3. **Infiltration:** Inject 5-10mL 1% lidocaine or similar local anesthetic around the digital nerve on one side (should feel resistance as needle penetrates tissues)
4. **Repeat other side:** Withdraw needle, reposition on opposite side of finger, inject same volume
5. **Avoid mistakes:** Do NOT inject into finger tip (high pressure can damage tissue); inject at digit base where space allows fluid spread
6. **Onset:** Anesthesia develops within 5-10 minutes
7. **Duration:** Typically 45-90 minutes

**Effectiveness:** Blocks pain/temperature sensation while often preserving some motor function (patient may retain weak grip). Excellent for superficial procedures (suture removal, abscess drainage, nail bed repair).

### Wrist Block (Hand Anesthesia)

Blocks three nerves (radial, ulnar, median) at wrist level provides anesthesia to entire hand.

**Median nerve block (middle/ring/index fingers, palm):**
1. **Identify landmarks:** Palpate flexor carpi radialis tendon at wrist (prominent tendon on volar wrist)
2. **Needle insertion:** Inject 8-10mL 1% lidocaine just medial (inner) to this tendon, perpendicular to skin
3. **Depth:** Penetrate approximately 0.5-1cm (should meet slight resistance as needle reaches nerve sheath)

**Ulnar nerve block (little/ring finger ulnar side, palm):**
1. **Identify landmarks:** Palpate flexor carpi ulnaris tendon at wrist (medial to radial nerve)
2. **Needle insertion:** Inject 8-10mL 1% lidocaine alongside this tendon
3. **Depth:** Similar to median (0.5-1cm)

**Radial nerve block (dorsal/back of hand):**
1. **Identify landmarks:** Radial nerve more superficial; palpate radial artery on volar wrist (lateral side)
2. **Needle insertion:** Inject 5-10mL 1% lidocaine subcutaneously along radial artery (doesn't require deep penetration)
3. **Spread:** Infiltrate in fan pattern to block nerve distribution

**Duration:** 2-3 hours typically; provides excellent anesthesia for hand surgery.

### Ankle Block (Foot Anesthesia)

Blocks five nerves around ankle provides anesthesia to entire foot.

**Posterior tibial nerve (sole of foot):**
1. **Identify landmarks:** Behind medial malleolus (bony prominence on inner ankle); palpate groove between bone and tendon
2. **Injection:** 10mL 1% lidocaine in this groove, perpendicular insertion
3. **Depth:** 1-2cm (slightly deeper than digital blocks)

**Sural nerve (lateral foot/sole):**
1. **Identify landmarks:** Between medial and lateral malleoli at ankle level
2. **Injection:** 10mL 1% lidocaine subcutaneously at this level

**Superficial peroneal nerve (dorsum/top of foot):**
1. **Identify landmarks:** On dorsum of ankle, between tibialis anterior and peroneus tendons
2. **Injection:** 10mL 1% lidocaine in subcutaneous tissue (no deep infiltration needed)

**Deep peroneal nerve (anterior/top of foot):**
1. **Identify landmarks:** Between tibialis anterior and extensor hallucis longus tendons on dorsum
2. **Injection:** 10mL 1% lidocaine perpendicular, 1cm depth

**Saphenous nerve (medial foot/ankle):**
1. **Identify landmarks:** Medial ankle at saphenous vein location
2. **Injection:** 5mL 1% lidocaine subcutaneously

**Onset/Duration:** 10-15 minutes onset; 2-3 hours duration. Excellent for foot surgery (abscess, wound repair, nail procedures).

:::warning
Nerve blocks carry risk of vascular puncture (especially radial artery block). Aspirate before injecting to verify needle not in vessel. If blood returns, withdraw and reposition. Do NOT inject if needle in artery (causes local tissue ischemia and nerve damage).
:::

## 12. Escharotomy for Circumferential Burns

Escharotomy is surgical incision through burned tissue (eschar) to relieve pressure from circumferential burns. Without this procedure, circulation to limbs/torso becomes compromised, causing tissue death and amputation risk.

### Recognition of Circumferential Burn Requiring Escharotomy

**Signs that escharotomy needed:**
- **Circumferential (360-degree) burn** to limb or torso
- **Loss of pulses distal to burn** (radial pulse in wrist disappears even though burn on upper arm)
- **Cyanosis/blackening of skin distal to burn** (tissue not perfused)
- **Severe pain disproportionate to injury** (tissue ischemia pain)
- **Paresthesias (tingling/numbness) distal to burn** (nerve ischemia)

**Timing:** Perform escharotomy within hours of recognizing compromise; waiting risks irreversible tissue death.

### Anatomical Approach - Limb Escharotomy

**Arm escharotomy:**
1. **Incision line:** Longitudinal line on medial (inner) arm, avoiding axilla (armpit) and antecubital fossa (elbow)
2. **Depth:** Cut through eschar (burned tissue) until subcutaneous fat visible; this relieves tension
3. **Length:** Entire circumference of arm as needed (may require bilateral incisions if tight)
4. **Location specifics:** Medial arm from shoulder to wrist, avoiding neurovascular bundle (on inner arm between biceps and brachialis muscles)

**Leg escharotomy:**
1. **Incision line:** Medial thigh from groin to ankle, AND lateral leg from hip to ankle (may need bilateral to fully relieve pressure)
2. **Depth:** Through eschar to subcutaneous fat (provides immediate pressure relief)
3. **Avoid:** Avoid peroneal nerve on lateral leg (just below knee)
4. **Check restoration:** After incisions, palpate pulses (should return if circulation was compromised)

**Torso escharotomy (if circumferential chest burn):**
1. **Incision lines:** Two longitudinal incisions along midaxillary line (mid-side) bilaterally from armpit to groin
2. **Additional incisions:** May need anterior/posterior incisions if severe
3. **Goal:** Relieve tension on chest wall to allow breathing (circumferential burns restrict rib cage expansion)

### Procedural Technique

1. **Prepare skin:** Cleanse eschar with alcohol/iodine
2. **Anesthesia:** Full-thickness eschar is non-sensate (already dead tissue); anesthesia often unnecessary, but use if available or if cutting near viable tissue
3. **Incision:** Use scalpel or surgical knife; make incision decisively through burned tissue
4. **Penetration:** Cut until subcutaneous fat visible (fat appears yellow/white); stop cutting when burn tissue relieved
5. **Avoid deeper structures:** Do NOT cut deeper than needed (risk of damaging muscles, nerves, vessels)
6. **Restore circulation:** Check pulses immediately distal to burn; should return if compression relieved
7. **Bleeding:** Minimal expected from eschar (dead tissue); if brisk bleeding, you've cut into viable tissue—apply pressure

**Post-escharotomy:**
- Monitor pulses frequently (hourly initially)
- If pulses lost again, compression still exists—may need additional incisions
- Cover incisions with dressings
- Prepare for evacuation/transfer (escharotomy stabilizing measure; does not treat underlying burn—patient needs burn center care)

:::warning
Circumferential burn escharotomy in field setting prevents amputation but does not eliminate burn complications (fluid loss, infection, respiratory failure). Perform procedure only if transport to facility delayed >few hours. If facility accessible within 1-2 hours, arrange evacuation instead (surgeons perform optimal escharotomy).
:::

## 13. Emergency Cricothyrotomy (Surgical Airway)

Cricothyrotomy is emergency surgical airway creation by incising membrane between cricoid and thyroid cartilage. Performed only when: airway obstructed, intubation impossible, and patient unable to ventilate (true life-or-death emergency).

### Anatomical Landmarks

**Palpable landmarks on neck (anterior):**
- **Thyroid cartilage (Adam's apple):** Most prominent anterior neck structure
- **Cricoid cartilage:** Below thyroid; firm ring-shaped cartilage
- **Cricothyroid membrane:** Space between thyroid and cricoid (approximately 5-10mm height, 10mm width)

**Technique for landmark identification:**
1. Palpate thyroid cartilage at neck midline (prominent ridge)
2. Slide finger inferiorly (downward) until reaching notch (small depression)
3. Continue past notch to next firm structure—this is cricoid cartilage
4. Space between thyroid and cricoid is cricothyroid membrane

### Surgical Technique

**Preparation:**
- **Patient positioning:** Neck extended (head tilted back) to expose anterior neck fully
- **Prepare skin:** Cleanse neck with alcohol/iodine if time allows
- **Equipment needed:** Scalpel, tracheostomy tube (size 6-8 if available) or improvised tube (plastic straw, large IV catheter with needle removed)

**Incision:**
1. **Vertical incision:** Make 1-1.5cm vertical incision through skin at midline, over cricothyroid membrane
2. **Blunt dissection:** Using gloved finger or scissor tip, open underlying tissues without cutting cartilage
3. **Identify membrane:** Palpate cricothyroid membrane; once exposed, is firm landmark

**Airway creation:**
1. **Horizontal incision:** Make small horizontal incision directly through cricothyroid membrane
2. **Size:** 5-10mm opening typically sufficient for breathing
3. **Depth:** Cut through membrane only; do NOT cut deeper (risk of damaging vocal cords below)

**Tube placement:**
1. **Insert airway:** Place tracheostomy tube or improvised tube through opening
2. **Depth:** Advance tube approximately 2-3cm through opening
3. **Cuff inflation (if available):** Inflate cuff if tube has one (seals airway against aspiration)
4. **Secure:** Tape or tie tube to neck to prevent dislodgement
5. **Ventilation:** Begin ventilation immediately if not spontaneously breathing

**Post-procedure:**
- Monitor breathing; should improve immediately
- Reassess ability to ventilate (auscultate lungs for air movement)
- Monitor for bleeding from wound
- Secure airway to prevent displacement

:::warning
Cricothyrotomy carries 1-3% mortality in trained hands; higher in untrained practitioners. Only attempt if: (1) complete airway obstruction confirmed, (2) intubation/other airway management impossible, (3) patient would die without intervention. Do NOT practice this procedure; perform only in genuine emergency.
:::

## 14. Compartment Syndrome Recognition and Fasciotomy

Compartment syndrome occurs when tissue swelling (from crush injury, fracture, severe burn) increases pressure within closed muscle compartment, compromising blood flow and nerve function. Without surgical relief (fasciotomy), tissue dies and amputation may result.

### Recognition: The Five P's

Early recognition critical; compartment syndrome progresses rapidly (tissue death in 6-12 hours if untreated).

**Clinical signs (Five P's):**
1. **Pain:** Severe, out of proportion to injury; worsens with passive stretch of muscles in compartment
2. **Paresthesias:** Tingling, numbness (nerve ischemia)
3. **Pallor:** Pale, mottled appearance of skin distal to compartment
4. **Pulselessness:** Absent pulses (late finding; means tissue death imminent)
5. **Paralysis:** Weakness or inability to move (late finding; indicates nerve/muscle damage)

**Early vs. late findings:**
- **Early (first 2-4 hours):** Pain with passive stretch, paresthesias, pain out of proportion
- **Late (4-12 hours):** Pulselessness, paralysis, pallor (at this point, tissue damage likely already started)

**Location clues:** Compartment syndrome most common in anterior leg (after crush injury or fracture), followed by forearm, hand, foot. Identify which compartment involved based on anatomy.

### Anatomy and Fasciotomy Approaches

**Leg fasciotomy (anterior tibial compartment):**
1. **Identify compartment:** Anterior leg between tibia (shin bone) and fibula (side bone)
2. **Incision site:** Longitudinal incision between tibia and fibula, approximately 2cm lateral to tibial crest
3. **Depth:** Cut through fascia (white tissue layer wrapping muscles) under skin; length of incision 20-30cm from below knee to ankle
4. **Extent:** Release entire fascia length; muscle should bulge through incision once pressure relieved

**Forearm fasciotomy (if crush injury to arm):**
1. **Incision sites:** Two longitudinal incisions on forearm—one on volar (palm) side, one on dorsal (back) side
2. **Volar:** Incision from elbow to wrist along inner forearm
3. **Dorsal:** Similar location on back of forearm
4. **Depth:** Cut through fascia; should see muscle bulge when pressure relieved

**Hand fasciotomy:**
1. **Incisions:** Longitudinal incisions in each web space between fingers
2. **Location:** From wrist to fingertips, splitting each interosseous space
3. **Depth:** Through fascia between metacarpal bones

### Procedural Technique

1. **Expose compartment:** Longitudinal incision over compartment
2. **Incise fascia:** White fascial layer beneath skin; incise longitudinally with scalpel
3. **Release:** Once incised, muscle should expand/bulge through opening
4. **Extent:** Incise entire length of compartment; may need multiple incisions if compartment extensive
5. **Check perfusion:** After fasciotomy, skin color should improve within minutes (if viable tissue)
6. **Assess neurovascular:** Recheck distal pulses and sensation; should improve if caught early

**Complications during fasciotomy:**
- **Excessive bleeding:** If cutting vascular tissue, apply direct pressure
- **Difficulty identifying compartment:** Palpate to distinguish muscle compartments; feel for tense, swollen muscle
- **Inadequate release:** If symptoms persist after fasciotomy, compartment may not be adequately opened—consider additional incisions

**Post-fasciotomy:**
- **Leave wound open:** Do NOT close incision; swelling continues, would re-compress tissue
- **Dressing:** Loose, non-constricting dressing over open fasciotomy
- **Elevation:** Elevate limb to reduce swelling
- **Monitor:** Watch for signs of improvement (pain relief within hours, skin color normalization)
- **Secondary closure:** After swelling resolves (3-7 days), skin grafting or closure may be needed

:::warning
Compartment syndrome is surgical emergency; delay in fasciotomy (>6-12 hours) results in permanent muscle/nerve death and likely amputation. If suspected, perform fasciotomy immediately. False negatives (missing compartment syndrome) more dangerous than false positives (over-treating with unnecessary fasciotomy).
:::

## 11. Common Mistakes (Troubleshooting)

**Experienced field surgeons learn from mistakes—usually catastrophic ones.** Here are the most common errors that kill patients:

### Pre-Operative Mistakes

**Inadequate sterilization:**
- Using water that was boiled once days ago
- Assuming "clean" equals "sterile"
- Mixing sterile and non-sterile instruments
- **Prevention:** Boil for full 20 minutes immediately before surgery

**Operating on wrong diagnosis:**
- Not taking time to assess before opening
- Assuming it's appendicitis without examining for other causes
- Operating for pain that might improve with medical management
- **Prevention:** Spend 30 minutes assessing if urgency allows. Ask other people for second opinion.

**Inadequate pain control:**
- Operating with insufficient local anesthesia
- Patient moving, contaminating field, causing complications
- Post-operative pain preventing healing and eating
- **Prevention:** Overdose on local anesthesia rather than underdose. Combine methods. Reassess frequently.

### Operative Mistakes

**Losing orientation in surgical field:**
- Not knowing which structure is which
- Cutting the wrong tissue
- Damaging neurovascular structures
- **Prevention:** Identify landmarks before surgery. Work slowly. Palpate tissue to verify structure.

**Inadequate hemostasis (stopping bleeding):**
- Closing wound with bleeding vessel still leaking
- Patient develops hematoma that becomes infected or causes compartment syndrome
- **Prevention:** Spend time clamping vessels. Tie them securely. Irrigate to visualize bleeding points.

**Contamination of sterile field:**
- Touching sterile instrument with non-sterile glove
- Allowing non-sterile cloth near wound
- **Prevention:** Designate one person to maintain sterile field. Everyone else keeps distance.

:::warning
The two most common errors—inadequate sterilization and wrong diagnosis—account for >50% of post-operative deaths in field settings. Master these two before attempting any surgery.
:::

### Post-Operative Mistakes

**Inadequate follow-up:**
- Dressing once then ignoring wound
- Not monitoring for fever or spreading infection
- **Prevention:** Daily wound inspection minimum. Check dressings at least daily. Monitor vital signs.

**Removing sutures too early:**
- Facial wound re-opens because sutures removed at day 3 instead of day 5
- Extremity wound gaps because sutures removed before wound stable
- **Prevention:** Follow timing guidelines. If wound appears weak, leave sutures longer.

**Ignoring signs of infection:**
- Fever brushed off as normal post-op
- Spreading redness ignored until sepsis
- **Prevention:** Any fever >101°F is concerning. Any spreading redness warrants antibiotics immediately.
