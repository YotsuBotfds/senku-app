**Important:** The safest way to reduce bites is usually not a homemade spray. Use clothing barriers, sleeping nets, fans, and water control first. If you can buy a labeled skin repellent, follow the label exactly. Do not mix random chemicals on skin.
:::

## Overview: Best Protection Comes From Layers

No single trick stops every insect. The most reliable approach is to stack several low-risk defenses:

1. Cover skin.
2. Sleep under a net.
3. Reduce standing water and breeding sites.
4. Place camp and sleeping areas away from heavy insect activity.
5. Use a labeled repellent on exposed skin only when needed.

This guide is about reducing bites in ordinary life and field conditions. It does not promise complete protection, and it does not replace local public-health advice in malaria, dengue, tick, or other disease areas.

## Clothing Barriers: The Safest First Line

Clothing is often the most effective improvised protection because it does not depend on chemicals.

### What to wear

- Long sleeves and long pants
- Closed shoes instead of sandals
- Socks that cover the ankle
- A hat or cap with a brim
- Loose clothing that still covers the skin well

### How to improve clothing protection

- Tuck pants into socks when mosquitoes or ticks are heavy.
- Tuck shirts into pants if biting insects are getting under clothing.
- Choose tightly woven cloth when possible.
- Repair holes, torn cuffs, and loose neck openings.
- Change out of sweaty clothes if possible, because sweat and body odor can attract insects.

### What clothing cannot do

- It will not stop every bite if insects can get inside the collar, cuffs, or waistband.
- Thin or loose fabric may still let mosquitoes bite through.
- Clothing does not replace a net when sleeping.

## Nets: The Best Sleep-Time Barrier

A bed net or sleep net is one of the safest ways to prevent bites at night.

### Basic rules for nets

- Tuck the edges fully under the mattress, pad, or sleeping mat.
- Make sure the net does not touch bare skin.
- Check for holes and patch them.
- Shake the net out before use so insects are not trapped inside.
- Use a crib or child sleep space that the net fully covers, with no gaps.

### If you only have one net

- Put the net over the person most at risk first: infants, children, pregnant people, or anyone with a history of strong reactions to bites.
- Use the net every night, not only in bad weather or when insects seem bad.

### What not to do with nets

- Do not burn the net or place it near open flame.
- Do not spray unknown chemicals on a net unless the product label says it is meant for that use.

## Camp Layout: Put the Sleeping Area in the Right Place

Where you sleep and cook can matter as much as what you put on your skin.

### Better camp placement

- Sleep away from standing water, marshy ground, ditches, and puddles.
- Avoid setting the bed, cot, or sleeping mat right next to brush, trash, or animal bedding.
- Keep food waste covered and removed quickly.
- Separate sleeping space from animal pens if possible.
- Keep doors, screens, and wall openings closed at dusk and dawn when biting insects are often most active.

### Useful airflow

- A moving fan can reduce biting pressure indoors.
- If there is a breeze, place the sleeping area where air moves through it rather than in a stagnant corner.
- Keep the sleeping area dry and uncluttered so insects have fewer hiding places.

### Reduce attractants near camp

- Keep sweat-soaked clothes out of sleeping areas.
- Wash or wipe off food residue and body odor when you can.
- Avoid leaving fruit, meat scraps, or garbage uncovered.

## Standing Water Reduction: Remove Breeding Sites

For mosquitoes and some other insects, standing water is the problem you can often fix most directly.

### Check these places

- Buckets, barrels, cans, tires, trays, and plant saucers
- Gutters and drain channels
- Tarps that hold rainwater
- Low spots around sheds and tents
- Leaks that create damp soil or puddles

### What to do

- Empty containers that do not need to hold water.
- Turn buckets and tubs upside down when not in use.
- Cover rain barrels and storage tanks tightly.
- Keep gutters clear.
- Fill or drain small puddles and tire ruts when possible.
- Fix leaks that create persistent wet spots.

### Important limit

- Reducing standing water helps over time, but it will not stop every insect immediately.
- If the area has a large natural water source nearby, focus on personal barriers and nets too.

## Smoke and Fire Positioning

Smoke can reduce some insects outdoors, but it is not a safe general repellent for enclosed spaces.

### Safer use

- Use smoke only outdoors or in very open, well-ventilated spaces.
- Keep smoke downwind from people whenever possible.
- Place the smoke source away from sleeping areas, food, and children.
- Use it as a short-term aid, not as your main protection.

### Do not do this

- Do not smoke up a sleeping room or small shelter.
- Do not sleep in a smoky space to chase insects away.
- Do not rely on smoke if someone has asthma, chronic cough, or breathing problems.

Smoke can irritate lungs and make breathing worse even when it seems to keep bugs down.

## Safer Repellent Choices When You Need Something on Skin

If you need a skin repellent, the lowest-risk option is usually a commercial product that is clearly labeled for skin use and used exactly as directed.

### Safer choices

- Use a labeled repellent if you can get one.
- Apply only to exposed skin.
- Reapply only as the label says.
- Wash it off when you no longer need it.
- Keep it away from eyes, mouth, and irritated or broken skin.
- Do not let children handle repellents themselves; an adult should apply it.
- Wash treated skin and clothing after returning indoors when practical.

### Important limit

Most skin repellents help with mosquitoes, ticks, and some biting flies. They do **not** solve lice or flea problems well. For lice or fleas, focus on clothing, bedding, pets, and living-space control instead of putting more repellent on skin.

### If you do not have a labeled repellent

- Use clothing barriers first.
- Use a fan or air movement indoors.
- Move the sleeping area away from breeding sites.
- Try to keep skin covered instead of inventing a homemade chemical mix.

### Plant-based ideas: use caution

Some plant odors may seem to help for a short time, but they are inconsistent and often overclaimed. A strong smell is not the same as reliable bite prevention. If you try any plant-based idea, treat it as an extra layer only, not your main defense.

## What Not to Put on Skin

Do not use these as insect repellents:

- Gasoline
- Kerosene
- Diesel fuel
- Bleach
- Ammonia
- Pesticide concentrates
- Undiluted essential oils
- Vinegar as a skin repellent
- Toothpaste
- Cooking oil as a repellent
- Urine
- Unknown herbal poultices
- Diatomaceous earth on skin

These can irritate skin, poison the person, or make the problem worse. Some of them are flammable, and some can be absorbed through the skin.

## What To Do After a Bite

If bites still happen:

- Wash the skin gently with soap and water.
- Do not scratch if you can avoid it.
- Use a cool cloth for itching and swelling.
- Watch for spreading redness, pus, fever, or increasing pain.

Seek urgent help if there is trouble breathing, facial swelling, fainting, a rapidly spreading rash, or a severe reaction.

## Practical Checklist

If insect bites are a problem today, do these in order:

1. Put on long sleeves, long pants, socks, and closed shoes.
2. Tuck clothing openings and patch gaps.
3. Sleep under a net if it is nighttime.
4. Move the bed or camp away from standing water and trash.
5. Turn over or empty water containers.
6. Use a fan or airflow if you have one.
7. Use a labeled skin repellent only if needed.
8. Avoid homemade chemical mixes on skin.
9. Do not treat lice or flea problems as if they were mosquito problems.

## When This Is Not Enough

Get medical advice if:

- A bite becomes infected.
- You have a fever after repeated insect exposure.
- The skin reaction is severe or keeps getting worse.
- A tick, flea, or other insect exposure is linked to illness.
- Someone has breathing trouble or signs of an allergic reaction.

The goal is not to make insects disappear. The goal is to make bite risk low enough that daily life stays manageable and sleep stays possible.
