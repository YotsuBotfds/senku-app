## Overview: Connector and Hardware Choices for Wood Joinery

If the frame layout, brace geometry, or joint proportions are still undecided, go to <a href="../timber-framing-joinery.html">Timber Framing and Joinery</a>. If you already know the wood joint and need the connector, fitting, fastener, or hinge, this is the right guide.

:::info-box
**Quick routing:** Use this guide when the structural layout is already chosen and the remaining decision is what hardware to make or install. For frame layout, mortise-and-tenon sizing, brace angles, load paths, and raising, see <a href="../timber-framing-joinery.html">Timber Framing and Joinery</a>.
:::

Modern construction relies on factory-manufactured fasteners (nails, bolts, screws) that are often unavailable or unreliable in off-grid scenarios. Learning to forge essential hardware (nails, bolts, hinges, latches) enables self-sufficiency in construction and repair.

![Joinery fasteners and hardware making reference diagram](../assets/svgs/joinery-fasteners-hardware-1.svg)

**Key principles:**

1. **Material recycling:** Salvaged steel (old tools, vehicle parts, scrap) provides excellent raw material
2. **Quality control:** Hand-forged hardware is often superior to low-grade industrial fasteners (fewer impurities, better fit)
3. **Specialization:** Custom hardware (non-standard sizes, unique designs) is possible via forging

**This guide covers:**
- Nail forging (cut nails, wire nails)
- Screw cutting and thread forming
- Bolt and nut production
- Rivet making and installation
- Hinge forging (strap, T, butt styles)
- Latch and catch mechanisms
- Hook and eye production
- Chain link making
- Tempering and final assembly

:::warning
Metalworking requires proper safety precautions: eye protection (sparks), hand protection (gloves for handling hot material), ventilation (forge fumes), and proper tool handling. Never rush; quality is safer than speed.
:::

## Nail Forging: Cut Nails and Wire Nails

### Cut Nails (Square/Rectangular Shank)

Cut nails are forged from flat bar stock by cutting and forming. Traditional construction heavily used cut nails; they hold wood better than round wire nails due to square shank creating mechanical grip.

**Nail sizing standards:**

| Penny Size | Length (mm) | Diameter (mm) | Typical Use |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|------------|
| **2d (10 penny)** | 25 | 2.0 | Trim, thin boards |
| **4d** | 38 | 2.5 | Small framing |
| **6d** | 40 | 2.8 | Framing, siding |
| **8d** | 65 | 3.3 | Heavy framing |
| **10d** | 75 | 3.8 | Very heavy framing |
| **16d** | 90 | 4.4 | Timber construction |

**Forging process (simplified):**

1. **Stock preparation:** Cut flat bar stock (typically 3–5mm thick, width matching nail body width)
2. **Length cutting:** Use hardy or cut hardie to cut to nail length (~25mm excess for forming)
3. **Taper head:** Heat tip white-hot, rotate and hammer to flatten slightly; create point by striking at angle
4. **Head formation:** Heat near handle, hammer up around square mandrel or hardy to form head
5. **Final shape:** Tap to refine, cool in water or oil

**Head styles:**
- **Plain head:** Flat or slightly domed
- **Rosette head:** Decorative, made by hammering around fluted mandrel
- **Clench ring:** Formed ring on shank for extra holding power (obsolete but historical)

### Wire Nails from Salvage

**Material:** Recycle old wire (fence wire, electrical wire, spring steel) into round-shank nails.

**Process:**

1. **Stock preparation:** Straighten salvaged wire (anneal if brittle, then straighten on tapered mandrel)
2. **Cut to length:** Use hardy or shears to cut wire
3. **Taper point:** Heat one end yellow-hot, hammer on rounded stake to form sharp point
4. **Head formation:** Heat opposite end, hammer around mandrel to form head

**Advantages:** Easier than cut nails (no flattening required), salvage material zero-cost.

**Disadvantage:** Slightly lower holding power than cut nails (round shank vs. square).

### Nail Tempering

Nails require tempering for hardness without brittleness.

**Process:**

1. **Quench:** Cool hot nail in oil or water (creates hard martensitic structure)
2. **Temper:** Heat to dark straw color (~200°C), identified by oxide color: straw-yellow = good hardness, blue = too soft

**Color chart (by temperature):**
```
Pale straw (160°C): Very hard, slightly brittle
Straw yellow (180–200°C): Ideal for nails (medium hardness + toughness)
Brown (220°C): Softer, more ductile
Purple (250°C): Springs
Blue (300°C): Very soft, tools
```

**Identification without temperature gauge:** Heat nail to color in forge, observe oxide film:
- Very pale = too hard (quench immediately)
- Golden straw = good for nails (let cool in air or warm water)
- Dark brown = probably too soft (reheat slightly, retemper)

## Screw Cutting and Formation

Forged screws are laborious but possible with hand tools. Most practical approach: salvage threads from old bolts/screws, reshape.

### Screw Thread Cutting (By Hand)

**Tools needed:**
- Lathe or thread-cutting die (if available)
- Taps and dies (traditional hand tools)
- Bench vise

**Process (using tap/die set):**

1. **Blank preparation:** Forge or salvage blank bolt/rod (diameter matching desired screw size)
2. **Mark length:** Mark thread-cutting length with scribe
3. **Die application:** Heat blank very slightly (warm, not hot; helps die cut without slipping), place in sturdy vise
4. **First cut:** Fit die around blank, align square to blank axis (critical), turn die clockwise (creating right-hand threads)
5. **Gradual cuts:** Multiple passes (turn die ~2–3 rotations per pass); after each pass, back off die 1/4 turn to clear chips
6. **Depth control:** Stop when thread depth reaches ~70% of blank diameter (safety margin for shank strength)

**Threading difficulty:** Manual thread cutting is very labor-intensive and requires significant skill. In off-grid contexts, it's often more practical to:
- Salvage old screws and reforge heads if needed
- Use simple screw-like fasteners (wooden dowels with spiral grooves)
- Substitute bolts with nuts + washers for quick fastening

### Screw Head Forming

**Slotted head (slot for screwdriver):**

1. **Head formation:** Heat near blank end, hammer around mandrel to form rounded/conical head
2. **Slot cutting:** Once cool, use saw or chisel to cut vertical slot (perpendicular to grain)

**Phillips head or square drive:** Requires specialized dies or lathe; impractical for hand forging.

:::tip
For off-grid fastening, prioritize bolts/nuts (easier to make, reusable) over screws. Screws are luxury fasteners; bolts are practical.
:::

<section id="bolt-nut-production">

## Bolt and Nut Production

### Bolt Forging

**Process:**

1. **Stock selection:** Use salvaged rod or bar stock (diameter matching desired bolt size)
2. **Length cutting:** Cut to bolt length + head stock (~1.5× diameter excess)
3. **Head formation:**
   - Heat end to bright cherry-red
   - Place in bolt header (if available) or shape over stake
   - Hammer radially around stake, forming hexagonal/round head
   - Refine shape with light taps
4. **Shank straightness:** Cool, place in vise, tap gently to remove bends
5. **Thread formation:** See screw-cutting section (salvage threads when possible)

**Head styles:**

- **Round head:** Simple, forged directly over rounded stake
- **Hexagonal head:** Forged in hexagonal header or shaped with side taps
- **Square head:** Traditional, shaped with four anvil hits on corners

**Standard sizes:**

| Diameter | Thread | Length (typical) | Hex Width |
|----------|--------|-----------------|-----------|
| **6mm** | M6 (1.0) | 20–40mm | 10mm |
| **8mm** | M8 (1.25) | 25–50mm | 13mm |
| **10mm** | M10 (1.5) | 30–60mm | 15mm |
| **12mm** | M12 (1.75) | 40–80mm | 18mm |

### Nut Production

Nut forging is simpler than bolt production (no threading required on exterior).

**Process:**

1. **Stock:** Use salvage rod or forge from short blank (~15mm long for M6, scales with size)
2. **Shape:**
   - Heat to bright cherry-red
   - Place in nut die/anvil pattern (hexagonal cutouts or square impressions)
   - Strike to impress hex/square form
3. **Thread tapping:**
   - Cool completely
   - Clamp in vise, apply tap (same as bolt threading)
   - Multiple passes, clearing chips between passes
   - Stop at appropriate depth (ensure bolt fits without binding)

**Nut sizing (inside diameter):**

| Bolt Size | Metric Thread | Hex Width | Thickness |
|-----------|---------------|-----------|-----------|
| **M6** | 6.0 | 10mm | 5mm |
| **M8** | 8.0 | 13mm | 6.5mm |
| **M10** | 10.0 | 15mm | 8mm |

**Tempering:** Nuts benefit from light tempering (heat to pale straw, cool in air) for toughness; they don't require extreme hardness like screws.

:::forging-temp
**Forging temperature identification (by color):**
- Black heat (barely visible) — too cool, don't forge
- Dark red (burgundy) — getting workable
- Cherry red (bright red) — excellent forging temperature
- Orange — getting hot, risk of scaling
- Yellow/white — very hot, rapid oxidation, reduced metal life
:::

## Rivet Making and Installation

Rivets are permanent fasteners (cannot be removed) suited for overlapping sheet metal or heavy-duty joints.

### Rivet Forging

**Process:**

1. **Stock:** Cut short lengths of rod (slightly larger diameter than desired rivet shaft)
2. **Head formation:** Heat to cherry-red, place rod end on stake (not anvil — to protect stake), hammer on rod tip to form head
3. **Cool:** Allow to cool slightly
4. **Trim shaft:** Cut shaft to appropriate length (slightly longer than material thickness + head height)

**Rivet head styles:**

- **Button head:** Rounded/domed (most common)
- **Flush head:** Flat or slightly countersunk
- **Cone head:** Tapered (decorative, strong)

### Rivet Installation

**Process:**

1. **Hole drilling:** Drill hole through overlapped materials; hole diameter ~0.2mm larger than rivet
2. **Insert rivet:** Heat rivet to cherry-red (100–200 degrees below quenching temperature; rivet must stay soft)
3. **Set:** Place hot rivet in hole from underneath; strike hammer blows on exposed head, deforming hot shaft to fill hole and form second head
4. **Hammer angle:** Strike at slight angle, rotating rivet periodically to form uniform head

**Skill requirement:** High — perfect timing (temperature control) and even striking produce good rivets. Cold riveting (no heating) is weaker but possible for small rivets.

**Uses:** Boiler construction, sheet metal panels, high-stress applications where bolts aren't practical.

## Hinge Forging

### Strap Hinges (Long, Heavy-Duty)

Strap hinges are the simplest hinges to forge; they're essentially two flat bars with holes and a pin joint.

**Forging process:**

1. **Leaves:** Forge two flat bars (typically 50–100mm long, 25–40mm wide, 6–8mm thick)
2. **Taper ends:** Taper one end of each leaf to rounded point (for aesthetic appeal and to prevent splinters)
3. **Hole punching:** Heat, place on hardy, punch 3–4 holes per leaf (for bolting to frame/door)
4. **Pin hole:** Larger hole at one end (12–16mm diameter) accepts pintle pin
5. **Pintle pin:** Forge round pin (12–16mm diameter, 50–100mm long) with slight flange at one end to prevent migration

**Assembly:**
- Sandwich pin between two leaves
- Pin enters hole in one leaf, sits on flange
- Allows rotation (door swing)

**Advantages:** Simple, strong, easily repaired if pin wears.

### T-Hinges (Mid-Size, Decorative)

T-hinges have horizontal leaf and vertical leaf forming "T" shape.

**Forging:**

1. **Horizontal leaf:** Flat bar (50–75mm wide, 6–8mm thick)
2. **Vertical leaf:** Bar bent into "L" or formed to vertical position (30–50mm wide)
3. **Pin joint:** Large hole at intersection allows pin to pass through both leaves
4. **Holes:** 3–4 bolt holes per leaf for fastening

**Advantage:** More visually refined than strap hinges; excellent strength.

### Butt Hinges (Small, Mortise-Mount)

Butt hinges are compact, designed for mortising into door/frame edge.

**Challenges:** Requires precision; difficult to forge without specialized dies.

**Simplified approach:**
1. Forge two small rectangular leaves (~40mm × 15mm, 4mm thick)
2. Form pin hole at one end
3. Salvage actual hinge pin from old hardware (easier than forging small pin)

**Note:** Butt hinges are more practical to salvage and reuse than to forge from scratch.

## Latches and Catch Mechanisms

### Norfolk Latch (Traditional Heavy)

Norfolk latch is a mechanical fastener using lever and rotating bolt.

**Components:**

1. **Bolt:** L-shaped rod or forged U-shape, rotates horizontally on pivot pins
2. **Lever:** Long bar pivots above bolt; lifting lever raises bolt
3. **Keeper:** Strike plate on frame catches bolt when pressed down

**Forging:**

1. **L-bolt:** Forge from rod; heat, bend 90° at appropriate point
2. **Lever bar:** Flat bar with holes for pivot pins
3. **Pivot pins:** 6–8mm diameter rods serve as hinge pins
4. **Strike plate:** Flat steel bolted to door frame, shaped to catch bolt

**Assembly:**
- Mount pivot pins to door frame
- Bolt rotates on pins below lever
- Pressing down on lever lifts bolt; releasing lets spring/weight lower bolt into keeper

### Thumb Latch (Medieval/Simple)

Thumb latch uses thumb pressure on external lever to retract bolt.

**Components:**

1. **Bolt:** Wooden or metal peg inside door, held by leather loop
2. **Latch lever:** External lever connected via rod to bolt mechanism
3. **Spring:** Simple loop or leaf spring returns bolt to locked position

**Simplest construction:**
- Bolt: Wooden peg sliding in drilled holes
- Latch: Leather strap looped around peg, attached to external lever
- Pressing lever pulls leather strap, retracting peg; releasing lever allows peg to extend

### Barrel Bolt (Simple Slide)

Barrel bolt is essentially a round rod that slides within a housing.

**Forging:**

1. **Rod:** Smooth cylindrical shaft (12–16mm diameter), length matching door thickness + 50mm
2. **Housing:** Forged or fabricated sleeves/guides mounting on door face
3. **Handle:** Small cross-bar welded to rod end for gripping/turning

**Advantages:** Very simple, reliable, easy to repair.

## Hooks, Eyes, and Chain Links

### Hook and Eye Fastening

Simple but effective for light-duty applications (cabinets, latches, hanging).

**Eye forging:**

1. Heat round rod (6–8mm diameter)
2. Bend around tapered mandrel to form loop
3. Overlap ends slightly, weld or bolt together
4. Smooth any sharp edges

**Hook forging:**

1. Heat one end of rod
2. Taper and curve into hook shape (typically 20–40mm radius)
3. The opposite end becomes mounting point (drilled for bolt/rivet)

**Installation:** Bolt eye to frame, hook to door or sliding part. Hook engages eye for light holding.

### Chain Link Making

Chain links are useful for secured tools, suspended components, or decorative hardware.

**Simple chain forging (looped links):**

1. **Stock:** Use round rod (4–6mm diameter for light chain, 8–12mm for heavy)
2. **Coil:** Wrap rod around mandrel (forming circular loops)
3. **Cut:** Cut coil to separate individual loops
4. **Weld:** Heat overlapping ends until cherry-red, hammer to fuse
5. **Link:** Connect welded loops to previous links, building chain

**Enhanced chain (open-and-close links):**

1. Forge loops as above
2. Leave one end of each link open (do not fully weld)
3. When installing, slip new link through previous, then weld the open end
4. Allows custom chain length without completely re-forging

**Strength:** Hand-forged chain suitable for suspension loads up to ~50kg per link (depends on material and welding quality). Test with proof load before trusting with critical applications.

## Tempering and Hardening Hardware

### Hardening (Quenching)

Hardening converts steel to a harder but brittler state through rapid cooling.

**Process:**

1. **Heat:** Bring steel to bright cherry-red (not hotter; hotter steel will degrade)
2. **Quench:** Plunge into cold medium:
   - **Water:** Fastest cooling, best hardness, risk of cracking
   - **Oil:** Slower cooling, slightly less hard, lower crack risk (safer)
   - **Brine (salt water):** Intermediate, historical standard
3. **Cool completely:** Leave in quench medium until no longer warm (5–10 min)

**Quenching risk:** Rapid cooling creates internal stresses. Large items (bolts >12mm) risk cracking; smaller items (nails, hinges) are safer.

**Reducing crack risk:**
- Use oil instead of water
- Pre-warm quench medium to ~20–40°C
- Don't plunge suddenly; cool gradually by withdrawing from heat first
- Larger items may need slow-quench (oil) + air cooling

### Tempering (Stress Relief)

Tempering reduces brittleness while maintaining hardness by reheating quenched steel to lower temperatures.

**Temperature control (without thermometer):**

Oxidation colors indicate temperature:

```
Pale straw (160–180°C):  Very hard, slightly brittle — nails, cold chisels
Straw yellow (180–200°C): Ideal for nails, screws — medium hardness + flexibility
Brown (220–240°C):        Softer, more flexible — springs, tools needing toughness
Purple (250–280°C):       Spring temper — leaf springs, coils
Blue (300–320°C):         Very soft — not useful for most hardware
```

**Tempering process:**

1. **Quench hardened part:** Cool from bright red quench
2. **Polish surface:** Use wire brush to clean oxide layer (allows color observation)
3. **Reheat slowly:** Place on hot anvil or in warm section of forge; watch oxide color develop
4. **Stop at correct color:** When color reaches desired shade, cool immediately in air or water
5. **Result:** Part now has desired hardness + toughness balance

**Example (nail tempering):**
1. Quench nail from bright cherry-red in oil
2. Polish surface with wire brush
3. Reheat gently until oxide color reaches golden straw
4. Cool in air or warm water
5. Result: Hard nail that won't shatter if struck at angle

:::warning
Tempering success depends on careful color observation. Work in dim light (dark forge helps), compare to reference samples if possible, and practice on less critical items first.
:::

## Assembly and Installation Best Practices

### Bolt Assembly Sequence

1. **Clean threads:** Remove any dirt/scale
2. **Insert bolt:** Pass through aligned holes
3. **Hand-thread nut:** Turn nut by hand until snug (avoid cross-threading)
4. **Tighten:** Use two wrenches (one on bolt head, one on nut) to tighten firmly
5. **Final check:** Bolt should not turn freely; slight resistance = good tension

**Tightening force:** Do not over-tighten (risk of stripping threads or shearing bolt). For wood applications, snug enough that bolt cannot rotate under moderate hand force.

### Rivet Installation Quality Control

1. **Temperature:** Rivet must be hot (cherry-red) but not smoking or orange
2. **Set:** Strike riveted end with steady, controlled blows (not wild swings)
3. **Rotation:** Rotate rivet slightly between strikes for symmetric head formation
4. **Result:** Both rivet heads should be uniform, with minimal gaps at joint

**Inspection:** Pull or flex joint to ensure rivet isn't loose (loose rivet will rattle/flex).

### Hinge Installation

1. **Alignment:** Doors must hang square; use shims if frame is out of plumb
2. **Hinge spacing:** Top hinge at 150–200mm from door top; bottom hinge at 150–200mm from bottom; middle hinge (if used) centered
3. **Bolt pattern:** Bolts distributed across hinge leaves; tighten evenly (no cross-threading)
4. **Operation test:** Door should swing smoothly; no binding at top/bottom

### Hardware Maintenance

**Regular inspection:**
- Check bolt/nut tightness (monthly for doors/windows)
- Lubricate hinges with light oil (prevents squeaking, extends life)
- Inspect for rust (clean with wire brush, apply oil)
- Tighten loose fasteners immediately (prevent further loosening)

**Seasonal adjustment:**
- Winter: Bolts may loosen due to wood shrinkage; re-tighten
- Summer: Wood expands; watch for hinge strain (loose bolts again)

## Hardware Production Workflow

| Item | Sequence | Estimated Time |
|------|----------|-----------------|
| **Nail** | Cut → taper point → form head → temper | 2–3 min/nail |
| **Bolt** | Cut → form head → thread → temper | 10–15 min/bolt |
| **Nut** | Forge hex → thread → temper | 5–8 min/nut |
| **Hinge** | Forge leaves → punch holes → form pin → assemble | 30–45 min/hinge |
| **Rivet** | Form head → test fit → assemble → install | 2–3 min/rivet |
| **Latch** | Forge lever → form bolt → assemble → test | 45–60 min/latch |

**Safety checklist:**
- [ ] Eye protection (sparks)
- [ ] Hand protection (gloves for hot material)
- [ ] Secure workspace (organized, clear)
- [ ] Cool quench medium pre-prepared
- [ ] Proper ventilation (forge fumes)
- [ ] Fire extinguisher nearby
- [ ] First aid kit accessible
- [ ] No loose clothing or jewelry
