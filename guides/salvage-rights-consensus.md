<section id="overview">

## Overview: Why Salvage Rights Matter

In any post-infrastructure scenario, salvage becomes a primary source of materials, tools, fuel, and technology. Without functioning supply chains, communities must extract value from the built environment left behind. This creates an immediate and often contentious question: who owns what has been abandoned?

Without clear salvage governance, communities experience predictable failures. Hoarding of critical resources by early movers creates resentment and inequality. Dangerous sites attract unprepared scavengers who get injured or killed. Duplicate salvage runs waste time and labor. Conflicts over claimed materials escalate into violence. Valuable items are destroyed through careless extraction.

Establishing salvage rights is not an academic exercise — it is survival infrastructure as essential as water purification or shelter construction. Communities that address salvage governance early tend to develop more stable economies, fewer internal conflicts, and more efficient resource utilization than those that operate under informal or contested norms.

This guide covers the core frameworks for salvage governance, practical dispute resolution mechanisms, hazardous site access rules, and methods for building community consensus around resource allocation. For the physical techniques of salvage operations, see <a href="../building-materials-salvage.html">Building Materials Salvage</a> and <a href="../scavenging-logistics-team-ops.html">Scavenging Logistics & Team Ops</a>.

<section id="foundational-frameworks">

## Foundational Frameworks for Salvage Rights

### First-Finder Rights

The simplest salvage model grants ownership to whoever finds and claims a resource first. This has historical precedent in maritime salvage law, homesteading claims, and informal scavenging norms.

**Advantages:**
- Easy to understand and explain
- Rewards initiative and risk-taking
- Requires minimal administrative overhead
- Natural incentive to explore and map new sites

**Disadvantages:**
- Favors physically able individuals and those with transportation
- Creates a "gold rush" mentality that encourages recklessness
- Can result in hoarding of strategically important materials
- Disadvantages families, elderly, injured, and caregivers
- No mechanism for community-critical resource prioritization

:::warning
Pure first-finder systems consistently produce dangerous outcomes. When individuals race to claim resources, they skip safety assessments, enter structurally compromised buildings alone, and handle hazardous materials without precautions. Multiple historical accounts from disaster zones document fatalities directly attributable to competitive salvage behavior.
:::

**Practical modifications to first-finder:**
- **Claiming limits:** No individual may claim more than they can transport in one trip
- **Use-it-or-lose-it:** Claimed resources revert to community pool if not used within 30 days
- **Critical resource exemption:** Medical supplies, fuel, communications equipment, and weapons are automatically community property regardless of who finds them
- **Registration requirement:** Claims must be logged with a community designee within 24 hours

### Community Property Model

Under this framework, all salvaged materials belong to the community and are distributed through a governance process. Individuals conduct salvage on behalf of the collective.

**Advantages:**
- Ensures equitable distribution
- Allows strategic prioritization (medical supplies before luxury items)
- Reduces dangerous competition
- Supports organized, safer salvage operations
- Community can assign specialists to appropriate tasks

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires functioning governance infrastructure
- Can reduce individual motivation to take salvage initiative
- Administrative burden of tracking, storing, and distributing resources
- Vulnerable to corruption if governance is weak

:::tip
The community property model works best when combined with a clear compensation system. Salvage teams who bring back materials should receive priority allocation, extra rations, or labor credits. This maintains the incentive structure while keeping distribution equitable. See <a href="../economics-trade.html">Economics & Trade</a> for currency and credit systems.
:::

### Hybrid Models

Most successful post-disruption communities adopt hybrid approaches:

**Tiered ownership model:**
- **Tier 1 — Community property:** Medical supplies, fuel, weapons, ammunition, communications equipment, industrial tools, generators, water purification supplies
- **Tier 2 — Shared with finder priority:** Building materials, food stores, clothing, household goods — community tracks inventory but finder gets first selection
- **Tier 3 — Personal property:** Personal effects, decorative items, books, entertainment, items of sentimental value — finder keeps

**Zone-based model:**
- Designated zones within the community's territory are assigned to specific teams or individuals
- Prevents duplicate exploration and territorial conflicts
- Allows systematic, thorough extraction rather than cherry-picking
- Requires a mapping and assignment system — see <a href="../rural-scavenging-guide.html">Rural Scavenging Guide</a> for area assessment methods

<section id="establishing-rules">

## Establishing Rules Through Consensus

### The Initial Convention

When a community first forms or first addresses salvage governance, it needs a founding agreement. This should happen as early as possible — before conflicts arise, not after.

**Preparation steps:**

1. **Inventory existing claims.** Before proposing new rules, document what people have already salvaged. Retroactive seizure of goods creates immediate resentment and resistance. Grandfather existing holdings.
2. **Identify critical needs.** What does the community urgently need? Medical supplies, tools, shelter materials, fuel? This shapes which categories get community-property treatment.
3. **Assess the territory.** How much salvageable material is accessible? Abundance reduces conflict; scarcity increases it. Realistic assessment prevents over-regulation of plentiful goods.
4. **Map stakeholders.** Who are the active salvagers? Who has transportation? Who has specialized skills (electricians, mechanics, medical personnel)? Their buy-in is essential.

**Running the convention:**

Hold a public meeting with all community members, not just salvagers. Non-salvagers are resource consumers and have legitimate interests in distribution. Use a structured format as described in <a href="../governance-democratic-systems-frameworks.html">Governance & Democratic Systems</a>.

Present proposed frameworks and allow open discussion. Record objections. Seek consensus rather than majority vote — a salvage system that 40% of the community considers illegitimate will be widely violated.

:::info-box
**Consensus does not mean unanimity.** It means that every participant can live with the outcome, even if it is not their preferred option. A practical test: ask each person "Can you support this framework, or will you actively work against it?" If someone will actively work against it, their concerns must be addressed before finalizing.
:::

### Documentation and Communication

Once agreed upon, salvage rules must be written down and publicly posted. Memory-based agreements degrade rapidly and produce conflicting interpretations.

**Essential elements of a salvage charter:**

- **Resource categories** with clear definitions and examples
- **Claiming procedures** (how, when, where to register)
- **Distribution procedures** for community property
- **Time limits** on claims (use-it-or-lose-it periods)
- **Safety requirements** (minimum team size, equipment, site assessment)
- **Prohibited conduct** (theft from other teams, sabotage, entering restricted sites)
- **Consequences** for violations (graduated: warning, loss of salvage privileges, community service, expulsion)
- **Amendment process** (how rules can be changed as circumstances evolve)

### Evolving Rules Over Time

Salvage conditions change. Early post-disruption, easy surface-level salvage is abundant. Over months, remaining salvage requires more effort, specialized skills, and greater risk. Rules must adapt.

**Phase 1 (0–3 months):** Liberal first-finder rules with critical resource exemptions. Priority is speed and volume. Most materials are easily accessible.

**Phase 2 (3–12 months):** Shift toward zone assignment and team-based operations. Easy salvage is depleted. Remaining targets require planning and coordination.

**Phase 3 (1+ years):** Systematic extraction requiring tools, demolition, and specialized knowledge. Community-directed operations become the norm. Individual freelance salvage becomes marginal.

Build review triggers into the charter: "These rules will be reviewed at 3 months, 6 months, and annually thereafter, or whenever requested by petition of 10% of community members."

<section id="dispute-resolution">

## Dispute Resolution for Salvage Claims

Even with clear rules, disputes will arise. Competing claims to the same site, disagreements about resource categorization, accusations of hoarding or theft, and arguments about fair distribution are all predictable.

### Common Dispute Types

**Simultaneous discovery:** Two parties claim they found the same cache independently. Without witnesses or documentation, this is difficult to adjudicate.

**Boundary disputes:** In zone-based systems, disagreements about where one zone ends and another begins. Especially common in urban environments where building interiors span multiple parcels.

**Category disputes:** "This generator is a personal tool, not community infrastructure." Arguments about which tier a resource belongs to.

**Extraction damage:** One team's demolition work damages materials claimed by another team. Collapse of a wall destroys items in an adjacent room.

**Hoarding accusations:** Community members suspect a salvager is hiding or understating finds. Difficult to prove without invasive searches.

### Three-Tier Resolution System

**Tier 1 — Direct negotiation.** The parties involved try to resolve the dispute themselves. This works for minor disagreements between people who have an ongoing relationship. Set a time limit (48 hours). If unresolved, escalate.

**Tier 2 — Mediation.** A neutral community member mediates the dispute. The mediator does not decide — they facilitate agreement between the parties. See <a href="../dispute-mediation-restorative-justice.html">Dispute Mediation & Restorative Justice</a> for mediation techniques. Mediators should be rotated to prevent the perception of bias.

**Tier 3 — Arbitration.** A panel of three community members (one chosen by each party, one by the governance council) hears both sides and issues a binding decision. This is the final internal resolution. See <a href="../justice-law.html">Justice & Law</a> for structuring arbitration panels.

:::tip
Keep written records of all dispute resolutions. Over time, these create precedent that makes future disputes easier to resolve. A community that has ten prior rulings on simultaneous-discovery disputes can point to established principles rather than arguing from scratch each time.
:::

### Prevention Through Design

The best disputes are the ones that never happen. System design can prevent most salvage conflicts:

- **Pre-registration of salvage runs.** Before departing, teams register their target location and expected return time. If two teams register the same target, they coordinate or the first registrant has priority.
- **Standardized claim markers.** Physical tags or paint marks placed on claimed materials, with the claimant's identifier and date. Tampering with markers is a serious offense.
- **Transparent inventory.** Community stockpiles are publicly inventoried and the records are accessible to all members. Reduces suspicion of favoritism in distribution.
- **Regular distribution events.** Scheduled, public distribution of community-property resources. Everyone can see what is available and how it is allocated.

<section id="hazardous-sites">

## Hazardous Site Access Governance

Not all salvage targets are safe. Chemical plants, hospitals, fuel depots, industrial facilities, partially collapsed structures, and flooded basements all present serious hazards. Governance must address who can access these sites and under what conditions.

:::danger
**Hazardous site access without proper governance kills people.** Uncontrolled access to chemical facilities, fuel storage, and structurally compromised buildings is responsible for more post-disaster deaths than many people realize. An untrained individual entering a building with compromised load-bearing walls, a basement with accumulated toxic gases, or a warehouse containing unknown chemicals may not survive the visit. Restrict access and require trained teams.
:::

### Site Classification System

**Green sites:** Standard residential and retail buildings with no structural concerns. Open to any registered salvager.

**Yellow sites:** Buildings with moderate structural damage, elevated positions requiring fall protection, or minor chemical hazards (household cleaners, paint, solvents). Require buddy system (minimum 2 people) and basic PPE.

**Red sites:** Significant structural damage, industrial chemicals, fuel storage, medical waste, asbestos, or confined spaces. Restricted to trained teams with appropriate equipment. Require written authorization from the salvage council.

**Black sites:** Extreme hazards — nuclear facilities, heavy chemical plants, biological hazards, catastrophic structural failure. Off-limits until assessed by specialists. Entering a black site without authorization should carry the most serious consequences the community enforces.

For assessing structural integrity before entry, see <a href="../structural-safety-building-entry.html">Structural Safety & Building Entry</a>. For handling hazardous materials found during salvage, see <a href="../medical-salvage-safety.html">Medical Salvage & Safety</a>.

### Access Authorization Process

For red and black sites:

1. **Site assessment.** A qualified assessor (construction experience, hazmat awareness) surveys the exterior and accessible areas. Documents hazards, recommended PPE, entry/exit routes, and estimated value of salvageable materials.
2. **Risk-benefit analysis.** Is the potential salvage worth the risk? A fuel depot with 500 gallons of diesel justifies significant risk. A warehouse of office furniture does not.
3. **Team selection.** Assign personnel with relevant skills and physical capability. Minimum team size for red sites: 3 people (2 inside, 1 safety watch outside with communication).
4. **Equipment check.** Verify PPE, tools, communication devices, and first aid supplies before departure.
5. **Authorization.** Salvage council or designated authority grants written permission with specific scope (what to extract, time limit, areas to avoid).
6. **Debrief.** After the operation, the team reports findings, updated hazard assessment, and remaining salvageable materials. This information updates the site classification.

### Training Requirements

Community members who want to access yellow or red sites should complete basic training:

- Structural hazard recognition (load-bearing walls, floor integrity, roof condition)
- Chemical hazard awareness (labeling systems, common industrial chemicals, exposure symptoms)
- Basic PPE usage (respirators, gloves, eye protection, hard hats)
- Buddy system protocols and communication
- Emergency procedures (extraction of injured team member, decontamination)

This training can be delivered by community members with relevant pre-disruption experience (construction workers, firefighters, industrial workers, military personnel) and formalized through the community's <a href="../apprenticeship-knowledge-transfer.html">Apprenticeship Systems</a>.

## Inter-Community Salvage Relations

When multiple communities share a geographic area, salvage territory becomes a source of inter-community conflict. Establishing agreements with neighboring groups prevents dangerous confrontations.

### Territorial Agreements

**Buffer zones.** Designate areas between community territories that are open to all parties or off-limits to all. This prevents ambiguous encounters.

**Shared high-value sites.** Large facilities (hospitals, warehouses, factories) may contain enough material for multiple communities. Negotiated sharing agreements — alternating access days, dividing the facility into sectors, or joint operations — reduce conflict.

**Trade for access.** One community may have skills or resources another needs. Trading salvage access rights for medical care, agricultural products, or technical expertise creates mutually beneficial relationships. See <a href="../economics-trade.html">Economics & Trade</a> for trade framework structures.

### Enforcement and Violations

**Trespass by outsiders.** When non-community members are found salvaging in claimed territory, the response should be proportional:
1. First encounter: Inform them of the territorial claim and escort them out
2. Repeated violations: Formal complaint to their community leadership
3. Persistent violations: Restrict trade and cooperation with the offending community
4. Violent confrontation is a last resort and almost always counterproductive

:::warning
Armed confrontation over salvage resources almost never produces a net positive outcome. The materials gained rarely outweigh the cost in injuries, deaths, and the permanent deterioration of inter-community relations. Negotiate first, negotiate again, and negotiate a third time before considering any other approach. See <a href="../conflict-resolution-diplomacy.html">Conflict Resolution & Diplomacy</a>.
:::

<section id="record-keeping">

## Record-Keeping and Transparency

Effective salvage governance requires documentation. Without records, disputes multiply, inventory is lost, and distribution becomes opaque.

### What to Track

- **Site registry.** Every explored site: location, classification (green/yellow/red/black), assessed value, hazards, extraction status, assigned team
- **Claim log.** Date, claimant, location, items claimed, category classification
- **Distribution records.** Community-property items: what was distributed, to whom, when, for what purpose
- **Dispute log.** Disputes filed, resolution method, outcome, rationale
- **Incident log.** Injuries, near-misses, structural collapses, hazardous exposures — critical for updating site classifications and safety protocols

:::tip
Assign a dedicated record-keeper for salvage operations. This role can be filled by someone physically unable to do field salvage but who is organized and literate. It is a genuinely important contribution, not a consolation role. Accurate records prevent more conflicts than any mediation system. For long-term preservation of these records, see <a href="../archival-records.html">Archival & Records</a>.
:::

### Public Access

All records except sensitive security information (weapons caches, fuel reserves, medical narcotic locations) should be publicly accessible. Transparency is the single most effective tool against accusations of corruption, favoritism, and hoarding. Post summaries in a public location, and make detailed records available on request.

<section id="implementation-checklist">

## Implementation Checklist

Starting salvage governance from scratch:

1. **Week 1:** Identify active salvagers and stakeholders. Inventory existing claims and holdings. Assess available territory.
2. **Week 2:** Draft a proposed framework (hybrid model recommended). Circulate for review. Collect feedback.
3. **Week 3:** Hold a community convention. Negotiate and finalize the charter. Appoint initial administrators (record-keeper, site assessor, mediation panel).
4. **Week 4:** Implement registration system. Begin site classification. Distribute claim markers. Post rules publicly.
5. **Monthly:** Review disputes, incidents, and distribution equity. Adjust rules as needed.

The goal is a system that is fair enough that most people comply voluntarily, clear enough that disputes can be resolved by reference to written rules, and flexible enough to adapt as conditions change. Perfect fairness is unattainable — functional fairness is the target.

For broader governance structures that can house salvage administration, see <a href="../governance.html">Governance</a> and <a href="../community-organizing.html">Community Organizing</a>.
