## Overview

Urban agriculture provides food security and community resilience in areas where arable land is scarce or degraded. Post-industrial cities contain thousands of abandoned lots, contaminated brownfields, and degraded soils where conventional agriculture is risky but community gardens are transformable. This guide covers practical assessment of contamination, immediate growing strategies in isolation systems, and long-term phytoremediation approaches to restore productive soil.

:::warning
**Soil Contamination Hazard**
Urban soils frequently contain lead, arsenic, cadmium, and industrial residues that accumulate in edible crops. EPA residential soil screening levels (SSLs) are: lead 400 ppm (play areas) / 1,200 ppm (bare soil), arsenic 0.68 ppm, cadmium 70 ppm, and chromium (hexavalent) 0.31 ppm — soils exceeding these thresholds require remediation or isolation before food production. Always test soil before growing food — especially on former industrial, commercial, or roadside sites. Do not assume soil is safe based on appearance. Raised beds with imported certified clean soil may be the safest option for food production on suspect sites. Heavy metals cannot be removed by composting or amendment alone. Consult local extension services or environmental agencies for site-specific guidance.
:::

<section id="urban-agriculture-advantages">

## Urban Agriculture Advantages

### Why Urban Agriculture Works

**Land abundance:** Vacant lots, rooftops, street medians, parking areas, and abandoned industrial sites exist in almost every city. Most cities contain 5-25% vacant buildable land.

**Infrastructure proximity:** Water is nearby (storm drains, gutters, municipal water if accessible), population centers reduce transport distance, existing buildings provide shelter/shade for specialized growing, fencing and structures already present.

**Microclimate advantages:** Urban heat island effect extends growing season (warmer nights, fewer frosts). Buildings provide wind protection. Pavement and structures reflect extra sunlight (beneficial in temperate zones with lower sun angles).

**Existing water systems:** Rainwater harvesting from rooftops, parking lots, and streets provides irrigation without tapping potable supplies. Some cities have storm drainage accessible for non-potable reuse.

**Waste stream integration:** Urban areas generate compost feedstock (kitchen waste, landscaping debris), reducing transportation costs. Recycled materials (containers, pallets, bricks) abundant and cheap.

**Population concentration:** Community gardens serve local market immediately (no transport infrastructure needed). Fresh produce reaches consumers within hours of harvest.

### Disadvantages and Challenges

**Contamination:** Heavy metals (lead from paint/gasoline), petroleum residues, industrial chemicals, PCBs from transformers, and persistent organics contaminate many urban soils.

**Traffic and air pollution:** Nearby roads deposit particulates and exhaust compounds on plants. Aerosol lead near major roads can exceed safe soil levels.

**Soil compaction and poor structure:** Urban soils compacted by vehicle/equipment use. Low organic matter. High clay content limiting drainage.

**Space constraints:** Lots are often small and oddly shaped, limiting rotational grazing or diversity.

**Regulatory:** Some cities restrict food growing in public spaces. Building codes may prohibit rooftop gardens. Safety inspections required for public food systems.

Understanding these tradeoffs determines strategy: do you isolate (raised beds with imported clean soil) or remediate (clean in place)?

<section id="contamination-assessment">

## Contamination Risk Assessment

### Common Urban Contaminants

**Lead:**
- Sources: Pre-1978 exterior house paint, leaded gasoline (cars pre-1980), old industrial equipment
- Concentrations: Background 0-5 ppm; contaminated urban soils 100-1000+ ppm
- Risk: Bioavailable to leafy greens; root crops; directly ingested on produce surface
- Tolerance level: EPA RAGS soil remediation goal ~400 ppm for residential; many recommend <50 ppm for food gardens

**Cadmium:**
- Sources: Industrial plating, batteries, phosphate fertilizers (legacy use), photographic emulsions
- Concentrations: Background <1 ppm; contaminated sites 5-50+ ppm
- Risk: Bioavailable to leafy greens; accumulates in legumes; long soil persistence
- Tolerance: <0.5 ppm desired for food gardens

**Arsenic:**
- Sources: CCA-treated wood (pressure-treated lumber, pre-2004), old pesticides, industrial sites
- Concentrations: Background 1-10 ppm; CCA sites 10-100+ ppm (directly under wood)
- Risk: Root uptake moderate; mostly surface-soil adhered; long persistence
- Tolerance: <10 ppm desired

**Petroleum hydrocarbons:**
- Sources: Gas stations, parking areas, roads, underground storage tanks (USTs), refineries
- Persistence: Months to years depending on type and conditions
- Risk: Direct ingestion; plant toxicity; biodegradation products may be toxic
- Tolerance: <100 ppm practical for food areas; <50 ppm safer

**PCBs (Polychlorinated biphenyls):**
- Sources: Old transformers, capacitors, old electrical equipment, landfill contamination
- Persistence: Decades in soil; bioaccumulate in food chain
- Risk: Primarily bioaccumulation in animal products (chickens, goats); plants less affected
- Tolerance: <1 ppm practical for food areas

**Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs):**
- Sources: Dry cleaners, paint shops, chemical storage, landfills, industrial sites
- Persistence: Variable; many degrade over years
- Risk: Direct inhalation and uptake; variable toxicity
- Tolerance: Highly variable by compound; generally <1 ppm

### Site History Investigation

**Step 1: Identify what was there before**

Public records:
- Local sanitation department records (landfill, waste disposal, illegal dumping)
- Fire department reports (chemical spills, industrial fires)
- EPA Superfund database and Brownfields database (searchable by address)
- State environmental agency records (spill reports, permitted industrial facilities)
- Historical maps and photos (Google Earth historical, library archives)
- Deed and title searches (previous uses, liens for contamination)

**Step 2: Classify risk by previous use**

**Highest risk sites:**
- Gas stations (petroleum, BTEX compounds)
- Dry cleaners (tetrachloroethylene, heavy metals)
- Auto repair shops (oil, metals, solvents)
- Industrial plating shops (heavy metals, cyanide)
- Battery recycling (lead, acid)
- Pesticide manufacturing (arsenic, mercury, organophosphates)
- Mining or smelting (heavy metals, arsenic)

**High risk sites:**
- Parking lots (petroleum, lead from exhaust)
- Highways and major roads (lead, PAH compounds, road salt)
- Landfills (mixed contamination)
- Old dumps (mixed contamination)
- Painted structures (lead paint dust)

**Moderate risk sites:**
- Residential areas (lead paint if pre-1978, older gasoline deposits)
- Old orchards (arsenic from historical pesticides)
- Areas near industrial zones (airborne deposition)

**Lower risk sites:**
- Parks, greenspaces, low-traffic areas
- Newer construction areas (post-1990)
- Agricultural land not previously industrial

**Step 3: Preliminary visual/olfactory assessment**

**Visual indicators of contamination:**
- Unusual discoloration (dark stains suggest petroleum; white/blue deposits suggest heavy metals or salts)
- Dead zone around specific area (plants avoid contaminated soil)
- Slag or industrial debris
- Corroded metal objects or rusty residues
- Visible paint chips or coatings

**Smell indicators:**
- Petroleum/gasoline smell (volatile hydrocarbons)
- Pungent chemical smell (likely solvents or VOCs)
- Sulfur smell (likely heavy metal sulfides or industrial processes)

**Note:** Absence of visible contamination does NOT mean soil is clean. Assessment requires testing.

<section id="soil-testing">

## Simple Soil Testing

### Plant Bioassay (Free, No Equipment)

Bioassay provides rough assessment of whether soil is toxic to plants, though not quantitative measurement of contaminants.

**Procedure:**
1. Collect soil samples from site and control site (known uncontaminated area)
2. Plant identical seeds (bean, radish, or lettuce) in small pots of site soil and control soil
3. Place in identical light/water conditions
4. After 2 weeks, measure plant height, leaf color, leaf damage
5. Repeat with 2-3 seed types for robustness

**Interpretation:**
- Stunted growth compared to control: likely soil toxicity or contaminant uptake
- Leaf chlorosis (yellowing) in test but not control: heavy metal or nutrient deficiency
- Identical growth: soil likely acceptable or contaminant not plant-available

**Limitations:** Sensitive plants (lettuce, radish) but not definitively quantitative. Does not identify specific contaminant or measure concentration.

### pH Testing (Inexpensive Strips)

Soil pH affects metal bioavailability and remediation strategy.

**Procedure:**
1. Collect soil sample; remove debris and large stones
2. Mix soil with distilled water (1:1 ratio by weight)
3. Stir vigorously; let settle 30 seconds
4. Insert pH strip into liquid portion
5. Compare color to reference chart

**Interpretation:**
- pH 6.5-7.5: Neutral, optimal for most vegetables
- pH < 6.5: Acidic; heavy metals more bioavailable (higher risk for lead, cadmium uptake); lime amendment needed
- pH > 7.5: Alkaline; less concern for heavy metal uptake; nutrients less available

**Cost:** $0.10-0.50 per test (bulk pH strips).

### Visual & Smell Assessment as Indicator

**Petroleum contamination indicators:**
- Visible oily residue
- Gasoline/petroleum smell at surface
- Brown or black discoloration
- Soil appears water-repellent (hydrophobic)

**Heavy metal contamination indicators:**
- Whitish or bluish surface deposits (if visible)
- Dead zone around metal debris (smelter slag, old batteries)
- Unusual discoloration (reddish-brown for iron, bluish for copper compounds)

### Basic Lab Testing (Regional)

For definitive assessment, soil test is necessary. Services available:

**University extension labs:** Most US universities offer soil testing ($10-30 per sample). Tests include:
- Total nutrient levels (nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium)
- pH, organic matter percentage
- Heavy metals (lead, cadmium, arsenic, mercury) — may require special request
- Sometimes cost extra for metals panel

**Commercial environmental labs:** $100-500 per sample depending on contaminant panel. Often required for Superfund or regulatory compliance.

**Sampling procedure (for any lab test):**
1. Collect 5-10 sub-samples from different areas within site
2. Mix thoroughly to get representative composite sample
3. Send 500g sample to lab with clear documentation of location and concern
4. Include request for specific contaminants relevant to site history
5. Interpret results with lab guidance

**Realistic numbers:** A small lot (0.1 acres) requires minimum 2-3 samples from different zones ($60-150 total). Initial assessment worth investment if contamination uncertain.

<section id="raised-bed-isolation">

## Raised-Bed Isolation Strategy

Most practical immediate approach: eliminate contamination risk by isolating growing space from contaminated soil.

### Raised-Bed Barriers and Construction

**Barrier principle:** Prevent root contact with contaminated soil below. Recommended minimum 18-24 inches deep for root crops (carrots, potatoes, beets); 12 inches acceptable for shallow-rooting crops (lettuce, herbs).

**Barrier materials:**

**Landscape fabric (recommended):**
- Geotextile barrier (3-5 oz minimum weight) prevents root penetration while allowing drainage
- Cost: $0.20-0.50/sq ft
- Installation: Lay over existing soil before adding raised bed frame
- Durability: 5-10 years before degradation

**Gravel base:**
- 2-3 inches of clean gravel (2-4 mm diameter)
- Separates raised bed from direct contact with underlying soil
- Cost: $0.10-0.20/sq ft
- Drainage: Excellent but may allow capillary rise of contaminated water in wet conditions
- Best combined with fabric underneath

**Plastic sheet (if contamination extreme):**
- 6-mil or thicker polyethylene or HDPE (high-density) plastic
- Completely blocks water exchange with underlying soil
- Risk: Prevents drainage; may cause waterlogging if not perfectly sloped
- Use only for heavy metal/persistent contamination; overkill for most scenarios

**Combination approach (recommended for safety):**
1. Lay landscape fabric over existing soil
2. Layer 2-3 inches clean gravel on top
3. Construct raised bed frame (wood, composite, cinder blocks)
4. Fill with imported clean soil

### Raised-Bed Frame Construction

**Wood framing (simple, inexpensive):**
- 2×10 or 2×12 untreated lumber (~$15-30 per 8-foot board)
- Assemble with bolts or nails to desired dimensions (4×8 ft standard; 2×4 ft acceptable)
- Line inside with landscape fabric to prevent soil spillage
- Untreated wood lasts 5-8 years before rot; replace when degraded
- Cost per 4×8 ft bed: $40-60 materials + labor

**Composite lumber (long-lasting):**
- Recycled plastic or plastic+wood blend
- More expensive ($40-80 per 8-foot board) but lasts 20+ years
- No splinters; no rot issues; less maintenance
- Cost per bed: $80-150 materials + labor

**Cinder block or concrete:**
- Durable (20+ years) but require stable foundation
- Can be stacked without mortar for simple assembly
- Cost: $2-3 per block; 4×8×2 ft high bed requires ~48 blocks = $100-150
- Blocks not food-safe if painted with solvent-based paint; use clear or latex paint

**Composite plastic buckets or containers:**
- Cost-effective for small spaces ($5-10 per 5-gallon bucket)
- Excellent for balconies, rooftops, window-box growing
- Portable (move if needed)
- Stack multiple for larger growing area

### Imported Clean Soil

**Specifications:**
- Heavy metal levels: Lead <50 ppm, cadmium <0.5 ppm, arsenic <10 ppm
- pH: 6.5-7.5 (neutral, optimal for vegetables)
- Organic matter: 5-15% (helps water retention and nutrient availability)
- No debris (rocks, glass, plastic, contaminated fragments)

**Sources:**
- Bagged garden soil: Expensive ($0.50-1.50 per liter) but guaranteed clean
- Bulk landscape suppliers: $25-50 per cubic yard delivered (cheaper for large volumes)
- Compost facilities: Finished compost $30-60/cubic yard; good quality if from food/yard waste only
- Mushroom farms: Often sell spent substrate (composted sawdust + manure) cheaply ($15-25/cubic yard); high fertility
- Combine multiple sources: Foundation soil (cheaper) + 20-30% compost or finished mulch (adds fertility and structure)

**Soil volume calculation:**
- 4×8 ft bed, 18 inches deep = 48 cu ft ÷ 27 = 1.78 cubic yards
- Rule of thumb: 27 cubic feet = 1 cubic yard

**Cost example:** 4×8 ft bed
- Landscape fabric + gravel: $15-25
- Wood frame: $50
- 2 cubic yards imported soil: $50-100
- **Total: $115-175 per bed**

For small communities, bulk purchasing (3-5 beds together) reduces per-unit cost.

:::tip
**Soil reuse:** If raised beds are productive for 10+ years, soil becomes increasingly depleted and compacted. Options: 1) Add 4-6 inches fresh compost annually to refresh, 2) Remove and replace soil every 7-10 years, 3) Share cost of new soil across multiple community gardeners.
:::

<section id="container-gardening">

## Container Gardening at Scale

For severely contaminated sites or where raised beds aren't feasible, container systems provide complete isolation and flexibility.

### Container Types and Economics

**Salvaged containers (cheapest):**
- 5-gallon buckets (food-grade plastic, ex-pickles/cooking oil): Free-$0.50 each if sourced from restaurants/bakeries
- Food-grade plastic crates: $1-3 each
- Wooden wine boxes or crates: Free-$2 each (lined with plastic or fabric)
- Large terracotta pots: $5-15 each used/damaged but functional

**Cost per unit:** $0-3; entire lot can be established for <$100

**Cost per growing volume:** 5-gallon buckets provide 0.02 cubic yards each. To equal one 4×8 raised bed (1.78 cubic yards), need ~90 buckets and 90+ liters clean soil.

**Commercial containers:**
- Grow bags (recycled plastic or burlap): $2-10 per 5-20 liter capacity
- Fabric pots: $1-5 per 5-20 liter capacity
- Stackable planters or tower systems: $20-50 per unit but vertical integration

### Container Soil Mix

Containerized plants need well-draining but water-retentive mix (different from garden soil):

**Recipe (by volume):**
- 40% finished compost (nutrients, structure)
- 30% peat moss or coconut coir (water retention, aeration)
- 20% perlite or vermiculite (drainage, aeration)
- 10% worm castings (fertility, beneficial organisms)

**Example:** 5-gallon bucket = 1.25 cu ft
- 0.5 cu ft compost + 0.375 cu ft peat + 0.25 cu ft perlite + 0.125 cu ft castings

**Cost:** Bulk materials cost $0.02-0.05 per liter; per bucket mix ~$0.10-0.25

**Alternative (simpler):** 50% finished compost + 50% drainage material (perlite, aged bark, coconut coir).

### Drainage and Watering Systems

**Manual watering (lowest cost, high labor):**
- Hand watering with hose; daily for containers in summer
- Labor: 15-30 min per 20-30 containers daily
- Cost: $0 (if municipal water); higher if hauling water

**Drip irrigation (moderate cost, minimal labor):**
- Soaker hoses or drip lines fed from main line
- Timer ($15-40) automates irrigation
- Cost: $50-150 for system serving 50-100 containers
- Labor: 5 min daily to monitor, weekly to adjust

**Passive wicking (no electricity):**
- Container sits on capillary mat or terra cotta wicking strips submerged in water basin
- Useful for small container collections; does not scale
- Cost: $10-30 per basin

### Vertical Growing Systems

Maximize yield from limited space:

**Stacked containers:**
- Stack 3-4 buckets vertically, each with drainage hole allowing water to cascade down
- Each level accessible for planting/harvesting
- Footprint 1-2 sq ft, height 3-4 ft; yields equivalent to 10-15 sq ft traditional bed
- Useful for lettuce, herbs, strawberries
- Cost: $20-50 for structure + containers

**Wall-mounted pockets (living walls):**
- Fabric pockets attached to trellis or frame against wall/fence
- Each pocket holds 1-2 liter soil; plant leafy greens or herbs
- Footprint minimal; vertical space maximized
- Cost: $100-300 for 30-50 pocket system

**Tower and pyramid systems:**
- Commercial systems ($100-500) stackable containers with irrigation
- High yield per footprint
- Useful for strawberries, herbs
- More expensive but viable for community demonstrations

## Phytoremediation as Long-Term Cleanup

Phytoremediation uses plants to extract, immobilize, or degrade contaminants in soil. Over years to decades, repeated cycles can reduce contamination to safe levels.

### Hyperaccumulator Plants

Some plants preferentially accumulate heavy metals in their tissues. Planting, harvesting, and removing these plants progressively reduces soil contamination.

**Lead accumulation:**
- Sunflowers (Helianthus annuus): Documented lead uptake, widely promoted for phytoremediation
- Brassicas (cabbage, broccoli): Moderate lead uptake
- Legumes (beans, peas): Moderate uptake
- Use: Plant on contaminated area, allow to grow for full season, harvest above-ground biomass, remove from site for proper disposal
- Rate: Sunflowers can remove 10-50% of bioavailable lead per season if conditions right; multiple seasons required for significant cleanup

**Cadmium accumulation:**
- Indian mustard (Brassica juncea): Highest cadmium accumulator known
- Lettuce, other leafy greens: Moderate uptake
- Use: Same as lead; harvest before seeds mature to prevent secondary contamination
- Rate: Indian mustard can remove 20-100% bioavailable cadmium per season; 2-3 seasons usually sufficient

**Arsenic reduction:**
- Willows: Absorb and metabolize arsenic compounds
- Chinese brake fern (Pteris vittata): Documented arsenic hyperaccumulator but not cold-hardy in temperate zones
- Use: Longer-term (years), particularly for low-level arsenic
- Rate: Slow accumulation; multiple years needed

### Phytoremediation Implementation

**Site preparation:**
1. Amend soil pH if needed (above 6.5 for lead, below 7.5 for cadmium) to optimize plant growth
2. Add organic matter (compost, aged manure) to improve soil structure and plant growth
3. Establish weed-free area (weeds compete with remedial plants)

**Planting phase:**
1. Select hyperaccumulator appropriate to primary contaminant and climate
2. Plant at high density (maximize root mass and uptake)
3. Example for lead: Sunflowers 12 inches apart in 3-foot rows = ~4 plants per square meter
4. Irrigate and fertilize to maximize growth (more biomass = more uptake)

**Harvesting phase:**
1. When plants reach senescence (late season for annuals) or after designated growth period
2. Cut above ground; roots remain in soil (dead roots improve structure and harbor mycorrhizal fungi)
3. For toxic accumulations, do not compost with food waste; segregate

**Disposal of contaminated biomass:**
- Small volumes: Dry and burn (ash to hazardous waste disposal) or bury in deep pit on site away from water or living areas
- Larger volumes: Coordinate with environmental agency; may be accepted at hazardous waste facility or composted separately
- Regulatory note: Sunflower ash from highly contaminated sites may exceed hazardous waste thresholds; check local regulations

**Repeat cycles:**
1. Allow 1-2 seasons between harvests for soil recovery
2. Typical remediation timeline: 3-5 years of annual harvest for moderate contamination; 5-10+ years for high contamination
3. Monitor soil via occasional testing (yearly or every 2-3 years) to track contamination decline

### Combining Phytoremediation with Food Production

Parallel growing of food and remediation:

**Strategy:** Use raised beds + containers for food (safe, isolated), dedicate areas to ground-level phytoremediation plants.

**Example layout (0.5 acre lot):**
- 2000 sq ft raised beds and containers for food production (isolated with clean soil)
- 1000 sq ft sunflowers for lead remediation
- 500 sq ft willows for long-term arsenic remediation
- 500 sq ft reserved for future expansion

**Timeline:** After 5 years of sunflower cycling, soil lead drops sufficiently; remove raised bed isolation; transition to direct ground planting for less-sensitive crops (root crops before leafy greens).

## Soil Amendment Protocols

### pH Adjustment

Most urban soils are acidic due to acid rain and industrial deposition.

**Raising pH (for acidic soils <6.5):**

**Lime (calcium carbonate):**
- Application: 50-100 lbs per 1000 sq ft
- Effect: Raises pH 0.5-1.5 points; effectiveness depends on soil texture
- Timeline: Effects develop over weeks; full effect in 2-3 months
- Cost: $10-20 per 50-lb bag ($200-400 per 1000 sq ft)
- Additional benefit: Provides calcium, reduces bioavailability of lead and cadmium

**Wood ash:**
- Application: 20-40 lbs per 1000 sq ft (thinly spread, don't accumulate; excess salt)
- Effect: Raises pH 0.3-0.8 points
- Timeline: Weeks to months
- Cost: Free if sourced from wood burning; $5-10/bag if purchased
- Additional benefit: Potassium, magnesium; improves soil structure

**Recommendation:** Use lime for permanent raise; incorporate 2-3 inches deep. Retest pH 8 weeks later.

**Lowering pH (for alkaline soils >7.5):**

**Elemental sulfur:**
- Application: 25-50 lbs per 1000 sq ft
- Effect: Lowers pH 0.5-1.0 points; very slow
- Timeline: 3-6 months for significant effect
- Cost: $20-40 per bag
- Requires moisture and microbial action; slow but effective

**Aluminum sulfate:**
- Application: 100-200 lbs per 1000 sq ft (high rates)
- Effect: Lowers pH 0.5-1.5 points; quicker than sulfur
- Timeline: Weeks to 2 months
- Cost: $30-50 per 50-lb bag
- Additional concern: Adds aluminum (monitor if concerned)

**Organic matter addition (slower, safer):**
- High-quality compost lowers pH slightly; primary benefit improves structure and biology
- Application: 2-4 inches incorporated
- Effect: Gradual pH adjustment via organic acids and biological activity

### Contaminant Immobilization

Amendment to reduce bioavailability without removing contaminant:

**Biochar (activated charcoal):**
- Application: 5-10% by weight (100-200 lbs per 1000 sq ft for 6-inch depth)
- Mechanism: Binds heavy metals and organic compounds to surface; reduces plant uptake
- Effect: Reduces bioavailability 20-50% without removing contaminant
- Timeline: Immediate upon incorporation
- Cost: $0.50-2.00 per lb ($50-200 per 1000 sq ft)
- Best use: Supplement to other strategies, not replacement

**Organophosphates or phosphate amendments (for heavy metals):**
- Application: 1-2% by weight (targeted, not whole-site)
- Mechanism: Forms insoluble metal phosphates; reduces availability
- Effect: Moderate reduction in bioavailability
- Timeline: Weeks
- Cost: $100-300 per treatment

**Lime (as above):**
- Secondary benefit: Higher pH reduces lead and cadmium mobility
- Application as pH amendment inherently immobilizes metals

### Organic Matter Enhancement

Most urban soils are deficient in organic matter (typical 2-3%; should be 5-10% for productive gardens).

**Amendments:**
- Finished compost: $30-60/cubic yard (2-3 inches incorporated)
- Composted bark or aged wood chips: $15-30/cubic yard
- Manure-based compost: $20-40/cubic yard
- Worm castings: $100-200/cubic yard (expensive but high quality; use 1 inch mixed in)

**Protocol:**
1. Test existing organic matter (send soil sample to lab with request)
2. Add amendment to reach 5-8% organic matter
3. Incorporate 2-4 inches into top 6-8 inches of soil
4. Retest after 1 season to confirm improvement
5. Annual maintenance: Add 1 inch fresh compost yearly (refreshes nutrients and structure)

<section id="crop-selection">

## Safe Crop Selection by Contamination Type

Different crops accumulate different contaminants. Choose based on identified contamination.

### Risk Hierarchy by Contamination Type

**Lead-contaminated soil:**
- **Avoid:** Leafy greens (lettuce, spinach, kale), herbs — highest uptake
- **Caution:** Root crops (carrots, potatoes, beets) — moderate uptake; peel before eating reduces risk
- **Safer:** Fruiting crops (tomatoes, peppers, beans, squash) — fruit tissue low uptake; roots do accumulate but fruit clean
- **Safest strategy:** Focus on fruiting crops; use raised beds for greens

**Cadmium-contaminated soil:**
- **Avoid:** Leafy greens, legumes (peas, beans, soybeans) — highest uptake
- **Caution:** Root crops, especially potatoes — moderate uptake
- **Safer:** Fruiting crops, though uptake variable by variety
- **Safest strategy:** Avoid site entirely; use raised beds only

**Arsenic-contaminated soil:**
- **Avoid:** Rice (hyperaccumulates arsenic in grain)
- **Caution:** Root crops; leafy greens less affected; arsenic partially surface-adhered
- **Safer:** Fruiting crops, grains other than rice
- **Safest strategy:** Raised beds for leafy greens; ground planting for fruiting crops

**Petroleum-contaminated soil:**
- **Avoid:** None directly toxic to plants, but soil toxicity affects growth
- **Caution:** High PAH (polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon) areas — some compounds phytotoxic
- **Safer:** Phytoremediation or raised beds while cleaning
- **Safest strategy:** Raised beds short-term; long-term phytoremediation with willows/grasses

**PCB-contaminated soil:**
- **Risk:** Low plant uptake; primary risk animal grazing/chickens accumulate in fat tissue
- **Acceptable:** Any vegetables; avoid animal production directly on site
- **Safest strategy:** Vegetable production acceptable; chicken/goat grazing not recommended

### Practical Crop Plan by Contamination Level

**Mild contamination (low ppm, <100 lead equivalent):**
- Fruiting crops: tomatoes, peppers, squash, beans, peas
- Root crops: carrots, beets (peel after harvest)
- Direct ground planting acceptable

**Moderate contamination (100-500 ppm equivalent):**
- Fruiting crops only in ground
- All leafy greens in raised beds with clean soil
- Hybrid approach: raised beds for sensitive crops, ground for tolerant ones

**Severe contamination (>500 ppm):**
- Raised beds or containers for all food crops
- Long-term phytoremediation of ground area
- Ground planting deferred 5+ years

<section id="water-management">

## Water Source Assessment and Management

### Rainwater Harvesting

Most practical water source for urban gardens.

**Collection:** From rooftops, parking lot runoff (if not heavily trafficked), green roofs.

**Storage:** 55-gallon drums, cisterns, or rain barrels.

**Sizing:**
- 1 inch rainfall on 1000 sq ft roof = ~620 gallons captured
- Summer watering needs: 1-2 inches per week for vegetables = 600-1200 gallons per week
- Calculate days of buffering needed; size storage accordingly

**Quality:** Rainwater generally good for food crops; may contain light particulates (filter through cloth before use) and atmospheric fallout (lead in urban areas — be aware but usually minor contributor).

**Treatment:**
- Simple settling (let sit in tank 24 hours; particulates drop)
- Cloth filtration (cheesecloth, screen)
- No chemical treatment needed for vegetable watering

**Cost:** Used 55-gallon drums $5-15 each; gutters $0.50/linear foot.

### Well Testing (if applicable)

If using well water, test for:
- Bacterial contamination (coliform test)
- Heavy metals (lead, cadmium, arsenic — full panel)
- Nitrates (fertilizer runoff contamination)

Test cost: $30-150 depending on panel. University extension labs often perform well testing.

### Municipal Water

If using city water:
- Generally safe for food production (tested for safety)
- Chlorine and fluoride present (safe at typical levels for irrigation)
- Cost: Varies; typically $5-15 per 1000 gallons in cities
- Consider cost for frequent watering (daily in summer)

### Irrigation Water Contamination

If source suspected of contamination:

**Mercury, arsenic, pesticides, solvents present:**
- Do not use for edible crops
- Raise beds with imported water source preferred

**High nitrates (>20 ppm):**
- Likely fertilizer runoff
- Acceptable for most crops, though high nitrate vegetables (spinach, lettuce) may exceed safe levels if already high
- Test leafy greens after harvest if concerned

**High salts (white deposit accumulation):**
- Typical for groundwater in arid regions, road salt near highways
- Use rainwater or lower-salt source when possible
- Amend soil with gypsum and leaching to reduce salt accumulation

## Building Infrastructure for Urban Farms

### Season Extension Techniques

Urban microclimates allow season extension with minimal equipment:

**Cold frames (salvaged windows):**
- Build simple box (2×3 feet) from wood
- Place salvaged window or plastic sheet on top at angle (south-facing)
- Traps heat; allows growing 2-4 weeks beyond normal season
- Cost: $0 (salvaged materials) to $20 (new window)

**Row covers (fabric):**
- Lightweight fabric over plants on hoops
- Protects from frost; extends season 2-4 weeks each end
- Cost: $0.10-0.20/sq ft
- Reusable for 5+ years

**Cloches (individual plant covers):**
- Mason jars, plastic milk jugs, commercial plastic cloches over individual plants
- Protects delicate seedlings; extends season
- Cost: $0 (recycled) to $2 per cloche

**Urban heat retention:**
- Buildings and pavement retain heat; use as microclimate
- Plant gardens on south-facing sides of buildings
- Use dark mulch to absorb/retain heat

### Structural Assessments for Rooftop Gardens

If considering rooftop growing:

**Load-bearing capacity:**
- Residential roofs typically support 20-40 lbs per square foot
- Container gardens: 4×8 bed with soil = ~4000 lbs (500 lbs/sq ft local)
- Most residential roofs cannot support traditional gardens
- Lightweight: 5-gallon containers (40 lbs full) spaced; 4-6 per 100 sq ft manageable

**Structural engineer assessment:** Required for loads >20 lbs/sq ft; cost $500-2000 but essential

**Drainage:** Roof slope must drain; water should not pool. Container gardens with drainage holes acceptable; raised beds problematic.

**Wind:** Rooftop wind speeds 1.5-2x ground level. Use shorter varieties, stake plants, or build windbreak.

**Access:** Roof must be safe to work on; stairs, railings required for human safety.

**Feasibility:** Most residential roofs better left for solar; ground-level and container gardens more practical.

### Vertical Growing Systems

Maximize yield from space constraints:

**Trellis systems:** Peas, beans, cucumber, squash climb 6-8 feet, producing heavily while occupying small footprint.

**Wall planters:** Fabric pockets mounted on walls/fences; useful for herbs, strawberries, lettuce.

**Hanging baskets:** Suitable for herbs, small tomatoes, trailing plants like nasturtium.

**Combination:** 50-100 sq ft ground space + vertical systems on fences/walls yields food equivalent to 200+ sq ft traditional garden.

## Composting in Urban Environments

### Pathogen-Safe Composting Temperatures

Hot composting (aerobic decomposition reaching 130-150°F) kills most pathogens. Critical for safety if composting human or animal waste.

**Pathogen kill temperatures and times:**
- E. coli: 140°F for 1 hour, or 113°F for 5 days
- Salmonella: 140°F for 5 minutes
- Viable weed seeds: 140°F for 30 minutes
- Most pathogens: 130°F for 30 minutes adequate

**Temperature management:**
- Carbon:nitrogen ratio 25:1-30:1 optimal for heat generation
- Moisture 50-60% (not soggy, not dry)
- Aeration: Turn pile weekly to maintain high temperature
- Brown materials (leaves, straw, wood chips) are carbon; green (grass, food waste, manure) are nitrogen

**Timeline:** Hot pile method = 30-90 days for fully finished compost (temperature peaks weeks 1-2, drops weeks 3-6 as pile metabolizes).

**Monitoring:** Use thermometer probe; pile should maintain >130°F weeks 1-3.

### Separating Contaminated Compost

If site contaminated and you're composting on-site, ensure end product is safe.

**Risk:** Heavy metals accumulate in compost if feedstock or water contains them. Risk low if feedstock is clean (food scraps, yard waste).

**Protocol:**
1. Do not use finished compost if source contamination certain
2. If in doubt, use compost only for non-food landscaping (ornamentals, trees, shrubs)
3. For food gardens, compost from trusted clean sources (community agricultural sites, farmer's markets, grocery stores)

**Acceptable feedstock for food-garden compost:**
- Food waste (fruits, vegetables, grains, coffee grounds)
- Yard waste (leaves, grass, small branches — no pesticide-treated herbicide-treated material)
- Paper and cardboard (shredded, no glossy/colored ink)

**Not acceptable:**
- Meat, bones, fats (attract rodents; slow decomposition; disease risk)
- Diseased plant material (spreads disease)
- Chemically treated wood or sawdust (residual herbicides/pesticides)
- Animal waste (unless specifically hot-composted to safety temps)

## Food Safety Testing and Monitoring

### Harvest Testing

Once growing on remediated or isolated beds, periodic testing ensures safety:

**Soil-plant transfer testing:**
- After first season, harvest samples of crops grown on site
- Send leaves (for lead/cadmium) and root crops (all metals) to lab
- Request analysis for contaminants of concern
- Cost: $50-200 per sample

**Interpretation:**
- Lead in leafy greens >0.2 ppm problematic (FDA guidance <0.1 ppm)
- Cadmium in any crop >0.05 ppm problematic
- If levels low, continue monitoring yearly; if high, adjust practices (more lime, less bioavailable form)

### Long-Term Monitoring Schedule

**Year 1-2:** Frequent testing (monthly during season) to establish baselines

**Year 3+:** Quarterly testing (spring, summer, fall); annually if results consistently safe

**Soil retesting:** Every 2-3 years if phytoremediation ongoing; annually if on raised beds with imported soil (to track maintenance needs)

### Consumer Safety

If community garden producing food for broader population:

**Documentation:**
- Keep records of soil test results, amendments applied, crop testing
- Display summary at garden: "This produce tested 2026 and meets EPA guidelines"

**Labeling:** Consider signage "Grown in remediated soil — tested safe for consumption" if site has history; builds confidence.

**Guidance:** Recommend washing produce thoroughly (removes surface dust/particles) but note deeper contaminants absorbed into tissue cannot be removed by washing.

## Conclusion

Urban agriculture transforms contaminated or vacant land into productive community food systems. Initial strategy typically combines isolated raised beds (immediate production) with long-term soil amendment and phytoremediation. Within 3-5 years, many sites become safe for direct ground planting. Success requires testing for contamination, understanding crop selection by contaminant type, and consistent amendments to build soil health. Community engagement and training ensure sustained participation and food security benefits.
