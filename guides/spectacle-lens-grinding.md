Spectacles correct vision by using curved lenses to focus light onto the retina. Without access to commercial eyewear, understanding basic optics and lens fabrication enables self-correction.

:::routing

<section id="optics-basics">

## Optics Basics

### Light Refraction

Light bends when passing through a transparent material at an angle. The degree of bending depends on material density (refractive index).

**Refractive indices:**
- Air: 1.00
- Water: 1.33
- Glass: 1.5–1.8 (depends on composition)
- Crown glass: ~1.52
- Flint glass: ~1.65

Higher index = stronger bending = thinner lens for same power.

### Lens Types

**Convex (plus) lens:**
- Thicker in center than edges
- Bends light rays toward center (converges)
- Creates enlarged upright image at close distances
- Used for farsightedness (hyperopia)
- Focal length is positive

**Concave (minus) lens:**
- Thinner in center than edges
- Bends light rays outward (diverges)
- Creates reduced virtual image
- Used for nearsightedness (myopia)
- Focal length is negative

**Spherical vs. aspheric:**
- **Spherical:** Constant curvature; adequate for most uses
- **Aspheric:** Variable curvature; reduces edge distortion; complex to grind

### Focal Length and Power

**Focal length (f):** Distance from lens center where parallel light rays converge (or appear to diverge from).

**Lens power (diopters, D):** Reciprocal of focal length in meters.

Diopters = 1 / focal length (meters)

**Examples:**
- f = 0.5 meters → Power = 1/0.5 = +2 diopters (convex, moderate correction)
- f = 1.0 meter → Power = 1/1.0 = +1 diopter (weak convex)
- f = -1.0 meter → Power = 1/(-1.0) = -1 diopter (weak concave)

**Typical prescriptions:**
- Mild myopia: -0.5 to -3 diopters
- Moderate myopia: -3 to -6 diopters
- Severe myopia: -6 to -10+ diopters
- Mild hyperopia: +0.5 to +2 diopters
- Moderate hyperopia: +2 to +4 diopters

<section id="vision-assessment">

## Vision Assessment Without Equipment

### Acuity Testing

**Pinhole test (no lenses needed):**
1. Make small hole in opaque card (2–3mm diameter)
2. Look through hole; vision improves temporarily
3. Indicates refractive error (if pinhole helps, lens may help)
4. Does NOT determine lens power

**Distance vision:**
- Stand 20 feet from object (or use proportional distance indoors)
- Identify smallest readable text or detail
- Compare each eye separately

**Near vision:**
- Hold text 12–14 inches from eyes
- Smallest readable text indicates near vision capability

### Simple Prescription Estimation

**For myopia (nearsightedness):**
- Point where vision blurs: Test distance
- If sharp at 18 inches, ~3 diopter myopia (1/0.45m ≈ -2.2)
- If sharp at 6 inches, severe myopia (~-5 to -8 diopters)

**For hyperopia (farsightedness):**
- Test near vision; if blurred, likely hyperopia
- Amount of blurring helps estimate correction

**Astigmatism:**
- Lines in one direction blurry, others clear
- Requires cylindrical (toric) lens; difficult to produce
- Spherical approximation may partially help

### Presbyopia (Age-Related)

Need for reading glasses increases with age due to lens hardening.

- 40 years: +1 to +1.5 diopter reading lens
- 50 years: +2 to +2.5 diopters
- 60 years: +2.5 to +3.5 diopters
- 70+ years: +3 to +4 diopters

Bifocal solution: Half lens is +1 to +2 stronger for reading (see section on bifocals).

<section id="lens-blank-preparation">

## Lens Blank Preparation

### Glass Sources

**Options:**
- Glass made locally (see glass-making guide)
- Salvaged bottle glass (clear bottles; green/brown unsuitable)
- Salvaged optical glass (camera lenses, old spectacles, scientific instruments)
- Recycled optical materials (discarded glasses, magnifying glasses)

**Quality requirements:**
- Transparent and colorless (iron impurities create tint; acceptable but slightly reduce light)
- Bubble-free (bubbles distort image)
- Hard enough to grind without shattering
- Uniform thickness if casting flat plate

### Forming Blanks from Flat Glass

**For simple spherical lenses:**

1. **Heat glass disk:**
   - Cut flat glass circle roughly the desired lens size (slightly oversized)
   - Heat in furnace or kiln to softening temperature (~650°C)
   - Do NOT bring to molten state (becomes unworkable)

2. **Sag (slump) over mold:**
   - Place heated glass disk over stone or clay mold (carved to target curvature)
   - Gravity pulls glass into curved shape
   - Cool slowly to prevent cracking
   - Target shape: roughly spherical convex or concave

3. **Rough shaping:**
   - Allow to cool completely
   - Use grinding stone to rough-shape to desired curvature

**For stronger curvatures:**
- Repeat the sagging process with new glass
- Use progressively deeper molds

### Salvaged Lens Polishing

If using salvaged optical lenses:

1. **Clean thoroughly:**
   - Wipe with soft cloth
   - Use gentle brush (bristles) to remove dust
   - Avoid scratching with hard particles

2. **Assess condition:**
   - Look for chips, major scratches, or coating damage
   - Minor scratches may be polishable
   - Deep cracks: lens unusable

3. **Polish if necessary:**
   - Use fine abrasive (rouge powder, tin oxide) with soft cloth
   - Gentle circular motions
   - Restore clarity if optical surfaces are slightly clouded

<section id="grinding-tools">

## Grinding Tools and Techniques

### Grinding Stones

**Abrasive grades (by particle size):**
- Coarse: #100–#220 (removes glass quickly; high scratches)
- Medium: #320–#600 (moderate removal; medium scratches)
- Fine: #800–#1200 (slow removal; fine scratches)
- Very fine: #1500–#2000 (polishing; minimal scratches)
- Polishing: #3000+ or rouge powder (final polish)

**Grinding stone tools:**
1. **Rotating wheel** (ideal but requires power):
   - Stone wheel on axle rotated manually (crank) or by foot treadle
   - Consistent pressure maintains uniform curve
   - Risk: Large stone wheels are dangerous (pinch hazard)

2. **Hand-grinding stone:**
   - Flat block of abrasive stone or sandpaper glued to block
   - Slower but safer; portable
   - More control over pressure and curve

3. **Shaped grinding stone:**
   - If target curvature is known, custom-shaped stone stone improves efficiency
   - Convex stone for grinding concave lens
   - Concave stone for grinding convex lens
   - Very difficult to shape perfectly

### Manual Grinding Process

**Starting with flat glass blank:**

1. **Coarse grinding (remove excess material):**
   - Rub glass disk against coarse grinding stone
   - Use circular motions
   - Monitor curvature frequently (check against target shape)
   - Turn glass 45° occasionally for even grinding
   - Remove ~1mm thickness per 5 minutes

2. **Medium grinding (refine shape):**
   - Switch to #400–#600 stone
   - Continue circular motions
   - Reduce pressure (finer stone is more delicate)
   - Monitor frequently; approach target curve carefully

3. **Fine grinding (approach final form):**
   - Switch to #1000+ stone
   - Lighter pressure
   - Very frequent monitoring
   - Goal: Smooth surface, target curvature visible

4. **Polishing (final finish):**
   - Use rouge powder (iron oxide) or tin oxide
   - Apply with soft cloth or felt pad
   - Gentle circular motions for 2–5 minutes
   - Brings surface to optical clarity
   - Wipe clean with soft cloth

### Measuring and Monitoring Curve

**Simple focal length test (see next section):**
- Frequently test focal length during grinding
- Allows progressive refinement toward target power

**Visual inspection:**
- Light reflection from surface indicates curve
- Regular reflection = spherical; distorted = aspheric (undesirable)

**Comparing lenses:**
- If salvaging old spectacles, compare curve to target lens
- Hold both against light; similar curves look similar

</section>

<section id="focal-length-measurement">

## Focal Length Measurement

### Focal Length Test

**Setup:**
- Sunny location or bright lamp
- Screen (white paper or cloth) mounted vertically
- Lens to be tested
- Measuring ruler

**Convex lens (plus):**
1. Hold lens between light source and screen
2. Move lens back and forth until sharp image of light source forms on screen
3. Distance from lens to sharp image = focal length
4. Calculate diopters: 1 / focal_length_in_meters

**Concave lens (minus):**
- Concave lenses don't form real image (light diverges)
- Use combination with known convex lens:
  1. Place concave lens against known convex lens
  2. Test combined focal length
  3. Calculate concave power: 1/combined = 1/convex + 1/concave
  4. Rearrange: 1/concave = 1/combined - 1/convex

### Reciprocal Equation

If you have reference lens of known power P1 and test unknown lens P2:

Combined power = P1 + P2
Focal length of combination = 1 / combined power

Therefore: P2 = (1 / focal_length_combination) - P1

### Iterative Refinement

1. Grind lens partially
2. Test focal length
3. Compare to target diopters
4. If stronger needed: Continue grinding (removes material from center of convex; edge of concave)
5. If weaker needed: Reduce pressure; use finer abrasive
6. Repeat until within ±0.25 diopters of target

**Tolerance:** ±0.5 diopters is acceptable for most vision correction.

</section>

<section id="frame-fabrication">

## Frame Fabrication

### Wire Frames (Simplest)

**Materials:**
- Copper or iron wire (1.5–2.5mm diameter)
- Solder or strong adhesive (for assembly)

**Design:**
1. Form two circular loops (one per lens) from wire
2. Loops should match lens diameter (typically 35–50mm)
3. Connect loops with bridge (wire across nose bridge)
4. Add arms extending back over ears

**Assembly:**
1. Solder wire joints (requires heat source; see metalworking guide)
2. Or lash with thread/leather strips (less durable but feasible)
3. Test fit on face; adjust loops if uneven

**Securing lenses:**
- Groove wire loop or use wire clips
- Adhesive (resin, pitch, or plant glue) to hold lens
- Or: Wrap lens edge with leather strips to hold in place

### Wooden Frames

**Materials:**
- Hardwood (oak, walnut) or softer wood (pine with leather reinforcement)
- Leather strips for reinforcement
- Solder or adhesive

**Design:**
1. Carve or shape two eye sockets (holes slightly larger than lens)
2. Create bridge between eye sockets
3. Attach temples (arms) with leather hinge or wood joints
4. Reinforce temples with leather binding

**Lens mounting:**
1. Lens sits in carved socket
2. Leather strap wraps around lens edge and wood frame
3. Glue or lash leather to wood

### Salvaged Frames

Most practical option (if salvage available):
1. Remove broken lenses
2. Insert new ground lenses
3. Secure with adhesive or existing retention mechanism
4. Adjust bend/fit as needed

</section>

<section id="fitting-adjustment">

## Fitting and Adjustment

### Pupillary Distance

Distance between pupils determines proper lens alignment.

**Measurement:**
- Stand face-to-face with helper
- Helper measures distance from center of left pupil to center of right pupil
- Typical range: 55–75mm

**Application:**
- When installing lenses, position lens center ~55–70mm apart
- Misalignment causes eye strain and discomfort

### Height and Angle

**Optical center height:**
- Lens center should align with pupil height
- Too high/low causes looking through wrong part of lens
- Adjust frames to position lens center at pupil level

**Pantoscopic angle:**
- Frame tilts slightly forward (8–10 degrees)
- Reduces reflections and improves comfort
- Gradually adjust bend of temple if needed

### Temple Length

**Fitting:**
- Temples should reach past ears to temple notch (behind ears)
- Adjust by bending temple slightly or cutting/adding material
- Loosely seated: Frames slip down; too tight: Uncomfortable

### Bridge Fit

**Comfort:**
- Bridge should rest on nose without pinching
- Minor pressure distributes weight
- Adjust by carefully bending bridge if metal/wire, or adding padding (leather or cloth)

</section>

<section id="magnifying-glass">

## Magnifying Glass Construction

Convex lens becomes magnifier with focal length short enough to bring object into focus very close to eye.

**Target focal length:** 5–10cm for comfortable reading magnification (5–10x power).

**Construction:**
1. Grind simple convex lens (high power; ~10 diopters = 0.1m focal length)
2. Mount in simple frame or handle
3. Hold 5–10cm from object being viewed
4. Image appears magnified

**Magnification:** Magnification = 25cm / focal_length_in_cm (approximately)

- Focal length 5cm → ~5x magnification
- Focal length 10cm → ~2.5x magnification

</section>

<section id="sun-protection">

## Sun Protection

### Protecting Eyes in Bright Conditions

**Darkened lenses (sunglasses):**
1. Use darker glass (if glass-making allows) or add iron oxide coloring
2. Or: Smoke lens (heat glass until surface blackens slightly)
3. Or: Coat lens with clay mixed with carbon/charcoal (hardens when dry)

**Effective shading:**
- Reduces transmitted light by 75–90%
- Protects against UV (though not ideal as UV filter)
- Prevents glare and strain

**Optional UV protection:**
- Not achievable in low-tech fabrication (requires special coatings)
- Regular dark lenses provide some protection by reducing pupil dilation
- Long-term UV exposure → cataracts (preventable by wearing any shade)

### Snow Blindness Prevention

In high-reflection environments (snow, water, sand):
- Darker lenses essential
- Slitted glasses (narrow horizontal opening; blocks reflected rays) effective supplement
- Closed glasses with small gaps reduce 90%+ of reflection

</section>

<section id="bifocals">

## Bifocal Lenses

Simple bifocal solution without grinding two lenses:

**Two-part mounting:**
1. Main lens: Distance correction
2. Small secondary lens: Reading correction
3. Mount secondary lens in lower half of frame
4. User looks up through main lens (distance), down through secondary (reading)

**Secondary lens power:**
- Calculate as: Reading power = Distance power + Reading addition
- If -2 diopter distance correction and +2.5 diopter reading addition needed
- Secondary lens = -2 + 2.5 = +0.5 diopters

**Mounting:**
- Simple approach: Frame two separate lenses in same mounting
- Lower lens smaller, covering reading area
- Upper lens covers full opening
- Position dividing line at pupil height when looking straight

</section>

<section id="troubleshooting-vision">

## Troubleshooting and Limitations

| Problem | Likely Cause | Solution |
|---|---|---|
| Lens scratches after grinding | Using stone too coarse; or contamination | Use finer stone; ensure surface clean before grinding |
| Blurry edges | Spherical abberation or tilt | Grind for more uniform curve; check frame fit |
| Uneven power between eyes | Inconsistent grinding | Test each lens; regrind as needed |
| Lens slides down | Frame too loose | Adjust bridge; add padding; tighten temples |
| Eye strain with new glasses | Too strong correction or prism effect | Reduce diopters slightly; check pupillary distance |
| Can't achieve target power | Glass too thick/thin or improper curve | Reselect blank or adjust curve more carefully |

### Limitations

- **Astigmatism:** Requires cylindrical lens; difficult to grind by hand
- **Presbyopia in myopes:** May need bifocals or accept lower distance vision
- **High myopia/hyperopia:** May be impossible to correct adequately by grinding (requires very thick or very thin lens)
- **Color deficiency (color blindness):** Cannot be corrected with simple lenses

</section>

<section id="conclusion">

## Conclusion

Spectacle fabrication requires understanding basic optics, careful lens grinding to achieve target focal length, and proper frame fitting for comfort and optical alignment. While hand-grinding limits precision compared to commercial manufacture, adequate vision correction is achievable with patience and iterative testing. Focal length measurement is critical; frequent testing during grinding allows progressive refinement toward target prescription.

</section>
