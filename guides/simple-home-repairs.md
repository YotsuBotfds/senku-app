:::warning
**Know when to stop and call it structural.** If a wall is bowing, a ceiling is sagging, a floor is soft or bouncing, a roof is dropping, a beam is cracked, a stair is loose, or a door/window is suddenly out of square after a storm or impact, do not keep patching blindly. Those are signs of structural movement, rot, or water damage that may need a builder or engineer rather than a quick repair.
:::

Most home repairs are complaint-driven: a door will not close, a window leaks air, a wall has a small hole, a drain backs up a little, or a valve or latch sticks. Start by naming the failure mode, then choose the smallest repair that makes the home safe, usable, and less exposed to weather and wear. If the complaint is active water from above, a wet ceiling, or rain entry, hand off to the roof leak guide first.

If you are unsure what kind of problem you have, or you are asking "what should I check first," start with three complaint-first questions:

1. Is it an active leak, a wet ceiling, or water coming from above?
2. Is it letting in air, pests, or dirt through a door, window, or wall opening?
3. Is it preventing a door, window, valve, or latch from moving normally?

If the answer is yes, you probably have a repair that can be triaged in place.

:::info
**First-check: home repair failure modes**
Before reaching for tools, run this quick scan:

1. **Active water dripping, soaked materials, or a wet ceiling?** -> identify the source first; roof leaks go to [Roof Leak Emergency Repair](roof-leak-emergency-repair.html), tank/container leaks go to [Water Storage Tank Maintenance](water-storage-tank-maintenance.html)
2. **Ceiling sagging, wall bowing, or floor bouncing?** -> structural; stop patching and escalate
3. **Water near wiring, outlets, or metal fixtures?** -> electrical hazard; shut off power before touching anything
4. **Gas smell or utility odor near the repair area?** -> utility hazard; stop work, ventilate, and evacuate if strong
5. **Broken glass or exposed sharp edges?** -> safety hazard; secure the opening before weatherproofing
6. **Stuck door, window, valve, or latch?** -> alignment or dirt; if it is a hand pump or shutoff, hand off to [Hand Pump Repair and Maintenance](hand-pump-repair-maintenance.html) before forcing it
7. **Stored water is green, slimy, cloudy, or stale-smelling?** -> container or source problem; check [Water Storage Tank Maintenance](water-storage-tank-maintenance.html) and [Questionable Water Assessment and Clarification](questionable-water-assessment-clarification.html)
8. **Draft, gap, or air leak?** -> seal gap; check weatherstripping and close the opening

If the problem does not match any of these, it is likely cosmetic or low urgency, and it probably belongs in a later section rather than an emergency guide.
:::

## 1. How To Triage A Household Repair

Before fixing anything, decide whether the problem is urgent, temporary, or safe to leave alone for a while.

### Repair categories

| Problem type | What it looks like | What to do first |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Urgent water entry | Active leak, dripping, soaked materials, wet ceiling | Protect contents, contain water, patch or cover immediately |
| Urgent safety issue | Loose stair, broken glass, exposed wiring, falling piece | Make area safe and keep people away |
| Functional problem | Sticking door, jammed latch, drafty window, slow drain | Repair soon, but not necessarily right this minute |
| Cosmetic problem | Small scuffs, hairline cracks, chipped paint | Monitor or schedule later |

### Ask what failed

Most repairs start with one of these failures:

- Something cracked.
- Something shifted.
- Something wore out.
- Something loosened.
- Something clogged.
- Something is out of alignment.

If you know the failure type, you can often pick the right fix without overdoing it.

:::tip
Use the simplest repair that solves the problem. Many household failures do not need a permanent rebuild. They need a temporary seal, an adjustment, or a replacement part.
:::

## 2. Roof Patch Triage

For active leaks, rain entry, emergency tarping, tracing the entry point, and interior water damage, use the dedicated guide: [Roof Leak Emergency Repair](roof-leak-emergency-repair.html).

The general checklist here covers only the triage decision:

- **Active water dripping or soaked materials?** -> [Roof Leak Emergency Repair](roof-leak-emergency-repair.html) — do not patch from this guide.
- **Ceiling sagging or bulging?** -> structural hazard; see the emergency guide, then stop and escalate.
- **Water near wiring, outlets, or metal fixtures?** -> electrical hazard; shut off power before touching anything.
- **Roof surface wet, steep, icy, or visibly failing?** -> do not climb. Use ground-based or attic-based containment from the emergency guide.
- **Stain or past leak, no active water now?** -> treat as a monitoring item. Check for soft wood, mold, and the cause before closing it up.

If the problem is not an active leak, the remaining sections of this guide cover doors, windows, walls, drainage, valves, and general safety escalation.

## 3. Sticking Doors, Loose Latches, And Jammed Hardware

Doors and latches usually fail for simple reasons: swelling, settling, dirt, rust, or loose screws.

### Sticking door checklist

1. Open and close the door slowly to find where it rubs.
2. Look for scraped paint or shiny wood at the contact point.
3. Tighten hinge screws first.
4. Lubricate hinges if they squeak or bind.
5. Plane, sand, or trim only the high spot if the door is swollen or warped.

### Common door fixes

- Tighten hinge screws that have worked loose.
- Replace stripped screws with longer ones.
- Clean dirt from the latch and strike plate.
- Lubricate metal moving parts lightly.
- Adjust the strike plate if the latch misses.

### Jammed latch or lock

If a latch sticks:

- Check for misalignment between latch and strike plate.
- Look for rust, dirt, or bent metal.
- Try lubrication first.
- Replace broken springs or bent parts if cleaning does not help.

If a lock is not functioning smoothly, do not force it. Forcing hardware can break the key, snap the latch, or damage the door frame.

## 4. Broken Window Panes And Drafty Windows

Broken glass is both a safety hazard and a weather problem. Treat it in two steps: make it safe, then make it weather-tight.

### If a pane is broken

1. Keep hands away from loose shards.
2. Wear gloves if available.
3. Remove unstable broken pieces carefully.
4. Sweep and pick up all fragments.
5. Cover the opening with cardboard, plastic, plywood, or board until you can replace the glass.

### Replacing a small pane

- Measure the opening carefully.
- Cut or fit the new pane slightly smaller than the frame allowance.
- Clean the frame before installing.
- Use glazing compound, putty, or appropriate fasteners to hold it in place.
- Seal the edges so air and water cannot enter.

### Drafty window triage

If the window is intact but cold air is coming through:

- Check whether the sash is fully closed.
- Inspect weatherstripping.
- Seal gaps with removable film, rope caulk, tape, or a temporary barrier.
- Check for warped frames or failed seals.

### When a window problem is structural

If the frame is badly rotted, the wall around the window is shifting, or multiple windows suddenly stop fitting properly, the problem may be more than a window repair. That suggests movement, water damage, or framing failure.

Stop and escalate if rot extends beyond a small local patch, if the frame crumbles under light probing, or if the wall around the opening is soft, stained, and sagging.

## 5. Small Wall Holes, Cracks, And Surface Damage

Small wall damage is common and usually easy to fix. The key is to separate cosmetic damage from active movement.

### Small hole repair

For nail holes, dents, or small punctures:

1. Clean loose debris from the hole.
2. Fill with patching compound, putty, mud, plaster, or a suitable filler.
3. Let it dry fully.
4. Sand smooth.
5. Repaint or reseal if needed.

### Larger holes

For holes that are too large to fill directly:

- Use a backing piece if needed.
- Patch with mesh, wood, or board depending on the wall type.
- Apply compound in layers rather than trying to fill everything at once.

### Wall cracks

Hairline cracks are often cosmetic. Wider cracks, stair-step cracks, or cracks that grow over time may indicate settling or structural movement.

Check whether the crack:

- Reopens after repair
- Runs near doors or windows
- Gets wider with time
- Comes with sticking doors or sloping floors

If so, do not just keep filling it. Investigate the cause.

## 6. Minor Drainage Problems

Water should move away from the house, not toward it. Small drainage issues can become foundation, mold, or erosion problems.

### Common drainage fixes

- Clear leaves, mud, or debris from gutters and downspouts.
- Extend downspouts so water leaves the foundation area.
- Regrade soil so it slopes away from the building.
- Fill shallow channels where water pools against walls.
- Keep splashback away from siding and window openings.

### Signs drainage is becoming a bigger problem

- Water pooling near the foundation after ordinary rain
- Damp basement or crawl space
- Moldy odors near the lower wall
- Soft soil, erosion, or sinking around the building edge
- Water stains that return after every storm

If water is repeatedly entering the same area, stop treating it as a one-time patch. Look for the route water is taking and fix that path.

## 7. Jammed Valves, Hinges, And Other Small Moving Parts

Many home failures are just moving parts that no longer move cleanly.

### Jammed valve or tap

1. Turn gently, not forcefully.
2. Check for mineral buildup, rust, or dirt.
3. Clean the exposed part.
4. Lubricate only with a suitable material for the part.
5. Replace seals, washers, or worn pieces if the part still sticks.

If the part is a water shutoff valve, slow down:

- Do not force a corroded valve with a long handle or extra leverage.
- Stop if the stem twists, the body moves in the wall, or the packing nut starts leaking.
- If the shutoff is badly rusted or feels like it may snap, treat replacement as a plumbing job rather than a quick hardware adjustment.

### Jammed latch or hinge

- Clean the mechanism.
- Tighten mounting screws.
- Replace bent or broken pieces.
- Avoid brute force that tears the hardware out of the frame.

If the part is corroded through, cracked, or visibly distorted, replacement is better than repeated adjustment.

</section>

<section id="unsafe">

## 8. When To Stop And Treat It As Unsafe

Some repairs are not home fixes. They are warning signs.

Stop and get help if you see:

- Sagging roof or ceiling
- Bulging wall
- Rot that makes wood soft or crumbly over a wide area
- Cracked beam, post, or joist
- Floor movement or bounce that was not there before
- Electrical damage near water
- Gas smell or other utility hazard
- Large glass area with unstable framing

:::danger
Do not keep "making it work" if the building is shifting, settling suddenly, or failing after water damage, impact, or fire. Patching over structural movement can hide a serious hazard.
:::

<section id="kit">

## 9. A Small Repair Kit Helps A Lot

A basic household repair kit does not need to be fancy. A few reliable tools solve many problems:

- Screwdrivers
- Adjustable wrench
- Hammer
- Utility knife
- Pliers
- Tape measure
- Putty knife
- Sandpaper
- Sealant or caulk
- Nails, screws, and washers
- Plastic sheet or tarp
- Work gloves

If you keep only one habit, keep the habit of fixing small damage early. Small repairs are cheaper, safer, and easier than rebuilding after neglect.

<section id="examples">

## 10. Fast Answers To Common Problems

- Leaking roof: see [Roof Leak Emergency Repair](roof-leak-emergency-repair.html) — contain water first, then tarp or patch the source.
- Sticking door: tighten hinges and check where it rubs.
- Broken window: make it safe, then cover and replace the pane.
- Small wall hole: clean, fill, sand, and repaint.
- Drafty window: seal gaps and replace failed weatherstripping.
- Drainage issue: move water away from the house.
- Jammed valve or latch: clean, lubricate, realign, or replace.

If the repair is no longer about a small part and starts involving the frame, beam, roof line, or foundation, it has likely moved beyond simple home repair.
