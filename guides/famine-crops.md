## Why Famine Crops Matter

Famine crops are plant species selected and cultivated specifically to survive environmental stress and provide reliable nutrition when conventional crops fail. In emergency scenarios—whether caused by drought, flooding, pest infestations, or economic collapse—these resilient plants can mean the difference between survival and starvation.

### Key Advantages

:::card
#### Environmental Resilience

Famine crops tolerate extreme conditions: severe droughts, poor soil quality, excessive rainfall, pest pressure, and temperature fluctuations. Where main crops fail, these plants thrive.
:::

:::card
#### Caloric Efficiency

Selected for high calorie production per unit of land and water. Many famine crops yield 2-3x more calories per acre than conventional grains in adverse conditions.
:::

:::card
#### Nutritional Completeness

Strategic combinations provide complete proteins, essential micronutrients, and dietary diversity with minimal inputs. Some species like amaranth and moringa alone meet multiple nutritional requirements.
:::

:::card
#### Storage Stability

Many famine crops preserve for years in simple conditions. Grains dry to low moisture, roots store in ground or cool spaces, and seeds maintain viability without special facilities.
:::

:::card
#### Low Input Requirements

No chemical fertilizers, pesticides, or sophisticated equipment needed. Growth from cuttings, volunteer seeds, and minimal tillage reduce dependency on supply chains.
:::

:::card
#### Multi-Part Utilization

Many species provide food from leaves (greens), seeds (grains), roots (carbohydrates), and tubers. No waste, maximum nutrition extraction.
:::

## Selection Criteria for Famine Crops

When choosing which famine crops to grow, consider these evaluation criteria to match plants to your environment and needs:

### Calorie Density

Measured in calories per 100g of edible material. Prioritize crops yielding 200+ calories per 100g for concentrated energy in small growing space. Root crops like cassava (160 cal/100g) provide bulk calories; grains like sorghum (329 cal/100g) concentrate calories in seeds.

### Growth Speed

Crop cycle from planting to harvest determines production frequency. Fast-cycling crops (60-90 days) like buckwheat and amaranth allow multiple harvests or rapid response to crisis. Slower crops (6-12 months) like cassava suit long-term storage strategy.

### Soil Tolerance

Critical for marginal land. Evaluate:

-   pH range acceptance (acidic vs. alkaline)
-   Drainage requirements (drought vs. waterlogged)
-   Fertility needs (fertility-demanding vs. pioneer species)
-   Salinity tolerance (coastal or arid regions)
-   Heavy metal accumulation risk

### Storage Characteristics

Evaluate moisture content at harvest, shelf life without special storage, temperature/humidity requirements, pest susceptibility, and whether in-ground storage preserves quality. For long-term preservation methods (drying, canning, fermenting), see **[Food Preservation](food-preservation.html)**.

### Nutritional Profile

Assess protein percentage, amino acid completeness, micronutrient density (especially calcium, iron, vitamin A, C), and whether crop addresses your dietary gaps.

### Water Efficiency

Calculate calories or nutrients per liter of water needed. In arid zones, prioritize sorghum, millet, amaranth, chia. In wet tropics, taro and cassava excel. For irrigation system design and water-delivery methods, see **[Irrigation & Water Management](irrigation-water.html)**.

### Pest and Disease Pressure

Crops with natural defenses (bitter compounds, hard seeds, toxic leaves) require less intervention. Assess pest populations in your region and disease pressure.

### Cassava / Manioc (Manihot esculenta)

**Calories:** 160/100g

**Growth:** 8-12 months

**Climate:** Tropical (zones 9-12)

**Protein:** 1.4g/100g

Cassava is the world's most important tropical famine crop, feeding over 800 million people. A single mature plant yields 5-15 pounds of edible starch-rich roots. Unlike grains requiring annual replanting, cassava persists 2-3 years, allowing long-term security.

#### Why Cassava Excels

-   Produces food when nothing else grows (extreme drought, poor soil, intense heat)
-   Stores underground naturally for 1-2 years; roots left in ground remain fresh
-   Propagates from cuttings (no seed infrastructure needed)
-   Yields 15-20 million calories per acre—higher than any grain in tropical regions
-   Utilizes all plant parts: roots for starch, leaves for greens (higher protein than roots)

#### Cyanide Content and Processing - CRITICAL FOR SURVIVAL

LIFE-THREATENING: Raw or improperly processed cassava causes KONZO (tropical ataxic neuropathy)—permanent paralysis of legs from cyanide accumulation. This is not a minor concern. Cassava roots contain cyanogenic glucosides that MUST be removed through proper processing. Improper processing = paralysis and death.

Cassava roots contain naturally-occurring cyanogenic glucosides that convert to cyanide if improperly processed. This is NOT a reason to avoid cassava—proper processing is simple and traditional methods eliminate toxins completely when done correctly.

##### CRITICAL CASSAVA CYANIDE REMOVAL PROCESS

:::danger
**CRITICAL — Cassava Cyanide Poisoning:** Raw or improperly processed cassava contains cyanogenic glucosides (linamarin) that release hydrogen cyanide (HCN) when consumed. Acute cyanide poisoning can be fatal. Chronic exposure causes konzo (irreversible paralytic disease) and tropical ataxic neuropathy.

**Mandatory Processing Steps:**
1. **Peel thoroughly** — the peel contains the highest cyanide concentration
2. **Soak in water for 3-5 days** minimum, changing water every 24 hours
3. **Grate or pound** the soaked cassava to increase surface area
4. **Sun-dry for 2-3 days** or roast thoroughly — heat drives off remaining HCN
5. **Boil in fresh water** before consumption — boiling alone is NOT sufficient without prior soaking

**Sweet vs bitter cassava:** Bitter varieties contain 5-10× more cyanide than sweet varieties. If unsure, treat ALL cassava as bitter and follow full processing. Never eat raw cassava. Never drink the soaking water.
:::

Konzo develops from chronic low-dose cyanide consumption. Populations eating cassava without proper processing develop paralysis within months. This is not theoretical—it has killed thousands in Africa and Asia. Proper processing MUST be followed without exception.

#### Cassava Processing Flowchart

![Famine Crops: Emergency Food Production diagram 1](../assets/svgs/famine-crops-1.svg)

#### Processing Methods

-   **Boiling:** Cut peeled roots into chunks, boil 20-40 minutes until tender. Boiling eliminates cyanide completely. Result tastes like potato. Most reliable method.
-   **Grating and Sun-Drying:** Peel, grate finely, spread on cloth 3-5 days in sun. Cyanide volatilizes during drying. Final product: gari (granules) or cassava flour. Stores 1+ years.
-   **Fermentation:** Traditional method—grate, ferment 3-7 days, squeeze juice, dry. Flavor improves, cyanide eliminated.

**Fact:** Properly processed cassava is completely safe. The cyanide converts to volatile gases during heating or drying—it does not accumulate in the body. Billions of people eat cassava daily worldwide.

#### Growing Cassava

-   **Propagation:** From woody stems (cuttings), not seeds. Cut mature stems into 6-8 inch pieces, plant upright 3-4 inches deep. Roots develop in 3-4 weeks.
-   **Spacing:** 3 feet x 3 feet or 4 feet x 3 feet (wider spacing = larger roots)
-   **Climate:** 60-70°F minimum; prefers 75-85°F. Tolerates 15-100 inches annual rain.
-   **Soil:** Grows in poor, acidic, sandy soil where other crops fail. Does not require fertility.
-   **Maintenance:** Minimal weeding first 3-4 months. Pest-free in most regions.
-   **Harvest:** 8-12 months. Roots continue producing years 2-3 if left in ground.

#### Leaf Nutrition (Cassava Tops)

Fresh cassava leaves contain 32g protein per 100g (dried), plus vitamins A, C, and minerals. Young leaves cook like spinach. Extremely valuable as protein supplement.

### Sorghum (Sorghum bicolor)

**Calories:** 329/100g

**Growth:** 4-5 months

**Climate:** Warm to hot (zones 6-10)

**Protein:** 8.7g/100g

Sorghum is the world's fifth most important cereal crop, feeding 500+ million people. It thrives where maize fails: extreme heat, severe drought, poor soil, and saline conditions. A single sorghum plant produces 1-2 ounces of grain; yields of 2-3 tons per acre are common.

#### Why Sorghum Excels

-   Drought-tolerant deep root system accesses water unavailable to shallow-rooted crops
-   Tolerates temperature extremes (110°F+) with minimal water
-   Alkaline-tolerant (grows in saline soils poisonous to wheat/corn)
-   Bird-resistant varieties exist (silica coating on seeds deters birds)
-   4-5 month cycle allows production in marginal seasons
-   Grain stores 10+ years without special preservation
-   Entire plant usable: grain (food), stalks (animal feed, biofuel), leaves (fodder)

#### Growing Sorghum

-   **Planting:** Direct seed after last frost when soil reaches 60°F. Space seeds 4-6 inches apart, 0.5 inch deep.
-   **Spacing:** Rows 18-36 inches apart (narrow = higher yield, wider = easier weeding)
-   **Climate:** Prefers 65-75°F for growth; tolerates 40-110°F range
-   **Soil:** pH 5.0-8.5; tolerates poor, dry, saline, and alkaline soils
-   **Water:** 15-25 inches seasonal (vs. 40+ for maize). Survives 10-15 inches if spaced widely.
-   **Harvest:** 120-150 days. Cut panicles when grain crushes but doesn't powder (milk-stage = wetter).

#### Sorghum Types

-   **Grain sorghum:** Large seeds, high yield (2-3 tons/acre), sweet grain varieties exist
-   **Forage sorghum:** Tall plants, leafy, excellent animal feed or silage
-   **Sweet sorghum:** Sugar-rich stalks, fermentable (syrup/ethanol), grain secondary benefit
-   **Bird-resistant varieties:** Hard-seeded types resist bird damage during ripening

#### Processing

-   Thresh by hand (beat heads) or foot to separate grain from chaff
-   Winnow to remove light chaff (toss in air, chaff blows away)
-   Grind whole grain in mill or stone mortar for flour
-   Boil whole grains like rice for porridge
-   Germinate grains to increase nutrient bioavailability (sprouting)

### Millet (Multiple Species)

**Calories:** 378/100g (pearl)

**Growth:** 3-4 months

**Climate:** Hot/dry (zones 7-10)

**Protein:** 11g/100g

Millet encompasses several grain species: pearl millet (most common), finger millet, foxtail millet, and proso millet. Collectively, millets feed 1 billion people in Africa and Asia. Extremely drought-tolerant and nutrient-dense, millets thrive in conditions that destroy other cereals.

#### Why Millet Excels

-   Extreme drought tolerance: survives on 8-16 inches rain annually (vs. 15-20 for sorghum)
-   Super-fast growth: 60-100 days to maturity (fastest grain crop)
-   Poor soil tolerance: grows in sandy, eroded, low-fertility soils
-   High micronutrient density: rich in iron, magnesium, phosphorus, B vitamins
-   Small grain size requires no threshing equipment—hand-stripping works fine
-   Grain stores 15+ years if dry

#### Millet Types

-   **Pearl Millet (Pennisetum glaucum):** Largest grain, highest yield (2-4 tons/acre), most nutritious. Grain 2-4mm diameter. Best all-around choice.
-   **Finger Millet (Eleusine coracana):** Small grain, excellent amino acid profile. Traditional in Africa. Wetter soils tolerated. 2-4 month cycle.
-   **Foxtail Millet (Setaria italica):** Small grain, quick 60-day cycle, extremely fine soil tolerance. Used for birdseed commercially.
-   **Proso Millet (Panicum miliaceum):** Ancient crop, nutritious, drought-tolerant, grows where others fail.

#### Growing Millet

-   **Planting:** Direct seed 2-3 weeks after last frost, 0.25-0.5 inch deep
-   **Spacing:** Rows 12-18 inches apart, thin seedlings to 3-6 inches
-   **Climate:** Prefers 70-85°F; tolerates 50-110°F
-   **Soil:** pH 5.0-7.5; grows in sand, gravel, and compacted clay where crops won't establish
-   **Water:** 10-20 inches seasonal; survives 8 inches in good conditions
-   **Harvest:** 60-100 days. Cut heads when grain is hard (won't dent with thumbnail)

#### Harvesting & Processing

-   Cut panicles when fully dry and grain kernels hard
-   Rub between palms or feet to shell grain (no threshing equipment needed)
-   Winnow by hand-tossing in shallow basket to remove chaff
-   Grind in mortar, hand mill, or stone grinder for flour
-   Whole grains: boil 15-20 minutes for porridge similar to rice

</section>

<section id="amaranth">

### Amaranth (Amaranthus species)

**Calories:** 371/100g (grain)

**Growth:** 60-90 days

**Climate:** Hot (zones 7-11)

**Protein:** 13.6g/100g (grain), 3.3g/100g (greens)

Amaranth is a dual-purpose crop providing both highly nutritious greens (early harvest) and protein-rich grain (late harvest). A single plant produces thousands of tiny seeds packed with complete amino acids, lysine (missing in most grains), magnesium, and iron.

#### Why Amaranth Excels

-   Complete protein grain: rare for plant foods, amaranth contains all 9 essential amino acids with 13.6% protein
-   Dual harvest: harvest leaves continuously as greens (cooked like spinach), then grain at maturity
-   Heat-thriving: prefers 75-95°F, minimal water needed, thrives in summer when other crops bolt
-   Fast-growing: 60-90 days grain, 30-45 days for greens
-   Poor soil tolerance: grows in degraded soils, phytoremediation capability
-   Pest-resistant: minimal pest/disease pressure
-   Nutritional density: grain and leaves both mineral-rich, including calcium and iron
-   Storage: 20+ year shelf life when dry

#### Growing Amaranth

-   **Planting:** Direct seed in warm soil (65°F+), 0.25 inch deep. Very fine seeds—mix with sand for even distribution.
-   **Spacing:** Rows 18 inches apart, thin to 8-12 inches within row
-   **Climate:** Minimum 60°F; thrives 75-95°F; tolerates extreme heat and full sun
-   **Soil:** pH 6.0-7.5; grows in poor, compacted, sandy, or clay soils. Not salt-tolerant.
-   **Water:** 15-18 inches seasonal; very drought-tolerant once established
-   **Harvesting greens:** Pinch top 4-6 inches at 30-45 days, plant continues growing and branches
-   **Harvesting grain:** 60-90 days, cut entire plant when seed heads turn brown, dry indoors 2-3 weeks

#### Grain Processing

-   Dry harvested plants indoors on tarp 2-3 weeks
-   Rub seed heads vigorously in hands over bowl—tiny seeds release from chaff
-   Winnow by tossing in shallow basket or fanning chaff away
-   Grind seeds in mortar, hand mill, or blender for flour
-   Pop grain like popcorn (heat skillet, add grain, shake until pops) for breakfast cereal
-   Cook whole grain by boiling 20-25 minutes like rice

#### Nutritional Profile

Amaranth grain: 13.6g protein, 7g fat, 65g carbohydrates, 6.7g fiber, 248mg calcium, 7.6mg iron per 100g. Lysine content (0.46g per 100g) is 40% higher than wheat, making it ideal for combining with grains to create complete proteins.

### Moringa (Moringa oleifera)

**Protein (leaves):** 27g/100g (dried)

**Growth:** Perennial, harvest year-round

**Climate:** Tropical/subtropical (zones 9-12)

**Cost:** Single tree feeds family

Moringa oleifera is the most nutritionally dense plant on Earth for its resources required. A single tree produces pounds of leaves annually, containing complete protein, all vitamins A, C, calcium, and iron at extraordinary concentrations. Traditional use across Africa and Asia for both nutrition and medicine.

#### Why Moringa Excels

-   Complete protein in leaves: all 9 essential amino acids, 27g per 100g dried (vs. 13g for meat)
-   Vitamin density: 4x more vitamin A than carrots, 7x more C than oranges, 3x more potassium than bananas
-   Perennial: single tree produces 15+ years with minimal care
-   Fast initial growth: reaches 10 feet in 18 months for continuous harvesting
-   Drought-tolerant: survives 6+ months without rain once established
-   Propagates from cuttings: no seed infrastructure needed
-   All parts edible: leaves (greens), pods (immature: okra-like; mature: seeds), seeds (oil), roots (medicinal)
-   Minimal pest/disease pressure in most regions

#### Growing Moringa

-   **Propagation:** Cuttings preferred (1-year-old woody stems, 10-18 inches long). Roots develop in 4-6 weeks. Can also grow from seed (overnight soaking)
-   **Spacing:** 15-20 feet apart if growing tree form; 6-8 feet for shrub form (coppiced)
-   **Climate:** Minimum 50°F; prefers 75-85°F. Tolerates 40-110°F range.
-   **Soil:** pH 6.3-7.0; tolerates poor, sandy, clay soils; very drought-tolerant once established
-   **Water:** 20-40 inches annual; survives 8-10 inches
-   **Pruning strategy:** Coppice (cut to 3-foot stubs annually) to maintain tender leaves and shrubby growth vs. tall single trunk
-   **Harvesting:** Pick young leaves continuously; trim branches every 2-3 months for regrowth

#### Processing Moringa

-   **Fresh leaves:** Cook like spinach (3-5 minutes), adds to soups, stews, curries. Flavor is peppery, similar to horseradish.
-   **Leaf powder:** Dry leaves in shade 1-2 weeks, pound to powder. Stores 1+ year. Mix into porridge, flour, soups for nutrition boost. 1 tablespoon = major nutrition dose.
-   **Leaf tea:** Steep fresh or dried leaves 5-10 minutes, drink as beverage
-   **Seed pods:** Immature (green) pods boil 10-15 minutes, eat like okra. Tender, mild flavor.
-   **Seeds:** Mature seeds roast, pound into flour; or extract oil (moringa oil used for skin/hair)

#### Nutritional Profile (Dried Leaves)

Per 100g: 27g protein, 47g carbohydrates, 3g fat, 19g fiber, 2185mg calcium, 28.2mg iron, 16,500 IU vitamin A, 220mg vitamin C. One ounce (28g) of dried moringa powder provides: 7.6g protein, 1.3g fiber, 620mg calcium (62% RDA), 7.9mg iron (44% RDA), 4620 IU vitamin A (92% RDA), 61.6mg vitamin C (103% RDA).

:::warning
**Caution:** Moringa leaves have small laxative effect in large quantities. Start with moderate amounts (1-2 tablespoons powder daily) to allow digestive adaptation. Pregnant women should consult healthcare provider before high consumption.
:::

### Chia (Salvia hispanica)

**Calories:** 486/100g

**Growth:** 5-6 months

**Climate:** Warm to hot (zones 8-11)

**Protein:** 16.5g/100g

Chia is an ancient Aztec superfood, recently rediscovered. Small black seeds pack omega-3 fatty acids, fiber, protein, and minerals in extraordinary density. A single chia plant produces thousands of tiny seeds that store for years and expand when wet (creating gel useful for emergency water retention).

#### Why Chia Excels

-   Nutritional density: 486 calories, 16.5g protein, 31g fiber per 100g seeds
-   Omega-3 content: 5.8g per ounce (highest plant source; more than most fish)
-   Storage stability: 15+ year shelf life if dry and cool
-   Drought-tolerant: minimal water needs once established
-   No processing required: eat seeds raw; no grinding, cooking, or soaking necessary (though soaking improves digestion)
-   Self-seeding: if allowed to drop seeds, volunteers grow next season
-   Compact growth: fits in small spaces, containers viable
-   Hydration gel benefit: seeds absorb 10x their weight in water, useful for water storage in hot climates

#### Growing Chia

-   **Planting:** Direct seed or start indoors. Soil temperature 70°F+ for germination. Press seeds on soil surface (light-dependent germination).
-   **Spacing:** Rows 12-18 inches apart, thin to 6-12 inches within row
-   **Climate:** Prefers 65-75°F; tolerates 40-95°F. Does not tolerate heavy frost.
-   **Soil:** pH 6.5-7.5; prefers well-drained soil but tolerates poor soil
-   **Water:** 20-24 inches annual; very drought-tolerant, needs minimal irrigation
-   **Harvest:** 5-6 months, when flowers dry and seed pods brown (harvest when 50% brown to prevent shattering)

#### Processing

-   Dry harvested plants on tarp 2-3 weeks until seeds fall free
-   Rub seed heads to release seeds; winnow chaff
-   No further processing needed—consume whole seeds raw
-   Optional: soak seeds overnight in water to create gel (absorbs 10x weight in liquid)

#### Nutritional Profile

Per 100g: 486 cal, 16.5g protein, 31g fiber, 31g fat (5.8g omega-3 ALA), 42g carbohydrates, 715mg calcium, 7.7mg iron. Single ounce (28g) provides: 138 calories, 4.7g protein, 8.7g fiber, 8.7g fat including 1.6g omega-3 (essential for vegans/vegetarians lacking fish).

### Buckwheat (Fagopyrum esculentum)

**Calories:** 343/100g

**Growth:** 60-90 days

**Climate:** Cool to warm (zones 3-9)

**Protein:** 12g/100g

Buckwheat is technically a pseudocereal, not a true grain, but nutritionally equivalent to grains. It grows in 60-90 days—the fastest staple crop—making it ideal for spring emergency production or fall salvage crops. One ounce of seeds per square meter yields 1 pound of harvested grain.

#### Why Buckwheat Excels

-   Ultra-fast growth: 60-90 days seed to harvest (faster than all true grains)
-   Poor soil pioneer: grows on depleted, compacted, acidic soils unsuitable for other crops
-   Gluten-free: safe for celiac disease and gluten sensitivity
-   Complete protein: contains all 8 essential amino acids (rare for plants)
-   Mineral density: high in manganese, magnesium, copper, phosphorus
-   Bee forage: beautiful flowers attract pollinators; buckwheat honey production possible
-   Soil conditioning: allelopathic (inhibits weeds), green manure improvement when plowed under
-   No processing equipment needed: hulled kernels (groats) used raw or cooked

#### Growing Buckwheat

-   **Planting:** Direct seed after last frost, 0.75-1.25 inches deep. Can plant spring, early summer, or late summer for fall harvest.
-   **Spacing:** Broadcast at 40-50 lbs/acre or rows 12 inches apart
-   **Climate:** Prefers 55-75°F; tolerates 40-85°F. Sensitive to frost (kills plant).
-   **Soil:** pH 5.0-7.0; grows on acidic, sandy, clay, and poor soils. Does not tolerate saline or waterlogged soils.
-   **Water:** 15-20 inches seasonal; drought-tolerant, no irrigation usually needed
-   **Harvest:** 60-90 days when 75% of seeds turn brown (further ripening on stalk after cutting)

#### Processing

-   Cut plants at base when 75% brown, allow to dry in swath 3-5 days
-   Thresh by hand, beating dried plants over cloth or tarp to release seeds
-   Winnow to remove chaff (much lighter than seeds)
-   Commercial hulling removes hard seed coat, leaving groats (edible kernel)
-   Home solution: boil whole seeds 15-20 minutes (hull softens, separates during chewing), or pound gently in mortar and winnow out hulls

#### Cooking Methods

-   **Kasha:** Toast groats in dry skillet 2-3 minutes, add 2:1 water ratio, simmer 15 minutes. Nutty flavor.
-   **Buckwheat flour:** Grind roasted groats in mill or blender, use in pancakes, bread, pasta, porridge
-   **Sprouting:** Soak seeds 1-2 hours, drain, sprout 2-3 days in damp cloth for enzyme activation and nutrient enhancement

<section id="sweet-potato">

### Sweet Potato (Ipomoea batatas)

**Calories:** 86/100g

**Growth:** 4-6 months

**Climate:** Warm/hot (zones 7-11)

**Protein:** 1.6g/100g

Sweet potato is a calorie-dense root crop and major global staple. Unlike cassava (starch-only), sweet potato provides carbohydrates, fiber, and beta-carotene. Yields 10-20 tons per acre in favorable conditions. Stores 4-6 months in cool conditions and years in proper cellaring.

#### Why Sweet Potato Excels

-   Dual production: edible tubers (main harvest) and edible leaves (nutritious greens, 3-4x per season)
-   Calorie-dense roots: 86 calories per 100g, but yields so large = 6-20 million calories per acre
-   Beta-carotene richness: orange varieties provide 961 mcg/100g (100%+ daily need)
-   Storage capability: 4-6 months in cool (55-60°F), humid conditions; cured tubers last longer
-   Propagates from slips: no seed required, single potato produces multiple harvests
-   Pest-resistant in most regions: few serious pests
-   Nutrient-dense leaves: greens contain calcium, iron, vitamins A, C, and protein

#### Growing Sweet Potato

-   **Propagation:** From slips (sprouted vines from stored tubers). Place tuber in sand/soil, keep moist 4-6 weeks until slips develop (4-8 vines per tuber). Rooted slips ready to plant.
-   **Spacing:** 12-18 inches apart in rows 36-48 inches apart (wider spacing = larger tubers)
-   **Climate:** Minimum 60°F; prefers 75-85°F. Cold soil reduces growth and disease tolerance.
-   **Soil:** pH 6.0-7.0; grows in sandy, clay, and poor soils. Well-drained essential to prevent rot.
-   **Water:** 30-40 inches seasonal; requires consistent moisture during tuber development (3-4 months)
-   **Harvest:** 4-6 months, before first frost. Dig gently to avoid bruising.

#### Processing & Storage

-   **Curing:** Store 2 weeks at 80°F, 85% humidity (basement with wet towels). Speeds wound healing, increases storage life to 6+ months.
-   **Cool storage:** 55-60°F, 80-85% humidity. Check monthly, remove sprouted/rotting specimens.
-   **Cooking:** Boil, bake, steam, or fry. Sweeter and creamier than regular potatoes.
-   **Preservation:** Can, freeze, dehydrate, or ferment for long-term storage

#### Nutritional Profile

Per 100g (raw): 86 cal, 1.6g protein, 20g carbohydrates, 3g fiber, 961 mcg beta-carotene (105% RDA), 2.4mg vitamin C, 39mg calcium, 0.7mg iron. Orange varieties provide excellent vitamin A; white varieties are less nutritious but still calorie-dense; purple varieties contain anthocyanins (antioxidants).

</section>

<section id="taro">

### Taro (Colocasia esculenta)

**Calories:** 112/100g

**Growth:** 6-12 months

**Climate:** Tropical/subtropical (zones 9-12)

**Protein:** 1.5g/100g

Taro is a staple tropical root crop cultivated for 10,000 years. Unlike cassava and sweet potato (sun-loving), taro tolerates shade and thrives in wetland conditions, making it valuable for marginal lands. Yields 6-12 tons per acre. Propagates from cormels (bulblets).

#### Why Taro Excels

-   Shade-tolerant: grows under tree canopy where sun crops cannot (intercropping potential)
-   Wetland capability: tolerates waterlogged conditions, swamps, and standing water
-   Calorie yield: 6-12 tons per acre = 6-14 million calories per acre
-   Dual production: corms (main cob) and cormels (side bulblets) harvested separately
-   Edible leaves: taro leaves (callaloo) cooked as greens, higher nutrient density than roots
-   Storage: roots last 2-3 months in cool, humid storage
-   Propagates from cormels: no seed infrastructure
-   Starch properties: slow-digesting resistant starch, good for blood sugar control

#### Growing Taro

-   **Propagation:** From cormels (small bulblets from mature plant). Select healthy cormels 1-2 ounces, plant upright.
-   **Spacing:** 18-24 inches apart (dense spacing for maximum yield)
-   **Climate:** Minimum 60°F; prefers 75-85°F. Requires high humidity.
-   **Soil:** pH 5.5-7.0. Prefers organic-rich, moist soils. Tolerates poor soils if adequately watered.
-   **Water:** 80-100 inches annual; can tolerate flooding. Ideal in rainy tropical climates or with irrigation.
-   **Shade:** Tolerates 50-80% shade, useful under tree canopy
-   **Harvest:** 6-12 months. Dig when leaves yellow (corms mature). Main corm harvested, cormels left for replanting or secondary harvest.

#### Processing & Cooking

-   **Raw:** Never eat raw—contains calcium oxalate crystals causing mouth irritation
-   **Boiling:** Peel, cube, boil 10-15 minutes. Boiling breaks down oxalates completely.
-   **Roasting:** Whole corms bake at 375°F for 45-60 minutes, cool, peel, eat
-   **Leaves:** Young leaves boil 5-10 minutes (callaloo), remove stems, cook like spinach
-   **Processing:** Can, freeze, ferment for preservation

#### Oxalate Content

:::warning
**Important:** Raw taro contains calcium oxalate crystals causing mouth/throat irritation. Cooking (boiling especially) breaks down oxalates completely, making taro completely safe. If you have kidney stone history, consult healthcare provider before high consumption (oxalates affect stone formation in susceptible individuals).
:::

</section>

<section id="jerusalem-artichoke">

### Jerusalem Artichoke / Sunchoke (Helianthus tuberosus)

**Calories:** 73/100g

**Growth:** Perennial (harvest year 2+)

**Climate:** Temperate zones (zones 3-9)

**Protein:** 2g/100g

Jerusalem artichoke is a North American native perennial requiring virtually zero maintenance. Plant once, harvest yearly for 10+ years. Unlike cassava/sweet potato (annual replanting), sunchokes persist indefinitely. Tubers store underground naturally; just dig what you need.

#### Why Jerusalem Artichoke Excels

-   True perennial: plant once, harvest forever (10+ year production from single planting)
-   Zero-care crop: no watering, feeding, weeding, or pest management needed
-   In-ground storage: tubers stay fresh in soil, dig as needed through winter
-   Extreme cold tolerance: tolerates -30°F winters, regrows from underground rhizomes
-   Poor soil excellence: grows in compacted, sandy, acidic, alkaline, and contaminated soils
-   Inulin-rich: prebiotic fiber beneficial for gut bacteria, low glycemic index
-   Yield: 2-3 tons per 100 sq ft once established
-   Self-propagating: volunteer tubers self-sow next season; essentially unlimited reproduction

#### Growing Jerusalem Artichoke

-   **Planting:** Spring (after frost risk) or fall. Plant whole small tubers or cutting from larger tuber (minimum 1 inch size). Plant 4-6 inches deep, 12 inches apart.
-   **Spacing:** 18-24 inches apart (wider = larger tubers, denser = more tubers total)
-   **Climate:** Perennial from zones 3-9. Tolerates -30°F winters.
-   **Soil:** Grows in any soil condition. Prefers pH 6.0-7.5 but tolerates 4.5-8.5.
-   **Water:** Minimal. Tolerates 10-50 inches annual rain.
-   **Maintenance:** Zero. Weeds are actually beneficial (protect soil). Can become invasive—contain if concerned.
-   **Harvest:** Year 2+ (year 1 plants focus on establishing rhizomes). Dig tubers after frost (sweeter after cold), harvest as needed through winter

#### Processing & Cooking

-   **Raw:** Peel and eat raw like radish (crunchy, slightly sweet)
-   **Boiling:** Peel, cube, boil 10-15 minutes, soften like potato
-   **Roasting:** Whole or cubed, roast 375°F for 30-45 minutes
-   **Preservation:** Can be left in ground indefinitely. For out-of-ground storage, keep in cold (35-40°F), humid conditions; lasts 2-3 months.

#### Inulin and Digestion

:::warning
**Note:** Jerusalem artichokes contain inulin, a prebiotic fiber. Excessive consumption may cause digestive gas/bloating until gut bacteria adapt. Introduce gradually (small portions initially, increasing over weeks). Cooking or fermenting may reduce inulin content and digestive effects.
:::

</section>

<section id="cattail">

### Cattail (Typha latifolia)

**Calories:** 67/100g (rhizome, raw)

**Growth:** Wild perennial, self-harvesting

**Climate:** All zones (ubiquitous wetland)

**Protein:** 1.1g/100g

Cattail is a wild perennial wetland plant providing food from multiple plant parts simultaneously: spring shoots (asparagus-like), summer pollen (protein-rich flour), fall corms (potato-like tubers), and year-round rhizomes. No planting required—harvest from natural wetlands or establish in constructed ponds.

#### Why Cattail Excels

-   Zero cultivation: grows wild in wetlands; simply harvest. No soil preparation, planting, weeding, feeding, or maintenance.
-   All-season harvest: spring shoots, early summer pollen, late summer corms, fall/winter rhizomes provide year-round food
-   Multiple useful parts: young shoots, pollen, corms, rhizomes all edible; none waste
-   High carbohydrate density: rhizomes and corms are carbohydrate-rich (67 cal/100g raw, 385 cal/100g dried)
-   Wetland tolerance: thrives in swamps, pond edges, marshes, and water-logged areas unsuitable for crops
-   Self-propagating: rhizomes spread indefinitely once established
-   Minimal pest/disease: no diseases or pests of concern
-   Medicinal benefits: traditional use for digestive and circulatory health

#### Harvesting Cattail Parts

-   **Spring shoots (March-May):** Pull young shoots 6-12 inches from water, peel like leek, eat raw or boiled. Tastes like cucumber.
-   **Early summer pollen (May-July):** When cattail heads are dense with yellow pollen, shake into container. Mix with flour (1:1 ratio) for bread/pancake. Extremely nutrient-dense (protein, fat, B vitamins).
-   **Late summer corms (August-September):** Corms are bulges at base of stalks. Peel, eat raw (crunchy) or cook like potato. Starchy, satisfying.
-   **Fall/winter rhizomes (October-April):** Dig root stalks underwater, rinse in flowing water to remove mud/starch. Process by pounding (wet or dry) to separate starch from fiber. Starch settles, fiber floats. Starch is nutritious carbohydrate flour.

#### Rhizome Starch Processing

-   Dig rhizomes from wet ground (they float, making extraction easier underwater)
-   Rinse thoroughly in flowing water or pond
-   Pound wet rhizomes in mortar or stone bowl (traditional method)
-   Suspend pounded material in water in bucket, stir, let settle 10-15 minutes
-   White starch settles to bottom; fiber floats on top
-   Pour off water and fiber carefully, collect starch slurry
-   Dry starch on cloth or paper 2-3 weeks
-   Result: nutritious flour equivalent to rice starch

#### Nutritional Profile

Cattail rhizomes (raw): 67 cal, 1.1g protein, 15g carbohydrates, 0.3g fat, 1.1g fiber per 100g. Pollen: extraordinarily dense in protein (8g/100g), fat (12g/100g, much omega-3 ALA), vitamins/minerals. Corms: starchy, potato-like (more carbohydrate-dense than rhizomes).

</section>

<section id="quinoa">

### Quinoa (Chenopodium quinoa)

**Calories:** 368/100g

**Growth:** 5-7 months

**Climate:** Cool to temperate (zones 5-9)

**Protein:** 14g/100g

Quinoa is an Andean grain offering complete protein (all 9 essential amino acids) and remarkable environmental tolerance. Unlike most crops, quinoa thrives at high altitudes, tolerates salt-laden soils, and handles cold/heat extremes. One plant produces thousands of seeds in 5-7 months.

#### Why Quinoa Excels

-   Complete protein: rare plant food with all 9 essential amino acids in optimal ratios
-   Altitude tolerance: thrives at 7,000-12,000 feet where other crops fail
-   Salinity tolerance: grows in soil too saline for most crops (8+ mmhos/cm conductivity)
-   Cold/heat resilience: tolerates frost and 95°F+ temperatures
-   Drought-tolerant: minimal water needs once established
-   Saponin removal note: outer seed coating contains saponin (bitter, not toxic) easily removed by rinsing
-   Storage stability: 10+ year shelf life if dry and cool

#### Growing Quinoa

-   **Planting:** Direct seed after last frost, 0.5 inch deep. Soil temperature 50°F+.
-   **Spacing:** Rows 18-24 inches apart, thin to 6-12 inches within row
-   **Climate:** Prefers 60-70°F but tolerates 40-90°F. Frost-sensitive (plant dies at 27°F).
-   **Soil:** pH 6.0-8.0; tolerates saline, alkaline, poor, and sandy soils. Drought-tolerant.
-   **Water:** 15-20 inches seasonal; very drought-tolerant, no irrigation usually needed
-   **Harvest:** 5-7 months when leaves yellow, seed heads brown, grain hard (doesn't dent with thumbnail)

#### Processing

-   Harvest entire plants when fully mature and dry
-   Dry on tarp in sun 2-3 weeks until seeds thresh free easily
-   Rub seed heads to release grain, winnow chaff
-   Rinse seeds thoroughly 3-4 times in water to remove saponin coating (causes soapy taste if not removed)
-   Dry rinsed seeds in sun 1-2 days, store dry

#### Cooking

-   Boil 1 cup quinoa with 2 cups water, simmer 15 minutes, rest 5 minutes. Fluffy texture, mild flavor.
-   Toast dry seeds in skillet before boiling for nuttier flavor
-   Grind seeds in mill for flour (baking, porridge, thickener)

#### Nutritional Profile

Per 100g: 368 cal, 14g protein, 64g carbohydrates, 6g fat, 7g fiber, 47mg calcium, 8.3mg iron, 196mg magnesium. Lysine content (0.7g/100g) is higher than most grains, making it valuable for vegetarian diets and as grain supplement for lysine deficiency.

</section>

## Planting and Harvesting Guide by Climate Zone

For general planting calendars, companion planting, and crop rotation, see **[Agriculture & Gardening](agriculture.html)**. The climate-specific guides below focus on famine-crop timing and water strategies.

### Tropical (Zones 11-12: 60-70°F min winter)

:::card
#### Best Famine Crops

**Year-round growth possible:** Cassava, Moringa, Taro, Cattail

**Seasonal optimization:** Planting timing maximizes rainfall/dry season alignment

-   **Cassava:** Plant year-round; harvest 8-12 months. Prefer wet season planting.
-   **Moringa:** Plant year-round; harvest year-round (coppice every 2-3 months). Perennial once established.
-   **Taro:** Plant in wet season; harvest dry season. Tolerates flooding.
-   **Millet/Sorghum:** Plant during dry season (minimal water needed), harvest end of dry season
:::

### Subtropical (Zones 9-10: 32-50°F min winter)

:::card
#### Best Famine Crops

**Primary:** Amaranth, Sorghum, Millet, Chia, Sweet Potato, Buckwheat, Quinoa

**Seasonal:** Cassava (zones 9B+), Taro (protected areas)

-   **Spring planting:** Amaranth (April-May), Sorghum (May), Millet (April-May), Buckwheat (April-June)
-   **Fall planting:** Buckwheat (August-September), some millet varieties
-   **Summer harvest:** Amaranth (July-August), Chia (August-September)
-   **Fall harvest:** Sorghum, Millet, Quinoa, Buckwheat (September-October)
-   **Winter storage:** Sweet Potato, Jerusalem Artichoke (in-ground storage)
:::

### Temperate (Zones 5-8: 0-32°F min winter)

:::card
#### Best Famine Crops

**Primary:** Amaranth, Buckwheat, Quinoa, Sweet Potato, Jerusalem Artichoke

**With frost protection:** Chia (zones 8+), Sorghum (zones 7+)

-   **Spring planting (after last frost):** Amaranth (May), Sorghum (May), Buckwheat (May), Quinoa (May)
-   **Summer growing:** Minimal intervention. Control weeds spring/early summer.
-   **Fall harvest (before first frost):** Amaranth (September), Sorghum (September-October), Buckwheat (August-September), Quinoa (September)
-   **Winter storage:** Jerusalem Artichoke, Sweet Potato in cellar or cold storage
-   **Quick crops:** Buckwheat twice per season (May and August) maximizes output
:::

### Cold (Zones 3-4: -20 to 0°F min winter)

:::card
#### Best Famine Crops

**Primary:** Amaranth, Buckwheat, Quinoa, Jerusalem Artichoke

-   **Spring planting (after last frost date):** Amaranth (late May), Buckwheat (late May), Quinoa (late May)
-   **Summer:** Intense growing season. Long daylight maximizes growth.
-   **Fall harvest (before first frost, 90-120 days after planting):** All spring-planted crops
-   **Perennial food:** Jerusalem Artichoke (plant once, harvest yearly 10+ years without replanting)
-   **Storage focus:** Emphasize perennials and long-storage crops due to short growing season
:::

### Arid/Desert (Zones 8-10, 10-15 in annual rainfall)

:::card
#### Best Famine Crops

-   **Sorghum/Millet:** Plant with first rain. Tolerates entire season on 10-20 inches rain.
-   **Amaranth:** Plant in early summer when soil warm, extend into fall. Heat-lover, drought-tolerant.
-   **Chia:** Minimal water. Plant in spring, harvest late summer.
-   **Jerusalem Artichoke:** Plant once, dig tubers during drought when nothing else produces
:::

<section id="seed-saving">

## Seed Saving for Each Species

### Grain Crops (Sorghum, Millet, Amaranth, Quinoa, Buckwheat)

-   **Selection:** Allow healthiest, most vigorous plants to mature for seed. Mark before flowering/seeding.
-   **Isolation:** Grains are generally self-pollinating (low cross-contamination risk). Distance (50+ feet) between varieties prevents majority cross-pollination.
-   **Harvesting:** Cut plants when 75-90% of seeds brown/hard. Dry on tarp 2-3 weeks.
-   **Threshing/cleaning:** Thresh grain by hand/foot to release seeds. Winnow to remove chaff. Store dry.
-   **Storage:** Dry seeds in shade/dry location (below 10% moisture). Store in sealed containers in cool, dry place. Viability: 5-15 years.
-   **Yield note:** 1 plant produces thousands of seeds. 100 plants provide year-supply for family of 4-6.

### Cassava, Sweet Potato, Taro (Vegetative Propagation)

-   **No seeds needed:** These propagate from plant cuttings, not seeds.
-   **Cassava:** Cut mature stems (1 year+) into 6-8 inch pieces. Store upright in cool, dry place. Plant when next season arrives. Cuttings viable 3-4 months.
-   **Sweet Potato:** Store mature tubers in cool (55-60°F), humid place. Sprout (slips) develop. Take rooted slips for next season planting. Tubers store 4-6 months, providing winter supply + seed slips spring.
-   **Taro:** Save small cormels from harvest for next season. Store in cool, humid place. Plant when soil warm.
-   **Advantage:** No need for seed infrastructure. Single mature plant produces dozens of propagules.

### Moringa (Cuttings + Seeds)

-   **Propagation choice:** Cuttings (fastest, clones parent) or seeds (slower, genetic diversity).
-   **Cuttings:** Take 10-18 inch woody stem pieces from 1+ year old trees. Root in moist sand/soil, 4-6 weeks. Viable indefinitely if stored cool-humid.
-   **Seeds:** Allow some seed pods to mature on tree (brown, dry). Extract seeds (winged). Store dry in cool place. Viability: 3-5 years.
-   **Advantage:** Perennial tree provides indefinite cuttings without replanting. Minimal seed infrastructure.

### Jerusalem Artichoke (Self-seeding + Tuber Division)

-   **Self-seeding:** If allowed, plants drop seeds autumn; volunteer plants sprout spring (free crop, no seed saving effort).
-   **Tuber division:** Dig tubers at harvest, save some for replanting (minimum 1 inch size). Store in cool, humid place 2-3 months, or leave in ground.
-   **Advantage:** Once established, essentially unlimited production without any seed/cutting management. Weeds are fertilizer for next year.

### Chia (Easy Seed Saving)

-   **Selection:** Allow healthiest plants to mature fully, flowers dry completely.
-   **Harvesting:** Cut entire plants, dry 2-3 weeks on tarp.
-   **Threshing:** Rub seed heads to release seeds. Seeds drop freely when fully ripe.
-   **Storage:** Dry seeds in sun 1-2 days. Store in sealed containers in cool, dry place. Viability: 15+ years.

### General Seed Storage Tips

-   Cool, dry, dark storage extends viability (unheated closet, root cellar ideal)
-   Sealed containers prevent moisture/pest infiltration
-   Desiccant packets (silica gel) keep seeds dry if humidity is concern
-   Label clearly with crop name, variety, harvest date
-   Test germination each year (count seeds, soak 24hrs, watch for sprouting; calculate % germination rate)
-   Save more seeds than minimum needs (15-20% extra accounts for germination losses)

## Combining Crops for Complete Nutrition

### Nutritional Challenge in Famine Scenarios

Single crops rarely provide complete nutrition. Starch staples (grains, roots) provide calories but lack complete protein. Meat/fish/dairy unavailable in collapse scenarios. Strategic crop combinations create nutritionally complete diet from plant sources.

### Complete Protein Combinations

Grains lack lysine (essential amino acid). Legumes (beans, peas) are high in lysine but require reliable rainfall/fertility. Famine crops that are lysine-sufficient (eliminating legume dependency):

:::card
#### Option 1: Grain + Legume

**Any grain + Beans/Peas** creates complete protein. Traditional combinations (rice+beans, corn+beans, wheat+lentils) proven effective.

Famine crops: Sorghum/Millet + drought-tolerant beans (cowpea, chickpea)
:::

:::card
#### Option 2: Lysine-Rich Grain Alone

**Amaranth, Quinoa, Buckwheat** are complete proteins without legume pairing.

Advantage: No legume infrastructure needed. Disadvantage: lower total protein (13-14g vs. 18-20g with grain+legume combo).
:::

:::card
#### Option 3: Grain + Green Leaf

**Grain + Moringa/Cassava Leaves/Amaranth Greens** provides additional protein and micronutrients.

Cassava leaves: 32g protein (dried), 100g calcium, 28mg iron per 100g. Moringa leaves: 27g protein (dried) plus vitamins.
:::

:::card
#### Option 4: Chia as Protein Supplement

**Chia added to any grain** boosts protein (16.5g/100g) and omega-3s dramatically.

1 tablespoon chia (7g) adds: 1.2g protein, 1.2g fiber, 1.6g omega-3. Affordable nutrient boost.
:::

### Micronutrient-Complete Diet

Target key micronutrients often deficient in famine scenarios:

#### Vitamin A (preventing blindness)

-   **Sweet Potato (orange varieties):** 961 mcg per 100g (100%+ daily need)
-   **Moringa (dried leaves):** 16,500 IU per 100g (330%+ daily need)
-   **Cassava leaves:** High in beta-carotene

#### Vitamin C (preventing scurvy)

-   **Moringa (fresh leaves):** 220mg per 100g (366% daily need)
-   **Cassava fresh roots:** 20mg per 100g
-   **Amaranth greens:** Vitamin C when fresh

#### Iron (preventing anemia)

-   **Moringa (dried leaves):** 28.2mg per 100g (157% daily need)
-   **Amaranth:** 7.6mg per 100g
-   **Pearl Millet:** 8mg per 100g
-   **Chia:** 8.7mg per 100g

#### Calcium (preventing bone disease, tetany)

-   **Moringa (dried leaves):** 2,185mg per 100g (219% daily need)
-   **Chia:** 631mg per 100g (63% daily need)
-   **Amaranth:** 159mg per 100g

### Sample Daily Diet (1,500 calories)

Practical example combining multiple famine crops:

-   **Breakfast (400 cal):** Cassava porridge (boiled root, 200g) + moringa leaf powder (1 tbsp, ~3g) stirred in = 320 cal, 4g protein, 100% vitamin A, 80% calcium
-   **Mid-day (400 cal):** Sorghum grain cooked with dried amaranth leaves (cooked greens, 100g) + chia seeds (1 tbsp, 7g sprinkled on) = 350 cal, 8g protein, high iron
-   **Evening (500 cal):** Buckwheat porridge (cooked grain, 150g) + roasted sweet potato (100g) + handful fresh cassava or amaranth leaves (50g) = 400 cal, 6g protein, high vitamins A/C
-   **Snack/supplement:** Chia soaked in water (1 tbsp daily) = 138 cal, 4.7g protein, omega-3, fiber

**Daily totals:** ~1,500 calories, 25-30g protein (adequate for adult), complete amino acids (with legume inclusion), full micronutrient coverage, 15-20g fiber (healthy digestion).

<section id="mistakes">

## Common Mistakes with Famine Crops

#### Mistake 1: Assuming Single Crop Provides Complete Nutrition

**Error:** Planning diet around just cassava or sweet potato.

**Problem:** Single starch crop provides calories but lacks complete protein, adequate micronutrients, and dietary diversity. Long-term reliance causes deficiency diseases (kwashiorkor from protein lack, anemia from iron deficiency, scurvy from vitamin C deficiency).

**Fix:** Grow minimum 3-4 species covering protein (legumes or lysine-rich grains like amaranth), vitamin A source (sweet potato, moringa), iron source (moringa, millet, chia), and caloric staple (grain or root).

#### Mistake 2: Skipping Cassava Processing

**Error:** Eating raw or improperly processed cassava, or avoiding cassava due to "cyanide fear."

**Problem:** Raw cassava is toxic. Improper processing (peeling only, no heating/drying) concentrates cyanide. However, PROPER processing (boiling 20-40 min or sun-drying 3-5 days) eliminates 100% of cyanide, making it completely safe. Avoiding cassava eliminates highest-calorie staple crop (~20M cal/acre).

**Fix:** ALWAYS boil or sun-dry cassava before eating. 20 minutes boiling eliminates all cyanide completely. Billions of people eat cassava daily safely worldwide. Boiling is safest, simplest method.

#### Mistake 3: Planting Wrong Crops for Climate

**Error:** Growing cassava in zones 5-6, or quinoa in humid tropics.

**Problem:** Crop failure despite effort/resources. Cassava dies in frost; quinoa rots in humidity.

**Fix:** Match crops to your climate zone (see Climate Guide section). If in cold zone, prioritize Jerusalem Artichoke (perennial, no replanting) and buckwheat (fast enough to mature before frost). In tropics, cassava/moringa/taro excel.

#### Mistake 4: Inadequate Seed/Cutting Storage

**Error:** Storing seeds in wet cellar, or cassava cuttings in dry location.

**Problem:** Seeds rot or germinate prematurely. Cassava cuttings desiccate and lose viability.

**Fix:** Seeds = cool (below 60°F), dry (below 10% moisture), dark. Cassava cuttings = cool-humid (50-60°F, 80%+ humidity). Store upright, cover lightly with damp sand.

#### Mistake 5: Harvesting Seeds Too Early

**Error:** Collecting grain/seeds when still green or soft.

**Problem:** Immature seeds have low germination rate. Wasted seed-saving effort.

**Fix:** Harvest only when seeds are fully hard and dry. Thumb-test: seed should NOT dent with thumbnail pressure. Allow additional drying 2-3 weeks after harvest on tarp/in barn.

#### Mistake 6: Not Accounting for Pest/Disease Losses

**Error:** Planning production assuming 100% yield (no losses to pests, disease, weather).

**Problem:** Actual yields 20-50% lower than expected. Insufficient food security.

**Fix:** Plan for 30-50% losses conservatively. Grow 2-3x the minimum needed to ensure supply after losses. Diversify species (pests rarely affect ALL crops simultaneously).

#### Mistake 7: Neglecting Soil Fertility Over Time

**Error:** Assuming famine crops need zero soil preparation/fertilization, then depleting soil after multiple years.

**Problem:** Yields decline dramatically years 2-5 as soil nutrients exhausted.

**Fix:** Even hardy famine crops benefit from compost, green manure (legume cover crop), or basic fertility management. Rotate crops. Apply mulch. Famine crops' advantage is tolerance of poor soil, not complete independence from all nutrients.

#### Mistake 8: Processing Without Equipment/Knowledge

**Error:** Planning production without understanding processing. Expecting commercial mill/processor available in collapse scenario.

**Problem:** Cannot convert grain to flour. Cannot process cassava safely. Abundant crop becomes inedible.

**Fix:** Learn hand-processing methods BEFORE planting. Practice grinding grain in stone mortar. Practice boiling cassava. Practice winnowing. Have hand mill available. Simple tools (rocks, cloth, baskets) sufficient for processing if you know how.

#### Mistake 9: Storage Conditions Too Warm/Wet

**Error:** Storing grain/seeds in warm kitchen or damp basement.

**Problem:** Insect infestation, mold growth, premature sprouting. Year's harvest spoiled.

**Fix:** Cool (below 60°F), dry (below 10% moisture), dark storage. Sealed containers prevent insect infiltration. Check periodically for moisture/pests. If insects present, freeze grain 48-72 hours (kills larvae).

#### Mistake 10: Overestimating Land Area Needed

**Error:** Believing famine crop production requires large acreage.

**Problem:** Unnecessarily large garden/field, difficult to manage.

**Fix:** Reality check: 1 cassava plant (3x3 feet spacing) produces 10-15 lbs roots. 100 plants = 1,000-1,500 lbs = family year-supply. Equivalent to ~1,000 sq feet (50x20 ft bed). Moringa tree: single mature tree provides year's greens for family (10x10 sq ft). Smaller is manageable and sustainable.

## Cassava Cyanide Removal: Critical Processing Protocol

:::danger
**CRITICAL:** Raw cassava contains cyanogenic glycosides that convert to deadly cyanide gas. Improper processing = acute cyanide poisoning (rapid death). Follow protocol exactly.
:::

### Cyanide Content by Cassava Type

:::highlight-box
**Bitter cassava (Manihot esculenta):** 50-200+ ppm cyanide equivalent (requires full processing)  
**Sweet cassava:** 10-50 ppm (less dangerous but still requires processing)  
**Safe level for consumption:** <10 ppm (equivalent of <1 mg HCN per kg fresh weight)
:::

### Soaking Method (Traditional, Most Reliable)

1.  **Preparation:** Peel cassava roots completely (skin removed with knife or peeler). Discard all peel material.
2.  **Cut into chips:** Cut into 1/2 to 3/4 inch thick slices or 1/4 inch thin slices (thinner = faster cyanide leaching)
3.  **Soak in fresh water:** Place chips in large container with water (ratio: 3 parts water to 1 part cassava by weight). Water must completely cover cassava.
4.  **Change water daily:** Remove soaking water completely every 24 hours. Discard water (contains dissolved cyanide). Replace with fresh water.
    -   Thin slices: 3-4 days of soaking minimum
    -   Thick chips: 5-7 days of soaking minimum
    -   Complete cyanide removal verified when: water stays clear after 24-hour soak (no bitter/almond smell)
5.  **Dry or cook immediately:** Once soaking is complete, dry in sun (2-3 days, turn frequently) or cook immediately.

### Cooking Method (Fermentation + Heat)

**Gari fermentation (West African method, faster):**

1.  **Grate peeled cassava:** Fresh cassava is grated into fine particles
2.  **Ferment 2-7 days:** Place grated cassava in cloth-lined containers. Weight down under cloth to express liquid. Ferment at room temperature (lactobacillus fermentation reduces cyanide, develops flavor).
3.  **Press to dry:** After fermentation, press/squeeze out remaining liquid. Spread on cloth and sun-dry (2-3 days).
4.  **Toast lightly:** Cook in dry pan over fire, stirring constantly for 5-10 minutes until warm (further cyanide destruction via heat).
5.  **Safety check:** Properly processed gari should have no bitter/almond taste

### Critical Safety Rules

-   **NEVER consume raw cassava:** Even small amounts can cause acute cyanide poisoning
-   **NEVER skip processing:** Cassava is only safe after soaking (7+ days) OR fermentation + heat treatment
-   **Bitter almond smell = danger:** If soaked cassava smells like bitter almonds, cyanide is still present. Continue soaking.
-   **Taste test (minimal):** After processing, take tiny taste. Any bitterness/burn = incomplete processing. Discard batch.
-   **Store safely:** Dried cassava products (gari, cassava flour) keep 6+ months in sealed containers if fully processed

## Potato Greening & Solanine Toxicity: Cooking Does NOT Remove

### What Green Discoloration Means

Green coloring on potatoes indicates exposure to sunlight during growth or storage. The green color (chlorophyll) doesn't cause toxicity, but it's a marker that the potato has also produced **solanine**, a neurotoxic alkaloid.

### Solanine: The Real Problem - COOKING DOES NOT REMOVE

CRITICAL: Cooking temperatures (boiling, baking, frying) DO NOT destroy solanine. It is heat-stable and will persist through normal cooking. The ONLY method to remove solanine is to cut away all green tissue before cooking.

:::highlight-box
**Solanine facts:**

-   Alkaloid toxin produced when potatoes are exposed to light
-   **NOT destroyed by cooking, boiling, frying, or any heat treatment**
-   Can cause nausea, diarrhea, headache, burning mouth/throat, neurological issues
-   Acute toxicity requires >2-3 mg per kg body weight (rare at normal consumption levels)
-   Accumulates in green parts and "eyes" of potato
:::

### Prevention

-   **Storage:** Store potatoes in complete darkness (black plastic bags, buried in soil, basement with no light)
-   **Never expose potatoes to sunlight:** Even brief exposure during harvest/transport/display causes greening
-   **Use potatoes quickly:** 2-3 weeks storage in darkness prevents greening

### If Greening Has Occurred

-   **Do NOT cook and eat:** Remove and discard completely
-   **Remove affected areas:** Cut away ALL green parts deeply (solanine concentration is highest in green/eye areas)
-   **Green extending into flesh:** If green is extensive, discard entire potato (toxin has distributed)
-   **Prevention lesson:** Solanine accumulation = potato was stressed/stored badly. Better to use fresh potatoes from dark storage only.

## Acorn Tannin Leaching: Multiple Methods

### Why Leaching Is Essential

Acorns contain bitter, astringent tannins that cause stomach upset and can inhibit nutrient absorption. Proper leaching removes tannins, making acorns edible and nutritious (high in fat, carbs, and minerals).

### Water Leaching Method (Slowest, Easiest)

1.  **Shell and coarsely grind:** Remove acorn shells manually or by pounding. Grind shelled acorns into coarse meal (mortar & pestle or food processor).
2.  **Cold water soak:** Place acorn meal in cloth bag or jar. Submerge in cold water (ratio: 3 parts water to 1 part acorn meal). Change water daily.
3.  **Duration:**
    -   White oak acorns: 3-5 days (lower tannin content)
    -   Red oak acorns: 7-14 days (high tannin content, longer leaching required)
    -   Taste test each day: When acorn meal tastes mild/slightly sweet, leaching is complete
4.  **Dry:** After leaching, spread meal on cloth and dry in sun (2-3 days) or low oven (200°F, 2-4 hours).
5.  **Storage:** Dried acorn meal keeps 6+ months in sealed containers.

### Hot Water Leaching Method (Faster)

1.  **Shell and grind:** Same as above
2.  **Boiling water soak:** Pour boiling water over acorn meal (3:1 ratio). Let sit 15-30 minutes, drain. Repeat 3-5 times with fresh boiling water until water runs clear and taste is mild.
3.  **Advantages:** 2-3 hours total vs 1-2 weeks for cold soaking
4.  **Note:** Some nutrients leach out faster, but convenience may outweigh slight nutrient loss

### Buried/Flowing Water Method (Traditional)

-   Place leached acorn meal in permeable bag
-   Submerge in flowing stream/creek for 3-5 days (current constantly refreshes water)
-   Tannin removal is rapid; traditional method when flowing water is available

### Using Leached Acorn Meal

-   Mix 1:1 with wheat flour for bread (acorn flour is dense)
-   Cook as porridge (acorn + water, simmer until thick)
-   Roast lightly for acorn "coffee"
-   Nutritional content: Similar to chestnuts; higher fat than other nuts

## Pokeweed Preparation: Two Boil Protocol

:::warning
**WARNING:** All parts of pokeweed contain saponins and oxalates. Young shoots are edible if prepared correctly (two separate boilings). Roots, berries, and seeds are ALWAYS toxic.
:::

### Safe Parts & Timing

-   **Safe to eat:** Young shoots (spring) when <6-8 inches tall, before leaves fully expand
-   **Never eat:** Roots (always contain high toxin levels), mature leaves, berries, seeds
-   **Harvest timing:** Early spring only when shoots are tender; mature plant becomes toxic throughout

### Two-Boil Cooking Method (Standard)

1.  **First boil (remove oxalates & saponins):**
    -   Cut shoots into 3-4 inch pieces
    -   Place in pot of water; bring to boil
    -   Boil for 5 minutes
    -   Drain completely; discard boiling water
2.  **Second boil (final cooking):**
    -   Rinse shoots in fresh water
    -   Place in fresh pot of water; bring to boil
    -   Boil for 10-15 minutes until tender
    -   Drain well; shoots are now safe to eat
3.  **Serve:** Season with salt, butter, vinegar like asparagus or other greens

### Critical Safety Rules

-   NEVER skip the first boil - discard that water
-   NEVER eat any part of mature pokeweed (only young spring shoots)
-   NEVER eat roots under any circumstances (toxin-rich even after cooking)
-   NEVER eat berries or seeds
-   Properly prepared young shoots are safe and nutritious (vitamins A, C, minerals)

## Rhubarb Leaf Oxalic Acid: Kidney Failure Risk

:::danger
**CRITICAL:** Rhubarb leaves contain extremely high oxalic acid (500-3000 ppm). Consumption can cause acute kidney failure. NEVER eat rhubarb leaves.
:::

### The Danger

-   **Oxalic acid content:** Rhubarb stems (edible): 400-500 ppm; Rhubarb leaves (toxic): 2000-3000 ppm
-   **Mechanism:** Oxalic acid binds calcium, forming crystals in kidneys. High doses cause: hypocalcemia (low blood calcium), kidney pain, kidney failure
-   **Acute poisoning:** Ingestion of large amount of leaves (150-250g raw leaves) can cause vomiting, diarrhea, severe abdominal pain, kidney dysfunction
-   **Chronic risk:** Regular consumption of leaves damages kidney function over time

### Safe Rhubarb Use

-   **Eat ONLY the stems:** Stems have manageable oxalic acid levels; cooking reduces bioavailability further
-   **Avoid if you have:** Kidney disease, kidney stones, gout, or rheumatoid arthritis (oxalates exacerbate these)
-   **Remove all leaf material:** Cut rhubarb below the leaf entirely. Discard leaves completely.
-   **Cooking note:** Boiling rhubarb stems in water and discarding water removes some oxalates

### Accidental Poisoning

If rhubarb leaves are consumed:

-   Mild symptoms: Nausea, diarrhea within 1-2 hours
-   Severe symptoms: Abdominal pain, burning mouth/throat, vomiting, kidney pain
-   Emergency: Seek medical care if large quantity consumed
-   Treatment: Supportive (hydration, electrolytes, kidney monitoring)
