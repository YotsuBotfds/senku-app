## Overview

Survival myths spread because they are memorable, not because they work. This guide is the myth-check front door; answer the myth here, then route to the practical guide for the actual steps whenever the user needs a real plan, symptom triage, or field protocol.

This guide is written for direct myth-busting prompts like:

- "Should I drink urine?"
- "Can I eat snow directly?"
- "Should I suck out snake venom?"
- "Should I put on a tourniquet for a snakebite?"
- "Can I get water from a cactus?"
- "Can I navigate by moss on trees?"

</section>

<section id="water-myths">

## Water Myths

For the actual water plan, use the [water purification](./water-purification.md) guide; if heat stress is part of the problem, use [heat illness & dehydration](./heat-illness-dehydration.md). This section only settles the myth and points to the right guide.

### Myth: "Urine is an emergency water source."

**No.**

Urine is mostly water, but it also carries salts and waste that your body is trying to remove. Drinking it adds solute load and often worsens nausea and dehydration. It is not a durable water source.

There is no general emergency exception where urine becomes a good hydration plan.

Better move:

- seek shade
- reduce exertion
- ration sweat, not just water
- find and purify actual water

### Myth: "Snow is a fast hydration shortcut."

**No, not as a primary plan.**

Eating snow directly cools the body, wastes energy, and usually gives less water than people think. Melt it first whenever possible. If you must use snow immediately, melt a small amount in the mouth only to relieve extreme thirst while you work on a real water plan.

### Myth: "Any cactus can be used for safe drinking water."

**No.**

Most cacti do not provide useful clean water, and some species contain irritating or unsafe fluids. Time spent hacking random cactus is usually worse than moving toward shade, drainage, dew capture, or a known water source.

</section>

<section id="snakebite-myths">

## Snakebite Myths

For the field protocol, use the [wildlife encounter protocols](./wildlife-encounter-protocols.md) guide. If this is a real bite, do not keep reading for extra improvisation here; hand off to the wildlife guide.

### Myth: "You should suck out snake venom."

**No.**

Cutting and sucking a snakebite does not meaningfully remove venom and often adds tissue damage, contamination, and delay.

### Myth: "Put a tourniquet on every snakebite."

**No.**

A tight arterial tourniquet can destroy the limb and worsen local injury. It is not standard first aid for ordinary snakebite.

Better move:

- get the patient away from the snake
- keep them still
- splint and immobilize the limb
- remove rings or tight items before swelling
- evacuate

</section>

<section id="bleeding-myths">

## Bleeding and Wound Myths

For serious limb bleeding, use the [trauma & hemorrhage control](./trauma-hemorrhage-control.md) guide. This page only clarifies the tourniquet myth and the one real exception.

### Myth: "Tourniquets are only a last-resort measure."

**Also false.**

For severe limb hemorrhage, an early tourniquet is lifesaving. The real rule is:

- use a tourniquet for **life-threatening extremity bleeding**
- do **not** use one for snakebite, minor cuts, or routine pain control

Blanket anti-tourniquet advice and blanket pro-tourniquet advice are both bad.

</section>

<section id="food-myths">

## Food Myths

If eating causes illness, route to the [toxicology & poisoning response](./toxicology-poisoning-response.md) guide. If the user needs symptom management or exposure response, stop at the handoff and do not turn this page into a food-safety protocol.

### Myth: "Healthy-looking meat is safe to eat raw."

**No.**

Healthy-looking animals still carry parasites and pathogens. Raw or undercooked meat, organs, and blood create avoidable infection risk. Cook thoroughly unless the situation is truly catastrophic and you understand the tradeoff.

### Myth: "A little spoilage is harmless if you are hungry enough."

**No.**

Hunger does not neutralize toxin or bacterial contamination. Smell and appearance are imperfect screens. When food poisoning is likely, bad food can take out the whole group.

</section>

<section id="navigation-myths">

## Navigation Myths

For actual route-finding, use the [navigation](./navigation.md) guide. Treat this section as a myth filter, not a substitute for route planning.

### Myth: "Moss always grows on the north side of trees."

**No.**

Moss grows where moisture, bark texture, and shade allow it. In some places that correlates loosely with north-facing shade, but it is too unreliable to navigate by.

Use real navigation anchors instead:

- sun path
- stars
- terrain
- map and compass
- confirmed route features

### Myth: "One natural sign is enough."

**No.**

Navigation should be cross-checked. Any single clue can be wrong.
