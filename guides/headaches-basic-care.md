:::warning
**Medical Disclaimer:** Most headaches are not dangerous, but some are warning signs of a serious problem. This guide is for common headaches from tension, dehydration, heat, missed meals, sleep loss, mild illness, and eye strain. If a headache is sudden, severe, after a head injury, or comes with neurologic symptoms, get urgent medical help if possible.
:::

<section id="overview">

## Overview: Common Headaches And When They Are Usually Mild

This guide is for questions like:

- "I have a dull headache from stress."
- "My head hurts after not drinking enough water."
- "What helps a headache from heat or a missed meal?"
- "My eyes hurt and I have a headache from screen strain."
- "When is a headache an emergency?"
- "My head hurts every day and I keep taking pills."
- "I have a headache with a cold or fever — is it just the illness?"

If the headache comes with a high fever, stiff neck, confusion, or rash, treat it as a red flag — see [First Aid & Emergency Response](../first-aid.html) now. If the headache is part of a broader illness (cold, flu, body aches), start with [Common Ailments: Recognition & Basic Home Care](../common-ailments-recognition-care.html) for overall symptom management, then return here for headache-specific care.

If the main problem is a nosebleed, start with [Nosebleeds: Basic Care & When To Worry](../nosebleeds-basic-care.html). If the headache follows a blow to the head, face, or nose, treat that as an injury problem first and follow [First Aid & Emergency Response](../first-aid.html).

Many headaches come from everyday triggers rather than a dangerous disease. Common causes include:

- tension, stress, or clenching the jaw
- dehydration
- heat exposure or sun exposure
- missing meals or low blood sugar
- poor sleep
- eye strain from close work, screens, or poor lighting
- mild viral illness or a cold
- caffeine withdrawal or abruptly cutting back after heavy daily use
- medicine side effects or frequent use of pain-relief medicines

A headache is more likely to be simple if it is familiar, gradual, and improves with rest, fluids, food, or a quieter environment.

**Do not assume a headache is harmless just because headaches are common.** A headache that is new, different, severe, or paired with other symptoms deserves more caution.

</section>

<section id="simple-causes">

## Common Mild Causes

### Tension headache

Often feels like:

- pressure or tightness on both sides of the head
- a band-like ache around the forehead or back of the head
- pain linked to stress, long work, jaw clenching, or neck tension

### Dehydration headache

Often feels like:

- headache after heat, sweating, vomiting, diarrhea, or too little drinking
- dry mouth, dark urine, dizziness, or thirst with the headache

### Heat headache

Often feels like:

- headache after working in the sun or a hot room
- fatigue, heavy sweating, weakness, or nausea

### Missed-meal headache

Often feels like:

- headache when you have not eaten for many hours
- shakiness, weakness, irritability, or feeling "off"

### Eye-strain headache

Often feels like:

- forehead pain or pain behind the eyes
- worse after reading, close work, screens, or poor light
- sore, tired eyes or blurred vision

### Mild illness headache

Often comes with:

- a cold, mild fever, body aches, or tiredness
- congestion or sore throat

### Daily or near-daily headache

This deserves extra caution even if the pain is not dramatic.

- headaches that happen most days of the week can come from poor sleep, eye strain, stress, caffeine withdrawal, medicine side effects, or medication-overuse headaches
- repeated self-treatment with pain relievers can sometimes keep headaches cycling instead of ending them
- if headaches are becoming frequent, track sleep, water, heat, meals, caffeine, illness, and medicine use instead of just taking more pills

</section>

<section id="basic-care">

## Basic Care That Helps Most Simple Headaches

### 1. Stop and lower the trigger

- Leave bright sun, heat, or smoke.
- Put down close work and screens if eye strain is likely.
- Rest in a quiet, dim room if possible.
- Loosen tight hats, head coverings, glasses, or hair styles that pull on the scalp.

### 2. Drink water slowly

- Sip water or an oral rehydration drink.
- If you have been sweating a lot, use fluids plus some salt and food.
- Do not chug huge amounts all at once if you feel nauseated.

### 3. Eat if you may have skipped meals

- Try a small snack or meal with some carbohydrate and a little protein.
- Examples: bread, fruit, soup, crackers, rice, porridge, beans, or leftovers.
- If you are shaky or weak, eat sooner rather than later.

### 4. Rest, but do not isolate completely if you feel otherwise well

- Short rest often helps.
- Gentle movement, stretching the neck and shoulders, and walking a little can help a tension headache.
- Long bed rest is usually not needed for a simple headache.

### 5. Use a cool or warm compress

- A cool cloth on the forehead or eyes may help with heat, migraine-like pain, or eye strain.
- A warm cloth on the neck and shoulders may help with tension headaches.
- Use the one that feels better.

### 6. Reduce eye strain

- Increase lighting so you do not squint.
- Take breaks from screens and close work.
- If you need to read, hold the page farther away and use better light.
- If one eye is much worse than the other, or vision is suddenly blurred, treat that more seriously.

### 7. Consider simple pain relief if available and safe for you

- Use only as directed on the package or by a clinician.
- Avoid mixing medicines that contain the same pain reliever.
- Do not keep taking pain medicine day after day for frequent headaches without getting checked. Repeated use of pain relievers for headaches can itself keep headaches coming back.
- If you are dehydrated, vomiting, have kidney disease, stomach ulcers, are on blood thinners, or are pregnant, be cautious and seek guidance before using pain medicine.

</section>

<section id="what-not-to-do">

## What Not To Do

- Do not keep pushing through heat exhaustion or dehydration.
- Do not ignore headache after a fall, hit, or car crash.
- Do not take extra pain medicine trying to "cover up" a dangerous headache.
- Do not assume a severe headache is just stress if it is sudden or unusual.
- Do not give a child or infant adult medicines unless the label or a clinician says it is safe.

</section>

<section id="when-to-worry">

## Red Flags: Get Help Urgently If You Can

### Head injury warning signs

Seek urgent help if the headache follows a head injury and there is:

- loss of consciousness, even briefly
- vomiting after the injury
- confusion, unusual sleepiness, or behavior change
- seizure
- worsening headache after the hit
- blood or clear fluid from the nose or ears

### Stroke-like warning signs

Get urgent help if the headache comes with:

- face drooping
- arm weakness
- speech trouble
- numbness on one side
- sudden confusion
- trouble walking, seeing, or balancing

### Meningitis or serious infection warning signs

Treat as urgent if the headache comes with:

- stiff neck
- fever with severe headache
- confusion or reduced alertness
- rash that looks purple, dark red, or does not fade when pressed
- strong light sensitivity
- vomiting with worsening headache

### Sudden severe headache warning signs

Get urgent help for:

- a headache that starts suddenly and is very severe
- the "worst headache of my life"
- a headache that reaches maximum intensity within minutes
- a headache that is very different from the person's usual headaches

### Other red flags

Seek help if the headache is accompanied by:

- persistent vomiting
- new seizure
- fainting
- severe eye pain or sudden vision loss
- headache during pregnancy with swelling, high blood pressure, or vision changes
- headache with fever after travel, wound infection, or another serious illness

</section>

<section id="when-it-is-probably-simple">

## When It Is More Likely A Simple Headache

It is more likely to be a common headache if:

- it came on gradually
- it feels like pressure, tightness, or a mild-to-moderate ache
- it matches a known trigger such as dehydration, missed meals, heat, sleep loss, or eye strain
- it improves after water, food, rest, or a quieter room
- there are no neurologic red flags or head injury signs

Even then, keep watching it. If a headache keeps returning, gets worse, or starts to behave differently, it deserves more caution.

</section>

<section id="prevention">

## Preventing Repeat Headaches

- Drink enough water through the day, especially in heat or during physical work.
- Do not skip meals for long periods.
- Sleep as regularly as you can.
- Take breaks from screens and close work.
- Use good lighting for reading, sewing, repair work, and other detail tasks.
- Protect your head and eyes from heat and sun.
- Stretch your neck and shoulders if your work is tense or repetitive.
- If headaches happen often, keep a simple note of sleep, food, water, heat, and work triggers.

Repeated headaches are worth tracking because the pattern is often the clue.

</section>

<section id="faq">

## Short Answers To Common Questions

### "Is this headache from dehydration?"

Dehydration is more likely if you have been sweating, have dark urine, feel thirsty, or have been working in heat. Try fluids first.

### "Can a missed meal cause a headache?"

Yes. If you have not eaten for a long time, try a small meal or snack.

### "Can caffeine changes cause headaches?"

Yes. If you usually take caffeine every day and suddenly stop or cut back hard, withdrawal can cause headache, fatigue, and irritability.

### "What if it feels like my eyes are causing it?"

Eye strain often causes forehead or behind-the-eye pain after reading, screens, or poor lighting. Rest the eyes and improve light.

### "When should I stop treating it at home?"

Stop home care and seek urgent help if the headache is sudden, severe, after head injury, or comes with weakness, confusion, fever and stiff neck, or vision changes.

### "When is it too frequent to keep guessing?"

If headaches are happening daily, near-daily, or often enough that you keep reaching for pain medicine every week, stop treating them as a simple one-off problem and get checked if you can.

</section>
