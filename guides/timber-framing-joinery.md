## Overview

Timber framing is a structural system in which wooden beams are joined using mortise-and-tenon joinery, held together by wooden pegs, without nails or bolts. This technique, used for millennia, creates strong, flexible, and repairable structures that can last 300+ years when properly maintained.

This guide covers the design, layout, cutting, and assembly of timber frame joints and the construction sequence for raising a frame. Success requires sharp hand tools, precision layout, proper timber selection, and team coordination during assembly and raising.

:::info-box
**Quick routing:** This guide focuses on *structural timber framing* — mortise-and-tenon, scarf joints, knee braces, and frame raising. For simple household repairs (loose chair leg, shelf bracket, wobbly table), see <a href="../simple-home-repairs.html">Simple Home Repairs</a> and <a href="../woodcarving-furniture.html">Woodcarving & Furniture</a>.
:::

## Timber Selection and Seasoning

**Species selection:**

Choose timbers based on local availability, strength class, and durability:
- **Oak:** Excellent strength and rot resistance; hard to work but durable 200+ years
- **Chestnut:** Strong, rot-resistant; rare in many regions; historically preferred
- **Douglas fir/Larch:** Good strength-to-weight; more prone to rot if not protected
- **Locust/Black walnut:** Rot-resistant, strong; expensive; excellent durability

Avoid softwoods (pine, spruce) for load-bearing frames unless protected with roof cover and ventilation.

**Size selection:**

Timbers are typically:
- **Posts (columns):** 8×8" to 12×12" (200–300 mm) for main supports; 6×6" (150 mm) for secondary posts
- **Beams (main girders):** 8×12" to 12×14" (200×300 to 300×350 mm) for primary structure
- **Girts (horizontal bracing):** 6×8" to 8×10" (150×200 to 200×250 mm)
- **Braces & knee braces:** 4×6" to 6×8" (100×150 to 150×200 mm)

Use a structural calculator or local building code to determine beam sizing for your span and load.

**Seasoning (drying):**

Green (freshly cut) timber contains 40–60% moisture by weight. As it dries, it shrinks 5–15% along the grain and 8–12% across the grain. Uneven drying causes warping and cracking.

**Air drying method** (ideal for timber framing):
- Stack timbers horizontally with wooden spacers (20–30 mm) between each layer
- Spacers must be aligned vertically to prevent sagging
- Expose to air but cover the top to prevent rain; allow airflow on all sides
- Dry for 6–12 months for 200 mm thick material (1 year per 25 mm thickness)
- Target moisture content: 15–20% (use a pin-type moisture meter to verify)

Kiln drying is faster (2–4 weeks) but more expensive and may cause casehardening (hard exterior, wet interior). If kiln-dried, allow 1–2 weeks acclimation to outdoor humidity before cutting joints.

**Grading and inspection:**

Select timbers with:
- Straight grain (avoid twisted logs)
- Minimal knots in high-stress zones (near joints)
- No large splits (small surface checks are acceptable)
- No rot or insect damage (tap with a hammer; hollow sound indicates damage)

Plan for 5–10% waste (splits during drying, mistakes in layout).

<section id="mortise-tenon-basics">

## Mortise-and-Tenon Joinery Basics

A mortise is a rectangular hole; a tenon is a rectangular projection that fits into the hole. The joint is held by wooden pegs driven through aligned holes.

**Joint anatomy:**
- **Mortise:** Hole cut into the receiving timber (e.g., post)
- **Tenon:** Projecting tab cut from the joining timber (e.g., beam end)
- **Peg holes:** Matching holes drilled through mortise and tenon, offset to create a wedging action as the peg is driven
- **Shoulder:** The step between the mortise face and the body of the tenon (prevents twisting)

**Proportions for a typical joint:**

For an 8×8" post and 6×8" beam:
- **Tenon length:** 5–6" (130–150 mm), leaving 2–3" stub in the post
- **Tenon thickness:** 2–2.5" (50–60 mm), typically 1/3 the beam depth
- **Tenon width:** Full width of beam (5–6")
- **Mortise depth:** Tenon length (e.g., 6")
- **Peg diameter:** 0.75–1" (19–25 mm), typically iron-hard wood (oak, hickory)
- **Peg offset:** 1–1.5" ahead of the mortise back wall (creates wedging action)

:::info-box
The tenon should fill 60–80% of the mortise cross-section. This leaves room for wood swelling and shrinkage without binding, while maintaining sufficient bearing area for load transfer.
:::

## Connection Design Details and Failure Analysis

### Mortise-and-Tenon Sizing Rules

Proper sizing of mortise-and-tenon joints ensures they transfer loads safely without crushing the wood or creating weak points. The rules balance strength with wood grain integrity.

**Tenon thickness rule:** The tenon should be 1/3 to 1/2 the depth of the receiving member (post). Deeper tenons weaken the post by removing too much material from its center; shallower tenons reduce bearing area.

**Example:** A 12-inch deep post receiving a beam can safely accommodate a tenon 4–6 inches thick. This leaves 3–4 inches of wood on each side of the mortise, maintaining post strength.

**Tenon length rule:** The tenon should penetrate 50–75% of the post width in the receiving direction. A full-width penetration (through mortise) weakens the post too much and creates pegging challenges. A partial mortise (blind) should be 1/2 to 2/3 the post width.

**Example:** A post 8 inches wide (in the direction of tenon penetration) should have a mortise 4–5 inches deep, leaving 3–4 inches of solid wood behind.

**Peg hole offset rule (Draw-boring effect):** The peg hole in the mortise should be offset 1–1.5 inches toward the tenon from the back wall of the mortise. When the peg is driven, this offset pulls the tenon tight against the mortise shoulder, creating a wedging action that holds the joint under load.

**Visual check:** Before pegging, the mortise and tenon should mate with 0.5–1 inch of clearance at the back (inside) of the mortise. This space allows the peg to draw the joint tight.

### Lap Joint Proportions

Lap joints (also called lap-scarf) connect two timbers side-by-side, each contributing half its depth to the joint.

**Proportions for typical lap joint:**

- **Lap depth:** 1/2 the depth of each beam (e.g., 8-inch beams lap 4 inches each, total 8-inch overlap at the joint)
- **Lap length (overlap):** 3–4× the beam depth (for 8-inch beam, 24–32 inches of overlap)
- **Peg spacing:** two pegs minimum, spaced 6–8 inches apart along the overlap
- **Peg diameter:** 0.75 inch (slightly smaller than mortise-and-tenon joints since lap joints are less stressed in shear)

**Advantage:** Lap joints are simpler to cut than mortise-and-tenon and more forgiving of minor alignment errors. Load transfer is through peg shear and wood contact pressure.

**Loading:** Lap joints work well for bending loads (resisting sagging) but are weaker in tension (pulling apart). Use where tensile forces are minimal.

### Through-Bolting and Edge Distances

Where iron bolts are available, through-bolting provides superior connection strength, especially for large timbers or high loads.

**Edge distance rule:** Bolt holes should be at least 2–3 times the bolt diameter from any cut edge or grain weakness. This prevents the wood from crushing or splitting at the bolt.

**Example:** A 1-inch diameter bolt requires 2–3 inches of clearance from the edge of the timber. A 3-inch diameter bolt requires 6–9 inches.

**Spacing between bolts:** Multiple bolts in a line should be spaced 4–6 bolt diameters apart. Closer spacing concentrates stress; wider spacing reduces load sharing.

**Washer size:** Large washers (4–6 inch diameter) distribute bolt pressure over a wider area, preventing the bolt head or nut from crushing into the wood.

### Failure Mode Identification and Prevention

Understanding how timber frame connections fail helps prevent catastrophic collapse. Connections typically fail in one of four modes:

**Mode 1: Splitting Along Grain**

Splitting occurs when the wood is pulled apart along its grain, typically due to tension forces or during peg driving. The grain acts like a natural plane of weakness.

**Causes:**
- Peg driven too hard or too quickly, expanding wedge-like and splitting the timber
- Tenon undersized, concentrating stress in limited wood area
- Grain running parallel to the splitting direction

**Prevention:**
- Drive pegs slowly with moderate mallet strikes; stop when peg is fully seated
- Use properly-sized tenons and mortises
- Avoid cutting joints perpendicular to grain if possible
- Pre-drill peg holes slightly undersized to compress rather than expand the wood

**Mode 2: Crushing at Connection Points**

Wood crushes (yields) under concentrated compressive stress, typically where the tenon bears against the mortise shoulder or where a peg pushes through the wood.

**Causes:**
- Undersized mortise depth or bearing area
- Peg diameter too large for the wood strength
- Poor contact between tenon shoulder and mortise shoulder (gaps prevent load sharing)

**Prevention:**
- Ensure tenon shoulders are square and fully seated in the mortise
- Use appropriately-sized pegs (1/3 the tenon width is the rule)
- Apply loads gradually during assembly to allow wood to compress elastically

**Mode 3: Lateral Instability and Twisting**

Lateral instability occurs when the joint rotates or racks (shifts sideways) under load, typically in columns or braced frames.

**Causes:**
- Insufficient lateral bracing (knee braces missing or undersized)
- Double tenons or haunched mortise-and-tenon not properly seated
- Pegs loose or failed

**Prevention:**
- Install knee braces at 45° angles connecting post and beam
- Use haunched mortise-and-tenon in columns with large beams
- Double-peg high-stress connections for redundancy
- Ensure pegs are properly driven and test-tapped for tightness

**Mode 4: Moisture-Related Warping and Joint Loosening**

As timber dries or swells seasonally, joints can loosen, especially if pegs are not properly offset (draw-bored).

**Causes:**
- Tenon shrinks more than mortise, creating gap
- Peg holes misaligned (not draw-bored), reducing draw-boring effect
- Green timber used without proper seasoning, excessive post-assembly shrinkage

**Prevention:**
- Season timber to 15–20% moisture content before cutting joints
- Use draw-boring (offset peg holes) to create a self-tightening action as wood moves
- Expect initial settling; allow 1–2 years before final peg adjustment
- Re-tap pegs periodically during the first year as wood settles

### Detailed Peg Proportions

**Standard Peg Sizing:**
- **Peg diameter rule:** 1/3 the tenon width. For 2.5" wide tenon, use 0.75–1" peg.
- **Peg length:** Penetrate fully through mortise and tenon (typically 3–4 inches) plus 0.5" projection for cutting flush later.
- **Peg taper:** 1:8 ratio—gradual taper from head to point prevents binding as peg compresses during driving.

**Peg Hole Offset (Draw-Boring):**
- **Purpose:** As peg is driven, offset creates a wedging action that pulls tenon tight into the mortise shoulder.
- **Offset amount:** 1–1.5" forward of the mortise back face (toward the tenon).
- **Drilling sequence:** Clamp mortise and tenon together, drill through both in one operation. Offset by moving the drill bit slightly as it passes through the mortise to the tenon.
- **Multiple pegs:** If using 2–3 pegs per joint, offset each equally; space pegs 2–3" apart.

### Lap Joint Proportions

Lap joints connect beams side-by-side with half the wood depth from each member.

**Design:**
- **Lap depth:** 1/2 the beam depth (for 8" beam, 4" lap)
- **Lap length:** 3–4× the beam depth (for 8" beam, 24–32" overlap)
- **Peg holes:** Two pegs per lap joint, spaced 4–6" apart
- **Peg diameter:** 0.75" (slightly smaller than mortise-and-tenon for exposed joint)

**Advantages:** Simple to cut, forgiving of minor alignment errors, strong for bending forces.

### Scarf Joint Proportions (Detailed)

When extending the length of a timber, scarf joints splice two pieces end-to-end.

**Half-Lap Scarf:**
- **Scarf angle:** 30–45° (30° is more forgiving; 45° is stronger)
- **Scarf length:** Calculated as depth ÷ tan(angle). For 12" beam at 40°: 12 ÷ 0.84 ≈ 14.3"
- **Overlap:** 4–6× beam depth (for 12" beam, 48–72" overlap)
- **Pegs:** Spaced 6–8" apart along the overlap (minimum 3–4 pegs for strength)
- **Peg diameter:** 0.75–1"

**Mortise-and-Tenon Scarf (High-Performance):**
- Complex: combines M&T joinery within the scarf overlap
- Used for bridges, load-bearing beams where maximum strength required
- Overlap still 4–6× depth; M&T typically at center of overlap
- Peg offset critical for draw-boring effect

**Dovetail Scarf (Specialized):**
- Interlocking dovetail geometry prevents lateral separation
- Used in premium frames where disassembly unlikely
- More labor-intensive; requires high skill
- Extremely strong in tension and shear

### Knee Brace Joint Proportions

Angled braces connecting post and beam require careful sizing.

**Standard Knee Brace:**
- **Angle:** 45° is ideal; 30–60° acceptable
- **Tenon dimensions:** Proportions match the smaller member (usually the brace)
  - Brace 6×6": tenon 2–2.5" thick, 5–6" long
  - Peg diameter: 0.75"
- **Mortise in both post and beam:** Full-depth mortises receive tenon ends
- **Both tenons pegged:** Redundancy prevents rotation
- **Spacing on frame:** 3–6 meters between braced bays, depending on load

### Mortise Depth Guidelines by Joint Type

- **Through mortise:** Depth equals tenon length + 1/4" (for tenon projection and pegging)
- **Blind mortise:** Depth equals tenon length (tenon fully contained, no projection)
- **Partial mortise (in-frame members):** Depth = 2/3 to 3/4 of receiving member width (preserves structural integrity at back)

### Quality Control Checklist for Joinery

After cutting all joints and before assembly:

1. **Fit test:** Tenon slides into mortise with light force (no forcing, no looseness). Mark any tight spots with pencil.
2. **Shoulder check:** Mortise shoulders should sit flush with tenon shoulders when seated. No gaps.
3. **Peg hole alignment:** Drill peg holes and verify they align cleanly through mortise and tenon. Adjust offset if needed.
4. **Peg fit:** Pegs should slide into holes with slight friction (not loose, not requiring mallet force).
5. **Assembly sequence:** Practice full assembly without pegs to verify all joints mate correctly. Disassemble and prepare for frame raising.
6. **Documentation:** Mark each joint with Roman numerals or letters (Bay I, Position A, etc.) for correct re-assembly during raising.

## Mortise-and-Tenon Variations

**Through mortise-and-tenon:**
- Tenon passes completely through the post
- Peg holes are drilled through both sides
- Strongest joint; tenon is visible on the back of the post
- Used for visible frame members (less aesthetically refined but easier to repair)

**Blind mortise-and-tenon:**
- Tenon is completely hidden within the post
- Mortise does not extend through the timber
- Requires precise mortise depth to match tenon length
- More refined appearance; slightly weaker because peg holes do not extend through the full depth

**Haunched mortise-and-tenon:**
- Top portion of the mortise is partially blocked by a "haunch" (raised portion of tenon)
- Prevents twisting of the joint
- Used when beam depth is small relative to post width
- More complex to layout and cut

**Tusk tenon (mortise with wedge):**
- Tenon extends through the post with a large projecting tab (tusk)
- A removable wooden wedge locks the joint
- Used for temporary or adjustable connections (assembly scaffolding, changeable bridge timbers)

## Layout and Cutting Mortise-and-Tenon Joints

**Tools needed:**
- **Framing square:** For squaring lines and angles
- **Mortise chisel:** 1.5–3" wide, sharp; bevel-edge or socket type
- **Mallet:** Wooden or rubber; 1–2 kg
- **Saw:** Hand saw (ripsaw for along-grain cuts; crosscut for across-grain)
- **Drill and auger bits:** For peg holes
- **Marking gauge:** For consistent mortise width
- **Pencil/scribe:** For marking lines

**Layout process:**

1. **Mark tenon dimensions on the beam end:**
   - Square a line across the beam face 5–6" from the end
   - Mark the tenon width (typically full beam width minus 0.5–1" total if double tenon)
   - Mark the tenon thickness (1/3 to 1/2 the beam depth, centered or offset)
   - Use a marking gauge to mark the tenon shoulder on all four sides

2. **Mark mortise on the post:**
   - Find the center of the post face in the receiving direction (usually top–bottom)
   - Square a line from the tenon layout position to the post surface
   - Mark the mortise width (same as tenon thickness) using the marking gauge
   - Mark the mortise depth (use a depth gauge or mark a line on the post edge)

3. **Double-check layout:** The mortise must align with the tenon in both directions. Test-fit a board with the same dimensions to confirm.

**Cutting the mortise:**

1. Drill auger holes into the mortise area to remove bulk material (auger bit diameter ≈ mortise width minus 1")
2. Use a mortise chisel to clean out the holes and square the edges
3. Work from both sides (if through-mortise) to avoid tearout
4. Undercut slightly (1–2 mm) so the tenon bottom never contacts the mortise bottom (allows wood movement)

**Cutting the tenon:**

1. Mark the four faces with a knife to define the tenon shoulder and thickness
2. Saw along the shoulder lines with a crosscut saw, working on two opposite sides first
3. Saw along the grain (ripsaw) to separate the tenon cheeks from the scrap
4. Test-fit frequently; the joint should slide together by hand with light force

:::warning
Oversized mortises and undersized tenons lead to loose, weak joints. Tight joints are preferable; timber will swell slightly after installation, tightening further. Plan for a sliding fit that becomes snug after initial settling.
:::

## Peg Construction and Driving

Pegs lock the mortise-and-tenon joint, transferring shear forces and preventing racking (twisting). They are typically made from tough, dense wood: oak, hickory, or locust.

**Peg preparation:**

1. Split or saw peg blanks from heartwood of a rot-resistant species
2. Rough peg size: 1.5× final peg diameter (e.g., 1.5" square blanks for 1" pegs)
3. Plane/round the pegs by hand or on a shaving horse, tapering slightly toward the point (1:8 taper)
4. Dry pegs thoroughly (6–12 months) until they are harder than the frame timber

**Peg hole drilling:**

1. Clamp the mortise and tenon assembly together
2. Drill the peg hole (hole diameter = peg diameter, or slightly smaller for tight fit)
3. **Offset the hole** in the mortise slightly forward (1–1.5") from the tenon's back wall
4. This offset creates a wedging action: as the peg is driven, the tenon is pulled tightly into the mortise shoulder
5. Drill through both mortise and tenon in one operation to ensure alignment

**Peg driving:**

1. Lubricate the peg with a thin oil or water to reduce friction
2. Drive the peg with a wooden mallet, hitting straight (avoid glancing blows)
3. The peg will compress slightly, jamming tightly
4. Once driven ~50 mm (tenon is fully seated), stop; do not over-drive
5. Cut the peg flush with the post surface using a sharp chisel or saw

:::tip
Drive the peg slowly, checking progress every few strikes. If the peg jams, the mortise and tenon may not be fully seated. Stop, disassemble, and check for fit. A stuck peg driven too hard can split the timber.
:::

## Load Path Tracing Through Connections

Understanding how loads transfer through a timber frame's joints is fundamental to design and diagnosis. A complete load path traces forces from the point of application (roof, people, animals, wind) through each joint to the foundation. Broken or weak connections at any point cause local failure and potentially cascade failure of the entire structure.

### Vertical Load Path: Roof to Foundation

A typical load path for a roof structure follows this sequence, with each connection designed to transfer forces safely:

**1. Roof loads originate at the peak (ridge):**
- Dead load (roof weight): shingles, boards, framing = 10–20 lbs per sq ft
- Live load (snow, workers): 20–50 lbs per sq ft
- Total design load: 30–70 lbs per sq ft of roof area
- Load concentration at each rafter is calculated: roof area per rafter × design load = load in pounds

**Example calculation:** A 24-foot-wide barn with 12:12 pitch (45° slope). Each rafter carries a triangular roof section. For rafters spaced 24 inches on center:
- Roof area tributary to one rafter = 24 feet (ridge-to-wall) × 2 feet (spacing) × 1.414 (slope factor for 45°) = 67.8 sq ft
- At 50 lbs/sq ft design load: 67.8 × 50 = 3,390 lbs concentrated at the rafter connection to the plate

**2. Loads transfer through rafters to the plate:**
- Rafters (sloped members) carry the bending load, with maximum bending moment at the midspan
- Rafter thrust (lateral component from sloped loading): As the rafter is loaded vertically, the roof loads push the rafter outward horizontally. This "thrust" must be resisted by the plate-to-post connection.
- Thrust force = roof load × (rise ÷ span). For a 24-foot span with 12:12 pitch (12 feet rise): thrust per rafter = 3,390 × (12÷24) = 1,695 lbs pushing outward
- Each rafter's end bears on the top plate (horizontal member at wall top)
- Bearing area: full rafter width (typically 6 inches) bearing on plate top = 6 inches × rafter depth
- Force at each rafter: vertical component (3,390 lbs) transfers to the plate vertically; lateral thrust (1,695 lbs) pushes the connection sideways

**3. Plate connections are critical - the mortise must handle both vertical and lateral forces:**
- Rafter tenons mortise into the top plate
- Mortise must be deep enough to resist the thrust (lateral force) without crushing
- Rafter tenon shoulder bears vertically; the mortise depth resists the lateral thrust
- Peg offset (draw-boring) is essential: as pegs are driven, they pull the rafter tight, creating friction that resists lateral thrust
- A loose rafter connection allows racking (lateral movement), creating gaps at the plate and progressive structural failure
- The mortise shoulders must be square and fully seated; any gaps reduce the bearing area and increase crushing risk

**Example mortise design for 3,390 lbs vertical + 1,695 lbs lateral:**
- Rafter depth: 8 inches; at 45° load: tenon thickness = 3 inches (resists lateral force well)
- Mortise depth: 6 inches (tenon penetrates 6 inches into the 10-inch plate width)
- Peg offset: 1.5 inches (creates strong draw-boring effect as forces try to pull the rafter outward)
- This configuration resists the 1,695 lbs thrust with the bearing area created by the offset pegs and the friction developed

**4. Loads flow down through posts:**
- Posts are typically 8×8 or larger, centered below the plate
- Roof load plus plate weight concentrates at each post
- Load = (tributary roof width) × (load per sq ft) + plate weight
- For our example barn: (24 feet width ÷ 2 posts on each wall = 12-foot tributary width) × 50 lbs/sq ft = 600 lbs per post from roof, plus plate weight (typically 50-100 lbs), total ~700 lbs concentrated at the post top
- Post carries this load in pure compression (no bending if frame is stable and braced)

**5. Post-to-sill connection transfers load to foundation:**
- Posts mortise into the sill (base timber resting on stone/brick foundation)
- Sill-to-foundation connection: sill is typically shimmed and leveled on foundation
- Force transfers through bearing area (post tenon shoulder) not through pegs
- Pegs primarily resist lateral forces and racking (wind or earthquake)
- Post tenon must be sized to carry the concentrated load without crushing the sill timber
- Crushing risk: if post tenon is small (e.g., 2 inches × 6 inches = 12 sq inches) and load is 700 lbs, bearing stress = 700÷12 = 58 psi. Wood can handle ~150–200 psi compression perpendicular to grain, so this is safe. However, if design is poor and tenon is undersized or load is underestimated, crushing of the sill mortise can occur
- Safe bearing area rule: tenon bearing area (sq inches) ≥ load (lbs) ÷ 150 psi

**6. Sill rests on stone/brick foundation:**
- Stone or brick foundation disperses point loads over a wider base through bearing pads (stone shims)
- The sill should rest on multiple stones or an even stone surface, not on just one point
- Load spreads at ~45° angle downward and outward in the soil
- Shallow foundations (2–3 feet deep) are adequate for gravity-loaded timber frames. However, frames subject to overturning moments (tall frames in wind) require deeper, wider foundations to resist tipping forces

### Lateral Load Path: Wind and Earthquake Forces

Lateral forces (wind, earthquake, impact) require a different load path than vertical loads:

**1. Wind pressure on walls:**
- Wind pushes horizontally against vertical wall surface
- Load acts on a wall panel area (e.g., 10 feet wide × 8 feet tall = 80 sq ft)
- Wind force: 20 lbs per sq ft × 80 sq ft = 1,600 lbs pushing horizontally

**2. Horizontal forces are resisted by frame bracing:**
- Knee braces (angled members connecting post and beam) are the primary lateral load carriers
- Knee brace angle: 45° is ideal; transfers load at equal parts vertical and horizontal
- Alternative: X-bracing (two diagonal members crossing, pinned at center)

**3. Knee brace to post/beam connections:**
- Both ends of the brace are mortised (one into the post, one into the beam)
- Brace carries compression (being pushed) and sometimes tension (being pulled)
- Connection strength must match brace strength—a strong brace with weak connections fails

**4. Load transfers to lateral support system:**
- Horizontal load flows through braced frame members to the foundation
- Multiple braces distribute load; a single braced bay carries lateral forces for several bays
- Typical spacing: knee braces every 2–3 bays (20–30 feet apart)

### Combined Vertical and Lateral Load Paths

Real structures experience combined loading. A moment (twisting force) arises when wind pushes on a tall structure:

**Example: 30-foot tall frame, 20-foot tall wall:**
- Wind force: 40 lbs/sq ft × (20 ft wide × 20 ft tall) = 16,000 lbs pushing horizontally at mid-height
- Moment (twisting force): 16,000 lbs × 10 feet = 160,000 foot-pounds
- This moment is resisted by the base connection: one corner in compression, opposite corner in tension

**Connection design for moment resistance:**
- Posts at frame corners must be anchored to the sill/foundation with redundant fastening
- Tensile force pulls one corner post upward; this requires:
  - Through-bolts anchoring the post to the foundation
  - Or a wide sill mortise with deep post tenon creating friction and bearing resistance
- Compressive force pushes the opposite corner down; bearing area is usually adequate

### Diagnostic Load Path Analysis

When a timber frame shows damage or deflection, tracing the load path reveals the cause:

**Symptom: Excessive roof deflection (sag), but pegs seem tight**
- Possible cause: rafter connections are inadequate; load is transferring but creating local crushing/bending of the connection area
- Solution: inspect rafter mortises for crushing signs; if found, reinforce the connection with additional pegs or bolts

**Symptom: Visible gaps opening at the plate-to-post connection**
- Possible cause: roof load is causing the post to bend sideways (lateral instability)
- Solution: roof load transfer through the plate is twisting the post. Install or reinforce knee braces to stabilize the structure laterally

**Symptom: Cracking emanating from a peg hole**
- Possible cause: peg is too tight or draw-boring is too aggressive. As wood dries, the structure is being pulled apart by the pegs
- Solution: loosen or remove and re-peg with less offset; pegs should draw the joint tight but not pre-stress the wood

**Symptom: One section of wall bulging outward**
- Possible cause: wind load is being concentrated on that section due to missing or failed lateral bracing
- Solution: inspect knee braces for cracks or loose connections; repair or rebuild as needed

:::warning
Never assume a load path is adequate without verification. Test suspected weak connections with load testing (proof loading). A connection that appears tight may transmit load with excessive deformation. Measure deflection under known loads to verify actual performance.
:::

</section>

<section id="scarf-joints">

## Scarf Joints for Length Extension

When a single timber is too short, two pieces are joined end-to-end using a scarf joint. This joint must transfer bending moment and shear force over its length.

**Half-lap scarf (simplest):**
- Cut a 45° angle at the end of each timber (scarf angle 30–45°)
- Overlap them with the grain parallel (end-grain to end-grain at the joint face)
- Peg with two wooden pegs through the overlap
- Length of overlap: 4–6× timber depth (e.g., for an 8" beam, 32–48" overlap)

**For an 8×12" beam with 40° scarf angle:**
```
Scarf length = beam depth / tan(40°) = 12" / 0.84 ≈ 14.3"
Overlap: 6× depth = 72"
Total scarfed section: ~72" long
```

**Advantages of scarf joints:**
- Simple layout and cutting
- Pegs can be spaced along the overlap for even load distribution
- Forgiving: slightly imperfect cuts still transfer load

**Disadvantages:**
- Long overlap requires significant timber length
- Plane scarf angle is perpendicular to grain, tending to split

**Alternative: Mortise-and-tenon scarf:**
- More complex; involves a short mortise-and-tenon within the scarf overlap
- Used when timber length is limited
- Primarily in very high-performance frames (bridges, heavy industrial buildings)

</section>

<section id="knee-braces">

## Knee Braces and Lateral Bracing

Knee braces are angled timbers connecting a post and beam to resist lateral (wind and earthquake) forces and reduce beam deflection.

**Design:**

- Angle: 45° is ideal for combined bending and shear resistance; 30–60° range is acceptable
- Size: 4×6" to 6×8" (100×150 to 150×200 mm) typical
- Both ends mortise-and-tenon into the post and beam
- Typical span: 3–6 m between posts with bracing every 2–3 m

**Layout for 45° knee brace:**

1. Measure equal distances from the post-beam corner along both the beam and post (e.g., 1 m each for a 1.4 m brace)
2. Connect these points with a line; this line is the centerline of the brace
3. Offset the centerline by the brace width on both sides to mark the brace edges
4. Mark mortises on post and beam

**Lateral bracing in a frame:**

Full frames require diagonal bracing to resist racking:
- **X-bracing:** Two diagonal members crossing, pinned at center
- **Knee braces at corners:** Knee brace + corner post + girt combination
- **Girts + infill:** Horizontal girts with diagonal infill (boards or timber)

Typically, lateral bracing is installed on every 2–3 bays to maintain frame rigidity.

<section id="connections">

## Plate and Sill Connections

**Sill connection (base of posts):**

Posts are mortised into a sill beam (horizontal member at ground level) using a mortise-and-tenon joint. The sill rests on a stone or brick foundation.

- **Sill timber:** Typically 8×12" or larger, made of rot-resistant wood
- **Post tenon:** Often extends 6–8" into the sill, with larger cross-section than upper mortises
- **Peg depth:** Through-peg to maximize shear transfer

**Plate connection (top of frame):**

A top plate (girt running across the top of posts) carries roof loads and ties the frame together laterally.

- **Connection:** Posts mortise into the plate with large tenons
- **Roof beam:** Typically mortises into the plate as well
- **Pegging:** Double-pegged (two pegs per joint) for redundancy

**Corner posts:**

At frame corners, the post supports two perpendicular beams. Both beams are mortised into the post, creating a complex joint:

- Offset the mortises horizontally and vertically so they do not intersect
- Tenons may be at different depths (one deeper than the other)
- Peg each mortise-and-tenon independently

**Connections to foundation:**

- Use wooden shims under the sill to level it and protect from moisture
- Raise the sill 150–300 mm above grade to avoid splash-back water
- In historical work, pegs are sometimes wedged through the sill and foundation stone
- In modern work, an epoxy-anchored foundation bolt with a steel angle is an alternative (though less traditional)

<section id="frame-raising">

## Frame Raising Sequence

Building a timber frame on the ground and raising it in place is the traditional method. It requires planning, safety equipment, and a team.

**Pre-raising preparation:**

1. **Build the frame flat:** Assemble the frame components (posts, beams, braces) on a flat ground area, fully jointed and pegged. This is the "bent" — one transverse unit of the frame.

2. **Test fit:** Ensure all joints are snug and the frame is square (measure diagonals; they should be equal).

3. **Inspect pegs:** All pegs should be driven fully; tap any loose pegs before raising.

4. **Prepare the foundation:** The sill beam must rest on a level, prepared surface (compacted gravel or stone pad). Mark the exact position of the sill.

**Raising the frame:**

1. **Attach guide ropes:** Tie ropes to the top of the frame (typically at the ridge or beam center).

2. **Use mechanical advantage:**
   - Manual: Ropes over a pulley, 4–8 people pulling
   - Mechanical: Come-along, block and tackle, or small crane

3. **Lift slowly:** Raise the frame a few centimeters every minute; do not jerk. Watch for binding or shifting.

4. **Brace temporarily:** As the frame tilts up, install temporary diagonal braces (2×4 lumber) to keep it stable until the sill lands on the foundation.

5. **Level and check position:** Once the sill touches the foundation, measure to ensure proper alignment. Adjust before the next bent is raised.

6. **Repeat:** Raise successive bents and connect them with beams and tie-braces.

7. **Install final roof structure:** Once all bents are in place and connected, install purlins (roof beams) and rafters.

:::warning
Never raise a frame in wind or poor visibility. Have spotters watching for binding, rope snags, and foundation contact. Use proper fall protection if working at height (>1.5 m). Assign one person as the safety coordinator to call "halt" if any hazard appears.
:::

<section id="tools-and-skills">

## Essential Tools and Skill Development

**Hand tools:**

- **Mortise chisel set:** 0.5", 0.75", 1", 1.25", 1.5", 2" sizes
- **Framing square:** 24" arm length minimum
- **Hand saws:** Ripsaw (4–5 tpi) and crosscut (8–10 tpi)
- **Auger bits:** Set of 0.5" to 2" sizes
- **Marking gauge and knife:** For consistent lines
- **Wooden mallet:** 1–1.5 kg

**Skills to develop:**

1. **Sharp tool maintenance:** Mortise chisels must be honed every 1–2 hours of work. Dull tools require excessive force, causing mistakes and tool damage.

2. **Layout precision:** Layout errors are amplified when cutting. Double-check all dimensions before cutting.

3. **Saw technique:** Cut along the line, not to the line. The saw kerf thickness (2–3 mm) must be accounted for.

4. **Mallet control:** Striking a chisel requires a smooth, controlled swing, not a hard blow. Aim for consistent pace and depth.

5. **Team communication:** During frame raising, establish clear signals (hand signals, bell, or radio) for stop, go, and emergency halt.

**Learning pathway:**

- Start with small joints (e.g., 4×4" timber sample projects)
- Progress to full-size practice joints before attempting frame construction
- Many timber-frame organizations offer week-long workshops combining instruction and hands-on practice

<section id="maintenance">

## Long-Term Maintenance and Repair

**Annual inspection:**
- Check pegs for looseness; tighten or re-peg if necessary
- Inspect for rot, especially in the sill and lower posts (most vulnerable)
- Look for large cracks radiating from joints (may indicate component failure)

**Seasonal movement:**
- Expect visible gaps between joints as timber shrinks and swells seasonally
- These are normal and do not indicate structural failure
- Pegs may need re-driving after 1–2 years if gaps appear

**Repair of failed pegs:**
- Drill out the old peg carefully (use a drill press with a spade bit)
- Chisel out any remaining peg fragments
- Insert a new peg of the same dimensions

**Timber replacement:**
- If a structural member rots, it must be replaced
- This typically requires temporary bracing and disconnection of adjacent members
- Hire a timber-frame specialist for complex repairs

A properly built timber frame is a multi-generational structure. With attention to drainage, ventilation, and pegs, timber frames can outlast nearly any other building system.
