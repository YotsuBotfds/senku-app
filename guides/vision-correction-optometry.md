boundary-only intake before optometry or medical handoff: what vision difficulty
is happening, when it started, whether it is one eye or both eyes, whether the

## 1\. Overview: Vision Impairment Post-Collapse

Vision correction ranks among the most critical survival skills in any collapse scenario. Without access to modern optometry, millions will face debilitating vision impairment. The ability to test vision, grind lenses, and manufacture eyeglasses becomes essential medical knowledge.

This guide covers the complete process of field optometry: from identifying refractive errors to manufacturing functional spectacles using improvised materials and traditional grinding techniques.

:::tip
Use this guide for symptom-first vision testing, prescription selection, and frame/lens matching. If you need the underlying optics of focal length, aberrations, or instrument design, hand off to [Optics & Lens Science (Detailed)](optics-lens-science-detailed.md).
:::

:::tip
Approximately 25-30% of adults require some form of vision correction. In collapse scenarios, this percentage rises significantly as stress and malnutrition affect ocular health.
:::

### Why This Matters

-   Vision impairment reduces safety and productivity by 40-60%
-   Untreated presbyopia (age-related) affects 90% of people over 45
-   Myopia (nearsightedness) increased 66% in last 30 years due to screen use
-   Corrected vision significantly improves quality of life and mental health
-   Craftspeople, gardeners, and leaders require sharp vision for survival tasks

:::note
Historical record: During the Great Depression and WWII, communities often had at least one person trained in basic lens grinding. This skill disappeared from general knowledge by 1970.
:::

### Prerequisites

-   Basic understanding of optics and light refraction
-   Manual dexterity and patience for precision work
-   Access to glass, polishing compounds, and grinding tools
-   Ability to perform basic mathematics (diopter calculations)
-   Materials for frame construction (wire, wood, or polymer)

## 2\. Understanding Vision Problems

The human eye focuses light onto the retina, where it's converted to neural signals sent to the brain. Refractive errors occur when the eye's shape or corneal curvature prevents proper focusing.

![👓 Vision Correction &amp; Field Optometry diagram 1](../assets/svgs/vision-correction-optometry-1.svg)

### Myopia (Nearsightedness)

The eye is too long or cornea is too curved. Light focuses in front of the retina, making distant objects blurry.

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Symptom</th><th scope="row">Severity Indicator</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Can't read signs at distance</td><td>Mild (up to -2.00 diopters)</td></tr><tr><td>Cannot focus beyond arm's length</td><td>Moderate (-2.00 to -5.00)</td></tr><tr><td>Only sees clearly within 6 inches</td><td>High (below -5.00)</td></tr></tbody></table>

### Hyperopia (Farsightedness)

The eye is too short or cornea is too flat. Light focuses behind the retina, making near objects blurry.

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Symptom</th><th scope="row">Severity Indicator</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Difficulty reading small text</td><td>Mild (up to +2.00 diopters)</td></tr><tr><td>Blurry vision at reading distance</td><td>Moderate (+2.00 to +5.00)</td></tr><tr><td>All distances blurry, especially close</td><td>High (above +5.00)</td></tr></tbody></table>

### Astigmatism

The cornea or lens is irregularly shaped, like a football rather than a sphere. Multiple focal points create blurry vision at all distances.

-   **Corneal astigmatism:** Irregular cornea shape (most common)
-   **Lenticular astigmatism:** Lens shape abnormality
-   **Mixed:** Combination of myopia/hyperopia with astigmatism

:::warning
Astigmatism is the most difficult refractive error to correct in field conditions and requires specialized lens grinding techniques.
:::

### Presbyopia (Age-Related)

The lens loses elasticity with age, reducing ability to focus on near objects. Typically begins around age 45.

-   Occurs in everyone eventually (not a disease)
-   Accelerated by close work and eye strain
-   Corrected with reading glasses (typically +1.00 to +3.50)
-   May be combined with other refractive errors

:::note
In collapse scenarios without modern nutrition, presbyopia may develop earlier (late 30s) due to oxidative stress and inflammation affecting lens proteins.
:::

## 3\. Simple Eye Testing: Snellen Chart Construction

The Snellen chart measures visual acuity by testing the ability to distinguish details at specific distances. A 20/20 line is readable at 20 feet; 20/200 is readable only at 20 feet that a normal eye sees at 200 feet.

### DIY Snellen Chart Construction

Building a field-usable vision chart requires precision with letter sizing and spacing.

Print or draw letters with precise proportions: Each letter occupies a 5x5 grid of "modules" (small units), with strokes 1 module thick.
Use Arial or similar sans-serif font family for consistency and clarity.
Mount on high-contrast background (black letters on white, or white on black).
Ensure uniform lighting: avoid glare and shadows during testing.
Calibrate distance: most testing is done at 20 feet (6 meters). Mark position clearly.

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Visual Acuity</th><th scope="row">Letter Height at 20 ft</th><th scope="row">Top Line Letter Size</th><th scope="row">Indication</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>20/200</td><td>87.6 mm</td><td>88.5 mm</td><td>Legally blind threshold</td></tr><tr><td>20/100</td><td>43.8 mm</td><td>44.3 mm</td><td>Needs correction</td></tr><tr><td>20/70</td><td>30.6 mm</td><td>31.0 mm</td><td>Mild impairment</td></tr><tr><td>20/40</td><td>17.5 mm</td><td>17.7 mm</td><td>Moderate impairment</td></tr><tr><td>20/20</td><td>8.75 mm</td><td>8.87 mm</td><td>Normal vision</td></tr></tbody></table>

### Testing Protocol

Have patient cover one eye with occluder (not hand, which allows peeking).
Start from largest (top) line; patient reads progressively smaller lines.
Record smallest line readable with ≤2 errors as visual acuity for that eye.
Repeat for other eye, then test both eyes together.
Allow 2-3 seconds per line for accommodation.

### Interpreting Results

-   **20/20:** Normal vision; no correction needed for distance
-   **20/30-20/60:** Mild myopia; correction recommended for safety
-   **20/100+:** Significant myopia; vision correction essential
-   **Difference between eyes:** Indicates possible astigmatism or anisometropia (different prescriptions each eye)

:::tip
Always test in consistent lighting conditions. Test during consistent times of day, as vision can vary with fatigue, time of day, and ambient light intensity.
:::

<section id="pinhole-test">

## 4\. Pinhole Test for Refractive Errors

The pinhole test uses a simple aperture to improve vision regardless of refractive error. It works by reducing spherical aberration and increasing depth of field.

### How It Works

A pinhole (diameter 1-2mm) allows only light rays that travel straight through the eye's center, bypassing imperfections in corneal curvature and lens shape.

![👓 Vision Correction &amp; Field Optometry diagram 2](../assets/svgs/vision-correction-optometry-2.svg)

### Construction of a Pinhole Occluder

Take a piece of thin metal (aluminum foil, thin brass, or steel) about 1mm thick.
Create a hole using a needle or fine drill bit: target diameter 1.0-1.5mm (smaller = sharper, but dimmer).
Use a piece of foam or rubber as backing to prevent eye injury.
Mount in a handle made from wood, bone, or plastic for comfortable holding.

### Testing Procedure

1.  Have patient look at eye chart without correction and note visual acuity
2.  Place pinhole occluder in front of eye, keeping distance ~1 inch
3.  Ask patient to look through pinhole and read chart again
4.  Compare acuity with and without pinhole

### Interpreting Results

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Result</th><th scope="row">Interpretation</th><th scope="row">Implication</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Vision improves significantly</td><td>Refractive error (myopia/hyperopia)</td><td>Lens correction will help</td></tr><tr><td>Vision improves slightly</td><td>Astigmatism with refractive error</td><td>Requires cylindrical lens</td></tr><tr><td>No improvement or worse</td><td>Possible corneal scarring or retinal damage</td><td>Glasses won't help; seek diagnosis</td></tr></tbody></table>

:::warning
If vision does not improve with pinhole test, the problem may not be a simple refractive error. Suspect cataracts, corneal scarring, or retinal disease.
:::

:::tip
Pinhole test is not a substitute for proper refraction but a quick diagnostic tool. It helps distinguish refractive errors from other causes of vision loss.
:::

<section id="lens-grinding">

## 5\. Lens Grinding Basics: Materials and Techniques

Grinding lenses is a precision craft that combines geometry, physics, and manual skill. The goal is to create a curved surface that focuses light at a precise distance from the lens.

### Routing Note: Choose the Lens Before You Grind

Keep the practical sequence in this guide: identify the refractive problem, confirm with pinhole and trial lenses, then select sphere/cylinder power and only then grind or source the matching lens. If you need to reason about focal length, image distance, or lens aberrations while choosing that power, use [Optics & Lens Science (Detailed)](optics-lens-science-detailed.md) for the underlying optics.

### Material Selection

#### Crown Glass

-   **Properties:** Low refractive index (1.52), low dispersion, excellent clarity
-   **Advantages:** Easy to work with, doesn't crack easily, maintains precision
-   **Disadvantages:** Thicker lenses for high prescriptions
-   **Source:** Recycled eyeglasses, old test lenses, window glass (less ideal)

#### Flint Glass

-   **Properties:** Higher refractive index (1.6-1.75), higher dispersion (more color fringing)
-   **Advantages:** Thinner lenses, more powerful corrections in compact form
-   **Disadvantages:** More difficult to grind, prone to chipping, requires skill
-   **Source:** Specialized optical suppliers or salvage from laboratory equipment

#### Plastic (Polycarbonate/CR-39)

-   **Properties:** Refractive index 1.55-1.58, lighter, safe (won't shatter)
-   **Advantages:** Impact resistant, lighter for high prescriptions, easier to shape
-   **Disadvantages:** Scratches easily, requires special polishing compounds, yellows with age
-   **Source:** Salvaged lenses, 3D printing in advanced scenarios

:::note
Crown glass from old eyeglasses is the most practical material for collapse scenarios. Verify glass type by checking old prescription: if labeled with refractive index, crown glass will be ~1.5.
:::

### Essential Tools for Lens Grinding

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Tool</th><th scope="row">Purpose</th><th scope="row">Improvisation</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Grinding wheel</td><td>Shape glass to rough curve</td><td>Emery wheel on lathe or hand-powered tool</td></tr><tr><td>Grinding stone (various grits)</td><td>Refine curve shape</td><td>Whetstone, sandstone, carborundum</td></tr><tr><td>Polishing pad</td><td>Create optical surface</td><td>Soft cloth with polishing compound (rouge, tin oxide)</td></tr><tr><td>Radius gauge</td><td>Measure curve accuracy</td><td>Manufactured gauges or home-made with known-diameter curves</td></tr><tr><td>Optical bench/straightedge</td><td>Check surface flatness</td><td>Polished steel rod or carefully made straightedge</td></tr><tr><td>Calipers</td><td>Measure lens thickness</td><td>Vernier calipers or improvised thickness gauge</td></tr></tbody></table>

### Basic Lens Grinding Process

Secure raw glass blank in a wooden or metal holder that protects hands and eyes from flying particles. Safety glasses essential.
Begin grinding with coarse stone (100-150 grit) to establish basic curve shape matching calculated radius.
Progress to medium grit (240-400) for better surface definition and to remove coarse scratches.
Use fine grit (600-1000) to prepare surface for polishing; check radius accuracy frequently.
Polish with fine compound (tin oxide or cerium oxide) on soft pad, applying moderate pressure in circular motions.
Test lens optically: look through it at known distance to verify focal length matches prescription.
Clean thoroughly before use to remove all grinding dust (critical for optical quality).

:::warning
Grinding glass creates fine sharp particles. Always wear: safety glasses, dust mask (N95 minimum), work gloves. Secure work area to prevent glass shards from becoming projectiles.
:::

### Achieving Precision

The key to quality lens grinding is understanding that the curve radius directly determines focal length:

Focal Length (mm) = Radius of Curvature (mm) × (Refractive Index - 1) / 2

For crown glass (n=1.52) with 500mm radius: focal length = 500 × 0.52 / 2 = 130mm = 7.7 diopters

:::tip
Keep a reference sample of each radius you create. This becomes invaluable for comparing work and troubleshooting. Mark clearly with radius value.
:::

<section id="lens-curves">

## 6\. Lens Curves and Diopter Calculations

A diopter (D) is the unit of lens power, defined as the reciprocal of focal length in meters: Power (D) = 1 / Focal Length (m).

### Single-Vision Lens Calculations

For a single refracting surface (plano-curved lens), the power depends on three factors:

Power (D) = (n₂ - n₁) / Radius of Curvature (m)
Where:
 n₂ = refractive index of glass
 n₁ = refractive index of air (1.0)
 Radius = radius of curvature in meters (positive for convex)

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Prescription (D)</th><th scope="row">Radius Crown Glass (mm)</th><th scope="row">Radius Flint Glass (mm)</th><th scope="row">Clinical Use</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>+1.00</td><td>1038</td><td>833</td><td>Mild hyperopia, reading add</td></tr><tr><td>+2.00</td><td>519</td><td>417</td><td>Moderate hyperopia</td></tr><tr><td>+3.00</td><td>346</td><td>278</td><td>Strong hyperopia/presbyopia</td></tr><tr><td>-1.00</td><td>-1038</td><td>-833</td><td>Mild myopia</td></tr><tr><td>-2.00</td><td>-519</td><td>-417</td><td>Moderate myopia</td></tr><tr><td>-3.00</td><td>-346</td><td>-278</td><td>Strong myopia</td></tr></tbody></table>

### Bicurve (Both Sides Curved) Lenses

For improved optical quality and thinner lenses, both surfaces are curved. The powers combine:

Total Power = Power Front Surface + Power Back Surface -
 (Center Thickness / Refractive Index) × Power Front × Power Back

For practical field work, a simpler approximation works when thickness is small:

Total Power ≈ Power Front + Power Back

### Understanding Cylinder Power (Astigmatism)

Astigmatism requires different focal lengths in two perpendicular meridians. This is corrected with cylindrical lenses:

-   **Spherical:** Same power in all meridians (affects both distance and direction equally)
-   **Cylindrical:** Power only in one meridian, zero power perpendicular (corrects astigmatism)
-   **Spherocylindrical:** Combination of sphere and cylinder (most prescriptions)

:::warning
Grinding cylindrical lenses requires advanced skill and specialized equipment. In field conditions, prioritize getting spherical corrections right first.
:::

### Prescription Format

Standard optical notation:

Sphere (Cyl) × Axis
Example: -1.50 (-0.75) × 180
 Sphere: -1.50 D (myopia)
 Cylinder: -0.75 D (additional myopia in 180° meridian)
 Axis: 180° (horizontal orientation of cylinder)

:::tip
When grinding for a customer without detailed prescription records, start with sphere correction only. Cylinder correction requires the patient to describe directional blurriness (e.g., "horizontal lines fuzzy, vertical OK").
:::

## 7\. Frame Manufacturing: Materials and Methods

The frame must hold lenses precisely, be comfortable for extended wear, and resist corrosion. Multiple material options exist for collapse scenarios.

### Wire Frames

Traditional method using flexible metal wire, excellent for low-resource scenarios.

#### Materials

-   **Stainless steel wire:** Best option; won't rust, durable, flexible
-   **Copper wire:** Good alternative; green patina prevents further corrosion
-   **Bronze/brass:** Acceptable; requires regular polishing to prevent tarnish
-   **Aluminum:** Avoid; oxidizes quickly, weak at joints

#### Construction Method

Form front frame shape: bend wire to create oval or rectangular eye openings, with bridge connecting both sides.
Create lens grooves: craft small notches or grooves in wire at precise locations where lens edges will rest.
Cut and polish lens edges: grind lens edges to fit wire grooves, ensuring uniform thickness at edge.
Secure lenses: set lenses in grooves, optionally using dental cement or thin binding wire to hold permanently.
Form temple (arm) extensions: bend additional wire segments at hinge points, extending backward to ears.
Create ear pieces: bend ends of temples into hoops that fit comfortably around ears.
Adjust bridge fit: fine-tune nose bridge to sit comfortably (should rest on nose, not pinch bridge or slip).

![👓 Vision Correction &amp; Field Optometry diagram 3](../assets/svgs/vision-correction-optometry-3.svg)

### Wood Frames

Lightweight, comfortable, and beautiful when properly finished.

-   **Materials:** Hardwood (walnut, oak, maple), must be dry to prevent warping
-   **Advantages:** Warm feel, easy to carve, adjustable
-   **Disadvantages:** Requires more skill, susceptible to humidity changes
-   **Construction:** Carve eye openings, bore holes for lens edges, create temple hinges with dowels

### Molded/Cast Frames

Using available polymers or recycled materials.

-   **Thermoplastic:** Salvaged plastic, heated and molded to frame shape
-   **3D Printed:** Advanced scenarios with functional plastic (PETG, nylon)
-   **Cast:** Metal casting techniques for aluminum or bronze frames

:::tip
Regardless of material, ensure the bridge width matches the wearer's interpupillary distance (IPD). Average adult IPD is 62-64mm, but ranges 54-74mm. Incorrect IPD causes eye strain and headaches.
:::

<section id="trial-lens-set">

## 8\. Trial Lens Set Creation

A trial lens set is a collection of standardized lenses used to determine exact prescription. Building one is a major project but invaluable for testing vision in your community.

### Lens-Selection Routing

If the complaint is "what lens should this person wear," stay in this guide and work from pinhole test to trial lenses to final prescription. If the complaint is "why does this lens focus here instead of there," move over to [Optics & Lens Science (Detailed)](optics-lens-science-detailed.md) for the focal-control math and aberration behavior.

### Minimal Functional Set

Start with these powers for most refractive errors:

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Power</th><th scope="row">Quantity</th><th scope="row">Purpose</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>+0.25, +0.50, +0.75, +1.00</td><td>2 each (OD, OS)</td><td>Mild hyperopia, reading adds</td></tr><tr><td>+1.50, +2.00, +2.50, +3.00</td><td>1 each</td><td>Moderate-strong hyperopia</td></tr><tr><td>-0.25, -0.50, -0.75, -1.00</td><td>2 each (OD, OS)</td><td>Mild myopia</td></tr><tr><td>-1.50, -2.00, -2.50, -3.00</td><td>1 each</td><td>Moderate-strong myopia</td></tr><tr><td>-4.00, -5.00, -6.00</td><td>1 each</td><td>High myopia</td></tr></tbody></table>

### Sourcing Lenses for Your Set

1.  Collect old eyeglasses and preserved trial lenses from medical facilities, optometrists, or salvage
2.  Grind custom lenses for powers not found in salvage materials
3.  Label each lens clearly with power (e.g., "+2.00 OD" for right eye, positive)
4.  Store in protective case, keeping lenses organized by power and eye
5.  Include carrying case or organized box with foam inserts

:::warning
Never mix prescription strengths in storage. Mislabeling causes significant errors during vision testing and customer dissatisfaction or worse.
:::

### Using Trial Lenses for Refraction

Start with pinhole test to establish whether vision improves with smaller aperture (likely refractive error).
Place weakest (+) lens in trial frame; ask if vision improves or worsens. If improves, progressively add stronger + lenses.
If no improvement with +, switch to (-) lenses; ask which power gives best clarity.
Fine-tune: once you find approximate power, try lenses in 0.25 D increments nearby.
Ask patient which of two powers looks clearer: "-1.00 or -1.25?" (never offer more than two choices)
Verify by reading eye chart: patient should read one line better than without correction.
Test other eye same way.
Test both eyes together: verify prescription doesn't cause imbalance or focusing strain.

:::note
During crisis, a simple trial set of ±0.50, ±1.00, ±1.50, ±2.00, ±3.00 will cover 80% of refractive errors. Expanding to 0.25 increments improves accuracy significantly.
:::

<section id="fitting-adjustment">

## 9\. Fitting & Adjustment: Ensuring Comfort and Function

Even perfect lenses fail if the frame doesn't fit properly. Proper fitting maximizes comfort, prevents headaches, and ensures intended correction.

### Fitting Measurements

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Measurement</th><th scope="row">Method</th><th scope="row">Typical Range</th><th scope="row">Impact if Wrong</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Interpupillary Distance (IPD)</td><td>Measure center to center distance between pupils</td><td>54-74 mm</td><td>Strain, image misalignment, headache</td></tr><tr><td>Bridge width</td><td>Measure inner edge of each lens eye</td><td>14-18 mm</td><td>Uncomfortable fit, slipping</td></tr><tr><td>Lens eye height</td><td>Vertical measurement of lens opening</td><td>28-38 mm</td><td>Limited field of view if too small</td></tr><tr><td>Temple length</td><td>Back of lens eye to ear piece end</td><td>120-150 mm</td><td>Slipping or pinching at ears</td></tr></tbody></table>

### Fitting Process

Measure patient's IPD: have them look straight ahead, mark pupil centers with ruler, measure distance.
Position frame on face: optical centers should align with pupils (within 2mm tolerance).
Check temple fit: should sit comfortably along temple bone without pinching or slipping.
Verify bridge: should rest on nose bridge without pressure, not touching too high (bridge of nose) or too low (tip).
Adjust if needed: wire frames can be bent carefully for fit; wooden frames may need carving adjustments.
Test comfort: have wearer wear for 15 minutes, checking for pressure points or slipping.

### Common Adjustment Issues and Solutions

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Problem</th><th scope="row">Cause</th><th scope="row">Solution</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Glasses slip down</td><td>Temple angle too wide, nose bridge too wide</td><td>Bend temples inward, increase nose bridge height slightly</td></tr><tr><td>Pressure on nose</td><td>Bridge too tight or resting wrong position</td><td>Widen bridge gently, adjust nose rest pads</td></tr><tr><td>Earache or temple pressure</td><td>Temple too short or angled wrong</td><td>Lengthen temples or adjust ear piece angle</td></tr><tr><td>Blurry at edges or centers</td><td>Lens optical centers misaligned with pupils</td><td>Reposition lenses in frame or refit frame to face</td></tr><tr><td>Headache after wearing</td><td>Prescription imbalance, IPD mismatch, or poor fit</td><td>Verify prescription and frame alignment; test with trial lenses</td></tr></tbody></table>

:::tip
When adjusting wire frames, heat gently in water bath first (if available) to make material more pliable. Never bend cold metal rapidly as it can snap or crack.
:::

## 10\. Magnifying Glasses for Near Work

Beyond vision correction, people need magnification for detailed work: sewing, reading, mechanical repair, jewelry making. Simple magnifiers extend productive years significantly.

### Magnification Calculations

Magnification power relates directly to diopter strength:

Magnification ≈ Diopter Power / 4
Examples:
 +4.00 D lens = 1× magnification (minimal)
 +8.00 D lens = 2× magnification
 +16.00 D lens = 4× magnification
 +20.00 D lens = 5× magnification

### Simple Hand Magnifier Construction

Source or grind a high-power positive lens (+8.00 to +20.00 D is typical).
Mount in a handle made of wood, bone, or molded plastic with opening roughly 25-35mm diameter.
Ensure lens is secure and centered; glue or mechanical clips work.
Add optional handle features: leather wrapping for grip, adjustable eye loop for consistency.

### Loupe Magnifier (Jeweler's Style)

-   High-magnification (10-20×) for very fine detail work
-   Compact, fits in pocket or worn on cord around neck
-   Requires steady hand and close working distance (25-50mm)
-   Most useful for craftspeople: jewelers, watch repairers, electricians

### Magnifying Light Box (Advanced)

For detailed work, combining magnification with light improves visibility dramatically:

-   Mount magnifier above task board with focused light source (oil lamp, daylight tube)
-   Creates hands-free workspace for detailed assembly or repair
-   Reduces eye strain significantly for prolonged work
-   Essential for craftspeople in collapse scenarios

:::note
A 3-4× hand magnifier covers 95% of practical needs for most people: reading, crafting, inspection. Higher magnifications useful but require steadier hands and closer working distance.

## 11\. Protecting and Maintaining Eyeglasses

In collapse scenarios, a pair of eyeglasses may be irreplaceable for months or longer. Protective practices extend their life significantly.

### Storage Best Practices

-   **Case:** Hard case provides best protection; soft cases acceptable if rigid inner lining added
-   **Environment:** Keep away from heat (>60°C), extreme cold, moisture, dust
-   **Position:** Store lens-up in case (lenses don't contact surfaces)
-   **Backup pair:** Maintain backup glasses (even if older prescription) for critical situations
-   **Children:** Keep safely stored away from children who may damage or lose

### Cleaning and Care

Rinse lenses with clean water or gentle breath moisture before wiping.
Wipe with soft cloth (microfiber, clean cotton) using gentle circular motions.
For stubborn residue, use mild soap and water; rinse thoroughly and dry completely.
Never use harsh chemicals, solvents, or abrasive cloths (paper towels, clothing).
Inspect regularly for scratches, loose lenses, bent frames, corroded hinges.
Check nose pads and ear pieces: replace or adjust if uncomfortable or worn.

### Minor Repairs

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Problem</th><th scope="row">Repair Method</th><th scope="row">Materials Needed</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Loose screw at hinge</td><td>Tighten with small screwdriver, add tiny drop of clear epoxy to prevent loosening</td><td>Screwdriver, epoxy adhesive</td></tr><tr><td>Cracked lens</td><td>If small crack, seal edge with clear epoxy to prevent spreading; otherwise replace lens</td><td>Clear epoxy, small brush</td></tr><tr><td>Bent frame</td><td>Gently heat in warm water (not boiling); bend carefully back to original shape</td><td>Warm water, patience</td></tr><tr><td>Worn nose pads</td><td>Replace with new pads (salvaged from old glasses) or pad with thin cork/felt</td><td>Replacement pads or cork</td></tr><tr><td>Corroded hinge</td><td>Clean with fine steel wool or wire brush; apply light oil (sewing machine oil)</td><td>Steel wool, oil</td></tr></tbody></table>

### Scratched Lens Prevention and Treatment

-   **Prevention:** Always use protective case, never set lenses-down on hard surfaces, use microfiber cloth for cleaning
-   **Minor scratches:** Often not visible once worn (refractive effect minimal if small)
-   **Visible scratches:** Polish gently with fine polishing compound (tin oxide, cerium oxide) on soft pad; limited success
-   **Deep scratches:** May require lens replacement if affecting central optical area

:::warning
Scratched lenses that affect central vision area should be replaced. Attempting to use severely scratched lenses causes eye strain, headaches, and reduced safety.
:::

### Prescription Verification Over Time

Vision changes gradually; periodically verify prescription remains correct:

-   Test with pinhole occluder every 6-12 months
-   Read eye chart with current glasses; should still read 20/20 or better
-   If vision degradation noted, perform full refraction with trial lenses
-   Update prescription if change >0.50 D detected

:::tip
Keep detailed records of each person's prescription and when glasses were made. This helps track changes over time and troubleshoot if new symptoms emerge.
:::

## 12\. Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

Learning from others' errors prevents wasted materials, time, and customer dissatisfaction.

### Prescription and Measurement Errors

-   **Mistake:** Confusing right and left eye prescriptions (transposing OD and OS)
-   **Result:** Customer receives completely wrong prescription; significant vision dysfunction
-   **Prevention:** Always mark OD/OS clearly on test lenses and during refraction; double-check before manufacturing

-   **Mistake:** Testing vision in poor lighting or with reflections
-   **Result:** Inaccurate refractive error measurement; customer receives wrong prescription
-   **Prevention:** Test in consistent, bright, non-glare environment; repeat test multiple times if patient seems uncertain

-   **Mistake:** Not measuring interpupillary distance; assuming standard 62-64mm
-   **Result:** Optical centers misaligned with pupils; headaches, eye strain, image distortion
-   **Prevention:** Measure IPD for every customer without exception

### Lens Grinding Errors

-   **Mistake:** Not checking glass cleanliness before grinding; dust particles scratch lens during process
-   **Result:** Scatched lenses requiring restart of entire grinding process
-   **Prevention:** Inspect glass carefully; wipe thoroughly with clean cloth; grind in clean space away from dust sources

-   **Mistake:** Grinding radius incorrectly; miscalculating required radius for desired diopter power
-   **Result:** Lenses have wrong power; customer receives ineffective or harmful prescription
-   **Prevention:** Double-check radius calculation before grinding; verify against reference samples; test optically before finishing

-   **Mistake:** Stopping polishing too early; leaving scratches from grinding stone on optical surface
-   **Result:** Lenses appear hazy, optically defective; low-quality vision through glasses
-   **Prevention:** Continue polishing until surface is completely smooth and clear; inspect under good light before declaring finished

### Frame and Fitting Errors

-   **Mistake:** Making bridge too wide for patient's face; glasses slide down or don't sit properly
-   **Result:** Customer must constantly adjust glasses; comfort reduced; vision interrupted
-   **Prevention:** Measure face width and bridge fit carefully; test frame fit before finalizing lens installation

-   **Mistake:** Using low-quality materials in frame that degrade (rust, warp, crack) after weeks of use
-   **Result:** Customer returns with damaged glasses; loses confidence in your craftsmanship
-   **Prevention:** Invest in quality materials (stainless steel, hardwood, good polymer); test materials before committing to frames

:::note
Keep a log of every pair of glasses you make: customer name, prescription, frame type, date, materials used. This documents your work and helps identify patterns if problems emerge.
:::

### Safety Errors

-   **Mistake:** Not wearing proper eye protection during grinding; glass shards strike eyes
-   **Result:** Eye injury, potentially permanent vision loss
-   **Prevention:** Always wear full safety glasses during any grinding or cutting work

-   **Mistake:** Not warning customer about fragile glasses or prescription limitations
-   **Result:** Customer breaks glasses, thinks prescription is wrong, or relies on glasses unsuitable for their tasks
-   **Prevention:** Provide clear instructions on care and limitations; discuss visual tasks they need to perform and adjust prescription accordingly

:::warning
Document all instructions provided to customers about vision prescription, safe use, and care. This protects you if problems arise later and ensures patient safety.
:::
