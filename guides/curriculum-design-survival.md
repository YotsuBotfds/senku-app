## Curriculum Design Framework

A curriculum is an organized plan: what to teach, in what order, to whom, and why. Without curriculum design, teaching is haphazard—you teach what comes to mind, learners learn fragmented knowledge, knowledge gaps emerge.

Post-collapse communities have access to the Offline Knowledge Compendium (528 guides). You cannot teach everything equally; you must design what to teach, prioritize essential knowledge, and sequence skills logically.

This guide walks through:
1. **Assessing community needs** (what's actually required for survival)
2. **Categorizing knowledge** (core vs. elective)
3. **Designing progression** (what comes first, what depends on prerequisites)
4. **Building course sequences** (organized learning paths)
5. **Creating schedules** (practical implementation)

:::tip
**Principle:** Good curriculum reflects community reality, not abstract ideals. A coastal community's food preservation curriculum emphasizes fish preservation. An agricultural community emphasizes crops. Design to match your needs.
:::

</section>

<section id="community-needs">

## Step 1: Community Needs Assessment

Before designing curriculum, understand what your community actually needs.

### Critical Skills Inventory

Ask: **What skills are required for this community's survival and prosperity?**

Create list by function:

| Life Function | Critical Skills | Who Needs It | Urgency |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Food Acquisition** | Agriculture, hunting/fishing, food preservation, cooking | Everyone (food security) | Immediate |
| **Water** | Finding clean water, purification, storage | Everyone | Immediate |
| **Shelter** | Basic construction/repair, maintaining structures | 3-5 people minimum | Immediate |
| **Health** | Basic first aid, recognizing illness, herbal medicine | 2-3 people minimum | High |
| **Fire/Energy** | Fire starting, fuel management, heating | Everyone basic; 1-2 expert | Immediate |
| **Tools/Production** | Carpentry, metalworking, textile production | 2-5 people | High |
| **Preservation of Knowledge** | Record-keeping, teaching others, documentation | 1-2 people | High |
| **Leadership/Governance** | Decision-making, conflict resolution, planning | 3-5 people | High |
| **Safety/Defense** | Risk identification, hazard response, community protection | Variable | Medium-High |

### Depth Assessment

For each critical skill, determine depth needed:

**Basic:** All community members need baseline (water safety, fire safety, first aid)

**Competent:** Multiple people need working proficiency (food preservation, basic medicine, tool use)

**Expert:** 1-3 people need deep expertise (advanced medicine, advanced preservation, specialized trades)

**Breadth Assessment**

How many people need each skill?

- **Food preservation:** Everyone for basic home preservation; 3-5 for community scale
- **Metalworking:** 1-2 people unless trade-focused community
- **Medicine:** 2-3 people for basic; 1 for advanced
- **Agriculture:** Everyone for home garden; 2-5 for community scale

### Gap Analysis

What critical skills does your community currently lack?

- Who currently has medical knowledge? Is it sufficient?
- Who knows food preservation? Is knowledge concentrated in one elderly person?
- Who can repair structures? What happens when that person dies?

**Priority:** Communities training replacements for knowledge held by elderly members have highest urgency.

<section id="knowledge-types">

## Step 2: Categorizing Knowledge as Core vs. Elective

Not all guides are equally important. Organize systematically.

### Core Knowledge (Everyone)

Essential for survival and basic function. Everyone should achieve basic competency.

**Typical core curriculum:**
- Water safety and procurement (drinking untreated water kills)
- Basic food safety (spoilage and poisoning prevention)
- Fire safety (preventing burns, house fires)
- Basic first aid (treating wounds, recognizing serious illness)
- Food preservation (preventing starvation in winter/scarcity)
- Basic agriculture (growing own food)
- Sanitation (preventing disease)

**Achievement level:** All community members reach "Basic" or "Developing" level

**Timeline:** Ages 5-16 (primary education)

### Practical Core (Multiple People)

Skills needed at scale for community function. Multiple people must achieve proficiency.

**Typical practical core:**
- Advanced food preservation (community-scale production)
- Carpentry/construction (shelter, structures, tools)
- Basic medicine (beyond first aid; recognizing/treating illness)
- Water management (systems, contamination prevention, storage)
- Tool maintenance and repair
- Textile production (clothing)
- Fire management (beyond personal safety)

**Achievement level:** 2-5 people reach "Proficient" level per skill

**Timeline:** Ages 11-18 (apprenticeship), focused on motivated individuals

### Specialized Knowledge (Few People)

Advanced expertise in specific domains. 1-3 people need deep knowledge.

**Typical specialized knowledge:**
- Advanced medicine/surgery
- Advanced metalworking
- Complex construction/engineering
- Herbal medicine preparation
- Advanced preservation techniques
- Master-level teaching

**Achievement level:** 1-3 people reach "Advanced/Expert" level per specialty

**Timeline:** Ages 14-25+ (master-track)

### Elective Knowledge (Interest-Based)

Valuable for community but not essential for survival. Learners choose based on interest.

**Typical elective:**
- Advanced crafts (leatherworking, advanced weaving)
- Navigation and geography
- History and record-keeping
- Philosophy and ethics
- Creative skills (music, art, storytelling)
- Specialized trades (only if community has need)

**Achievement level:** Variable; depends on interest

**Timeline:** Age 12+ for motivated learners

### Decision Matrix

Use this to classify guides:

| Guide | Essential for Survival? | Multiple People Need It? | How Many? | Classification |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Water purification | Yes | Yes | Everyone | Core |
| Food preservation | Yes | Yes | 3-5 at proficiency | Practical Core |
| Advanced surgery | No (basic medicine suffices) | No (1-2 people) | 1-2 experts | Specialized |
| Leatherworking | No (clothing comes from textiles) | No | 1-2 | Elective |
| Metalworking | Depends on community | Yes | 2-5 | Practical Core OR Specialized |

<section id="sequencing">

## Step 3: Sequencing & Prerequisites

Not all skills can be taught simultaneously. Some have prerequisites; some depend on prior knowledge.

### Prerequisite Mapping

**Example: Food Preservation Chain**

```
Basic food safety (ages 5-7)
    ↓
Agricultural knowledge (ages 8-10)
    ↓
Food preservation awareness (ages 8-10)
    ↓
Specific preservation methods (ages 11-13):
    - Smoking
    - Drying
    - Salting
    - Fermentation
    ↓
Advanced preservation (ages 14-16):
    - Troubleshooting spoilage
    - Scaling to community
    - Teaching others
    ↓
Master-level knowledge (ages 18+):
    - Innovation and improvement
    - Documentation
    - Mentoring
```

### Logical Ordering

**Rule:** Foundational knowledge before advanced.

**Rule:** Safer skills before dangerous.

**Rule:** Knowledge before responsibility.

### Sample Prerequisite Chart

| Skill | Prerequisite | Can Begin Age | Reaches Competency |
|---|---|---|---|
| Basic first aid | Fire safety, basic understanding of anatomy | 8 | 12 |
| Medicine/diagnosis | Basic first aid, plant knowledge | 12 | 16+ |
| Carpentry | Tool use fundamentals, spatial reasoning | 8 | 14-16 |
| Metalworking | Fire safety, tool use, carpentry basics | 11 | 16+ |
| Advanced farming | Basic farming, soil knowledge, crop varieties | 12 | 15+ |
| Herbal medicine | Plant identification, basic chemistry, medicine | 11 | 16+ |

:::info-box
**Prerequisite discovery:** When designing curriculum, ask: "What must a learner know before learning this?" Write it down. Check: do we teach that first? If not, add it to curriculum before.
:::

<section id="design-example">

## Step 4: Sample Community Curriculum Design

Here's concrete example: **150-person agricultural community, post-collapse, needs-driven design**

### Community Needs Analysis

**Critical gaps identified:**
- Food preservation knowledge held by one elderly woman (age 78)
- Medicine knowledge concentrated in one person
- Metalworking knowledge only in one person (no backup)
- Agriculture broadly understood but no formal training

**Priority:** Train replacements for knowledge at risk of death/departure

### Core Curriculum Design

**Ages 5-7 (Foundation)**
- Water safety: identify contamination, never drink untreated
- Fire safety: danger zones, never touch
- Food safety: wash hands, avoid spoiled food
- Basic plant identification: safe plants vs. poisonous
- Animal safety: which animals are safe

**Duration:** Ongoing through ages 5-7, habit-focused

**Ages 8-10 (Basic Competency)**
- Fire starting and management (supervised)
- Water procurement: well, bucket, boiling
- Basic first aid: bandaging, recognizing serious injury
- Plant identification: 10+ edible species
- Tool use: knife, axe basics
- Cooking: preparing simple meals, heating water
- Agriculture: active participation in planting, harvesting, weeding

**Duration:** Ongoing, 30-45 minutes daily practice, 3-4 formal sessions weekly

**Ages 11-13 (Intermediate Skills)**

All youth continue with:
- Advanced agriculture: soil preparation, crop rotation, pest control
- Advanced water management: storage, testing, contamination prevention

Plus specialize in one of:
- **Path 1 - Food Preservation (3-5 youth):**
  - Smoking meat/fish
  - Drying vegetables/fruit
  - Fermentation (sauerkraut, kimchi)
  - Salting techniques
  - Storage management
  - Duration: 15-20 hours/week, 12-18 months

- **Path 2 - Medicine (2-3 youth):**
  - Advanced first aid
  - Plant medicine preparation
  - Recognizing common illnesses
  - Herbal remedy selection
  - Duration: 12-15 hours/week, 18+ months

- **Path 3 - Metalworking (1-2 youth):**
  - Forge operation
  - Tool creation
  - Hardware production
  - Duration: 20+ hours/week, 24+ months

**Ages 14-16 (Advanced Specialization)**

Continuing specialists deepen knowledge:
- Food preservation specialists: troubleshooting, scaling, teaching
- Medicine specialists: advanced diagnosis, complex treatments
- Metalworking specialists: complex tools, design, teaching

Plus additional practical core:
- All youth: advanced carpentry basics
- Cross-training: second skill (15-year-old food specialist learning basic carpentry for food storage design)

**Duration:** Primary apprenticeship, 20-30 hours/week

**Ages 17-18+ (Journeyman/Expert)**

- Transition to independent skilled work or master-track preparation

### Implementation Schedule

**Year 1:**
- Establish core curriculum for ages 5-7 and 8-10 (all children)
- Recruit and begin paths for ages 11-13 (identify 5-8 learners)
- Begin documenting existing knowledge from elderly practitioners

**Year 2:**
- Core curriculum established and running smoothly
- Intermediate specialists 12-18 months into training
- First apprentices reaching journeyman candidates

**Year 3:**
- First journeymen advanced and teaching
- Second cohort of apprentices recruited
- Knowledge documentation progressing
- Elderly knowledge-holder's knowledge increasingly distributed

**Year 4:**
- Robust redundancy: 2-3 practitioners per critical skill
- Community capacity increasing
- Knowledge transmission becoming self-sustaining

## Step 5: Weekly and Monthly Schedules

### Sample Weekly Schedule: 150-Person Community

**Community context:** Agricultural, seasonal variation acceptable

#### Winter Schedule (Nov-Feb, less agricultural demand)

**Monday-Friday:**
- **8:00-12:00 — School (ages 5-10):**
  - Literacy/numeracy (1.5 hr)
  - Practical skills (1.5 hr)
  - Cultural/health (1 hr)
  - Teachers: 2 educators + rotating specialist

- **13:00-17:00 — Apprenticeship Track (ages 11-18):**
  - Food preservation specialist: 2-3 hours with master
  - Medicine specialist: 2-3 hours with master
  - Metalworking specialist: 3+ hours with master
  - Others: carpentry/construction project work or supporting practical core

- **14:00-17:00 — Specialized electives (adults interested):**
  - Leatherworking workshop (1-2 adults)
  - Advanced textile workshop (3-4 adults)
  - Governance discussion/decision-making (community leadership)

**Evening (2-3 hours):**
- Storytelling/oral history gathering (elder sharing traditional knowledge)
- Community discussion/celebration

#### Summer Schedule (May-Sept, high agricultural demand)

**Monday-Wednesday, Friday-Saturday:**
- **Morning — Core agricultural work (everyone able)**
- **Afternoon — Intensive apprenticeships (continues but shorter):**
  - Food preservation specialists participate in preservation work
  - Medicine specialists attend to health needs
  - Metalworking continues (less weather dependent)
  - Carpentry adapted to seasonal needs

**Thursday (Community Learning Day):**
- **8:00-12:00 — Intensive training workshop** (rotating topics):
  - Week 1: Advanced preservation
  - Week 2: Medicine and health
  - Week 3: Metalworking demonstration
  - Week 4: Textile production
  - All interested community members participate

**Evening:**
- Reduced formal education (more daylight flexibility)
- Informal skill-sharing and practice

### Monthly Curriculum Progression

**Month 1 (September):**
- **Apprentices:** Introduction to food preservation autumn techniques
- **School children:** Plant harvesting and food safety review
- **Community:** Preparation for preservation season

**Month 2 (October):**
- **Apprentices:** Active smoking and drying of autumn harvest
- **School children:** Observation of preservation work
- **Community:** Preservation workshops for home use

**Month 3 (November):**
- **Apprentices:** Fermentation setup for winter, storage preparation
- **School children:** Finished preservation review, storage management
- **Community:** Evaluation and planning for coming year

This repeats annually with seasonal adjustment.

:::tip
**Flexibility matters.** Seasonal variation is not weakness—it's appropriate to community reality. Winter's indoor time is ideal for concentrated training. Summer's outdoor time is ideal for productive work integrated with learning.
:::

## Specialized Curriculum Examples

### Food Preservation Specialist Curriculum (18-month arc)

**Phase 1 (Months 1-3): Observation & Theory**
- Learn properties of different foods
- Observe smoking process (meat, fish)
- Learn preservation preservation logic (kill bacteria, remove moisture, inhibit growth)
- Learn safety protocols
- Learn equipment use

**Phase 2 (Months 4-9): Assisted Practice**
- Smoke meat under supervision
- Dry vegetables/fruit under supervision
- Test fermentation under supervision
- Begin recognizing readiness (color, texture, smell indicators)

**Phase 3 (Months 10-15): Guided Independence**
- Execute preservation independently
- Master recognizes quality before community use
- Troubleshoot problems
- Teach younger apprentices

**Phase 4 (Months 16-18): Mastery & Teaching**
- Full responsibility for community preservation
- Teaching newer apprentices
- Scaling production
- Documenting procedures

### Medicine Specialist Curriculum (24-month arc)

**Phase 1 (Months 1-4): Foundation**
- Plant identification (medicinal species)
- Basic anatomy
- Common illnesses and their presentations
- Herbal remedy preparation basics
- Safety and toxicity

**Phase 2 (Months 5-12): Assisted Practice**
- Observe diagnosis of actual cases
- Prepare remedies under supervision
- Assist with treatments
- Document cases and outcomes

**Phase 3 (Months 13-20): Guided Independence**
- Make diagnostic recommendations (reviewed by master)
- Prepare remedies independently (reviewed)
- Suggest treatments (master approves)
- Handle minor illnesses independently

**Phase 4 (Months 21-24): Advanced & Teaching**
- Independent diagnosis and treatment
- Teaching newer apprentices
- Advanced cases (childbirth, complex illness)
- Documentation and knowledge refinement

## Curriculum Assessment & Adjustment

Good curriculum is living document, adjusted based on results.

### Annual Review Questions

- Are learners achieving expected competency levels? (Observation shows they can do what's taught)
- Are knowledge gaps appearing? (Gaps in community capability?)
- Are prerequisites in right order? (Is something taught before prerequisite is ready?)
- Are specialists being produced on schedule? (Is food preservation apprentice on track for journeyman status?)
- Are learners engaged? (Attendance, attention, enthusiasm—is something boring or confusing?)
- Are we teaching what's actually needed? (Community feedback: "We wish someone knew X")

### Adjustment Mechanisms

**If apprentices struggling:**
- Extend timeline (no shame in longer apprenticeship)
- Change teaching approach (demonstration vs. lecture)
- Reduce load (too many topics simultaneously)
- Check prerequisites (do they have foundational knowledge?)

**If specialized knowledge not advancing:**
- Increase time commitment (more hours/week)
- Provide resources (better tools, materials)
- Pair with better mentor (different master might teach better)
- Recognize this path takes years (patience)

**If knowledge gaps appearing:**
- Add new curriculum area
- Recruit new apprentices
- Cross-train existing practitioners

**If community needs change:**
- Adjust priorities (different skill becomes urgent)
- Pivot apprenticeships (adapt to new needs)
- Maintain flexibility (curriculum serves community, not vice versa)
