## Overview

Rope and cordage are among humanity's oldest and most essential technologies. Before synthetic fibers, all cordage came from plants—fibrous inner bark (bast fiber) from plants like flax, hemp, nettle, dogbane, and milkweed. Even with modern synthetic options, natural fiber cordage remains superior for many applications: it grips better when wet, has lower environmental impact, and can be produced entirely from foraged or cultivated plants.

Plant fiber cordage involves three stages: fiber extraction (retting and processing), twisting individual fibers into yarn (spinning), and plying multiple yarns together into rope. A person with these skills can produce fishing line, twine, rope, and webbing suitable for countless purposes.

This guide focuses on extraction, basic spinning, and rope-making techniques.

## Bast Fiber Extraction from Fiber Plants

Bast fibers are the long, strong fibers in the inner bark of plants.

### Fiber Plant Species and Yield

**Flax (Linum usitatissimum):**
- **Fiber quality:** Finest and most valuable; produces smooth, strong yarn.
- **Yield:** 1 pound of retted, dried fiber per 5–8 pounds of fresh plant (straw).
- **Growth:** Annual; matures in 90–120 days. Grown throughout temperate regions.
- **Best for:** Fine linen cloth, sailcloth, fishing twine.

**Hemp (Cannabis sativa — non-drug cultivar):**
- **Fiber quality:** Coarser than flax; very strong, slightly rough.
- **Yield:** 1 pound of retted fiber per 3–5 pounds of fresh plant.
- **Growth:** Annual; matures in 100–150 days. Tolerates poor soil; native to central Asia, now grown globally (some regions require permits).
- **Best for:** Heavy rope, canvas, work textiles, twine.

**Nettle (Urtica dioica):**
- **Fiber quality:** Fine and strong, similar to flax.
- **Yield:** 1 pound of dried fiber per 10–15 pounds of fresh plant.
- **Growth:** Perennial weed; abundant in temperate zones; foraged wild or cultivated.
- **Best for:** Fine twine, fishing line, fine textiles. Often overlooked but excellent quality.

**Dogbane (Apocynum spp.):**
- **Fiber quality:** Fine, strong, elastic.
- **Yield:** Moderate; 1 pound of dried fiber per 8–12 pounds of plant.
- **Growth:** Perennial; wild North American plant, underutilized.
- **Best for:** Bowstring, fishing line, fine twine. Historical use by Native Americans.

**Milkweed (Asclepias spp.):**
- **Fiber quality:** Fine but shorter than flax; weaker individually but produces good cordage.
- **Yield:** 1 pound dried fiber per 8–10 pounds of plant.
- **Growth:** Perennial; wild plant across North America; abundant in late summer.
- **Best for:** Cordage, twine, emergency rope (foraged).

### Retting Process

Retting is soaking harvested plant stems to decompose the pectin that binds the bark to the woody core, allowing easier separation of the bast fiber.

**Harvesting:**

1. Cut plant stems near the ground when the plant has finished flowering (typically late summer to early fall).
2. Bundle stems together and hang to dry for 1–2 weeks in dry conditions.
3. Dried stems are easier to transport and store; retting occurs whether stems are fresh or dried, though fresh is faster.

**Water retting (most common):**

1. Submerge bundles of dried stems in clean water (a pond, stream, or large tub).
2. Weight them down with rocks or boards so they stay fully submerged; exposure to air promotes mold that weakens the fiber.
3. Retting time varies by temperature:
   - **Cold water (50–60°F):** 4–6 weeks
   - **Warm water (70°F+):** 2–3 weeks
   - **Very warm water (80°F+):** 1–2 weeks

4. **Check progress:** After 2 weeks, remove a stem and flex it gently. If the bark strips easily from the woody core, retting is complete.

5. **Drain and dry:** Remove bundles from water and spread on dry ground or grass in the sun for 2–4 days. The fiber is now separated from the woody core.

**Dew retting (field retting):**

1. Lay harvested and dried stems on the ground in a thin layer.
2. Leave exposed to rain and dew for 2–4 weeks.
3. Slower than water retting but requires no standing water.
4. Good for large quantities where space is available.

:::info-box
Retting water develops a distinctive smell—neither truly pleasant nor foul, but earthy and organic. This smell indicates successful microbial decomposition and is normal.
:::

### Breaking and Scutching

After retting, the dried stalks contain separated bast fibers still attached to the dried woody core. Breaking and scutching remove the woody material.

**Breaking:**

1. Lay dried, retted stems on a hard surface.
2. Using a wooden mallet or by striking with the flat side of a blade, break the stalks into short segments (6–12 inches).
3. Continue striking until the woody interior (shives) fractures and loosens from the fibers.

**Scutching (beating):**

1. Use a scutching tool (a flat wooden blade set in a frame) to beat the broken stalk pieces against a post or wooden block.
2. With each strike, more woody debris falls away and the fibers become more separated.
3. Repeat until the bundle is predominantly fiber with minimal woody debris remaining.

**Alternative (hand method):**

1. Hold a retted stalk and strike it repeatedly against the edge of a wooden or stone block, rotating as you strike.
2. This separates the fibers manually and is suitable for small quantities or demonstration purposes.

### Hackling (Fine Combing)

Hackling aligns the fibers and removes remaining short fibers and debris (called tow).

1. **Hackle tool:** A board with many long, fine teeth set in rows (similar to a coarse comb).
2. **Process:** Pull the bundle of fibers through the hackle teeth repeatedly, working from one end toward the other.
3. **Multiple passes:** Use progressively finer hackles to separate fibers into grades:
   - **Line fibers:** Long, fine fibers (used for spinning fine yarn/twine).
   - **Tow:** Short, coarse fibers (used for cordage or filling applications).

4. **Comb alignment:** After hackling, the fiber lies parallel and can be spun smoothly.

**Yield:** After retting, breaking, scutching, and hackling, final yield is approximately 30–50% of the original retted weight (depending on plant species and skill).

## Basic Fiber Spinning for Cordage

Unlike the detailed spinning techniques in the Spinning guide, cordage production often uses simpler, faster methods.

### Hand Spinning (Two-Hand Method)

The simplest method requires no tools.

1. **Prepare fiber:** Create a bundle about 1/4 inch in diameter and 12 inches long from hacklled, aligned fiber.
2. **Pinch and twist:** Pinch the fiber between the thumb and forefinger of one hand, drawing it out from the bundle while simultaneously twisting it clockwise with the other hand.
3. **Maintain tension:** The more tension you apply, the finer the yarn; less tension creates thicker, looser yarn.
4. **Continuous feed:** As the yarn builds up, gradually pinch the next section of fiber and feed it into the twist.
5. **Production rate:** A practiced hand spinner can produce 50–100 feet of cordage per hour by this method.

### Drop Spindle Spinning

Using a drop spindle (see Spinning guide) is slower but produces more uniform yarn.

1. Prepare fiber as above.
2. Spin using the drop spindle technique (spin downward, drafting fiber from the bundle).
3. Finer control of twist and thickness than hand spinning.
4. Production rate: 20–50 feet per hour depending on fineness.

### Wheel Spinning

A spinning wheel (foot-powered or hand-crank) accelerates production significantly.

1. Set up the wheel with the flyer and bobbin as described in the Spinning guide.
2. Feed the fiber through the orifice and allow the wheel's rotation to twist it.
3. Production rate: 100–200 feet per hour depending on fineness.

### Twist Direction and Single vs. Plied

For cordage, most producers use Z-twist singles (individual spun yarn), which are then plied together (twisted in the opposite direction) to lock and strengthen.

- **Single yarn:** Z-twist, about 6–8 twists per inch.
- **2-ply cordage:** Two Z-twist singles twisted together in S-direction.
- **3-ply cordage:** Three Z-twist singles twisted together in S-direction.

## Reverse-Wrap Two-Ply Technique

The reverse-wrap method is a traditional way to ply two singles into cordage by hand without needing a spinning wheel.

### Setup

1. **Prepare two singles:** Spin two lengths of single yarn to the desired thickness and length (at least 20–30 feet each to test the method).
2. **Anchor one end:** Secure one end of both singles to a fixed point (a fence post, tree branch, or vise clamp).
3. **Create slight tension:** The two singles should be slightly taut but not under high tension.

### Method

1. **Separate the singles:** Hold both singles loosely in one hand.
2. **Reverse wrap:** With the other hand, grab the two singles together and push your hand along their length, rotating your wrist clockwise (this twists the two singles together in the opposite direction of their individual twists).
3. **Maintain tension:** As you advance along the yarn, the S-twist (plying direction) tightens the two Z-twist singles together, creating a balanced 2-ply cordage.
4. **Continuous motion:** The key is maintaining consistent tension and rotation speed. Uneven motion creates lumpy cordage.

### Scaling Up

For longer cordage, the reverse-wrap can be scaled:

1. **Two-person method:** One person holds the anchored end; the other walks backward while twisting, creating longer 2-ply.
2. **Walking method:** Anchor the secured end to a fixed point. Walk backward while twisting, letting the cordage wind around your hand or onto a bobbin as it forms.

### Three-Strand Lay

Three-strand rope is stronger and more professional-looking than 2-ply.

1. **Prepare three singles:** Spin three separate lengths of yarn.
2. **Anchor at one end:** Secure all three to a fixed point.
3. **Braid-twist method:** Instead of plying two yarns, you'll twist three. This is more complex:
   - Over one, under one, over one, repeat.
   - As you advance, the three strands interlock in a triangular cross-section.
   - The result is a traditional round rope.

**Alternative (two-person method):**

1. Secure three singles to a fixed point.
2. Two people walk apart, creating tension.
3. A third person (or assistant) twists the three strands together as one pair walks backward and the other holds steady.
4. The tensioned strands lay together in a spiral, creating rope.

:::tip
Three-strand rope is the traditional method taught to sailors and riggers. It's strong, flexible, and can be easily spliced (joined end-to-end or attached to rings and loops).
:::

## Fiber Yield by Plant Species and Strength Testing

Different plants produce cordage with different properties.

### Fiber Yield Comparison

| Plant | Retted Fiber per 1 lb Fresh Stem | Final Cordage Yield (after all processing) | Best Uses |
|---|---|---|---|
| Flax | 0.12–0.25 lb | 0.04–0.10 lb cordage | Fine twine, fishing line |
| Hemp | 0.20–0.30 lb | 0.06–0.15 lb cordage | Heavy rope, twine, webbing |
| Nettle | 0.06–0.10 lb | 0.02–0.05 lb cordage | Fine twine, fishing line |
| Dogbane | 0.08–0.15 lb | 0.03–0.06 lb cordage | Fine cordage, bowstring |
| Milkweed | 0.10–0.15 lb | 0.03–0.07 lb cordage | Utility cordage, emergency rope |

**Planning:** If you want to produce 1 pound of finished cordage, you need approximately 10–20 pounds of fresh plant material, depending on species and processing efficiency.

### Strength Testing

Testing cordage strength requires a tensile load device. Improvised methods:

**Water bucket test:**

1. Tie one end of cordage to a fixed point.
2. Attach a bucket to the other end.
3. Gradually add water to the bucket until the cordage breaks.
4. Weigh the bucket + water. This approximates the breaking strength.

**Comparison:**

| Cordage Type | Breaking Strength |
|---|---|
| Single-ply flax twine (1/8") | 5–10 lbs |
| 2-ply hemp (1/4") | 50–100 lbs |
| 3-ply hemp (1/2") | 300–600 lbs |
| Commercial polypropylene rope (1/2") | 400–800 lbs |

Natural fiber cordage is competitive with synthetic rope in strength and has superior properties when wet (synthetic rope becomes slippery when wet; natural fiber grips better).

## Environmental Conditions for Fiber Processing

Retting and fiber processing are sensitive to temperature, moisture, and other environmental factors. Optimizing these conditions dramatically improves fiber quality and yield.

### Retting Conditions by Fiber Type

Retting success depends on temperature, moisture, and time. Environmental conditions determine decomposition speed and fiber quality.

#### Temperature Effects on Retting

**Cold water retting (50-60°F / 10-15°C):**
- Retting time: 4-6 weeks
- Best for: Flax and hemp (produces superior fiber quality)
- Fiber quality: Fine, strong, minimal damage to cellulose
- Disadvantage: Very slow; requires patience
- Typical use: Historical methods in temperate climates

**Warm water retting (70°F / 21°C):**
- Retting time: 2-3 weeks
- Best for: Most fibers (hemp, flax, nettle, dogbane, milkweed)
- Fiber quality: Good; balanced between speed and quality
- Efficiency: Optimal microbial decomposition rate
- Typical use: Spring/fall when ambient temperature is moderate

**Very warm water retting (80°F+ / 27°C+):**
- Retting time: 1-2 weeks
- Best for: Milkweed and nettle (more tolerant of heat degradation)
- Fiber quality: Adequate but slightly weaker than cool-water retting
- Risk: Excessively warm water can degrade cellulose and weaken fiber
- Typical use: Tropical climates or summer retting

**Frozen water retting (below 50°F / 10°C):**
- Retting time: 8-12 weeks (very slow)
- Mechanism: Freeze-thaw cycles rather than microbial action break pectin bonds
- Fiber quality: Good for some species; slower but chemical degradation is minimal
- Application: "Dew retting" in cold regions where water isn't available

:::tip
**Optimal temperature window:** 70-75°F (21-24°C) provides fastest retting without fiber degradation. If available, heat water bath using solar collectors or heated water to achieve optimal temperature.
:::

#### Moisture & Oxygen Requirements

**Submersion depth:**
- Fully submerged: Best for water retting (anaerobic decomposition, prevents mold)
- Partially submerged: Risk of mold and uneven retting
- Exposed (dew retting): Requires regular rain/dew for continued microbial activity

**Water quality:**
- Clean water (stream, pond): Ideal, contains naturally occurring retting bacteria
- Stagnant water: Contains algae and retting bacteria; works but may impart odor
- Chlorinated water: Chlorine inhibits retting bacteria; allow to dechlorinate 2-3 days before use
- Hard water (high minerals): Acceptable; doesn't significantly inhibit retting
- Polluted water: Unknown effects; use only clean water if possible

**Oxygen levels:**
- Anaerobic (no oxygen): Slightly slower retting but produces finer fiber and minimal odor
- Aerobic (with oxygen): Faster retting but risk of mold growth on exposed surfaces
- Weighted bundles stay submerged and anaerobic; exposed bundles become aerobic and moldy

### Fiber-Specific Retting Conditions Table

| Fiber | Water Temp | Duration | Moisture Level | Yield | Quality Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Flax | 50-70°F | 4-6 weeks | Fully submerged | 25% of fresh | Finest quality; patience needed |
| Hemp | 65-75°F | 2-3 weeks | Fully submerged | 30% of fresh | Coarse but very strong |
| Nettle | 70-75°F | 2-3 weeks | Fully submerged | 10% of fresh | Fine like flax; overlooked source |
| Dogbane | 70-75°F | 2-3 weeks | Fully submerged | 15% of fresh | Elastic and strong |
| Milkweed | 75-80°F | 1-2 weeks | Fully submerged | 15% of fresh | Tolerates warm water; slightly weaker |
| Bark fibers | 65-75°F | 1-2 weeks | Dew or warm water | 20-30% | Quick processing; good for cordage |

### Seasonal Harvesting Windows by Climate Zone

Different climates have optimal harvesting periods.

**Temperate climates (40-50°N latitude):**
- **Spring harvest (May-June):** Plant peaks flowering; ideal for flax and nettle
- **Fall harvest (August-September):** Plant matures; yields peak for hemp and dogbane
- **Winter retting (December-February):** Cold water retting; very slow but produces finest fiber
- **Optimal:** Fall harvest with spring/autumn warm-water retting

**Subtropical climates (25-40°N):**
- **Spring (March-May):** Primary growing season; reliable moisture and moderate temperature
- **Fall (September-October):** Secondary harvest; competing with crop season
- **Summer (June-August):** Very warm; fast retting but risk of fiber degradation
- **Optimal:** Spring harvest with immediate warm-water retting (2-3 weeks)

**Tropical climates (0-25°N):**
- **Dry season (November-March):** Lower humidity; fiber drying easier
- **Wet season (May-September):** High humidity; retting bacteria very active
- **Year-round retting possible:** Moisture and warmth always available
- **Optimal:** Harvest in transition to dry season (October) with warm-water retting starting November

**Cold climates (>50°N):**
- **Short growing season (June-August):** Only viable harvest window
- **Long cold retting (October-May):** Beneficial; cold water retting produces superior fiber
- **Winter (December-February):** Dew retting possible; freeze-thaw cycles break pectin
- **Optimal:** July harvest with fall/early winter cool-water or dew retting

### Weather Effects on Fiber Quality

**Rain during retting:**
- Heavy rain: Beneficial during dew retting; provides moisture and fresh microbes
- Light rain: Maintains moisture; beneficial
- None: Dew retting slows significantly

**Humidity during drying:**
- High humidity (>70%): Slows drying; risk of mold growth
- Moderate humidity (50-70%): Ideal drying condition; prevents brittleness
- Low humidity (<50%): Fast drying; fiber may become brittle

**UV exposure:**
- Full sun: Accelerates drying; slight yellowing but acceptable
- Shade drying: Slower but color preservation; better for fine fibers
- Indoor drying: Slowest; prevents UV damage; requires ventilation

### Testing for Retting Completion

**Visual indicators:**
- Bark separates easily from woody core when flexed
- Core becomes brittle and breaks when bent sharply
- Fibers release easily when twisted

**Field test procedure:**
1. Remove a sample stem from the retting bath
2. Flex it gently between two hands
3. If bark stays firmly attached, retting incomplete; return to water
4. If bark strips away easily and fibers separate, retting complete
5. If core crumbles easily, retting is complete (possibly slightly over-retting is OK)

### Over-Retting Consequences

Excessive retting (beyond fiber separation completion) degrades cellulose:
- Fiber becomes weaker and brittle
- Elasticity decreases; cordage is less flexible
- Yield decreases (fiber breaks during processing)
- Color darkens (lignin breakdown)
- Odor intensifies

**Prevention:** Check daily after week 2; remove bundles as soon as bark separates easily.

## Applications and Uses

Natural fiber cordage serves countless purposes.

### Fishing Line

**Production:**
1. Produce single-ply yarn from fine flax or nettle (approximately 0.5–1 mm diameter).
2. Soak in water to pre-saturate before use (reduces swelling as it absorbs water while fishing).
3. Test for strength before trusting with large catches.

**Properties:**
- Stronger when wet than synthetic line.
- Lower visibility in water (less transparent).
- Biodegradable and safe for aquatic environments.

### Twine and Binding Cordage

**Production:**
1. 2-ply cordage (1/8–1/4 inch diameter) from hemp, flax, or nettle.
2. Suitable for tying bundles, garden twine, binding hay, wrapping parcels.

**Advantages:**
- Economical (can be locally produced).
- Biodegradable; suitable for compostable applications.
- Good grip when wet.

### Rope and Heavy Cordage

**Production:**
1. 3-strand or multi-ply rope (1/2–1 inch diameter) from hemp or dogbane.
2. Used for hauling, securing loads, anchor lines, rigging.

**Splicing:**
- 3-strand rope can be spliced (the ends of two ropes woven together) to join them end-to-end.
- Splicing is stronger than tying a knot and leaves a smooth joint suitable for running through pulleys.

### Webbing and Fabric

**Production:**
1. Spin cordage from hemp or flax to a medium fineness (0.25–0.5 mm diameter).
2. Weave on a simple loom into webbing (narrow cloth) for belts, straps, or bands.
3. Weave into broader cloth for canvas, sailcloth, or sacking (rough cloth for storage sacks).
