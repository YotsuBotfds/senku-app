## Overview

Pollinators—bees, butterflies, moths, beetles, hummingbirds, and other animals—are essential for plant reproduction and food production. Approximately 75% of global food crops depend partly or wholly on pollinators. Pollinator populations are declining due to habitat loss, pesticide use, disease, and climate change. Creating pollinator habitat, growing native plants, minimizing pesticides, and managing existing spaces for pollinator health are critical conservation actions available to individuals and communities. This guide covers pollinator biology, habitat creation, native plant selection, nesting sites, and monitoring.

<section id="common-pollinators">

## Common Pollinators and Their Needs

### Bees (Most Economically Important)

**Honeybees (Apis mellifera):**
- Managed by beekeepers in hives.
- Generalist foragers; visit wide range of flowers.
- Colony size: 40,000–60,000 individuals per hive.
- Live 6 weeks (summer workers), several months (winter).
- Sting only when threatened; generally docile.
- Habitat: Floral resources (pollen, nectar); shelter from extreme weather; water.

**Bumblebees (Bombus species):**
- Robust, fuzzy bees; more cold-hardy than honeybees.
- Smaller colonies (50–400 individuals) than honeybees.
- Forage early spring and late fall when other bees are less active.
- Can manipulate flower temperature (wing vibration) to reach pollen in complex flowers.
- Habitat: Early spring forage (small herbaceous plants, flowering trees); summer flowers; nesting sites (abandoned rodent burrows, tall grass, bee hotels).

**Carpenter bees (Xylocopa species):**
- Large, mostly solitary bees; female digs tunnel in dead wood for nesting.
- Males aggressively defend territory but cannot sting.
- Early season pollinators (spring).
- Habitat: Dead standing wood (snags, dead trees, untreated wood for nesting); spring flowers.

**Sweat bees (Halictus, Augochlora species):**
- Small bees; attracted to human perspiration (hence name).
- Solitary or weakly social; nest in bare ground.
- Generalist foragers; visit variety of flowers.
- Habitat: Bare ground for nesting; varied flowering plants.

**Mining bees (Andrena species):**
- Solitary bees; nest in bare ground or sandy soil.
- Early spring pollinators; emerging as soon as first flowers bloom.
- Habitat: Bare ground for nesting; spring flowers (fruit trees, early wildflowers).

### Butterflies and Moths

**Butterflies:**
- Adult diet: Nectar and mineral water.
- Larval diet (caterpillars): Specific host plants (monarch caterpillars eat milkweed only; swallowtail caterpillars eat citrus, parsnip, fennel).
- Habitat: Diverse flowering plants (nectar sources); specific host plants for caterpillars; shelter (trees, dense shrubs).
- Lifespan: 2–6 weeks (adults); several months (some overwintering species).

**Moths:**
- Often nocturnal pollinators; frequently overlooked.
- Adult diet: Nectar, plant sap, fruit.
- Larval diet: Leaves of various plants (host plants vary widely).
- Habitat: Night-blooming flowers; host plants; shelter (dense vegetation, bark, dead wood).
- Lifespan: Few weeks to several months.

### Other Pollinators

**Beetles:**
- Important pollinators in some regions (especially tropical).
- Adult diet: Pollen, nectar, flowers themselves.
- Larval diet: Depends on species (wood, leaf litter, dung, plants).
- Habitat: Flowers; dead wood; decaying vegetation.

**Hummingbirds (Neotropics, North America):**
- Highly specialized for feeding on nectar.
- Require high-energy diet; visit hundreds of flowers daily.
- Territory-defending; often aggressive to other hummingbirds.
- Habitat: Tubular, bright-colored flowers (red, orange); flowering trees and shrubs.

**Wasps, flies, and others:**
- Various small insects also pollinate (though not primary pollinators).
- Often overlooked but collectively important.

<section id="pollinator-habitat">

## Creating Pollinator Habitat

Pollinator habitat consists of food sources (pollen and nectar), nesting sites, shelter from weather and predators, and water. For apiary-adjacent plantings, keep blooms staggered so forage stays available from early spring through frost; that landscape support pairs with the more hive-specific seasonal care in [Beekeeping](beekeeping.html).

### Floral Resources (Food)

**Blooming calendar:**

Continuous flowers from early spring to late fall/frost ensure food availability throughout the growing season.

**Spring (March–May):**
- Trees: Fruit trees (apple, pear, cherry, plum), maple, ash, willow, alder.
- Shrubs: Serviceberry, viburnum, currant, gooseberry.
- Herbaceous plants: Crocus, hellebore, lungwort, hepatica, trillium, bleeding heart.

**Early summer (June–July):**
- Trees: Linden, locust.
- Shrubs: Elderberry, spirea.
- Herbaceous plants: Alliums, coneflower, yarrow, phlox, salvia, catmint, liatris, strawberry.

**Mid-late summer (July–August):**
- Herbaceous plants: Coneflower (continuing), bee balm, bergamot, blazing star, sunflower, cosmos, dahlias.

**Fall (August–October):**
- Herbaceous plants: Aster, goldenrod, sedum, Physostegia.
- Plants producing seed heads (milkweed, teasel, thistle): Seeds feed birds; flowers feed late-season pollinators.

**Winter (November–March, in temperate regions):**
- Early-blooming shrubs and trees (as listed under spring).
- Evergreen herbaceous plants providing shelter.

### Flowering Characteristics Important to Pollinators

**Flower structure:**
- **Open flowers (cup-shaped):** Easily accessible to many pollinators (cosmos, dahlias, zinnia).
- **Clustered small flowers (umbel, compound):** Good for small insects (yarrow, alliums, parsley).
- **Tubular flowers:** Attract long-tongued bees and hummingbirds (salvia, bergamot, digitalis).
- **Complex flowers:** Reward specialized pollinators (orchid, iris); some bumblebees can "buzz pollinate" to shake pollen free.

**Flower color:**
- **Blue, purple, violet:** Strong attractant for many bees.
- **Yellow, orange:** Attractive to most bees and some butterflies.
- **Red:** Attractive to hummingbirds; many bees cannot see red.
- **White, pink:** Attractive to butterflies and some bees.
- **Patterns and lines:** Guide pollinators to nectar (often invisible to humans but visible in UV).

**Pollen and nectar abundance:**
- Plants vary in pollen and nectar quality and quantity.
- High-pollen plants: Roses, dahlias, sunflowers, borage, phacelia.
- High-nectar plants: Bergamot, bee balm, liatris, coneflower, sage, lavender.

### Plant Selection Strategy

1. **Choose at least 3–5 plant species blooming in each season** (spring, early summer, mid-summer, late summer, fall).
2. **Favor native species** (see next section).
3. **Include plants with various flower structures and colors.**
4. **Focus on abundance:** Plant in clusters (3–5 plants of same species) for higher visibility and efficiency.
5. **Include plants that produce seeds** (for seed-eating birds, winter shelter).

<section id="native-plant-selection">

## Native Plant Selection for Pollinators

Native plants have coevolved with local pollinators, often matching their life cycles and preferences better than non-native ornamentals.

### Benefits of Native Plants

**Pollinator compatibility:**
- Native bees and butterflies evolved with native plants; flowers match pollinator mouthparts and behavior.
- Timing aligns: Pollinators emerge when native plants flower.

**Reduced pest and disease:**
- Native plants adapted to local climate; fewer pest and disease problems.
- Reduced need for pesticides.

**Ecological support:**
- Native plants support native insects, birds, and other wildlife.
- Food web connections are intact.

**Low maintenance:**
- Adapted to local climate; less water, fertilizer, and care needed.
- Can establish deep root systems suited to local soil and water conditions.

### Identifying Native Plants

**Resources:**
- **Local native plant societies:** Maintain lists of native plants for your region.
- **State/provincial natural resource agencies:** Often publish native plant guides.
- **Universities or botanical gardens:** May have native plant nurseries or guides.
- **Field guides:** Regional wildflower guides identify native species.

**Definition of native:**
- Typically, "native" means plants that naturally occurred in the region before European settlement (in North America) or before significant human disturbance.
- Local/regional native plants are better than distant populations of the same species (local adaptation).

### Regional Examples (Temperate North America)

**Eastern deciduous forest region:**
- Spring: Bloodroot, hepatica, trillium, wild columbine, serviceberry.
- Summer: Bergamot (bee balm), black-eyed Susan, coneflower, milkweed, phlox.
- Fall: New England aster, ironweed, Joe-Pye weed, goldenrod.

**Tallgrass prairie region:**
- Spring: Pasque flower, prairie violet.
- Summer: Coreopsis, milkweed, purple coneflower, black-eyed Susan, blazing star (liatris), bee balm.
- Fall: Aster, goldenrod, blazing star (continuing).

**Western mountain region:**
- Spring: Wildflower mix (lupine, balsamroot, phlox, depending on elevation).
- Summer: Fireweed, Indian paintbrush, sage, sunflower varieties.
- Fall: Aster, goldenrod.

**Coastal/maritime regions:**
- Salt-tolerant natives vary by region.
- Consult local native plant guides for accurate selections.

### Preparing Habitat for Native Plants

**Soil preparation:**
- Remove invasive plants (pull, dig, solarize in clear plastic for 4–6 weeks, or smother with cardboard and mulch).
- Loosen soil if compacted.
- Add compost if soil is poor.
- Avoid adding excessive fertilizer (promotes weedy growth over native establishment).

**Planting:**
- Spring or fall planting is generally best (less water stress).
- Transplants are easier than seeds for many species.
- Spacing: Follow species recommendations; plants should not touch at maturity (room for growth).

**Mulching:**
- 1–2 inches of mulch suppresses weeds and retains moisture.
- Keep mulch away from plant stems (prevents rot).
- Avoid thick layers that compact and prevent water infiltration.

**Watering:**
- Water regularly (daily or every 2–3 days) for first month after planting until established.
- Gradually reduce watering as plants establish.
- Native plants are adapted to local rainfall; supplemental water usually unnecessary once established.

<section id="nesting-sites">

## Nesting Sites and Shelter

Pollinators need safe places to lay eggs and overwinter. Creating nesting habitat is essential, especially for solitary bees.

### Bee Hotels and Nesting Structures

**Hollow stem/tube structures:**
- Bumblebees, carpenter bees, and many solitary bees nest in hollow stems, wood tunnels, or reeds.
- **DIY bee hotel:**
  1. Drill holes (3–10 mm diameter) in untreated wood block or bundle reeds/bamboo.
  2. Mount on pole or tree at least 3 feet high, facing south-southeast (morning sun).
  3. Add roof (overhang) to protect from rain.
  4. Leave in place year-round (bees emerge in spring).
  5. Clean or replace tubes annually (late fall or spring) to remove parasites and mold.

**Mason bee tubes:**
- Solitary mason bees (Osmia) nest in small tubes (8–12 mm diameter).
- Tubes can be purchased or made from paper/bamboo.
- Similar placement as bee hotels.

**Bumblebee houses:**
- Underground or sheltered structures filled with dry straw or moss.
- Entrance hole at ground level or side.
- Often commercially available; DIY versions challenging.

**Carpenter bee habitat:**
- Dead trees (snags) left standing (if safe; trim branches overhead).
- Untreated wood blocks (protect from weather; replace annually as tunnel can become degraded).

### Ground-Nesting Habitat

Many solitary bees nest in bare ground or sandy soil. Creating bare-ground nesting areas:
1. Clear 2–4 sq. ft. patch of vegetation and mulch.
2. Loosen soil 6–12 inches deep.
3. Ideally sandy soil (better than clay).
4. Keep clear of new vegetation growth.
5. Leave in place year-round (bees emerge in spring).

**Ideal characteristics:**
- South or southeast-facing (warmth).
- Well-drained (no standing water; waterlogged soil kills nests).
- Protected from heavy disturbance.
- Near flowers (within 50–100 feet).

### Shelter from Weather

**Dense shrubs and trees:**
- Provide roosting and shelter for butterflies, moths, and some bees.
- Evergreens offer winter shelter; deciduous plants provide summer shade.

**Dead wood and leaf litter:**
- Shelter for overwintering insects.
- Habitat for decomposers and beneficial predators.
- Leave some areas unmulched; maintain fallen logs and leaf litter.

**Tall grasses and seed heads:**
- Shelter for insects and birds.
- Do not cut back until spring (allows insects to overwinter).

## Pesticide Alternatives and Integrated Pest Management

Pesticides, especially insecticides and neonicotinoids, directly kill pollinators or reduce their populations. Minimizing chemical inputs protects pollinators while reducing other environmental impacts.

### Integrated Pest Management (IPM)

IPM uses a combination of techniques to minimize pest problems while reducing chemical use:

1. **Cultural practices:**
   - Plant diverse varieties (pests target specific cultivars; diversity reduces total damage).
   - Encourage predators (birds, spiders, beneficial insects).
   - Remove infested plants or prune affected branches (limits pest spread).
   - Rotate crops (breaks pest life cycles).

2. **Biological controls:**
   - Release beneficial insects (ladybugs, parasitic wasps) that prey on pests.
   - Encourage native predators through habitat (shelter, water, diverse plants).
   - Use bt (Bacillus thuringiensis), a bacterial insecticide safe to pollinators.

3. **Physical controls:**
   - Hand-pick large insects (caterpillars, beetles).
   - Prune infested branches.
   - Exclude pests with row covers (fine mesh cloth over young plants; remove when flowers bloom to allow pollinators).
   - Water spray (strong spray of water knocks off soft-bodied insects; does not harm plants or pollinators).

4. **Chemical controls (last resort):**
   - Neem oil, insecticidal soap, sulfur: Lower toxicity; less harmful to pollinators if applied in evening after bees stop foraging.
   - Neonicotinoids, broad-spectrum insecticides: Highly toxic to bees; avoid entirely.
   - Always read labels; avoid products toxic to bees or butterflies.

### Safe Timing for Any Chemical Application

If any pesticide is necessary:
- Apply in early morning or late evening (after bees stop foraging, before sunrise).
- Never spray flowering plants when in bloom (do not spray flowers; spray leaves only, away from open blooms).
- Avoid drift to nearby flowering plants.
- Choose products with short residual time (break down quickly).

### Herbicide Caution

Herbicides kill plants that provide pollinator food and shelter. Minimize herbicide use:
- Manually pull weeds.
- Use mulch to suppress weeds.
- Accept some "weeds" (many are native wildflowers supporting pollinators).

## Seasonal Flowering Succession

Planning continuous blooms from spring through fall ensures pollinators have food throughout their active season.

### Spring (April–May, temperate zone)

**Fruit trees and shrubs:**
- Apple, pear, cherry, plum trees (excellent early forage).
- Serviceberry, currant, gooseberry, viburnum.

**Herbaceous perennials:**
- Hellebore, hepatica, trillium, wild columbine, bleeding heart, phlox, pulmonaria.

**Native wildflowers:**
- Bloodroot, trout lily, Jack-in-the-pulpit, Virginia bluebells, ramps.

### Early Summer (June–July)

**Shrubs:**
- Elderberry, spirea, roses.

**Herbaceous perennials:**
- Alliums, coreopsis, peonies, phlox, salvia, catmint, liatris, lavender, yarrow, bee balm.

**Native plants:**
- Black-eyed Susan, coneflower, bergamot, wild strawberry.

### Mid-Late Summer (July–August)

**Herbaceous perennials (continuing):**
- Bee balm, bergamot, blazing star, coneflower, yarrow, phlox.

**Annuals (if grown):**
- Sunflower, cosmos, zinnias, borage, phacelia.

**Native plants:**
- Milkweed varieties, ironweed, Joe-Pye weed.

### Fall (September–October)

**Perennials:**
- Aster, sedum, goldenrod, ironweed, Joe-Pye weed.

**Native plants:**
- Late-blooming asters, blazing star, goldenrod.

**Seed-producing plants:**
- Allow seed heads to persist (milkweed, teasel, thistle, coneflower, black-eyed Susan, sunflower).
- Seeds feed birds and provide insects (inside seeds) for bird food.

### Winter (November–March, temperate)

Minimal blooms; focus on shelter (evergreens, dense shrubs, dead wood) and seed resources.

## Bee Health Monitoring

Regular observation of pollinators reveals population trends and health issues.

### Observation Techniques

**Timed observation (20–30 minutes):**
1. Sit in one spot with good view of flowering plants.
2. Record all pollinators observed: bee species (if identifiable), count, behavior.
3. Note weather (temperature, wind, cloud cover; affects pollinator activity).

**Netting and observation (optional):**
1. Use insect net to gently catch bees/butterflies.
2. Observe briefly for parasites, disease signs, or identification.
3. Release unharmed.

**Pan traps (passive monitoring):**
1. Fill shallow pans or bowls (8–10 inches diameter) with water and a drop of dish soap.
2. Use different colors: Blue, yellow, white; pollinators are attracted and fall in.
3. Check daily; count, identify, and record.

### Health Indicators

**Healthy populations:**
- Regular observation of multiple bee species.
- Variety of body sizes and species.
- Active foraging behavior.
- Successful nest construction (in bee hotels, tubes, or ground).

**Population decline indicators:**
- Fewer bees observed despite good weather and blooming plants.
- Single species dominating (possible disease in other species).
- Dead or dying bees around nest sites.
- Abandoned nests.

**Disease signs (if examined closely):**
- Visible parasites (mites, flies).
- Discoloration, missing parts, deformities.
- Inability to fly or forage.

### Recording Data

Keep simple records:
- Date, time, location, weather conditions.
- Species observed (common names sufficient).
- Count or abundance estimate.
- Any observed problems.

Over time, records reveal trends: populations increasing, stable, or declining; seasonal changes; response to management changes.

## Simple Native Bee Management (Not Beekeeping)

Unlike honeybees (managed in hives), most native bees are solitary or weakly social. Supporting them requires habitat rather than active management.

### Bumblebee Encouragement

- Provide early-spring flowers (helps queens emerging from hibernation).
- Create nesting habitat (wild areas, unmown grass, protected nesting boxes).
- Avoid pesticides (colony-building is slow; pesticide kills can devastate populations).
- Protect nests (mark to avoid disturbance).

### Carpenter Bee Coexistence

- Tolerate nesting tunnels in wood (they are not aggressive; males cannot sting).
- Provide dead wood or untreated wood blocks for nesting.
- Accept minimal damage (bees are solitary; tunnels are small).
- Leave nests in place over winter (bees are inside).

### Solitary Bee Support

- Provide ground or stem nesting habitat (as described above).
- Maintain flowering plants year-round.
- Minimize soil disturbance during nesting season (spring; avoid digging, walking on bare-ground nesting areas).

## Community-Level Pollinator Conservation

Individual conservation is important; community efforts multiply the impact.

### Community Pollinator Habitat Network

**Concept:** Connected patches of pollinator habitat allow bees, butterflies, and other pollinators to move across landscapes, sustaining populations.

**Implementation:**
1. Identify community members willing to create or enhance pollinator habitat.
2. Map existing and planned habitat patches.
3. Identify corridors (greenways, riparian areas) connecting patches.
4. Coordinate plantings (sharing native plant sources, group purchasing).
5. Share monitoring data and observations.

### Collective Action

- **Seed exchanges:** Share native plant seeds within community.
- **Native plant nurseries:** Community-run small nurseries grow and share native plants.
- **Pollinator monitoring:** Coordinated observation across multiple sites reveals landscape-level trends.
- **Pesticide reduction:** Community commitment to reducing pesticide use on public and private lands.
- **Education:** School or community workshops on pollinator ecology and conservation.
