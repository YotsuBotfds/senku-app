## Overview: Medication Management in Survival Medicine

![Essential Medications Reference Guide](../assets/svgs/medications-5.svg)

![Medication Dosing and Administration Protocols](../assets/svgs/medications-6.svg)

### What This Guide Covers

Essential Medications is a comprehensive reference for drug selection, dosing, administration, and safe handling in resource-limited settings. This guide addresses the reality of post-collapse medicine: modern pharmaceutical supply chains collapse, requiring practitioners to work with whatever medications remain available in salvaged stockpiles, household medicine cabinets, veterinary suppliers, and community reserves. Understanding medication fundamentals—identification, potency assessment, appropriate dosing, contraindications, and dangerous interactions—separates effective treatment from harmful errors.

### Prevalence and Clinical Significance

In collapse scenarios, medication shortages follow predictable patterns: antibiotics become scarce within weeks as infections surge; analgesics deplete rapidly; psychiatric medications disappear as supplies exhaust; biologics requiring refrigeration spoil. Simultaneously, rates of infection, pain-related complications, and psychological trauma spike dramatically. Practitioners with medication knowledge become invaluable. A single individual understanding antibiotic selection, pediatric dosing, and drug interaction screening can prevent deaths and permanent disabilities across dozens of patients.

### Urgency Levels and Triage Framework

**Immediate (minutes to hours):** Anaphylaxis (epinephrine 0.3-0.5mg IM), severe bleeding (access to hemostatic agents), respiratory compromise. **Urgent (hours to 24 hours):** Bacterial infections with systemic signs (fever >39°C, tachycardia >120bpm, hypotension <90/60), trauma with severe pain, acute psychiatric crises. **Important (24-72 hours):** Localized infections without systemic signs, moderate pain, chronic condition management. **Deferred:** Minor discomfort, stable chronic diseases with available herbal alternatives. Establish community triage protocols early; medication rationing requires explicit frameworks to guide allocation decisions transparently.

If the question is really about sudden face droop, one-sided weakness or numbness, trouble speaking, heavy chest pressure, pain into the arm/jaw/back, cold sweat, or sudden shortness of breath, do not start by choosing a medicine from this guide. :::warning
### Recognition of Medication Emergencies

**Anaphylaxis:** sudden onset urticaria (hives), facial/tongue swelling, wheezing, hypotension within minutes of drug exposure. Requires immediate IM epinephrine. **Severe allergic reaction:** spreading rash, angioedema, respiratory distress. Stop medication immediately, monitor airway, administer antihistamine + corticosteroid. **Overdose toxicity:** varies by drug class (opioid: respiratory depression + pinpoint pupils; stimulant: tachycardia, hypertension, agitation; anticholinergic: hyperthermia, dilated pupils, confusion). Supportive care + specific reversal agents (naloxone for opioids). For detailed toxidrome recognition and antidote protocols, see <a href="../toxicology.html">Toxicology & Poison Management</a> and <a href="../toxicology-poisoning-response.html">Toxicology and Poisoning Response</a>. **Serotonin syndrome:** tremor, hyper-reflexia, mydriasis, hyperthermia, confusion from excess serotonin (SSRIs + tramadol). Stop causative agents immediately, cool patient, symptomatic treatment.
:::

## Historical Context and Pharmacy Evolution

### Pre-Antibiotic Medicine and the Collapse Scenario

Before antibiotics emerged in the 1940s, medical practice relied exclusively on environmental controls: strict sanitation, antiseptics (iodine, carbolic acid, boric acid), supportive care (fluids, nutrition, rest), and frank acceptance of high mortality rates from infectious diseases. Wounds that today receive a simple oral antibiotic resulted in amputation or death. A post-collapse scenario returns medicine to pre-antibiotic capabilities—a sobering reality that reshapes entire medical strategy. Prevention becomes paramount; infection prevention (sanitation, wound care, barrier techniques) is infinitely more valuable than attempting to treat established infections with inadequate pharmaceutical resources.

### Pharmaceutical Supply Chain History

Modern medications depend on complex supply chains: raw materials sourced globally, intermediate synthesis steps, strict quality control, cold-chain logistics for biologics, regulatory oversight ensuring purity and potency. A collapse scenario eliminates this infrastructure. Understanding pharmaceutical history illuminates survival priorities: maintain shelf-stable formulations (tablets, powders), avoid perishable medications (liquids, injectables requiring refrigeration), prioritize broad-spectrum agents over disease-specific treatments, and establish community-level stockpiling and compounding capabilities to extend available resources.

### Pharmacological Principles in Resource-Constrained Settings

Drug absorption (variable by route and formulation), metabolism (hepatic and renal elimination), and elimination half-life determine dosing intervals and total dose requirements. In survival medicine, understanding these principles guides dosing adjustments for: renal failure (common in collapse—dehydration, hypertension, trauma), hepatic compromise (malnutrition, infection), extremes of age (infants, elderly), and drug interactions. Accurate dosing prevents both treatment failure (underdosing) and toxicity (overdosing), critical when replacement medications may be unavailable.

## Medication Identification and Classification

### Visual Identification Methods

Tablets and capsules: imprint codes identify manufacturer and formulation. Keep imprints intact and legible; blurred or worn imprints indicate possible storage damage or counterfeiting. Check color, shape, size, and imprint codes against pharmaceutical references (maintain physical drug reference books—digital access unreliable post-collapse). Liquids: Keep them clear unless suspension (uniform cloudiness); separation, particulates, or discoloration indicate degradation. Injectables: Keep clear solutions clear; cloudiness or particles suggest contamination. Always verify drug name, concentration, and lot number before administering.

### Drug Classification Systems

Therapeutic classification (antibiotic, analgesic, cardiovascular) groups drugs by clinical effect. Chemical classification (penicillin, macrolide, NSAID) groups by molecular structure and mechanism. Legal classification (controlled substance) determines prescribing/dispensing restrictions. In survival settings, maintain classification knowledge to enable rapid medication selection: respiratory infection → consider spectrum coverage (gram-positive, atypical); pain management → consider NSAID efficacy, GI risk, and opioid alternatives. Cross-classification (a drug may be both analgesic and anti-inflammatory) guides rational polypharmacy.

### Pharmaceutical Forms

Tablets (compressed powder): stable, long shelf-life, precise dosing. Capsules (gelatin-enclosed powder): similar stability, harder to divide for pediatric dosing. Liquids (aqueous or alcohol-based solutions/suspensions): shorter shelf-life, variable stability, required for patients unable to swallow solids. Powders (requiring reconstitution): extremely stable before reconstitution, but reconstituted product stability limited (often 7-14 days refrigerated). Injectables (ampules, vials, prefilled syringes): highly variable stability depending on formulation and temperature. In survival stockpiling, prioritize tablets/capsules over liquids and powders.

## Shelf Life and Potency Assessment

![Medication shelf-life decision tree for assessing package integrity, storage conditions, expiration date, potency, and disposition](../assets/svgs/medications-1.svg)

### Shelf-Life Estimation and Potency Testing

Official expiration dates assume ideal storage (cool, dry, light-protected). Room temperature storage reduces shelf-life by 30-50% compared to labeled expiration. Visual inspection reveals obvious degradation: tablets becoming soft/sticky (moisture absorption), discoloration, visible mold (humidity damage), or separation in suspensions. Sensory tests (appearance, smell, taste if safe to do so) identify gross contamination or degradation. In survival settings without laboratory capability, assume: tablets maintain 60-70% potency at expiration if stored reasonably well; liquids maintain 50-60% potency; anything stored in warm/humid conditions loses potency faster.

### Potency Loss Acceleration Factors

Heat accelerates degradation exponentially (each 10°C temperature increase roughly halves remaining shelf-life). Humidity causes moisture absorption, especially for tablets and capsules, promoting decomposition. Light exposure (UV, visible) degrades photosensitive drugs (tetracyclines, certain vitamins). Oxygen exposure promotes oxidation. Contamination (bacterial, fungal) from improper storage or handling destroys efficacy. Temperature cycling (freeze-thaw) damages some formulations. In survival stockpiling, temperature stability is paramount; even without ideal humidity control, a cool location extends shelf-life significantly.

### Recognizing Degradation Signs

Tablets: hardness change (too soft indicates moisture, too hard indicates improper storage), color shift (yellowing common with age), crumbling or powdering (structural breakdown). Capsules: gelatin brittleness (dry storage) or softness (humidity), color fading, separation of capsule halves. Liquids: cloudiness (precipitation, bacterial growth), separation (emulsion breakdown), visible particles, color change, altered viscosity. Injectables: cloudiness, particles (especially critical—never inject particulate matter), color change, label separation. Any of these findings warrant disposal if alternatives available; use only if no viable alternative exists and patient benefit outweighs risk.

## Storage Conditions and Environmental Factors

### Optimal Storage Environment

Temperature: 68-77°F (20-25°C) is standard; avoid freezing (damages some formulations), avoid heat above 86°F (30°C). Many medications tolerate brief excursions but cumulative heat exposure reduces shelf-life. Humidity: below 50% relative humidity preferred; above 60% promotes moisture absorption. Light: opaque/amber containers prevent light degradation. Air: sealed containers protect from oxidation. For survival stockpiles: basements, underground shelters, or caves provide cool stable temperatures. In hot climates, evaporative cooling systems or burial in insulated containers extend shelf-life. Avoid: attics (heat extremes), bathrooms (humidity), direct sunlight locations, areas subject to temperature cycling (uninsulated garages).

### Medication Categorization by Storage Requirement

Room temperature, dry: most tablets, capsules, powders. Refrigerated (35-46°F): biologics (vaccines), some liquid suspensions (amoxicillin suspension), certain injectables. Light-protected: tetracyclines, some liquid formulations, certain vitamins. In survival settings without refrigeration, refrigerated medications degrade faster but often remain partially effective; prioritize non-refrigerated formulations. Clearly label all containers with: drug name, concentration, expiration date, storage requirements, and any special handling instructions. Color-coded labels aid rapid identification during emergencies.

### Humidity and Desiccant Management

Silica gel packets (color-changing desiccants indicate saturation), calcium chloride, or activated charcoal placed in medication containers reduce humidity. Replace desiccants when saturated. For large storage containers, maintain sealed environment with periodic desiccant replacement. Vacuum-sealing medications in oxygen-barrier materials (mylar bags with oxygen absorbers) extends shelf-life dramatically (15+ years for stable drugs like doxycycline). In survival stockpiles, vacuum-sealed, desiccated, cool storage represents optimal preservation.

### Acetaminophen (Paracetamol)

Dose: 325-650mg every 4-6 hours (critical: max 3-4g daily—exceeding this risks acute liver failure). Mechanism: analgesic and antipyretic, but NOT anti-inflammatory. Used for fever, mild-moderate pain. Lower GI upset risk than NSAIDs. Many combination products (cold remedies, cough/cold combinations, pain relievers) contain acetaminophen; careful label review prevents accidental overdose. Hepatotoxicity risk elevated in: chronic alcoholics, patients with liver disease, prolonged fever requiring extended dosing. In survival settings, acetaminophen is ideal for fever management when NSAIDs contraindicated.

### Opioid Analgesics: Codeine and Morphine

Codeine: mild-moderate pain, 15-60mg every 4-6 hours (often combined with acetaminophen or aspirin in tablet form). Often used in cough suppressant formulations. Low addiction risk compared to morphine, but still habit-forming. Metabolized to morphine; poor metabolizers (genetic variation) experience minimal analgesia. Morphine: moderate-severe pain, 5-10mg intramuscular or subcutaneous, or 10-15mg oral every 3-4 hours. Onset: IM/SC 5-30 minutes; oral 30-60 minutes. Monitor respiratory rate carefully—respiratory depression is primary concern. Maintain naloxone (opioid reversal) available (0.4-2mg IV or IM reverses overdose/respiratory depression within 2-3 minutes). Expect constipation with opioid use; concurrently initiate laxatives (docusate, senna, polyethylene glycol).

## Antibiotics and Antiinfectives

![Drug interaction matrix showing common salvaged medication categories and dangerous combinations](../assets/svgs/medications-2.svg)

### Beta-Lactam Antibiotics: Penicillins and Cephalosporins

Amoxicillin: broad-spectrum oral, 500mg three times daily or 875mg twice daily, effective for strep throat, otitis media, urinary tract infections, respiratory infections. Allergy history essential (10-15% general population, though true severe IgE-mediated reactions lower). Cross-reactivity with cephalosporins possible but uncommon (5-10%). Amoxicillin-clavulanate: combines amoxicillin with clavulanic acid (beta-lactamase inhibitor), overcoming gram-negative resistance. Cephalexin: oral cephalosporin, 500mg four times daily, useful if penicillin allergy (10% cross-reactivity risk, but generally safe). Both excellent for skin/soft tissue, respiratory, UTI, with minimal drug interactions.

### Macrolide and Fluoroquinolone Antibiotics

Azithromycin: Z-pack formulation (500mg day one, then 250mg daily x 4 days) for community-acquired pneumonia, sexually transmitted infections, atypical infections (Mycoplasma, Chlamydia). Long tissue half-life allows once-daily dosing. Caution: inhibits CYP3A4 metabolism (increases levels of many drugs); GI side effects common. Doxycycline: 100mg twice daily, broad-spectrum covering gram-positive, gram-negative, atypicals, spirochetes (Lyme disease, rickettsial). Essential for malaria prophylaxis, meningococcal prophylaxis. Photosensitivity risk (sun protection essential). Chelates with calcium/iron/magnesium; separate dosing from antacids, dairy, iron supplements. Fluoroquinolones (levofloxacin, ciprofloxacin): broad-spectrum, excellent for gram-negative infections, bone infections, atypical coverage. Reduced absorption with antacids, iron, zinc (space doses 2+ hours apart). Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole (TMP-SMX): double-strength tablet twice daily, effective for UTI, Pneumocystis prophylaxis, gram-negative coverage.

### Antibiotic Stewardship in Survival Medicine

Use narrowest spectrum antibiotic appropriate for identified organism (broad-spectrum wastes resources, promotes resistance development). For guidance on infection prevention techniques that reduce antibiotic demand, see <a href="../infection-control.html#antibiotic">Infection Control — Antibiotic Stewardship</a>. Avoid antibiotics for viral infections (ineffective, wastes critical supplies). Complete full prescribed course even if symptoms resolve (incomplete course promotes resistance, increases relapse risk). For severe infections, combination therapy broadens coverage: example—penicillin G plus gentamicin for serious gram-positive cocci. Monitor for adverse reactions (rash, GI upset, photosensitivity, anaphylaxis). Reduce doses for renal insufficiency (aminoglycosides, fluoroquinolones require dose adjustment; beta-lactams generally safe but accumulate in renal failure). Document all antibiotic therapy and patient response for future reference.

## Cardiovascular Medications

### Beta-Blockers and ACE Inhibitors

Beta-blockers (metoprolol, propranolol, atenolol): reduce heart rate, contractility, and blood pressure; used for hypertension, heart failure, angina, arrhythmias. Dose: metoprolol 25-100mg twice daily; propranolol 10-40mg three times daily; atenolol 25-100mg daily. Cautions: sudden withdrawal risks rebound hypertension/tachycardia; taper gradually if discontinuing. Contraindicated in decompensated heart failure (negative inotropic), severe bradycardia, asthma/COPD (can cause bronchospasm). ACE inhibitors (lisinopril, enalapril, ramipril): block angiotensin II formation, reducing vascular resistance and aldosterone; used for hypertension, heart failure, post-MI cardioprotection. Dose: lisinopril 5-20mg daily; enalapril 5-20mg daily. Cautions: hyperkalemia risk (especially with renal impairment or NSAIDs); dry cough common (20-30% of patients); angioedema rare but serious.

### Diuretics and Cardiac Glycosides

Loop diuretics (furosemide): potent diuretics for acute pulmonary edema, heart failure, hypertension. Dose: 20-80mg daily to twice daily. Cautions: hypokalemia (supplement potassium or use potassium-sparing diuretic concurrently); ototoxicity (hearing loss) with high doses. Thiazide diuretics (hydrochlorothiazide): milder, used for chronic hypertension. Cardiac glycoside (digoxin): increases cardiac contractility, slows AV node conduction; used for heart failure, atrial fibrillation rate control. Narrow therapeutic window (0.5-2 ng/mL); toxicity causes nausea, arrhythmias, confusion. Interact with NSAIDs, diuretics, certain antibiotics; require careful monitoring.

## Psychiatric Medications

### SSRIs and Anxiolytics

Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs—sertraline, fluoxetine, paroxetine): first-line for depression, anxiety, OCD, PTSD. Dose: sertraline 50-200mg daily; fluoxetine 20-80mg daily. Onset: 2-4 weeks for full efficacy. Cautions: serotonin syndrome if combined with other serotonergic agents (tramadol, dextromethorphan, MAO inhibitors); sexual dysfunction, weight gain, GI upset common. Benzodiazepines (lorazepam, diazepam, alprazolam): short-acting anxiolytics/sedatives for acute anxiety, insomnia, seizure management. Dose: lorazepam 0.5-2mg two to three times daily; diazepam 2-10mg twice daily. Cautions: dependency risk (avoid long-term use); respiratory depression (especially IV); CNS depression if combined with alcohol/opioids. Use lowest effective dose for shortest duration.

### Antipsychotics and Mood Stabilizers

Typical antipsychotics (haloperidol, chlorpromazine): block dopamine; used for acute psychosis, severe agitation. Dose: haloperidol 0.5-5mg IM/IV for acute agitation. Extrapyramidal side effects common (dystonia, akathisia, parkinsonism). Atypical antipsychotics (olanzapine, risperidone, quetiapine): newer agents with lower extrapyramidal risk but metabolic side effects (weight gain, glucose intolerance). Lithium: mood stabilizer for bipolar disorder; narrow therapeutic window (0.6-1.2 mEq/L); toxicity causes tremor, confusion, renal/thyroid dysfunction. Requires frequent monitoring; dehydration, NSAIDs, thiazides increase lithium levels.

**CRITICAL: Pediatric Doses Are NOT Simply Reduced Adult Doses.** Children have fundamentally different pharmacokinetics than adults — different absorption rates, metabolism speeds, organ maturity, and body composition. Calculate a child's dose independently using weight-based formulas (mg/kg); never divide or reduce an adult dose. Fractional adult doses (e.g., "give half the adult dose") ignore these differences and frequently result in dangerous overdosing or ineffective underdosing. Always calculate: weight (kg) x dose per kg = total pediatric dose. When in doubt, consult pediatric dosing references rather than guessing from adult dosing.
:::

### Weight-Based Dosing Fundamentals

Most pediatric medications calculate as: (weight in kg) × (mg/kg dose) = total dose. Accurate weight is essential; use calibrated scale if available. If scale unavailable, estimate weight from age: rough formula is (age in years × 2) + 8 = approximate weight in kg (valid approximately 1-10 years). Pediatric patients have different pharmacokinetics than adults: faster metabolism (may require higher mg/kg doses), variable GI absorption, immature renal/hepatic function (especially infants <6 months). Never reduce adult doses proportionally; use age/weight-appropriate formulations and dosages.

## Pediatric Medication Safety

### Boundary Replacement for Medication Dosage Requests

This guide should not provide a dosage calculator, dose conversion, medication selection, tapering, substitution, compounding, or treatment plan. For a medication safety question, keep support limited to label and identity uncertainty, possible duplicate ingredients, allergy or prior reaction, child or pregnancy exposure, expired or storage-damaged medicine, unknown pills, do-not-take or hold-and-call boundaries, poison-control/pharmacist/clinician handoff, and medication records.

Do not use this section to calculate a dose, adjust a dose, clear an interaction, decide whether a medicine is safe for a specific person, or replace pharmacist or clinician review.

### Weight-Based Dosing Principles

Pediatric medication dosing is entirely weight-dependent. The single most critical error prevention measure is accurate weight measurement. Always use a calibrated scale when available. If scale unavailable, estimate weight from age: for children 1-10 years, approximate weight (kg) = (age in years × 2) + 8. Never guess or use "average" weights. Calculate total dose as: **weight (kg) × dose per kg = total dose in mg**. Round to nearest available formulation strength (never split tablets unless specifically designed for this).

### Common Dosing Errors in Pediatrics

**Error 1: Using adult dosing formulas without adjustment**
- WRONG: Adult receives 500mg antibiotic, so give child "half" = 250mg (ignores weight differences)
- RIGHT: Calculate based on weight—20kg child receiving 25 mg/kg antibiotic = 500mg (same amount, but correctly derived)

**Error 2: Confusing mg/kg/day vs. single dose**
- WRONG: Amoxicillin "50 mg/kg/day" interpreted as single dose for 20kg child = 1000mg per dose (would give 3-4g/day total)
- RIGHT: 50 mg/kg/day = total daily amount divided by number of doses. For 20kg child: 1000mg daily ÷ 3 doses = ~333mg per dose

**Error 3: Using household measuring spoons instead of calibrated devices**
- WRONG: "One teaspoon" of liquid antibiotic (actual volume varies: 3-6mL depending on spoon)
- RIGHT: Use calibrated syringe marked in mL (5mL syringe minimum)

**Error 4: Forgetting to adjust for renal/hepatic function in infants (<6 months)**
- Newborns and premature infants have immature kidney/liver function
- Require longer dosing intervals or lower doses
- Example: Standard antibiotic dosing every 6 hours in older children may be every 8-12 hours in infants

:::danger
**NEVER use adult doses for children.** Always calculate weight-based doses independently, even if dose "seems reasonable." Medication errors are leading cause of preventable pediatric deaths. Double-check all calculations before administration.
:::

### Dangerous Drug Classes in Pediatrics

**Tetracyclines (doxycycline, tetracycline):**
- NOT for children under 8 years (causes permanent tooth discoloration)
- Use alternative antibiotics (amoxicillin, cephalexin, azithromycin)

**Fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin):**
- Avoid in children unless life-threatening infection and no alternatives (tendon rupture risk)
- Use only if senior physician approval in true emergency

**Macrolide antibiotics + certain medications:**
- Azithromycin + asthma medications can worsen asthma in some children
- Screen for underlying cardiac conditions (QT prolongation risk)

**NSAIDs in certain conditions:**
- Avoid NSAIDs if dehydration, renal disease, or severe sepsis (increase kidney injury risk)
- Acetaminophen safer in these scenarios

**Metronidazole:**
- Generally safe in children but extremely bitter
- Must disguise taste in flavoring or small quantity of food
- Do NOT exceed recommended dose (margin of safety narrow)

:::danger
**ABSOLUTE CONTRAINDICATIONS IN PEDIATRICS:**
- **Never give aspirin** to children with fever from viral illness (Reye syndrome risk—fatal)
- **Never use adult-formulation codeine** products (children metabolize unpredictably; risk of respiratory depression)
- **Never inject medications IV without proper training** (infiltration causes tissue damage in small veins)
- **Never crush extended-release tablets** (releases entire dose at once)
:::

Before administering ANY pediatric medication, verify:
- [ ] Child's current weight (in kg)
- [ ] Medication name and strength (matches prescription exactly)
- [ ] Calculated dose (mg/kg × weight = total dose)
- [ ] Frequency and route (oral, IM, IV, topical)
- [ ] Age-appropriate formulation available
- [ ] Allergies (especially antibiotics)
- [ ] Drug interactions (check against all current medications)
- [ ] Expiration date of medication (not expired)
- [ ] Correct measurement device (syringe, not spoon)
- [ ] Independent double-check by second person (if available)
- [ ] Documentation (medication name, dose, time, who gave it, child's response)

<section id="complications">

## Complications and Life-Threatening Signs

### Severe Allergic Reactions and Anaphylaxis

:::danger
**Anaphylaxis requires immediate action:** Symptoms appear within minutes of drug exposure (or rarely, up to 30 minutes). Signs include: rapid pulse (>120bpm), hypotension (<90/60 systolic), stridor or wheezing (airway swelling), profound urticaria (hives covering body), facial/tongue/lip swelling (angioedema). **Immediate action:** Inject epinephrine IM (0.3mg for children 15-30kg, 0.5mg for adults or repeat every 10 minutes) into lateral thigh. Establish airway access if respiratory compromise evident (prepare for emergency tracheostomy if necessary). Administer IV fluid bolus (1-2L normal saline rapidly) for hypotension. Position supine with legs elevated. Consider second-line agents: IV antihistamine (diphenhydramine 25-50mg), IV corticosteroid (methylprednisolone 1-2g). Transport to definitive care immediately if available. Do not discharge patient for 4-8 hours post-event (biphasic reactions occur in 5-15% of cases).
:::

### Drug-Induced Organ Toxicity

:::danger
**Acute liver failure from acetaminophen:** For the complete acetaminophen overdose management protocol including N-acetylcysteine dosing, see <a href="../toxicology-poisoning-response.html#specific-antidotes">Toxicology and Poisoning Response — Specific Antidotes</a>. Ingestion >8g in 24 hours (or cumulative overdose) causes hepatocellular necrosis. Symptoms appear 24-72 hours post-ingestion: jaundice, right upper quadrant abdominal pain, elevated transaminases (ALT/AST >1000 IU/L). Risk factors: chronic alcohol use, pre-existing liver disease, malnutrition. Management: discontinue acetaminophen immediately, supportive care (IV fluids, glucose monitoring for hypoglycemia, coagulopathy management with fresh frozen plasma if bleeding). N-acetylcysteine (NAC) 140mg/kg loading dose, then 70mg/kg every 4 hours x 17 doses if available (most effective if given within 24 hours). Prognosis dependent on degree of liver necrosis; severe cases require transplant (unavailable in collapse scenario).
:::

:::danger
**Nephrotoxicity from aminoglycosides or NSAIDs:** Aminoglycoside antibiotics (gentamicin, tobramycin) and NSAIDs cause direct renal tubular damage. Signs: rising serum creatinine (>1.5x baseline), decreasing urine output (<500mL/day), muddy-brown urine (tubular casts), rising potassium (hyperkalemia). Risk factors: pre-existing renal disease, dehydration, concurrent nephrotoxic drugs. Management: discontinue causative agent immediately, maintain hydration (IV crystalloid 500mL-1L over 1-2 hours), assess for hyperkalemia (peaked T waves on ECG)—treat with calcium gluconate 10% (10mL IV), then insulin 10 units + dextrose 25g IV, or loop diuretic if volume overloaded. Monitor creatinine daily. Recovery may take days to weeks even after drug cessation.
:::

### Serious Adverse Effects Requiring Medication Cessation

**Fluoroquinolone-induced tendon rupture:** Risk increases with age >60, concurrent corticosteroid use. Sudden-onset sharp pain in tendon (Achilles, patella, shoulder), loss of function. Discontinue fluoroquinolone immediately, immobilize, consider surgical repair if available. **Macrolide-induced QT prolongation and arrhythmia:** Baseline ECG QT interval >0.44 seconds increases risk. Azithromycin or erythromycin can further prolong QT, causing Torsades de Pointes (polymorphic ventricular tachycardia). Symptoms: palpitations, syncope, presyncope. Discontinue immediately, check electrolytes (especially potassium—<3.5mEq/L worsens), administer IV magnesium (1-2g) if available. **SSRI-induced hyponatremia:** Occurs within 2 weeks of initiation; symptoms: nausea, headache, confusion, hyponatremia <125mEq/L. Discontinue SSRI, restrict free water, administer hypertonic saline (3%) only if symptomatic (seizure risk). **Benzodiazepine-induced respiratory depression:** With concurrent opioids or alcohol, CNS depression can cause hypoventilation. Monitor respiratory rate (maintain >10bpm); if <8bpm, administer flumazenil 0.2mg IV (caution—may trigger seizures in benzodiazepine-dependent patients).

:::tip
### Monitoring for Adverse Effects During Treatment

Establish a simple monitoring protocol: baseline assessment (vital signs, weight, allergies), daily brief check-ins (fever, rash, new symptoms), vital sign documentation (especially for agents affecting heart rate/blood pressure). For antibiotics: observe for rash (mild—can continue, monitor; spreading—stop immediately), GI upset (reduce dose if mild, stop if severe diarrhea—possible C. difficile colitis). For opioids: monitor respiratory rate (maintain >10bpm), sedation level, bowel function (initiate laxatives concurrently). For cardiac medications: monitor pulse (maintain 50-100bpm for most), blood pressure (systolic >90mmHg), ankle edema (worsening heart failure). For psychiatric medications: mood changes, behavioral worsening (paradoxical reaction), movement abnormalities (antipsychotics).
:::

Fluoroquinolones: chelate with multivalent cations (calcium, magnesium, iron, zinc); absorb 50% less if taken with antacids, iron supplements, dairy. Metronidazole: disulfiram-like reaction with alcohol (severe nausea, vomiting, headache); counsel patient to avoid alcohol during and 48 hours after treatment. ### Simple Compounding Techniques for Survival Pharmacies

Powder reconstitution: many antibiotics supplied as powders (amoxicillin, cephalexin) requiring reconstitution with sterile water for oral suspension. Mix powder with recommended volume water, shake vigorously until fully dissolved, label with: drug name, concentration (mg/mL), expiration date (usually 7-14 days refrigerated), storage requirements. Measurement: use calibrated measuring devices (graduated syringe, not household spoon—inaccurate). Filtration: if compounded medication appears cloudy/contains particles, filter through sterile gauze before administering (prevents injection into blood vessels). Concentration adjustments: if higher or lower concentration needed, use formula C1V1=C2V2 (C=concentration, V=volume). Example: 250 mg/5mL stock, need 125 mg/5mL: mix equal volumes stock solution with sterile water.

### Tablet and Capsule Manipulation

Crushing tablets (for difficulty swallowing): crush in mortar/pestle, mix thoroughly with small amount applesauce/pudding if acceptable (check drug compatibility). NOT suitable for: enteric-coated tablets (designed to dissolve in intestine), extended-release formulations (crushing releases entire dose at once—toxicity risk), sensitive medications. Capsule opening: acceptable for many drugs; pour powder contents into food/liquid. Verify drug stability if opened (some degrade rapidly in water). Tablet splitting: only appropriate for uncoated, scored tablets; use pill splitter for accuracy. Do NOT split extended-release or enteric-coated tablets.

### Basic Sterile Technique for Injectables

Aseptic preparation: clean hands with soap/water, alcohol wipe injection site on vial/ampule, use new sterile needle/syringe for each preparation. Ampule opening: file neck, wrap in gauze, break at scored line away from body. Filter needle recommended (removes glass fragments). Vial access: cleanse rubber septum with alcohol, insert needle at slight angle, draw medication. Needle changes: draw with one needle (dulls quickly), inject with fresh needle (reduces patient discomfort, tissue trauma). Verify: dose accuracy, drug name, expiration, no cloudiness/particles in solution before injecting.

<section id="failure-modes">

## Failure Modes and Degradation Signs

### Visible Signs of Medication Degradation

Tablets: discoloration (yellowing common with age, but brown/black concerning), softness/stickiness (moisture absorption), hardness/crumbling (improper storage), mold or growth (discard immediately). Capsules: color fading, separation of capsule halves, brittle/broken gelatin, mold. Liquids: cloudiness (precipitation, contamination, emulsion breakdown), visible particles, color change (yellowing acceptable age, brown/black concerning), separation of layers (unmixed suspension indicates settling, remix gently), altered viscosity (thinner/thicker than expected suggests degradation or evaporation). Injectables: same as liquids—never inject if cloudy, particulate, or discolored (particle injection risks vessel occlusion, tissue damage, embolism). Powders for reconstitution: caking or clumping (moisture absorption), discoloration, mold.

### Clinical Signs of Treatment Failure

Antibiotic failure: infection worsens despite appropriate therapy after 48-72 hours, or doesn't improve after 7-10 days. Causes: under-dosing, resistant organism, inadequate source control (abscess needs drainage), renal failure (drug accumulation, paradoxically reducing effectiveness of some agents), noncompliance, wrong diagnosis. Solutions: verify dosing accuracy, increase dose if under-dosed, culture specimen if possible (identify organism/resistance), assess for surgical source (abscess, retained foreign body), check renal function. Analgesic failure: pain not controlled despite appropriate dose. Causes: inadequate dose (increase), opioid tolerance (increase dose, rotate agents), neuropathic pain (different agent needed), psychological component. Management: reassess pain characteristics, adjust dose, consider multimodal approach (NSAID + acetaminophen + opioid).

### Medication Contamination and Adverse Reactions

Bacterial contamination: any visible growth, cloudiness in sterile preparation, foul odor. Discard immediately; never attempt to sterilize by heating (destroys active drug). Particulate contamination: glass fragments (from broken ampules), mold, undissolved powder. Filter or discard depending on severity/alternative availability. Allergic reactions: rash (can continue if mild, stop if spreading), urticaria/hives, angioedema (facial swelling—consider anaphylaxis risk), respiratory symptoms (stop immediately, consider anaphylaxis—administer epinephrine 0.3-0.5mg IM if available). Severe reactions warrant immediate cessation, supportive care, and documentation for future avoidance.

<section id="troubleshooting">

## Troubleshooting Common Medication Problems

### Treatment Not Working: Antibiotic Failure

**Scenario:** Patient with bacterial infection shows no improvement after 3-5 days of antibiotics, or worsens despite therapy.

**Diagnostic approach:** (1) Verify correct diagnosis—is this truly bacterial infection? Viral infections (colds, influenza, most sore throats) do not respond to antibiotics; obtaining cultures if possible distinguishes bacterial from viral. (2) Confirm appropriate drug selection for suspected organism—respiratory infection may require coverage of atypical organisms (doxycycline, azithromycin), skin infection coverage may differ (streptococci vs. staphylococci). (3) Assess dosing adequacy—recalculate dose based on patient weight; verify patient is actually taking medication as prescribed (compliance issues common, especially with TID/QID dosing). (4) Evaluate for source control failure—abscess or localized collection requires drainage in addition to antibiotics; bacteria sequestered in pus have reduced antibiotic exposure. Obtain imaging (ultrasound, manual palpation) to detect drainable collections.

**Solutions:** Increase antibiotic dose if under-dosed (consult previous dosing table), switch to different antibiotic class if organism likely resistant (e.g., Staphylococcus aureus—use alternative if penicillin resistance suspected), consider combination therapy for serious infections (penicillin + gentamicin for gram-positive cocci with concern for enterococci), perform surgical source control (incision + drainage for abscess), optimize supportive care (hydration, nutrition, rest). Document antibiotic trial and response for future reference (patient history of this infection + response guides future treatment).

### Analgesic Failure: Pain Not Controlled

**Scenario:** Patient receiving pain medication reports inadequate relief; pain persists or worsens.

**Assessment:** (1) Clarify pain characteristics—location, intensity (0-10 scale), quality (sharp/dull/burning/aching), worse with movement (somatic) vs. constant (neuropathic). (2) Determine medication history—which drugs tried, at what doses, for how long. (3) Assess for tolerance—opioid tolerance develops with prolonged use; patient may require dose escalation. (4) Evaluate for complications—unrecognized fracture, expanding infection (abscess), thrombosis (DVT causing calf pain). (5) Consider psychological factors—anxiety, depression amplify pain perception.

**Solutions for inadequate opioid analgesia:** Increase dose 25-50% (if baseline dose standard or low). Rotate to different opioid class (morphine → hydromorphone, or vice versa). Combine modalities: acetaminophen + NSAID + opioid + topical agents achieves better pain control than monotherapy. For neuropathic pain (burning, shooting quality): add gabapentin 100-300mg TID (off-label, but useful for nerve pain), or pregabalin if available. For somatic pain with inflammation: high-dose NSAID (ibuprofen 800mg TID) + opioid combination. For musculoskeletal pain: immobilization + NSAID + gentle mobilization as tolerated.

:::info-box
### Pain Control Multimodal Strategy

Combine multiple agents targeting different pain pathways: **Nociplastic (inflammatory) pain:** NSAIDs + acetaminophen + topical agents (heat, ice, menthol). **Neuropathic pain:** gabapentin, pregabalin, local anesthetics (lidocaine patch). **Opioid-responsive pain:** morphine, hydromorphone (titrate carefully for respiratory depression). **Psychological component:** anxiolytics (lorazepam 0.5-1mg TID), antidepressants (low-dose amitriptyline 10-50mg QHS), relaxation techniques. Reassess pain relief 1-2 hours after dose (acute); schedule regular reassessments for chronic conditions.
:::

### Managing Adverse Effects While Continuing Necessary Medication

**GI upset from antibiotics:** Take with food (if not contradicted by specific drug), take H2 blocker (famotidine 20mg BID) or antacid (aluminum hydroxide/magnesium hydroxide 30mL QID), address diarrhea with probiotics or bismuth subsalicylate if available. **Photosensitivity from doxycycline:** avoid sun exposure, wear protective clothing, apply high-SPF sunscreen (if available). Symptoms: severe sunburn-like reaction disproportionate to sun exposure. Discontinue doxycycline if severe; switch to azithromycin or amoxicillin if infection allows. **Constipation from opioids:** initiate bowel regimen concurrently: docusate 100-200mg BID (stool softener), senna 17-34mg QHS (stimulant), polyethylene glycol (Golytely) 17-34g daily (osmotic). Monitor for impaction (absent bowel sounds, abdominal distension, overflow diarrhea)—manually disimpact if necessary and mild, or consider anesthetic lubricant + gentle manipulation. **Dizziness from blood pressure medications:** ensure adequate hydration (1-2L daily), avoid rapid position changes, reduce dose if systolic drops below 90mmHg persistently. Monitor orthostatic vital signs (blood pressure supine vs. standing after 1-3 minutes; drop >20 systolic abnormal).

## Seasonal Storage Challenges

### Temperature Extremes in Different Climates

Tropical/hot climates: sustained heat (>86°F) accelerates degradation. Mitigation: bury medications in insulated underground caches (earth provides temperature buffering), use evaporative cooling with damp cloth over storage container, maintain reflective covering to reduce heat absorption, avoid attics/direct sunlight. Monitor desiccant packs closely (saturate faster in humid environments). Cold climates: freezing temperatures may damage some formulations (especially liquids, emulsions). Store indoors in root cellars or insulated basements. Avoid repeated freeze-thaw cycles (damages stability). Temperate climates: seasonal swings less extreme; still avoid uninsulated storage. Monitor humidity especially in spring (melting snow) and fall (dampness).

### Humidity Management Across Seasons

Spring/summer: higher humidity risk. Increase desiccant usage, check for moisture absorption weekly. Summer: heat + humidity compound degradation; priority move to cool storage. Fall: leaf debris may clog ventilation; clean filters/openings to maintain air circulation. Winter: dry cold advantageous for storage stability; open storage areas briefly when humidity high to promote evaporation (cold air holds less moisture). Use silica gel color-changing packs (change color when saturated—replace immediately). Vacuum-sealed, oxygen-absorbed medications resistant to seasonal swings; prioritize long-term storage in this format.

<section id="field-adaptations">

## Field Adaptations: Medication Administration Without Ideal Equipment

### Injectable Administration Without Sterile Syringes

**Scenario:** Patient requires injectable antibiotic (penicillin G, gentamicin) or analgesic (morphine), but pre-filled syringes exhausted. Available: glass ampules, improvised supplies.

**Improvised needle/syringe options:** Glass pipettes (chemistry/lab equipment) can be sharpened on whetstone and used as needles if sterile (boil 10 minutes). Alternatively, salvage sterile syringes from medical waste (hospitals, clinics often abandoned in collapse), re-use single-patient syringes only (boil 10 minutes between uses—not ideal, risks infection, but better than no treatment in desperate situations). Cotton IV bags with attached tubing + needle can serve as improvised IV delivery if necessary.

**Safer alternative—convert to oral:** If injectable medication available in tablet form, crush tablet (verify crushability first—do not crush ER/enteric formulations), dissolve in small amount water/juice, administer orally. Absorption slower, but avoids injection risks (infection, vessel damage, air embolism). Example: convert morphine injectable to morphine liquid preparation (see compounding section).

:::tip
### Maximizing Sterilization Without Modern Equipment

**Boiling sterilization:** bring water to rolling boil, submerge equipment 10 minutes minimum. Effective for: glass, metal, most plastics (risk of melting if plastic thin). Remove using sterile forceps or tweezers. Allow to cool on clean cloth. Boiling removes bacteria/viruses but not prions (transmissible spongiform encephalopathy—rare, but avoid re-using equipment from neurological patients if possible). **Flame sterilization:** pass needle/syringe through open flame rapidly (don't heat excessively—will warp plastic). Effective but requires skill. **Alcohol soaking:** 70% isopropyl alcohol (if available) soaked for 5 minutes reduces but doesn't eliminate contamination. Not adequate sole sterilization, but useful adjunct after boiling. **Sun drying:** UV rays have sterilizing effect; clean equipment left in direct sunlight 2-3 hours provides some disinfection (not equivalent to boiling, but better than nothing).
:::

### Oral Medication Administration Challenges

**Difficulty swallowing tablets:** A patient with stroke, difficulty swallowing, or dysphagia cannot take whole tablets/capsules. Try these solutions: (1) Crush non-ER tablet, mix with small amount applesauce/pudding/honey (lubricates throat), administer. Verify crushability—do not crush: enteric-coated, ER, capsules (exception: certain capsules can be opened, powder sprinkled on food). (2) Request liquid formulation if available (cough syrups, liquid suspensions). (3) Dissolve tablet in small amount warm water (if soluble), form "slurry," administer via mouth. (4) If patient unconscious/unable to swallow: try alternative routes—rectal suppository (if formulation available), transdermal patch (limited drugs available in patch form), intramuscular/subcutaneous injection (if trained and supplies available).

**Medication refusal due to taste/GI upset:** Bitter medications often rejected, especially by children. Mitigation: mix with strong-flavored food (chocolate pudding, honey, peanut butter), mask bitter taste with sugar/juice, take concurrently with food (if drug not contraindicated on empty stomach). For GI upset: take with food, reduce dose (check for sub-therapeutic response after dose reduction), switch formulation (liquid vs. tablet may have different GI effects).

### Handling Medication Storage Failures in Field Settings

**Refrigerated medication exposed to heat:** Reconstituted amoxicillin suspension, insulin, certain biologics require refrigeration; if power loss, medications warm rapidly. Potency loss occurs within hours at room temperature (more slowly if temperature 60-70°F). Assessment: if medications left unrefrigerated <2 hours in cool environment, likely still viable. If >4 hours at room temperature (>72°F), potency degraded significantly. Discard if alternatives available; use expired medications only if patient benefit clear and alternatives exhausted. Document use in patient record.

**Water contamination of powder medications:** Accidentally water-exposed powders (amoxicillin powder, sulfamethoxazole powder) absorb moisture, begin caking/clumping (sign of degradation). If minor water exposure and powder still dry: spread powder on clean cloth, allow air drying 24-48 hours (slow). If extensively water-damaged: discard if alternatives available. Humid environments: keep desiccant packs in containers; replace weekly. Store in airtight containers; repackage medications to smaller containers if opened (reduces air exposure).

**Temperature extremes during transport:** Medications in uninsulated vehicles experience rapid temperature swings (freezing at night, heat during day). Mitigation: insulate with blankets, pack in cooler with ice packs (for heat exposure) or thermal blankets (for cold exposure). Monitor temperature if possible (even basic/cheap thermometer helps). High-altitude transport: pressure changes have minimal effect on most medications (concern mainly for pressurized inhalers). Shake vials/bottles gently before use after transport (settling may occur).

:::info-box
### Quick Assessment: Is This Medication Safe to Use?

Ask sequentially: (1) **Expiration date?** If expired, assess storage conditions (cool/dark better than hot/humid). If recently expired and well-stored, often 60-80% potent. Use only if benefits outweigh risks. (2) **Appearance?** Tablets soft/discolored, capsules separated, liquids cloudy = likely degraded. Discard if alternative available. (3) **Smell?** Pungent or off-odor indicates chemical breakdown. Discard. (4) **Intended dose reasonable?** Verify dosing matches standards (compare to reference if available). (5) **Patient allergy documented?** If yes for this drug class, do NOT use. (6) **Drug interactions with current medications?** Scan mental checklist of major interactions; if yes, hold drug or substitute. (7) **Risk-benefit favorable?** If yes to above and no major interactions, medication likely safe to use.
:::

### Medication Identification Without Reference Materials

**Scenario:** Salvaged medications lack labels or imprint codes are worn. No access to pharmaceutical reference books or digital resources.

**Identification by visual characteristics:** Tablet color, shape, and size provide clues. White round tablets are common for acetaminophen, aspirin, many generic drugs. Orange/tan oblong tablets suggest ibuprofen (common formulation). Blue diamonds or ovals suggest certain benzodiazepines (diazepam) or other psychoactive medications. Pink/peach round tablets common for amoxicillin (250-500mg). Capsules: red/white or orange/white are common antibiotic formulations. Size differences may indicate strength variation (smaller often lower dose). Document observed characteristics and attempt cross-reference with any available books or knowledge within community (retired pharmacists, nurses).

**Identifying by mechanism and effect (empiric testing):** Carefully test small doses on trusted individuals to determine effect. Diuretics (loop/thiazide) produce increased urination within 1-2 hours. Beta-blockers slow heart rate (takes 30-60 minutes). Opioids produce drowsiness/analgesia. Antibiotics show no immediate effect (weeks to treat infection). Antihistamines cause drowsiness. This approach carries risk—only use for non-critical medications or when identification stakes low. Document findings for future reference.

**Organizing unidentified medications by probable category:** Group by physical similarity (all white round tablets together; all clear liquids together; all capsules). This reduces confusion risk. Within groups, test lowest-risk medications first (analgesics before opioids, antihistamines before antibiotics). Maintain separate sealed containers for each identified group with detailed description cards (color, shape, size, texture, taste, smell if safe, observed effects).

### Adapting Dosing When Weight Scales Unavailable

**Scenario:** Field setting with no access to calibrated scales for pediatric weight estimation.

**Visual/clinical estimation methods:** Newborns typically 3-4kg; 1-year-olds 8-10kg; 2-year-olds 12-15kg; 5-year-olds 16-20kg; 10-year-olds 25-30kg. Pre-pubescent children roughly (age in years × 2) + 8 = approximate weight in kg. Use multiple estimations: (1) Age-based calculation (above formula), (2) Parental recall if available ("She weighed 20kg at last doctor visit"), (3) Visual comparison to known objects (child approximately size of large bag of rice ≈ 10kg?). Average estimates to reduce error. For critical dosing, err toward conservative (lower) dose, monitor response, escalate if inadequate effect rather than risk toxicity from over-dosing.

**Relative dosing approach:** If exact weight unknown, use fraction of adult dose based on estimated age. Very rough guideline: <2 years ≈ 1/4 adult dose; 2-5 years ≈ 1/3 adult dose; 5-12 years ≈ 1/2 adult dose. This is less precise than weight-based but functional in crisis. Always verify this approach doesn't produce absurd results (example: adult aspirin 500mg; 1-year-old would receive ≈125mg—reasonable; but if calculator error produces 5mg for 10-year-old, recalculate).

## Community Pharmacy Protocols

### Establishing Inventory and Distribution Systems

Centralized pharmacy: designate secure location with climate control, staff trained in pharmacology/dosing, inventory tracking system (ledger or digital if power available). Maintain: medication list with indications/dosing, expiration tracking, allocation protocols prioritizing critical conditions (infections, pain management, cardiovascular emergencies). Satellite clinics: issue medications from central pharmacy with usage tracking; regular restock/inventory reconciliation. Distribution protocols: prioritize treatments for life-threatening conditions, restrict broad-spectrum antibiotics to documented infections (prevent resistance), educate patients on proper use (complete courses, appropriate indications). Consider: community education on medication storage/identification (reduces dependency on central pharmacy, increases patient autonomy).

### Staff Training and Continuity

Identify individuals with pharmaceutical knowledge (nurses, retired pharmacists, physicians) for core training team. Establish training curriculum covering: medication identification, dosing calculations (especially pediatric), administration routes/techniques, drug interactions, storage requirements, adverse reaction management, inventory management. Cross-train multiple individuals (reduce dependency, ensure continuity if key person unavailable). Maintain written protocols/references (books, laminated dosing cards, interaction charts) accessible to all staff. Regular drills: practice pediatric dosing calculations, injections on simulation models, manage mock adverse reactions to maintain competency.

### Resource Allocation and Ethical Frameworks

In resource-scarcity scenarios, establish allocation frameworks: life-threatening infections receive antibiotics; minor infections managed supportively (topical agents, herbal remedies) if possible. Opioid analgesics reserved for severe pain (fractures, late-stage cancer) rather than minor discomfort. Consider: establish community committee to address ethical dilemmas (rationing decisions), maintain transparency in allocation decisions to preserve community trust. Document decisions/rationale for future reference and learning. Cultivate herbal production/storage as supplemental therapy for pain, fever, GI symptoms, wound care — extending limited pharmaceutical supplies. ## Common Mistakes and Consequences

### Medication Administration Errors

**Mistake: Crushing extended-release (ER) tablets to facilitate swallowing.** Consequence: the entire dose releases immediately, causing toxicity. Example: extended-release metoprolol 200mg crushed = 200mg immediate absorption instead of gradual 24-hour release, resulting in severe bradycardia (heart rate <40bpm), hypotension, syncope. Prevention: Verify which tablets can be crushed before administration (do NOT crush: ER formulations, enteric-coated tablets, most capsules marked "SR" or "XR"). For patients unable to swallow, request a liquid formulation from pharmacy, or contact the pharmacist for crush-safe alternatives.

**Mistake: Confusing similar-sounding drug names.** Consequence: administering wrong medication entirely. Classic examples: Lopressor (metoprolol—beta-blocker) vs. Toprol-XL (metoprolol XR) confusion; Celebrex (celecoxib—NSAID) vs. Celexa (citalopram—SSRI) confusion. Prevention: verify full drug name, strength, and indication before administration. Use pharmacy labels rather than relying on memory. Establish "verification protocol": read label aloud before drawing/administering.

**Mistake: Using household spoons for liquid medication dosing.** Consequence: imprecise dosing (household spoons vary 5-15mL; medication spoons standardized to 5mL). Example: cough syrup dosed via household tablespoon (15mL actual volume) instead of 5mL prescribed = 3x overdose, risking toxicity. Prevention: always use calibrated measuring device (oral syringe, graduated medicine cup), never household spoon or "eyeballing."

:::warning
### Critical Medication Safety in Community Settings

-   Allergy documentation: maintain detailed records of patient allergies; review at each encounter. Document reactions specifically (rash vs. anaphylaxis—latter absolutely contraindicates drug class, former may allow rechallenge under observation).
-   Dose verification: always use "5 rights" (right patient, drug, dose, route, time). For pediatric patients, calculate independently and verify; second-person check reduces medication errors by 50%+.
-   Drug interaction screening: before prescribing, review all current medications. Maintain interaction references (physical books, if digital unavailable). Hold/substitute medications if serious interactions identified.
-   Controlled substance tracking: if opioids/benzodiazepines available, establish strict dispensing records, lock storage, minimize diversion risk through careful monitoring.
-   Expiration date enforcement: use oldest stock first (FIFO), discard expired medications if safer alternatives available, but document when expired medications used (with patient consent) in desperate situations.
:::

### Dosing and Calculation Errors

**Mistake: Calculating pediatric dose from adult dose by dividing by age or weight non-linearly.** Consequence: severe under/over-dosing. Correct formula: (child's weight in kg) × (mg/kg dose) = total pediatric dose. Example error: 5-year-old (18kg) needs amoxicillin 25-50 mg/kg/day; if practitioner divides adult 500mg dose by 5 (child's age) = 100mg (correct is 450-900mg/day). Prevention: use weight-based dosing tables, calculate independently, verify with second person before administration.

**Mistake: Misunderstanding dosing intervals (TID vs. QID vs. BID).** Consequence: patient receives drug too frequently or infrequently, causing toxicity or treatment failure. BID = twice daily (12 hours apart); TID = three times daily (8 hours apart); QID = four times daily (6 hours apart). Example: antibiotic prescribed QID; patient takes it BID (misreads label) = 50% under-dosing, treatment failure. Prevention: educate patient on frequency using clock times ("8am, 12pm, 4pm, 8pm" for QID), provide written instructions, use alarm reminders if available.

**Mistake: Not adjusting medication doses for renal/hepatic impairment.** Consequence: drug accumulation, toxicity. Example: patient with creatinine 3.0 (severe renal impairment) receives standard ciprofloxacin 500mg BID × 14 days = drug accumulation, tendon rupture risk, QT prolongation. Prevention: assess renal/hepatic function clinically (if labs unavailable, observe for: dehydration/oliguria indicating renal impairment, jaundice/ascites indicating hepatic impairment). Reduce doses 25-75% for impairment. Extend dosing intervals. Monitor for adverse effects closely.

### Medication Interaction Oversights

**Mistake: Prescribing warfarin + NSAIDs without monitoring INR or dose adjustment.** Consequence: supratherapeutic INR, spontaneous bleeding (GI hemorrhage, intracerebral hemorrhage). Prevention: screen all medications for interactions before prescribing; if unavoidable combination, verify INR 3-5 days after initiating NSAID (expect need for warfarin dose reduction), educate patient on bleeding signs (blood in stool/urine, unexplained bruising, hemoptysis), provide written interaction list.

**Mistake: Combining SSRIs + tramadol or SSRIs + dextromethorphan without recognizing serotonin syndrome risk.** Consequence: serotonin syndrome (tremor, hyper-reflexia, clonus, hyperthermia >38.5°C, confusion, agitation). Mortality 3-5% if untreated; severe hyperthermia causes rhabdomyolysis. Prevention: educate prescribers on serotonin syndrome risk; avoid combination, or if necessary, use lowest doses, monitor closely for: tremor, muscle rigidity, fever. Treat immediately: discontinue serotonergic agents, cool patient actively (ice packs, cool IV fluids), supportive care.

**Mistake: Prescribing doxycycline with dairy products or iron supplements without spacing doses.** Consequence: chelation reduces antibiotic absorption by 50%+, treatment failure. Prevention: educate patient to take doxycycline 1-2 hours before/after dairy, supplements, antacids. Provide written timing instructions.

## Advanced Pharmaceutical Considerations

### Metabolism Adjustments in Renal and Hepatic Failure

Renal failure: kidneys eliminate many drugs (aminoglycosides, fluoroquinolones, beta-lactams, NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors); reduced function causes drug accumulation. Dose reduction necessary to prevent toxicity. For mild renal impairment (eGFR 30-60): reduce dose 25-50%. For severe (eGFR <30): reduce 50-75% or extend dosing interval. Monitor renal function (if possible via serum creatinine, or estimate from clinical presentation: dehydration, oliguria, edema). Hepatic failure: liver metabolizes many drugs (opioids, benzodiazepines, certain antibiotics, NSAIDs). Cirrhosis/severe hepatitis impairs metabolism; reduce dose 25-50%, extend dosing intervals. Avoid: NSAIDs (risk GI bleed, renal failure in cirrhotic patients), acetaminophen if overdose risk (liver damage). Prioritize: drugs renally eliminated or requiring minimal metabolism.

### Drug Accumulation and Toxicity Syndromes

Digoxin toxicity: narrow therapeutic window; toxicity presents as: nausea, vomiting, visual disturbances (yellow vision), arrhythmias, confusion. Causes: renal failure (reduced elimination), dehydration, hypokalemia, drug interactions (NSAIDs, verapamil increase levels). Management: check digoxin level, correct hypokalemia, hold dose if toxicity suspected, administer digoxin-specific antibody fragments if severe. Aminoglycoside toxicity: nephrotoxicity (rising creatinine, oliguria), ototoxicity (hearing loss, vertigo). Risk factors: prolonged therapy, renal impairment, concurrent nephrotoxic drugs (NSAIDs, contrast agents). Monitor: serum creatinine, hearing function if possible; limit duration, reduce dose for renal impairment. Opioid toxicity: respiratory depression (slowed breathing, confusion), miosis (pinpoint pupils), hypotension. Reversal: naloxone 0.4-2mg IV/IM (repeat every 2-3 minutes if needed). Assess for other drug use (benzodiazepines, alcohol) increasing depression risk.

## Extreme-Age Dosing: Neonatal Through Geriatric

Medication metabolism changes dramatically across the human lifespan. Neonates, premature infants, and elderly patients require fundamentally different dosing than young adults. Applying standard adult doses without adjustment causes either treatment failure (underdosing) or life-threatening toxicity (overdosing).

### Neonatal and Infant Dosing (Birth to 12 Months)

Neonates differ from older children in multiple ways: immature hepatic metabolism (liver enzymes not fully developed until 3-6 months), immature renal function (glomerular filtration rate 20% of adults until 3 months), higher body water composition (80% vs. 60% in adults), lower plasma protein binding (affects drug distribution), and immature blood-brain barrier (increased CNS penetration for many drugs).

**General Principles:**
- **Premature infants (<37 weeks gestation):** Use chronological age (time since birth) for first 8-10 weeks, then transition to adjusted age (chronological age minus weeks born early)
- **Term neonates (0-4 weeks):** Use simple weight-based dosing; monitor for toxicity closely
- **Infants 1-12 months:** Begin transitioning toward older pediatric dosing by 3-6 months as organ maturity improves

Pediatric dosing transitions gradually toward adult dosing as children grow and organs mature.

(Weight in kg) × (dose per kg) = total dose in mg

### Geriatric Dosing (Age 65+ Years)

Elderly patients have dramatically altered pharmacokinetics: reduced hepatic metabolism (30-50% slower drug clearance), reduced renal function (creatinine clearance declines 8-10 mL/min per decade after age 40), altered body composition (increased fat, decreased water—lipophilic drugs accumulate), decreased plasma protein binding (increases free drug levels), and increased CNS sensitivity (greater sedation, confusion from psychoactive drugs).

**Creatinine Clearance Estimation (Cockcroft-Gault):**
CCr (mL/min) = [(140 - age) × weight in kg × (0.85 if female)] / (72 × serum creatinine)

For elderly patients, calculate actual creatinine clearance rather than assuming normal renal function from serum creatinine alone.

**Geriatric Dosing Principles:**
- **Start low, go slow:** Begin at 25-50% of standard adult dose, titrate upward based on response and tolerance
- **Reduce frequency:** Longer dosing intervals (example: digoxin every 36-48 hours instead of daily)
- **Monitor drug levels:** For narrow-therapeutic-window drugs (digoxin, warfarin, phenytoin), check levels 1-2 weeks after initiation and after any change
- **Account for renal function:** Reduce doses by percentage equivalent to creatinine clearance reduction

### Interaction Decision Tree for Polypharmacy

When patient on multiple medications requires additional drug:

1. **Check for absolute contraindications** (avoid entirely): SSRI + MAOI, warfarin + NSAIDs (unless critical indication and close monitoring), opioid + benzodiazepine (unless for end-of-life comfort)
2. **Check renal/hepatic function:** If impaired, adjust doses of drugs metabolized by affected organ
3. **Check for additive toxicity:** Two drugs with similar side effects (example: two anticholinergics) increase risk significantly
4. **Check serum drug levels:** For narrow-therapeutic-window drugs (digoxin, phenytoin, lithium, warfarin), obtain baseline level if possible
5. **Monitor for clinical changes:** Increased side effects, loss of efficacy, new symptoms suggesting toxicity
6. **Plan monitoring:** Which labs (electrolytes, renal function, digoxin level) and timing (3-5 days post-start for interactions, then periodically)

## Alternative Administration Routes and Field Adaptations

In resource-limited settings, oral medications may be unavailable, unabsorbed, or refused. Understanding alternative routes (rectal, sublingual, transdermal, topical, IM, IV) and dosing adjustments expands therapeutic options.

### Rectal Administration (Suppository/Liquid)

**Advantages:** Bypasses GI absorption variability, useful in vomiting/unconsciousness, blood supply rich (systemic absorption)
**Disadvantages:** Patient reluctance, less precise dosing than oral, potential rectal irritation

**Suitable Medications:**
- **Acetaminophen:** 325-650mg suppository every 4-6 hours (same dosing as oral)
- **Ibuprofen:** 200-400mg suppository (weaker absorption than oral; may need higher dose)
- **Diazepam:** 5-10mg rectal gel for seizure control (faster than IV administration)
- **Morphine:** 10-20mg rectal dosing every 4 hours (variable absorption; may need 1.5× oral dose)
- **Misoprostol:** 800mcg rectal for uterine atony (alternative to IM/IV)

**Technique:** Lubricate suppository with water-soluble lubricant or saliva, insert past anal sphincter (2-3 cm), retain 15-30 minutes minimum before bowel movement. Absorption occurs over 15-60 minutes.

### Sublingual Administration

**Advantages:** Rich blood supply under tongue, rapid absorption, bypasses GI metabolism
**Disadvantages:** Limited drug options suitable for sublingual, patient must keep medication under tongue 2-5 minutes, salivary clearance variable

**Suitable Medications:**
- **Nitroglycerin:** 0.3-0.6mg sublingual tablet for angina (onset 2-3 minutes; remains standard field treatment)
- **Misoprostol:** 200mcg sublingual tablets for uterine atony (slower than IV, but acceptable alternative; takes 5-10 minutes)
- **Lorazepam:** 1-2mg sublingual for acute anxiety/agitation (faster absorption than oral; useful for unconscious patients if given as liquid)

**Technique:** Place tablet/liquid under tongue, patient holds without swallowing for 2-5 minutes. Do not give water. Saliva accelerates dissolution. Once dissolved, swallowing normal.

### Transdermal Patches

**Advantages:** Continuous delivery, bypasses GI metabolism, reduced dosing frequency
**Disadvantages:** Slow onset (hours to days), variable skin absorption, limited drug options available

**Available Options:**
- **Fentanyl patch:** 12-100 mcg/hour for severe chronic pain (onset 12-24 hours; not suitable for acute pain)
- **Nicotine patch:** 7-21 mg/24h for smoking cessation (onset hours; chronic use only)
- **Scopolamine patch:** 1.5mg over 72 hours for motion sickness (reduces nausea)

**Application:** Clean, dry skin; apply to arm, behind ear, or torso. Rotate sites with each new patch (prevents skin irritation). Do not cut patches (releases entire dose at once). Onset slow—unsuitable for acute emergencies.

### Intramuscular (IM) Administration

**Advantages:** Rapid absorption (10-30 minutes for most drugs), bypasses GI issues, suitable for unconscious patients
**Disadvantages:** Tissue irritation, nerve/vessel injury risk, limited volume (max 3-5 mL per site)

**Common IM Medications:**
- **Epinephrine:** 0.3-0.5mg IM for anaphylaxis (lateral thigh, vastus lateralis muscle)
- **Morphine:** 5-10mg IM every 3-4 hours for pain (absorption faster than oral)
- **Magnesium sulfate:** 10g IM (5g in each buttock) for eclampsia
- **Antibiotics:** Many (gentamicin, ceftriaxone) available for IM use when IV unavailable
- **Lorazepam:** 2-4mg IM for acute agitation/seizure

**Technique:** Clean injection site with alcohol wipe. Insert needle perpendicular at 90 degrees into muscle (vastus lateralis thigh safest, deltoid arm alternative, gluteal buttock less preferred—sciatic nerve injury risk). Aspirate to confirm no blood vessel. Inject slowly. Withdraw needle. Pressure with gauze 1-2 minutes.

**IM Dosing Differences:** Most IM drugs approximately equal to IV dosing, but absorption slower (onset 10-30 minutes vs. immediate IV). Some drugs concentrate in muscle (morphine lasts longer IM than IV; gentamicin IM absorption sufficient for most infections).

### Intravenous (IV) Administration

**Advantages:** Immediate onset, precise dosing, useful for medications requiring specific timing
**Disadvantages:** Infection risk (septic thrombophlebitis, bacteremia), tissue damage if infiltration, requires sterile technique

**Common IV Medications in Field Conditions:**
- **Dextrose 50% (D50):** 25 mL IV for hypoglycemia (immediate glucose repletion)
- **Epinephrine:** 1mg IV (1:10,000 dilution) every 3-5 minutes for cardiac arrest
- **Magnesium sulfate:** 4-6g IV over 20 minutes for eclampsia (see <a href="../obstetric-emergencies.html">Obstetric Emergencies</a> for eclamptic seizure protocols)
- **Oxytocin:** 10 units IV in 500mL saline for hemorrhage (infuse over hours, not bolus) or IM for postpartum hemorrhage control (see <a href="../obstetric-emergencies.html">Obstetric Emergencies</a> and <a href="../midwifery.html">Midwifery & Childbirth</a> for dosing specifics)
- **Ergot alkaloids (ergotamine/methylergonovine):** 0.2-0.4mg IM for postpartum hemorrhage control (contraindicated in hypertension or preeclampsia)
- **Normal saline:** Fluid resuscitation for hemorrhage/shock
- **Ceftriaxone:** 1-2g IV every 12 hours for serious infections

**IV Access Maintenance:** Flush IV line with saline every 4-6 hours if not running continuous infusion (prevents clot formation). Rotate peripheral IV sites every 72 hours (reduces infection risk). Check for infiltration (swelling around IV site, coolness, pain) and replace if present.

**Emergency IV Push Technique (If No Infusion Pump):** Some medications (epinephrine, calcium gluconate, naloxone) given as rapid "push":
1. Draw medication in syringe
2. Clean IV port with alcohol wipe
3. Insert needle into port, withdraw slightly to confirm blood return (confirms IV position)
4. Inject medication over 3-5 seconds (rapid injection)
5. Flush with 10mL saline immediately after

### Topical Administration

**Advantages:** Local effect, minimal systemic absorption, suitable for wound care
**Disadvantages:** Limited systemic effect, variable absorption

**Common Topical Uses:**
- **Antibiotic ointment (bacitracin, Neosporin):** On wounds, prevents infection
- **Hydrocortisone cream (1%):** Allergic rashes, dermatitis
- **Iodine solution:** Wound antisepsis, prep for procedures
- **Lidocaine cream (5%):** Topical anesthesia (not through intact skin; useful for procedure prep)

### When to Stop Using a Medication Immediately

These are stop signs, not "wait and see" symptoms. Hold the next dose, review the total daily amount from all sources, and do not re-dose until the cause is clear.

- **Anaphylaxis signs:** rapid heartbeat, hives spreading, tongue/throat swelling, wheezing → STOP, inject epinephrine IM immediately.
- **Severe allergic rash:** if spreading rapidly, blistering, or involving face → STOP immediately; risk of Stevens-Johnson syndrome (life-threatening).
- **Vomiting/abdominal pain (acute on medication):** suggests acute toxicity or adverse effect → STOP, re-assess diagnosis.
- **Respiratory depression:** respiratory rate <8/min, altered consciousness with opioids → STOP, give naloxone IM immediately.
- **Severe hypotension:** systolic <80mmHg on cardiac/blood pressure meds → STOP, elevate legs, IV fluids.
- **Uncontrolled bleeding:** on anticoagulants/NSAIDs, observe blood in urine/stool, severe bruising → STOP, elevate, apply pressure.
- **Severe liver/kidney symptoms:** jaundice (yellow skin), dark urine, oliguria (minimal urine output) → STOP, hydrate, assess for organ failure.

In all cases: document what happened, when it started, what was given, patient response, and decision to stop or modify. Use this information to guide future treatment decisions.

## Extreme-Age Dosing: Neonatal Through Geriatric Calculations

Extreme age groups (neonates <7 days, infants, elderly >75 years) have pharmacokinetically distinct requirements from older children and younger adults. Safe dosing requires understanding developmental organ function and physiological changes.

### Neonatal and Premature Infant Dosing (Birth to 7 days)

Neonates have immature liver and kidney function; metabolism and elimination radically slower than older infants. General principle: reduce standard infant doses by 50-75% and extend dosing intervals by 50-100%.

**Metabolic immaturity factors:**
- **Liver maturity:** Cytochrome P450 enzymes develop gradually; full function by 3-6 months. Neonates (<7 days) have approximately 30-40% of adult metabolic capacity.
- **Renal function:** Glomerular filtration rate (GFR) approximately 30-40% of adult rate at birth; reaches adult function by 3-6 months.
- **Plasma protein binding:** Reduced binding affinity increases free drug concentration; higher toxicity risk.

**Neonatal weight-based dosing adjustment:**

| Age Group | Dosing Adjustment vs. Older Infant | Typical Interval Extension | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| Preterm <32 weeks | Reduce 50-75%; extend intervals | Q12-24h instead of Q6-8h | Amoxicillin 10-15 mg/kg Q12h vs. 25-50 mg/kg Q8h |
| Preterm 32-37 weeks | Reduce 40-60% | Q8-12h instead of Q6h | Gentamicin 5 mg/kg Q12h vs. 7.5 mg/kg Q8h |
| Full-term neonate 0-7 days | Reduce 25-50% | Q8-12h instead of Q6-8h | Cephalexin 25-30 mg/kg Q12h vs. 25-50 mg/kg Q6h |
| Infant 7 days-3 months | Reduce 25-40% | Q8h instead of Q6h | Standard infant dosing with longer intervals |

**Specific neonatal antibiotics (illustrative examples):**

| Drug | Preterm Dose | Full-term Neonate | Age 1-3 months | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Penicillin G | 25,000-50,000 units/kg Q12h | 50,000 units/kg Q8-12h | 50,000 units/kg Q6-8h | Critical: gram-positive coverage for GBS (Group B Strep) |
| Gentamicin | 5 mg/kg Q24h (or Q18h if preterm <32 weeks) | 5 mg/kg Q12h | 7.5 mg/kg Q8h | Monitor renal function closely; ototoxicity risk |
| Cefotaxime | 50 mg/kg Q12h | 50 mg/kg Q8-12h | 50 mg/kg Q6-8h | Preferred for CNS infections (meningitis) |
| Ampicillin | 25-50 mg/kg Q12h (preterm); 25-50 mg/kg Q8h (term) | 25-50 mg/kg Q8-12h | 25-50 mg/kg Q6h | Adjust for gestational age |

**Critical monitoring for neonates:**
- Serum creatinine and urine output (oliguria suggests kidney impairment)
- Seizures (may indicate drug toxicity or hypoglycemia)
- Feeding tolerance (diarrhea, vomiting suggests GI upset or overgrowth)
- Jaundice (some drugs increase bilirubin displacement, raising free levels)

:::warning
Neonatal dosing errors carry extremely high mortality. When in doubt, use lowest dose, extend interval, and monitor renal function closely. Do NOT use standard infant dosing in neonates <7 days without explicit dose adjustment. Consult references if available.
:::

### Geriatric Dosing (Elderly >75 years)

Elderly patients have altered pharmacokinetics from decreased organ function, reduced body composition, and increased disease burden. General principle: start lower, go slower.

**Geriatric physiological changes:**
- **Body composition:** Increased body fat (hydrophobic drugs accumulate), decreased total body water (hydrophilic drugs more concentrated)
- **Kidney function:** GFR declines ~1% per year after age 30; 75-year-old typical GFR ~50% of young adult
- **Liver metabolism:** Modest decline; clearance may be 20-40% lower than younger adults
- **Protein binding:** Altered due to malnutrition, disease; increases free drug concentration

**Geriatric weight-based adjustments:**

| Drug Category | Standard Adult Dose | Geriatric Adjustment (Age 75+) | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| Antibiotics (renal eliminated) | Fluoroquinolones 500mg BID | Reduce 25-50% OR extend interval (500mg daily instead of BID) | Reduced GFR; drug accumulation risk |
| ACE inhibitors | Lisinopril 10mg daily | Start 5mg, titrate slowly to 10mg as tolerated | Increased sensitivity; hypotension risk |
| Benzodiazepines | Diazepam 5-10mg TID | Reduce 50-75% (2-5mg daily-BID); preferred: shorter-acting agents (lorazepam) | Prolonged CNS effects; fall/cognitive impairment risk |
| Opioids | Morphine 10-15mg Q4h | Start 5mg Q6h, titrate cautiously | Increased sensitivity; respiratory depression/constipation severe |
| Digoxin | 0.5-1mg loading, 0.25mg daily maintenance | Reduce 25-50% (0.125mg daily typical for elderly) | Narrow therapeutic window; renal elimination reduced |

**Geriatric-specific dosing approach:**
1. Start with 50% of standard adult dose
2. Titrate slowly (increase every 5-7 days rather than every 1-2 days)
3. Monitor closely for adverse effects (dizziness, confusion, falls, GI upset)
4. Assess for drug interactions (elderly average 5-8 concurrent medications)
5. Reduce dose for renal impairment (routine in elderly)
6. Document blood pressure, heart rate, cognition baseline and with each adjustment

**Common geriatric prescribing errors:**
- Using standard adult doses in elderly → toxicity
- Combining multiple drugs without interaction screening → adverse events
- Rapid dose escalation → intolerance
- Ignoring renal function changes → accumulation
- Using long-acting formulations in cognitive decline → overdose risk

### Dosing in Intermediate Age Groups (Adolescent, Young Adult)

Adolescents 12-16 years: transitional pharmacokinetics; most tolerate adult dosing if weight >40kg. Verify reference specifically—some drugs require extended pediatric dosing into older adolescence.

Young adults 18-50 years: standard reference dosing applies if no renal/hepatic impairment or concurrent disease.

**Quick reference for weight-based transition:**
- <20kg: use pediatric dosing (weight-based mg/kg)
- 20-40kg: use pediatric dosing or adult dosing (whichever lower); verify reference
- >40kg with normal organ function: standard adult dosing acceptable

## Drug Interaction Matrix and Dangerous Combinations

Beyond individual interactions covered above, this section provides quick-reference combinations to absolutely avoid or manage carefully.

### High-Risk Combinations to Avoid

| Drug 1 | Drug 2 | Mechanism | Clinical Consequence | Management |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Warfarin | NSAIDs (especially long-acting) | Inhibited platelet function + displacement from protein binding | Supratherapeutic INR; GI/intracerebral hemorrhage | AVOID if possible; if necessary, reduce warfarin 20-30%, check INR 3-5 days, education on bleeding signs |
| ACE inhibitors or ARBs | NSAIDs + Diuretics ("triple whammy") | Reduced renal perfusion; hyperkalemia | Acute kidney injury; hyperkalemia-induced arrhythmias | Avoid triple combination; if must use, reduce diuretic, check K+ and creatinine at 3-5 days, monitor for oliguria |
| SSRIs (high dose) or Tramadol | Dextromethorphan or other serotonergics | Serotonin excess | Serotonin syndrome (fever, rigidity, autonomic instability); mortality 3-5% | AVOID combination; if unavoidable, use lowest doses, monitor for tremor/hyperthermia, have cooling measures ready, discontinue immediately if syndrome suspected |
| Metronidazole | Alcohol (including alcohol-based cough syrups) | Disulfiram-like reaction (blocks aldehyde dehydrogenase) | Severe nausea, vomiting, headache, facial flushing, possible cardiovascular collapse (rare) | EDUCATE patient to absolutely avoid alcohol during and 48 hours after metronidazole; check cough syrup formulation (many contain alcohol) |
| Gentamicin | Loop diuretics (furosemide) | Both toxic to inner ear; additive ototoxicity | Hearing loss, tinnitus, vertigo; often permanent | Use lowest effective doses; extend gentamicin dosing interval; check hearing baseline and periodically; counsel on ototoxicity risk |
| Macrolide antibiotics (azithromycin) | Antiarrhythmics (especially QT-prolonging: sotalol, amiodarone) OR certain antihistamines | Additive QT prolongation | Torsades de Pointes (polymorphic VT); syncope; sudden cardiac death | AVOID combination; baseline ECG (check QT <0.44s), avoid macrolides in known QT disease; if used, check electrolytes (K+ >3.5, Mg2+ >2), monitor closely |
| Doxycycline | Dairy, Iron supplements, Antacids | Chelation reduces absorption | 50% reduction in antibiotic level; treatment failure | Space doses: doxycycline 2h before/after dairy/iron/antacids; educate patient; consider fluoroquinolone alternative if timing impossible |

### Dangerous Dosing Combinations (Safe Drugs, Wrong Amounts Together)

- **Acetaminophen + opioid combination products (Tylenol #3, Vicodin, Percocet):** Patients inadvertently overdose on acetaminophen by taking combination product + additional acetaminophen for fever. Maximum daily acetaminophen should never exceed 3-4g; combination products limit options. Solution: prescribe acetaminophen and opioid separately; educate on max daily acetaminophen from all sources.

- **Aspirin overdose:** Exceeding 4g daily causes salicylate toxicity (respiratory alkalosis progressing to metabolic acidosis, hyperthermia, confusion). Solution: verify total aspirin dose from all sources including cold remedies; educate on max dosing; monitor pH if toxicity suspected.

:::warning
**Mandatory interaction screening before prescribing any new medication:** (1) Verify all current medications patient taking (including over-the-counter, herbal, illicit if disclosed). (2) Scan for major interactions (check reference list above + any available drug interaction resource). (3) If serious interaction identified: choose alternative medication, OR reduce doses of one/both drugs, OR extend dosing intervals, OR monitor closely for toxicity. (4) Document decision and patient education given.
:::

## Alternative Administration Routes and Adjusted Dosing

When oral or standard IM/IV routes unavailable or ineffective, alternative routes deliver medication with dose adjustments accounting for bioavailability differences.

### Rectal Administration (Suppository or Liquid Enema)

**Indications:** Patient cannot swallow (vomiting, unconscious, post-operative); faster than oral absorption; avoids first-pass hepatic metabolism.

**Bioavailability:** 70-90% absorption compared to oral (higher peak levels). Many drugs available as suppositories; liquid formulations can be administered rectally if suppository unavailable.

**Common rectal medications and adjusted doses:**

| Drug | Oral Dose (Standard) | Rectal Dose | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Acetaminophen | 325-650mg Q4-6h | 325-650mg Q4-6h (same) | Higher absorption rectally; monitor total not exceed 4g/day |
| Ibuprofen | 400-600mg Q6h | 400-600mg Q6h (may reduce 10-20%) | Rectal slightly better absorbed; GI upset less |
| Diazepam | 5-10mg PO | 5-10mg rectal (acute seizure: 10mg rectally effective) | Rapid absorption; used for acute seizure termination |
| Metronidazole | 250-500mg TID PO | 500mg BID rectal | Excellent rectal absorption; useful if cannot tolerate PO |
| Indomethacin | 25-50mg BID-TID PO | 25-50mg BID rectal | Used for patent ductus arteriosus in neonates |

**Administration technique:**
1. Position patient on left side
2. If suppository: insert past internal sphincter with lubrication (water-soluble gel preferred; oil impairs absorption)
3. If liquid: use syringe without needle or enema catheter; deliver 30-60mL slowly into rectum
4. Retain 15-30 minutes if possible (difficult with stimulant effect of some drugs)
5. Clean area with damp cloth afterward

**Cautions:** Hemorrhoids, rectal fissures, colitis contraindicate rectal route. Diarrhea reduces absorption; wait until bowel stable. Some patients refuse rectal medication—document refusal; respect dignity.

### Sublingual Administration (Under the Tongue)

**Indications:** Rapid absorption bypassing first-pass hepatic metabolism; useful for: nitroglycerin (angina), lorazepam (acute anxiety), misoprostol (uterine atony).

**Bioavailability:** 75-90% absorption; peak levels in 5-15 minutes (much faster than oral). Avoid swallowing—drug must dissolve under tongue.

**Common sublingual medications:**

| Drug | Typical Sublingual Dose | Onset | Clinical Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nitroglycerin | 0.3-0.6mg SL tab or spray | 2-5 min | Acute angina; repeat Q5min x 3 doses, then call for help |
| Lorazepam | 1-2mg SL (tab crushed under tongue) | 10-15 min | Acute anxiety, agitation; faster than oral |
| Misoprostol | 400-800 mcg SL | 5-15 min | Uterine atony; alternative if IM access not available |

**Administration technique:**
1. Place tablet under tongue
2. DO NOT swallow
3. Allow to dissolve completely (5-15 minutes depending on drug)
4. Rinse mouth afterward
5. Monitor effect; document time and response

**Limitations:** Bitter taste may cause nausea; dry mouth reduces absorption. Some formulations (liquid, spray) better tolerated than tablets.

### Transdermal Patches (Skin Application)

**Indications:** Continuous delivery; avoids GI issues; often smaller dose needed (dose reduction 25-50% vs. oral due to avoidance of first-pass metabolism and steady-state delivery).

**Bioavailability:** Variable (40-85% depending on drug), but provides sustained levels over 24-72 hours. No dose spikes/troughs.

**Common transdermal medications and dosing:**

| Drug | Typical Patch Dose | Duration | Clinical Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fentanyl | 25-100 mcg/h patch | 72 hours | Opioid-tolerant patients only; equivalent to 90-360mg daily morphine |
| Nitroglycerin | 0.3-0.6 mg/h patch | 24 hours (10-14 hours nitrate-free) | Remove 10-14h daily to prevent tolerance |
| Scopolamine (hyoscine) | 0.5mg patch | 72 hours | Motion sickness, secretion reduction (terminal sedation) |
| Lidocaine | 5% patch, 1-3 patches daily | 12 hours on, 12 hours off | Localized pain; neuropathic pain relief |

**Application technique:**
1. Cleanse skin with soap/water; dry completely
2. Apply to hairless area (chest, upper arm preferred; abdomen acceptable)
3. Press firmly for 30 seconds
4. Note application time; do not massage
5. Rotate site each application (prevent skin irritation)
6. Remove old patch before applying new (prevent overdose)
7. Fold used patch in half (drug side inward) and dispose safely (opioid patches retain significant drug after use—theft/accidental exposure risk if discarded improperly)

**Bioavailability note:** Patches deliver through skin barrier (slower onset); not suitable for acute situations. Onset 6-12 hours typical; not interchangeable with immediate-release formulations.

:::tip
Transdermal patches provide superior medication adherence (patient only thinks about it every 3 days) and steady dosing compared to multiple daily pills. In collapse scenarios, preserving patch supply protects patients with opioid tolerance, cardiac patients requiring nitrates, and others dependent on continuous dosing.
:::

### Topical Administration (Skin Application - Non-Transdermal)

Topical agents deliver medication to skin/superficial tissues without systemic absorption (goal is local effect).

**Common topical medications:**
- **Hydrocortisone cream (1%):** anti-inflammatory for dermatitis; apply BID-TID
- **Neomycin ointment:** antibiotic for minor wounds; apply BID-TID
- **Acyclovir cream (5%):** antiviral for herpes; apply Q4-6h
- **Clotrimazole cream (1%):** antifungal for tinea; apply BID x 2-4 weeks
- **Lidocaine cream (2.5%) or patch:** local anesthetic for pain
- **Salicylic acid ointment:** keratolytic for warts, calluses

**Application technique:**
1. Cleanse area, pat dry
2. Apply thin layer (coin-sized amount typical for small area)
3. Rub in gently; do not occlude unless specifically instructed
4. Wash hands after (if not applying to hands)
5. Reapply 2-4 times daily depending on indication

**Systemic absorption consideration:** Some topical agents (corticosteroids, salicylic acid) absorb systemically if applied to large areas or under occlusion. High-potency steroids on face or intertriginous areas risk atrophy; low-potency preferred for sensitive areas.

### Inhalation Administration (Aerosolized/Inhaled Medications)

Inhalation delivers medication directly to respiratory tract (lungs/bronchi); rapid onset for respiratory symptoms.

**Common inhalable medications:**
- **Albuterol (salbutamol):** bronchodilator for asthma/COPD; 1-2 puffs Q4-6h
- **Ipratropium:** anticholinergic bronchodilator for COPD; 2 puffs QID
- **Corticosteroid inhalers (fluticasone, budesonide):** anti-inflammatory for asthma maintenance; 1-2 puffs BID-TID
- **Epinephrine (racemic):** croup treatment; nebulized

**Inhaler use technique:**
1. Shake metered-dose inhaler (MDI) vigorously (30 seconds)
2. Attach spacer if available (increases lung deposition, especially in children/elderly)
3. Exhale completely
4. Place mouthpiece in mouth or use spacer; seal lips
5. Press canister and simultaneously begin slow deep inhalation over 4-6 seconds
6. Hold breath 10 seconds
7. Exhale slowly
8. Wait 1-2 minutes between puffs if multiple doses

**Storage:** Keep at room temperature; avoid freezing (can damage actuator). Check that mouthpiece not clogged; test spray before patient use.

**Dosing note:** Inhalation doses 10-20x lower than systemic due to high bioavailability in lungs. Do NOT substitute inhaled dose for oral/IV equivalent.
