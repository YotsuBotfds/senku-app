## Introduction to Rainwater Harvesting

Rainwater harvesting is the collection and storage of precipitation for later use. In many climates, annual rainfall exceeds household water needs significantly. Capturing and storing this "free" resource reduces dependence on wells, municipal systems, and trucked water—critical in low-connectivity environments or communities facing water scarcity.

A household system can supply 30-100% of water needs depending on climate, roof area, and storage capacity. A community system can provide drinking water, irrigation, and emergency reserves. This guide covers roof catchment, intake sizing, collection yield, conveyance, overflow path planning, filtration, treatment for potability, and system sizing. It is the right guide when the question starts with collecting rain, catching runoff, routing gutters, or preventing collection overflow. Daily-use budgets, reserve duration, and per-person rationing belong in the rationing guide. Tank cleaning, stale taste, algae, slime, and container-condition troubleshooting belong in the tank-maintenance guide.

## Part One: Understanding the System

### System Overview

A complete rainwater harvesting system has five components:

1. **Catchment surface:** Roof that collects precipitation
2. **Conveyance:** Gutters, downspouts, and pipes moving water to storage
3. **First-flush diverter:** Device removing initial rain (washes roof dust and debris)
4. **Storage:** Tanks or cisterns holding collected water
5. **Treatment and use:** Filtration, disinfection, and distribution to household/community

### Rainfall Data and Local Factors

Annual rainfall varies dramatically by geography. Research local historical data to size your system.

**Global rainfall ranges:**
- Tropical regions: 60–400+ inches/year (1500–10,000+ mm)
- Temperate zones: 20–60 inches/year (500–1500 mm)
- Arid regions: <10 inches/year (<250 mm)
- Mediterranean: 15–40 inches/year (400–1000 mm)

**Finding rainfall data:**
- National weather service / meteorological agency data (most reliable)
- Agricultural extension offices
- Historic weather station records
- Local farmers and long-time residents (traditional knowledge)

**Seasonal distribution matters:**
- If 80% of rain falls in one season, storage must cover the dry season
- Bi-modal rainfall (two wet seasons) allows smaller storage
- Year-round rain simplifies system design

### Water Demand Estimation

Calculate daily per-capita water needs:

**Minimum (survival):** 1 gallon/person/day (drinking + cooking only)

**Hygiene baseline:** 3–5 gallons/person/day (drinking, cooking, basic washing)

**Comfortable living:** 15–25 gallons/person/day (above + laundry, bathing)

**Agricultural use (irrigation):** 500–5000 gallons/day for a vegetable garden (depends on garden size and climate)

**Livestock:** 5–10 gallons per animal per day

**Example household calculation:**
- Family of 4 at 5 gallons/person/day = 20 gallons/day = 7,300 gallons/year
- Garden irrigation (2,000 sq ft): 3,000–5,000 gallons/year
- **Total annual need:** ~10,000–12,000 gallons

This calculation guides storage sizing and feasibility assessment.

## Part Two: Roof Catchment Design

### Catchment Yield Calculation

**Formula:** Water collected = roof area × rainfall × runoff coefficient

Where:
- **Roof area** = horizontal projection (not sloped surface)
- **Rainfall** = annual precipitation in inches
- **Runoff coefficient** = fraction of rain reaching storage (accounts for loss to evaporation, splash, gutter spillage)

**Runoff coefficients by material:**
- Metal (tin, corrugated steel, aluminum): 0.80–0.95 (excellent)
- Tile or slate: 0.75–0.90
- Asphalt shingles: 0.65–0.80 (variable; new is better than old)
- Concrete/sealed: 0.85–0.95
- Wood shingles: 0.60–0.75 (absorbs some water; deteriorates)
- Cloth (thatched): 0.20–0.50 (very low; unsuitable)

**Example calculation:**
- Roof area: 1,500 sq ft
- Annual rainfall: 40 inches
- Runoff coefficient: 0.80 (asphalt shingles)
- Yield = 1,500 × 40 × 0.80 = 48,000 gallons/year

This roof can supply 48,000 ÷ 365 = 131 gallons/day—well above typical household needs.

### Roof Material Selection and Improvement

**Best materials for potable water:**
- Metal (standing seam, corrugated): Excellent; expensive; lasts 50+ years; excellent runoff
- Concrete tile: Good; moderately expensive; heavy; excellent runoff
- Slate or ceramic tile: Excellent but very expensive

**Acceptable materials:**
- Quality asphalt shingles: Adequate if new; moderate cost; requires more frequent filter replacement
- Sealed or painted surfaces: Good if coating is food-safe and non-toxic

**Materials to avoid:**
- Creosote-treated wood: Toxic; contaminates water
- Painted wood with lead paint: Toxic
- Copper: Will turn water blue-green (copper compounds); safe but cosmetically undesirable
- Asbestos shingles: Avoid inhalation during installation; water safety is secondary concern
- Thatch or cloth: Insufficient runoff; introduces organic matter

**Improving existing roofs:**
1. If planning new installation, choose metal or quality tile
2. If converting existing roof, verify material; if asphalt shingles, change filters more frequently
3. New sealant/paint: Use food-grade, non-toxic paint or sealant

### Gutter and Downspout Design

**Gutter sizing:**
- Gutters capture water flowing off the roof slope
- Size based on rainfall intensity (peak hour rainfall, not average)
- Undersized gutters overflow; oversized ones collect debris

**Standard gutter sizes:**
- 4-inch (residential): Handles up to 1 inch/hour rainfall on 500–750 sq ft roof
- 5-inch (residential/light commercial): Handles up to 1 inch/hour on 750–1,200 sq ft
- 6-inch (commercial): Handles up to 1 inch/hour on 1,200–1,500+ sq ft

**Slope:** Gutters must slope toward downspouts (minimum 1/12 inch drop per linear foot, or roughly 1/4 inch per 3 feet).

**Downspout sizing:**
- One 2-inch downspout per 600 sq ft of roof (approximate)
- Larger roofs require multiple downspouts
- Downspout must deliver water without splashing or overflow

**Material choices:**
- Aluminum: Lightweight; affordable; durable; adequate for most climates
- Galvanized steel: Durable; slightly acidic (can leach zinc if unlined); gradually corrodes
- Vinyl/plastic: Lightweight; adequate; less durable than metal (degrades in UV)
- Copper: Excellent; expensive; beautiful; leaches copper (blue-green tint to water)
- Wood (lined): Traditional; requires maintenance; adequate if not deteriorating

**Gutter maintenance:**
- Remove leaves and debris quarterly
- Ensure downspouts drain fully (not clogged)
- Check slope annually; adjust if sagging
- Inspect for rust or damage before rainy season

### First-Flush Diverters

The first rain washes the roof, collecting dust, bird droppings, pollen, and debris. First-flush diverters exclude this contaminated initial water from storage.

**How they work:**
1. Water from roof enters diverter before reaching storage tank
2. Initial flow (typically 10–30 gallons) fills a chamber
3. When chamber is full, a float valve activates; clean water diverts to storage
4. After rain, the chamber drains slowly, preparing for the next storm

**Commercial first-flush diverters:**
- Disk-type: Float-activated; simple; moderate cost
- Tipping bucket: Mechanical; reliable; more expensive
- Combination valve: Integrated with overflow prevention

**DIY first-flush diverter:**
1. Install a 4-inch vertical pipe (6–12 feet tall) on the downspout before the tank
2. Cap the pipe; attach a manual drain valve at the bottom
3. After rain begins, water fills the vertical pipe (10–20 minutes)
4. Once settled, manually open drain valve to empty the pipe
5. After several minutes of rain, water quality is acceptable; close valve; begin collecting to tank

This crude but functional approach requires attention but costs nothing.

**Trigger rule of thumb:** Divert first 10 gallons per 1,000 sq ft of roof area. (For a 1,500 sq ft roof, divert the first 15 gallons.)

## Part Three: Storage Solutions

### Storage Volume Calculation

**Formula:** Required storage = (annual water need – recoverable water) × safety margin

Where safety margin accounts for drought, spillage, and consumption surges.

**Example:**
- Annual need: 10,000 gallons
- Recoverable rainfall: 48,000 gallons (from earlier calculation)
- Surplus is adequate; even 50% of annual need (5,000 gallons) provides 60 days of supply in a dry period

**For areas with seasonal dry periods:**
- If 6-month dry season: storage should hold 6 × 30-day × daily_need
- Example: 5 gallons/person/day × 4 people × 180 days = 3,600 gallons minimum
- Add 50% margin for safety: 5,400–6,000 gallons

### Tank Options

**Above-ground plastic tanks:**
- Cost: $0.50–2.00 per gallon
- Lifespan: 10–25 years
- Material: Food-grade polyethylene (HDPE #2)
- Advantages: Affordable; easily movable; various sizes (100–10,000 gallons)
- Disadvantages: Degrades in UV (requires shade); temperature fluctuations affect water quality; vulnerable to damage
- Best for: Temporary systems, small systems, rental properties

**Underground cisterns (buried tanks):**
- Cost: $1.00–3.00 per gallon (includes excavation)
- Lifespan: 20–50 years (depends on material)
- Advantages: Stable temperature; protected from UV; invisible; frost-protected in cold climates
- Disadvantages: Expensive installation; difficult to service; requires reliable drainage
- Best for: Permanent systems; high-rainfall areas; large volumes

**Ferrocement cisterns (reinforced concrete):**
- Cost: $0.80–2.00 per gallon (DIY construction is labor-intensive)
- Lifespan: 30–50+ years
- Material: Wire mesh and Portland cement
- Advantages: Very durable; local materials available in many regions; can be large
- Disadvantages: Requires skilled labor; concrete leaching raises pH (requires aging); repair is difficult
- Best for: Permanent systems; communities with concrete-building tradition

**Lined pits/ponds:**
- Cost: $0.20–0.50 per gallon (minimal material cost)
- Lifespan: 5–20 years (depends on liner)
- Material: Plastic HDPE liner, clay seal, or concrete
- Advantages: Extremely affordable; simple construction
- Disadvantages: Evaporation losses (15–30%/year); algae growth; vulnerable to animal contamination
- Best for: Large agricultural systems; temporary emergency storage

**Building materials:**
- Ceramic tiles lined with sand and gravel (traditional)
- Wooden barrels or vats (limited capacity; requires maintenance)
- Stone/brick structure with clay/cement sealing (labor-intensive; durable)

### Tank Placement and Setup

**Above-ground tank placement:**
- Elevated (on platform) for gravity-fed distribution, or use pump
- Shaded location (reduces algae; moderates temperature)
- On-site (minimizes conveyance loss)
- Stable ground (prevents settling/damage)
- Accessible for cleaning and maintenance

**Underground cistern placement:**
- Below frost line (in cold climates)
- Away from septic systems (minimum 50 feet; prevents contamination)
- Below highest water table (prevents groundwater seeping in)
- Accessible via inspection/cleanout opening (4-inch diameter minimum)

### Tank Features

**Inlet:**
- Strainer screen (¼-inch mesh) removes large debris
- Float valve or overflow pipe (prevents overfilling)
- Cleanout access (cover for sediment removal)

**Outlet:**
- Bottom drain valve (for sediment removal and emergency drainage)
- Upper outlet (1–2 feet below water surface) for normal water withdrawal (leaves sediment at bottom)
- Separate outlet for pump intake

**Overflow:**
- Pipe or channel directing excess water away from foundation
- Directs to garden, other tank, or greywater system
- Prevents tank rupture and foundation water damage

**Venting:**
- Small vent opening (1/4-inch diameter) covered with fine mesh
- Allows air in/out during filling/draining
- Prevents vacuum during drainage

**Inspection access:**
- Removable cover or access port (4–6 inches diameter)
- Allows internal inspection, cleaning, sediment removal
- Must be secured to prevent debris/animal entry

## Part Four: Filtration and Treatment

### Pre-Storage Filtration

Removing particles before storage reduces sediment, extends tank life, and improves water quality.

**Coarse strainer (first stage):**
- ¼-inch mesh screen in downspout or first-flush device
- Removes leaves, insects, large debris
- Easy cleaning; prevents filter clogging

**Sand/gravel filter (second stage):**
- 12–18 inches sand (fine to medium)
- Removes fine particles, some pathogens, some organic matter
- Simple construction:
  1. Place fine gravel (1–2 inches) at bottom
  2. Add medium sand (8–10 inches)
  3. Add fine sand (2–4 inches)
  4. Allows water to flow through slowly (1 gallon per minute or less)
- Clean by backflushing (reverse water flow) when flow slows

**Activated charcoal filter (optional; improves taste/odor):**
- 6–12 inches activated charcoal (granular or chunk)
- Removes chlorine, odor, some dissolved contaminants
- Replace annually or when water taste degrades
- DIY: Spread charcoal in a tray; expose to air for 24 hours before use

### Post-Storage Filtration (Before Use)

For irrigation or outdoor use, coarse filtration is sufficient. For household/drinking use, additional treatment is necessary.

**Fine sediment filter:**
- 5-micron or 1-micron filter cartridge
- Removes particles, fine silt, some bacteria/protozoa
- Requires replacement every 3–6 months (depends on sediment load)
- Cost: $5–25 per cartridge

**Cloth/mesh filter:**
- Washable cloth or mesh (25–100 microns)
- Reusable; zero replacement cost
- Less effective than ceramic but adequate for pre-filtration
- Wash weekly during use

### Treatment for Potable Water

:::warning
**Safety critical:** Untreated rainwater may contain pathogens (bacteria, viruses, protozoa), nitrates, and other contaminants. Never drink untreated rainwater unless testing confirms safety.
:::

**Disinfection methods:**

**Boiling:**
- Kills most pathogens (requires 1 minute at rolling boil; longer at altitude)
- Simple; fuel-intensive; suitable for small quantities
- Removes some dissolved gases but not chemicals

**UV light:**
- Damages DNA of pathogens; kills most bacteria and viruses; less effective on protozoa
- Requires electricity; no residual protection
- Combine with filtration for best results

**Chlorine:**
- Kills most bacteria/viruses; partially effective on protozoa
- Add 2 ppm (parts per million) for disinfection: ~8 drops of 6% household bleach per gallon
- Allow 30 minutes contact time
- Leaves residual protection in system
- Taste can be mitigated by allowing water to sit exposed to air 12+ hours

**Iodine:**
- Portable; kills most pathogens
- Add 5 ppm: ~4 drops of 2% iodine solution per quart
- Allow 30 minutes contact time
- Not suitable for long-term use (health concerns); best for emergency/portable supplies

**Solar disinfection (SODIS):**
- Fill clear bottles; expose to direct sunlight 6+ hours
- UV radiation kills pathogens
- Very slow; requires many bottles; suitable for emergency small quantities only

**Combination approach (most effective):**
1. Pre-filtration (sand/gravel) and first-flush diversion
2. Storage (settling removes some particles)
3. Fine filtration (5-micron cartridge)
4. Disinfection (chlorine or boiling)
5. Post-disinfection settling (for chlorine, optional but improves taste)

### Testing for Safety

**Visual/sensory inspection:**
- Color: Clear (cloudiness indicates particles)
- Odor: Fresh or neutral (sulfur, musty, or algal odors indicate problems)
- Taste: Neutral (after chlorine is aerated away; off-tastes suggest contamination)

**Simple chemical tests:**
- **Nitrate test strips:** Excess nitrates indicate animal/septic contamination
- **Hardness test:** High hardness indicates mineral content (not necessarily harmful but affects taste)
- **pH test strips:** Neutral pH (6.5–7.5) is ideal; low pH indicates corrosion risk, high pH indicates hardness

**Microbial testing (if accessible):**
- Bacterial culture (fecal coliform, E. coli): Most reliable safety indicator
- Test via local health department or private lab
- Safe water: <1 CFU/100ml fecal coliform (most national standards)

**Frequency of testing:**
- New system: Test weekly until 3 consecutive safe results
- Established system: Test monthly during use; after major changes to roof/tank
- After visible contamination event: Test before use

## Part Five: System Sizing and Design

### Worked Example: Household System (Family of 4, Temperate Climate)

**Parameters:**
- Location: Temperate region, 45 inches annual rainfall
- Roof area: 1,500 sq ft (horizontal projection)
- Runoff coefficient: 0.80 (asphalt shingles)
- Daily household water need: 20 gallons/day × 4 = 80 gallons/day
- Garden irrigation: 50 gallons/day (seasonal)
- Total annual need: 36,500 gallons (conservative estimate with 20-day average need)
- Dry season: 4 months (winter with minimal rainfall)

**System capacity calculation:**

Annual rainfall yield:
- 1,500 sq ft × 45 inches × 0.80 ÷ 12 inches/foot = 45,000 gallons/year

This exceeds annual need (36,500 gallons) by 8,500 gallons. Feasible for full supply.

Storage for dry season (4 months):
- Daily need during dry months: 80 gallons × 30 × 4 = 9,600 gallons
- Add 25% safety margin: 12,000 gallons

Recommendation:
- Install 12,000–15,000 gallon tank (two 6–8,000 gallon tanks in series provides redundancy)
- Use first-flush diverter (divert first 15 gallons)
- Install sand/gravel filter pre-storage
- Provide fine filtration (5-micron cartridge) before household use
- Disinfect with chlorine or UV

### Dry-Season Management

In areas with extended dry seasons, storage capacity may be insufficient to cover full demand. Options:

1. **Reduce demand:** Lower water use during dry season (shorter showers, minimal garden irrigation)
2. **Increase storage:** Build larger cistern (costs escalate nonlinearly; doubling volume doesn't double cost)
3. **Supplement with backup water:** Well, municipal water, or trucked water for dry season
4. **Prioritize uses:** Allocate stored water to drinking/cooking; defer bathing and irrigation

**Rainwater calendar:**
- Plan with local climate: when is rainy season? How long is dry season?
- Size storage to balance probability of insufficient supply vs. cost of oversizing
- Accept trade-offs: most systems supply 60–80% of annual need; supplement with other sources

## Part Six: Community-Scale Systems

### Scaling Principles

Household systems scale to community through:
- Larger catchment surface (multiple roofs, ground collection)
- Larger storage (multiple tanks, gravity-fed distribution)
- Central treatment (single filtration/disinfection point serving multiple homes)

### Multi-Building System Design

**Catchment sources:**
- Residential roofs (primary)
- Agricultural/industrial buildings
- Ground catchment (sealed areas: pavement, courts)
- Natural catchment (hillsides) if sealed/lined

**Distribution:**
- Gravity-fed (elevated tank serving downhill users)
- Communal treatment facility
- Pump station (if natural elevation insufficient)

### Community Cistern Design

**Large ferrocement cistern (10,000+ gallons):**

Materials (per 10,000 gallons):
- Portland cement: 200–250 bags
- Sand: 10 cubic yards
- Gravel: 5 cubic yards
- Rebar/wire mesh: 500–800 lbs
- HDPE plastic sheet or lime-based plaster (interior lining)

Construction approach:
1. Excavate pit (12×8 foot footprint, 4 feet deep = 13,000-gallon volume)
2. Compact soil; add gravel base (4 inches)
3. Place rebar grid (6-inch spacing)
4. Pour concrete base (4 inches)
5. Build walls with reinforced concrete (6 inches thick)
6. Apply interior sealant (lime plaster or plastic liner) to prevent seepage
7. Install inlet, outlet, overflow, and access features
8. Allow concrete to cure (14–28 days) before filling

Cost: ~$0.80–1.50 per gallon (DIY labor saves significantly)

### Community Governance

For shared rainwater systems:
- Establish collection rights (who owns harvested water?)
- Define allocation rules (per-household ration, priority uses)
- Assign maintenance responsibility (cleaning tanks, filter replacement, monitoring)
- Create emergency protocols (drought triggers, rationing thresholds)
- Track consumption (meter connections if rationing needed)

## Part Seven: Maintenance and Troubleshooting

### Regular Maintenance Schedule

**Monthly:**
- Inspect tank for leaks or corrosion
- Check overflow function
- Monitor water level (ensure adequate supply)

**Quarterly:**
- Remove sediment from tank bottom (open drain valve; let sediment flow out)
- Clean gutters and downspouts (remove leaves, debris)
- Inspect filter condition (replace if necessary)

**Annually:**
- Deep cleaning: drain tank; scrub interior; inspect for damage
- Test water quality (if used for potable water)
- Repair gutters, seals, or fittings as needed

### Troubleshooting

**Algae growth in tank:**
- Caused by: Light infiltration, nutrients (nitrogen/phosphorus)
- Prevention: Opaque tank; minimize splash/aeration
- Treatment: Drain and clean tank; add charcoal filter; reduce light exposure

**Low pH (acidic water):**
- Caused by: Atmospheric CO2, vegetation decay
- Effect: Corrosion of metal fittings
- Solution: Add crushed limestone to filter; test pH regularly

**High turbidity (cloudy water):**
- Caused by: Sediment, algae, fine particles
- Solution: Increase settling time; upgrade fine filtration; improve pre-storage filtration

**Mosquito breeding in tank:**
- Prevention: Sealed tank; fine mesh on vent; drain standing water in gutters
- Solution: Remove mosquito larvae; ensure tank is properly sealed

**Overflow flooding foundation:**
- Solution: Reroute overflow away from building; install greywater diversion; larger tank

## Part Eight: Seasonal and Emergency Preparedness

### Drought Planning

- Monitor water level; calculate days of supply remaining
- Reduce non-essential use early (don't wait until tank is empty)
- Activate backup water source before emergency (well, neighbor's source, distribution system)
- Communicate with household/community before rationing is necessary

### Backup Systems

- Well with hand pump (if feasible)
- Greywater recycling (shower/laundry water reused for toilet flushing or irrigation)
- Adjacent system shared with neighbor (mutual aid)
- Emergency stockpile (3–7 days bottled water for drinking/cooking if all else fails)

## Conclusion

Rainwater harvesting provides water independence, reduces vulnerability to supply interruptions, and creates resilience through distributed storage. Whether household, community, or emergency supplementation, a properly designed system with appropriate storage, filtration, and treatment provides safe, reliable water. The systems described here require no electricity, work in low-connectivity environments, and can be maintained by community members with basic training. Combined with other water sources (wells, greywater recycling), rainwater harvesting is a cornerstone of water security in any region.
