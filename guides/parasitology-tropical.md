## Overview

Parasitic and tropical diseases are leading causes of morbidity and mortality in resource-limited settings. This guide covers the most common life-threatening infections encountered in tropical and subtropical regions: malaria, intestinal parasites, dengue, typhoid, yellow fever, cholera, and dysentery. Rapid recognition and field-appropriate treatment are essential—delays of hours can mean the difference between recovery and death.

:::info-box
**Key Principle:** Fever pattern recognition is your diagnostic tool in the field. Each disease has a characteristic fever curve. Malaria shows cyclical fevers (every 48 or 72 hours). Typhoid shows sustained high fever. Yellow fever shows remission then recurrence. These patterns guide treatment decisions when lab tests are unavailable.
:::

</section>

## 1\. Malaria: Recognition and Treatment

Malaria is a parasitic infection transmitted by Anopheles mosquitoes. Recognition by fever pattern is crucial, as rapid treatment is essential for survival. Malaria kills within weeks if untreated and becomes increasingly difficult to treat as it progresses. Early treatment within 24 hours of symptom onset significantly improves survival rates.

### Types by Fever Pattern

The fever pattern is the key distinguishing feature of different Plasmodium species:

-   **Tertian Fever (48-hour cycle):** Fever spikes every 48 hours (every other day). Patient experiences chills for 15 minutes, fever at 40-41°C for 2-3 hours, then heavy sweating and return to normal. P. vivax and P. ovale cause this pattern. Patient feels well on "off" days.
-   **Quartan Fever (72-hour cycle):** Fever occurs every 72 hours (every third day). Much rarer, caused by P. malariae. Extremely severe chills and high fevers.
-   **Quotidian (Daily fever):** Fever spikes every day. Usually indicates mixed infection or severe P. falciparum malaria. This is the most dangerous pattern.

### Clinical Presentation

-   **Early symptoms (Days 1-3):** Non-specific fever, headache, muscle aches, fatigue, nausea. Easy to confuse with common illnesses.
-   **Characteristic fever (Days 3-7):** Sudden high fever (often 40-41°C), violent shaking chills, severe headache, vomiting, abdominal pain. Sweat profusely until temperature normalizes.
-   **Severe malaria:** Cerebral malaria (confusion, seizures, coma), acute renal failure (dark urine, no urine output), severe anemia (pale, weak, breathless), pulmonary edema (difficulty breathing), metabolic acidosis (rapid weak breathing), hypoglycemia (altered mental status).

![Parasitology &amp; Tropical Diseases diagram 1](../assets/svgs/parasitology-tropical-1.svg)

Characteristic fever patterns help identify the parasite species. Tertian fever (every other day) is most common in non-severe malaria.

:::danger
**Cerebral Malaria Warning:** If patient develops confusion, delirium, seizures, altered consciousness, or coma—this is a medical emergency. Cerebral malaria has 15-20% mortality even with treatment. Evacuate immediately to facility capable of IV antimalarials and supportive care. Do not delay waiting for fever patterns to clarify.
:::

:::warning
**Severe Malaria Red Flags:** Dark urine, no urine output (renal failure), severe anemia (pale, weak, breathless), difficulty breathing (pulmonary edema), rapid weak breathing (metabolic acidosis). Any of these indicates progression to severe malaria. Begin emergency treatment and arrange evacuation.
:::

### Treatment Options

If available, artemisinin-based drugs are most effective. Artemisinin comes from sweet wormwood (Artemisia annua). In extremity, quinine from cinchona bark can be used, though less effective.

#### Artemisinin (Sweet Wormwood - Artemisia annua)

**If artemisinin tablets/powder available:**  
Adult: 200 mg oral daily for 3 days, then complete with partner drug (if available)  
  
**Sweet Wormwood Tea (emergency preparation):**  
\- Harvest fresh or dried leaves  
\- 1 tablespoon dried leaves per cup boiling water  
\- Steep 10 minutes, strain  
\- Drink 3 times daily for 5 days  
\- Bitter taste, but highly effective

#### Quinine from Cinchona Bark

**Quinine Extraction (historical method):**  
\- Powdered cinchona bark contains 5-10% quinine  
\- Adult dose: 650 mg (2-3 teaspoons powder) every 8 hours for 7 days  
\- Take with food to reduce nausea  
\- Tinnitus (ringing ears) is sign of adequate dose  
\- If tinnitus severe, reduce dose by 25%  
  
**Side effects:** Hypoglycemia, cinchonism (tinnitus, deafness, blurred vision), thrombocytopenia. If available, take with glucose or food.

### Supportive Care

-   **Hydration:** Critical. Give fluids constantly. Oral rehydration solution is ideal, but water works. Patient will be in and out of sweating phases.
-   **Cooling:** During high fever, use cool water sponging. Remove excess clothing. Keep in cool, shaded area.
-   **Nutrition:** Small frequent meals. Broths, rice, bananas. Patient will have no appetite during acute phase.
-   **Anti-fever medication:** Paracetamol or ibuprofen for comfort (not for routine fever reduction; fever is beneficial).
-   **Prevention of complications:** Watch for cerebral malaria signs (confusion, seizures, altered consciousness—requires immediate evacuation).

:::tip
**Antimalarial Dosing in Field:** Write down treatment schedules. Artemisinin requires daily dosing for 3 days, then completion with partner drug. Quinine requires dosing every 8 hours. Missed doses reduce efficacy. Use a marked cup or container to track doses taken. Set reminders every 8 hours for quinine dosing (morning 6am, afternoon 2pm, evening 10pm).
:::

### Malaria Treatment Response Timeline

Monitor these milestones—if not progressing, reassess diagnosis:
- **24-48 hours:** Fever begins to decline, patient feels slightly improved
- **3-5 days:** Fever resolves, appetite returns, patient can sit up
- **1 week:** Energy returning, color improving in dark-skinned patients (less pale)
- **2 weeks:** Full appetite restored, light activities tolerated
- **No improvement by 48 hours:** Consider alternative diagnosis (typhoid, dengue, other). Consider treatment failure/drug resistance.

## 2\. Intestinal Parasites

Intestinal parasites affect billions globally, especially in areas without sanitation. Most are diagnosed by stool examination and can be treated with herbal or pharmaceutical remedies. Symptoms include abdominal pain, diarrhea, anemia, malnutrition, and intestinal obstruction.

### Common Intestinal Parasites

#### Roundworm (Ascaris lumbricoides)

-   **Symptoms:** Abdominal cramping, bloating, occasional passage of worms in stool, growth stunting in children, malnutrition.
-   **Diagnosis:** Adult worms visible in fresh stool. Eggs visible under microscope in stool.
-   **Severe signs:** Intestinal obstruction (severe abdominal pain, inability to pass stool, vomiting), respiratory symptoms (worms migrating through lungs).

#### Hookworm (Necator americanus, Ancylostoma)

-   **Symptoms:** Severe anemia (pale, weak, shortness of breath), abdominal pain, protein loss, malnutrition. Can be fatal in severe cases.
-   **Diagnosis:** Hookworm larvae visible in fresh stool samples. Anemia on examination. Protein in urine (dipstick).
-   **Transmission:** Soil-contaminated skin contact. Preventable by wearing shoes and improving sanitation.

#### Tapeworm (Taenia spp.)

-   **Symptoms:** Passage of worm segments in stool (look like rice grains), weight loss, vitamin B12 deficiency (if Diphyllobothrium), abdominal discomfort.
-   **Diagnosis:** Visible segments in stool or on underwear. Eggs under microscope.
-   **Transmission:** Undercooked beef or pork containing cysts.

#### Giardia (Giardia lamblia)

-   **Symptoms:** Acute watery diarrhea, abdominal cramping, bloating, malabsorption of fats (oily stool), yellow color. Can become chronic.
-   **Diagnosis:** Cysts or trophozoites in stool samples. "Steatorrhea" (fatty stool).
-   **Transmission:** Contaminated water. Can be transmitted person-to-person.

![Parasitology &amp; Tropical Diseases diagram 2](../assets/svgs/parasitology-tropical-2.svg)

Different parasites produce characteristic symptoms and microscopic findings. Identification guides treatment selection.

### Treatment Protocols

#### Pumpkin Seeds (Cucurbita pepo)

**For Roundworm and Tapeworm:**  
\- Consume 100-150 grams (handful) of raw or slightly roasted pumpkin seeds  
\- Eat on empty stomach in morning  
\- Consume castor oil (30 ml) 2 hours later (acts as laxative to expel worms)  
\- Repeat for 3 consecutive days  
\- Safe for children and pregnant women  
\- Effectiveness: 70-80%

#### Papaya Seeds (Carica papaya)

**For Hookworm and mixed infections:**  
\- Collect seeds from ripe papaya  
\- Dry seeds thoroughly (3-4 days in sun)  
\- Grind to powder: 1 tablespoon powder = ~50 seeds  
\- Adult dose: 1 tablespoon powder mixed with honey, twice daily for 7 days  
\- Child dose: 1 teaspoon twice daily  
\- Can also make tea: steep 1 tablespoon crushed seeds in 1 cup water, drink morning and evening

#### Wormwood (Artemisia vulgaris or A. absinthium)

**For Giardia and mixed parasites:**  
\- Dried leaves: 1 teaspoon per cup boiling water  
\- Steep 5-10 minutes, strain  
\- Drink 3 times daily before meals for 2 weeks  
\- Bitter—can mix with honey or other herbs  
\- Avoid during pregnancy  
\- Effectiveness: 60-75%

#### Garlic (Allium sativum)

**For all intestinal parasites (preventive and mild cases):**  
\- 2-3 cloves minced with food daily  
\- Can be made into paste: pound cloves with salt, spread on bread  
\- Raw garlic most effective (cooking reduces efficacy)  
\- Safe for chronic use  
\- Effectiveness: 40-60%

### Stool Examination Technique

You can identify parasites with simple equipment:

1.  **Collect fresh sample:** Obtain stool sample, preferably within 2 hours of passage. Use clean container.
2.  **Prepare slide:** Mix small amount of stool (pea-sized) with drop of saline solution on glass slide.
3.  **Examine:** Cover with glass coverslip, examine under microscope (40x magnification) or magnifying glass (10x).
4.  **Look for:** Eggs (small oval structures), larvae (thin mobile structures), adult worms (rare but obvious), cysts (round structures with internal organization).
5.  **Repeat:** Take samples on 3 consecutive days—parasites shed eggs irregularly.

### Prevention

-   **Hygiene:** Wash hands before eating and after defecation. Teach children especially.
-   **Water treatment:** Boil water, use bleach (2 drops per liter, wait 30 minutes), or ceramic filters.
-   **Food safety:** Cook meat thoroughly to 71°C (160°F) internal temperature.
-   **Sanitation:** Use latrines, never defecate in open areas. Properly dispose of human waste.
-   **Footwear:** Wear shoes to prevent hookworm transmission through skin.

:::warning
**Never Crush Worms Mechanically:** Purgatives alone (castor oil) without anthelmintic medication can rupture worms, releasing antigens that cause severe allergic reactions, anaphylaxis, or angioedema (swelling of throat/airways). Always use herbal or pharmaceutical antihelmintics first, then use laxatives to expel dead worms.
:::

### Intestinal Parasite Treatment Sequence

**Correct Order:** Anthelmintic medication → Wait 24 hours → Laxative

**Example (Roundworm with pumpkin seeds):**
1. Day 1 morning: Pumpkin seeds on empty stomach
2. Day 1, 2 hours later: Castor oil 30ml
3. Days 2-3: Repeat (pumpkin seeds → castor oil)
4. Day 4: Normal meals resume, patient may pass worms in stool
5. Days 5-7: Monitor for reinfection symptoms

:::tip
**Herbal Treatment Documentation:** Make a simple chart tracking parasite treatment:
- Date started medication
- Type of parasite (if known)
- Medication used and dose
- Laxative used and time administered
- Date of expected worm passage
- Follow-up symptoms monitoring

This helps identify treatment failures and plan retreatment schedules.
:::

### Stool Examination Safety

When examining stool samples for parasites, use precautions:
- Wear disposable gloves at all times
- Work over newspaper or plastic, not bare surface
- Never touch mouth, face, or food during examination
- Dispose of contaminated materials in fire or sealed container
- Wash hands thoroughly with soap for 20 seconds after examination
- Disinfect slides with bleach or burn them

## 3\. Tropical Fevers: Dengue, Typhoid, Yellow Fever

Tropical fevers are often indistinguishable clinically but differ in transmission, progression, and outcomes. Rapid recognition is essential as some are rapidly fatal.

### Dengue Fever

Transmitted by Aedes mosquitoes (day-biting, aggressive species). Incubation 3-14 days. Usually non-fatal but uncomfortable and occasionally severe.

-   **Symptom pattern:** Sudden high fever (39-40°C), severe muscle and joint pain (break-bone fever), headache, eye pain, rash appearing 3-4 days after fever onset (starts on chest, spreads to face/limbs, spares palms/soles)
-   **Course:** Biphasic—fever breaks day 3-4, returns for 24-48 hours. Total 5-7 days acute illness.
-   **Recovery:** Weakness and depression for weeks after acute fever.
-   **Severe dengue:** Dengue hemorrhagic fever (DHF)—bleeding (petechiae, bruising, nosebleeds), severe plasma leakage (shock, pulmonary edema), thrombocytopenia (low platelets). Mortality 1-5% with treatment.

### Typhoid Fever

Bacterial infection transmitted via contaminated food/water. Incubation 6-30 days. More serious than dengue; requires antibiotics.

-   **Symptom pattern:** Week 1—gradually rising fever with headache, body aches, abdominal pain, constipation (or diarrhea). Week 2—high sustained fever (40-41°C), headache, delirium, sometimes rose spots (small rose-colored rash), hepatosplenomegaly (enlarged liver/spleen), relative bradycardia (pulse slower than expected for temperature).
-   **Key differentiator:** Fever is sustained (continuous high), not spiking. Patient appears toxic and confused.
-   **Severe complications:** Intestinal perforation (sudden severe pain, signs of peritonitis), myocarditis (heart failure), encephalitis.
-   **Untreated mortality:** 20-40%. With antibiotics: 1-2%.

### Yellow Fever

Viral infection transmitted by Aedes mosquitoes. Incubation 3-6 days. No specific treatment; mortality 15-50% in severe cases.

-   **Symptom pattern:** Acute onset fever (40-41°C), chills, headache, muscle pain, nausea/vomiting. Remission at day 3-4 (patient seems better). Recurrence of fever with jaundice (yellowing of skin/eyes), bleeding (hemoptysis, melena—bloody stool, epistaxis), hypotension, shock.
-   **Key differentiators:** Jaundice is prominent. Bleeding develops. Relative bradycardia (heart rate inappropriately slow for fever).
-   **Mortality phases:** 15% die during acute phase with fulminant hepatitis and hemorrhagic diathesis.

### Management of Tropical Fevers

#### Dengue Fever

-   **No specific antiviral treatment:** Supportive care only.
-   **Pain management:** Acetaminophen preferred (avoid NSAIDs which increase bleeding risk). Maximum 3 grams daily in divided doses.
-   **Hydration:** Critical, especially during remission period. Monitor for signs of shock—rapid weak pulse, low blood pressure, letharness.
-   **Platelet monitoring:** If platelet count drops below 100,000 with bleeding signs, risk of DHF. Transfusion necessary if severe bleeding.
-   **Recovery:** Gradual. Avoid strenuous activity for 2-3 weeks.

#### Typhoid Fever

**Antibiotic treatment (if available):**  
\- Fluoroquinolone: Ciprofloxacin 500 mg twice daily for 5-7 days (first-line in most areas)  
\- If resistance suspected: Cephalosporin (Ceftriaxone 1-2 g daily for 7-10 days)  
\- Macrolide: Azithromycin 500 mg daily for 5-7 days (for drug-resistant strains)  
  
**Without antibiotics:** Herbal treatment and supportive care. Mortality high.  
\- High fever sponging with cool water  
\- Frequent small meals (avoid constipating foods)  
\- Avoid NSAIDs (increase bleeding risk)  
\- Isolation precautions (patient is contagious)

#### Yellow Fever

-   **No specific antiviral:** Supportive care only.
-   **Bleeding management:** Avoid NSAIDs, aspirin (worsen bleeding). Use acetaminophen.
-   **Liver support:** Avoid hepatotoxic drugs. Maintain hydration.
-   **Isolation:** Patient is infective during first 3-5 days. Use mosquito netting and protective clothing around patient to prevent transmission.
-   **Prognosis:** If patient survives first week of hemorrhagic phase, prognosis improves significantly.

:::warning
**NSAIDs Contraindicated in Tropical Fevers:** Aspirin, ibuprofen, and naproxen increase bleeding risk in dengue and yellow fever. Use acetaminophen (paracetamol) only: 500 mg tablets, 1-2 tablets every 6 hours, maximum 3 grams daily (do not exceed 4 grams if using combination products). In yellow fever with bleeding, avoid all NSAIDs completely.
:::

### Tropical Fever Recovery Timeline

**Dengue:** Acute phase 5-7 days, but weakness persists for 2-3 weeks. Avoid strenuous activity to prevent relapse.

**Typhoid (treated):** Fever gradually resolves over 5-7 days. Do not stop antibiotics early. Even if fever resolves, continue full course (5-7 days) to prevent relapse and chronic carrier state (10% become chronic carriers without complete treatment).

**Yellow Fever:** If hemorrhagic phase is survived (days 4-7), recovery is possible but slow. Convalescence takes 4-8 weeks.

:::danger
**Chronic Carriers of Typhoid:** 1-5% of recovered typhoid patients become chronic carriers, shedding bacteria for years. Recovered patients must maintain exceptional hygiene (hand washing before eating, separate food prep areas) for life to prevent transmitting to others.
:::

## 4\. Vector Control Strategies

Preventing infection is better than treating disease. Vector control is critical in tropical disease prevention.

### Mosquito Control

Both Anopheles (malaria) and Aedes (dengue, yellow fever) breed in water and require standing water for larvae.

#### Standing Water Elimination

-   Empty all containers holding water: buckets, old tires, flower pots, coconut shells, roof gutters.
-   Keep water containers covered with tight-fitting lids.
-   Clear clogged drains and gutters.
-   Fill in puddles and low-lying areas that hold water.
-   Keep vegetation trimmed around water sources (removes resting places).
-   Drain or stock fish ponds (fish eat mosquito larvae).

#### Bed Nets and Screening

-   **Bed nets:** Most effective malaria prevention. Hang net over bed, tucked under mattress. Nets treated with permethrin insecticide last 3-5 years.
-   **Screen doors/windows:** Prevent mosquitoes from entering homes. Can be improvised with fine mesh cloth.
-   **Net repairs:** A single hole allows mosquitoes entry. Repair tears immediately with needle and thread.

#### Plant-Based Repellents

**Citronella Oil (Cymbopogon nardus):**  
\- Extract: Crush fresh leaves, distill or soak in alcohol  
\- Application: 10% citronella oil in coconut oil as skin repellent  
\- Effectiveness: 2-4 hours per application  
\- Safety: Apply to exposed skin, avoid face and eyes  
  
**Neem Oil (Azadirachta indica):**  
\- Extract: Crush or cold-press seeds  
\- Application: 5% neem oil in coconut oil as repellent  
\- Also works as larvicide: 5% neem oil kills mosquito larvae in breeding water  
\- Effectiveness: 3-5 hours  
\- Smoke: Burn neem leaves in evening to repel mosquitoes

### Tick Control

-   **Prevention:** Wear long sleeves and long pants in endemic areas. Tuck pants into boots.
-   **Body checking:** Inspect entire body daily for attached ticks, especially groin, armpits, hairline.
-   **Removal:** Grasp tick with tweezers as close to skin as possible, pull straight out with steady pressure. Do not twist. Discard in fire or seal in container.
-   **Never:** Squeeze tick body, apply hot objects, or use petroleum jelly—causes tick to regurgitate pathogens into wound.
-   **Repellent:** Apply permethrin (if available) or neem oil to clothing.

### Fly Control

-   **Sanitation:** Proper waste disposal, covered latrines, no exposed food, clean living areas.
-   **Screening:** Keep flies away from food, patients, and wounds with screens/netting.
-   **Fly traps:** Improvised traps—fermented fruit in bottle with small opening, or sticky paper. Attracts and traps flies.
-   **Exclusion:** Keep garbage in sealed containers, bury waste promptly.

:::info-box
**Vector Control Materials Checklist (Field Kit):**
- Fine-mesh screening material (for door/window repairs)
- Permethrin insecticide (if available) or neem seeds for oil extraction
- Citronella leaves or seeds (grow locally or dried)
- Tweezers (for tick removal)
- Thin mesh mosquito nets (spare for repairs)
- Needle and thread (for net repairs)
- Container covers/lids (for water storage)
- Ceramic water filters or cloth filters
- Bleach or iodine tablets (for water treatment)

These items can be improvised from locally available materials if commercial options unavailable.
:::

## 5\. Water-borne Diseases: Cholera and Dysentery

### Cholera

Caused by Vibrio cholerae, transmitted via contaminated water. Incubation 1-3 days. Causes massive fluid loss and can be fatal within hours without treatment.

-   **Symptoms:** Sudden onset of watery diarrhea (rice-water stool—clear, odorless, profuse), vomiting, severe dehydration (dry mucous membranes, sunken eyes, no urine, shock—weak rapid pulse, cool clammy skin), muscle cramps.
-   **Progression:** Can lose 10+ liters of fluid in 24 hours. Death occurs from shock and electrolyte depletion.
-   **Mortality:** 1-2% with proper rehydration treatment, 50% untreated.

#### Emergency Treatment: Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS)

#### ORS Recipe

**Household ORS Composition:**

-   1 liter clean water
-   6 teaspoons sugar (or honey)
-   1/2 teaspoon salt
-   Mix thoroughly

**Administration:**

-   Give patient small frequent sips—5-10 ml every 5 minutes
-   If patient vomits, wait 5 minutes then resume slower
-   Continue even after diarrhea stops
-   For severe dehydration, goal is 100 ml/kg over 4 hours
-   Total daily fluid requirement can exceed 20 liters

**Signs of adequate rehydration:**

-   Return of normal skin turgor (skin springs back when pinched)
-   Normal mental status
-   Adequate urine output (clear, pale)
-   Disappearance of sunken eyes

#### Antibiotic Treatment

**If antibiotics available (adjunct to ORS, not replacement):**  
\- Doxycycline 300 mg single dose  
\- Cipro 500 mg twice daily for 3 days  
\- Reduces diarrhea duration by 1-2 days but not essential if ORS adequate

#### Prevention

-   Boil water (rolling boil for 1 minute kills all cholera vibrios)
-   Bleach: 2 drops per liter, wait 30 minutes
-   Improve sanitation—isolate latrines from water sources
-   Hand washing with soap and clean water
-   Careful food handling

### Dysentery

Inflammatory diarrhea caused by bacterial (Shigella, Salmonella) or parasitic (Entamoeba histolytica) infection. Characterized by bloody diarrhea.

-   **Shigellosis (bacterial):** Incubation 1-3 days. Acute diarrhea, blood/mucus in stool, abdominal cramping, fever. Usually self-limiting 5-7 days.
-   **Amebiasis (parasitic):** Incubation weeks to months. Chronic diarrhea with bloody mucus, abdominal pain, tenesmus (persistent urge to defecate). Can invade liver (liver abscess).

#### Treatment

**Bacterial dysentery:**  
\- Supportive care with ORS is primary  
\- Antibiotics if available: Cipro 500 mg twice daily for 3-5 days  
\- Avoid antimotility drugs (loperamide)—prolong infection and increase complications  
  
**Parasitic dysentery (Amebic):**
\- Herbal: Wormwood (Artemisia) tea as in parasite section
\- If metronidazole available: 750 mg three times daily for 10 days
\- Follow with paromomycin 25-30 mg/kg daily for 7 days to clear intestinal cysts

:::warning
**Cholera Transmission:** Cholera remains contagious in patient's stool for 7-14 days after recovery, even if asymptomatic. Recovered patients must use separate latrines/toilets from others. Disinfect all contaminated surfaces with 0.5% chlorine solution (1 part bleach to 10 parts water). Do not allow recovered patient to prepare food for others for minimum 2 weeks.
:::

## 6\. Equipment & Materials for Parasitic Disease Management

### Diagnostic Equipment

**Microscopy Setup (if available):**
- Microscope (40x magnification minimum for parasite eggs)
- Glass slides (25 × 75 mm standard)
- Glass coverslips
- Disposable pipettes or glass rods for solution transfer
- Saline solution (0.9% salt solution) or distilled water
- Microscope cleaning materials (lens paper, ethanol)

**Field Diagnostic Alternatives:**
- Magnifying glass (10x magnification minimum)—can visualize some parasites, eggs
- Clear glass or plastic container for visual observation of stool samples
- White paper or tile background (helps visualize dark samples)
- Lighting source (sunlight, headlamp, or torch)

### Treatment & Medication Materials

**Herbal Medicine Preparation:**
- Grinding stone or mortar and pestle (for grinding seeds/bark)
- Drying rack or clothesline (for drying leaves/seeds)
- Measuring cups/spoons (standard kitchen utensils)
- Pots for boiling water (preferably stainless steel)
- Strainers/cheesecloth (for straining herbal preparations)
- Glass jars with lids (for storing dried herbs, up to 6 months at room temperature)
- Labels and marker (for dating herbal preparations)

**Pharmaceutical Equipment (if available):**
- Thermometer (digital or mercury, for monitoring fevers)
- Medication scales or measuring spoons (for accurate dosing)
- Pre-marked dosing cups (for syrups/liquids)
- Individual medicine containers or paper packets (for dividing doses)

### Monitoring & Documentation

**Assessment Tools:**
- Urine dipsticks (test for protein, signs of kidney damage)
- Timer or watch (for monitoring fever duration, medication timing)
- Pen and paper (for recording fever patterns, medication dates)
- Towels/cloths (for fever sponging, cool water treatment)

**Hygiene & Safety:**
- Disposable gloves (for handling stool samples, contaminated materials)
- Face mask (when patient has respiratory symptoms)
- Hand soap (for thorough hand washing)
- Disinfectant (bleach, alcohol, or iodine solution)
- Plastic bags for contaminated waste
- Newspaper/plastic sheeting (for work surfaces during stool examination)

## 7\. Recovery & Aftercare

### Post-Malaria Recovery

**Week 1-2 after fever resolution:**
- Continue rest—avoid strenuous activity for 2 weeks minimum
- Nutrition: Provide high-protein meals (eggs, beans, meat) to rebuild blood volume. Hematocrit drops 10-20% in malaria; protein helps regenerate hemoglobin.
- Iron supplementation: If available, ferrous sulfate 325 mg daily for 8 weeks (patient should have 6-10 grams of iron deficit to rebuild)
- Monitor for reinfection: Malaria recurs in 5-10% even after treatment; fever pattern suggests reinfection (vs. relapse)
- Avoid mosquitoes: Continue bed net use and protective clothing for 4 weeks post-recovery

**Week 3-4:**
- Light activity permissible (walking, slow work)
- Continue nutritional support
- Return to normal activity gradually

:::tip
**Post-Parasite Nutritional Recovery:** Parasites cause 15-30% nutrient malabsorption. After parasite treatment, provide 4-6 weeks of enhanced nutrition:
- Protein: 1.5 grams per kg body weight daily
- Iron-rich foods: Red meat, beans, fortified grains
- Vitamin A: Orange/yellow vegetables (carrots, squash)
- B vitamins: Whole grains, legumes
- Zinc: Meat, seeds, legumes
- Caloric intake: 10-15% above normal baseline during recovery
:::

### Post-Tropical Fever Recovery

**Dengue recovery (weeks 1-3):**
- Extreme fatigue common; avoid physical exertion for minimum 2 weeks
- Avoid NSAIDs entirely during recovery (can cause unexpected bleeding episodes)
- Maintain hydration—drink 2-3 liters fluid daily for 2 weeks
- If dengue hemorrhagic fever occurred, avoid exertion for 4-6 weeks
- Monitor for unusual bleeding (nosebleeds, gum bleeding); report if recurrent

**Typhoid recovery (weeks 1-4):**
- Must complete full antibiotic course even if fever resolves after 3-4 days
- Relapse occurs in 10-15% if treatment stopped early
- Return to normal diet gradually (soft foods first, then progression to normal)
- Strict hygiene for life (chronic carrier state possible)
- If available, hepatic function tests at 4 weeks post-treatment (liver involvement common)

**Yellow fever recovery:**
- Extremely prolonged (4-8 weeks)
- Avoid all strenuous activity for 6 weeks minimum
- High-protein, high-calorie diet to support hepatic recovery
- If jaundice present: Monitor for continuing liver dysfunction, dark urine, pale stool (indicate ongoing hepatitis)

### Post-Cholera/Dysentery Recovery

**Weeks 1-2:**
- Continue ORS for 3-5 days after diarrhea stops (prevents dehydration recurrence)
- Gradual diet progression: Broths → rice/bananas → normal foods (3-5 days)
- Electrolyte replenishment: If available, salt-sugar solutions with potassium (banana is good natural source)
- Monitor stool: Loose stools 1-2 weeks post-acute phase are normal; if bloody or excessive, consider relapse

**Weeks 3-4:**
- Return to normal diet
- Nutritional rehabilitation: Post-diarrheal malnutrition is common; provide high-quality protein
- Intestinal lining recovery takes 2-3 weeks; milk/dairy can cause temporary lactose intolerance (use rice-based foods instead)

:::warning
**Post-Diarrheal Lactose Intolerance:** The intestinal brush border that produces lactase is damaged by severe diarrhea. Milk consumed during recovery causes bloating and diarrhea. Use coconut milk, rice milk, or lactose-free preparations for 4 weeks. Dairy can resume after 4-6 weeks.
:::

## 8\. Troubleshooting Common Issues

### Treatment Not Working: 48-72 Hour Review

**If fever persists unchanged after 48 hours of artemisinin treatment:**

1. **Verify diagnosis:** Are you certain of malaria? Reassess fever pattern:
   - If fever is continuous (not cyclical), consider typhoid instead
   - If rash is prominent, consider dengue
   - If patient is vomiting blood, consider yellow fever

2. **Check adherence:**
   - Did patient actually take all doses? (Some refuse due to bitter taste)
   - Confirm doses given, record each dose
   - If missed doses, resume treatment schedule

3. **Drug resistance:** Plasmodium falciparum has artemisinin resistance in Southeast Asia
   - If available, switch to alternative: Quinine if artemisinin unavailable
   - If quinine unavailable, continue artemisinin but increase monitoring frequency

4. **Complications:** Has patient developed severe malaria signs?
   - Check urine color (dark = renal failure)
   - Check mental status (confusion = cerebral involvement)
   - Evacuate if severe signs present

### Parasite Treatment Failure

**If patient symptoms persist after 2 weeks of herbal antihelmintic:**

1. **Reinfection vs. treatment failure:**
   - Review timeline: Was patient reexposed to contaminated water/food?
   - In areas with high transmission, reinfection common within 2-3 months

2. **Wrong parasite identification:**
   - Giardia can mimic roundworm symptoms
   - Entamoeba (ameba) requires different treatment than bacterial dysentery
   - Repeat stool exam if possible, or treat empirically for mixed infections

3. **Inadequate dosing:**
   - Double-check dose calculations against body weight
   - Verify medicinal plant potency (dried herbs lose efficacy after 6 months; fresh preferable)
   - Consider pharmaceutical antihelmintic if available (albendazole 400 mg single dose, repeat in 2 weeks)

4. **Reinfection prevention failure:**
   - Treatment won't work if patient continues exposure
   - Address water source (boil, filter, add bleach)
   - Ensure all household members treated (parasite infections are familial)

### Dehydration During Rehydration Treatment

**If patient showing worsening dehydration signs despite ORS intake:**

1. **Verify ORS composition:** Confirm correct recipe (6 teaspoons sugar, 1/2 teaspoon salt per liter). Incorrect ratios reduce absorption.

2. **Increase intake volume:**
   - If patient vomiting, reduce bolus size to 5 ml every 2-3 minutes instead of 5-10 ml every 5 minutes
   - Increase frequency rather than volume
   - If patient unconscious, IV fluids required (cannot give ORS to unconscious patient without aspiration risk)

3. **Worsening diarrhea despite ORS:**
   - If diarrhea volume increasing, IV therapy required
   - If diarrhea stable or decreasing, continue ORS (can take 6-12 hours for fluid shifts to normalize)

4. **Complicating factors:**
   - Some patients cannot absorb ORS due to malabsorption or concurrent conditions
   - In severe cholera with 50+ stools per day, IV replacement may be necessary
   - If IV therapy unavailable and patient deteriorating, prepare for possible death; comfort care essential

:::danger
**Signs Requiring Evacuation During Cholera/Dysentery:**
- Severe shock (pulse barely palpable, patient unresponsive)
- Inability to maintain fluid intake (persistent vomiting)
- Mental status changes (delirium, altered consciousness)
- Persistent high fever (suggests secondary bacterial infection)
- Blood in diarrhea worsening despite treatment (suggests Shigella dysentery or other invasive pathogen)

These situations exceed field management capability.
:::

### Medication Side Effects

**Quinine toxicity (tinnitus, blurred vision, hypoglycemia):**
- Monitor for tinnitus starting day 2 (sign of therapeutic dose)
- If tinnitus severe (patient cannot hear), reduce dose by 25%
- Give with food and glucose to reduce nausea and hypoglycemia
- If hypoglycemic symptoms (shaking, confusion, sweating), give sugar immediately and reduce dose

**Metronidazole (if used for amebic dysentery) side effects:**
- Metallic taste (normal, not harmful)
- Nausea: Take with food
- Dark urine: Normal, not concerning
- Peripheral neuropathy (if used >2 weeks): Unlikely in standard 10-day course

**Wormwood overdose:**
- Excessive bitter plant material can cause nausea/vomiting
- Reduce concentration (use less leaf) or increase steeping water
- If vomiting develops, stop wormwood and resume when nausea settles

<section id="field">

## 9\. Field Adaptations & Resource-Limited Settings

### Treatment When Only Water Available

**Malaria without artemisinin or quinine:**
- Sweet wormwood if available locally (grows as weed in some regions)
- Fever management with cool water sponging and rest
- Supportive care buys time for natural immune response (some patients recover even without antimalarials, but mortality 20-40%)
- If any fever-reducing plants available (willow bark, tea leaves), use them
- Prepare for possible death; ensure comfort care

**Parasites without anthelmintic:**
- Pumpkin seeds, papaya seeds, garlic available in most tropical regions
- Begin with these while preparing for possible evacuation if severe
- If patient becoming malnourished (weight loss, weakness), priority shifts to caloric support even if parasites not cured

**Cholera/severe diarrhea without ORS supplies:**
- Emergency ORS recipe: 1 liter clean water + 6 teaspoons any sugar (honey, table sugar, fruit juice concentrate) + 1/2 teaspoon salt
- If no salt available: Use soy sauce (high salt content, 1 tablespoon per liter), seawater (diluted to 1:4 with fresh water), or fermented vegetables (preserved in salt)
- If no sugar: Honey, fruit juice, tree sap can work, though slightly less effective for glucose absorption
- Effectiveness is lower than standard ORS but can prevent death from dehydration

### Treatment When Only Herbs Available

**Herbal antimalarials (empirical):**
- Artemisia (wormwood, mugwort, sweet wormwood): Leaves steeped in hot water, 3 cups daily for 7 days
- Cinchona bark: If trees available locally, harvest inner bark, dry, grind to powder: 1 teaspoon 3x daily for 7 days
- Quinine taste is very bitter; add honey or mix with other beverages

**Herbal antihelmintics (empirical for mixed parasites):**
- Garlic + papaya seeds + pumpkin seeds: Combine all three for broader coverage (patient unlikely to object to one if others fail)
- Wormwood + citrus: Add lemon/lime juice to wormwood tea to improve palatability and may enhance antiparasitic effect

**Herbal antifebriles (for symptom management):**
- Willow bark (salicylate content, similar to aspirin): 1 tablespoon bark pieces in water, boil 10 minutes, drink 3x daily
- Lemongrass tea: 5-6 fresh leaves in boiling water, drink 2-3 cups daily
- Ginger (Zingiber officinale): Fresh root, grated into hot water, drink warm 3x daily (also aids digestion)

### Diagnostic Shortcuts When Microscopy Unavailable

**Presumptive diagnosis approach:**

*Patient presents with fever + history of being in malaria area → Treat for malaria immediately*. Do not wait for confirmation if patient deteriorating.

*Patient with fever + severe headache + sustained high fever (not cyclical) + abdominal pain → Treat presumptively for typhoid* (requires antibiotics; try to get cipro if possible)

*Patient with fever + rash + joint pain + mild symptoms → Likely dengue, supportive care only*

*Patient with sudden watery diarrhea + rice-water appearance + severe dehydration → Cholera until proven otherwise. Start ORS immediately.*

*Patient with bloody diarrhea + cramping + fever → Dysentery. Avoid antimotility drugs. ORS + antibiotics if available.*

### Infection Control in Field Settings

**Isolation measures with minimal resources:**
- Malaria patient: Bed net essential (prevents mosquitoes from biting and spreading to others)
- Dengue patient: Same (Aedes mosquitoes infect others during first 3-5 days)
- Yellow fever patient: Mosquito net; handle all blood/bodily fluids with gloves
- Cholera/dysentery patient: Separate latrine (50+ feet minimum from water source), separate eating utensils, dedicated caregiver if possible
- Typhoid patient: Strict hand hygiene by caregivers (typhoid spread via contaminated hands)

**Waste handling:**
- Diarrheal disease waste: Burn or bury 3 feet deep
- Contaminated linens: Boil if possible, or sun-dry for 24+ hours (UV destroys most pathogens)
- Needle/sharp disposal: No needles in field settings ideally, but if present: bury or burn in secure container

## 11\. Common Mistakes & Prevention

### Critical Errors to Avoid

-   **Using antimotility drugs in dysentery or cholera:** Loperamide (Imodium) and diphenoxylate (Lomotil) can trap pathogens, increase complications, and prolong illness. Use only after diarrhea is resolving.
-   **Assuming malaria is malaria without fever pattern:** Fever patterns are diagnostic. Treating all fevers as malaria misses typhoid (which requires antibiotics) and yellow fever (which is fatal untreated).
-   **Delaying ORS administration in cholera:** Every minute of delay increases mortality. ORS should be started immediately and continued through entire acute phase.
-   **Over-treating malaria with quinine without monitoring:** Quinine can cause severe hypoglycemia, cinchonism, and thrombocytopenia. Monitor for tinnitus (sign of adequate dosing) and reduce if severe.
-   **Crushing infected worms instead of using anthelmintics:** Mechanical trauma to worms (from purgatives without medication or physical trauma) can release parasitic antigens and cause severe allergic reactions and anaphylaxis.
-   **Treating intestinal parasites without addressing underlying malnutrition:** Parasites cause malnutrition; treating parasites without nutritional support fails. Provide protein, iron, vitamins during recovery.
-   **Incorrect squeezing of tick during removal:** Squeezing tick body can inject pathogen-laden saliva into wound, increasing infection risk. Always remove by pulling on mouth parts.
-   **Starting antibiotics too early in dengue:** Dengue is viral; antibiotics do not help but promote resistance. Avoid NSAIDs which increase hemorrhage risk.
-   **Assuming recovered malaria patient is immune:** Recovery from one malaria species does not confer immunity to others. Reinfection is common.
-   **Ignoring warning signs of severe disease:** Cerebral malaria, dengue hemorrhagic fever, yellow fever with jaundice, and cholera shock require immediate evacuation/advanced care. Do not delay.
-   **Stopping antibiotics early in typhoid:** Stopping ciprofloxacin after fever resolves at day 3-4 invites relapse and chronic carrier state. Complete full 5-7 day course always.
-   **Presuming immunity after malaria recovery:** Recovery from P. vivax does not confer immunity to P. falciparum or other species. Reinfection common; maintain prevention (bed nets, antimalarials if available).
-   **Misidentifying amebic vs bacterial dysentery:** Both cause bloody diarrhea, but amebic requires metronidazole + paromomycin (2 drugs, 17 days total), while bacterial needs only antibiotics. Stool exam (if available) can differentiate: amebic has trophozoites; bacterial is purely inflammatory.
-   **Over-relying on herbal remedies in severe malaria:** Herbal artemisia tea is 60-75% effective compared to pharmaceutical artemisinin (95%+). In severe malaria with cerebral signs, pharmaceutical drugs are non-negotiable; herbal therapy alone unacceptable. Push for evacuation/pharmaceutical access.
-   **Confusing relative bradycardia:** In dengue, pulse should increase with fever (normal). If pulse is slow despite high fever (bradycardia), suspect typhoid or yellow fever instead. Pulse patterns differentiate these diseases.
-   **Inadequate follow-up after parasite treatment:** Cure rates for herbal treatments are 60-80%; always repeat stool exams 2-4 weeks after treatment to confirm eradication. If parasites remain, retreatment necessary.
-   **Neglecting rehydration volume in cholera:** ORS works only if volume is sufficient. A patient with 50 diarrheal stools in 24 hours can lose 10-20 liters. Small frequent sips must be maintained despite high intake volume. Track input carefully.
-   **Assuming fever resolution = cure:** Many patients have fever-free periods (dengue remission day 3-4, typhoid fever charts) before fever returns. Do not discharge patient as "cured" until full symptom resolution and appropriate time has passed.

:::tip
**Documentation Protocol for Tropical Diseases:**

Create a patient card with:
- **Name and age**
- **Symptom onset date** (day 1 = when fever started)
- **Fever pattern** (draw a simple fever curve if possible, marking when fever spikes and when it breaks)
- **Medications given** (name, dose, date/time given)
- **Daily vital signs** (temperature, pulse rate, breathing rate)
- **Complications noted** (bleeding, confusion, vomiting, inability to drink)
- **Treatment response** (fever declining, staying same, worsening)
- **Date of last dose** and **target end date** for treatment

This record guides evacuation decisions and ensures consistency if patient transferred to another caregiver. Write dates as YYYY-MM-DD to prevent confusion.
:::

## Tropical Shelter and Environment Management

Tropical environments demand specific shelter designs that manage humidity, insects, and heat while remaining repairable and sustainable.

### Raised Platform Shelter Design

Building 3-4 feet above ground keeps inhabitants away from ground-level insects, moisture, and predators. The platform improves air circulation and drainage.

| Component | Specifications | Purpose |
|-----------|-----------------|---------|
| Post Base | 4x4 inch wood, 3 feet into ground | Resist rot, support weight |
| Platform Height | 3-4 feet above grade | Avoid most insects and moisture |
| Decking | Bamboo or split logs, gaps for drainage | Allow water drainage, improve airflow |
| Roof Height | 6-7 feet peak above platform | Ventilation, heat dissipation |
| Walls | Open-sided or half-walled | Airflow while maintaining privacy |

**Roofing for Heavy Rain:** Tropical downpours deliver 2-4 inches per hour. Create a layered roof with steep pitch (45+ degrees) to shed water quickly.

**Thatched Roofing:** Layer palm fronds, banana leaves, or dried grass. Start at the bottom and layer upward, overlapping each layer by 6 inches. Tie materials firmly to roof frame. A well-made thatch roof sheds water completely while allowing air circulation underneath.

**Leaf Roofing Alternative:** Large leaves (20+ inches) can be layered like shingles. Secure with thin vines or cordage.

### Jungle Movement Techniques

**Machete Technique:** Cut only where necessary. Wiping a machete through vines is faster and quieter than chopping. Cut ahead and underfoot to clear walking path. Carry the machete with blade trailing, not forward, to prevent stumbling.

**Navigation:** Jungle is disorienting. Use compass bearings constantly. Blazing trees (cutting small marks) creates a return path. Drop rock cairns or tied vine bundles at decision points.

**Water Sources:** Follow water downhill. Animal trails often lead to water. Listen for water sounds—a compass bearing toward the sound usually succeeds.

**Dealing with Density and Humidity:** Jungle exhausts quickly due to heat, humidity, and poor footing. Move slowly and steadily, not fast. Breath control prevents panic in dense vegetation. Stay hydrated—drink frequently even if not thirsty. Dehydration impairs judgment and encourages dangerous decisions.

:::tip
**Jungle Safety:** Make noise while walking—most animals avoid humans if warned. Clear shelter perimeter. Inspect shoes and gear before putting on. Avoid snake habitats (tall grass, water edges) in early morning and evening when snakes hunt.
:::

## Tropical Food Sources & Coastal Foraging

Tropical regions provide abundant plant and animal food year-round, but proper identification prevents poisoning.

### Year-Round Tropical Fruits

| Fruit | Identification | Edible Parts | Preparation |
|-------|-----------------|---------------|--------------|
| Coconut | Large seed pods, brown husk, 3 spots at end | Meat, milk inside mature nuts | Crack with blade, eat raw or roasted |
| Banana | Distinctive bunches, yellow when ripe | Fruit, mature fruit only | Peel and eat raw |
| Papaya | Tree-fruit, yellow-orange skin when ripe | Flesh and seeds, unripe for cooking | Eat raw ripe, cook unripe as vegetable |
| Mango | Oblong fruit, reddish-green to yellow | Sweet flesh inside | Peel and eat raw, avoid latex sap |
| Pineapple | Distinctive crown leaves, green to yellow body | Fruit flesh inside | Cut crown, peel skin, eat raw |

:::warning
**⚠ WARNING:** Never eat tropical plants without positive identification. Many poisonous fruits mimic edible varieties. When uncertain, avoid.
:::

### Edible Tubers and Root Vegetables

**Taro:** Underground corms look like potatoes. Must be cooked—raw taro contains calcium oxalate crystals causing mouth irritation. Boil 20-30 minutes until soft. Grows in wet areas.

**Cassava (Yuca):** Looks like thin potatoes. White or yellow interior. Peel, cut into chunks, boil until tender (30-40 minutes). Must be cooked thoroughly—raw cassava contains cyanogenic compounds.

**Sweet Potato:** Purple or orange tubers. Bake, boil, or roast. Fully edible when cooked. Very nutritious.

### Tropical Protein Sources

**Fish:** Coastal and river areas provide excellent fish. Make simple traps from woven branches. Fish in early morning or evening. Handline fishing with improvised hooks works well.

**Crustaceans:** Freshwater crabs and shrimp are excellent protein. Look under rocks in streams. Saltwater areas have crabs, lobsters, and shrimp. Boil to kill parasites.

**Insects:** Termites, ants, and grubs are protein-rich. Remove heads and legs, roast over flame. Some cultures rely heavily on insects for protein.

**Leafy Greens:** Many common tropical plants are edible leaves: amaranth, malabar spinach, and morning glory shoots. Boil to remove any bitterness and ensure safety. Cook thoroughly to kill parasites.

### Coastal Foraging Strategy

Low tide exposes tide pools and rocky areas rich in food. Plan foraging for 2-3 hours around lowest tide. Work backwards toward shore as tide rises to avoid being cut off.

| Resource | Location | Season | Yield |
|----------|----------|--------|-------|
| Crabs | Under rocks at tide line | Year-round | 10-20 medium crabs per hour |
| Snails | Rocky outcrops | Year-round | 30-50 snails per hour |
| Seaweed | On rocks and sand | Year-round | 50+ pounds harvestable daily |
| Octopus | Reef crevices | Varies by location | 1-3 per trip if present |

**Seaweed and Sea Vegetables:** Many seaweeds are highly nutritious. Dulse, nori, and kelp are commonly edible. Red and green seaweed generally safer than brown (which accumulates heavy metals). Rinse thoroughly in fresh water to remove salt and debris. Eat raw or cooked. Dried seaweed can be stored for months and rehydrated in soups or stews.

**Coconut Palms at the Shoreline:** Coconut palms provide multiple resources: drinking water inside nuts, edible meat, fiber for rope, fronds for thatch, and fuel. A single mature palm produces 10-50 nuts yearly depending on age and variety.

<section id="salt-water-challenges">

## Salt Water Challenges and Management

Coastal survival introduces unique hazards related to salt water exposure.

### Dehydration and Saltwater

:::info-box
**Critical Rule:** Never drink seawater. Osmotic pressure forces water out of cells, worsening dehydration and causing death. The only exception is small amounts mixed with fresh water (less than 2% salt) in desperate emergency.
:::

Coastal inhabitants must maintain freshwater reserves. Collect rainwater aggressively. Coconuts provide internal water (not actually pure coconut milk—that comes from grating the meat). Morning dew can be collected from leaves using cloth.

### Salt Accumulation on Skin

Saltwater exposure causes chafing and infection. Bathe in freshwater daily if available. If only saltwater available, dry completely after exposure to minimize skin damage. Cotton or linen clothing reduces chafing compared to other materials.

### Saltwater Preservation Method

Salt is precious in post-industrial survival. Coastal inhabitants can produce salt through solar evaporation. Dig shallow ponds, pump or carry seawater to ponds, and allow sun evaporation. Daily tending and reshaping channels concentrates salt. Collect salt crystals and store dry.

## Hurricane and Typhoon Preparation

Tropical cyclones strike with little warning and destroy structures. Preparation is essential.

### Early Warning Signs

| Sign | Timeline | Action |
|------|----------|--------|
| Rapid barometer drop | 12-24 hours before | Begin securing materials |
| Unusual ocean swell | 24-48 hours before | Move to high ground, gather supplies |
| Distinctive cloud patterns | 24+ hours before | Monitor for storm direction |
| Wind increasing steadily | 6-12 hours before | Seek shelter, secure final items |

### Storm-Resistant Shelter Design

**Location:** Choose elevation above storm surge. Avoid exposed ridges (wind acceleration). Place shelter in forest clearings where trees provide wind break but won't fall on structure.

**Structure:** Low profile, aerodynamic shape. Peaked roof sheds wind better than flat. Heavy materials (stone, earth) absorb impact. Build into hillside if possible—earth provides wind barrier.

**Anchoring:** Tie roof down with cables or heavy rope to frame and ground anchors. Walls must be substantial. Loose materials (thatch, palm fronds) must be secured thoroughly or removed before storm.

### Storm Preparation Timeline

**48 Hours Before:** Move boats ashore and anchor with multiple lines. Secure loose materials. Fill all water containers—water systems often break in hurricanes. Gather food to last 2+ weeks. Identify shelter location.

**12-24 Hours Before:** Complete all outdoor securing. Bring in moveable items. Board up windows if time allows. Secure doors from inside with braces. Prepare emergency supplies in shelter.

**During Storm:** Stay in shelter. Do not go outside for any reason. Wind-driven rain finds every opening—prepare for water infiltration. Keep water buckets inside to catch leaks. Monitor structural integrity.

**Post-Storm Recovery:** After storm passes, widespread disease outbreaks follow from contaminated water and sanitation breakdown. Maintain boiled water only until water systems are tested and cleared. Ensure toilet functions isolated from food sources. Establish hygiene protocols immediately.

## Venomous Creature Management

Tropical regions host many venomous creatures. Avoidance and identification are primary defenses.

### Snake Identification and Avoidance

| Characteristic | Venomous (Usually) | Non-Venomous |
|-----------------|-------------------|---------------|
| Head Shape | Triangular, wider than neck | Rounded, aligned with neck |
| Eyes | Vertical slits | Round pupils |
| Behavior | Aggressive, stands ground | Flees immediately |
| Color | Often bright or banded | Camouflaged, dull |

**Prevention:** Make noise while walking—most snakes avoid humans if warned. Clear shelter perimeter. Inspect shoes and gear before putting on. Shake out bedding in morning. Avoid snake habitats (tall grass, water edges) in early morning and evening when snakes hunt.

### Treatment of Envenomation

**Immediate Response:** Immobilize the bitten area. Keep it at heart level or below. Remove jewelry. Do not attempt to suck venom or apply tourniquets (outdated and harmful). Compress the bitten area with a bandage, similar to treating a sprain.

**Transport to Medical Care:** Snake venom effects develop over hours. Get medical care within 2-4 hours if possible. If alone in wilderness, remain calm and rest—activity increases venom circulation. Mark the bite time on skin with charcoal or ash.

:::info-box
**Antivenom Note:** Antivenom is specific to snake type. Knowing which snake bit you helps medical professionals, but don't capture the snake—photograph it if safe, or note distinctive characteristics.
:::

### Scorpions and Spiders

**Scorpion Prevention:** Tropical scorpions hide in shoes, clothing, and gear. Shake out all items before use. Inspect sleeping area thoroughly. Scorpion stings rarely fatal but extremely painful. Immerse sting area in hot water (as hot as tolerable without burning) for 20-45 minutes to reduce pain.

**Spider Hazards:** Most tropical spiders are harmless. Avoid reaching into dark spaces. Use light to inspect before inserting hands. Wear gloves when handling unknown materials.

### Insect Stings

**Wasps and Hornets:** Remove stinger by scraping (don't pinch). Apply cool compress. Elevation reduces swelling. Allergic reactions (anaphylaxis) require immediate medical attention—if available, access antihistamine or adrenaline immediately.

**Prevention:** Don't disturb nests. Move slowly around flying insects. Cover food and refuse to avoid attracting wasps.

:::warning
**⚠ Tropical Venomous Creature Safety:** Most venomous creatures avoid humans unless threatened. The key strategy is avoidance. Wear protective footwear, use light when moving in darkness, clear sleeping areas, and maintain situational awareness. Panic and rapid movement increase bite risk.
:::
