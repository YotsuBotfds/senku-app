## Common Illness Treatment

![Field Medicine Diagnosis Framework](../assets/svgs/field-medicine-diagnosis-treatment-1.svg)

![Field Medicine Treatment Protocols](../assets/svgs/field-medicine-diagnosis-treatment-2.svg)

### Fever Management

**Fever Treatment Protocol:**

1.  **Identify source:** Determine if fever is from infection, inflammation, or medication reaction
2.  **Physical cooling:** Remove excess clothing and blankets
3.  **Hydration:** Drink plenty of water, electrolyte solutions (see ORS recipe below)
4.  **Cool compresses:** Apply to forehead, under arms, groin (major blood vessels)
5.  **Sponging:** Use lukewarm water (not cold) on skin to encourage heat loss through evaporation
6.  **Medication:** Acetaminophen 650-1000mg every 4-6 hours OR ibuprofen 400-600mg every 6-8 hours
7.  **Monitoring:** Check temperature every 2-4 hours
8.  **When to worry:** Fever >104°F (40°C), fever in infant, persistent fever >7 days, fever with stiff neck or rash

:::info-box
**Note on Fever:** Low fever is body's defense mechanism. Only treat if uncomfortable or if fever becomes dangerously high. Many infections resolve within 3-7 days with supportive care alone.
:::

### Dehydration Treatment

**Rehydration Solution (ORS) Recipe:**

1.  Boil 1 liter of clean water and cool to room temperature
2.  Add 6 teaspoons (30ml) of sugar or honey
3.  Add 1/2 teaspoon (2.5g) of salt
4.  Add 1/4 teaspoon (1.25g) of salt substitute (potassium chloride) if available
5.  Stir until dissolved
6.  Solution is good for 24 hours at room temperature, longer if refrigerated

**Dehydration Management by Severity:**

1.  **Mild (dry mouth, thirst):** Drink sips of water frequently, ORS solution
2.  **Moderate (dry mouth, weakness, dizziness, decreased urination):** ORS solution 50-100ml per kg body weight over 4 hours, then maintenance (5-10ml/kg for each loose stool)
3.  **Severe (confusion, extreme weakness, no urine output):** Need IV fluids - seek immediate professional help. Until then, give ORS solution in small frequent doses
4.  Continue giving fluids after symptoms improve to prevent relapse
5.  Gradually return to normal diet once able to eat

### Respiratory Infections

:::note
**Common Cold/Upper Respiratory Infection:**

-   **Symptoms:** Runny nose, sneezing, sore throat, mild cough, congestion, malaise
-   **Treatment:** Rest, fluids, throat lozenges, honey for cough, saline nasal drops, fever reduction
-   **Duration:** 3-7 days, no antibiotics needed (viral)
:::

:::note
**Bronchitis:**

-   **Symptoms:** Persistent dry or productive cough (>1 week), chest discomfort, fatigue, shortness of breath
-   **Viral treatment:** Rest, fluids, cough suppressant at night, expectorant during day, honey, steam inhalation
-   **Bacterial signs:** Colored sputum (yellow/green), fever, progression - may need antibiotics (amoxicillin 500mg three times daily)
-   **Recovery:** 2-3 weeks typical
:::

:::note
**Pneumonia (Requires Antibiotics):**

-   **Symptoms:** High fever, chest pain with breathing, productive cough, shortness of breath, fatigue
-   **Treatment:** Amoxicillin 500mg three times daily OR doxycycline 100mg twice daily for 7-10 days
-   **Supportive care:** Fluids, rest, fever management, oxygen if available
-   **Warning signs:** Severe shortness of breath, confusion, blue lips - seek professional help immediately
:::

### Gastrointestinal Illness

**Acute Gastroenteritis (Food Poisoning/Viral Gastro):**

1.  **Initial phase:** Rest digestive system - no solid food for first 6-12 hours
2.  **Fluids:** Sip water, broth, ORS solution frequently in small amounts
3.  **Anti-nausea:** Ginger tea, peppermint tea, avoid strong smells
4.  **Medication:** Bismuth subsalicylate (Pepto-Bismol) or loperamide (Imodium) for diarrhea - BUT avoid if fever or bloody stools present
5.  **Gradual feeding:** After 12-24 hours, introduce bland foods (crackers, rice, toast, broth)
6.  **Recovery timeline:** Most viral gastro resolves in 24-48 hours; bacterial may last 3-5 days
7.  **Antibiotics:** Only if bacterial (prolonged illness, bloody stools, fever) - ciprofloxacin 500mg twice daily or doxycycline 100mg twice daily

:::warning
**Severe Dehydration Signs:** No urination for 8+ hours, extreme weakness, confusion, rapid heartbeat, dry mouth and eyes. This is life-threatening in children and elderly - aggressive rehydration needed.
:::

### Urinary Tract Infections

:::note
**Symptoms:**

-   Dysuria (painful urination)
-   Frequency (frequent urination, especially at night)
-   Urgency (sudden strong need to urinate)
-   Suprapubic pain (pain above pubic bone)
-   Fever, chills, lower back pain (indicates upper UTI/pyelonephritis)
:::

**UTI Management:**

1.  **Prevention:** Drink plenty of water, urinate frequently, wipe front to back (females), void after intercourse
2.  **Home care:** Increased fluid intake, cranberry juice (mild benefit), heat application to lower abdomen
3.  **Antibiotics:** Ciprofloxacin 250mg twice daily for 3 days (uncomplicated) or 7-14 days if complicated/pyelonephritis
4.  **Alternative antibiotics:** Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole DS one tablet twice daily for 3-7 days
5.  **Pain management:** Acetaminophen or ibuprofen
6.  **When to treat urgently:** Fever >101°F, flank pain, nausea/vomiting (upper UTI) - need antibiotics immediately

### Skin Infections

:::note
**Cellulitis (Spreading Bacterial Skin Infection):**

-   **Signs:** Red, warm, swollen, tender area; may have fever, red streaking (indicates serious)
-   **Treatment:** Antibiotic immediately - amoxicillin-clavulanate 500/125mg three times daily OR doxycycline 100mg twice daily for 7-10 days
-   **Care:** Elevate affected area, warm compresses, clean gently daily
-   **Warning:** Red streaking toward heart indicates systemic infection - urgent medical attention needed
:::

:::note
**Abscess (Localized Collection of Pus):**

-   **Signs:** Localized swelling, fluctuance (feels like fluid inside), warmth, pain
-   **Early treatment:** Warm compresses may allow drainage, antibiotics
-   **If need drainage:** See field surgery section for incision and drainage procedure
-   **Do NOT squeeze:** Risks pushing infection deeper
:::

:::note
**Fungal Infections (Tinea, Athlete's Foot, Candida):**

-   **Signs:** Itching, redness, white coating or scaling
-   **Treatment:** Antifungal cream (miconazole, clotrimazole) applied twice daily for 2-4 weeks
-   **Keep dry:** Good hygiene, keep area clean and dry
-   **Oral for severe:** Fluconazole 150mg as single dose (if available) for yeast infections
:::

### Dental Emergencies

**Tooth Pain Management:**

1.  **Immediate relief:** Ibuprofen 600-800mg or acetaminophen 650-1000mg
2.  **Topical:** Clove oil (eugenol) on affected area - natural anesthetic
3.  **Salt water rinse:** Dissolve 1/2 teaspoon salt in 8oz warm water, rinse 2-3 times daily
4.  **Cold compress:** Apply to cheek for 15 minutes at a time to numb pain
5.  **Avoid:** Very hot/cold foods, hard foods, chewing on affected side
6.  **If abscess:** Oral antibiotics - amoxicillin 500mg three times daily for 7 days

**Knocked Out Tooth (Avulsed Tooth):**

1.  If tooth is intact and fresh, recovery is possible with prompt action
2.  Handle by crown only, not the root
3.  Rinse tooth gently with saline (don't scrub)
4.  Try to place back in socket - bite gently on gauze to hold in place
5.  If can't place, keep tooth in saline or milk in a container
6.  Seek emergency dental care as soon as possible (best within 1 hour)

![Offline Medical Reference diagram 4](../assets/svgs/medical-reference-4.svg)

:::card
#### Pulse (Heart Rate)

**How to measure:**

-   Use radial artery: place 2-3 fingers (index, middle, ring) on inner wrist below thumb
-   Or carotid artery: place 2 fingers on side of neck between muscle and trachea
-   Count beats for 60 seconds, or count for 15 seconds and multiply by 4
-   Note regularity: regular or irregular rhythm
-   Note strength: strong, weak, or thready (barely palpable)
:::

:::card
#### Respiration Rate

**How to measure:**

-   Watch chest/abdomen rise and fall (one complete breath = one in + one out)
-   Count for 60 seconds at rest
-   Note effort: labored (difficult) or easy
-   Note pattern: regular or irregular
-   Assess depth: shallow, normal, or deep breathing
:::

:::card
#### Blood Pressure

**Manual Measurement (without machine):**

-   Approximate systolic: locate radial pulse, apply pressure to arm until pulse disappears, note when it returns = systolic
-   Full reading requires stethoscope and cuff (may not be available)
-   Estimate as normal (pulse palpable), low (weak/absent pulse), or high (flushed face)
:::

:::card
#### Temperature

**Measurement Methods:**

-   **Oral:** Thermometer under tongue, 3 minutes, no hot/cold drinks first
-   **Axillary (armpit):** Less accurate, add 1°F to reading
-   **Rectal:** Most accurate, use 1 minute
-   **No thermometer:** Feel forehead and compare to yourself; can feel for fever
:::

### Capillary Refill Test

**Indicator of Circulation and Shock:**

1.  Press on fingernail or skin until it blanches (turns white)
2.  Release pressure
3.  Normal: color returns in <2 seconds (good circulation)
4.  Delayed: returns in >2 seconds (indicates poor circulation, shock)

### Pupil Response Test

**Indicator of Neurological Status:**

1.  In dark environment, observe pupil size
2.  Shine light in one eye
3.  Normal: pupil constricts (gets smaller) in 1-2 seconds
4.  Both pupils should respond equally (consensual response)
5.  Abnormal: dilated pupils (pain, intoxication, serious head injury), pinpoint pupils (opioid use, stroke)
6.  Unequal pupils: possible head injury or stroke

## Field Surgery Basics

![Field Surgery Procedures and Techniques](../assets/svgs/field-medicine-diagnosis-treatment-5.svg)

![Field Surgery Complications Management](../assets/svgs/field-medicine-diagnosis-treatment-6.svg)

:::warning
**Critical Legal and Ethical Note:** Field surgery should ONLY be attempted when professional medical help is unavailable and the condition is immediately life-threatening. Use of these procedures without proper training and in situations where medical help is available could result in serious harm or legal consequences.
:::

### When Surgery is Necessary in Field Conditions

:::note
**Situations Warranting Surgical Intervention:**

-   Uncontrolled life-threatening hemorrhage not responding to pressure/tourniquets
-   Tension pneumothorax (collapsed lung with breathing difficulty)
-   Airway obstruction that cannot be cleared by other means
-   Compartment syndrome (muscle swelling cutting off circulation)
-   Large abscess with systemic infection/sepsis
-   Foreign body deeply embedded causing major bleeding or organ dysfunction
-   Bowel perforation with peritonitis
:::

### Abscess Drainage (I&D - Incision and Drainage)

**Procedure for Simple Abscess Drainage:**

1.  **Preparation:** Sterilize area with iodine or alcohol. Gather sterile supplies: scalpel, gauze, drainage material
2.  **Anesthesia:** If available, inject local anesthetic (lidocaine) around abscess perimeter
3.  **Incision:** Make small incision (0.5-1cm) over point of maximum fluctuance (feels like fluid)
4.  **Direction:** Incise along skin lines when possible to minimize scarring
5.  **Expression:** Gentle pressure to express pus (may be copious)
6.  **Irrigation:** Flush cavity with saline to remove debris
7.  **Drainage:** May leave small drain (gauze wick) if abscess was large
8.  **Dressing:** Sterile gauge packing if leaving open to drain
9.  **Follow-up:** Change dressing daily, watch for signs of continued infection

### Wound Exploration and Foreign Body Removal

**Removing Embedded Objects:**

1.  **Do NOT remove:** Objects impaled in chest, abdomen, or large vessels - stabilize and seek help
2.  **Assess:** Determine depth and proximity to vessels/nerves
3.  **Anesthesia:** Local or regional anesthesia if available
4.  **Exposure:** Gently open wound to visualize object without traumatizing tissue
5.  **Remove:** Slow, careful extraction along the angle it entered - pulling at wrong angle causes more damage
6.  **Hemorrhage:** If bleeding occurs, apply direct pressure
7.  **Irrigation:** Thoroughly irrigate wound after removal
8.  **Closure:** Close wound or leave open depending on contamination/infection risk

### Emergency Airway Management

**Jaw Thrust Maneuver (if neck injury suspected):**

1.  Position behind patient's head
2.  Place hands on either side of patient's face
3.  Use thumb to hold mouth open while jaw is thrust forward
4.  This opens airway without moving neck

**Head Tilt-Chin Lift (if no neck injury):**

1.  Place hand on forehead, tilt head back gently
2.  With other hand, lift chin forward
3.  This straightens airway

**Emergency Cricothyrotomy (Last Resort for Complete Airway Obstruction):**

1.  **When:** Complete airway obstruction (choking not relieved, severe facial trauma, tongue obstruction) AND cannot intubate
2.  **Anatomy:** Locate cricoid cartilage (hard ring below Adam's apple)
3.  **Incision:** Make small horizontal incision through skin and membrane between cricoid and thyroid
4.  **Insertion:** Insert tracheostomy tube or needle (if available) into opening
5.  **Breathing:** Person should be able to breathe through opening
6.  **Stabilize:** Secure tube to prevent dislodgement
7.  **Seek help:** This is emergency measure only - get to professional help immediately

### Improvised Surgical Instruments

:::note
**Sterilization Methods (Off-Grid):**

-   Boiling: 20+ minutes in water kills most pathogens
-   Fire: Hold metal instruments in flame for 30 seconds
-   Pressure cooker: 15-20 minutes at high pressure (most effective if available)
-   Alcohol: 70% isopropyl for 5+ minutes (not complete sterilization but helps)
:::

:::note
**Improvised Tools:**

-   **Scalpel:** Sharp knife blade, razor blade, or glass shard (sterilized)
-   **Forceps:** Tweezers, pliers (sterilized) for holding/removing objects
-   **Needle/thread:** Sewing needle and thread (sterilized) for suturing
-   **Syringe:** Improvise with bulb or plunger-type syringe for irrigation
-   **Tourniquet:** Belt, cloth strip, or cord
-   **Dressings:** Clean cloth, boiled and dried
:::

## Childbirth & Women's Health

### Normal Labor Stages

:::note
**Stage 1 - Labor (Contractions and Cervical Dilation):**

-   **Early/Latent Phase:** Contractions 15-30 minutes apart, mild to moderate intensity, woman can walk and talk. May last 6-8 hours or longer
-   **Active Phase:** Contractions 3-5 minutes apart, intense, woman focuses on breathing. Lasts 3-5 hours. Cervix dilates from 4-7cm
-   **Transition Phase:** Contractions 2-3 minutes apart, very intense, overwhelming sensation. Lasts 15 minutes to 1 hour. Cervix dilates to 10cm (fully dilated)
-   **Duration:** First time mothers average 12-14 hours; subsequent pregnancies may be faster
:::

:::note
**Stage 2 - Pushing (Delivery of Baby):**

-   **Duration:** 20 minutes to 2 hours (shorter in experienced mothers)
-   **Process:** Woman feels urge to push with contractions, pushes with each contraction
-   **Baby descent:** Crowning (top of head visible), then full emergence
-   **Assistance:** Guide head out gently, help body follow
:::

:::note
**Stage 3 - Afterbirth (Delivery of Placenta):**

-   **Duration:** 5-30 minutes after baby is born
-   **Contractions:** Usually milder contractions help expel placenta
-   **Signs:** Increased vaginal bleeding, urge to push, placenta appears at introitus
-   **Delivery:** Placenta comes out naturally with gentle traction on umbilical cord
-   **Cord clamping:** Wait 30-60 seconds before clamping to allow blood transfer to baby
:::

### Emergency Delivery Procedure

**Assisting With Delivery:**

1.  **Preparation:** Wash hands, sterilize any instruments, gather clean cloths and materials
2.  **Position:** Mother on back or semi-sitting, legs elevated, perineal area accessible
3.  **Crowning:** When head is visible, guide gently with downward and backward motion
4.  **Head delivery:** Support head as it comes out, check for umbilical cord around neck (nuchal cord) - if present, gently slip over head
5.  **Body delivery:** With next contraction, guide body out by gently supporting shoulders
6.  **Suction:** Gently clear mouth/nose of mucus if possible
7.  **First breaths:** Dry baby immediately, stimulate to cry
8.  **Cord clamping:** Wait 30-60 seconds, then clamp/tie cord in two places, cut between

### Cord Care and Placenta Delivery

**Cord Management:**

1.  If no clamps available: tie cord with sterile string/cloth in two places, cut between with sterile scissors or blade
2.  After cutting, leave stump 1-2 inches long
3.  Keep cord stump clean and dry
4.  Fold diaper below cord to air-expose it
5.  Cord separates naturally in 5-14 days
6.  Watch for signs of infection: redness, swelling, discharge with foul odor

**Placenta Delivery:**

1.  After baby is born, continued contractions help expel placenta
2.  Do NOT pull on cord excessively (risk of retained placenta or uterine inversion)
3.  Gentle traction on cord with other hand supporting uterus from above
4.  When you feel it separate, placenta should come out
5.  Inspect placenta for complete delivery (should be intact with all lobes)
6.  If placenta retained after 30 minutes, may need manual removal or medical intervention

### Postpartum Hemorrhage

:::warning
**Critical:** Postpartum hemorrhage is leading cause of maternal mortality. Recognize and treat aggressively.
:::

**Recognition and Treatment:**

1.  **Normal bleeding:** Heavy flow (soaking pad in 1-2 hours) is normal for first 24 hours
2.  **Hemorrhage signs:** Soaking more than one pad per hour for more than 2 hours, dizziness, weakness, rapid pulse, cold/clammy skin
3.  **Causes:** Uterine atony (uterus not contracting), retained placenta, tissue tears, infection
4.  **Massage:** Firmly massage uterus (just below navel) in circular motion to encourage contraction
5.  **Medications:** Oxytocin 10 units IM if available (causes uterine contraction), methergine if available (ergot derivative)
6.  **Fluids:** Start IV fluids (saline) if available to maintain circulation
7.  **Packing:** If bleeding won't stop, pack vagina with clean cloth/gauze
8.  **Seek emergency help:** Hemorrhage is medical emergency - professional care critical

### Breastfeeding Basics

:::note
**Initiation and Basics:**

-   **First feeding:** Begin within first hour of birth if possible
-   **Position:** Baby's mouth should cover entire areola (dark area), not just nipple
-   **Frequency:** Feed 8-12 times per day initially (2-3 hours apart) or on demand
-   **Duration:** 10-20 minutes per side, empty one breast before switching
-   **Milk production:** Increases with frequent emptying
-   **Colostrum:** First milk is yellowish, nutrient-rich, laxative effect helps baby pass meconium
:::

:::note
**Common Problems:**

-   **Sore nipples:** Ensure proper latch, air dry between feeds, lanolin cream
-   **Engorgement:** Breasts hard and painful - express some milk to soften, frequent feeding
-   **Mastitis:** Infection of breast tissue - firm, red, painful area, fever. Treat with heat, frequent feeding, antibiotics if available
-   **Low milk supply:** Increase frequency of feeding, ensure deep latch, hydration
:::

### Common Pregnancy Complications

:::note
**Preeclampsia (Pregnancy-Induced Hypertension):**

-   **Signs:** Blood pressure >140/90, swelling (especially face/hands), headache, visual disturbances, upper abdominal pain
-   **Serious:** Can progress to eclampsia (seizures) or HELLP syndrome
-   **Treatment:** Bed rest, monitor blood pressure, antihypertensive medications if available
-   **Urgent:** Delivery of baby is definitive treatment - seek obstetric care
:::

:::note
**Gestational Diabetes:**

-   **Screening:** Glucose screening at 24-28 weeks
-   **Management:** Diet control (low sugar), monitor blood glucose if possible
-   **Risk:** Large babies, delivery complications
-   **Resolution:** Usually resolves after delivery
:::

:::note
**Placental Abruption (Premature Separation):**

-   **Signs:** Vaginal bleeding, abdominal pain, uterine tenderness, shock symptoms
-   **Severity:** Can vary from minor to life-threatening
-   **Treatment:** Seek emergency care - may need emergency delivery to save mother and baby
:::

:::note
**Placenta Previa (Placenta Covering Cervix):**

-   **Signs:** Vaginal bleeding (painless) in second/third trimester
-   **Management:** Pelvic rest, avoid penetrating exams (risk of hemorrhage)
-   **Delivery:** Requires cesarean section - vaginal delivery would cause severe hemorrhage
:::

## Sanitation & Disease Prevention

### Latrine Construction for Off-Grid Living

**Pit Latrine Construction:**

1.  **Location:** At least 50 meters (150 feet) downhill from water source, away from living areas
2.  **Dig pit:** 1-2 meters deep (or until hits bedrock/water table)
3.  **Diameter:** 1-1.5 meters wide
4.  **Superstructure:** Build simple structure with seat/platform over pit (wood frame with hole)
5.  **Privacy:** Enclose with cloth/wood walls
6.  **Covering:** Keep seat covered when not in use (prevents flies, odor)
7.  **Maintenance:** Add ash or lime periodically to reduce odor and pathogens
8.  **Closure:** When full (3-5 years), fill with soil and build new latrine elsewhere

**Composting Toilet (Better Option):**

1.  **Container:** Large bucket or drum with seat attached
2.  **Material:** After each use, add 2-3 handfuls of carbon material (dry leaves, wood chips, sawdust)
3.  **Moisture:** Keep contents slightly moist (like wrung-out sponge)
4.  **Aeration:** Ensure air flow (compost needs oxygen)
5.  **Decomposition:** Over 6-12 months, waste breaks down into harmless compost
6.  **Emptying:** Remove finished compost (safe for garden but not vegetables you eat raw)
7.  **No flies:** Regular covering with carbon prevents odor and insects

### Hand Hygiene Critical Points

:::note
**When to Wash Hands:**

-   Before preparing food
-   Before eating
-   After using toilet
-   After contact with feces (diaper change, latrine use)
-   Before treating wounds or handling medical supplies
-   After handling infected materials
-   After animal contact
:::

**Proper Hand Washing (Without Running Water):**

1.  Pour water (boiled and cooled if necessary) over hands
2.  Apply soap (if available) or ash/sand (abrasive, can substitute)
3.  Scrub all surfaces: palms, backs, between fingers, fingernails, wrists
4.  Scrub for at least 20 seconds
5.  Rinse with clean water until soap is gone
6.  Dry with clean cloth (cloth should be washed regularly)
7.  If no water: alcohol-based sanitizer works, or use clean cloth to wipe dirt off first then sanitize

### Water Safety and Purification

:::note
**Water-Borne Diseases:**

-   **Bacterial:** Cholera, typhoid, dysentery, E. coli
-   **Viral:** Hepatitis A, polio (rare), norovirus
-   **Parasitic:** Giardia, cryptosporidium, hookworm
-   **Chemical:** Heavy metals, arsenic (source-dependent)
:::

**Water Purification Methods (Multi-Barrier Approach Best):**

1.  **Boiling:** Most reliable. Boil for 1 minute (3 minutes if at high altitude) kills all pathogens. Cool and store in clean container
2.  **Filtration:** Sand and gravel filter removes sediment and some bacteria; doesn't kill all pathogens
3.  **Chemical:** Bleach (2-8 drops per liter depending on clarity) kills bacteria/viruses but not parasites. Wait 30 minutes
4.  **Iodine:** Iodine tablets or liquid kills most pathogens but not cryptosporidium. Follow product instructions
5.  **Combination:** Filter first (removes sediment), then boil or chemical treatment most effective

### Quarantine Procedures

**Isolating Contagious Illness:**

1.  **Isolation room:** Separate sick person from others if possible (one room, separate entrance if available)
2.  **Bedding:** Dedicate clean bedding; wash separately after illness
3.  **Food/drink:** Use separate dishes, utensils; wash in hot water with soap
4.  **Hygiene:** Sick person washes hands frequently; helpers wear masks if available
5.  **Coughing/sneezing:** Cover with tissue or elbow, dispose properly
6.  **Duration:** Respiratory illness - isolate for 5+ days or until fever-free for 24 hours without medication
7.  **Gastrointestinal:** Isolate until diarrhea resolves; strict hand hygiene critical
8.  **Return to activity:** When well enough and no longer contagious

### Basic Epidemiology Concepts

:::note
**Disease Transmission Routes:**

-   **Respiratory:** Coughing, sneezing, talking (droplets travel ~6 feet)
-   **Fecal-oral:** Poor hygiene, contaminated water/food
-   **Contact:** Touching infected person or contaminated surface
-   **Vector:** Insect bites (mosquito, tick)
-   **Bloodborne:** Blood or body fluid contact
:::

:::note
**Prevention Strategies:**

-   **Hand hygiene:** Single most important prevention measure
-   **Clean water:** Prevents waterborne diseases
-   **Food safety:** Cook meat thoroughly, avoid cross-contamination
-   **Sanitation:** Proper latrine/waste disposal
-   **Respiratory hygiene:** Cover coughs, separate sick people
-   **Vaccination:** If available, prevents many diseases
-   **Pest control:** Reduce insect/rodent populations
:::

**Disease Outbreak Response:**

1.  Identify sick person(s) and symptoms
2.  Isolate from others if contagious disease suspected
3.  Trace contacts: who has been in close contact in last 1-14 days (depends on disease)
4.  Monitor contacts for symptoms: twice daily temperature and symptom checks
5.  Keep contacts quarantined for incubation period (varies by disease)
6.  Treat symptomatic cases with available medications/supportive care
7.  Enhance hygiene measures: handwashing, food safety, sanitation
8.  Seek professional help if outbreak overwhelms local capacity

### Vector-Borne Disease Prevention

:::note
**Mosquito-Borne Illnesses (Malaria, Dengue, Zika, Yellow Fever):**

-   **Prevention:** Eliminate standing water (breeding grounds), use screens, sleep under nets
-   **Protective wear:** Long sleeves/pants during dawn/dusk (peak mosquito hours)
-   **Repellent:** DEET, picaridin, or lemon eucalyptus oil
-   **Recognition:** Fever, chills, body aches, headache
-   **Malaria treatment:** Artemether-lumefantrine or other antimalarial if available
:::

:::note
**Tick-Borne Illnesses (Lyme disease, Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever):**

-   **Prevention:** Avoid tall grass/brush, wear protective clothing, check body for ticks
-   **Tick removal:** Use tweezers, pull straight out (don't twist), save for identification
-   **Lyme disease signs:** Bullseye rash, flu-like symptoms, joint pain
-   **Early treatment:** Doxycycline 100mg twice daily for 14-21 days (most effective if started early)
:::

## Recognizing and Treating Infection

### Early Signs of Infection (First 48 Hours Post-Injury)

-   Redness: Pink to red skin surrounding wound, spreading outward
-   Warmth: Area feels warm to touch compared to surrounding skin
-   Swelling: Increasing puffiness around wound (can worsen over hours)
-   Pus: Yellow/greenish drainage (indicates bacterial infection)
-   Odor: Foul smell from wound (suggests bacterial overgrowth)
-   Pain: Throbbing or progressive pain despite treatment
-   Red streaking: URGENT: Red lines extending from wound toward heart indicate spreading infection

### Systemic Infection Signs (Spreading Beyond Local Wound)

-   Fever: Temperature above 100.4°F (38°C), usually appearing 12-24 hours after infection begins
-   Chills: Body shaking, often precedes fever spike
-   Swollen lymph nodes: Lumps in neck, armpit, or groin on same side as infected wound
-   Fatigue: Overwhelming tiredness, weakness
-   Headache: General malaise, joint aches
-   Nausea/vomiting: Indicates significant infection
-   Rapid pulse: Heart rate above 100 bpm at rest
-   Mental changes: Confusion, delirium (dangerous sign - means infection is severe)

### Infection Management by Stage

:::note
**Stage 1: Early Localized Infection (First 24-48 hours)**

1.  Increase wound cleaning frequency to 4-6 times daily
2.  Use hot water (as hot as can tolerate without burning) with honey, garlic, or thyme wash
3.  Apply raw honey poultice and cover; change 2-3 times daily
4.  Drain any visible pus: gently express by pressing around wound margins (allows drainage)
5.  Elevate infected area above heart level to reduce swelling and improve drainage
6.  Internal treatment: Garlic + turmeric internally 2-3 times daily for antibacterial effect
7.  Monitor closely: If not improving in 24 hours, escalate treatment
:::

:::note
**Stage 2: Spreading or Worsening Infection**

1.  More aggressive drainage: If safe, open any abscess pocket to allow pus drainage
2.  High-dose herbal treatment: Garlic 4-5 cloves crushed 3-4 times daily, turmeric + ginger paste 3 times daily
3.  Continue frequent wound washing (4-6 times daily minimum)
4.  Poultice changes every 4-6 hours
5.  Cold compress 15-20 min at a time if fever develops (to help regulate body temperature)
6.  Increase fluids significantly (prevents dehydration from fever)
7.  If fever develops, monitor vital signs every 4 hours
8.  Watch for red streaking (emergency sign)
:::

:::note
**Stage 3: Systemic Infection (Fever + Spreading Redness)**

1.  This is a medical emergency. Try everything possible to slow infection
2.  Keep infected area immobilized to slow spread
3.  Aggressive internal treatment: Maximum herbal antibacterials
4.  If amputation is necessary for survival: Have realistic discussion about what signs indicate this
5.  Maintain hydration and nutrition despite nausea
6.  If no improvement in 48-72 hours despite aggressive treatment, consider amputation (slow progression of infection to vital organs is fatal)
:::

## Oral Rehydration Solution - Exact Ratios

:::info-box
**Critical for survival:** In off-grid conditions with diarrhea, vomiting, or extreme heat, oral rehydration solution (ORS) is life-saving. It replaces water AND electrolytes lost through fluid loss.
:::

:::highlight-box
**WHO Standard Oral Rehydration Solution Recipe (Low-Osmolarity)**

-   **Salt (sodium chloride):** 2.6 grams per liter (or 1/2 teaspoon per liter)
-   **Sugar (glucose):** 13.5 grams per liter (or 2.5 tablespoons per liter)
-   **Potassium chloride (salt substitute):** 1.5 grams per liter (optional but ideal if available)
-   **Water:** 1 liter (clean, boiled if possible)
-   **Optional flavoring:** Orange juice or lemon juice (also provides potassium)
:::

:::highlight-box
**Field-Expedient ORS (No Measuring Equipment)**

In 1 quart (liter) clean water:

-   **Salt:** Pinch in 3 fingers (about 1/2 teaspoon)
-   **Sugar:** 2-3 big handfuls or approximately 2-3 tablespoons
-   **Stir thoroughly** until dissolved
:::

### Administration Protocol

-   Mild dehydration: Drink 50 mL (3 tablespoons) every 5-10 minutes, conscious and can swallow
-   Moderate dehydration: Drink 100 mL (6 tablespoons) every 10-15 minutes
-   Severe dehydration with vomiting: Drink 5-10 mL (1-2 teaspoons) every 2-3 minutes, very slowly to prevent vomiting
-   Continue until: Urine becomes pale/clear, thirst is quenched, person feels better
-   Duration: May need to continue ORS for 4-24 hours depending on fluid loss cause

### Alternative Fluid Sources (When ORS Not Available)

-   Coconut water: Natural electrolyte balance, excellent if available
-   Broth with salt: Contains salt and some nutrients
-   Sugar water with salt pinch: Better than plain water
-   Avoid: Plain water only (dilutes remaining electrolytes), caffeine/alcohol (increases loss)

## Epidemic & Pandemic Response

### Recognizing Outbreak Patterns

Early detection of outbreaks is critical for containment. Before modern surveillance systems, communities identified outbreaks by recognizing unusual patterns of illness.

**Key outbreak indicators:**

-   **Temporal cluster:** Multiple cases of same disease appearing within days (not weeks/months)
-   **Geographic cluster:** Cases concentrated in specific area or institution (school, market, gathering place)
-   **Demographic cluster:** Specific age group or population affected (children, elderly, occupation-specific)
-   **Rapid increase in cases:** Doubling of cases every 2-7 days suggests active spread

**Calculate attack rate (indicates outbreak severity):**

Attack Rate (%) = (Number of people who became ill / Total people exposed) × 100

**Attack rate interpretation:**

-   <1%: Sporadic cases, endemic level
-   1-5%: Small outbreak, limited spread
-   5-20%: Moderate outbreak, active transmission
-   \>20%: Severe outbreak, rapid person-to-person spread

**Example calculation:** If a school has 500 students and 45 develop diarrhea in one week, the attack rate is (45/500) × 100 = 9%. This 9% rate indicates moderate outbreak, suggesting a contamination event (water, food) or infectious agent spread.

### Infection Control Without Modern Supplies

**Improvised PPE (Personal Protective Equipment)**

**Face coverings (for respiratory protection):**

-   **Cloth masks:** Layer 3-4 pieces of tight-weave cotton (bed sheets, T-shirts); effectiveness ~50-60% vs. N95's 95%, but infinitely better than bare face
-   **Makeshift N95:** Multiple cloth layers + one layer of silk or chiffon (electrostatic properties); more filtration than plain cloth
-   **Mask fastening:** Use ear loops from cloth strips, elastic bands, or tie around head; mask must seal edges to be effective
-   **Duration:** Replace when damp (sweat/breath moisture reduces effectiveness); reusable masks should be washed daily in hot water
-   **Fit testing:** Mask is effective only if properly sealed; check seal by inhaling sharply (should feel air resistance, not leaks at edges)

**Eye protection (goggles/face shields):**

-   **Improvised goggles:** Clear plastic bags/wrapping secured around eyes with elastic bands; protects from splashing of body fluids
-   **Face shields:** Clear plastic sheets (from windows, packaging) attached to a headband; covers entire face
-   **Alternative:** Swim goggles if available; not ideal but better than nothing for aerosol exposure
-   **Cleaning:** Wipe with cloth after patient contact; disinfect with available solutions

**Gowns/protective clothing:**

-   **Washable gowns:** Cotton or plastic aprons worn over clothing; change between patients or when visibly soiled
-   **Plastic alternatives:** Heavy plastic sheeting can be fashioned into simple garments or worn as overlay
-   **Sleeve protection:** Forearms are frequent contact points; wrap with cloth or plastic secured with tape
-   **Foot protection:** If handling highly contaminated materials, wrap feet with plastic bags secured with elastic bands

**Gloves:**

-   **Reusable options:** Rubber household gloves (dish gloves) can be washed and reused; most durable option
-   **Cloth options:** Tightly woven cotton gloves provide minimal protection but better than bare hands
-   **Plastic alternatives:** Plastic bags as glove liners with cloth gloves on top; secure with tape at wrist
-   **When gloves unavailable:** Focus intensely on hand hygiene instead; gloves provide false sense of security if hands not washed after glove removal

**Hand hygiene with limited water**

**Hand washing protocol (requires ~5 liters/person/day):**

1.  Rinse hands with small amount of water (cup, not running)
2.  Apply soap (bar, powdered, or soap made from ash + oil)
3.  Rub vigorously: palms, between fingers, back of hands, wrists (20-30 seconds)
4.  Rinse with second cup of clean water
5.  Dry on clean cloth or in air

**Soap alternatives when soap unavailable:**

-   **Ash soap:** Combine wood ash with grease/oil in 1:2 ratio; let sit 1-2 weeks; strains off top layer (actual soap); effective but harsh
-   **Vinegar rinse:** After soap/water wash, final rinse with diluted vinegar (1 part vinegar to 4 parts water) increases antimicrobial effect
-   **Saltwater wash:** 1 teaspoon salt per cup of water; antimicrobial but less effective than soap
-   **Alcohol-based:** Any >60% alcohol (spirits) as hand sanitizer when water unavailable; less effective than washing but better than nothing

**Water conservation strategies:**

-   Hand sanitizer stations at entrance to patient areas: smaller volume needed
-   Footbaths at exits: single bucket can do multiple people (change water every 4-6 people)
-   Prioritize handwashing at critical times: before eating, after toilet, before/after patient contact
-   Dry hand rubbing with cloth between water-based washes (removes visible dirt before water wash)

**Surface decontamination with available chemicals**

### Setting Up an Isolation Ward

**Airflow direction (negative pressure concepts):**

In a pandemic/epidemic, if you have multiple sick patients, separating them into designated area reduces transmission to healthy population. True negative pressure requires equipment, but passive ventilation helps:

-   **Window placement:** Position sick patients in area with windows that can be opened; air flows OUT of patient area to outside (away from rest of facility)
-   **Door direction:** If possible, patient area has separate entrance/exit not shared with healthy areas
-   **Ventilation:** Keep windows/doors open unless weather prevents it; moving air reduces pathogen concentration
-   **Airflow indicator:** Light candle near window; if smoke exits outward, good airflow. If smoke comes inside, seal that window and use another.
-   **Decontamination zones:** Create buffer zones between patient area and clean areas

### Common Epidemic Diseases and Field Identification

![Epidemic Disease Recognition and Management](../assets/svgs/field-medicine-diagnosis-treatment-7.svg)

![Epidemic Response Workflow](../assets/svgs/field-medicine-diagnosis-treatment-8.svg)

![Disease Surveillance and Tracking Systems](../assets/svgs/field-medicine-diagnosis-treatment-9.svg)

**Cholera**

**Transmission:** Contaminated water/food (faecal-oral route). Extremely rapid onset and severe dehydration.

**Field identification:**

-   **"Rice-water" stools:** Watery diarrhea with white/gray flecks, looks like rice water; unique to cholera
-   **Rapid onset:** Diarrhea + vomiting can start within 12 hours of ingestion
-   **Severe dehydration signs:** Sunken eyes, dry skin that doesn't bounce back when pinched, weak/rapid pulse, low BP
-   **Shock risk:** Can progress to severe shock and death within 18-24 hours if untreated
-   **Fluid loss:** Up to 1 liter/hour; most rapid fluid loss of any diarrheal disease

**Treatment:** ORS is 99% effective. Give frequently in small amounts. If patient can't keep fluids down, IV fluids preferred (if available), otherwise continue ORS every few minutes. Zinc supplementation (if available) prevents future infections. Antibiotics (doxycycline if available) reduce duration.

**Typhoid Fever**

**Transmission:** Contaminated food/water (Salmonella bacteria). Slower onset than cholera.

**Field identification:**

-   **Rose spots rash:** Faint pink/red spots on trunk (pathognomonic sign); appears week 2
-   **Stepwise fever pattern:** Fever increases daily in steps over 3-4 days, then plateaus at 103-104°F (39-40°C)
-   **Headache, body aches:** Severe, often more prominent than respiratory symptoms
-   **Abdominal involvement:** Constipation (early) or diarrhea, tender abdomen
-   **Delirium:** Confusion, delirium, muttering; appears in untreated cases by week 3
-   **Splenomegaly:** Enlarged spleen common; can perforate (surgical emergency)

**Treatment:** Antibiotics essential (ciprofloxacin, ceftriaxone if available). Without antibiotics, mortality ~20%. With antibiotics, mortality <1%. Supportive care: fluids, acetaminophen for fever. Isolation for 1-2 weeks.

**Dysentery (Bloody Diarrhea)**

**Two types with different field presentations:**

-   **BACILLARY dysentery (Shigella):**
    -   Rapid onset (12-72 hours)
    -   Frequent small bloody stools (20-30+/day)
    -   Tenesmus (painful straining)
    -   High fever (103-104°F), severe cramping
    -   Often improves in 5-7 days without treatment
    -   Can spread rapidly in crowded conditions
-   **AMOEBIC dysentery (Entamoeba histolytica):**
    -   Slower onset (7-21 days)
    -   Fewer, larger bloody/mucoid stools (3-8/day)
    -   Less fever, milder cramping than bacillary
    -   Can have intestinal ulceration complications
    -   Recurrent symptoms if untreated; can become chronic
    -   Risk of amoebic liver abscess (fever, RUQ pain, hepatomegaly)

**Treatment:** ORS for both. Bacillary: antibiotics (ciprofloxacin, azithromycin) if available. Amoebic: specific anti-amoebic drugs (metronidazole preferred if available); luminal agent (paromomycin) to eliminate cysts.

**Influenza & Pneumonia**

**Field identification of influenza:**

-   Sudden onset fever (101-103°F), chills, malaise
-   Myalgias (muscle aches) prominent; worse than common cold
-   Headache, cough often appears after fever
-   Respiratory symptoms: cough (may become productive), sore throat
-   Duration: 3-7 days for uncomplicated illness

**Complications:** Pneumonia (fever not improving by day 5, worsening cough, shortness of breath), secondary bacterial pneumonia.

**Field identification of pneumonia:**

-   Productive cough (sputum production)
-   Shortness of breath; fast breathing (>30/min)
-   Chest pain, especially with deep breathing
-   Fever often present

**Treatment:** Antiviral drugs (oseltamivir/Tamiflu) most effective if given within 48 hours; supportive care, fluids, rest. For bacterial pneumonia: antibiotics (amoxicillin, azithromycin, or fluoroquinolone if available). Observe for deterioration; respiratory support.

**Measles**

**Field identification:**

-   **Prodrome phase (3-4 days):** Fever, cough, runny nose, conjunctivitis ("3 Cs: cough, coryza, conjunctivitis")
-   **Koplik spots (pathognomonic):** White spots on inside cheek appearing 2-3 days before rash
-   **Rash phase:** Starts on face, spreads downward over 3-4 days; flat red spots, maculopapular; blanches with pressure
-   **Fever pattern:** High fever (104-105°F) with rash
-   **Symptoms worsen with rash:** Cough, eye discharge peak with rash onset

**Complications:** Otitis media, pneumonia, encephalitis, subacute sclerosing panencephalitis.

**Treatment:** Supportive care; vitamin A supplementation (if available: 200,000 IU daily for 2 days). Antibiotics if secondary infection develops. Isolation: 4 days after rash onset.

**Pertussis (Whooping Cough)**

**Field identification:**

-   **Catarrhal phase (1-2 weeks):** Mild URI symptoms, runny nose, mild cough
-   **Paroxysmal phase (weeks 2-6+):** Severe coughing fits, followed by inspiratory "whoop"
-   **Coughing fits:** 5-10 rapid coughs followed by whoop; can last hours
-   **Post-tussive vomiting:** Coughing so severe it triggers vomiting
-   **Apnea:** Young children may have breathing pauses instead of whoop

**Complications:** Pneumonia (especially in infants), seizures, encephalopathy.

**Treatment:** Antibiotics early (azithromycin/erythromycin) shorten duration. Once coughing started, antibiotics less effective but reduce spread. Supportive care: keep airway clear, oxygen if hypoxic. Isolation: 21 days from cough onset (or 5 days after antibiotics).

**Diphtheria**

**Field identification:**

-   **Pseudomembrane:** Gray/white thick membrane on throat/tonsils; adherent
-   **Respiratory form:** Sore throat, low-grade fever; membrane can obstruct airway
-   **Cutaneous form:** Skin ulcers with gray membrane
-   **Nasal form:** Nasal discharge, crusting
-   **Systemic toxin effects:** Myocarditis, neuropathy (esp. cranial nerves causing paralysis)

**Treatment:** Antitoxin (if available, give immediately). Antibiotics (penicillin/erythromycin) kill bacteria. Supportive care: monitor airway, manage myocarditis and neuropathy. Isolation: until 2 consecutive negative cultures.

**Malaria (if in endemic area)**

**Field identification:**

-   **Fever cycles:** Patterns vary by species
-   **Fever pattern:** Shaking chills followed by high fever (104-105°F), followed by sweating
-   **Associated symptoms:** Headache, myalgias, weakness, nausea/vomiting
-   **Jaundice:** May develop with severe disease
-   **Splenomegaly:** Enlarged spleen common
-   **Severe malaria:** Cerebral malaria, severe anemia, acute kidney injury, pulmonary edema

**Treatment:** Antimalarial drugs critical. Supportive care: fever management, fluids, transfusion if severe anemia. Monitor for complications. Prevention: mosquito prevention.

### Water and Food Safety During Outbreaks

**Water safety:**

-   **Boiling:** Most reliable method; boil 1 minute kills most pathogens. Cholera killed in seconds, typhoid needs sustained boiling.
-   **Bleach treatment:** 2 drops of household bleach per liter of clear water; let sit 30 minutes. For turbid water, use 4 drops.
-   **Solar disinfection:** Fill clear bottles with water, leave in sun 6+ hours (UV radiation kills many pathogens)
-   **Filtration first:** Always filter/settle water to remove visible particles before bleaching or boiling

**Food safety:**

-   **High-risk foods during outbreaks:** Raw vegetables/fruits, shellfish, undercooked meat/eggs
-   **Safe preparation:** Wash hands thoroughly; cook meat/eggs fully; peel fruits/vegetables or cook them
-   **Storage:** Food poisoning risk increases if food not kept hot (>140°F) or cold (<40°F)
-   **High-transmission foods:** Foods prepared by infected handler without gloves/handwashing

### Body Handling and Burial Protocols for Infectious Deaths

In severe epidemics, deaths may occur faster than standard funeral practices allow:

-   **PPE for handlers:** Full protection when handling deceased
-   **Body washing:** Use gloved hands; minimize splashing
-   **Body wrapping:** Wrap in impermeable material to contain body fluids
-   **Burial location:** >2 meters deep, >30 meters from water source
-   **Mass burial pits:** Dig trenches 2+ meters deep; bodies in rows; 30 cm soil between rows; 1+ meter soil cover minimum
-   **Cremation:** Safest option for highly infectious deaths
-   **Documentation:** Record burial location; helps families locate graves

### Contact Tracing Without Technology

Identifying and monitoring exposed people slows disease spread:

-   **Interview infected person:** Ask detailed history of past 2 weeks
-   **Identify contacts:** Create list; prioritize close contacts
-   **Contact quarantine list:** Write names, addresses, last known location
-   **Physical monitoring:** Visit contacts daily, check temperature, ask about symptoms
-   **Documentation:** Keep contact list, monitor records
-   **Duration:** Monitor for incubation period (2-14 days for most respiratory, up to 21 days for some)
-   **Information privacy:** Keep contact lists confidential

### Epidemic Curve Tracking (Paper-Based)

Tracking number of new cases over time shows outbreak progression:

![Offline Medical Reference diagram 8](../assets/svgs/medical-reference-8.svg)

**How to create and use an epidemic curve:**

1.  **Set up paper graph:** Draw x-axis (days) and y-axis (number of new cases)
2.  **Daily data collection:** Each day, count NEW cases (not cumulative)
3.  **Plot on graph:** Mark point or draw bar at appropriate day/height
4.  **Mark interventions:** Draw vertical line when quarantine started, water changed, etc.
5.  **Interpretation:**
    -   **Rising curve:** Outbreak growing; transmission not controlled
    -   **Peak then decline:** Interventions working or outbreak burning out
    -   **Plateau:** Cases steady; ongoing transmission despite intervention
6.  **Use for decisions:** Curve shows whether to escalate/maintain/wind down precautions
