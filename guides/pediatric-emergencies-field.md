## Overview

Pediatric emergencies require different assessment and treatment approaches than adults. Children's physiology, communication limitations, and smaller margins for error make pediatric emergency care uniquely challenging in post-collapse field conditions. This guide covers the three most common life-threatening pediatric emergencies: sepsis, dehydration, and respiratory distress. For foundational infant and child care, see <a href="../infant-child-care.html">Infant & Child Care</a>. For systemwide sepsis recognition and antibiotics, see <a href="../sepsis-recognition-antibiotic-protocols.html">Sepsis Recognition, Escalation & Empiric Antibiotic Protocols</a>.

:::warning
Pediatric emergencies deteriorate rapidly. Early recognition and intervention are critical. When in doubt, escalate to higher level of care or seek consultation if any is available. Documentation of assessment and treatment is essential for continuity if the child can later access medical care.
:::

## Rapid Breathing, Lethargy & Won't Eat - Emergency Check

If the child is "breathing fast," "too sleepy," "floppy," or "won't drink/eat," treat this as an emergency screen first, not routine comfort-care.

Use this guide immediately when any of the following are present:
- fast breathing for age
- lethargy, weak cry, poor interaction, or unusual sleepiness
- poor feeding plus fever, vomiting, or reduced urine
- gray, mottled, or blue-tinged skin

If symptoms are mild and stable, see <a href="../baby-discomforts-teething-colic-diaper-rash.html">Baby Discomforts, Teething, Colic & Diaper Rash</a>. If there is any doubt, stay in this emergency pathway.

## Pediatric Assessment Fundamentals

### Age-Based Weight Estimation

Accurate weight is critical for medication dosing and fluid calculations. Use these estimations if scales are unavailable:

**Infants (0-12 months):**
```
Weight (kg) = (Age in months × 0.5) + 4
Example: 6-month-old = (6 × 0.5) + 4 = 7 kg
```

**Children (1-10 years):**
```
Weight (kg) = (Age in years × 2) + 8
Example: 5-year-old = (5 × 2) + 8 = 18 kg
```

**Adolescents (11-16 years):**
```
Weight (kg) = (Age in years × 3) + 7
Example: 14-year-old = (14 × 3) + 7 = 49 kg
```

If child is visibly overweight or small for age, adjust downward or upward by 5-10 kg as clinical judgment dictates.

### Heart Rate Norms by Age

Elevated heart rate is one of the earliest signs of shock, sepsis, or respiratory distress in children:

| Age | Normal Heart Rate | Concern (High) | Severe (Very High) |
|-----|------------------|----------------|--------------------|
| <1 year | 100-160 | >170 | >200 |
| 1-3 years | 90-150 | >160 | >190 |
| 3-6 years | 80-140 | >150 | >180 |
| 6-12 years | 70-120 | >140 | >170 |
| >12 years | 60-100 | >120 | >160 |

### Respiratory Rate Norms

Tachypnea (increased respiratory rate) indicates distress. Rates below normal may indicate fatigue or impending collapse:

| Age | Normal RR | Tachypnea (High) |
|-----|-----------|------------------|
| <2 months | 30-60 | >60 |
| 2-12 months | 25-35 | >50 |
| 1-3 years | 20-30 | >40 |
| 3-6 years | 20-25 | >35 |
| >6 years | 16-20 | >25 |

### Capillary Refill Time (CRT)

Indicator of perfusion quality (blood flow to extremities):
- **Normal:** <2 seconds
- **Borderline:** 2-3 seconds
- **Prolonged:** >3 seconds (indicates shock)

Test: Press fingernail bed for 1-2 seconds, release, and count time for color to return.

## Pediatric Sepsis Recognition & Treatment

### Sepsis vs. Severe Sepsis vs. Septic Shock

**Sepsis:** Infection + systemic inflammatory response
- Fever or hypothermia
- Tachycardia (increased HR)
- Tachypnea (increased RR)
- Normal or high blood pressure (still compensating)

**Severe sepsis:** Sepsis + early organ dysfunction
- Altered mental status
- Decreased urine output
- Mottled skin (vasoconstriction)
- Lactate elevation (if testable)
- Still has blood pressure

**Septic shock:** Sepsis + hypotension despite fluids
- Systolic BP <90 mmHg (adult equivalent in children; use age-specific norms)
- Altered consciousness
- Very high or very low temperature
- Gray or mottled skin throughout body

:::info-box
In children, **maintain normal blood pressure longer than adults** during shock compensation. A child may have normal BP but still be in early septic shock. Suspect sepsis in febrile child with rapid heartbeat, rapid breathing, or altered behavior.
:::

### Sepsis Assessment Protocol

1. **Temperature:** Use any available thermometer (oral, axillary, rectal)
   - Fever >38.5°C with other signs = concerning
   - Hypothermia (<36°C) in sepsis is worse than fever

2. **Mental status:**
   - Normal alertness = good sign
   - Irritability/lethargy = concerning
   - Unresponsiveness = critical

3. **Perfusion assessment:**
   - Capillary refill time
   - Skin temperature (warm vs. cold extremities)
   - Urine output (if catheter available; normal is >1 mL/kg/hour)

4. **Source identification:**
   - Respiratory (cough, crackles, hypoxia)
   - Urinary (dysuria, flank pain, cloudy urine)
   - Gastrointestinal (diarrhea, vomiting, abdominal tenderness)
   - Skin/soft tissue (wound infection, cellulitis, abscess)
   - Meningitis (stiff neck, petechial rash, sensitivity to light)

### Sepsis Treatment Protocol

**Immediate actions (first hour):**

1. **Place IV or intraosseous (IO) line** for fluid/medication access
2. **Fluid bolus:** 20 mL/kg of normal saline or lactated Ringer's IV over 10-15 minutes
   - Example: 20 kg child = 400 mL bolus
   - Reassess perfusion after bolus
   - May repeat if still in shock (maximum 60 mL/kg in first hour)

3. **Antibiotics:** Initiate within first hour if possible
   - **If community-acquired pneumonia suspected:** Ceftriaxone 50-80 mg/kg/day (max 2g) IV/IM
   - **If broad-spectrum (unknown source):** Cefotaxime 50 mg/kg IV q4-6h + Gentamicin 7.5 mg/kg/day IV
   - If NO IV access and infection is suspected, do NOT delay—treat with IM or oral antibiotics

4. **Maintain temperature control:** Do not actively cool febrile child; keep warm with blankets
5. **Monitor urine output:** Encourage drinking if conscious and able to swallow; if decreased, suspect shock

**Ongoing management:**

- Reassess perfusion every 30 minutes
- Monitor for fluid overload (crackles on lung exam, respiratory distress worsening)
- Identify and treat source of infection
- Consider vasopressors if available (epinephrine 0.05-0.1 mcg/kg/minute IV infusion) for refractory shock

:::danger
If child shows signs of septic shock (hypotension, unresponsiveness, mottled skin) and IV fluids do not improve perfusion within 30 minutes, escalate to higher level of care urgently. Septic shock mortality is high without advanced interventions.
:::

## Severe Dehydration Assessment & Rehydration

### Dehydration Classification

| Finding | Mild (5%) | Moderate (10%) | Severe (15%+) |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|----------------|
| Mental status | Normal | Alert to irritable | Lethargic, obtunded |
| Eyes | Normal | Slightly sunken | Sunken |
| Mucous membranes | Moist | Dry | Dry, sticky |
| Tears | Present | Decreased | Absent |
| Skin turgor | Normal | Decreased | Very decreased |
| CRT | <2 sec | 2-3 sec | >3 sec |
| Urine output | Normal | Decreased | Very low/absent |
| Thirst | Normal | Increased | May not respond |

### Causes of Dehydration in Children

**Gastrointestinal:** Diarrhea (most common), vomiting, inadequate intake
**Systemic:** Fever, sweating, burns, sepsis
**Renal:** Diabetes, glycosuria, polyuria
**Environmental:** Inadequate water availability, prolonged heat exposure

### Dehydration Treatment by Severity

**Mild dehydration (5%):**
- Oral rehydration solution (ORS): 50-100 mL/kg over 3-4 hours
- Home-made ORS: 1 liter water + 6 teaspoons sugar + 1/2 teaspoon salt
- Frequent small sips if child vomits
- Continue breast-feeding if applicable

**Moderate dehydration (10%):**
- IV bolus: 15-20 mL/kg of normal saline or LR over 30-60 minutes
- If no IV: Nasogastric tube feeding with ORS
- Reassess after bolus; repeat if needed
- Continue maintenance fluids after rehydration (see formula below)

**Severe dehydration (15%+):**
- IV bolus: 20 mL/kg of normal saline over 15-30 minutes
- May need second bolus if perfusion not restored
- Identify shock signs (CRT >3 sec, altered mental status)
- If shock present, treat as septic shock (IV access, vasopressors if available)

### Maintenance Fluid Calculations

After addressing shock, provide ongoing maintenance fluids based on weight:

**Holliday-Segar Method:**
```
First 10 kg:     4 mL/kg/hour
Next 10 kg:      2 mL/kg/hour
Each kg above 20: 1 mL/kg/hour
```

**Example:** 25 kg child
- First 10 kg: 10 × 4 = 40 mL/hour
- Next 10 kg: 10 × 2 = 20 mL/hour
- Remaining 5 kg: 5 × 1 = 5 mL/hour
- **Total: 65 mL/hour**

:::info-box
Maintenance fluids replace ongoing losses (urine, insensible loss). They do NOT include replacement of deficit from dehydration. Calculate deficit separately, then add maintenance + deficit for total hourly rate.
:::

## Respiratory Distress Triage

### Respiratory Distress Warning Signs

- Retractions (use of intercostal, subcostal, suprasternal muscles)
- Nasal flaring
- Grunting on expiration (sign of airway obstruction or severe hypoxia)
- Stridor (high-pitched breathing sound, upper airway obstruction)
- Wheeze (lower airway obstruction)
- Cyanosis (blue lips or nail beds)
- Altered mental status (hypoxia)

### Differential Diagnosis of Respiratory Distress

#### Croup (Laryngotracheobronchitis)

**Etiology:** Viral (parainfluenza most common), usually in children 6 months-3 years
**Presentation:** Barky "seal-like" cough, stridor, hoarseness, often night onset
**Examination:** Subglottic narrowing on X-ray if available; viral prodrome

**Treatment:**
- Supportive care (warm mist, cool air, hydration)
- Dexamethasone 0.6 mg/kg oral or IM (reduces inflammation)
- Epinephrine (1:1000) 0.01 mL/kg inhaled or nebulized for moderate-severe stridor
- Most resolve within 3-7 days

#### Epiglottitis

**Etiology:** Bacterial (Haemophilus influenzae, Streptococcus pneumoniae), now rare post-vaccination
**Presentation:** Sudden onset of drooling, dysphagia, tripod positioning, toxic appearance, fever
**Danger:** Rapid airway obstruction possible; high mortality if untreated

**Treatment:**
- **DO NOT examine throat** (may precipitate airway collapse)
- Immediate IV access
- Ceftriaxone 50-80 mg/kg/day IV
- Prepare for airway management (intubation or tracheostomy)
- Escalate urgently

:::danger
Epiglottitis is life-threatening. Child appears very ill, cannot swallow saliva, has stridor, and high fever. Do not delay antibiotics for imaging. This requires advanced airway management—escalate immediately.
:::

#### Foreign Body Aspiration

**Etiology:** Choking event (food, small objects)
**Presentation:** Sudden onset cough/stridor, possible cyanosis, asymmetric breath sounds
**Examination:** May see object in mouth/throat; asymmetric air entry on auscultation

**Treatment:**
- If object is clearly visible and reachable: remove it carefully with tweezers or gloved fingers.
- If the object is suspected but not visible: do not perform a blind finger sweep.
- If the child has complete obstruction and cannot cough, speak, cry, or breathe: use age-appropriate choking rescue from First Aid.
- If the child becomes unresponsive: lower them to the floor, call for help/AED if available, start CPR, and remove only a clearly visible object when the mouth is opened.
- Position in recovery position if breathing is restored and continue urgent monitoring.

#### Pneumonia (Bacterial or Viral)

**Etiology:** Bacterial (S. pneumoniae, Haemophilus, Streptococcus) or viral (RSV, influenza)
**Presentation:** Fever, cough, tachypnea, possible chest pain, grunting
**Examination:** Crackles on lung exam, consolidation visible on chest X-ray if available

**Treatment:**
- Oxygen if available (target SpO2 >90%)
- Antibiotics (ceftriaxone or amoxicillin depending on severity)
- Fluid support (oral or IV)
- Monitor respiratory status and perfusion

#### Bronchiolitis (RSV, viral)

**Etiology:** Viral (RSV most common), peak age 3-12 months
**Presentation:** Cough, wheezing, crackles, tachypnea, respiratory distress
**Examination:** Fine crackles and wheezing throughout lung fields

**Treatment:**
- Supportive care (oxygen, hydration)
- Salbutamol (albuterol) nebulization if wheezing present (0.15 mg/kg, max 2.5 mg)
- Monitor for worsening; most improve within 7-10 days

### Respiratory Distress Severity Assessment

**Mild distress:**
- RR slightly elevated
- No stridor or minimal stridor at rest
- No retractions or mild retractions
- Good air entry, alert mental status
- **Action:** Observe, supportive care, treat underlying cause

**Moderate distress:**
- RR significantly elevated
- Stridor audible at rest or with agitation
- Moderate retractions
- Still alert
- **Action:** Oxygen, medication (epinephrine for croup, bronchodilators), hydration

**Severe distress:**
- RR very elevated (or may be decreasing—sign of fatigue)
- Stridor at rest
- Severe retractions, nasal flaring, grunting
- Altered mental status, cyanosis
- **Action:** Escalate urgently; prepare for airway management

## Intraosseous (IO) Access in Children

When IV access is difficult or not possible, intraosseous (IO) access provides direct access to bone marrow for fluids and medications.

### Indications

- Shock or severe dehydration requiring rapid fluid bolus
- Cardiac arrest
- IV access unsuccessful after 2-3 attempts
- Child unresponsive or unable to cooperate

### IO Site Selection

**Proximal tibia (preferred):**
- 1-2 cm below tibial tuberosity, medial to tibial crest
- Easiest landmark, largest margin for error
- Suitable for all ages

**Distal femur:**
- 2 cm proximal to lateral femoral condyle
- Used in infants when tibial approach difficult
- Higher risk of knee damage

**Distal tibia:**
- 2 cm proximal to medial malleolus
- Last resort (difficult anatomy)

### IO Insertion Technique

1. **Identify landmark:** Palpate tibial tuberosity, mark insertion point 1-2 cm below, medial to crest
2. **Prepare site:** Clean with antiseptic if available; do NOT delay for sterile prep in emergency
3. **Anesthesia:** Infiltrate with local anesthetic if child conscious (1-2 mL lidocaine)
4. **Position:** Stabilize leg; place towel under knee for support
5. **Needle insertion:** Use 18-20 gauge needle (or IO-specific needle if available)
   - Insert at 45-90 degree angle
   - Apply steady pressure and rotational motion
   - Feel for "pop" or sudden decrease in resistance (entering bone marrow)
6. **Confirm placement:** Aspirate with syringe (should get bone marrow); flush with 10 mL saline
7. **Secure:** Tape/suture needle in place, dress site

### IO Medication/Fluid Administration

- All IV medications can be given IO
- Fluid boluses: 20 mL/kg normal saline over 10-15 minutes
- Medications same dose as IV; flush with saline after administration

:::info-box
IO access is temporary (24-48 hours maximum). Plan for formal IV access as soon as feasible. Complications include osteomyelitis and compartment syndrome if not monitored.
:::

## Medication Dosing by Age/Weight

### Common Pediatric Emergency Medications

**Epinephrine (IV bolus - cardiac arrest):**
- 0.01 mg/kg (1:10,000 concentration) IV every 3-5 minutes
- Example: 20 kg child = 0.2 mg (2 mL of 1:10,000)

**Atropine (bradycardia):**
- 0.02 mg/kg IV (minimum dose 0.1 mg, maximum 0.5 mg)
- Example: 10 kg child = 0.2 mg

**Sodium bicarbonate (severe metabolic acidosis):**
- 1 mEq/kg IV bolus
- Example: 15 kg child = 15 mEq (15 mL of 1 mEq/mL solution)

**Glucose (hypoglycemia):**
- IV: 0.5-1 g/kg D50 diluted (2 mL/kg of D50 = 1 g/kg glucose)
- Example: 20 kg child = 40 mL D50 diluted 1:1 with saline

**Calcium gluconate (hyperkalemia, hypocalcemia):**
- 10-20 mg/kg slow IV (20 mL/kg of 10% solution)
- Example: 15 kg child = 30 mL IV slowly over 2-3 minutes

## Neonatal Emergencies (Birth-28 days)

### Neonatal Resuscitation Basics

Most newborns require only drying, warmth, and stimulation; some need additional interventions.

**Initial steps (same for all newborns):**
1. **Dry** infant and provide warmth
2. **Position:** Head slightly extended (sniffing position)
3. **Suction:** Mouth first, then nose (gentle, using bulb syringe if available)
4. **Stimulate:** Dry rubbing or gentle tapping of soles

**Assess color and responsiveness:**
- Pink or blue extremities? (some peripheral cyanosis is normal)
- Vigorous cry? (indicates good Apgar)
- Movement good? (tone assessment)

If infant is vigorous: Routine care (keep warm, feed soon)
If apneic, gasping, or no cry: **Start ventilation.**

### Neonatal Ventilation

- **Rate:** 40-60 breaths per minute
- **Pressure:** Gentle (watch for chest rise)
- **Ratio:** If only one person available, ventilation only (no compressions); chest rise is primary sign of adequate ventilation
- **Oxygen:** Use if available; deliver at 40% if possible (if delivering face-mask ventilation manually)

### Neonatal Complications in Field

**Meconium aspiration:**
- If infant is vigorous: dry, warm, stimulate as normal
- If infant is non-vigorous and meconium-stained fluid present: gentle suctioning, avoid overly aggressive suctioning (may push meconium deeper)
- Monitor for respiratory distress in first 24 hours

**Suspected infection/sepsis:**
- Same assessment as older infants (fever, irritability, feeding intolerance)
- Antibiotic threshold is **lower** for neonates; treat presumptively if risk factors (prolonged rupture of membranes, fever in mother, maternal sepsis)
- Antibiotics: Ceftriaxone 50 mg/kg IV/IM + Gentamicin 7.5 mg/kg/day IV, or Ampicillin + Gentamicin

## Documentation & Continuity of Care

For every pediatric emergency, document:

1. **Age/weight:** (estimated if necessary)
2. **Chief complaint and vital signs:** HR, RR, BP (if taken), temperature, SpO2 (if available)
3. **Assessment findings:** Mental status, perfusion, specific exam findings
4. **Treatment given:** IV/IO access, fluids, medications (drug, dose, route, time)
5. **Response to treatment:** Improved/stable/worsened, vital signs after intervention
6. **Disposition:** Transferred to hospital, discharged to home, etc.

This documentation allows any subsequent provider to understand what was done and the child's response.
