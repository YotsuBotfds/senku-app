## Plastic Type ID (Recycling Numbers 1-7)

Every plastic item produced in North America (and most other regions) is marked with a recycling symbol: a triangle with a number 1–7 inside. This number indicates the plastic type. Understanding these categories is the foundation of safe plastic salvage and reuse.

### The Seven Plastic Types

### Detailed Properties

**#1 PET (Polyethylene Terephthalate):** Clear and strong, used for all beverage bottles and many food containers. Easy to identify by clarity and snap sound. Can be heat-formed with caution (see below). Good insulation properties when multiple layers are used.

**#2 HDPE (High-Density Polyethylene):** The workhorse plastic. More chemically resistant than PET and handles heat better. Milky white or translucent. Nearly indestructible and readily available from milk jugs, detergent bottles, and industrial containers.

**#3 PVC (Polyvinyl Chloride):** Flexible or rigid depending on additives. Excellent for plumbing but dangerous to heat due to chlorine gas release. Never burn or heat PVC in a survival situation.

**#4 LDPE (Low-Density Polyethylene):** Flexible and stretchy, the material of plastic bags. Lower melting point makes it unsuitable for heat applications. Readily available and useful as flexible sheets or gaskets.

**#5 PP (Polypropylene):** Similar strength to HDPE but can tolerate higher heat. Rigid when cool but becomes flexible when warm. Excellent for heat-forming. Common in takeout containers and bottle caps.

**#6 PS (Polystyrene):** Styrofoam when expanded. Lightweight and excellent insulation but brittle and prone to cracking. Can release styrene fumes if overheated. Useful for insulation but not for heat-forming.

:::warning
**Expanded Polystyrene Combustion Hazard:** Expanded polystyrene (EPS/Styrofoam) ignites at approximately 490°C (914°F) and burns rapidly, producing dense black smoke containing styrene monomer, carbon monoxide, and other toxic gases. Burning polystyrene generates dripping, flaming material that can cause severe burns and spread fire. Never burn polystyrene indoors or near occupied areas. When salvaging EPS, store away from heat sources and open flames.
:::

**#7 Other:** A catchall category. Polycarbonate (rigid, clear, very strong) is common in water cooler bottles and protective shields. Nylon is heat-resistant and strong, found in cable ties and gear. Without specific identification, treat #7 as questionable for heat application.

## Heat-Formable vs Dangerous Plastics

Heat-forming plastic—softening it to create new shapes—is a powerful technique for creating custom containers, seals, and components. However, not all plastics are safe to heat, and some release toxic fumes.

### Safe to Heat-Form

**#1 PET (with caution):** Can be softened in hot water (65–70°C / 150–160°F) but avoid extended high heat. Suitable for gentle forming of water bottle shapes or adding creases. Do not approach boiling as it begins degradation.

**#2 HDPE:** Very heat-stable. Can be softened in hot water or over a heat source to about 100°C (212°F). The material retains its properties well. Ideal for heat-forming projects.

**#5 PP (Polypropylene):** The best choice for heat-forming. Remains flexible even when softened and hardens again when cooled. Can be softened in boiling water (100°C / 212°F) or carefully over a flame. The material won't release fumes at safe forming temperatures. This is the preferred plastic for heat-formed projects.

### Dangerous When Heated

**#3 PVC:** Releases hydrochloric acid gas when heated, particularly above 160°C (320°F). Even lower heat application can release chlorine-containing fumes. Never heat PVC. If you must cut or drill PVC pipes, wear a respirator and work outdoors. Do not burn PVC plastic; the fumes are intensely toxic.

**#6 PS (Styrofoam):** Begins to decompose and releases styrene gas when heated above 100°C (212°F). The smell is distinctive and unpleasant. Exposure can cause dizziness, respiratory irritation, and headaches. Avoid heating; instead, use mechanical cutting for foam.

### Heat-Forming Techniques

**Hot Water Softening:** Heat water to 80–100°C (176–212°F) in a pot. Submerge the plastic item for 1–3 minutes until it becomes pliable. Remove with tongs and shape quickly before it cools. This method is safest and requires minimal equipment.

**Oven Forming:** Preheat an oven to 120–150°C (250–300°F). Place the plastic item on a baking sheet and heat for 2–5 minutes until soft. Remove with heat-protective gloves and shape over a form (wooden block, metal rod, or water-filled mold). Allow to cool in shape.

**Heat Gun:** If available, a heat gun allows controlled softening of specific areas. Keep the gun moving to avoid overheating and scorching the plastic. Work quickly; cooling happens fast.

### Mold Making for Repeated Shapes

Create a negative mold from wood, metal, or clay to shape multiple identical items. Heat plastic until pliable, press into the mold, and cool completely before removing. This is effective for producing custom containers, gaskets, or shaped components repeatedly. Sand-filled bags or water bags can also serve as molds for forming rounded shapes.

## Rubber Salvage

Rubber is an extremely durable material that remains functional long after most other salvage items. Tires, hoses, gaskets, and belts are everywhere and represent valuable raw materials.

### Tire Rubber

**Inner Tubes:** Salvaged from tires, inner tubes are one of the most valuable rubber salvage items. Applications include:

-   **Patches and Repair:** Cut sections can patch torn rubber, waterproof gaps, or seal small holes. Glue with contact cement or rubber cement.
-   **Straps and Ties:** Cut rubber strips make excellent waterproof cordage, tool handles, pack fasteners, and mechanical seals.
-   **Gaskets and Seals:** Cut to size and placed between mating surfaces, rubber strips prevent leaks and vibration.
-   **Slingshots:** Stretched inner tube rubber is ideal for survival slingshots. High elasticity and durability exceed most modern elastic materials.

**Tire Tread:** The outer rubber of a tire is extremely durable. Cut sections make excellent sole material for improvised shoes, door mats, or ground cover. The thick rubber resists puncture and wear.

### Hoses

**Automotive Hoses:** Radiator hoses, fuel hoses, and brake hoses are thick rubber. While the large hoses (radiator) are less useful, smaller hoses can be cut into sections for:

-   Flexible connectors between pipes and fittings
-   Mufflers or silencers for pumps
-   Gaskets between flanged connections
-   Hand grips on tools

**Garden Hoses:** Old garden hoses are extremely common and useful. Sections can serve as flexible joints, protective sleeves around sharp edges, or even as tubing for hand pumps or water systems.

### Gaskets and O-Rings

Every vehicle and appliance contains dozens of gaskets and O-rings in various sizes. Collect these and sort by size and material. Materials include:

-   **Silicone:** Heat-resistant, survives 200°C+. Ideal for high-temperature seals.
-   **Nitrile (Buna-N):** Oil-resistant, good chemical resistance. Common for general seals.
-   **Natural Rubber:** Good elasticity and low cost. Less chemical-resistant than synthetic options.

Keep gaskets in labeled containers organized by diameter. They're invaluable for repairing machinery or creating custom seals.

### Drive Belts

Rubber V-belts and timing belts from machinery are excellent salvage. They can be:

-   Repurposed between new pulleys to drive machinery
-   Cut and used as straps or cordage
-   Wrapped around handles or shafts for grip and vibration damping

Timing belts with teeth are particularly valuable as they provide positive drive without slipping.

## Chemical Leaching by Plastic Type

Salvaged plastic containers can leach chemicals into stored food and water. Understanding which chemicals leach from each plastic type, under what conditions leaching accelerates, and safe reuse guidelines prevents health hazards.

### Plastic Type Chemical Leaching Table

| Plastic Code | Type | Primary Leaching Chemicals | Conditions Accelerating Leaching | Health Hazards | Food-Safe Guidelines |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| #1 | PET | Acetaldehyde (in some formulations) | High temperature, UV exposure, acidic foods | Low toxicity; minimal risk at room temperature | Safe for cold/cool foods; avoid hot beverages |
| #2 | HDPE | Minimal; occasionally trace plasticizers | Only under extreme conditions | Very low hazard | Generally safe for all cold foods |
| #3 | PVC | Phthalates, lead (if anti-static agent), BPA (in some formulations) | Heat (>60°C), fatty foods, long-term storage | Endocrine disruption, reproductive issues | NOT recommended for any food storage |
| #4 | LDPE | Minimal leaching | Heat and UV; minimal hazard at room temperature | Very low | Safe for cold/room-temperature foods |
| #5 | PP | Minimal leaching | Only extreme heat (>100°C) | Very low | Safe for all temperatures including boiling |
| #6 | PS | Styrene, BPA (in some formulations) | Heat (>80°C), fatty/oily foods | Neurotoxic (styrene); endocrine disruptor (BPA) | NOT recommended for hot foods; avoid for any storage |
| #7 | Other | Varies; commonly BPA, BPS, phthalates | Highly dependent on specific composition; heat accelerates | Variable; potential endocrine disruption | Unknown composition = unsafe; avoid all food storage |

### Conditions That Accelerate Chemical Leaching

Understanding what conditions increase leaching helps predict contamination risk.

**Temperature effects:**
- Room temperature (20-25°C): Minimal leaching for most plastics
- Warm (40-60°C): Moderate leaching; PVC begins releasing chemicals
- Hot (>60°C): Rapid leaching; #3 PVC and #6 PS release significant chemicals
- Very hot (near boiling, >80°C): Extreme leaching; even "safe" plastics (#1 PET) begin releasing trace chemicals
- Freezing (<0°C): Dramatically reduces leaching (molecules less mobile); actually protective

**Food chemistry effects:**
- Acidic foods (pH <3): Acetic acid, lemon juice, vinegar accelerate leaching
- Fatty/oily foods: Fats dissolve plastic additives, accelerating leaching (this is why cooking oils in plastic containers is risky)
- Salty foods: Salt water slightly increases leaching rates
- Alcohol: Even low-alcohol beverages accelerate leaching significantly
- Plain water: Minimal leaching; water is safest storage medium

**Duration of storage:**
- Short-term (<1 month): Minimal leaching, even in marginal conditions
- Medium-term (1-6 months): Noticeable leaching from susceptible plastics if conditions are harsh
- Long-term (>6 months): Significant leaching accumulation; especially problematic for oil/fat storage
- Very long-term (>2 years): Continuous exposure even at room temperature can result in measurable chemical levels

**UV/light exposure:**
- Dark storage: Minimal leaching acceleration
- Indirect indoor light: Slight acceleration; UV-protective measures help
- Direct sunlight: Rapid photo-degradation and chemical leaching; avoid at all costs
- UV-filtering containers: Colored (amber/brown) bottles reduce UV exposure significantly

### Specific Chemical Hazards by Plastic Type

#### #1 PET Chemical Leaching

**Primary chemicals:** Acetaldehyde (in some formulations—depends on manufacturing)
- Source: Byproduct of PET synthesis; small amounts remain in final product
- Leaching conditions: Elevated temperature (>40°C) and prolonged storage
- Health effects: Acetaldehyde is a potential carcinogen at high doses (unlikely from plastic leaching)
- Risk level: Very low for typical food storage; significant only with repeated heating

**Best practices:** Store PET at room temperature; avoid refilling with hot liquids; do not reuse for hot foods

#### #3 PVC Chemical Leaching

**Primary chemicals:** Phthalates (plasticizers, most concerning), lead (possible anti-static agent in some older products), BPA (in some formulations)
- **Phthalates:** Disrupt endocrine system; reduce testosterone production; linked to reproductive problems
  - Leaching accelerates dramatically when heated or in contact with fatty foods
  - As little as 2-3 ppm (parts per million) in water can cause measurable biological effects in sensitive individuals
  - Long-term storage at room temperature can reach 1-5 ppm levels

- **Lead:** Highly toxic; affects brain development in children, cognitive function in adults
  - Found in PVC manufactured before 1990s; most modern PVC is lead-free
  - Leaches into acidic foods or water
  - Even trace amounts (>15 ppb) are dangerous for children

- **Recommendation:** NEVER use PVC for food/water storage; disposal or non-food use only

#### #6 PS (Polystyrene) Chemical Leaching

**Primary chemicals:** Styrene (the monomer), BPA (in some formulations)
- **Styrene:** Neurotoxic; causes dizziness, headache, nausea at high exposures; leaches significantly when heated
  - Hot foods/beverages dissolved in styrofoam cause rapid styrene release
  - Measurable styrene detected in food within minutes of hot liquid contact

- **BPA:** Endocrine disruptor; even at low doses affects development
  - Less commonly used in modern PS, but older products contain it

- **Recommendation:** Never use for hot foods; room-temperature only; cold water storage acceptable if necessary

#### #7 (Other) Chemical Leaching

**Primary chemicals:** Unknown; commonly BPA, BPS (BPA replacement, similarly problematic), phthalates
- BPA-free #7 plastics may use BPS (Bisphenol S) as replacement—equally concerning
- Polycarbonate (common #7 plastic) leaches BPA significantly, especially if scratched or heated
- **Recommendation:** Unknown composition = unsafe; avoid all food storage

### Health Hazards from Chronic Low-Level Leaching

**Endocrine disruption (from BPA, phthalates, BPS):**
- Interferes with hormone signaling
- Effects: Reduced fertility, altered sexual development, thyroid disruption
- Vulnerable population: Children and pregnant women most at risk
- Evidence: Strong epidemiological correlation between plastic food storage and endocrine disorders

**Neurotoxic effects (from styrene):**
- Chronic low-level exposure causes: headaches, fatigue, difficulty concentrating, mood changes
- Risk increases with age of exposure (children more vulnerable)
- Irreversible at high cumulative doses

**Carcinogenic potential:**
- Acetaldehyde (PET): IARC Category 2B (possibly carcinogenic)
- Styrene (PS): IARC Category 2B (possibly carcinogenic)
- BPA: IARC not classified, but animal studies show cancer promotion in some models
- Risk: Minimal from typical food storage; significant with intensive/prolonged exposure

### Safe-Use Guidelines by Plastic Type

**#1 PET:**
- Safe for: Cold water, juice, cold beverages
- Avoid: Hot liquids, acidic foods long-term, alcoholic beverages, fatty foods
- Storage: Cool, dark location; do not reuse beyond 1-2 times

**#2 HDPE:**
- Safe for: All foods and beverages (cold and room temperature)
- Avoid: Very long-term storage (>2 years) of fats/oils
- Best for: Milk, juice, water, general food storage
- Storage: Cool, dark location; can reuse multiple times

**#3 PVC:**
- Safe for: Non-food use only (tool storage, office supplies)
- Avoid: Any food or beverage storage, especially hot foods, fatty foods, alcoholic beverages
- Recommendation: Do not salvage for food use; use alternative plastics only

**#4 LDPE:**
- Safe for: Cold foods, water, room-temperature items
- Avoid: Hot foods, very long-term storage (>1 year)
- Storage: Cool, dark location

**#5 PP:**
- Safe for: All foods, hot and cold (safest plastic for food reuse)
- Best for: Takeout containers, yogurt tubs, bottle caps, reheatable items
- Storage: Any condition; most resistant to leaching

**#6 PS (Polystyrene/Styrofoam):**
- Safe for: Cold foods only, short-term storage (<1 week)
- Avoid: Hot foods, beverages, long-term storage, reheating
- Recommendation: Minimize use; switch to other plastics when possible

**#7 Other:**
- Safe for: Unknown—assume unsafe
- Recommendation: Do not use for food storage; determine specific plastic type before use

### Testing for Safe Previous Contents

Before reusing salvaged containers for food:

1. **Identify origin:** If label visible, it likely held food or beverage → safer
2. **Inspect interior:** Stains or residue indicates previous contents
   - Water stains = safe
   - Greasy/oily residue = chemical solvent or motor oil (unsafe for food)
   - Discoloration = chemical dyes or strong solvents (unsafe)

3. **Smell test:** Sniff the container after rinsing
   - Chemical smell = previous chemical storage (unsafe)
   - Slight plastic smell = normal (acceptable)
   - No smell = good sign (safe)

4. **When in doubt:** Do not use for food; use for non-food purposes instead

## Container Food-Safety Assessment

Reusing plastic containers for food storage is common but requires understanding which plastics are food-safe. The recycling number only tells part of the story; previous contents and storage conditions matter too.

### Inherently Food-Safe Plastics

**#1 PET, #2 HDPE, #4 LDPE, #5 PP:** These are approved by the FDA for food contact. Containers marked with these numbers were manufactured to be food-safe. If the container originally held food (water, juice, milk, yogurt), it was constructed to safe standards.

### Avoid for Food Storage

**#3 PVC:** Not approved for food storage. Contains chemical additives that can leach into food. Do not reuse PVC containers for food.

**#6 PS (Polystyrene):** Not approved for food storage. Contains potential endocrine disruptors. Do not store food in styrofoam or clear polystyrene containers.

**#7 Other:** Variable composition. Unless you know the specific plastic, avoid food storage. Some #7 items (polycarbonate) contain BPA, a concerning chemical.

### Assessing Previous Contents

Even food-safe plastics can become unsafe if they previously held hazardous materials. Ask yourself:

-   **What did this container originally hold?** Motor oil, pesticide, or industrial chemicals? Do not reuse.
-   **How long has it been empty?** Older containers may have absorbed odors or degraded.
-   **Is there any residue or smell?** If you cannot wash it clean, don't use it for food.

### Cleaning and Sanitizing

If a plastic container held food and is clean, washing with hot soapy water is sufficient. For containers held unknown contents, wash thoroughly and soak in a vinegar solution (1 part vinegar, 3 parts water) for several hours to remove odors and potential residues.

### Single-Use vs. Reusable

Some plastic containers are labeled "single-use" for legal reasons, not durability. A PET bottle labeled "single-use" is perfectly reusable for water, though the plastic degrades over time with UV exposure and repeated washing. In a survival situation, reusing food-safe containers multiple times is practical and safe. However, replace containers that show visible wear (cloudiness, brittleness, or cracks).

### Heat Storage Considerations

Do not store hot food or boiling water in plastic containers unless they're rated for that temperature. Heat accelerates chemical leaching and may warp the container. Allow food to cool before storing in plastic. The exception is #5 PP, which tolerates boiling water. Check the recycling number before storing hot contents.

## Flat Glass Cutting & Reuse

Window glass, mirrors, and glass panels from demolition are valuable salvage. Glass is chemical-resistant, transparent, and can be reused for many applications.

### Window Glass Recovery

Recovering glass from old window frames requires care to avoid cuts. Safety equipment is essential: cut-resistant gloves, eye protection, and long sleeves.

**Scoring Technique:** Place the glass on a flat surface. Using a glass cutter (a tool with a small wheel), draw a line across the glass with moderate, consistent pressure. Do not cut repeatedly; one smooth score is sufficient. The score creates a stress line.

**Running the Score:** Once scored, tap sharply along the line from underneath, or grip the glass on either side of the score and apply quick, firm pressure to snap it along the line. The glass should break cleanly. If it doesn't, re-score and try again.

**Finishing Edges:** Glass edges are sharp. Smooth them with a glass file or by grinding on a stone. This takes time but prevents nasty cuts.

### Glass Reuse Applications

-   **Greenhouse or Cold Frame Panels:** Glass allows sunlight in while retaining heat. Multiple panes can be fitted into wooden frames to create warm growing spaces.
-   **Solar Collector Covers:** Clear glass covers a solar water heater or air heater, allowing sunlight in while reducing convective heat loss.
-   **Mirrors (Silvered Glass):** Reflective surfaces direct light, concentrate solar energy, or serve as signaling mirrors. Silvered glass is more common than you'd think in salvage.

### Tempered Glass Identification and Limitations

Modern windows, car windows, and some doors use tempered (safety) glass. This glass is heat-treated to be stronger and safer, but it cannot be cut. Attempting to cut tempered glass causes it to shatter into small, harmless pebbles.

**Identifying Tempered Glass:** Look for corner markings or stamps indicating "tempered" or "safety." Car windows (except the windshield) are tempered. The glass often appears slightly cloudier than annealed (regular) glass.

If you need to reuse tempered glass, use it as-is without cutting. The small cube fragments when it does break are actually a safety advantage compared to large sharp shards.

## Bottle Glass Reuse vs Melting Stock

Glass bottles are ubiquitous salvage, available in unlimited quantities. While recycling systems exist, off-grid applications for bottles are abundant and practical.

### Bottles as Containers

Glass bottles are excellent for food and water storage. They're non-toxic, chemically inert, and durable. Salvaged bottles can store dried goods, preserved foods, water, or liquids indefinitely without degradation. Caps or cork stoppers create sealed storage. In a survival situation, clean bottles are priceless for water storage and transport.

### Bottles as Building Material

Stacking and mortaring glass bottles creates insulating walls, light-transmitting structures, and decorative elements. Bottles can be:

-   Mortared into walls (bottle ends visible, creating light and insulation)
-   Stacked in clear grout to create thermal mass
-   Used in transparent panels to allow light while reducing external visibility

This is particularly useful for off-grid structures where insulation and light control are important.

### Solar Water Disinfection (SODIS)

Clear plastic bottles (1–2 liters) filled with water and left in direct sunlight for 6–8 hours (or 2 days on overcast days) are disinfected by UV radiation. This is an effective water purification method for clear water that is already free of suspended solids and heavy contamination. Glass bottles work even better than plastic due to better UV transmission. Place bottles horizontally on a dark surface in full sun. This requires no chemicals or equipment and is effective in sunny regions.

### Bottle Cutting Technique (String-and-Fire Method)

Converting bottles into drinking glasses or containers requires cutting the bottle. The string-and-fire method is accessible with no special glass-cutting tools:

**Process:** Soak a cotton string in nail polish remover or acetone. Wrap it tightly around the bottle where you want to cut. Light the string on fire and let it burn until it's nearly consumed. Immediately submerge the bottle in ice-cold water. The thermal shock causes the glass to snap cleanly along the string line. File or smooth the cut edge.

This method takes practice but works reliably. Alternatively, score a line with a glass cutter and snap it as described in the Flat Glass section.

### Glass Melting Stock

While glass can theoretically be recycled by melting, this requires extremely high temperatures (1500°C / 2732°F). Achieving this without a specialized furnace is impractical for most survivors. Therefore, treat salvaged glass bottles as finished items for reuse, not as melting stock. Melt glass only if you have access to a proper glass furnace or kiln rated for glass melting, which is rare in survival situations.

<section id="optical-glass">

## Optical Glass

Optical glass components—lenses, mirrors, and prisms—have exceptional value in survival and off-grid applications. Unlike ordinary glass, optical glass is precisely shaped to focus or magnify light.

### Camera Lenses

**Fire Starting:** A camera lens or zoom lens element can focus sunlight to a small, hot point, igniting dry tinder. A single-element lens is less efficient than a multi-element compound lens, but both work. This is one of the most reliable methods to start fire with sunlight.

**Magnification:** Lenses can magnify small objects for detailed observation or repair work. A 2x to 5x magnification is typical for camera lenses, sufficient for reading small print or inspecting fine work.

**Signaling:** A polished lens can reflect sunlight for long-distance signaling, visible for miles on clear days.

### Binoculars

**Observation:** Binoculars extend vision range dramatically, essential for security, wildlife observation, and surveying distant areas.

**Fire Starting:** The objective lenses of binoculars are powerful converging lenses. They concentrate sunlight intensely and can ignite tinder quickly. Better than camera lenses for this purpose.

**Component Extraction:** If a binocular is broken, the individual lenses can be extracted and used separately for fire starting or magnification.

### Projector Lenses

Large projector lenses (from overhead projectors or cinema projectors) are powerful magnifying lenses. A 3–4 inch diameter projector lens concentrates sunlight into a intense beam, capable of melting materials or igniting combustibles. These are valuable for solar applications.

### Fresnel Lenses

Fresnel lenses are lightweight, flat lenses used in overhead projectors, rear-projection televisions, and aircraft landing lights. They consist of concentric circular grooves that bend light similarly to a thick curved lens but with minimal material. Salvaged Fresnel lenses are powerful concentrating lenses for solar applications.

**Solar Applications:** A Fresnel lens focuses sunlight into an intense spot, suitable for:

-   Fire starting (extremely effective)
-   Solar cooking (concentrated heat)
-   Solar water heating or distillation
-   Melting materials (metals, glass)

A large Fresnel lens (3 feet × 4 feet from old projection TV) can concentrate sunlight to a temperature of 300°C+ at the focal point. This is more powerful than a simple magnifying glass.

<section id="laboratory-glassware">

## Laboratory Glassware

Discarded laboratory glassware from schools, medical facilities, or research labs is valuable salvage. Each piece is designed for specific chemistry and heat applications.

### Beakers and Flasks

**Beakers:** Straight-sided glass containers with a spout, used for mixing and heating. Marked with volume graduations. Invaluable for chemical processing, water purification, and cooking experiments.

**Flasks:** Round-bottomed or conical flasks designed for distillation and reactions. Excellent for heating and mixing without spilling. Distillation flasks have side arms for collecting vapors.

### Glass Tubing and Stoppers

**Tubing:** Glass tubes connect apparatus together, carry heated gases or liquids without degradation. Essential for building distillation equipment or water purification systems.

**Stoppers:** Glass or rubber stoppers seal containers. Glass stoppers work with solvents that degrade rubber.

### Borosilicate vs. Soda-Lime Glass

**Borosilicate Glass (Pyrex, Schott):** Contains boron oxide, making it heat-resistant and suitable for direct flame heating. Thermal shock resistance is excellent; you can heat it on a stove and plunge it into cold water without breaking. Melting point is around 1700°C (3090°F). This is the most valuable laboratory glassware for survival applications. Look for the trade name (Pyrex) or "borosilicate" markings.

**Soda-Lime Glass:** Common window and bottle glass. Lower melting point (1700°C) and poor thermal shock resistance. Heating on a burner and sudden cooling causes breakage. Not suitable for direct flame applications, though you can use it to heat liquids indirectly (in a water bath).

**Identification:** Borosilicate glass is often clearly marked "Pyrex" or has a manufacturer's mark indicating heat-resistant glass. Hold it to the light; borosilicate appears slightly greenish or bluish. Soda-lime glass appears colorless or slightly brown.

### Laboratory Applications

-   **Water Distillation:** Heat water in a borosilicate flask connected via glass tubing to a condenser (coiled tube in cold water), collecting pure water as it evaporates and condenses.
-   **Chemical Processing:** Heating and mixing chemicals safely in controlled equipment.
-   **Alcohol Production:** Building a small still from laboratory glassware for fuel or solvent production.
-   **Solar Water Heating:** Black paint on borosilicate tubes creates a heat collector in sunlight.

## Plastic Degradation & Storage

Plastics degrade through environmental exposure: UV radiation (sunlight), heat, oxidation (air exposure), and microbial attack. Understanding degradation mechanisms allows proper storage to extend utility.

### UV Degradation Timeline

Exposure to sunlight rapidly degrades plastics through photochemical breakdown of polymer chains. PET bottles left in sun for 1-2 months become brittle and discolored. HDPE maintains properties longer but still yellows and embrittles over 6-12 months outdoor exposure. PP is relatively UV-resistant but still degrades over time. Storage implication: all salvaged plastics should be stored indoors (basement, attic, covered shed) away from direct sunlight.

### Thermal Degradation

Heat accelerates degradation. A plastic bottle stored in hot sun can reach 60-70°C, accelerating molecular breakdown. Storage in cool (below 20°C), dark locations extends useful life by 50-200%. Avoid storing plastics near heat sources (furnaces, wood stoves, hot water heaters). The difference between "summer attic storage" (hot) and "cool basement storage" is dramatic for long-term plastic preservation.

### Oxidation & Brittleness

Plastics exposed to air develop brittle zones as oxidation progresses. Sealed containers minimize this—an unopened plastic container remains usable indefinitely, while opened containers degrade visibly within years. Implication: store salvaged plastics in sealed bags or airtight containers. When opening salvaged plastic containers, immediately transfer contents to sealed storage.

:::warning
Brittle plastic can shatter suddenly under force or temperature change. A plastic container that was flexible when stored may become dangerously brittle. Test plastic flexibility before using—bend carefully to assess brittleness.
:::

## Practical Projects Using Salvaged Plastics

### Water Storage Containers

Food-grade (#1 PET, #2 HDPE, #5 PP) containers provide essential water storage in survival situations. PET bottles hold water for extended periods (3-6 months) if stored cool and dark. HDPE containers better for long-term storage (1-2 years). Verify containers previously held water or food (never use containers that held chemicals or unknowns). Clean with hot soapy water and vinegar rinse before storing water. Rotate water every 6-12 months to prevent biological growth.

### DIY Planter Containers

Cut salvaged plastic bottles or containers to create planters. Drainage holes must be drilled or punched at the bottom. Larger containers (milk jugs, large PET bottles) work for vegetable growing. Smaller containers (yogurt tubs) suit herbs or small plants. Advantage: excellent durability compared to paper pots; disadvantage: dark plastic can overheat soil in sun. Paint exterior white or light color to reflect heat.

<section id="identification-testing">

## Testing Plastics When Labels Are Missing

This guide does not own active electrical-burning scenarios. If the prompt involves a burning plastic smell near an outlet, a smell after a power outage, sparks, heat, smoke, a tripped breaker, or any energized appliance/wiring concern, stop and route immediately to the electrical-hazard owner instead of using salvage-identification logic.

**Hard fence:** the burn test below is never for active electrical, fire, hot-smoke, or unknown-overheating hazards. It is only for isolated, already-safe scrap after the hazard has been eliminated and the material is being identified for salvage or reuse.

### Burn Test (Scrap-Only Identification Method)

Hold a small scrap of plastic in tongs over flame only after the item has been fully removed from any active hazard context. Observe flame color, odor, and melting behavior. This is dangerous and not a triage step for burning outlets, smoke, or energized equipment. Do this outdoors with fire extinguisher present.

- **#1 PET:** Burns readily, releases sweet/fruity odor, produces black sooty smoke
- **#2 HDPE:** Burns with blue-yellow flame, releases paraffin/wax smell, smoke less sooty
- **#3 PVC:** Releases greenish flame, distinct pungent chlorine smell (do NOT inhale)
- **#4 LDPE:** Burns similar to HDPE, slightly higher soot
- **#5 PP:** Thin black smoke, sweet paraffin smell, burns readily
- **#6 PS:** Black soot, strong aromatic smell, melts readily
- **#7 Other:** Varies significantly; only use if you're certain of material

:::danger
Never taste plastic. Never breathe fumes directly. Chlorine gas from PVC burning causes respiratory damage. This test is dangerous; use only when identification is critical and no alternatives exist.
:::

:::warning
Do not use the burn test if you are investigating an outlet, breaker, appliance, wiring, or any source of hot smoke or electrical odor. In that scenario, treat it as an electrical/fire hazard, not a salvage-identification problem.
:::

### Sink Test

Place plastic sample in water. Different plastics have different densities:
- Floats immediately: LDPE, PP (low density)
- Sinks slowly: HDPE (neutral buoyancy range)
- Sinks immediately: PET, PVC, PS (higher density)

This is safe and gives quick qualitative identification. Not definitive but helpful when combined with other methods.

### Rigidity & Feel Test

Handle the plastic and assess characteristics:
- **Rigid but flexible when bent:** PP or HDPE
- **Very rigid, breaks/cracks when bent hard:** PS or PET
- **Flexible, stretchy:** LDPE
- **Rigid, won't bend at all:** PVC

Combined with visual appearance, this helps narrow identification.

<section id="environmental-hazards">

## Environmental Hazards & Contamination

### Microplastics & Shed Particles

Old plastics degrade and shed microplastics into food and water. Using heavily degraded salvaged containers for food storage risks microplastic contamination. If plastic appears brittle, discolored, or damaged, don't use for food or water storage. The aesthetic issues (cloudiness, discoloration) usually accompany structural degradation.

### Chemical Leaching Variables

Plastic containers previously used for chemicals or pesticides may leach residual contamination into water or food, even after washing. If you're unsure of previous contents, don't use for food storage. In survival scenarios, when container safety is uncertain, use for non-food storage (tools, seeds) instead of risking health.

:::tip
When in doubt about salvaged container safety, use it for non-food purposes. The risk of chemical contamination is real but unmeasurable without laboratory testing.
:::

<section id="storage-preservation">

## Storage & Long-Term Preservation of Plastics

### Organization System

Organize salvaged plastics by type and size: one bin for #2 HDPE, another for #5 PP, another for miscellaneous #1 and #4. This prevents mixing incompatible types and allows quick access when a specific plastic is needed. Inventory salvaged plastics—knowing quantities and availability prevents waste.

### Protection from Light & Heat

Store in sealed dark bins in a cool location. Keep away from windows. Covered storage (opaque bins, closed containers) prevents UV exposure. Cool storage (below 20°C) slows degradation dramatically. This is the single most important factor in preserving salvaged plastics.

### Moisture & Cleanliness

Store salvaged plastics clean and dry. Mold grows on food residue; ensure containers are completely dry before sealing. This applies especially to rubber items (tires, hoses) which support mold growth in damp conditions.

## Troubleshooting Salvaged Plastics

| Problem | Cause | Solution |
|---------|-------|----------|
| Plastic bottle is cloudy/yellowed | UV/heat degradation | Still usable if structure intact; avoid for food if degraded |
| Salvaged container has odor | Residual contents or degradation products | Soak in vinegar for hours; rinse thoroughly; may still be unsafe for food |
| Plastic is brittle/cracking | Advanced UV/thermal degradation | Replace; brittle plastic shatters unpredictably |
| Rubber is cracked/hard | Age-related hardening | Salvage for non-critical applications; replace critical seals |
| Heat-forming doesn't work | Plastic exceeds melting point or wrong type | Lower temperature, try different plastic type, or abandon forming attempt |
| Container leaks after heat-forming | Structural damage during heating | Use sealed adhesive to repair; discard if large leak |
| Glass won't cut cleanly | Using tempered glass or scoring incorrectly | Switch to annealed glass; practice scoring technique on scrap |

<section id="hazard-identification">

## Hazard Recognition

### Tempered Glass Risk

Attempting to cut tempered (safety) glass causes catastrophic shattering into pebbles. Identify tempered glass before attempting work: look for manufacturer stamps (usually in corners), test by tapping (tempered glass has higher pitch "ring" than annealed). When in doubt, assume safety glass and don't cut.

### Chemical Container Risks

Never reuse containers without knowing original contents. A jug that held pesticide, bleach, or industrial chemical may have absorbed residual contamination. Even "washed" containers may retain molecular-level contamination. If unsure of contents, discard the container or use only for storage of compatible materials (e.g., pesticide jug can store motor oil, but never food).

:::danger
Storing food in unknown-origin plastic containers risks slow poisoning from chemical leaching. This is an undetectable risk. In survival scenarios, err on the side of caution—only use containers with known safe history.
:::

:::warning
Do not repurpose this guide for an active electrical odor investigation. A burning-plastic smell near an outlet, especially during or after a power outage, is an electrical hazard until proven otherwise and must be handled by the electrical-hazard owner, not by plastic burn-test or salvage-ID behavior.
:::

### Sharp Glass Edges

Freshly cut glass has microscopically sharp edges. Wrap cut edges with tape or cloth for safety. File or grind edges smooth before handling repeatedly. Gloves protect against cuts but provide false confidence—work carefully regardless.
