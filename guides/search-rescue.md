<section id="overview">

## Search & Rescue Overview

Search and rescue operations save lives when people become lost, injured, or stranded. Whether coordinating with professional SAR teams or participating in community rescue efforts, understanding systematic search patterns, victim assessment, and safe extraction techniques is critical. This guide covers practical SAR procedures adaptable to different environments and team capabilities.

:::warning
**Critical SAR Principle:** Always prioritize rescuer safety. An injured or deceased rescuer creates additional victims. Never enter hazardous areas without proper training and equipment. If conditions exceed your capabilities, wait for professional responders.
:::

### Core SAR Principles

- **Rapid deployment:** Speed matters in first 24-48 hours when lost persons often self-rescue
- **Systematic coverage:** Random searching wastes resources; structured patterns ensure thorough area coverage
- **Team coordination:** Divided areas, clear communication, tracked search sectors prevent duplication
- **Evidence preservation:** Note victim signs, tracks, and environmental clues for pattern analysis
- **Victim behavior prediction:** Understanding likely victim actions guides resource allocation
- **Continuous assessment:** Expand search scope and adjust methods as time passes without victim located

</section>

<section id="search">

## Search Patterns

Systematic search patterns cover terrain methodically, minimizing chance of missing missing persons. Selection depends on terrain, visibility, team size, and victim likely location.

:::info-box
**Pattern Selection Key:** Grid searches excel in open terrain; sweep searches work for approximate locations; quadrant searches scale to large areas; route searches follow predictable victim paths. Often combine multiple patterns for comprehensive coverage.
:::

### Grid Search

Searchers advance in parallel lines across terrain, maintaining even spacing (5-20 meters apart depending on visibility). Useful in open terrain or fields. Requires coordination - all searchers maintain alignment. Visual coverage excellent if properly spaced. Time-intensive for large areas.

![Grid search pattern diagram](../assets/svgs/search-rescue-1.svg)

### Sweep Search

Searchers fan out from central location in widening circles. Useful when approximate lost person location known. Systematic expansion ensures all nearby terrain covered. Less effective in forests with poor visibility. Difficult to maintain formation in rough terrain.

### Quadrant Search

Divide area into four quadrants from central point. Searchers systematically cover each quadrant. Allows coordination of multiple teams. Useful for large areas. Teams rotate through quadrants ensuring complete coverage.

### Route Search

Follow likely travel routes: trails, ridgelines, water courses, power lines. Lost persons often follow distinctive terrain features. Route searches efficient when patterns of lost person behavior understood. Can be combined with grid or sweep searching.

### Probability Theory

Allocate search resources to areas where lost person most likely found. Factors: victim age (young children tend to stay closer), victim condition (injured persons move less), terrain difficulty (steep areas less likely), time missing (expands search area over time), victim behavior patterns.

![Expanding square search pattern from last known position](../assets/svgs/search-rescue-2.svg)

High-probability zones (closest to last known position) warrant intensive searching first. Once thoroughly searched with negative results, expand outward to lower-probability zones. This strategy maximizes resource efficiency and probability of finding victim.

### Search Coordination

Coordinate with agencies (police, fire, SAR groups). Establish incident command center, assign search leaders, divide area into manageable sections, track searched areas (prevent duplication), assign priorities (high probability areas first), establish timeline for expanding search.

### Search Pattern Comparison Table

| Pattern | Best For | Coverage | Speed | Terrain | Team Size |
|---------|----------|----------|-------|---------|-----------|
| Grid | Open fields, clearings | Excellent (if spaced right) | Moderate | Flat/rolling | 4-12+ |
| Sweep | Approximate location known | Good | Fast | Variable | 4-8 |
| Quadrant | Large areas, multiple teams | Good with coordination | Slow | Variable | 8-20+ |
| Route | Predictable paths | Variable | Fast | Linear features | 2-4 |
| Spiral | Point location, small area | Excellent | Slow | Variable | 2-6 |
| Hasty | Initial phase, rapid check | Poor | Very fast | Variable | 2-4 |

:::tip
**Hasty searches first:** Send 2-4 rapid teams along likely routes (trails, water, ridgelines) while search command organizes large-scale operation. Often finds lost person in first 2 hours without waiting for full SAR deployment.
:::

<section id="signaling">

## Signaling Methods

Attract rescuer attention through visible and audible signals.

### Visual Signals

Signal fires: three fires in triangle pattern international distress signal. Large smoke visible 20+ miles in clear conditions. Bright colors: clothing hung in trees, cloth markers, reflective materials. Ground-to-air signals: large letters/numbers arranged in ground (50+ feet), visible from aircraft. Mirrors: bright reflection visible from distance. Movement: waving, jumping attracts eye.

### Audio Signals

Three short sounds = distress signal (three whistle blasts, three gunshots, three horn blasts). Shelter sound: shout periodically. Echoes help searchers locate source. Maintain vocal cords - don't exhaust shouting (spaced calls more effective than continuous yelling).

### Light Signals

Flashlight: three short flashes international distress. Use during night. Strobe effects more visible (on-off-on-off pattern). Reflectors at night: shine light toward aircraft. Glow sticks: cheap, long-lasting light source. Fire provides continuous light signal at night.

### Ground-to-Air Signals

Large V = need help, X = cannot proceed, SOS = extreme distress (three dots-three dashes-three dots in Morse), F = need food/water, L = need medical attention. Arrange using rocks, logs, clothing - size 50+ feet. Bright colors more visible: orange, red, yellow. Multiple signals increase chances of recognition.

![Ground-to-air emergency signals and symbols](../assets/svgs/search-rescue-3.svg)

### Electronic Signals

If satellite communicator available: transmit distress signal with location. Personal locator beacon: activates search and rescue. Cellular phone: call emergency services if coverage available. Electronic devices preserve voice/location data better than physical signals.

:::warning
**Signal Effectiveness Varies:** Visual signals require clear sightlines (invisible in dense forest). Audio signals limited to 1-2 miles (echoes deceptive). Electronic signals most reliable but require equipment. Combine multiple signal types for redundancy.
:::

</section>

<section id="rescue-equipment">

## Rescue Equipment Essentials

Proper equipment enables safe rescue operations. Basic SAR team should have access to these items before beginning rescue operations.

### Core Equipment List

- **Rope systems:** 300+ feet dynamic rope (3/8" diameter minimum), carabiners, harnesses, anchors for technical rescue
- **Stretcher/litter:** Commercial or improvised (covered in litter construction section)
- **First aid:** Advanced trauma kit, CPR pocket mask, tourniquets, emergency blankets, splint materials
- **Communication:** Radio (walkie-talkies), whistle, written message materials
- **Lighting:** Flashlights (multiple with extra batteries), glow sticks for low-light marking
- **Navigation:** Maps, compass, GPS unit, terrain knowledge
- **Terrain tools:** Rope, web straps, pry bar, digging tools for terrain-specific rescue
- **Weather protection:** Heavy rope, tarps, emergency bivouac materials

### Equipment Maintenance

Inspect all rescue equipment before deployment:

:::info-box
**Rope Inspection:** Check for cuts, burns, discoloration, frayed sections. Rope exposed to UV deteriorates over time. Replace annually or after hard use. Store in cool, dry location away from chemicals.
:::

### Equipment Inspection Schedule

| Equipment | Inspection | Frequency | Discard If | Replacement |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Rope | Visual for cuts/burns, flex test | Before each use | Discolored, frayed, damaged | 5-10 years or after hard use |
| Harness | Stitching, hardware, padding | Before each use | Stitches torn, hardware cracked | 5-10 years or damaged |
| Carabiners | Gate function, cracks | Before each use | Gate stuck, bent, cracked | When damaged |
| First aid | Expiration dates, completeness | Monthly | Medications expired | As needed |
| Radios | Battery test, transmission | Before each operation | Won't power on or transmit | When failed |
| Litter | Joints, straps, load test | Monthly | Cracks in structure, frayed straps | When structurally compromised |
| Flashlights | Battery test, bulb function | Monthly | Weak/no light output | When failed |

<section id="assessment">

## Patient Assessment

Proper assessment prevents secondary injuries and guides treatment priorities.

### Scene Safety

Ensure scene safe before approaching: no hazardous materials, no ongoing threats, electrical hazards secured, unstable structures avoided. Rescuer injury creates more victims - prioritize own safety.

### Primary Survey (Life Threats)

1.  **Responsiveness:** Tap shoulders, shout "Are you okay?" Gauge consciousness level.
2.  **Airway:** Open airway, listen for breathing. Clear airway of obstructions.
3.  **Breathing:** Check for chest rise, listen for breathing sounds. If not breathing, begin rescue breathing if trained.
4.  **Circulation:** Check pulse (carotid artery in neck). If no pulse and trained, begin CPR.
5.  **Catastrophic Bleeding:** Control massive bleeding with direct pressure and tourniquet if needed.

### Secondary Survey (Injuries)

Head: check for consciousness, pupil response, drainage from ears/nose. Neck/Spine: assume spinal injury from mechanism (falls, high-impact trauma). Immobilize neck/spine immediately. Chest: check for rib fractures, breathing difficulty, shock signs. Abdomen: palpate for distension/tenderness (internal bleeding). Extremities: check for fractures, dislocations, pulses. Skin: color (pale = shock), temperature, moisture.

### Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS)

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Component</th><th scope="row">Score</th><th scope="row">Meaning</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Eye Opening</td><td>1-4</td><td>Spontaneous best, no response worst</td></tr><tr><td>Verbal Response</td><td>1-5</td><td>Oriented best, no words worst</td></tr><tr><td>Motor Response</td><td>1-6</td><td>Obeys commands best, no movement worst</td></tr></tbody></table>

Total GCS 3-15. Score 8 or below = serious injury, requires medical care immediately. Score 13+ = mild injury.

### Shock Recognition

Signs: rapid weak pulse, pale/cold skin, rapid breathing, anxiety, confusion, dilated pupils. Shock = inadequate blood flow to tissues. Can be fatal if untreated. Treatment: lay flat with feet elevated, keep warm, monitor closely, transport to medical care immediately.

:::info-box
**Transition to Medical Care:** Once victim stabilized and extracted safely, immediate transport to medical facilities becomes critical. See the <a href="../first-aid.html">First Aid guide</a> for comprehensive medical assessment and treatment protocols to apply during transport to care.
:::

<section id="extraction">

## Extraction Techniques

Moving injured persons safely requires proper technique to prevent secondary injury.

### Spinal Precautions

If spinal injury suspected: immobilize neck/spine, move as single unit, avoid twisting. Improvise spine board from wide board or lashed poles. Log-roll technique: keep body straight, roll 90 degrees as unit. Four people minimum (one per extremity, one for head/neck).

### Carrying Techniques

Hands-and-knees carry: one rescuer. Use when distance short and victim not seriously injured. Fireman carry: victim over shoulder, fireman position. Useful for conscious victims or unconscious victims without spine injury. Two-rescuer carry: one per side, linked beneath victim. Most stable for conscious victims.

![Litter and stretcher carry positions for two and four-person teams](../assets/svgs/search-rescue-4.svg)

### Litter Transport

Litter (improvised stretcher) allows injured to be transported without rescuers bearing weight. Essential for long distances. Construction details in next section. Minimum four carriers (one per corner) to prevent tilting and victim shifting.

### Difficult Terrain Extraction

Steep slopes: maintain control with ropes. Vertical extraction: use fixed ropes, secure victim to litter. Water rescue: keep airway above water, support cervical spine if trauma suspected. Cave rescue: minimize movement, assess route before extraction begins.

</section>

<section id="rope-rescue">

## Rope Rescue Techniques

Vertical or steep terrain rescue requires rope systems to control movement and prevent additional injuries.

### Rope Anchor Systems

Establish secure anchors before deploying rope rescue. Anchor points must support 5,000+ pounds minimum load. Multiple anchors distribute load:

- **Tree anchors:** Select large, healthy tree (8"+ diameter). Wrap rope around trunk at height 6-8 feet above ground. Test by pulling hard - tree should not shift. Avoid dead trees, split trunks, branches prone to breaking.
- **Rock anchors:** Find immovable large rock or boulder. Sling rope around rock at stable point. Ensure rope contacts solid rock, not loose edge. Use multiple anchor slings for redundancy.
- **Belay anchors:** Drive fixed pitons or expand bolts into rock face for vertical extraction. Requires specialized equipment and training. Professional rescue teams use these.

:::danger
**Anchor Failure = Victim Death:** Anchor testing is non-negotiable. Use the heaviest available person to test anchor under load before committing victim. If uncertain, establish multiple backup anchors and test extensively.
:::

### Lowering Systems

Controlled lowering prevents victim injury and controls descent speed:

1. **Belay setup:** Rescuer with training acts as belay (manual rope control). Requires practice to develop "feel" for proper descent rate (2-3 feet per second). Communicate constantly with lowering rescuer.
2. **Mechanical ascenders:** Mechanical devices control lowering rate automatically. Allow single rescuer to manage victim descent. Requires training to operate safely (incorrect setup causes uncontrolled descent).
3. **Friction devices:** Carabiners, rappelling rings, or friction hitches create controlled descent. Less refined than mechanical devices but functional with practice.

### High-Angle Rescue

Vertical rock faces or cliffs require specialized training. Basic principles:

- Victim secured to litter with ropes (prevents falling)
- Multiple ropes prevent single-point failure (if main rope breaks, backup holds)
- Anchor systems use pulleys to change rope direction (reducing load on single anchor)
- Continuous communication between all rescuers (radios essential)

:::warning
**Vertical Rescue Complexity:** Untrained rescuers attempting vertical extraction often cause additional injuries. Unless you have trained rope rescue team available, contact professional SAR for technical rescue. Professional teams have equipment, training, and experience for safe vertical rescue.
:::

</section>

<section id="litter">

## Litter Construction

Improvised litter allows efficient casualty transport over distance.

### Basic Litter Design

The improvised litter allows a team of four to safely transport an injured person over distance without bearing the victim's full weight continuously.

### Litter Components

-   **Two parallel poles:** 8-12 feet long, 4-6 inches diameter (saplings work), strong enough to support weight
-   **Cross braces:** Secure poles at proper distance with lashing at intervals
-   **Bed material:** Woven branches, cloth strips, rope sling, improvised webbing - create comfortable, weight-supporting surface
-   **Handles:** Rope or wood projecting from poles for carriers to grip

### Construction Steps

1.  Select straight poles, peel bark if available (prevents splinters)
2.  Lash parallel poles at both ends (1-2 feet from ends) with rope or cordage
3.  Add cross braces every 2-3 feet along poles
4.  Weave branches or cloth across braces to create bed
5.  Secure loose material with rope (prevent shifting)
6.  Test litter by placing heavy object (sandbag) and checking stability
7.  Add padding (leaves, cloth) under casualty

### Carrying Litter

Four carriers optimal: one at each pole end. Maintain even height - synchronized steps prevent jostling. Switch carriers every 15-20 minutes (prevents fatigue). In steep terrain, six carriers better (two pairs along sides for balance). Secure casualty to litter with rope (prevents falling during extraction).

</section>

<section id="water-rescue">

## Water Rescue Operations

Water rescue presents unique hazards: drowning risk, cold water immersion, moving current, and limited visibility. Water rescue should only be attempted by trained personnel with proper equipment.

### Drowning Recognition & Response

Drowning victims often cannot call for help (water fills airway). Signs:

- Mouth at water level (unable to shout)
- Head back, mouth open (trying to breathe)
- Eyes glazed, unable to focus
- Vertical body position (less common than popular misconception)
- Silent (no splashing or yelling)

:::warning
**Drowning Can Be Silent:** Observe swimmers constantly - drowning victims often look like they're treading water. Trust your instinct if someone looks distressed and unable to help themselves.
:::

### Water Rescue Approach

1. **Reach:** Use extended object (tree branch, pole, rope) to reach victim without entering water. Safest method - rescuer remains on shore.
2. **Throw:** Toss flotation device (life jacket, inflatable, rope) to victim. Victim grasps device and is pulled to shore.
3. **Wade:** Enter shallow water to help victim. Only if rescue cannot be done from shore. Use rope for safety (partner on shore controls rope).
4. **Swim rescue:** Trained swimmers only. Approach from behind to prevent panic drowning victim from pulling rescuer under. Support victim's airway above water.

### Cold Water Immersion

Immersion in water below 60°F (15°C) causes hypothermia quickly. Cold water response:

- **Immediate (0-1 minute):** Gasping reflex, possible water inhalation, temporary paralysis
- **Short-term (1-30 minutes):** Muscular failure, loss of consciousness, death possible
- **Long-term (30+ minutes):** Severe hypothermia, cardiac arrhythmias, death likely

:::info-box
**Cold Water Rescue Priority:** Get victim out of water immediately. Do not delay extraction for warm-water recovery attempts. Even unconscious, cold water can preserve life - perform CPR for extended periods (30+ minutes) if victim was submerged in cold water.
:::

### Swift Water Rescue

Moving water (rivers, flood conditions) creates additional hazards:

- **Current:** Pushes rescuer downstream, creates hazardous strainers (trees blocking current)
- **Strainers:** Debris that allows water through but traps people - extremely dangerous
- **Turbulence:** Hydraulics and whirlpools can trap or recirculate victims

Rescue approach:
- Position rescuers upstream of victim (not downstream - don't get swept into victim)
- Use rope systems to recover swept victims (throw bag, fixed rope across current)
- Do not enter main current without professional swift water training

<section id="helicopter">

## Helicopter Operations

Helicopter rescue efficient but requires specialized training and coordination.

### Helicopter Approach

Never approach helicopter with rotor running - rotor blade impact fatal. Wait for signal from crew. Approach from side or rear (away from tail rotor). Duck low (unnecessary since rotors high, but instinctive). Follow crew instructions explicitly.

### Ground-to-Helicopter Communication

Position downwind of landing zone. Mark landing zone with bright colors or ground-to-air signals. Clear landing zone of debris (blown by rotor downwash). Secure loose items (flying debris injures people). Helicopter crew will signal when ready for litter/patient loading.

### Patient Hoisting

If landing not possible, helicopter hoists patient. Secure patient in basket or litter with rope. Patient in horizontal or semi-seated position depending on injuries. Hoist slowly upward. Rescuer rides up with patient if possible to monitor. Separate rescue hoist removes rescuer after patient safety secured.

### Aircraft Safety

Main rotor clearance 30+ feet. Tail rotor clearance 5-10 feet (much more dangerous - blade rotation blurs vision). Never move toward aircraft if unsure of clearance. Rotor noise prevents hearing warnings - watch crew hand signals. Static electricity can build up - let crew ground aircraft before touching equipment.

### Night Helicopter Operations

Incredibly dangerous. Rely on crew experience and training. Ground teams light landing zone (flashlights pointing up acceptable, don't shine at helicopter). Avoid sudden movements (rotors are invisible at night - crew operates by instruments). Follow all crew directions exactly.

</section>

<section id="lost">

## Lost Person Behavior

Understanding behavior patterns helps search teams predict victim location.

### Children (Under 12)

Tend to remain close to initial location (typically within 1/4 mile). Hide from searchers (fear of strangers). More active during day, rest at night. Follow water and light. Can be confused/disoriented despite small distances from home. Search thoroughly around home first before expanding search area.

### Elderly/Dementia

Wandering without clear direction. Tend toward familiar routes from memory. Can become disoriented quickly. Don't follow marked trails - take arbitrary directions. May try to return "home" (childhood home, not current residence). Less responsive to calls. Higher mortality rate due to falls, exposure.

### Athletes/Adventurers

Continue in direction they were heading despite problem (injury, lost). Tend to fight situation - exert themselves further (accelerates injury/exhaustion). May be confident they can self-rescue despite serious injury. Search far from initial point (multiple miles). Check challenging terrain (where adventurers attempt to go).

### Panicked Persons

Make poor decisions, sometimes dangerous ones. May run away from rescuers (mistaken for threat). Climb high (attempting to see surroundings). May ignore advice/warnings. Patient, non-threatening approach essential. Announce presence clearly before approaching.

### Injured Persons

Remain in place if serious injury. May be unconscious/non-responsive. Can be protected from elements (under trees, in caves). Search systematically - injured people don't travel. Check all shelter possibilities along victim's path.

### Behavior Prediction Factors

<section id="common-mistakes">

## Common SAR Mistakes

Understanding frequent errors helps prevent costly failures and victim deaths.

### Planning & Coordination Errors

- **No clear command structure:** Multiple uncoordinated teams search same areas, miss others entirely. Establish single search coordinator directing all teams.
- **Poor communication:** Teams unsure of search area assignment, no way to report findings. Assign specific sectors, establish radio check-in schedule, mark found areas on map.
- **Inadequate resources:** Starting search without enough personnel or equipment. Early estimate of scope often too optimistic - call in assistance early rather than late.
- **Wrong pattern for terrain:** Using grid search in dense forest (poor visibility) when sweep search would be more effective. Select pattern matching actual conditions.

:::tip
**Search Organization Template:** Establish incident command center (single location), designate search coordinator, divide area into sectors with assigned teams, establish base station radio protocol, maintain sector map showing searched/unsearched areas, assign sector leaders responsible for area.
:::

### Victim Assessment Errors

- **Assuming victim conscious:** Unconscious victims don't respond to shouts/whistles. Use visual searches for unresponsive victims (search shelters, shaded areas where injured might rest).
- **Missing secondary injuries:** Focusing on obvious injury while missing life-threatening condition (internal bleeding, airway obstruction). Perform thorough secondary survey.
- **Moving victim improperly:** Moving spinal injury victim incorrectly causes paralysis. Immobilize suspected spinal injuries before transport.
- **Underestimating exposure:** Cold wet victim deteriorates rapidly. Get victim warm and dry immediately - active rewarming saves lives.

### Rescue Technique Errors

- **Approaching victim carelessly:** Panicked victim may pull rescuer underwater. Approach from behind, don't let victim grab you directly.
- **Inadequate anchors:** Using weak anchor (small tree, loose rock) for rope rescue. Anchor failure = victim death. Test anchors extensively with rescuer weight.
- **Single rope systems:** Using only one rope without backup. If main rope fails, victim falls. Use redundant rope systems for technical rescue.
- **Poor victim securing:** Failing to secure victim to litter during steep terrain extraction. Victim falls if litter tilted.

### Time Management Errors

- **Slow initial response:** Delaying search start wastes critical first hours. Begin hasty search immediately while command organizes large-scale operation.
- **Searching only likely areas:** Assuming victim in logical location, ignoring unlikely but possible areas. Victims sometimes end up in unexpected places - search thoroughly, not just logical areas.
- **Not expanding search:** Keeping search small when victim not found quickly. If 50% of probable area searched and victim not found, they're likely in remaining 50% - expand search radius.

</section>

<section id="safety-protocols">

## SAR Safety Protocols

Rescuer safety must be prioritized to avoid creating additional victims. Standard safety protocols protect teams during operations.

### Pre-Operation Safety Briefing

Every SAR operation should include safety briefing:

- **Hazard assessment:** Terrain hazards (cliffs, water, wildlife), weather conditions, environmental dangers
- **Route safety:** Primary routes and bailout routes for each team
- **Communication protocol:** Radio frequencies, check-in schedule, emergency signal codes
- **Equipment check:** Verify all team members have required equipment, radios functional, first aid accessible
- **Rescuer limits:** Establish maximum search time before rotation (fatigue increases errors), maximum operating time before professional takeover

:::warning
**Fatigue Kills Rescuers:** Tired rescuers make mistakes. Establish 2-4 hour maximum search time per team, rotate teams, provide rest periods. Exhausted rescuers are liabilities, not assets.
:::

### Teamwork Requirements

- **Never search alone:** Maintain buddy system - teams of at least 2-4. Lone searchers become additional victims if injured.
- **Team leader accountability:** Team leader responsible for route, pace, hazard assessment, all team member safety.
- **Radio check-ins:** Teams check in every 15 minutes via radio. Failure to check in = immediate supervisor alert and rescue mobilization.
- **Lost team procedures:** If team unable to locate, other teams search for lost team using same systematic methods.

### Hazard-Specific Protocols

| Hazard | Risk | Control |
|--------|------|---------|
| Water crossing | Drowning, hypothermia | Use rope systems, cross at safest point, never alone |
| Cliff/steep terrain | Falls, serious injury | Use fixed ropes, avoid if untrained in rope rescue |
| Weather | Hypothermia, heatstroke | Monitor conditions, establish time limits, rotate teams |
| Wildlife | Animal attack, venom | Use noise while moving, carry deterrent, identify animal signs |
| Nighttime search | Disorientation, injuries | Use headlamps, limit night searching, resume at dawn |
| Dense forest | Getting lost, injuries | Mark route with rope/tape, compass navigation, establish turnaround time |

:::info-box
**Weather Window Management:** SAR conditions often decline rapidly. Establish time limits for search operations - if victim not found before weather deteriorates, professional teams with helicopter capability may be only option. Push too hard and teams become victims too.
:::

### SAR Equipment By Operation Type

|---|---|---|---|
| Land search, daytime | Compass, maps, flashlight, first aid, radio | GPS, rope, whistle | $200-500 |
| Night search | Headlamps, extra batteries, ground lights, strobe | Thermal imaging (if available) | $500-1500 |

| Helicopter extraction | Litter with tie-downs, communication system | Hoist basket, medical equipment | $50000+ (professional resource) |

<section id="water-rescue">

## Water Rescue Operations

Water rescue operations are among the most dangerous SAR scenarios. Swift water, cold temperatures, and drowning risks create extreme hazard. Untrained rescuers frequently become victims themselves.

:::danger
**TRAINING REQUIRED:** Water rescue demands specialized training. Over 50% of untrained water rescuers drown attempting rescue. Do NOT enter water without proper training, PFD, and rope systems. If victim in water, contact professional water rescue teams immediately.
:::

### Hazards in Water Rescue

**Swift Water Hazards:**
- **Current force:** Even shallow fast-moving water (>3 ft/s) can sweep persons off feet. Chest-deep water traveling 5+ ft/s is impassable even with ropes
- **Obstacles:** Logs, rocks, entrapment in submerged obstacles; sweepers (overhanging branches) knock victims underwater
- **Strainers:** Objects that water flows through (grates, downed trees) trap persons; body passes through but head catches
- **Hydraulics:** Recirculating water at dams or waterfalls creates "keeper" hydraulics that trap swimmers
- **Hypothermia:** Cold water causes shock, muscle failure, unconsciousness (can occur in minutes)

**Victim Behavior in Water:**
- **Panic:** Drowning victims thrash, unable to shout for help (air used for breathing, not vocalizing)
- **Cold water immersion response:** Gasping, rapid breathing, loss of motor control; person can inhale water within seconds
- **Exhaustion:** Swimming/struggling depletes energy; victim may give up quickly in cold water
- **Submersion:** Conscious victims go underwater; unconscious victims sink; rescue timing critical (brain damage begins in 4-6 minutes)

### Water Rescue Techniques (Professional Level)

**Reach:** Longest rescue tool; rescuer stands on shore extending pole, stick, or rope to victim. No rescuer entry water. Use when water condition allows reach.

**Throw:** Rescuer throws flotation device (ring buoy, throw bag) to victim. Victim grabs and holds; rescuer pulls ashore or to safety. Use for victims able to respond.

**Towing:** Rescuer enters water wearing PFD, towing line, and safety rope anchored on shore. Approaches victim from behind/side. Victim may panic and pull rescuer under.

**Boat:** If water/current too strong for wading rescue, use boat or raft. Approach from upstream; position boat to deflect victim downstream to shallow water.

**Advanced techniques:** Swift water rescue harnesses, carabiners, and rope systems; helicopter hoist for stranded victims. Requires extensive training.

### Swift Water Crossing Protocol

If team must cross moving water:

1. **Scout crossing:** Identify safest location (widest, shallowest, slowest current)
2. **Use ropes:** Establish safety line anchored upstream; persons cross using rope system
3. **Method:** Link arm-in-arm to increase stability; keep side perpendicular to current
4. **Upstream safety:** Always have rope backup on upstream side to catch person if swept
5. **One-at-a-time:** Never allow more than one person in water without rope backup
6. **Bailout routes:** Establish where person would be swept to if rope fails; ensure safe exit

:::warning
**Cold Water Kills:** Immersion in water below 60°F causes rapid hypothermia. Victims lose consciousness in 10-20 minutes, brain damage in 4-6 minutes. Resuscitate all cold water victims even if unresponsive (they may revive if core temperature restored).
:::

## Avalanche Rescue Operations

Avalanche rescue is a specialized, technical operation. Success depends on rapid burial victim location, burial depth assessment, and excavation timing. Untrained responders rarely succeed; professional avalanche rescue teams trained for months.

:::danger
**AVALANCHE TRAINING MANDATORY:** Do NOT enter avalanche terrain without proper training, equipment, and beacon systems. More people die attempting rescue than the original avalanche. Do NOT go back up if avalanche active. Contact professional mountain rescue immediately.
:::

### Avalanche Hazards

**Burial Hazards:**
- **Asphyxiation:** Most avalanche deaths (90%) result from airway obstruction; even buried victims with airlift may suffocate if not excavated quickly
- **Trauma:** Avalanche forces can cause severe internal injuries, broken bones, head trauma
- **Cold:** Burial temperature is below freezing; hypothermia begins immediately
- **Pressure injury:** Tons of snow weight crushes chest, preventing breathing

**Rescue Hazard:**
- **Secondary avalanche:** Digging in unstable slope risks triggering another avalanche burying rescuers
- **Burial depth:** Victim buried 2+ meters has <25% survival; digging to that depth takes 45+ minutes even with trained team

### Avalanche Rescue Timeline

**Critical window: First 15 minutes** — Survival drops 90% → 25% after first 15 minutes. Speed essential.

**Minutes 0-5:** Spot burial location if possible (last seen spot, debris pattern). If beacon available, switch to search mode.

**Minutes 5-15:** If burial depth <1 meter and victim unburied quickly, survival good. Victim breathing air pocket or airway open.

**Minutes 15-45:** Burial depth becomes critical. Digging rescue is long process; victim survival poor even if dug out.

**45+ minutes:** Survival extremely poor unless victim in air pocket or not fully buried.

### Search Techniques (Professional)

**Visual search:** Scan slope debris, looking for body parts, colored clothing, equipment

**Beacon search:** If victim carrying avalanche transceiver, rescuer switches beacon to search mode. Signal strength and direction guide to burial site.

**Probe:** Metal rods (3-4 meters) poked into snow to locate body. Time-consuming but effective if beacon absent.

**Excavation:** Once located, systematic digging to expose victim without causing additional trauma. Usually takes 30-60 minutes for 2-3 meter depth.

### Prevention is Best Rescue

- **Avoid avalanche terrain:** Don't travel in avalanche zones during high-risk periods (new snow, warming, heavy rain on steep slopes)
- **Check forecasts:** Avalanche forecasts available in mountain regions; avoid areas with high-danger forecasts
- **Avalanche safety equipment:** Transceivers, probes, shovels, airbags for trained groups
- **Partner system:** Never travel alone; travel with trained partners carrying rescue equipment
- **Terrain management:** Travel on ridge lines, avoid slope traverses; if must cross slope, minimize time exposed

:::tip
**Avalanche rescue success rate:** When victim buried >2 meters: 10-25% survival. When buried <1 meter: 90%+ survival. Prevention and rapid location are everything.
:::
