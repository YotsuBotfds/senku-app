**Medical Disclaimer:** Ear pain can come from wax, swimmer's ear, middle-ear infection, a foreign body, injury, or a problem that needs urgent care. This guide is for practical first aid and prevention. If pain is severe, the person is very young, or symptoms are getting worse, use medical care.
:::

<section id="overview">

## Overview

Earache is a symptom, not a diagnosis. The same complaint can come from:

- Earwax pressing on the canal
- Swimmer's ear or irritated ear canal skin
- Middle-ear pressure after a cold
- Ear infection
- Tooth, jaw, or throat pain felt in the ear
- Foreign body, trauma, or a burst eardrum

This guide is built for searches such as:

- "My child's ear hurts"
- "How do I remove earwax safely?"
- "What helps an earache without antibiotics?"
- "How do I prevent swimmer's ear?"

If you only need the shortest possible answer: use pain medicine if safe, warm compresses, keep the ear dry, do not put anything deep into the canal, and get help fast if red flags are present.

</section>

<section id="my-child-ear-hurts">

## If Your Child's Ear Hurts

Most ear pain in children is not dangerous, but some cases need prompt care.

### Start with these questions

- Is there fever?
- Is there drainage from the ear?
- Did the pain start after swimming, a cold, or an injury?
- Is the child acting very sick, dizzy, confused, or hard to wake?
- Is there swelling behind the ear or the ear sticking out more than usual?

### Use home care first when the child is otherwise well

<div class="quick-answer">

- Give acetaminophen or ibuprofen if the child can take them safely.
- Use a warm compress on the ear for 10 to 15 minutes at a time.
- Keep the ear dry.
- Let the child rest and drink fluids.
- Do not put cotton swabs, hairpins, or tools into the ear.

</div>

### Call a clinician soon if

- Pain lasts more than 24 to 48 hours
- Fever is present
- The child has repeated ear infections
- There is hearing loss, drainage, or a recent cold
- The child is under 6 months old

### Seek urgent care now if

- The child is hard to wake, confused, or very ill
- There is facial weakness
- There is severe dizziness or vomiting
- There is swelling or redness behind the ear
- There was a blow to the head, blast, or foreign body
- There is bloody drainage or sudden hearing loss

</section>

<section id="what-helps">

## What Helps an Earache Without Antibiotics

Not every earache needs antibiotics. Some ear pain comes from pressure, irritation, or a viral illness that improves on its own.

### Comfort measures

<div class="care-card">

1. **Pain relief**
   - Acetaminophen or ibuprofen may help if the person can take them safely.
   - Follow age-appropriate dosing and check for allergies, stomach disease, kidney disease, liver disease, or medication conflicts.

2. **Warm compress**
   - Place a warm, not hot, cloth against the outer ear for short periods.
   - Do not use heat that can burn skin.

3. **Keep the ear dry**
   - Avoid swimming until pain is improving.
   - Use a shower cap or a dry cotton ball coated with petroleum jelly at the entrance of the ear for bathing if water tends to enter.

4. **Support drainage and pressure relief**
   - If the pain followed a cold, swallowing, chewing, and nasal saline may help with pressure.
   - For older children and adults, gentle yawning or chewing may reduce pressure.

5. **Rest and fluids**
   - Dehydration and poor sleep make pain feel worse.

</div>

### What not to do

- Do not put oil, vinegar, alcohol, peroxide, or drops into the ear if there is a hole in the eardrum, ear tubes, drainage, severe pain, or uncertainty about the diagnosis.
- Do not use ear candles.
- Do not scrape the canal with cotton swabs.
- Do not keep trying home remedies if pain is worsening.

If the pain is from swimmer's ear or a clear ear infection, home care can reduce discomfort but may not be enough. Persistent or worsening symptoms need evaluation.

</section>

<section id="earwax-safe-care">

## How to Remove Earwax Safely

Earwax is normal. It protects the ear canal, traps dust, and helps skin stay healthy. The goal is not to make the canal perfectly clean. The goal is to remove wax only when it is causing trouble.

### When wax can be left alone

- No pain
- No hearing change
- No fullness
- No drainage
- No repeated blockage

### Signs wax may be causing a problem

- Blocked or muffled hearing
- Fullness or pressure
- Mild itchiness
- Trouble using earbuds or hearing aids
- Wax visible at the opening of the canal

### Safe options

<div class="care-card">

1. **Wipe only the outer ear**
   - Use a washcloth on the outside part only.
   - Never push anything deeper than the opening.

2. **Soften wax**
   - A few drops of mineral oil, glycerin, or saline may soften wax in some people.
   - Only use softening drops when there is no known eardrum hole, ear tubes, active drainage, severe pain, or recent ear surgery.

3. **Gentle irrigation only when appropriate**
   - Irrigation is not for everyone.
   - Do not irrigate if there is pain, drainage, diabetes with ear symptoms, a history of ear surgery, a known perforation, or uncertainty about the diagnosis.
   - If a clinician recommends irrigation, use lukewarm water and gentle pressure.

4. **See a clinician for stubborn blockage**
   - Office removal is safer when wax is hard, deep, or repeated.

</div>

### Do not use

- Cotton swabs deep in the canal
- Hairpins, bobby pins, keys, toothpicks, or tweezers
- Ear candles
- Random chemical cleaners when the eardrum status is unknown

### Special caution

If the ear hurts, drains, smells bad, or the person has fever or hearing loss, do not assume it is "just wax." Wax removal should wait until a clinician has ruled out infection or eardrum injury.

</section>

<section id="swimmers-ear">

## Swimmer's Ear Prevention

Swimmer's ear is irritation and infection of the ear canal skin. It is more likely after swimming, sweating, scratching, or frequent water exposure.

### Prevention basics

<div class="care-card">

- Dry the ears gently after swimming or bathing.
- Tilt the head to let water drain out.
- Use a clean towel on the outer ear only.
- Avoid scratching the canal.
- Do not use cotton swabs to "dry" the ear.
- Wear swimmer's ear plugs if a person gets repeated water-triggered ear pain.

</div>

### Drying drops

Some people use drying drops after swimming, but they are not for everyone.

Do not use drying drops if:

- There is ear pain already
- There is drainage
- Ear tubes are present
- A perforated eardrum is known or possible
- The ear has recently been injured or operated on

If there is a history of swimmer's ear, a clinician can advise whether drying drops are appropriate and which formula is safest.

### What increases risk

- Frequent swimming
- Wet earbuds or hearing aids
- Cotton swab use
- Eczema or skin irritation in the canal
- Dirty water exposure

</section>

<section id="hearing-protection">

## Hearing Protection Basics

Protecting hearing matters even when the ear is otherwise healthy. Loud noise can permanently damage the inner ear.

### Good habits

- Move away from the noise source when possible
- Take quiet breaks during long loud exposures
- Lower volume on headphones and earbuds
- Use hearing protection for lawn work, power tools, concerts, fireworks, and other loud events

### Simple rule of thumb

If you have to raise your voice to talk to someone at arm's length, the noise may already be harmful.

### Earplug basics

- Foam earplugs work if they are inserted correctly and fully sealed
- Earmuffs can be easier for beginners and for short intense noise
- Double protection may help for very loud tools or explosions

### Extra caution for children

- Children should not use oversized protection that does not seal well
- Keep young children away from fireworks and very loud machinery

</section>

<section id="danger-signs">

## Red Flags for Infection or a Dangerous Ear Problem

<div class="red-flag">

### Get urgent medical care now if there is:

- Swelling or redness behind the ear
- Ear pushed forward or sticking out
- Facial weakness
- Severe dizziness, trouble walking, or repeated vomiting
- High fever or a child who looks very ill
- Bloody drainage after injury
- Sudden hearing loss
- Severe headache, stiff neck, confusion, or sleepiness
- A foreign body that cannot be removed easily
- Chemical exposure in the ear

</div>

### Get prompt evaluation within a day or two if:

- Pain is not improving
- Drainage is present
- Hearing is reduced
- Pain started after swimming and is getting worse
- The ear is tender when the outer ear is pulled
- There is a long history of ear problems

### Why these matter

These signs can point to swimmer's ear, a middle-ear infection, a burst eardrum, mastoiditis, a foreign body, a significant injury, or a deeper infection that should not be managed at home.

</section>

<section id="common-questions">

## Common Questions

### "What helps an earache without antibiotics?"

Use pain medicine if safe, warm compresses, and rest. Keep the ear dry. If symptoms are from pressure after a cold, swallowing, chewing, and nasal saline may help. If pain worsens or fever/drainage appears, get medical care.

### "How do I remove earwax safely?"

Only wipe the outer ear. If wax is soft and there is no drainage, pain, ear tube, or known perforation, softening drops may help. Hard, deep, or recurrent wax is safer to remove in clinic.

### "When should I worry?"

Worry if there is fever, drainage, swelling behind the ear, dizziness, facial weakness, sudden hearing loss, head injury, or a child who is very unwell.

### "Can I swim with ear pain?"

Not until the pain is improving and the cause is known. Swimming can worsen swimmer's ear and may be unsafe if the eardrum is injured.

</section>

<section id="summary">

## Quick Summary

- Earache has many causes; not all need antibiotics.
- Use pain relief, a warm compress, rest, and a dry ear for simple comfort care.
- Do not put tools or cotton swabs into the canal.
- Remove wax only gently, and only when it is clearly safe.
- Prevent swimmer's ear by drying the ear and avoiding trauma.
- Protect hearing from loud noise.
- Seek urgent care for fever with worsening pain, drainage, swelling behind the ear, facial weakness, severe dizziness, sudden hearing loss, or a very ill child.

</section>
