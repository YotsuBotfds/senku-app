:::warning
**Toxic heritage cultivars:** Some heritage varieties, especially in crops like cassava, beans, and bitter almonds, contain plant toxins (cyanogenic glycosides, lectins) that require specific preparation (soaking, cooking, fermentation). Consuming them improperly causes poisoning. Always verify traditional preparation methods before consuming unfamiliar heritage varieties. Consult elders who know the specific cultivar being grown.
:::

<section id="overview">

## Overview

Ethnobotany is the study of how people use plants. Heritage crops and landraces (locally-adapted varieties) carry centuries of agricultural knowledge and genetic diversity. Modern industrial agriculture relies on 3 crops (rice, wheat, maize) for 60% of global calories—creating vulnerability to pests, disease, and climate change. Heritage crop diversity is resilience; it is also cultural preservation.

This guide covers surveying plant knowledge, documenting uses, and establishing seed conservation networks that protect genetic diversity and empower communities.

</section>

<section id="plant-use-categories">

## Plant Use Categories and Classification

Plants serve multiple roles. Documenting these uses is the foundation of ethnobotany.

### Classification Scheme

| **Category** | **Definition** | **Example** |
|---|---|---|
| Food | Edible parts (fruit, seed, leaf, root) | Corn, beans, squash |
| Medicine | Healing properties; medicinal preparations | Willow bark (pain relief), garlic (antibacterial) |
| Fiber | Textile, rope, paper, weaving materials | Flax, hemp, nettles |
| Dye | Pigments for coloring textiles, leather | Indigo, madder, walnut hull |
| Construction | Building materials, tools, furniture | Oak (structural), bamboo (poles), willow (baskets) |
| Livestock feed | Fodder, forage for animals | Clover, alfalfa, tree leaves |
| Fuel | Firewood, charcoal, oil | Oak, birch, plant oils |
| Ritual/spiritual | Ceremonial use; cultural significance | Tobacco, sage, peyote |
| Ecological | Nitrogen fixers, soil builders, pioneer species | Alders, legumes, invasive control |
| Toxicology | Poisonous; pest control; research value | Arsenic-accumulating plants, insecticidal neem |

**Multiple categories (polyvalent plants):**

Some plants serve many roles; documentation must capture all uses.

```
Example: Squash
- Food: Seeds and flesh edible; stores well
- Ecological: Nitrogen-fixing companion (intercropped with corn)
- Material: Dried gourds as containers, tools
- Medicine: Seeds for digestive health
- Fiber: Dried stems usable for binding, weaving

Comprehensive documentation includes all uses; ignorance of one use may result in loss of knowledge when variety disappears.
```

## Ethnobotanical Survey Methods

**Goal:** Document which plants are used, how, and why; who uses them; how knowledge is transmitted.

### Interview Protocol

**Approach:**

- Build trust (explain purpose; ensure confidentiality if requested).
- Use open-ended questions (allow storytelling, context).
- Record with permission (written notes and/or audio; photography of plants).
- Verify information with multiple respondents (cross-check accuracy).

**Questions:**

```
1. What plants do you use from the forest/field?
2. What do you use each plant for?
3. Which parts do you use? When do you harvest?
4. How do you prepare it? How much do you use?
5. Why is this plant better than store-bought alternatives?
6. Who taught you? How did you learn?
7. Have you noticed changes (plant availability, traditional practices)?
8. Are there plants that are becoming rare/lost?
```

**Documentation template:**

```
Plant name (common + scientific if known)
Habitat (where it grows; specific conditions)
Uses (food, medicine, material, ceremonial, etc.)
Parts used (leaf, root, seed, bark, whole plant)
Harvest season (when; how long cycle)
Preparation method (raw, cooked, dried, extracted, etc.)
Amount used (dose, frequency, duration)
Cultural significance (spiritual, historical, family tradition)
Knowledge holder (name, age, contact if willing to share)
Cross-references (other users, traditional text mentions)
Photos (plant, habitat, dried specimen, prepared form)
```

### Biodiversity Walk

Conducting a guided walk with a knowledgeable community member documents habitat and abundance.

**Method:**

```
1. Recruit an experienced plant user (elder, farmer, healer).
2. Walk a familiar area (1–2 hours, known route).
3. Pause at plants; record:
   - Identification (scientific name if possible)
   - Abundance (common, occasional, rare)
   - Habitat association (soil type, water, shade)
   - Uses mentioned by guide
   - Changes noticed (increase/decrease, reason given)
4. Photograph; mark GPS location if digitizing
5. Compare walks over years (trends, seasonal variation)
```

**Timeline:** 5–10 walks per habitat type (seasonal variation); accumulation over multiple years reveals trends.

## Heritage Seed Varieties and Landrace Preservation

**Landrace:** A locally-adapted variety developed over generations by farmers. Traits: resilient to local conditions, culturally significant, often superior taste/nutrition.

**Heritage variety:** Heirloom seed varieties grown before industrial agriculture (pre-1950s), maintained through traditional farming.

### Trait Documentation

Before preserving seeds, document why they matter:

| **Trait** | **Significance** | **Example** |
|---|---|---|
| Taste/nutrition | Culinary quality; traditional recipes | Black beans (earthier flavor than commercial) |
| Climate resilience | Tolerance to drought, cold, pests | "Bloody Butcher" corn (drought-tolerant, heirloom) |
| Growing characteristics | Yield, maturity timing, companion benefits | Dent corn (livestock feed vs. sweet corn, fresh eat) |
| Seed production | Self-fertile, easy to harvest, seed saving | Bean varieties (self-pollinating; true-to-type seed) |
| Traditional uses | Cultural/medicinal/material value | Dent corn (nixtamalization, flour, masa) |
| Rarity | Risk of extinction; urgency to conserve | Locally-known landraces with few seed-keepers |

### Seed Saving Practices

**Selection criteria:**

```
Grow-out multiple plants to maintain genetic diversity
Select for:
1. Vigor (healthy, disease-resistant)
2. Trait expression (desired characteristics: size, color, flavor)
3. Isolation (prevent cross-pollination with other varieties)

Keep plants in isolation 100+ feet from other varieties (pollen travel distance varies by crop)
```

**Seed production workflow:**

```
Year 1: Obtain seeds; grow diverse plants (>6, ideally 20+)
Year 2: Select best individuals; let them set seed; harvest, dry, store
Year 3: Grow out saved seeds; compare to original; save again if traits stable

After 3–5 years: Variety is stable, established in local growing conditions
```

**Maintenance:**

```
Viable seeds: 3–10 years depending on crop
Re-grow every 3 years to maintain viability
Cull off-types (plants that don't match expected traits)
Save larger seed populations (100+ seeds per variety minimum, 500+ ideal)
```

## Seed Sovereignty and Community Networks

Seed sovereignty is the right of farmers and communities to maintain, select, use, and exchange seeds—free from corporate or state control.

### Seed Saving Cooperatives

**Structure:**

```
Community seed library/bank
    ↓
Farmers save seeds; deposit excess
    ↓
Library catalogs, stores, lends seeds to members
    ↓
Farmers grow out, return seeds + surplus
    ↓
Library distributes to other members

Cost: $0 to community members (library covers storage, admin via donations)
Benefit: Access to adapted, cheap seeds; cultural preservation
```

**Operation:**

```
Spring: Distribute seeds to interested growers; track who takes what
Summer: Monitor growing crops; document traits
Fall: Collect matured seeds from participating farmers
Winter: Clean, test viability, store in cool/dry location
Spring (next year): Repeat; distribute to new farmers + replenish lenders
```

**Governance:**

- Decisions by consensus or simple majority (community-determined).
- Seeds remain public property (never sold; shared freely).
- Documentation of varietal traits and growing results.

### Seed Exchange Networks

**Regional scale:** Multiple communities exchange varieties; expands access.

**Method:**

```
1. Community A documents varieties (10 tomato types, 5 bean types, etc.)
2. Documents are shared (written, photos, online database)
3. Community B expresses interest in tomato variety "Bloody Mary"
4. Exchange: Shipment of "Bloody Mary" + "Old Homestead" beans to B
5. Community B sends "Big Mama" tomato + "Purple Pod" beans to A
6. Both document how varieties perform; share results

Annual exchange: 5–10 varieties; multi-year partnerships develop
```

**Benefits:**

- Genetic diversity increase (each community expands seed stock).
- Resilience (if local variety fails, trade partners provide backup).
- Knowledge exchange (growing conditions, recipes, cultural significance).
- Cost savings (free vs. commercial seed).

### Documentation for Sharing

**Minimum information per variety:**

```
Plant name (common + scientific)
Flavor/quality description
Days to maturity
Yield (est. lbs per plant or per area)
Pest/disease tolerance
Growing conditions (sun, water, soil)
Propagation notes (direct seed vs. transplant, spacing)
Cooking/preparation uses
Cultural or family significance
Growing region (where it was developed)
Seed viability (how long viable; best harvested when)
Contact person (if variety is proprietary to family/community)
```

**Format:**

- Printed card (seed packet + description); easy to trade.
- Digital database (searchable; geographic distribution).
- Community cookbook (traditional recipes featuring varieties).

## Germplasm Conservation and Long-Term Storage

Germplasm (genetic material in seed) must be preserved for decades and centuries.

### Storage Conditions

**Ideal:** Cool (40°F), dry (20–30% humidity), dark.

```
Temperature increase: Every 10°C rise halves seed lifespan
Humidity increase: Every 1% moisture increase halves lifespan

Example: Seeds stored at 70°F, 50% humidity
Expected life: 2–3 years

Same seeds at 40°F, 20% humidity:
Expected life: 20–30 years

Ideal: Freezer storage (-18°C): Extends viability 50–100 years
```

### Storage Methods

**Home/small-scale (1–10 varieties):**

```
1. Dry seeds thoroughly (5–10% moisture content)
2. Store in sealed envelope in cool, dry place (closet, basement)
3. Check periodically (no moisture condensation; no mold)
4. Grow-out every 3 years to maintain vigor
```

**Community bank (50–200 varieties):**

```
1. Clean and dry all seeds to uniform standard
2. Store in sealed jars with desiccant (silica gel) in cool closet
3. Database tracking: Variety name, quantity, year acquired, viability test results
4. Viability testing: Germinate sample every 2 years; record % germination
5. Replace stored stock if germination drops <80%
```

**Large collection (1,000+ varieties):**

```
Requires climate-controlled storage
Backup copies at multiple locations (redundancy)
Professional seed bank involvement (coordination, standards)

Example: "Crop Trust" seeds stored in Svalbard Global Seed Vault
Frozen (-18°C) in Norwegian Arctic; essentially permanent storage
Backup for regional seed banks worldwide
```

### Viability Testing

**Simple germination test:**

```
1. Obtain sample of stored seeds (10–100 seeds per variety)
2. Moisten paper towel; place seeds; roll; place in plastic bag
3. Keep moist, warm (70°F) for 7–14 days
4. Count sprouted seeds; calculate germination percentage

Expected result:
- Fresh seed: >90% germination
- 3-year storage: 80–90%
- 5-year storage: 70–80%
- 10-year storage: 50–70% (dependent on initial quality, storage conditions)

If <50%: Replace stored stock; grow out new seed for storage
```

## Documenting Cultural Significance

Plants carry cultural meaning beyond their practical use. Documentation preserves this context.

### Cultural Domains

| **Domain** | **Questions** | **Example** |
|---|---|---|
| Culinary tradition | Traditional recipes; feast occasions; seasonal uses | Three Sisters (corn, beans, squash) intercropping; cultural significance in ceremonies |
| Medicinal tradition | Treatments; practitioners; preparations | Willow bark as traditional pain relief; codification in materia medica |
| Sacred/spiritual | Ceremonial use; origin stories; spiritual properties | Tobacco in Native American ceremony; sage in smudging |
| Symbolism | What plant represents; cultural meaning | Olive branch as peace; laurel as honor |
| History | Plant origin; migration stories; historical events | Varieties named after places or events ("Irish Lumper", "Cherokee Trail of Tears beans") |
| Social/economic | Subsistence; trade; livelihood; equity | Women's role in seed-saving; commercial value; access equity |

### Documentation Format

**Narrative account (from knowledge holder):**

```
"We have grown Grandmother's corn for 80 years. The story is that she brought seeds from [original place] when the family moved here in 1940s. The corn is sweet and stores well. We use it for [traditional food]. Every fall, my family gathers to harvest and process. Children learn the skill. Now my grandson is interested in seed-saving. This is how knowledge passes."
```

**Structured record:**

```
Variety: "Grandmother's corn"
Knowledge holder: Name, age, family relationship
Origin story: Where seeds came from; how they arrived in community
Cultural uses: Traditional dishes, ceremonies, seasonal meanings
Family significance: Who grows it; how it's transmitted; who is responsible
Current status: Growing? If not, why discontinued? Possibility of recovery?
Contact/consent: Permission to document; any restrictions on sharing knowledge?
Photos/recordings: Visual documentation; recorded stories (with permission)
```

### Ethical Considerations

**Informed consent:**

- Explain how information will be used (publication, archive, database).
- Ask explicitly for permission; respect refusals.
- Distinguish public knowledge vs. sacred/restricted knowledge.

**Benefit-sharing:**

- Clearly state who benefits from documentation (researchers, community, corporate).
- Consider compensation for knowledge-holders (payment, copies of publication, credit).
- Establish protocols to prevent biopiracy (intellectual property theft).

**Sovereignty:**

- Communities retain rights to their knowledge.
- Documentation should be accessible to community first (not just outsiders).
- If research is published, offer community first option to approve/edit.

## Integration Example: Regional Heritage Crop Initiative

**Region:** Rural county; 10 farming communities; historical crops going extinct.

**Goal:** Document varieties; establish seed bank; revitalize traditional crops; create income from specialty seeds/crops.

**Year 1: Survey and Documentation**

```
Activities:
- Interview 50+ farmers, elders, healers (2 per community)
- Document 100+ varieties (crops, traits, cultural significance)
- Collect seeds where still growing
- Photograph plants, seeds, preparation methods

Output:
- Database: 100 varieties with full documentation
- Photo archive: 300+ images
- Oral histories: Transcribed interviews (30 hours audio)
- Community report: Summary of findings (printed, distributed)

Labor: 500 person-hours (researcher + community volunteers)
Cost: $15,000 (travel, equipment, volunteer coordination, printing)
```

**Year 2: Seed Bank Establishment**

```
Activities:
- Establish community seed bank (cooperative structure)
- Grow out documented varieties (test viability, document traits)
- Train seed keepers (4 per community) in seed-saving, storage
- Develop governance (who decides what, how conflicts resolved)

Output:
- 8 community seed banks operational
- 500 seed packets available for distribution
- Trained network of 40 seed keepers
- Manual: "How to Save, Store, and Share Seeds" (10 pages, translated)

Labor: 1,000 person-hours (combined staff + volunteer training)
Cost: $25,000 (storage materials, training, seed testing, documentation)
```

**Year 3–5: Network Development and Use**

```
Activities:
- Annual seed exchanges between communities
- Grow-outs and trait documentation (ongoing)
- Community education (workshops, school programs)
- Specialty market development (heirloom seeds for sale; heirloom crops for farmers market)

Output (Year 5):
- 200+ varieties in active circulation
- 5+ communities sharing seeds regularly
- 20 farmers growing heirloom crops commercially (farmers market, restaurants)
- $50,000/year revenue from seed and crop sales
- 500+ community members participating

Sustainability:
- Revenue covers operational costs
- Volunteer commitment stable (participation rewarding)
- Knowledge transmission to younger generation underway
- Regional identity (proud of heritage crops)

Cost (cumulative 5 years): $75,000
Revenue (Year 3–5): $150,000+
Net: Self-sustaining; economically viable
```
