## Overview
Hand pumps are simple, durable, and often repairable with basic tools. When they fail, the cause is usually one of a small number of problems: loss of prime, air leaks on the suction side, worn leather seals, stuck check valves, clogged screens, frozen water, or cracked parts. The fastest way to restore flow is to diagnose the failure in order instead of replacing parts randomly.
This guide covers the most common repairs for shallow-well and lift-style hand pumps, including pitcher pumps, deep-well hand pumps with rods, and similar manual water pumps.

:::info
**First-check: hand pump failure modes**
Before replacing parts, rule out these common causes in order:

1. **Is the water level still within the pump's lift limit?** -> low source water or the wrong pump for the depth
2. **Did you reprime with clean water?** -> lost prime is the fastest problem to rule out
3. **Are suction-side joints, packing, or hose connections leaking air?** -> tighten and inspect for cracks or loose fittings
4. **Does the pump draw briefly and then drain back down?** -> check valve or foot valve problem
5. **Is the handle stiff, frozen, or binding?** -> seal wear, corrosion, freeze damage, or a bent rod/plunger
6. **Does the water look dirty, smell off, or come from a questionable storage source?** -> route to [Questionable Water Assessment and Clarification](questionable-water-assessment-clarification.html) or [Water Storage Tank Maintenance](water-storage-tank-maintenance.html)
7. **Is the intake screen clogged or full of grit?** -> clean the screen or intake before assuming the pump body is bad
:::

## Quick Troubleshooting Order
If a hand pump is not drawing water, check the problem in this order:
1. Verify the well still has water and the water level is within the pump's lift limit.
2. Reprime the pump with clean water.
3. Check for air leaks at joints, fittings, packing, and hose connections.
4. Inspect the leather cup or piston seal for wear, stiffness, or damage.
5. Check the inlet and outlet check valves for sticking or debris.
6. Clean the screen, foot valve, strainer, or intake.
7. Inspect for freezing, cracks, or a broken rod/plunger.
If the pump works briefly and then stops, suspect a suction leak, weak check valve, or a worn seal that cannot hold prime.
## Common Failure Modes
| Symptom | Likely Cause | Best First Action |
|---|---|---|
| Pump handle moves but no water comes out | Lost prime, air leak, stuck check valve | Reprime and inspect suction-side fittings |
| Water flows weakly | Worn leather seal, partial clog, low water level | Check seal and clean intake/screen |
| Pump loses water after sitting | Leaky foot valve or check valve | Clean or replace the valve |
| Handle feels stiff or jerky | Dry leather, corrosion, frozen line, bent rod | Lubricate, thaw safely, inspect for binding |
| Water spurts with air | Air leak on suction side or cracked pump body | Tighten joints and inspect for cracks |
| Sand or grit in water | Damaged screen or intake too low | Clean screen and raise intake if possible |
## My Hand Pump Is Not Drawing Water
This is the most common field complaint. In most cases, the pump is not "broken" in a major way; it has lost prime or is pulling air instead of water.
### Step-by-Step Diagnosis
1. Pour clean water into the pump head until the chamber is full.
2. Pump slowly 10 to 20 strokes.
3. If water appears and then fades, the pump has a suction leak or weak check valve.
4. If nothing appears, open the pump head and inspect the valve and seal.
5. If the handle moves too easily, the pump may be drawing air through a cracked part or disconnected suction line.
6. If the handle is very hard to move and no water appears, suspect a blockage, frozen line, or seized valve.
### Loss of Prime
Loss of prime means the pump and suction line are full of air instead of water. Hand pumps depend on a water-filled column to create suction on the upstroke. If air enters the system, the pump cannot lift water.
**Common causes of lost prime:**
- Dry or leaking foot valve
- Loose pipe joint or cracked fitting
- Worn leather cup or piston seal
- Pump not used for a long time
- Water level dropped below the suction limit
**How to reprime:**
1. Shut off any outlet or close the discharge path if the pump has one.
2. Remove the priming plug or open the top fill point.
3. Pour clean water into the pump body until full.
4. Replace the plug and pump in short, steady strokes.
5. If water starts to move, continue until the line is fully charged.
6. If the pump loses prime again immediately, inspect for an air leak or bad valve.
If the pump cannot be re-primed repeatedly, the foot valve or lower check valve is not sealing.
## How to Replace the Leather Seal
A worn leather seal is one of the most common reasons a hand pump still moves but will not lift water effectively. Leather seals dry out, shrink, crack, or wear thin over time.
### Signs the Leather Seal Needs Replacement
- The pump requires much more effort than normal
- Water output is weak even after priming
- The pump works only when manually lubricated or soaked
- You find torn, hardened, curled, or missing leather
### Replacement Procedure
1. Disassemble the pump head and remove the piston rod or plunger.
2. Note the seal orientation before removal.
3. Remove old leather, packing, rings, washers, or retaining hardware.
4. Clean the piston and bore with a rag and mild brush.
5. Inspect the bore for scoring, rust, or sharp edges.
6. Soak the new leather seal in clean water or light oil if appropriate for the pump design.
7. Install the seal with the lip facing the correct direction so it seals on the working stroke.
8. Reassemble and test with a few slow strokes before full operation.
### Leather Seal Fit
The seal must be snug enough to hold pressure but not so tight that the pump binds. If the seal is too loose, it will leak past the piston. If it is too tight, the handle will become hard to move and the leather may wear out quickly.
For temporary field use, a slightly oversized leather seal can sometimes be trimmed to fit, but a seal that is far too loose should be replaced rather than shimmed indefinitely.
### Leather Seal Care
- Keep leather slightly oiled or conditioned if the pump design allows it
- Do not let the pump sit dry for long periods if avoidable
- Flush out grit before it scores the seal
- Replace hard or cracked leather before total failure
## Stuck Check Valves
Check valves make the pump one-way: they let water move in the correct direction and stop it from flowing back. If a check valve sticks open or fails to seat, the pump will not hold prime.
### Signs of a Check Valve Problem
- Pump draws water briefly, then it drains back down
- Handle suddenly becomes easier or emptier than normal
- Water does not stay in the pump body after priming
- Sand, rust, or scale is visible near the valve seat
### Cleaning a Stuck Valve
1. Disassemble the valve housing.
2. Remove dirt, rust, scale, and grit.
3. Rinse the seat with clean water.
4. Check that the ball, flap, or poppet moves freely.
5. Reassemble and test the valve by blowing or drawing air through it if appropriate.
For ball check valves, the ball must seat smoothly and not stick in a pitted cage. For flap or umbrella valves, the flexible element must not be warped, swollen, or cracked.
### When to Replace
Replace the valve if the seat is badly scored, the spring is broken, the flap is torn, or corrosion prevents reliable seating. A pump with a weak valve may work for a while, but it will keep losing prime.
## Clogged Screens and Intakes
Clogged screens restrict water flow and can make a healthy pump look weak. This is especially common in sandy wells, shallow surface intakes, and pumps left unused for long periods.
### Symptoms of a Clogged Screen
- Pump output drops gradually over time
- Sediment appears in the first water drawn
- Pump needs many strokes before any water appears
- Pump works better after resting, then degrades again
### Cleaning the Screen
1. Remove the intake assembly if possible.
2. Brush or rinse the screen from the clean side outward.
3. Remove algae, silt, scale, and rust flakes.
4. Check for torn mesh or bent intake parts.
5. Reinstall and reprime the pump.
If the screen keeps clogging, the intake may be too low in the water column or too close to sediment. Raise it if the well setup allows it.
## Frozen Lines and Frozen Pump Heads
Frozen water blocks movement and can crack the pump body, split fittings, or damage seals. Thawing must be slow and controlled.
### How to Thaw a Frozen Pump Safely
1. Stop pumping immediately.
2. Open the discharge path or outlet if possible so pressure cannot build.
3. Warm the pump head with towels soaked in hot water, a safe electric heater, or a hair dryer on a low setting.
4. Work from the exposed pump head outward toward the pipe or line.
5. Keep warming gradually until water movement returns.
6. Pump gently at first and inspect for cracks or leaks.
**Safe thawing methods:**
- Warm water poured over the exposed body
- Electric heat source kept at a safe distance
- Heated room or insulated pump house
- Heat packs around the pump head and exposed fittings
**Do not use:**
- Open flame
- Torch heat
- Boiling water on fragile plastic parts
- Hammering frozen fittings
If a frozen line cracked the pump body or a fitting, repair the damage before pumping again. Thawing only solves the ice; it does not fix the rupture.
## Can I Improvise a Pump Gasket?
Yes, sometimes. A temporary gasket can restore service when the original part is unavailable, but the material must be compatible with clean water and strong enough to seal.
### Good Improvised Gasket Materials
- Leather
- Clean rubber from potable-water-safe tubing or seals
- Gasket paper
- Fiber sheet
- Cork for low-pressure covers
- Inner tube rubber for non-critical temporary sealing
### Poor Choices
- Foam that soaks up water
- Brittle cardboard
- Oily rags
- Materials that shed fibers into the water path
- Sealants not rated for potable water in drinking-water service
### Making a Temporary Gasket
1. Clean both mating surfaces completely.
2. Cut the gasket material slightly larger than the flange.
3. Punch bolt holes cleanly so the gasket does not tear.
4. Tighten fasteners evenly in a cross pattern.
5. Recheck after the first pumping session.
If the gasket is on the suction side, even a tiny leak can ruin prime. For that reason, a temporary gasket should be treated as a short-term repair, not a permanent fix.
## Reassembly and Test
After any repair, test the pump in stages.
1. Reassemble all parts without forcing threads or fasteners.
2. Prime the pump fully.
3. Pump slowly and watch for water, leaks, and air bubbles.
4. Increase to normal speed only after the pump is drawing steadily.
5. Inspect every joint, cap, and gasket for seepage.
6. Listen for clicking, grinding, or squealing that could indicate a valve or seal problem.
If the pump works after repair but fails again later, the repair was probably successful only on the symptom. Recheck the suction side and check valves for the underlying cause.
## Preventive Maintenance
Regular care prevents most pump failures.
### Weekly or Monthly
- Pump until water runs clear and steady
- Check for loose bolts and fittings
- Wipe down the handle, rod, and exposed metal
- Look for rust, leaks, and cracked seals
- Verify the pump primes easily
### Seasonal
- Lubricate moving parts if the pump design allows it
- Inspect leather seals for drying or hardening
- Clean the intake screen and check valve
- Protect exposed piping before freezing weather
- Make sure the pump house or well cap excludes insects, dirt, and debris
### Long-Term Storage
- Drain or winterize the pump if it will not be used
- Keep spare seals, gaskets, and check valve parts on hand
- Store leather parts dry and clean until needed
- Protect metal parts from corrosion
## Field Repair Kit
A compact repair kit can solve many failures without a full rebuild:
- Spare leather seal or cup
- Assorted gasket material
- Small brush and rag
- Screwdrivers and wrenches
- Thread sealant appropriate for potable water
- Light lubricant
- Replacement fasteners and washers
- Clean priming container
- Small flashlight or headlamp
## Practical Rule
If a hand pump is not drawing water, assume an air leak or valve problem first, not a catastrophic failure. Most manual pumps can be restored in the field with cleaning, priming, seal replacement, or a simple gasket repair.
