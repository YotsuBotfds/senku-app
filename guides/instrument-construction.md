## Drum Construction

Drums are fundamental to any musical culture and among the easiest instruments to construct. They require only a hollow chamber, stretched membrane, and striking implement.

### Frame Drum (Simple, Versatile)

Frame drums are flat drums with membrane stretched across simple wooden ring. Used globally in traditional music.

**Materials:**

<table><thead><tr>
<th>Component</th>
<th>Options</th>
<th>Notes</th>
</tr></thead><tbody><tr>
<td>Frame</td>
<td>Wooden ring carved/bent from wood or bundled branches</td>
<td>8-14 inches diameter is practical size</td>
</tr><tr>
<td>Head (membrane)</td>
<td>Animal hide (goat, sheep, rabbit), fish skin, or parchment</td>
<td>Fresh hide stretched and dried; parchment is processed skin</td>
</tr><tr>
<td>Fastening</td>
<td>Sinew (animal tendon), plant fiber cord, or wooden pegs</td>
<td>Must withstand tension without stretching</td>
</tr><tr>
<td>Handle (optional)</td>
<td>Carved wooden handle mortised through frame</td>
<td>Allows one-handed playing</td>
</tr></tbody></table>

**Construction steps:**

1. Create circular frame: carve from solid wood, or steam-bend sapling into ring and bind ends with cord/sinew
2. Select animal hide (fresh or pre-processed parchment)
3. If using fresh hide: scrape clean, remove hair, and stretch on drying frame until completely dry
4. Soak dried hide briefly (10-15 minutes) to make pliable
5. Lay hide over frame and stretch toward edges
6. Using sinew or cord, lash hide to frame edges, starting at opposite points and working around circumference
7. Adjust tension evenly as you lash (uneven tension creates poor sound)
8. Allow to dry completely (3-7 days), which tightens head further
9. Test tone: strike with hand or soft stick. Tone should be warm, resonant, without rattles

### Log Drum (Slit Drum)

Carved from single log, slit drums produce multiple pitches when struck at different points. Simple construction, very durable.

**Process:**

1. Select suitable wood log (12-30 inches long, 8-12 inches diameter; hardwood preferred)
2. Mark slit location down length of log: typically one or two parallel slits running 6-10 inches, offset toward center
3. Using chisel and mallet, carefully carve slit(s) into log, removing wood between marked lines
4. Carve walls of slit smoothly and evenly (wall thickness 1-2 inches affects tone)
5. Smooth all cut surfaces with stone or scraper
6. Mount on stand or suspend from support allowing free vibration
7. Strike different areas along log length: different points produce different pitches

**Tuning slit drums:** Wall thickness varies pitch. Thinner walls (closer to 1 inch) produce higher pitches; thicker walls (2 inches) produce lower pitches. Adjust pitch by careful chiseling (reduction only, cannot be reversed).

### Tension Drum (Headed Frame with Tuning)

**For more sophisticated sound and pitch control:**

1. Create sturdy wooden frame on cylindrical base (8-10 inches deep)
2. Stretch hide over top (as frame drum)
3. Instead of permanent lashing, use tuning system: leather straps run from top membrane down to base
4. Wooden toggles or pegs inserted through strap holes adjust tension
5. Tightening lashes raises pitch; loosening lowers pitch
6. Allows player to modify pitch during performance (common in African and Asian drums)

## String Instruments

String instruments are sophisticated but achievable with careful construction. They require understanding of tension, wood resonance, and pitch relationships.

### Simple Lyre (Most Practical String Instrument)

**Historical instrument, simple to construct, versatile in sound:**

**Materials:**

<table><thead><tr>
<th>Part</th>
<th>Material</th>
<th>Specifications</th>
</tr></thead><tbody><tr>
<td>Frame arms</td>
<td>Curved wood pieces</td>
<td>2 identical pieces, 12-18 inches long, 1.5 inches wide</td>
</tr><tr>
<td>Soundboard</td>
<td>Thin wood panel (spruce or similar)</td>
<td>1/4 inch thick, 6x8 inches minimum</td>
</tr><tr>
<td>Yoke (bridge)</td>
<td>Carved wood</td>
<td>Connects top of arms, 4-6 inches wide</td>
</tr><tr>
<td>Base</td>
<td>Carved wood</td>
<td>Connects arm bottoms, 4-6 inches wide</td>
</tr><tr>
<td>Strings</td>
<td>Plant fiber or animal gut</td>
<td>7-10 strings for basic lyre</td>
</tr></tbody></table>

**Construction:**

1. Create two identical curved arms (like elongated parentheses shape, 12-18 inches tall)
2. Connect arms at top with carved yoke piece (horizontal bar)
3. Connect arms at bottom with base piece
4. Attach soundboard (wooden resonating chamber) to one side between base and yoke
5. Drill small holes in yoke and base for string attachment points (7-10 pairs of holes)
6. String assembly: attach string to bottom hole, run up one side of frame, over fixed bridge (small ridge carved into yoke), and back down other side to complete loop
7. Adjust string length and tension to reach desired pitch
8. Test each string: pluck and listen to pitch

### Monochord (Single-String Teaching Instrument)

**Simplest string instrument, used historically for musical teaching:**

1. Create simple rectangular frame (12 inches long, 4 inches wide)
2. Attach single string under tension across frame length
3. String pitched by tightening or loosening tension screw
4. Movable bridge placed under string allows demonstration of pitch relationships
5. Player plucks string or bows with horsehair bow
6. Useful for teaching pitch, intervals, and string vibration

### Bow Construction (For String Instruments)

**For instruments played with bow rather than plucking:**

1. Create bow: curved wood (12-18 inches long) bent under tension
2. At each end, create attachment point for horsehair (or plant fiber strands)
3. Horsehair bundle should be 1/2 to 1 inch diameter, loosely attached so tension can be adjusted
4. Tension mechanism: wooden toggle or screw allows player to increase/decrease bow hair tension
5. Tighter tension produces louder, more aggressive sound; loose tension softer, subtle sound
6. Rosining: coat horsehair with powdered pine resin (collected and ground from pine trees) to increase grip on strings

### Guitar/Lute Construction Basics

**For a more complex plucked instrument:**

1. **Soundboard:** Thin wood (~1/8 inch) spruce or similar, 12 x 8 inches minimum
2. **Back and sides:** Darker wood (oak, maple) for structural support and resonance
3. **Neck:** Hardwood 24+ inches long, straight grain, 1.5 inches wide
4. **Headstock:** Extended neck with tuning pegs drilled into it
5. **Fretboard (optional):** Glued to neck; thin wooden strips mark string positions for standardized pitches
6. **Bridge:** Small wooden block on soundboard; strings run over it to anchor point
7. **Bracing inside:** Wooden supports inside soundboard (X-bracing or ladder pattern) transfer string vibration efficiently
8. **Tuning pegs:** Wooden pegs or carved screws that tighten/loosen strings

**Assembly:** Glue neck to body; attach soundboard; install frets; string and tune. Project takes 20-40 hours if materials are prepared.

## Wind Instruments

Wind instruments are accessible for beginners and can be constructed from readily available materials. Sound quality depends on internal dimensions and hole placement.

<!-- SVG: instrument-construction-1.svg: Wind instrument bore diameter and hole spacing relationships -->

### Simple Flute (Vertical Flute)

**Basic vertical flute from hollow reed or wood dowel:**

**Materials:**

- Hollow tube (bamboo, reed, or carefully bored wood dowel), 8-12 inches long, 1/2-3/4 inch diameter
- Cutting tool (knife or file)
- Measuring tool

**Construction:**

1. Select tube with one natural end closed (reed nodes or carved cap)
2. Carve notch in open end for embouchure (mouth piece): V-shaped cut or beveled edge
3. Hold tube vertically in mouth with embouchure against upper lip/teeth
4. Blow gently across notch to produce sound (takes practice to find exact angle)
5. Drill holes in tube body: start with 4-6 holes spaced along length
6. Hole placement critical: spacing determines available pitches
7. Hole diameter typically 1/4 inch for standard flute

**Basic hole spacing (for C major scale, 8 holes total):** Measure from mouthpiece end. Holes at approximately: 2", 3", 3.5", 4.5", 5", 6", 6.75", 7.5" (spacing varies with exact tube diameter and desired tuning).

### Bore Diameter Tolerances

The bore (internal diameter) of a wind instrument is the most critical dimension affecting pitch and tone quality. This table shows typical bore specifications and acceptable tolerances for common wind instruments.

| Instrument | Bore Diameter | Tolerance Range | Notes |
|-----------|--------------|-----------------|-------|
| Flute (vertical) | 16–20 mm (0.63–0.79") | ±0.5 mm | Narrow bore produces brighter, higher pitch; wider bore is mellow, lower pitch |
| Fife or small flute | 12–14 mm (0.47–0.55") | ±0.3 mm | Very small; sensitive to bore variation; 0.2 mm change = noticeable pitch shift |
| Recorder-style flute | 14–18 mm (0.55–0.71") | ±0.5 mm | Bore taper (narrower at top) improves intonation |
| Panpipe tube | 8–15 mm (0.31–0.59") | ±0.5 mm | Length more critical than bore for tuning; bore affects tone color |
| Whistle | 10–15 mm (0.39–0.59") | ±0.8 mm | Wider tolerance; tone varies but still playable |
| Reed pipe (clarinet-style) | 12–18 mm (0.47–0.71") | ±1 mm | Bore taper critical; should narrow by 0.1 mm per cm toward reed end |
| Horn or bugle | 8–25 mm taper | ±1.5 mm at widest | Conical bore; gradual taper matters more than diameter |

**How bore variation affects pitch and tone:**

- **Bore too narrow (−0.5 mm):** Pitch rises 30–50 cents (about 1/3 semitone); sound becomes brighter and more strident
- **Bore too wide (+0.5 mm):** Pitch drops 30–50 cents; sound becomes mellower but less focused
- **Bore uneven (varying diameter within the tube):** Uneven intonation; some notes sharp, others flat; sound is "fuzzy"
- **Bore with ridges or roughness:** Increased friction slows air; pitch drops slightly; sound loses clarity

**Measurement methods using improvised tools:**

1. **Feeler gauge method:** Cut thin strips of wood or metal in graduated thicknesses (0.5 mm, 1 mm, 1.5 mm, 2 mm). Try to insert through bore. The last one that passes easily indicates bore diameter within 0.5 mm.

2. **String wrap method:** Wrap a thin string around the circumference at the bore's narrowest point. Measure circumference, then calculate diameter: D = circumference / π. Accuracy ±1 mm.

3. **Water fill method:** Fill the instrument with water at a known diameter section; measure the volume. For a cylindrical bore: volume = π × (D/2)² × length. Rearrange to solve for D.

4. **Calipers (crude):** Fashion calipers from two thin pieces of wood or bone, joined by a pivot. Use to measure internal diameter directly.

**Maintaining bore consistency during construction:**

- **Hand-drilling:** Drill in short bursts (1–2 cm at a time); withdraw frequently to clear chips
- **Ream smoothing:** After drilling, use a wooden dowel wrapped with fine sandpaper (80–120 grit) twisted through the bore to smooth ridges
- **Sand taper:** For conical bores (horns), use a tapered dowel wrapped with sandpaper
- **Final polish:** Use very fine sandpaper (220 grit) for a smooth, quiet surface

:::tip
**Testing bore quality:** Blow air through the unplugged instrument. You should hear a consistent "whistle" without crackles or buzzes. Crackles indicate roughness or blockages.
:::

---

### Wind Instrument Bore Calculation

**Understanding how bore diameter affects pitch:**

The acoustic properties of a wind instrument depend on the internal bore diameter and length. A wider bore drops pitch; a narrower bore raises pitch for the same length.

**Quick formula:** For cylindrical bore, pitch is inversely related to effective length:
- Length 20 cm, 1/2" bore: approximately C (note)
- Length 40 cm, 1/2" bore: approximately C one octave lower
- Same length, 1/4" bore: approximately one octave higher

**Practical approach:** Drill your first flute with spacing based on measurement. Test by playing. If pitch is too low, make new holes slightly closer to the mouthpiece (shortens effective sounding length). If pitch is too high, space holes farther back.

**Multiple instruments for ensemble:** If building instruments for community use, make one flute precisely, then build others with identical spacing. This ensures they play in harmony together.

### Tuning and Pitch Control

**Hole position determines pitch:** Holes closer to mouthpiece = higher pitch when uncovered; holes farther away = lower pitch. Moving a hole slightly forward raises pitch, moving backward lowers pitch.

**Fine tuning:** Slightly plugging hole with cork or wax can adjust pitch fine. Covering multiple holes produces intermediate pitches.

### Whistle or Pipe

**Simpler than flute, good first wind instrument:**

1. Use closed tube with sealed mouthpiece end
2. Carve small channel from mouthpiece to interior of tube (air whistle mechanism)
3. Drill 2-3 finger holes for pitch variation
4. Shorter tube = higher pitch
5. Sound character determined by whistle design and hole sizes

### Reed Pipe

**Wind instrument with reed (vibrating element):**

1. Create double-reed: cut flat reed materials (thin wood or processed cane)
2. Two reeds bound together near end, they vibrate against each other creating buzzing sound
3. Reed vibration pitch controlled by reed thickness and length
4. Attach reeds to end of tube or pipe
5. Finger holes allow pitch modification
6. Reed instruments produce brash, penetrating sound ideal for outdoor use

**Reed thickness guide:**
- Thin reed (0.5-1mm): High pitch, bright sound, less air resistance
- Medium reed (1-1.5mm): Mid-range pitch, standard tone
- Thick reed (1.5-2mm): Low pitch, dark sound, requires strong air pressure

### Panpipe (Simple Polyphonic Instrument)

<!-- SVG: instrument-construction-2.svg: Panpipe tube diameter and length relationships for chromatic scale -->

**Multiple tubes lashed together, each plays one note:**

1. Cut 8-12 tubes from reed or bamboo, each progressively shorter (7", 6.75", 6.5", etc. down to 4")
2. Bundle tubes side by side with open ends aligned at bottom
3. Seal top end of each tube (natural node or carved cap)
4. Lash tubes together with cord to create fixed bundle
5. Player blows across open ends (each tube sounds one note)
6. By blowing across different tubes, plays melody
7. No hole positioning needed; each tube is separate pitch source

**Panpipe tuning:** The tube length determines pitch directly. Use your reference pitch (established whistle from tuning section below) to tune by length:
- Longest tube (fundamental frequency): Reference pitch
- Each shorter tube: Proportionally shorter (2:1 ratio = octave higher; 3:2 ratio = perfect fifth)
- Arrangement: Longest to shortest left to right is most intuitive

**Advanced design (notched rim):** Cut a V-notch in the top rim of each tube where the player blows. This directs air more efficiently and requires less air pressure than flat-topped tubes.

## Percussion from Found Materials

Percussion instruments require minimal construction and maximum creativity with materials.

<!-- SVG: instrument-construction-3.svg: Percussion membrane thickness and tone relationships -->

### Percussion Membrane Selection and Tension

For drums, the membrane (head) is critical. Different materials and thicknesses produce distinct tones.

**Membrane material properties:**

| Material | Thickness | Tone Character | Durability | Difficulty |
|----------|-----------|-----------------|------------|-----------|
| Goat hide | 1-2mm | Warm, mellow | 1-2 years | Moderate |
| Sheep skin | 0.5-1.5mm | Bright, responsive | 6-12 months | Moderate |
| Fish skin | 0.5mm | Very bright, sharp | 3-6 months | Difficult |
| Rawhide (processed) | 1-2mm | Balanced tone | 2-3 years | Easy |
| Deer hide | 2-3mm | Deep, resonant | 2-3 years | Moderate |

**Tension impact:**
- Loose membrane: Low pitch, sustains sound longer, softer tone
- Medium tension: Balanced pitch and tone (ideal for most purposes)
- High tension: High pitch, snappy attack, shorter sustain

### Shakers and Rattles

**Containers filled with sound-producing material:**

<table><thead><tr>
<th>Construction</th>
<th>Sound Character</th>
<th>Materials Needed</th>
</tr></thead><tbody><tr>
<td>Dried gourds or seed pods filled with pebbles</td>
<td>Sharp, bright rattle</td>
<td>Gourd, small pebbles, sinew to seal</td>
</tr><tr>
<td>Coconut shells with beans/seeds inside</td>
<td>Medium-bright rattle</td>
<td>Coconut shell, dried beans, binding cord</td>
</tr><tr>
<td>Animal hide sewn into pouch with pebbles</td>
<td>Muted, warm rattle</td>
<td>Animal hide, leather needle/sinew, pebbles</td>

<td>Wooden cylinder with holes filled with lead shot or pellets</td>
<td>Complex, multiple tones</td>
<td>Wood tube, lead/pellets, wooden caps</td>

**Advanced rattle design (multi-chamber):**

Create a shaker with multiple internal chambers separated by wooden dividers. Each chamber filled with different materials (pebbles, beans, sand) creates a layered, complex sound. This is closer to professional maracas and produces more interesting rhythmic texture than single-material shakers.

### Clappers and Strikers

**Instruments creating sharp percussion sounds:**

- Wooden clappers: two carved wooden blocks struck together (carved bowl shapes fit in hand)
- Bone clappers: similar to wood, made from bone or antler (sharper tone than wood)
- Stone strikers: smooth rocks struck together (sharp, penetrating sound)
- Shell clappers: large shells (scallop, clam) struck together (bright, resonant sound)

### Gongs and Tam-tams

**Suspended metal or wood resonators struck with mallets:**

1. Metal gong: if metal available, suspend from cord and strike with soft mallet (complex, rich tone)
2. Wooden gong alternative: carve from solid wood, 12-18 inches diameter, 1-2 inches thick. Suspend and strike with mallet. Tone less bright than metal but effective
3. Mallet selection affects tone: soft mallets produce warm tone, harder strikers produce sharp attacks

### Triangle and Mallets

**From scrap metal:**

1. Bend metal rod into triangle shape (or U-shape simpler)
2. Suspend from cord or hold by string through corner hole
3. Strike with metal or wooden striker/mallet
4. Produces clear, penetrating tone carrying long distances

**Mallet selection impact:**

| Mallet Head | Tone | Best For |
|-----------|------|----------|
| Soft yarn/felt wrapped | Warm, mellow | Gentle, musical tone |
| Hard wood | Bright, sharp | Cutting through group sound |
| Metal | Harsh, metallic | Dramatic effects, special purposes |
| Plastic | Medium-bright | General use, affordable |

### Idiophone (Self-Resonating) Instruments

Beyond triangles, create pitched percussion from natural materials:

**Stone chimes:** Collect flat stones of varying sizes. Suspend from cord. Each stone rings at different pitch when struck with mallet. Create a tuned set by experimenting with stone size and thickness.

**Wood blocks:** Carve blocks from hardwood with different internal chamber sizes. Seal one end, leave one end open. Different sizes produce different pitches when struck.

**Hanging bells:** Cast from bronze or salvage from old bells. Suspend and strike. Complex resonance produces multiple pitches simultaneously.

**Bamboo tubes:** Cut bamboo segments of different lengths. Suspend in frame. Strike with mallet. Length determines pitch (shorter = higher).

## Tuning and Pitch Control

Understanding pitch relationships allows better instrument construction and ensemble playing.

### Pitch Fundamentals

**String instruments:** Pitch determined by string length, thickness, and tension. Formula: pitch = tension / (length × mass per unit length). In practical terms:

- Shorter strings = higher pitch
- Thinner strings = higher pitch
- Higher tension = higher pitch

**Wind instruments:** Pitch determined by tube length (shorter = higher) and resonance mode (open vs. closed ends).

**Percussion:** Pitch determined by object mass and stiffness. Larger or heavier = lower pitch; smaller or lighter = higher pitch.

### Musical Scale Systems

**Simple whole-number ratios produce pleasing intervals:**

<th>Interval</th>
<th>Frequency Ratio</th>
<th>Description</th>
</tr></thead><tbody><tr>
<td>Octave</td>
<td>2:1</td>
<td>Double frequency (very important)</td>
</tr><tr>
<td>Perfect fifth</td>
<td>3:2</td>
<td>Most consonant melodic interval</td>
</tr><tr>
<td>Perfect fourth</td>
<td>4:3</td>
<td>Consonant, slightly less than fifth</td>
</tr><tr>
<td>Major third</td>
<td>5:4</td>
<td>Consonant, smaller interval</td>
</tr><tr>
<td>Major second</td>
<td>9:8</td>
<td>Smallest step in major scale</td>
</tr></tbody></table>

**Practical application:** If fundamental note has frequency F, octave above = 2F, perfect fifth above = 1.5F, perfect fourth above = 1.33F. Musicians use these ratios to tune instruments and compose melodies.

### Tuning by Ear

**Without instruments, tune using human hearing:**

1. Two strings should sound same note: play together, listen for beats (wobbling sound). Adjust tension until beats disappear
2. Octave interval: second note should sound like higher version of first note (test by comparing)
3. Fifth interval: distinctive relationship where second pitch feels naturally higher, musical
4. Practice develops ear: after 2-4 weeks, most people can identify major intervals by ear

### Creating Reference Pitch

**For consistent tuning across community:**

1. Establish standard pitch: pick any note and define as "standard A" (traditional Western standard)
2. Create tuning instrument: carefully construct whistle or flute that always sounds this standard pitch
3. All musicians tune to this reference
4. Establishes consistency allowing ensemble playing

## Music's Role in Community Morale

Beyond entertainment, music serves critical psychological, organizational, and cultural functions in survival communities.

### Psychological Benefits

**Music's effects on human psychology:**

- Rhythmic activity synchronizes nervous systems (playing together creates bonding)
- Melody stimulates memory and emotional engagement (songs become group identity)
- Singing releases endorphins (natural mood elevation)
- Familiar music reduces stress and improves sleep quality
- Group musical activity improves social cohesion (singing together builds unity)

:::info-box
**Science-Backed Benefit:** Regular musical activity in communities shows measurable improvements in mental health, reduced depression rates, and improved social cooperation. In survival scenarios, maintaining musical traditions is as important as food production.
:::

### Organizational Function

**Using music to coordinate activities:**

- Work songs: rhythmic music coordinating group labor (harvest songs, rowing songs, construction rhythms)
- Signal songs: specific melodies indicate activities (wake-up songs, meal songs, end-of-day songs)
- Ceremonial music: marks important events (births, deaths, seasonal transitions)
- Celebration music: marks successful harvests, completed projects, community milestones

### Cultural Preservation

**Music as cultural survival mechanism:**

- Oral tradition: songs preserve history, genealogy, and values when writing unavailable
- Identity maintenance: music connects communities to heritage and ancestors
- Teaching tool: children learn moral lessons through songs
- Spiritual connection: music facilitates ceremonial and religious practices

### Community Music Practices

**Integrate music into settlement routines:**

- Weekly music gathering: regular time for musical practice and learning
- Instrument training: teach children instrument construction and playing
- Song preservation: teach traditional songs to new generation
- New composition: create contemporary songs about community experiences
- Celebration occasions: scheduled musical performances marking seasons and achievements
