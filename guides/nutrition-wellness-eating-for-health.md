When professional nutrition counseling is available, consult it.

<section id="overview">

## Overview: Why Nutrition Matters in Survival Settings

Adequate nutrition maintains immune function, wound healing, mental clarity, and physical strength—all critical for survival. Malnutrition causes weakness, infection vulnerability, slow wound healing, and decreased capacity to work.

In survival settings, food is usually scarce. Maximizing nutritional value of available food prevents deficiency diseases (scurvy, pellagra, beriberi, rickets) that cause serious illness and death without modern treatment.

**Key principle:** Balanced nutrition prevents disease; deficiency diseases are deadly and common in resource-limited settings where fresh fruits, vegetables, and diverse foods are unavailable.

<section id="macronutrients">

## Macronutrients: Carbohydrates, Protein, Fat

These three nutrient categories provide energy and building blocks for body structures.

### Carbohydrates (Carbs)

**Function:** Primary energy source

**Sources in austere settings:**
- Grains (wheat, rice, corn)
- Legumes (beans, lentils, peas) — also protein source
- Tubers (potatoes, yams, cassava)
- Honey (if available)

**Importance:**
- Brain and muscles prefer glucose (from carbs)
- Provide quick energy
- Support fiber intake (if whole grains/legumes)

**How much:** 45–65% of daily calories should be carbohydrates

**Note:** Refined carbs (white rice, white flour) are stripped of nutrients and fiber. Whole grains provide more nutrition.

### Protein

**Function:** Building and repair of muscles, organs, enzymes, antibodies, hormones

**Sources:**
- **Animal:** Meat, fish, eggs, dairy (milk, cheese) — complete protein (all essential amino acids)
- **Plant:** Beans, lentils, nuts, seeds, whole grains — incomplete individually but complementary (rice + beans = complete)
- **Insect:** Crickets, grasshoppers, termites — surprisingly complete protein and nutrient-rich; cultural acceptance varies

**Importance:**
- Required for muscle maintenance and recovery
- Needed for immune function and wound healing
- Supports pregnancy/lactation, child growth

**How much:**
- **Adult at rest:** 0.8 g per kg body weight daily (55 kg woman = 44 g protein)
- **Adult with moderate activity:** 1.2–1.4 g per kg
- **Heavy labor/recovery from illness:** 1.6–2.0 g per kg

**Critical in survival:** Injuries and illness increase protein needs. Avoid protein deficiency when recovering.

### Fat

**Function:** Energy storage, hormone production, vitamin absorption, inflammation control, insulation

**Sources in austere settings:**
- Oils/animal fats (cooking fat from meat)
- Nuts and seeds
- Fish (especially fatty fish like salmon—omega-3 fats)
- Eggs
- Grains and legumes (small amounts)

**Importance:**
- Most energy-dense nutrient (9 calories per gram vs. 4 for carbs/protein)
- Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) require dietary fat for absorption
- Essential fatty acids (omega-3, omega-6) required for brain, heart, inflammation control

**How much:** 20–35% of daily calories as fat

**Note:** Rendered animal fat stores well and preserves (important in austere settings). Fish oils are excellent but oxidize quickly.

## Micronutrients: Vitamins & Minerals

Essential for countless body functions; deficiency causes serious disease.

### Key Vitamins

| Vitamin | Function | Deficiency Disease | Sources |
|---------|----------|------------------|---------|
| **Vitamin A** | Vision, immune, skin | Blindness, skin problems | Orange/yellow vegetables, liver, eggs, leafy greens |
| **Vitamin B1 (Thiamine)** | Energy metabolism, nerves | Beriberi (nerve damage, weakness) | Whole grains, legumes, pork, nuts |
| **Vitamin B2 (Riboflavin)** | Energy metabolism | Cheilosis (cracked lips), skin issues | Eggs, dairy, almonds, mushrooms |
| **Vitamin B3 (Niacin)** | Energy metabolism, DNA | Pellagra (dermatitis, diarrhea, dementia) | Meat, fish, peanuts, mushrooms, whole grains |
| **Vitamin B6** | Protein metabolism, immune | Anemia, nerve damage | Chickpeas, bananas, salmon, potatoes |
| **Vitamin B12** | Nerve, blood cells | Anemia, nerve damage | Animal products only (meat, fish, eggs, dairy) |
| **Vitamin C** | Immune, collagen, antioxidant | Scurvy (bleeding, weakness, infection susceptibility) | Fresh fruits/vegetables, potatoes, sauerkraut (fermented) |
| **Vitamin D** | Bone health, immune, calcium absorption | Rickets (bone deformity), osteomalacia | Sunlight, fatty fish, egg yolks, mushrooms |
| **Vitamin E** | Antioxidant, cell protection | Nerve damage (rare) | Nuts, seeds, oils, leafy greens |
| **Vitamin K** | Blood clotting, bone | Bleeding tendency, poor bone health | Leafy greens, fermented foods |

### Key Minerals

| Mineral | Function | Deficiency Signs | Sources |
|---------|----------|-----------------|---------|
| **Iron** | Oxygen transport in blood | Anemia (weakness, fatigue, shortness of breath) | Meat, beans, spinach, fortified grains |
| **Calcium** | Bone strength, muscle, nerve | Osteoporosis, muscle cramps | Dairy, leafy greens, legumes, nuts |
| **Zinc** | Immune, wound healing, growth | Infection susceptibility, slow wound healing | Meat, nuts, seeds, legumes |
| **Iodine** | Thyroid function, metabolism | Goiter (neck swelling), developmental problems | Salt (iodized), seafood, seaweed |
| **Selenium** | Antioxidant, thyroid | Fatigue, possible immune issues | Meat, fish, nuts, grains |
| **Copper** | Energy, iron metabolism | Anemia, immune issues | Nuts, seeds, legumes |
| **Magnesium** | Muscle, nerve, bone | Muscle cramps, weakness | Leafy greens, nuts, seeds, whole grains |
| **Phosphorus** | Bone, energy metabolism | Weak bones, muscle weakness | Meat, fish, dairy, nuts |

<section id="caloric-needs">

## Caloric Needs: How Much to Eat

Caloric needs vary by activity level, age, sex, and body composition.

### Daily Caloric Needs by Activity Level

**Sedentary (light activity):**
- Adult female: 1,800–2,000 calories
- Adult male: 2,400–2,600 calories

**Moderate activity (exercise 3–5 days/week):**
- Adult female: 2,000–2,200 calories
- Adult male: 2,600–2,800 calories

**Heavy activity (daily labor, survival work):**
- Adult female: 2,200–2,600 calories
- Adult male: 2,800–3,400+ calories

**Pregnant/lactating:**
- Add 300–500 calories to base needs
- Increase protein 25 g more per day

**Children/adolescents:**
- Higher caloric density (need per kg body weight greater than adults)
- 6–10 years: 1,400–2,000 calories
- 11–13 years: 1,600–2,200 calories
- 14–18 years: 2,000–3,200 calories

**Elderly:**
- May decrease slightly (but nutrient needs stay high)
- Often 1,600–2,000 calories depending on activity

### Survival Caloric Scenarios

In severe resource scarcity:
- **Minimal survival:** 1,200–1,500 calories allows short-term survival but causes gradual weight loss and weakness
- **Moderate rationing:** 1,500–2,000 calories sustainable temporarily for sedentary population
- **Heavy work:** Requires 2,500–3,500+ calories; inadequate intake causes rapid deterioration

:::warning
**Severe caloric restriction** (<1,200 calories daily) causes rapid muscle loss, weakness, immune failure, and increased infection/death risk. Prioritize adequate calories in survival settings.
:::

<section id="food-groups">

## Food Groups: Balanced Nutrition in Austere Settings

With limited food diversity, emphasize available sources of each group:

### Grains & Starches (Primary Energy)

- **Best:** Whole grains (brown rice, whole wheat, oats, barley) retain nutrients
- **Available:** White rice, white flour, pasta (stripped nutrients but still calories + energy)
- **Portion:** 6 ounces daily minimum (half as whole grains if possible)

<div class="food-group">

**Surviving on grains alone is dangerous.** Beriberi (B1 deficiency) and pellagra (B3 deficiency) develop when diet is primarily refined white rice or white flour without other foods. Add legumes and any available proteins and vegetables.

</div>

### Proteins (Muscle & Repair)

- **Meat/fish:** Primary complete protein; prioritize in limited supply
- **Eggs:** Complete protein; if chickens available, prioritize eggs for sustainability
- **Legumes:** Beans, lentils, peas (combine with grains for complete protein)
- **Nuts/seeds:** Complete proteins; store well if protected from pests
- **Insects:** Crickets, grasshoppers, termites (complete protein, micronutrient-rich)

<div class="food-group">

**Minimum:** 2–3 servings daily (1 serving = 25–30 g protein = 3 oz meat, 1 egg, 1/2 cup beans, handful of nuts)

</div>

### Vegetables (Vitamins & Minerals)

- **Leafy greens:** Highest nutrient density (A, C, K, iron, calcium)
- **Orange/yellow:** Vitamin A, carotenoids (carrots, squash, sweet potato)
- **Cruciferous:** Broccoli, cabbage, kale (vitamin C, calcium, cancer-protective compounds)
- **Root vegetables:** Potatoes, turnips (store well, significant nutrients if skin eaten)
- **Fermented:** Sauerkraut, kimchi (probiotics, vitamin C, preserved vegetables)

<div class="food-group">

**Minimum:** 2–3 cups daily; if scarcity, prioritize leafy greens and orange vegetables for nutrient density

**Scarcity strategy:** Preserve through fermentation, drying, or canning; eat vegetable skins (most nutrients); save cooking water (nutrients leach into liquid)

</div>

### Fruits (Vitamins, Especially C)

- **Fresh:** Any available fruits (vitamin C, sugar energy)
- **Dried:** Raisins, dried berries (calorie-dense, stores well, but vitamin C reduced)
- **Preserved:** Canned, fermented (retains some nutrients)

<div class="food-group">

**Minimum:** 1–2 servings daily; if unavailable, supplementary vitamin C from other sources (see Deficiency Prevention)

</div>

### Dairy (Calcium, Protein, D)

- **Milk:** If available; pasteurization requires heat
- **Cheese:** Stores well; complete protein and calcium
- **Yogurt/fermented:** Probiotics; stores better than milk in warm climates

<div class="food-group">

**If unavailable:** Use non-dairy calcium sources (leafy greens, legumes, nuts)

</div>

### Fats & Oils (Energy, Vitamin Absorption)

- **Rendered animal fat:** Preserves well
- **Plant oils:** Nut oils, seed oils; oxidize relatively quickly
- **Fish oil:** Omega-3 fats; store in cool place

<div class="food-group">

**Minimum:** 1–2 tablespoons daily for vitamin absorption and hormone function

</div>

## Deficiency Diseases: Recognition & Prevention

Without food diversity, specific deficiency diseases emerge.

### Scurvy (Vitamin C Deficiency)

**Timeline:** 6–12 weeks of zero vitamin C

**Symptoms:**
- Bleeding gums, loose teeth
- Joint/muscle pain
- Slow wound healing
- Weakness
- Skin hemorrhages (bleeding under skin)
- Emotional changes (irritability)

**Prevention:**
- Fresh fruits/vegetables daily (even small amounts)
- Fermented vegetables (sauerkraut; fermentation preserves C)
- Potatoes with skin (significant vitamin C)
- Citrus if available
- Rose hips, pine needle tea (high vitamin C)

**Treatment:** Vitamin C-rich food; improvement in days, full recovery in weeks

### Pellagra (Vitamin B3/Niacin Deficiency)

**Timeline:** Months on diet of refined grain without other proteins

**Symptoms (4 D's):**
- **Dermatitis:** Skin changes (rash on sun-exposed areas)
- **Diarrhea:** GI upset
- **Dementia:** Confusion, personality changes, psychosis
- **Death:** If untreated, progressive to death

**Prevention:**
- Eat varied proteins (meat, fish, legumes, nuts)
- Whole grains (niacin-rich) better than refined
- Meat is niacin-rich

**Treatment:** Niacin supplementation or niacin-rich foods; symptoms improve in days with treatment

### Beriberi (Vitamin B1/Thiamine Deficiency)

**Timeline:** Months on white rice diet without other foods

**Symptoms:**
- Nerve damage: Numbness/tingling in extremities, weakness
- Wet beriberi: Heart failure, fluid retention
- Dry beriberi: Paralysis, muscle wasting

**Prevention:**
- Whole grains (thiamine-rich) vs. white rice
- Legumes, nuts (thiamine sources)
- Varied diet

**Treatment:** Thiamine supplementation or whole grain intake; nerve damage partially reversible if caught early

### Rickets (Vitamin D & Calcium Deficiency)

**Risk:** Children; also affects bone health in adults

**Symptoms in children:**
- Soft, misshapen bones
- Bowed legs
- Delayed growth
- Dental problems

**Prevention:**
- Sun exposure (UVB production of vitamin D; 15–30 minutes daily)
- Vitamin D foods: Fatty fish, egg yolks, mushrooms exposed to sun
- Calcium-rich foods: Dairy, leafy greens, legumes, nuts

**Treatment:** Vitamin D supplementation, sun exposure, calcium-rich diet; bone deformity may be permanent if severe

### Iron Deficiency Anemia

**Timeline:** Months of inadequate iron intake

**Symptoms:**
- Fatigue, weakness
- Shortness of breath
- Headache
- Pale skin/mucous membranes
- Difficulty concentrating

**Prevention:**
- Meat (most bioavailable iron)
- Legumes (lower bioavailability; improve with vitamin C)
- Leafy greens (absorb better with vitamin C)
- Cook in iron cookware (leeches iron into food)

**Treatment:** Iron-rich food, vitamin C (enhances absorption); improvement in weeks

</section>

## Basic Meal Planning for Austere Settings

With limited resources, plan to maximize nutrition:

### Simple Balanced-Meal Pattern

For routine general nutrition when no urgent red flags are present, build most meals from available foods rather than aiming for a perfect menu:

- A **starch or grain** for energy, such as rice, oats, corn, bread, pasta, potatoes, or other tubers
- A **protein food**, such as beans, lentils, peas, eggs, fish, meat, dairy, nuts, seeds, or a grain-legume combination
- A **fruit or vegetable if available**, especially leafy greens, orange vegetables, potatoes with skin, fermented vegetables, or any fresh produce that supplies vitamins and minerals
- A **small amount of fat** when available, such as cooking oil, rendered animal fat, nuts, seeds, dairy, eggs, or fatty fish
- **Water with meals and between meals**, with extra attention during heat, labor, fever, vomiting, or diarrhea

This pattern supports energy, repair, vitamin absorption, fiber, and micronutrients without requiring calorie counting. If one part is missing at a meal, try to include it later in the day. If food is scarce enough that portions must be allocated across people or days, use the rationing guide instead of this general meal-pattern guide.

:::warning
Do not treat routine meal planning as enough when there are danger signs: severe dehydration, confusion, fainting, persistent vomiting or diarrhea, swelling of the feet or belly with wasting, rapid or unexplained weight loss, bleeding gums, loose teeth, severe weakness, shortness of breath, numbness or tingling, poor wound healing, pregnancy/lactation concerns, child growth problems, chronic disease, refusal to eat or drink, or eating-disorder risk. Those need professional medical or nutrition assessment when available.
:::

### Sample Daily Meal Structure (Moderate Activity)

**Breakfast (20% calories, ~500 kcal):**
- Grain (rice, oats, bread): 1–2 cups
- Protein (egg, legume, meat if available): 1 serving
- Vegetable/fruit if available

**Mid-meal (if needed):**
- Nuts/seeds or starchy food for energy

**Lunch (30% calories, ~750 kcal):**
- Grain (rice, bread): 2 cups
- Legume-based dish (beans, lentils) or meat: 1–2 servings
- Vegetable: 1–2 cups

**Dinner (30% calories, ~750 kcal):**
- Grain (rice, potato): 1–2 cups
- Protein (meat, fish, legume): 1–2 servings
- Vegetable (cooked): 1–2 cups
- Fat (oil, fat): 1 tablespoon

**Snack/supplement (20% calories, ~500 kcal):**
- Nuts, seeds, dried fruit, extra starch

### Protein Complementing (Complete Protein without Meat)

When meat is unavailable, combine plant proteins:

- **Rice + beans:** Essential amino acids balanced (traditional in many cultures)
- **Corn + beans:** Complete protein
- **Legume + grain + vegetable:** Example: lentil soup with whole grain bread
- **Nuts/seeds + grain:** Example: peanut butter on whole wheat bread

<div class="nutrient-box">

**Key:** Eat complementary proteins in same meal or within day for amino acid balance.

</div>

<section id="special-needs">

## Nutrition for Specific Populations

### Pregnancy & Lactation

**Additional calories:** +300 during pregnancy, +500 during lactation

**Key nutrient increases:**
- **Protein:** Additional 25 g daily
- **Iron:** Doubled (pregnancy increases blood volume)
- **Calcium:** Maintain intake (babies draw from mother's bones if intake inadequate)
- **Folic acid:** Prevent neural tube defects (leafy greens, legumes, fortified grains)

**Food safety:** Avoid unsafe foods (unpasteurized dairy, raw meat, unwashed vegetables in contaminated water)

### Infants & Young Children

- **Breast milk:** Ideal first 6 months (complete nutrition)
- **Intro solids 6 months:** Nutrient-dense foods (iron-fortified grains, mashed vegetables, protein sources)
- **Protein:** Higher per kg than adults (critical for growth)
- **Iron:** Risk of deficiency when breastfeeding stops
- **Calcium/D:** Critical for bone development

### Elderly

- **Caloric needs:** May decrease slightly
- **Protein:** Increase slightly (maintain muscle mass against age-related loss)
- **Calcium/D:** Prevent osteoporosis
- **Chewing:** Softer foods if tooth loss/poor dentition
- **Hydration:** Often forgotten; increased risk of dehydration

### During Illness/Recovery

- **Calories:** Increase 10–20%
- **Protein:** Increase 25–50% (wounds and immune recovery need protein)
- **Fluids:** Maintain hydration
- **Soft, nutrient-dense foods:** Easier to digest, maximum nutrition

</section>

<section id="water-and-hydration">

## Water & Hydration

Water is essential; dehydration impairs all body functions.

### Hydration Status Assessment

- **Adequate:** Pale yellow urine, normal thirst
- **Mild dehydration:** Dark urine, increased thirst, mild weakness
- **Moderate dehydration:** Very dark urine, no urination for hours, weakness, dizziness
- **Severe dehydration:** Little/no urine, dizziness even lying down, mental confusion

### Daily Water Needs

- **Base:** 2–3 liters daily (roughly 1 liter per meal)
- **Hot climate/labor:** 3–5+ liters daily
- **Illness:** Increase (fever, diarrhea, vomiting increase losses)

### Best Hydration Practices

- Drink water with meals and between
- Use food with high water content (vegetables, fruits) to contribute hydration
- In hot climates: Salty foods help retain water
- Avoid excess caffeine/alcohol (diuretic, increase losses)

</section>

<section id="food-preservation-nutrition">

## Preserving Nutritional Value During Storage & Cooking

Food preparation methods affect nutrient content:

### Storage Impact

- **Fresh vegetables:** Lose vitamin C progressively; eat soon after harvest
- **Frozen:** Retain more nutrients than fresh if stored >few days
- **Canned:** Nutrients retained reasonably but brine/syrup adds salt/sugar
- **Dried:** Vitamin C lost but other nutrients concentrated
- **Fermented:** Vitamin C preserved, probiotics added, shelf-stable

### Cooking Impact

- **Boiling vegetables:** Leaches water-soluble vitamins into water; use water for soup/broth to retain nutrients
- **Steaming/stir-frying:** Minimal nutrient loss
- **Roasting:** Minimal loss but can reduce some B vitamins if very high heat
- **Overcooking:** Increases nutrient loss
- **Eating raw when possible:** Preserves most vitamins (when food safety allows)

### Maximizing Nutrition from Limited Food

- **Eat whole vegetables:** Skin has fiber and nutrients; peel only if necessary
- **Use cooking water:** Soups and stews retain leached nutrients
- **Eat organ meats:** Liver extremely nutrient-dense (especially vitamin A, iron)
- **Don't waste fish:** Bones edible (calcium); organs nutrient-rich
- **Ferment vegetables:** Preserves and enhances bioavailability

</section>

<section id="when-to-seek-help">

## When to Seek Professional Care

Consult health professionals for:

- **Unexplained weight loss** (>10% in months)
- **Suspected deficiency disease symptoms** (see sections above)
- **Severe anemia signs** (weakness, shortness of breath)
- **Growth problems in children** (delayed growth, developmental delays)
- **Pregnancy/lactation:** Nutrition counseling important
- **Chronic disease** (diabetes, kidney disease, etc.): Special dietary needs

Professional nutrition assessment is superior to self-management when available.

</section>
