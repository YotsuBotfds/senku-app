## Why Subject-Matter Experts Need Teaching Training

The most knowledgeable person is not automatically an effective teacher. A master blacksmith with 30 years of experience might be terrible at explaining concepts to beginners. An expert herbalist might lack patience with questions. Knowledge and teaching ability are separate skills.

Use this guide when the core problem is the instructor's skill at teaching:

- "I know the subject, but learners are not getting it"
- "I need to train a new teacher or coach an expert who teaches poorly"
- "How do I explain, demonstrate, and check understanding better?"
- "How do I handle mixed skill levels, questions, pacing, and feedback?"

If you already know how to teach and just need help running the lesson itself, start at [`education-teaching-methods`](education-teaching-methods.md).

- `i know the skill but not how to teach it` or `teach me to teach` -> this guide

- `learning space accessible and usable` -> `accessible-shelter-design`
- `i need the lesson plan, not teacher coaching` -> `education-teaching-methods`

Post-collapse communities cannot afford professional teacher educators. You must develop teaching capacity among the people who possess crucial knowledge. This requires teaching subject-matter experts (SMEs) how to teach effectively—a skill learnable through practice and feedback.

:::warning
**Critical distinction:** Knowing how to do something ≠ knowing how to teach it. A master carpenter can build anything but might assume "just watch and copy" is sufficient instruction. Effective teaching requires intentional methodology: explaining reasoning, breaking complexity into steps, checking understanding, adjusting when learners struggle.
:::

## Teaching Mindset for Subject-Matter Experts

Before methodology, cultivate the right mental approach to teaching.

### From Expert to Guide

Expert SME thinks: "This is obvious. Why can't they see it?" Their knowledge is intuitive—they can't remember the learning process they went through.

Teaching mindset: "They don't know yet. That's normal. My job is to make the invisible visible."

**Shift:** View teaching as translation, not judgment. Translate expert knowledge into learner-accessible format. When learner struggles, it's not stupidity—it's unclear explanation. Your responsibility to improve explanation.

### Patience and Empathy

Expert perspective: "I learned this alone. They should too."

Teaching perspective: "I learned this alone over years. I can compress that time by explaining well."

Patience is not optional. Every learner needs time to practice, make mistakes, get feedback, try again. Rushing creates incompetent practitioners who waste community resources later.

**Shift:** Budget ample time. Expect repetition. Celebrate small progress. View questions as evidence of engagement, not annoyance.

### Fixed vs. Growth Mindset

Fixed mindset: "Some people are natural learners. Others aren't smart enough for this skill."

Growth mindset: "Everyone can learn this skill with appropriate teaching and practice. My teaching approach must match their learning style."

**Shift:** When learner struggles, problem is how-to-teach, not learner's capability. Different people learn differently—verbal explanation works for one learner, demonstration for another, hands-on practice for third. Your job is finding what works.

## Lesson Planning Template for SMEs

Most SMEs have never planned a lesson. This template makes it systematic.

### Basic Lesson Plan Template (30-60 minute session)

```
LESSON PLAN TEMPLATE

Skill/Topic: [What will learners learn?]
Example: "Proper forge temperature recognition by color"

Learner Level: [Ages? Prior experience?]
Example: "Ages 12-13, no prior forge experience"

Objective: [What should learners be able to do at end?]
Example: "Identify three temperature stages (cherry red, bright orange,
yellow-white) by color and understand when each is appropriate"

Materials Needed: [What's required?]
Example: "Forge, metal sample pieces, striker, tongs, water bucket"

Time Budget:
- Introduction (3-5 min): Why this matters
- Demonstration (10-15 min): Show the skill
- Guided practice (15-20 min): Learners try with support
- Closing/Assessment (5-10 min): Check understanding

---

INTRODUCTION (3-5 minutes)
Purpose: Hook attention, explain relevance, preview learning

What you'll say:
"Today we're learning to recognize forge temperatures by color. This is
crucial because metal at wrong temperature breaks, won't shape right,
or becomes brittle. Getting this wrong can waste material and time.
You'll see the colors and learn what they mean."

---

DEMONSTRATION (10-15 minutes)
Purpose: Show exactly what you want them to do

Steps:
1. Heat metal slowly from room temperature
2. Explain what you observe: "Watch the color change..."
3. Stop at each stage: cherry red, bright orange, yellow-white
4. At each stage, explain: "This color means the metal is ready for..."
5. Repeat 2-3 times so patterns become clear

What to emphasize:
- Go slowly so they can see the color progression
- Name the colors explicitly ("This cherry red stage...")
- Say WHY each stage matters ("At bright orange, metal is plastic and
shapes easily. If cooler, it won't shape...")
- Ask questions: "What color is it now? What do you think comes next?"

---

GUIDED PRACTICE (15-20 minutes)
Purpose: Let learners attempt skill with support present

Setup:
1. Learner operates forge while you observe
2. As color changes, ask: "What color do you see now?"
3. Don't tell—guide: "What stage comes after cherry red?"
4. When learner struggles, help: "Let me show you again, then try..."
5. Affirm success: "Correct! You caught the cherry red stage."

---

ASSESSMENT / CLOSING (5-10 minutes)
Purpose: Check if they understood; plan next session

Assessment methods:
- Ask learner to name colors and describe stages
- Have learner predict: "If we heat it more, what's next?"
- Observe: Can learner identify color without prompting?

Close by:
- Celebrating what they learned: "You recognized three temperature
stages correctly today. That's the foundation."
- Previewing next: "Next session, we'll use that knowledge to shape
your first metal piece. You'll see how temperature affects shaping."
- Answering questions: "Any questions about what we learned?"

---

NOTES FOR NEXT TIME:
[What went well? What needs adjustment? Did any learner struggle?]
```

### Adapting to Your Teaching Context

**For hands-on skills (smithing, carpentry, food prep):**
- Demonstration + guided practice is primary method
- Keep introduction brief (learners want to do)
- Assessment is observation of actual work quality
- Next session builds on completion of this skill

**For knowledge-based teaching (herbalism, medicine diagnosis):**
- Explanation more prominent
- Use examples to make abstract concrete
- Assessment includes explanation by learner ("Tell me what you learned")
- Next session builds on knowledge gaps identified

**For mixed groups (different skill levels simultaneously):**
- Demonstration is core (reaches all)
- Guided practice involves pairs: advanced learner helps struggling learner
- Assessment checks each person's understanding individually

:::tip
**Lesson planning takes practice.** First few lessons feel awkward. You're thinking about what comes next while teaching. That's normal. After 10-15 lessons, planning becomes intuitive and takes less time. Persist through the awkward phase.
:::

## Assessment Methods for Non-Academic Skills

Assessment serves two purposes: (1) confirm learner understood, (2) identify what to teach next.

### Observation-Based Assessment

**Best for hands-on skills** (most practical for post-collapse context).

Watch learner perform the skill. Do they:
- Follow steps in correct order? (If not: unclear on sequence)
- Perform each step correctly? (If not: unclear on technique)
- Problem-solve when stuck? (If yes: deep understanding. If no: mechanical copy)
- Ask intelligent questions? (Shows they're thinking)

**Record simple notes:**
```
DATE: 2026-03-15
LEARNER: Marcus (age 13)
SKILL: Fire starting with flint

OBSERVATIONS:
✓ Gathers materials in correct order (tinder, kindling, fuel)
✓ Holds flint correctly
✓ Strikes at correct angle
~ Tinder moisture varies—sometimes too damp, recognizes problem
✗ Doesn't recognize when fire is dying and needs fuel added
? Asks good questions about wind effects

NEXT: Practice reading fire's stage (when to add fuel). Teach wind
management. Marcus is ready for solo practice on calm days.
```

### Demonstration Assessment

**Ask learner to show you.** Don't tell them what to do. Hand them materials. "Show me how to preserve meat by smoking."

Watch. They either can or can't. If stuck: "What would you do next?" If they don't know: that's the gap to address.

### Verbal Assessment

**Ask questions** to assess understanding:

**Literal:** "What are the three temperature stages we learned?"
- Tests memorization

**Conceptual:** "Why do we heat metal to bright orange and not cherry red for shaping?"
- Tests understanding of reasoning

**Application:** "You have a piece of metal at yellow-white. What's your next step and why?"
- Tests ability to apply knowledge

**Explanation:** "Explain to [another learner] why we need correct temperature."
- Teaching someone else reveals gaps in understanding

:::info-box
**Key principle:** If learner can do it + explain why + teach someone else = deep understanding. If they can only do it by copying = fragile learning. Design assessments to reach deep understanding.
:::

## Managing Different Learning Speeds

Learners progress at different rates. Some master fire starting in 2 weeks; others need 4-5 weeks. This is normal, not a problem if handled well.

### The Three-Speed Strategy

**Fast learners:** Need challenge to stay engaged. If you keep them on basic practice 5 weeks while others catch up, they get bored and behavior deteriorates.

**Pace:**
- Let them progress to next skill once they master current one
- Assign advanced variation of current skill ("Show me you can start fire in rain")
- Have them teach slower learners (teaching deepens their understanding)
- Introduce specialization: "You're exceptionally good at fire in wet conditions. Want to specialize in survival fire-starting?"

**Average learners:** Generally follow planned pace.

**Pace:**
- Standard progression through designed curriculum
- Supportive feedback
- Peer learning: pair with faster learner for peer teaching

**Slow learners:** Slower pace doesn't indicate inability—might indicate need for different teaching approach or prerequisite skills.

**Pace:**
- Reduce complexity: Break skill into smaller steps
- Change teaching method: If lecture-style isn't working, try demonstration + practice
- Check prerequisites: Do they have foundational skills? (Can they control their grip strength? Understand spatial relationships?)
- Increase practice: More repetitions before advancing
- Individual attention: Slower learners need more one-on-one feedback
- Patient encouragement: "You've improved [give specific example]. That's progress."

### Practical Implementation

**Stagger apprentice start dates.** Don't begin five apprentices simultaneously. Start one, wait 6 weeks, start second. This creates natural differentiation—experienced apprentices help newer ones.

**Use stations.** If you have multiple skills, create practice stations:
- Station A: New learners doing foundation practice
- Station B: Intermediate learners doing applied practice
- Station C: Advanced learners doing teaching/innovation

Learners rotate through as they advance.

**Flexible grouping.** Group learners by current skill level, not age. A 15-year-old might be learning fire starting alongside a 10-year-old if they both need it. No shame in mixed-age groups.

### When Learner Legitimately Struggles

Sometimes teaching adjustment isn't enough. Learner might have:
- Specific learning difficulty (dyslexia, dyscalculia, attention issues)
- Physical limitation affecting learning (hand strength, vision, hearing)
- Language barrier (learning to teach in unfamiliar language)
- Emotional block (anxiety about fire, past injury, learning trauma)

**Response:**
1. Identify the root: Observe carefully. "Maria shuts down when I correct her mistakes. She might have anxiety, not inability."
2. Adapt: Different approach for emotional block (gentler feedback) than cognitive difference (multisensory instruction)
3. Problem-solve: "What would help Maria learn better?"
4. Consider alternative path: Does learner genuinely need this skill? Could they specialize in different, related skill?
5. Never shame: Frame as "learning approach that works better" not "you can't do this."

:::warning
**Critical:** Don't confuse slower learning with inability. A slow learner might become excellent practitioner—just takes longer. Impatience with slow learners is a major reason knowledge transfer fails. Budget the time it takes.
:::

## Basic Classroom Management Without Formal Training

Classroom management = creating environment where learning happens. Not punishment—prevention.

### Establish Clear Expectations

Before teaching, learners must know what's expected.

**Explicit agreement:**
"In our smithing workshop, we:
- Arrive on time [your time, ready to work]
- Treat tools with respect [they're expensive and dangerous]
- Listen when someone's explaining [full attention, no side conversations]
- Ask questions [I want to know what's unclear]
- Help each other [stronger apprentices support newer ones]
- Leave workspace clean [so next group can work safely]"

Write this down if literate community. Reference it when someone violates expectation. "Remember our agreement about listening? Let's focus."

### Create Physical Safety

- Clear pathways (no tripping hazards)
- Dangerous tools stored safely when not in use
- Water/fire/first aid readily accessible
- Adequate light for close work
- Temperature reasonable (not too hot/cold for concentration)

Safety = prerequisite for learning. If learners are anxious about physical danger, they can't concentrate.

### Structure Time Clearly

Learners need to know: "What's happening when?"

**Sample schedule (2-hour session):**
- 0:00-0:10 — Greeting, preview what we're learning, safety reminders
- 0:10-0:25 — Demonstration
- 0:25-1:40 — Guided practice (with breaks if needed)
- 1:40-2:00 — Cleanup, reflection on learning, closing

Communicate this: "We have 2 hours. First, I'll show you. Then you'll practice. At the end, we'll clean up." This reduces anxiety ("What's happening?") and helps learners budget attention.

### Manage Behavior Issues

Most behavior issues stem from: unclear expectations, boredom, frustration, physical discomfort, or peer conflict.

**Prevention strategies:**
- Clear expectations (stated above)
- Adequate challenge (not too easy, not too hard—find sweet spot)
- Immediate feedback (catches misunderstanding early before frustration builds)
- Movement breaks (especially for younger learners; standing still is hard)
- Positive attention (notice and affirm good behavior: "I see you focusing on technique")

**When behavior issue arises:**

1. **Don't overreact.** Child goofing off might just be bored or confused. Correct calmly.

2. **Address the root.** "Kai, you're not focusing. What's wrong? Too hard? Too easy? Confused about something?"

3. **Adjust.** If bored—increase challenge. If frustrated—simplify. If confused—explain differently.

4. **Separate if necessary.** Serious disruption: remove learner temporarily. "Take 10 minutes. Come back when you're ready to focus." Usually works better than punishment.

5. **Don't humiliate.** Correct privately if possible. Public criticism creates shame and resentment.

### Handling Questions

Learners asking questions = good sign (they're engaged and thinking). Encourage them.

**Effective responding:**
- "Good question." (Affirm)
- Answer if straightforward
- If complex: "Let me show you." (Demonstrate)
- If learner should figure out: "What do you think?" (Guide discovery)
- "I don't know. Let's figure it out together." (Honest is better than making up)

Never mock questions. "That's a dumb question" shuts down engagement.

### Managing Groups with Wide Variation

When teaching mixed ages/skills simultaneously:

**Pair advanced with less advanced:**
- "Rosa, you're strong at this. Can you watch Milo and give feedback?"
- Teaching others deepens Rosa's understanding
- Milo gets peer support
- You're freed to work with others

**Assign meaningful independent work:**
- Advanced learners get: challenging projects, teaching tasks, exploration
- They're not just "done waiting"—they're progressing

**Rotate focus:**
- You can't teach everyone equally every minute
- Sometimes focus on beginners (demonstration, direct instruction)
- Sometimes focus on advanced (challenge, feedback)
- Confident intermediate learners work independently

## Giving Effective Feedback

Feedback is how learners improve. Bad feedback wastes time; good feedback accelerates learning.

### Characteristics of Effective Feedback

**Specific:** "Good job" is useless. "Your angle on the hammer strike is correct—you're hitting at 45 degrees and following through" is useful.

**Immediate:** Don't wait weeks to give feedback. Same day is ideal. Next session is acceptable. Weeks later = feedback is forgotten.

**Actionable:** Learner knows what to do differently. "Better" doesn't help. "Next time, angle the hammer slightly more toward your body" does.

**Balance positive + improvement:** "Your fire starting is much faster than last week. Next, work on recognizing when to add kindling—you're waiting too long."

**Respect learner:** Feedback is about skill, not person. "Your technique here needs work" not "You're clumsy."

### Feedback Conversation

**Format:**
1. "Here's what you did well: [specific positive]"
2. "Here's what needs improvement: [specific area]"
3. "How do we improve it? [show or practice together]"
4. "Try again with that in mind"
5. "See? Better. Keep practicing this."

**Example:**
"Your fire is building nicely—tinder is catching and kindling is catching. What I noticed: the fire was struggling to catch the larger wood. Next time, transition to bigger kindling gradually—not jumping straight to big sticks. Let me show you the size progression. Then try again."

### Receiving Feedback from Learners

Learners might say: "I don't understand your explanation" or "This is boring" or "Can we try a different way?"

**Don't get defensive.** They're telling you how to teach better.

**Respond:** "Tell me more. What would help you understand?" Then adjust. This models that feedback is gift, not criticism.

## Simple Documentation System

Keep records of each learner's progress. You don't have time for elaborate notes, but simple records help.

### Minimal Documentation

**Per learner, keep:**

```
NAME: [Learner]
DATES STARTED: [Date]

SESSION LOG:
Date | Skill Practiced | Competency | Notes
2026-02-01 | Fire starting | Assisted | Gathers materials correctly, struggles with flint angle
2026-02-08 | Fire starting | Guided ind. | Flint angle improved, tinder moisture recognition needed
2026-02-15 | Fire starting | Independent | Successfully starts fires on calm days, wind difficult
2026-02-22 | Fire in wind | Assisted | First attempt, needs practice

ADVANCEMENT DECISION:
Recommend advancement to guided practice in rain fires: [date]
Recommend advancement to journeyman status: [date] [council vote]
```

### What This Tells You

- Did learner progress as expected?
- What skills are solid vs. still developing?
- When is learner ready to advance?
- If learner repeats years later, you know history

### Simple Assessment Rubric

Rather than complex rubrics, use three-point scale:

**Competency Levels:**
- **Beginning:** Learner can attempt skill with assistance; frequent errors; doesn't understand reasoning
- **Developing:** Learner performs skill with occasional errors; understands most reasoning; needs reminders
- **Proficient:** Learner performs skill correctly most of the time; understands reasoning; explains to others

Record this for each skill at each checkpoint. Shows progression and readiness for advancement.

:::tip
**Keep it simple.** You don't have time for lengthy documentation. Simple notes help YOU remember and track progress. That's sufficient.
:::

## Five Core Teaching Skills to Practice

These can be learned through practice and self-reflection.

### 1. Clear Demonstration

**What it is:** Showing the skill clearly so learners understand.

**Practice:**
- Slow down. Go 50% slower than normal.
- Name each step: "First, I gather materials. Second, I arrange them. Third, I strike flint."
- Explain reasoning: "I use birch bark because it catches easily. If I used damp grass, it wouldn't catch."
- Repeat 2-3 times so patterns are clear
- Ask questions mid-demo: "What do you predict happens next?"

### 2. Asking Good Questions

**What it is:** Using questions to guide thinking rather than just telling.

**Practice:**
- Instead of "Do this," ask "What comes next?"
- Instead of "You're wrong," ask "What do you see? How might that affect the result?"
- Instead of explaining, ask "Why do you think we do it that way?"

### 3. Patience with Repetition

**What it is:** Allowing and encouraging repeated practice without frustration.

**Practice:**
- Count how many times learner needs to practice before mastery
- Stay calm and encouraging for all 20+ repetitions
- Notice incremental improvement: "You're faster today than yesterday"
- Never say "I already showed you this" even if you've shown it 10 times

### 4. Individualized Adjustment

**What it is:** Changing approach based on learner's response.

**Practice:**
- "This explanation isn't working. Let me try differently."
- "You're bored with this—let me increase difficulty"
- "You're frustrated—let me break this into smaller steps"
- Test different approaches; use what works

### 5. Creating Safe Space for Mistakes

**What it is:** Making learners feel safe trying even though they'll fail.

**Practice:**
- Respond to mistakes positively: "Good try. Here's what to adjust."
- Normalize mistakes: "I make this mistake sometimes too. It just takes practice."
- Never shame: "That's why we practice"
- Celebrate attempts: "I like that you tried something new"
