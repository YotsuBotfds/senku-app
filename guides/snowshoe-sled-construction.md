## 1. Snowshoe Fundamentals

Snowshoes spread body weight over a larger area, allowing travel through deep snow without sinking. In snow climates, snowshoes are essential for winter survival and transportation.

### Why Snowshoes Matter

Deep snow makes walking impossible without snowshoes. A person sinking to the knee or waist expends 3-4 times the energy of normal walking, exhausts rapidly, and risks hypothermia. Snowshoes reduce sinking by 70-90%, dramatically extending travel distance and survival capability.

### Performance Metrics

**Flotation:** The load per unit area; lower is better.

- Without snowshoes: A 180-lb person over 16 sq inches of foot sole = 11 lbs/sq inch of pressure (sinking in snow)
- With snowshoes: Same person over 400 sq inches total = 0.45 lbs/sq inch (floating on surface)

**Weight:** Snowshoes should be light (2-4 lbs per pair) to minimize fatigue from stepping. Heavier snowshoes tire the legs.

**Efficiency:** Good snowshoes allow normal walking gait; poor designs require exaggerated stepping motions that exhaust quickly.

### Design Trade-Offs

**Large snowshoes (>60 sq inches per shoe):**

- **Pros:** Better flotation, suitable for very deep/fluffy snow
- **Cons:** Heavy, cumbersome, slow walking speed, tiring

**Small snowshoes (30-40 sq inches per shoe):**

- **Pros:** Light, fast, easy to use
- **Cons:** Less flotation, sink in deep powder, poor for steep terrain

**Moderate snowshoes (45-55 sq inches per shoe):**

- Best all-around choice for most winter travel

## 2. Frame Materials & Construction

Snowshoe frames must be strong, lightweight, and flexible. Historical designs use local materials: ash, birch, willow, or hickory for wood; bone or antler for reinforcement.

### Wood Selection

**Ash:** Straight-grained, strong, lightweight; the ideal snowshoe wood. Ash is hard to bend but provides excellent strength-to-weight ratio. Most traditional and quality modern snowshoes use ash.

**Birch:** Easier to bend than ash, good strength. Birch is more readily available in boreal regions; historical snowshoes use birch when ash is unavailable.

**Willow:** Lightweight and flexible; traditional in arctic regions. Willow bends easily but is weaker than ash; requires careful design to avoid failure.

**Hickory:** Very strong but heavy; slower to bend and more prone to breaking during bending. Acceptable if other woods are unavailable.

### Frame Shaping

**Harvesting and preparing wood:**

1. Select straight-grained saplings 1-1.5 inches diameter; avoid knots and blemishes
2. Split the sapling lengthwise into strips 0.75-1 inch wide and 0.5-0.75 inches thick
3. Allow the wood to dry slowly (2-4 weeks) in shade; fast drying causes cracking
4. Steam or soak the dried strips in hot water for 1-2 hours to soften for bending

**Bending the frame:**

1. **Heat the wood:** Soak in hot (not boiling) water until pliable (~30 minutes for softened wood)
2. **Shape the shoe:** Bend the wood carefully into a shoe shape (wider at the toe, narrower at the heel). Traditional shapes are teardrop or bear-paw (rounded ovoid).
3. **Support the curve:** Place the shoe in a wooden form or hold with bindings to maintain shape while drying
4. **Dry slowly:** Allow 2-4 weeks to dry before removing from the form; fast drying causes the frame to revert to its original shape

**Frame dimensions:**

For an average adult (150-200 lbs):

- Length: 36-42 inches (longer provides better flotation on deep snow)
- Width: 10-14 inches at the widest (midfoot) point
- Toe height: 2-3 inches above heel (aids walking and provides turning leverage)
- Heel overhang: 1-2 inches below the binding point (improves walking gait)

### Crosspieces & Reinforcement

One to three crosspieces (lacing supports) run perpendicular to the frame:

- **Single crosspiece:** Simplest; placed at 1/3 point from the toe; suitable for small snowshoes
- **Two crosspieces:** Placed at 1/3 and 2/3 points from the toe; more strength, suitable for heavier users
- **Three crosspieces:** Maximum strength; used in traditional arctic designs and very large shoes

Crosspieces are lashed (not glued) to the frame with babiche (rawhide) or cord, allowing flexibility and easy repair.

<section id="lacing-patterns">

## 3. Lacing Patterns

The lacing (webbing strung across the frame) distributes the wearer's weight and provides flotation. Lacing pattern affects performance.

### Babiche (Rawhide Cord)

Traditional snowshoe lacing uses babiche (strips of rawhide boiled and twisted into cord). Babiche is flexible, durable, and weather-resistant. Modern snowshoes use synthetic materials, but babiche remains unmatched for durability in extreme conditions.

**Making babiche:**

1. Skin a large animal (moose, elk, deer); preserve the hide dry
2. Soak the hide in water for 24 hours to rehydrate
3. Scrape off hair and flesh; the hide should be clean and pliable
4. Cut spiral strips from the hide, starting at the edge and spiraling inward (maximizes single strip length)
5. Twist the strips tightly to create cord; hang to dry over 1-2 weeks
6. The resulting cord is inelastic, strong, and water-resistant

**Using synthetic materials:** Paracord or braided nylon rope works as a substitute, though it is less durable than babiche in extended cold exposure (nylon becomes brittle at extreme cold).

### Bear Paw Lacing

The bear paw is the most common pattern: dense lacing throughout the shoe, providing even weight distribution and excellent flotation.

**Lacing procedure:**

1. Attach the babiche at one point near the heel
2. Weave back and forth across the frame (heel to toe, toe to heel) in parallel lines spaced 1.5-2 inches apart
3. Maintain slight tension on the babiche (not too tight; tight lacing restricts flexibility and breaks in cold)
4. Connect the babiche at the crosspieces with a lark's-head knot or loop that allows the lacing to slip slightly
5. Finish the babiche at the opposite heel

Result: A dense, even webbing covering the entire shoe. Excellent flotation and durability.

### Ojibwe (Anishinaabe) Lacing

This pattern uses two layers: a primary layer of parallel lacing and a secondary diagonal cross-pattern. The diagonals reinforce the primary lacing and improve sideways stability.

**Advantages:** Better handling on slopes and uneven terrain; aesthetically distinctive

**Disadvantage:** More time-consuming; approximately 30-40% more babiche required

### Alaskan Lacing

A hybrid pattern using heavy primary lacing with selective diagonal bracing. Quicker than Ojibwe but more stable than bear paw.

**Common in:** Arctic expeditions where speed of manufacture and durability are both critical

<section id="binding-systems">

## 4. Binding Systems & Attachment

The binding system connects the snowshoe to the boot and controls the shoe's motion relative to the foot.

### Traditional Binding (Rotational)

A traditional binding allows the heel to lift freely (rotating motion) while keeping the toe attached, mimicking natural walking gait.

**Components:**

- **Toe loop:** Firm leather or cord loop around the toe; holds the toe in the shoe
- **Heel loop:** Loose loop around the heel; allows free motion
- **Ankle strap (optional):** Leather strap around the ankle; prevents sideways rotation but allows heel lift

**Attachment to boot:**

1. Thread the toe loop through a sturdy loop or knot on the boot's insole just behind the toe
2. Tie the heel loop to the boot heel or calf with an adjustable knot
3. The strapping should be snug but not tight; allow slight movement

**Advantages:** Natural walking motion, less ankle fatigue, traditional design proven over centuries

**Disadvantages:** Less precise foot control on very steep terrain; may feel unstable to beginners

### Modern Fixed Binding (Heel Raised)

Modern bindings sometimes keep the heel elevated (in a fixed position) to reduce calf fatigue on long days.

**System:** The entire foot is held in a rigid frame that elevates the heel 1-2 inches. This reduces the range of motion required from the calf muscles.

**Disadvantages:** Unnatural gait; causes calf cramping on steep climbs; not recommended for traditional designs

### Quick-Release System

For emergencies, a quick-release binding allows rapid removal of the snowshoe if the user is falling or sinking.

**Design:** A thin cord runs from the toe loop to the heel loop with a breakaway connection (a slip knot or mechanical release) that opens under sudden load. Modern designs rarely use this due to danger of losing equipment.

<section id="sled-construction">

## 5. Sled & Toboggan Construction

Sleds and toboggans transport loads across snow. Unlike snowshoes (for people), sleds are pulled by animals or dragged by teams.

### Sled Types

**Runner sleds:** Two or more narrow wooden runners separated by a platform. Suitable for ice, hard-packed snow, and pulling with dogs or animals.

**Toboggans:** Single curved-up front (prow); flat bottom; excellent flotation on soft snow; preferred for human-hauled loads.

**Sleighs:** Large box-shaped sleds on runners; for bulk cargo or passenger transport with animal teams.

### Runner Design

**Runner materials:**

- **Wood:** Ash, birch, or hickory runners are light and strong. Runners are typically 2-4 inches wide, 1-1.5 inches thick, and 6-10 feet long.
- **Bone/antler:** Historical materials; durable but difficult to manufacture. Bone runners are rare in modern designs.
- **Steel/metal:** Excellent runners but heavy; requires industrial manufacturing.

**Runner shape:**

A good runner has:

- **Upturned toe (prow):** 4-8 inches of upturned front; allows the sled to climb over obstacles and breakable crust
- **Flat bottom:** The contact surface with snow; polishing or waxing reduces friction
- **Slight rocker:** A gentle curve from toe to tail; helps the sled turn and prevents the flat bottom from catching on inequalities in the snow surface

**Spacing:** Runners are typically 18-24 inches apart (narrow sleds) to 36-48 inches apart (cargo sleds). Wider spacing provides stability but increases weight.

### Toboggan Construction

Toboggans are easier to build than runner sleds and superior on soft snow.

**Materials:**

- **Planks:** 1-2 inch thick, 12-18 inches wide; use flexible wood (birch or ash, not oak)
- **Frame:** Bent-wood ribs similar to snowshoe construction provide lateral stability

**Build sequence:**

1. **Prepare planks:** Select long straight-grained boards. If unavailable, bend planks by steaming and clamping them together.
2. **Create the shell:** Overlap planks like roof shingles; each plank extends 1-2 inches past the one below. Attach with flexible joints (allow slight movement) using wooden pegs or lashing.
3. **Curve the prow:** The front plank curls upward 4-6 inches to climb over snow and obstacles. Steam and bend before final attachment.
4. **Add cross-members:** Occasional cross-ribs tie the planks together and prevent the toboggan from opening up like a book. Space ribs 3-4 feet apart.
5. **Attach cleats:** If the toboggan will be pulled up-slope, install cross-pieces on the runners (like snowshoe crosspieces) for grip.

**Toboggan dimensions:**

- Length: 5-8 feet
- Width: 18-24 inches
- Prow height: 6-12 inches
- Cargo capacity: 100-300 lbs depending on snow condition

<section id="load-distribution">

## 6. Load Distribution & Hitching

### Load Capacity

**Sleds:** Depends on snow condition, runner design, and pulling power

- Hard-packed or icy snow: 300-500 lbs per sled (minimal friction)
- Soft snow: 100-200 lbs per sled (high friction)
- Deep powder: 50-100 lbs (very high friction; may require multiple teams)

**Toboggans:** Better flotation; typically carry 30-50% more load than runner sleds on soft snow

### Balance & Centering

Load distribution affects handling:

- **Front-heavy:** The sled may nose-dive into snow obstacles; hard to steer
- **Rear-heavy:** The sled may track poorly; easier to go sideways downhill
- **Balanced:** Load is centered over the runners; optimal handling

For runner sleds, place heavy loads between the runners (centered). For toboggans, place heavy loads forward of center (better handling when loaded).

### Harness & Hitching Systems

**Single-team configuration:** One sled pulled by an animal (horse, mule, dog team)

- Attach the pulling harness securely to a central point on the sled frame
- Keep the pulling line horizontal or slightly downward angled (prevents the sled from tipping)

**Multi-sled configuration:** Multiple sleds in a line (tandem)

- The first sled attaches directly to the team
- Subsequent sleds attach to the sled in front using a rope or pole; space them 8-10 feet apart to prevent the rear sled from colliding if the front sled stops suddenly
- Tandem sleds require slightly more pulling force (friction from the trailing sled) but allow transport of more cargo with a single team

### Dog Sled Hitching

For dog teams (3-8 dogs per sled):

- **Lead dogs:** Positions closest to the sled; strongest and most obedient dogs
- **Team dogs:** Middle positions; provide power
- **Wheel dogs:** Closest to the sled; keep the sled centered and prevent sliding sideways

Dogs are harnessed in pairs or a single line. The sled is attached via a bridle (rope harness) to the dogs' harnesses.

## 7. Snow Condition Assessment

Performance varies dramatically with snow type.

### Wind-Compacted / Crust Snow

Dense, hard snow; sleds run easily with low friction. Snowshoes may break through the crust, sinking partially. Layer structure:

- Top layer: Hard crust (1-2 inches)
- Middle layer: Softer, partially consolidated snow
- Bottom layer: Potentially powder or ice

**Travel:** Sleds run well but can punch through thin crust. Snowshoes may sink. Move carefully and test crust strength before committing weight.

### Powder Snow

Light, unconsolidated snow; high resistance and poor bearing capacity. Typical of fresh snow or protected areas.

- Snowshoes essential (sinking 12+ inches without them)
- Sleds require minimum 400-500 lbs load capacity per dog team or animal
- Energy expenditure is 30-50% higher than on packed snow
- Progress is slow (1-2 mph) compared to packed snow (3-5 mph)

### Wet / Corn Snow

Heavy, sticky snow (spring or warm conditions); high friction and poor flotation.

- Snowshoes and sleds both perform poorly
- Equipment becomes clogged with snow
- Most efficient travel is in the early morning when the snow is still frozen

### Ice

Smooth, slippery ice requires special treatment.

- Snowshoes with cleats or spiked boots essential (prevent sliding)
- Sleds run very fast but are difficult to control; steerable sleds with edge control work better
- Avoid ice over water (fatal if you break through)

<section id="animal-teams">

## 8. Dog & Animal Teams

### Dog Sled Teams

Dogs are most efficient for sledding in arctic/subarctic regions. Historical Inuit and Alaskan teams demonstrate exceptional efficiency.

**Team size:** 3-12 dogs per sled, depending on load and distance

- Light expeditions: 3-4 dogs
- Standard cargo: 6-8 dogs
- Heavy cargo: 10-12 dogs

**Breeding for sledding:** Select for:

- Endurance (not speed; long-distance capability)
- Size (40-60 lbs; large enough to pull, small enough to feed)
- Temperament (calm, obedient, social)
- Coat density (cold tolerance)

**Historical note:** Siberian Huskies and Alaskan Malamutes were bred specifically for sledding and remain the standard.

**Training:** Dogs require 1-2 months of training to learn commands:

- "Hike!" or "Go!" → forward
- "Whoa!" → stop
- "Gee!" → right turn
- "Haw!" → left turn

### Horse & Oxen Teams

Horses and oxen pull sleds effectively, particularly for heavy cargo.

**Advantages over dogs:**

- Can pull heavier loads (2-5x more)
- Require less frequent feeding breaks on long journeys
- Can live on forage (grass, hay) rather than specialized dog food

**Disadvantages:**

- Slower than dogs (1-2 mph vs. 4-8 mph for dogs)
- Require shelter and more space
- More difficult to train

**Hitching:** Animals are typically hitched singly or in pairs to a central pulling pole/bridle. Balance and even pulling force are critical to prevent the sled from tracking sideways.

<section id="field-repairs">

## 9. Field Repair & Maintenance

### Common Failures & Fixes

**Cracked or broken runner/frame:**

- **Temporary repair:** Wrap the damaged area tightly with cord or cloth to stabilize it; reduce load if possible
- **Permanent repair:** Replace the damaged member if possible; lash a splint (small wooden reinforcement) over the crack

**Damaged lacing:**

- **Snowshoe:** Replace damaged babiche by unwinding the broken section and re-lacing; keep the shoe balanced
- **If babiche is unavailable:** Use paracord, natural fibers (willow bark), or other strong cord in a temporary repair; the shoe may feel different but remains functional

**Broken binding:**

- **Quick fix:** Tie the snowshoe on with cord looped around the boot ankle and the heel loop; it's not ideal but functional
- **Better repair:** Replace the binding with new leather or cord; bring spare bindings on expeditions

**Icing (snow accumulation):**

- Periodically knock ice off the underside of snowshoes and sleds with a stick or boot heel; ice buildup increases weight by 20-50%
- Wax the runner undersides to reduce icing (animal fat or plant-based wax work; modern ski wax is superior but may not be available)

### Seasonal Maintenance

**Before winter use:**

1. Inspect all lacing for damage; replace suspect sections
2. Check bindings; ensure attachments are secure
3. Repair any cracks in frames or runners
4. Wax runners to reduce friction

**After winter use:**

1. Clean all snow and ice
2. Hang in a dry location (not near direct heat, which causes rapid drying and cracking)
3. Inspect for damage; repair cracks or breaks
4. Allow to dry completely before storage

**Storage:**

- Store in cool, dry location (temperature extremes cause warping)
- Do not store near heat sources
- Protect wooden components from insects and rodents
- Check periodically; humidity can cause mold growth on leather or animal fiber
