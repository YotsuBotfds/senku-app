<div class="symptom-box">

**Start here if you have a symptom or everyday complaint and are not yet sure what section fits it.** This guide covers the most common everyday complaints:

- fever, chills, body aches, or "I feel sick" / "I don't feel good"
- runny or stuffy nose, sore throat, cough, sneezing — cold or flu
- nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, stomach cramps, or "my stomach hurts"
- burning when you pee, peeing often, urinary urgency, or bladder pressure
- itchy rash, mild skin bumps, pimples, or small infected cuts
- headache, muscle aches, or mild allergic sneezing / itch

If the complaint is really "is this a stroke?" or "is this a heart attack?", do not stay in this guide. Go straight to <a href="../first-aid.html">First Aid Fundamentals</a> if any of these are happening now:

- sudden face droop, one-sided weakness or numbness, trouble speaking, or sudden confusion
- heavy chest pressure or tightness, pain spreading into the jaw/arm/back, cold sweat, or sudden shortness of breath with nausea or dread

Practical first step: treat those patterns as emergencies first, call emergency services or begin the fastest evacuation available, and keep the person resting while you monitor breathing. Do not give food, water, or pills by mouth if stroke signs, confusion, or reduced alertness are present.

If something looks like an emergency (severe bleeding, trouble breathing, chest pain, loss of consciousness), go straight to <a href="../first-aid.html">First Aid Fundamentals</a>.

If the belly is swollen or hard, the person cannot keep anything down, and they are not passing stool or farting, do not stay in this guide. Treat that as a possible obstruction or surgical-abdomen problem and go straight to <a href="../acute-abdominal-emergencies.html">Acute Abdominal Emergencies</a> or <a href="../first-aid.html">First Aid Fundamentals</a>.

If you are not sure whether an abdominal complaint is minor or dangerous, do not stay in this guide. A hard or rigid belly, guarding, refusing to walk upright because of belly pain, focal severe tenderness, fever plus one very tender spot, worsening pain with movement, or vomiting plus distention and no stool/gas should route to <a href="../acute-abdominal-emergencies.html">Acute Abdominal Emergencies</a> first.

For medication choice, dosing, side effects, max daily dose, or interaction questions after you identify the symptom here, see <a href="../medications.html">Medications: Basic Pharmacology & Safe Use</a>.

If you already know the main complaint, use the matching section below first:

- urinary burning, peeing often, urgency, or bladder pressure: <a href="#urinary-infections">Urinary Tract Infections</a>
- itchy rash, bug bites, friction rash, or fungal skin irritation: <a href="../common-rashes-skin-irritation.html">Common Rashes & Skin Irritation</a>
- nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, or "can't keep anything down" without guarding, refusal to walk upright from belly pain, a hard belly, focal severe tenderness, fever plus one tender spot, or inability to pass stool or gas: <a href="#gastroenteritis">Gastrointestinal Illness</a>
- swollen or hard belly, cannot pass stool or fart, vomiting everything, or severe localized belly pain: <a href="../acute-abdominal-emergencies.html">Acute Abdominal Emergencies</a> first

</div>

<div class="red-flag">

### Red-flag fast track — go to First Aid NOW if ANY of these are present

- Trouble breathing, heavy chest pain or pressure, chest tightness with cold sweat or nausea, pain spreading into the arm/jaw/back, or coughing blood
- Sudden severe headache ("worst of my life"), confusion, seizures, or cannot be woken
- Sudden one-sided weakness or numbness, facial droop, trouble speaking, or sudden vision change
- Severe bleeding that does not slow with firm pressure after 10 minutes
- Vomiting blood or bloody diarrhea
- Fever >40.5 °C (105 °F) not responding to cooling/medication
- Signs of shock: rapid weak pulse, cold clammy skin, confusion, fainting
- Severe allergic reaction: throat tightness, tongue swelling, difficulty breathing

**If any of the above are present, stop reading this guide and go straight to [First Aid Fundamentals](../first-aid.html).**

</div>

<div class="home-care-steps">

### Watch at home — OK if ALL of these are true

- Alert, talking normally, breathing comfortably
- Fever <39.5 °C (103 °F) and responding to fluids/rest
- Drinking and keeping fluids down (urine pale yellow)
- Symptoms improving or stable over the last 12–24 hours
- No red-flag items from the list above

If watch-at-home criteria are met, use the sections below for comfort care. Re-check often; if anything worsens, move to the red-flag list.

## Overview: When Something Is "Just a Cold" vs. When It's Serious

Most acute illnesses are self-limited viral infections that resolve in days to weeks. Your immune system fights the infection and wins. However, some illnesses progress to serious complications. The key is distinguishing common minor illnesses from dangerous ones.

**Red Flag Principle:** If a patient has ANY red flag symptom, treat as potentially serious and seek professional help if available. Red flags indicate the immune system is overwhelmed or complications are developing.

![Severity assessment flowchart](../assets/svgs/common-ailments-recognition-care-1.svg)

## Fever: Managing & When to Worry

Fever (elevated body temperature) is a sign the body is fighting infection. Mild to moderate fever is usually the immune system working.

### Normal Temperature Ranges

- **Adult oral:** 36.5–37.5°C (97.7–99.5°F)
- **Rectal (more accurate):** 37.5–38°C (99.5–100.4°F)
- **Axillary (armpit, least accurate):** 36–37.5°C (96.8–99.5°F)

**Note:** Normal temperature varies by individual; time of day (lower morning, higher evening); and body location measured. A person's "fever" may be their normal elevated baseline.

### Fever Severity

- **Low-grade fever:** 37.5–38.5°C (99.5–101.3°F) — Usually viral; general malaise
- **Moderate fever:** 38.5–39.5°C (101.3–103.1°F) — Body working hard to fight infection
- **High fever:** >39.5°C (>103.1°F) — Risk of febrile seizures (especially children), delirium, heat stress

### When to Treat Fever

**DO treat fever if:**
- Patient is uncomfortable/in pain from fever
- Risk of febrile seizures (children 6 months–5 years with rapid fever spike)
- Very high fever (>40°C/104°F) risking heat stress
- Patient has underlying condition (heart disease, lung disease) where fever stress is dangerous

**DO NOT suppress fever if:**
- Patient feels okay despite fever
- Low-grade fever (body fighting infection effectively)

### Fever Management (Non-Medication)

- **Cool water applications:** Sponge with lukewarm (not cold) water; cooling body surface promotes heat loss
- **Hydration:** Drink plenty of water; fever increases fluid loss through sweating
- **Light clothing:** Remove excess blankets; allow body heat to escape
- **Rest:** Recovery requires energy; sleep aids immune system
- **Caloric intake:** Eat when able; infection uses extra calories

### Fever Management (Medication If Available)

- **Acetaminophen (Tylenol):** 500 mg–1 g every 4–6 hours, max 4 g/day; reduces fever and pain
- **Ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin):** 200–400 mg every 4–6 hours, max 1.2 g/day; reduces fever, inflammation, pain
- **Aspirin:** Not recommended in children (risk of Reye's syndrome); use carefully in adults with bleeding risk

:::danger
**High fever red flags requiring emergency care:**
- Fever >40.5°C (105°F) unresponsive to cooling/medication
- Fever with severe headache, stiff neck, confusion (possible meningitis)
- Fever with severe weakness, difficulty breathing
- Fever in infant <3 months (any fever requires evaluation)
- Fever lasting >10 days without improvement (possible serious infection)
:::

## Upper Respiratory Infections: Common Cold & Flu

Common viral respiratory infections (colds, flu, parainfluenza) are usually self-limited.

### Symptom Comparison: Cold vs. Flu

| Feature | Common Cold | Influenza (Flu) |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Onset** | Gradual (2–3 days) | Sudden (hours) |
| **Fever** | Rare or low-grade | Typical, often >38.5°C |
| **Fatigue** | Mild | Severe (weeks) |
| **Headache** | Uncommon | Common, severe |
| **Muscle aches** | Mild | Severe |
| **Respiratory** | Runny/stuffy nose, sore throat | Cough, chest discomfort |
| **Vomiting/diarrhea** | Rare (children sometimes) | Uncommon |
| **Duration** | 7–10 days | 7–14 days; weakness weeks |

### Home Care for Cold/Flu

<div class="home-care-steps">

**1. Rest:** Bed rest if fever present; light activity as tolerated

**2. Hydration:** Drink water, herbal tea, broth (aim for urine that is pale yellow)

**3. Nutrition:** Eat whenever hungry; light foods easier on queasy stomach (broth, soup, crackers)

**4. Symptom relief:**
   - Runny/stuffy nose: Elevate head with pillows; saline nasal drops/spray if available
   - Sore throat: Warm salt water gargles (1 teaspoon salt in 1 cup warm water); throat lozenges if available
   - Cough: Honey is effective for cough suppression (1–2 tablespoons for adults; not for infants <1 year); herbal teas
   - Aches/fever: Acetaminophen or ibuprofen if available

**5. Isolation:** Keep sick person away from others to prevent transmission (respiratory droplets spread infection)

</div>

### Cold/Flu: When It's Serious

<div class="red-flag">

**Seek medical care if:**
- Difficulty breathing (shortness of breath at rest or with minimal exertion)
- Chest pain or pressure
- Coughing blood or bloody phlegm
- Confusion or delirium
- Fever lasting >5 days or returning after improvement
- Severe headache (worst headache of life)
- Symptoms worsening after initial improvement (secondary bacterial infection)

</div>

:::tip
**Cough duration after flu:** Cough can persist 3–4 weeks after flu infection subsides. This usually indicates healing mucous membranes; not a sign of worsening disease.
:::

## Gastrointestinal Illness: Diarrhea, Vomiting, Nausea

Viral gastroenteritis ("stomach flu") and bacterial food poisoning cause acute vomiting and diarrhea. The primary danger is dehydration.

Complaint-first boundary: this section is for vomiting and diarrhea without guarding, refusal to walk upright because of belly pain, a hard / rigid belly, right-lower-quadrant pain that worsens with walking or coughing, one clearly localized tender spot with fever, or vomiting with distention and inability to pass stool or gas. If those red flags appear or the belly pain is clearly worsening instead of acting like routine gastroenteritis, leave this section and use <a href="../acute-abdominal-emergencies.html">Acute Abdominal Emergencies</a> first.

### Dehydration Severity Assessment

**Mild dehydration:**
- Thirst, dry mouth, slightly decreased urine output
- Mild weakness

**Moderate dehydration:**
- Persistent thirst, very dry mouth, decreased urine output, darker urine
- Dizziness, weakness, lethargy
- Dry mucous membranes

**Severe dehydration:**
- Extreme thirst (or unable to drink), very dry mouth, little/no urine output
- Dizziness even lying down, extreme weakness
- Confusion or altered mental status
- Rapid weak pulse
- **EMERGENCY:** Requires urgent rehydration (IV if available)

:::danger
**Dehydration is deadly, especially in children and elderly.** Diarrhea and vomiting lasting >6 hours require aggressive hydration. Without treatment, severe dehydration causes shock and death within hours.
:::

### Oral Rehydration Therapy (ORT)

This is THE critical treatment for gastroenteritis. Commercial ORS (oral rehydration salts) is ideal; homemade solution works.

**Commercial ORS:** Mix according to package directions (typically 1 liter water + packet)

**Homemade ORS:**
- 1 liter clean water
- 6 teaspoons sugar (or honey)
- 1/2 teaspoon salt
- 1/4 teaspoon potassium source (orange juice, banana, or coconut water if available)

**Administration:**
- Small frequent sips: 5–10 mL (1–2 teaspoons) every 5 minutes, even if vomiting
- Gradually increase volume as tolerated
- Continue until vomiting resolves, then return to normal diet
- For ongoing diarrhea: Replace volume lost with ORS (roughly 50 mL/kg for each kilogram body weight lost or 1 liter ORS per stool)

### Diet During Gastroenteritis

**During active vomiting:** Only sips of ORS; nothing else.

**As vomiting stops:** Bland foods:
- Crackers, toast (no butter initially)
- Plain rice
- Bananas
- Applesauce
- Plain broth
- Avoid dairy, high-fat foods, excess fiber initially

**Return to normal:** Gradually; within 48 hours if tolerating bland diet

### Home Care for Gastroenteritis

- **Rest:** Energy is needed for immune recovery
- **Keep warm:** Body loses heat through diarrhea sweating
- **Hygiene:** Wash hands after defecation; prevent spreading to others
- **Anti-diarrheal medications:** Avoid if possible (can trap bacteria inside); use only if diarrhea so severe it prevents hydration
- **Probiotics:** If available, may help restore gut bacteria

### Gastroenteritis: Red Flags

<div class="red-flag">

**Seek care if:**
- Severe abdominal pain with vomiting (possible appendicitis, bowel obstruction)
- Belly pain localizing to one spot, especially with fever or pain that worsens with walking, coughing, or bumps
- The person will not walk upright or keeps guarding the belly
- Swollen or hard belly, not passing stool or fart, and repeated vomiting (possible bowel obstruction)
- Bloody diarrhea (possible dysentery or serious bacterial infection)
- Signs of severe dehydration (see above)
- Vomiting everything (unable to drink); diarrhea continuing >5 days without improvement
- Diarrhea in very young child or elderly (higher dehydration risk)
- Signs of shock: rapid weak pulse, low BP, altered mental status

</div>

## Skin Infections: Impetigo, Cellulitis, Minor Abscesses

Minor skin infections are common in survival settings with poor hygiene and frequent minor cuts.

### Impetigo (Superficial Skin Infection)

**Cause:** Group A Streptococcus or Staph aureus

**Appearance:**
- Small pustules (pus-filled bumps) or honey-crusted lesions
- Usually on face, hands, or legs
- Non-painful or mildly painful
- Highly contagious by contact

**Home care:**
- Wash with soap and water 2–3 times daily
- Remove crusts gently
- Apply antibiotic ointment if available (bacitracin, neomycin)
- Keep uncovered if possible (air exposure helps)
- Isolate if in group setting (prevent spreading)
- Avoid scratching (spreads infection)

**When it's serious:** Cellulitis develops (see below); fever develops; infection spreads rapidly despite treatment

### Cellulitis (Deeper Skin Infection)

**Cause:** Bacterial invasion into deeper skin/subcutaneous tissue

**Appearance:**
- Redness, warmth, swelling, tenderness in localized area
- Poorly demarcated borders
- May have fever, lymphangitis (red streaks extending from wound)

**Home care:**
- Elevate affected limb (reduces swelling)
- Warm compresses 3–4 times daily
- Keep wound clean
- Antibiotic treatment: Systemic antibiotics required if available (amoxicillin, cephalexin, or others)
- Pain management: Acetaminophen or ibuprofen

:::danger
**Cellulitis is serious.** Without treatment, can progress to abscess, sepsis, or necrotizing infection. Red streaks extending toward heart indicate spreading infection (lymphangitis). Fever with cellulitis indicates systemic involvement. Seek medical care immediately if available.
:::

### Abscess (Localized Pus Collection)

**Appearance:** Painful, fluctuant (feels liquid inside), warm, red nodule

**Home care (if small and not infected systemically):**
- Warm compresses to promote drainage
- Gentle expression: Apply warm compress, then gently squeeze to encourage pus release (only if accessible)
- Keep clean
- Pain management

**Incision & drainage:** If abscess is large (>1 cm) and doesn't drain spontaneously:
1. Sterilize area with soap/water or alcohol if available
2. Use sterile needle or scalpel to puncture
3. Allow pus to drain
4. Keep clean; may need repeated gentle expression
5. Monitor for cellulitis spreading

:::warning
**Don't attempt I&D (incision and drainage) if:**
- You're not trained; risk of spreading infection deeper
- Located on face (high risk of serious complications)
- Signs of systemic infection (fever, chills)
- Rapid growth or worsening
:::

## Urinary Tract Infections (UTI)

UTIs are common, especially in women. Usually treatable; can progress to kidney infection if untreated.

If the question sounds like "I keep feeling like I need to pee often," "it burns when I pee," "I have urinary urgency," "can I watch this urinary symptom at home," or "when should I worry about peeing often," start with this section before jumping to unrelated home-care guides.

This section is for primary urinary complaints such as burning, frequency, urgency, bladder pressure, or incomplete emptying when bowel or rectal problems are not the main symptom.

If the question is really about the medication itself - antibiotic choice, dose, side effects, pregnancy safety, or interactions - stay with the urinary complaint here long enough to identify it, then hand off to [Medications: Basic Pharmacology & Safe Use](../medications.html).

### Symptoms

**Cystitis (bladder infection):**
- Dysuria (pain/burning on urination)
- Urinary frequency
- Urgency
- Suprapubic pain (above pubic bone)
- Negative or low-grade fever

**Pyelonephritis (kidney infection):**
- Fever, often high (>38.5°C)
- Flank pain (side, below ribcage)
- Nausea/vomiting
- Dysuria
- Systemic malaise

### Home Care for Cystitis

- **Hydration:** Drink copious water; dilutes urine, reduces pain
- **Urination:** Urinate frequently; emptying bladder prevents bacteria multiplication
- **Cranberry:** Cranberry juice (if available) may have antimicrobial properties
- **Urinary analgesic:** Phenazopyridine (Azo) turns urine orange, reduces pain; short-term use only
- **Antibiotics:** If available, amoxicillin, trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole, or others

### When Home Watchful Waiting Is Reasonable

- Mild burning, frequency, or urgency without high fever, flank pain, vomiting, pregnancy, or inability to urinate can justify a short home-care trial while you hydrate and monitor closely
- "Watch at home" should mean hours to 1-2 days of close monitoring, not ignoring persistent symptoms indefinitely
- For urinary-complaint-first questions, stay in this section first
- If the prompt is explicitly centered on constipation, rectal bleeding, anal pain, painful bowel movements, or trouble passing stool or gas, hand off to the constipation or hemorrhoid guides before using their warning-sign checklists
- Do not import bowel or rectal red flags into a urinary answer unless the prompt also clearly includes bowel or rectal symptoms
- If symptoms worsen instead of stabilizing, move to the serious-infection/red-flag checklist below

### When UTI Is Serious

<div class="red-flag">

**Seek care if:**
- High fever (>38.5°C) with flank pain (kidney infection)
- Nausea/vomiting preventing hydration
- UTI in pregnancy (major risk for serious infection)
- UTI with signs of sepsis (shock, altered mental status)
- Inability to urinate despite urgency (urinary retention)
- Recurrent UTIs indicating underlying problem
- Visible blood in the urine
- Symptoms are clearly worsening or not improving after 1-2 days of hydration and close monitoring
- New severe lower abdominal or bladder pain, or pain spreading into the back or side
- These urinary warning signs are not the same as rectal bleeding, bowel-obstruction, or hemorrhoid warning signs unless the prompt also includes bowel or rectal symptoms

</div>

:::tip
**UTI Prevention:** Women should wipe front-to-back, urinate after sex, stay hydrated, and avoid holding urine.
:::

</section>

## Minor Conditions: Headache, Muscle Pain, Allergies, Insect Bites

### Headache

**Common causes:** Dehydration, tension, infection, eye strain

**Home care:**
- Hydration (drink water; dehydration is common cause)
- Rest in quiet, dark room
- Cool compress on forehead
- Warm compress on neck (tension headache)
- Acetaminophen or ibuprofen
- Massage neck/shoulders

**Seek care if:**
- Severe headache with fever and stiff neck (meningitis)
- Sudden worst headache of life (possible hemorrhage)
- Headache with visual changes, weakness, loss of consciousness
- Headache persisting >72 hours despite treatment

### Muscle Pain & Strain

**Causes:** Overuse, minor trauma, tension

**Home care:**
- Rest (stop activity causing pain)
- Ice (if available) in first 24 hours; reduces swelling
- Heat (after 48 hours) promotes blood flow and healing
- Stretching and gentle movement as tolerated
- NSAIDs (ibuprofen) for pain and inflammation

### Allergic Reactions (Minor)

**Symptoms:** Itching, hives, runny nose, sneezing

**Causes:** Environmental (pollen, dust), food, insect sting

**Home care:**
- Remove allergen if possible
- Antihistamines if available (diphenhydramine, fexofenadine)
- Cool compress for itching
- Avoid scratching (can worsen)

:::danger
**Severe allergic reaction (anaphylaxis):**
Symptoms: Difficulty breathing, throat tightness, tongue swelling, dizziness, rapid weak pulse, confusion

This is a medical emergency requiring immediate epinephrine (if available) and evacuation.
:::

### Insect Bites & Stings

**Mosquito bites:**
- Itch relief: Calamine lotion, baking soda paste, cool compress
- Avoid scratching (scratching causes infection)
- Antihistamine cream if available

**Bee/wasp stings:**
- Remove stinger by scraping (don't pinch; squeezing venom sac injects more venom)
- Wash with soap and water
- Pain relief: Ice, NSAIDs, cool compress
- Antihistamine if available

**Tick removal:**
- Grasp tick close to skin with tweezers
- Pull straight out; don't twist or jerk (head remains if you twist)
- Disinfect bite area
- Save tick if possible (send for disease testing if available)

<section id="red-flags-summary">

## Red Flag Summary: When to Evacuate or Seek Emergency Care

ANY of the following warrant immediate medical evaluation:

<div class="red-flag">

**Respiratory:**
- Severe difficulty breathing (even at rest)
- Stridor (high-pitched breathing sound)
- Coughing blood

**Neurological:**
- Loss of consciousness
- Confusion or delirium
- Severe headache (worst ever) with fever and stiff neck
- Inability to wake or arouse
- Seizures

**Cardiovascular:**
- Severe chest pain or pressure
- Rapid heart rate unresponsive to rest (>120 bpm at rest)
- Fainting or loss of consciousness
- Shock signs: rapid weak pulse, low BP, cool clammy skin

**Abdominal:**
- Severe pain with vomiting
- Hard or swollen belly, guarding, or refusing to walk upright because of belly pain
- Vomiting plus no stool or gas, or fever with one very tender abdominal spot
- Vomiting blood
- Inability to hold down fluids (dehydration risk)
- Bloody diarrhea

**Systemic Infection Signs:**
- High fever (>40°C/104°F) unresponsive to cooling
- Fever with confusion
- Fever with widespread rash (possible meningococcemia)
- Signs of sepsis: fever, rapid heart rate, altered mental status

**Wound/Infection:**
- Spreading cellulitis despite treatment
- Red streaks extending from wound (lymphangitis)
- Pus with systemic symptoms

**Other:**
- Severe bleeding uncontrolled by direct pressure (>10 minutes)
- Suspected poisoning or severe allergic reaction

</div>

<section id="when-to-seek-help">

## When to Seek Professional Medical Care

The guidelines above cover most situations. In summary:

- **Mild illness with stable vital signs:** Home care is appropriate; watch for worsening
- **When watchful waiting stops being reasonable:** If symptoms are getting worse, new red flags appear, or the complaint is not improving as expected, move on from home care
- **Fever >48 hours:** Suggests non-viral infection; consider antibiotics if available
- **Any red flag symptom:** Seek professional evaluation immediately
- **Severe symptoms:** Don't wait; evacuate or call for help

Professional medical care is superior to home care whenever available.
