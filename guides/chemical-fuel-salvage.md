## Fuel Siphoning Safety

Fuel siphoning is a critical survival skill, but improper technique causes poisoning, fires, and explosions. Understanding proper equipment, technique, and hazards is essential for safe fuel transfer.

### Essential Equipment

Never improvise fuel siphoning equipment. Required items include a flexible hose (5/8" inner diameter, food-grade preferred), a squeeze bulb or hand pump (manual siphon pump, never mouth-siphon), containers rated for fuel storage, and a funnel with filter mesh. Keep a fire extinguisher (Type B for fuel fires) within immediate reach.

### The Critical Rule: Never Mouth-Siphon

Gasoline and diesel contain toxic hydrocarbons that cause immediate poisoning when ingested. Even small amounts aspirated into the lungs cause chemical pneumonia, respiratory failure, and death. Symptoms include nausea, dizziness, burning throat, and coughing. Use a hand pump or squeeze bulb starter every single time—no exceptions.

### Static Electricity Precautions

Fuel vapors are highly flammable. Static discharge can ignite gasoline at concentrations as low as 1.4% in air. Ground yourself before handling fuel by touching a metal component on the vehicle. Wear natural fiber clothing (cotton) which dissipates static better than synthetics. Remove plastic pump nozzles that can generate static. Use metal containers when possible and touch them to the vehicle frame to equalize potential.

### Container Selection

Never use glass containers—breakage causes spills and ignition. Never use milk jugs or food containers—they're permeable to fuel vapors and leach plasticizers into the fuel. Use approved fuel containers (Jerry cans with flame arrestor vents, metal drums with bungs, or heavy-duty plastic containers specifically rated for fuel). Red containers traditionally indicate fuel storage. Label containers clearly to prevent accidental use of contaminated fuel for cooking.

### Vehicle Tank Access

Modern vehicles have sealed fuel systems with check valves in filler necks—direct siphoning is impossible and dangerous. Locate the fuel line at the engine (metal or rubber hose connected to the fuel pump). With engine off and fuel line depressurized (wait 10 minutes after last run), carefully disconnect at the quickconnect fitting or by loosening the hose clamp. Insert tubing and gravity-drain into a container placed below the tank level. Alternatively, locate the fuel pump relay (check vehicle manual) and remove it to disable the electric pump—then manually operate the hand pump at the fuel line.

### Underground Storage Tank Access

Gasoline stations have fuel delivery ports buried in the ground near the pumps—marked as "+" symbols or labeled "Gasoline," "Diesel," or "ULP." These tanks typically contain 4,000-10,000 gallons. Access requires a delivery truck connector, making personal siphoning impractical. However, these tanks may remain pressurized and abandoned after the station closes. Never attempt to breach underground tanks directly—risk of explosion, toxic fume inhalation, and groundwater contamination is severe. Industrial recovery equipment and site permits are legally required. In survival scenarios, focus on vehicle tanks and portable drums.

## Fuel Degradation & Stabilization

Fuel quality declines over time through oxidation, microbial growth, and phase separation. Understanding degradation rates and stabilization methods ensures salvaged fuel remains usable.

### Gasoline Storage Life

Pure gasoline without additives remains usable for 3-6 months in sealed containers at cool temperatures. Modern gasoline blended with 10% ethanol (E10) degrades faster due to ethanol's hygroscopic properties—it absorbs water from air, leading to phase separation and corrosion. Ethanol-blended fuel degrades to unreliable condition in 2-3 months without stabilization.

### Fuel Stabilizer Application

Fuel stabilizers (typically containing antioxidants and corrosion inhibitors) extend gasoline life to 1-2 years when sealed properly. Add stabilizer immediately upon fuel acquisition—use per manufacturer's instructions, typically one ounce per five gallons. Stabilizers do not restore degraded fuel; prevention is essential. Common brands include Sta-Bil and PRI-G (marine-grade versions work for all applications).

### Diesel Stability

Diesel fuel is more stable than gasoline and remains usable 6-12 months without additives when stored in sealed containers. Diesel's primary degradation hazard is microbial growth—particularly diesel-degrading bacteria and fungi that form sludge in the tank, fouling fuel lines and injectors. Prevent by keeping tanks completely sealed (excluding air), maintaining cool temperatures, and adding biocide (typically used in agricultural diesel) if bacterial growth is suspected. Discoloration from clear to dark brown or black indicates microbial infection.

### Propane Indefinite Life

Propane stored in properly sealed containers has indefinite shelf life—the container itself is the limiting factor. Rust, corrosion, and seal degradation in the storage vessel determine propane usability. Inspect cylinders for rust (internal rust contaminates fuel), dents on the seam (pressure integrity risk), and leaks (apply soapy water; bubbles indicate escaping propane). Yellow, orange, or dark residue inside cylinder openings indicates rust and contamination.

### Kerosene Exceptional Stability

Kerosene is one of the most stable common fuels, remaining usable 2+ years in sealed containers. No additives required. Kerosene's thermal stability makes it ideal for backup heating and cooking fuel. Store in metal cans or heavy-duty plastic, away from sunlight. Kerosene's main hazard is water contamination (which causes incomplete combustion and carbon monoxide production in heaters).

### Fuel Quality Testing

Inspect color as a baseline indicator: gasoline should be clear to pale yellow, diesel pale gold to amber, kerosene pale yellow. Dark brown or black indicates oxidation or microbial contamination—discard. Smell for off-odors: fresh fuel has a distinctive hydrocarbon odor; sour, varnish-like, or chemical smells indicate degradation. Perform the water separation test by placing small fuel sample in a clear container with water; shake vigorously and let stand. Water settles at the bottom forming a separate layer. Significant water (more than 1% of fuel volume) indicates the fuel absorbed moisture or was previously contaminated. Degraded fuel appears cloudy, has visible particles, or separates into layers with ethanol-blended fuel showing a distinct aqueous layer at the bottom.

## Chemical Identification

Proper chemical identification prevents dangerous mixing, incorrect application, and poisoning. Use labels, safety data sheets, and systematic visual inspection before handling any salvaged chemical.

### Reading Labels

Original labels provide critical information: chemical name (both common and IUPAC), manufacturer, concentration/purity percentage, hazard warnings, and usage instructions. Preserve labels by photographing them or copying relevant information to waterproof storage. Labels include specific hazards (flammable, corrosive, toxic), first aid instructions, and storage requirements. Never discard labeled containers—the information is invaluable.

### NFPA Diamond System

The National Fire Protection Association diamond displays hazard levels in a four-quadrant format commonly found on industrial chemical containers. The top (red) quadrant indicates flammability (0=non-flammable to 4=extremely flammable). Right (yellow) quadrant shows reactivity/instability (0=stable to 4=explosive risk). Left (blue) quadrant displays health hazard (0=no hazard to 4=fatal/acutely toxic). Bottom (white) quadrant lists special hazards: "W" means hazardous if mixed with water, "OXY" indicates oxidizer, radioactive symbol for radiation hazards. Memorize the scale—higher numbers mean greater danger.

### GHS Pictograms

The Globally Harmonized System uses pictograms on container labels: flame icon (flammable), flame over circle (oxidizer), gas cylinder (compressed gas), skull and crossbones (acute toxicity), health hazard symbol (respiratory sensitizer/carcinogen), exclamation mark (irritant), corrosion symbol (skin/eye corrosive), exploding bomb (explosive), health hazard with wavy line (hazardous to aquatic environment). Understanding these at a glance prevents dangerous errors in chemical handling.

### Material Safety Data Sheets (MSDS) and Safety Data Sheets (SDS)

MSDS (older format) and SDS (current standard) documents contain comprehensive chemical information: composition, physical/chemical properties, hazard identification, first aid measures, fire-fighting procedures, accidental release measures, handling and storage, exposure controls, stability and reactivity data, toxicological information, ecological information, and disposal recommendations. Find SDS sheets at industrial supply stores, warehouses, factories, and online databases (manufacturers publish them freely). Photograph or copy critical sections when removing chemicals from original storage. Focus on hazard classification, compatible/incompatible materials, storage temperature/conditions, and emergency procedures.

### Unknown Chemicals: Never Guess

If a container lacks a label and you cannot identify its contents through appearance, smell, or properties, do not attempt to identify it empirically through testing. Unknown chemicals are survival liabilities. Segregate the container in a separate location away from other chemicals and human habitation. Mark it as "Unknown - Do Not Use." If you suspect its identity (e.g., found in an automotive section of a warehouse), research that chemical category and handle accordingly. Only use unknown chemicals if absolutely certain of identification through multiple verification methods (original location, container shape/construction, residue testing with known reference materials).

### Common Household vs. Industrial Equivalents

Household bleach (typically 3-5% sodium hypochlorite) is the dilute version of industrial bleach (12-15%). Industrial sites contain concentrated versions that require different handling and dilution. Automotive antifreeze contains glycol (ethylene glycol is extremely toxic, propylene glycol less so). Industrial solvents like trichloroethylene or perchloroethylene are far more hazardous than acetone. Agricultural anhydrous ammonia is a compressed gas far more dangerous than household ammonia solution. Research the industrial version of any chemical you identify to understand increased hazard levels in concentrated forms.

## Cleaning Product Chemistry

Common cleaning products have diverse survival applications beyond cleaning. Understanding their chemistry, degradation, and safe use prevents accidental poisoning and optimizes utility.

### Bleach (Sodium Hypochlorite)

Bleach is a primary water purification agent in survival scenarios. Household bleach typically contains 3-5% sodium hypochlorite with pH around 11-13. Use 2 drops per liter of cloudy water or 1-2 drops per liter of clear water; stir and wait 30 minutes before drinking (chlorine smell will dissipate if water is adequately treated). Bleach also disinfects surfaces by breaking down bacterial cell walls. Storage life: bleach degrades to salt water and water within 6 months of opening; sealed containers last 1 year. Dark brown discoloration indicates degradation—still usable for cleaning but unreliable for water purification.

:::danger
Never mix bleach with ammonia (produces toxic chlorine gas that causes instant respiratory damage and death). Never mix bleach with acids (produces hypochlorous acid vapor). These combinations are instantly lethal.
:::

Store bleach separately from all other chemicals in a cool, dark location. Protect from sunlight which accelerates degradation.

### Ammonia (Aqueous Ammonia Solution)

Household ammonia is typically 5-10% ammonia in water with a pH around 11. It dissolves grease and disinfects surfaces. The critical rule: never combine with bleach. The combination produces chlorine gas (Cl₂), a toxic gas that causes choking, coughing, foaming lungs, and respiratory failure—death can occur within minutes of exposure. Ammonia's survival applications are limited compared to bleach (which also disinfects). Store ammonia separately. Degradation: ammonia evaporates slowly from open containers, concentrating remaining solution. Sealed containers remain stable for 1+ year. Aqueous ammonia solutions are less hazardous than anhydrous ammonia (compressed gas form, extreme inhalation hazard).

### Hydrogen Peroxide (H₂O₂)

Available in multiple concentrations: 3% (household medicinal), 6-12% (hair bleaching), 30-35% (industrial), and 90%+ (oxidizer applications). The 3% form is safe for wound disinfection and minor first aid. Higher concentrations are oxidizers capable of ignition and require careful handling. Hydrogen peroxide decomposes naturally—3% solutions remain effective 1-2 years if sealed; higher concentrations degrade faster and may require stabilizers (check labels for acetanilide or similar compounds). For water treatment, 3% H₂O₂ is less effective than bleach but useful as backup: use 35-70 drops per liter (approximately 0.5 mL), stir well, wait 30 minutes. Hydrogen peroxide breaks down to water and oxygen, leaving no hazardous residue.

### Isopropyl Alcohol (Rubbing Alcohol)

Typically sold as 70% isopropyl alcohol in water (sometimes 91% concentrations available). The 70% concentration is optimal for disinfection—it penetrates cell walls better than pure alcohol. Survival applications include wound disinfection, equipment cleaning, solvent for fuel additives, and fire starter (high alcohol concentration burns). Store in sealed, cool locations; alcohol evaporates readily from open containers. Isopropyl alcohol remains stable 2+ years sealed. Never ingest—causes severe intoxication, seizures, and organ damage. In extreme fuel situations, isopropyl alcohol can extend fuel supply (though not ideal for engines). Keep away from flames when using as cleaning agent.

### Lye (Sodium Hydroxide)

A strong caustic base used in drain cleaners (often in crystalline pellet form) and industrial applications. Lye is essential for soap making: animal fat + lye + water produces soap through saponification.

:::warning
Lye causes severe chemical burns on skin contact—it liquefies tissue. Even small contact requires immediate water flushing for 15+ minutes (continue flushing even if pain subsides). Eye contact causes permanent blindness. Never ingest—causes mouth/throat burns and death.
:::

Safety protocol: wear protective gloves (nitrile resistant for short-term exposure) and eye protection. Always add lye to water (never water to lye—exothermic reaction causes spattering). Store in waterproof, airtight containers away from moisture; lye absorbs water and becomes liquid. Shelf life: several years in dry storage. Handle only when absolutely necessary and in well-ventilated areas.

## Paint & Solvent Recovery

Salvaged paints and solvents provide protective coatings, wood treatments, and valuable chemical supplies. Understanding paint types and solvent properties optimizes their survival utility.

### Latex Paint Limited Utility

Latex (water-based) paint contains pigments suspended in acrylic polymer emulsion. It has minimal survival applications compared to oil-based alternatives. Latex paint doesn't provide durable wood protection outdoors—it peels and degrades within 1-2 years. Its primary advantage is low toxicity and easy cleanup with water. In survival scenarios, latex paint's bulk and limited utility make it less valuable than oil-based variants. Shelf life: sealed latex paint remains usable 1-2 years; exposed latex paint dries as the water evaporates, rendering it unusable.

### Oil-Based Paint and Stain

Oil-based paints provide superior wood protection, durable finishes, and superior weathering compared to latex. Compositions include linseed oil or alkyd resin binders with mineral spirits as solvent. These paints seal wood effectively and last 5-7 years outdoors before repainting is needed. Salvaged oil-based paint is highly valuable. Store in sealed containers away from light; oil-based paint remains usable 5+ years sealed (tends to thicken slightly but remains effective). To thin thick paint, add mineral spirits slowly while stirring. Store cans inverted occasionally to remix settled pigments.

### Mineral Spirits

A petroleum-derived solvent essential for thinning oil-based paints, cleaning brushes, and dissolving certain contaminants. Mineral spirits have high evaporation rates—store in sealed containers in cool locations. Useful for equipment cleaning and as penetrating oil. Vapor inhalation causes dizziness and headache; use in ventilated areas. Flammable—keep away from ignition sources. Shelf life: 2+ years sealed.

### Acetone

A powerful, fast-evaporating solvent effective on fiberglass, some plastics, adhesives, and certain resins. Acetone is highly flammable (flashpoint -9°C) and requires careful storage away from heat sources. Inhalation causes dizziness and irritation. Acetone dissolves many plastics (polystyrene and acetate), making it useful for specialized applications but hazardous around certain storage containers. Shelf life: acetone evaporates readily even from sealed containers; use within 6-12 months. In survival scenarios, acetone's primary value is fiberglass repair and resin work.

### Toluene and Advanced Solvents

Toluene is a strong solvent used in some paints, adhesives, and industrial applications. Toluene exposure causes neurological effects (dizziness, confusion, headache) with repeated inhalation. Flammable and requires ventilation. Less commonly salvaged than acetone or mineral spirits. Similar storage and safety protocols apply. Exposure limits: keep inhalation exposure minimal by working outdoors or in ventilated spaces.

### Turpentine

A natural solvent distilled from pine resin, historically used in paints. Modern paints typically use mineral spirits instead. Turpentine has stronger odor and is slightly more hazardous than mineral spirits. Useful for thinning certain paints and oil-based products. Storage and safety similar to mineral spirits.

### Polyurethane and Varnish

Polyurethane and varnish provide hard, protective finishes over wood stain or bare wood. Both provide superior weathering and durability compared to paint alone. Polyurethane (oil-based or water-based) cures to a hard plastic-like finish; varnish cures to a hard resin finish. Both are valuable for long-term wood preservation. Oil-based polyurethane requires mineral spirits for cleanup and thinning; water-based polyurethane uses water. Shelf life: oil-based polyurethane 2+ years sealed; water-based formulations 1-2 years sealed. Storage: cool, dark locations to prevent yellowing (applicable to clear finishes).

### Storage and Ventilation Requirements

All solvents are flammable and require storage away from ignition sources (open flame, smoking, electrical sparks). Store in fire-rated cabinets when possible or in a separate, well-ventilated shed away from living areas. Volatile organic compounds (VOCs) evaporate from solvent containers—extended inhalation causes neurological effects. Never store solvents indoors in living spaces. Keep containers sealed tightly to minimize evaporation loss and vapor hazard. Store away from heat sources and direct sunlight (promotes evaporation and potential autocombustion in certain products). Temperature-stable storage (below 25°C ideally) extends shelf life.

## Lubricant Types & Substitutability

Proper lubrication maintains mechanical equipment function and extends tool life. Understanding lubricant types and their interchangeability prevents equipment damage while maximizing salvaged resources.

### Motor Oil (Automotive)

Motor oil lubricates engine components, cools internal surfaces, and protects against corrosion. Grades: 0W-20, 5W-30, 10W-40 (W indicates winter viscosity, primary number indicates hot viscosity). Survival use: general-purpose lubricant, chainsaw fuel mix (typically 2% oil), equipment maintenance. Store in sealed containers; motor oil remains stable 3+ years sealed but degrades with exposure to air (oxidation). Thickens in cold—warm slightly before use in winter conditions. Disposal: used motor oil contains heavy metals and should not be dumped in waterways or soil.

### Gear Oil and Hydraulic Fluid

Gear oil is thicker than motor oil (higher viscosity) designed for high-load gear systems. Hydraulic fluid is specially formulated for pressure transmission in hydraulic systems. Both are unsuitable as engine oil (incorrect viscosity causes poor lubrication). However, in emergency situations: gear oil can substitute for motor oil at reduced efficiency (use slightly thinner grade). Hydraulic fluid can substitute for motor oil if mineral-based (not if synthetic—incompatible with engine seals). These substitutions are temporary measures only. Shelf life: both stable 5+ years sealed if kept dry and clean.

### Grease

Grease is thickened lubricant designed for bearings and joints where liquid oil would run off. Composed of base oil (mineral or synthetic) thickened with soap (lithium, calcium, complex soaps). Grease remains in place under centrifugal force and temperature extremes better than liquid oil. Survival applications: bearing lubrication, joint protection, moisture barrier. Multiple types exist with different temperature ratings—multi-purpose lithium grease is most common and widely compatible. NLGI grade 1 and 2 (lower numbers indicate softer, more pumpable grease) are standard. Shelf life: 5+ years sealed. Grease can be mixed with motor oil as emergency substitute but provides less effective waterproofing.

### Penetrating Oil

Penetrating oils (such as WD-40 analogs) are thin oils with additives that penetrate corroded joints, reducing friction and allowing movement. Less viscous than motor oil; penetrating oils evaporate quickly. Survival applications: freeing stuck bolts, preventing rust (light protective coating), emergency lubricant for fine mechanisms. Not suitable for engine lubrication or continuous-use bearing protection. Shelf life: 5+ years sealed; volatile components evaporate slowly.

### Substitution Hierarchy

Establish substitution priorities when specific lubricants are unavailable: Motor oil can substitute for gear oil (thinner; less efficient but acceptable temporarily). Gear oil cannot substitute for motor oil (too thick; causes engine wear). Hydraulic fluid (mineral-based) can substitute for motor oil in emergencies. Penetrating oil cannot substitute for continuous-use lubrication (evaporates too quickly). Grease cannot substitute for liquid oil (cannot reach tight tolerances). In reverse: penetrating oil can thin grease into liquid lubricant consistency. Mixing different oil types: avoid mixing synthetic and mineral oils (incompatible additives). Mineral oils of different types (automotive, hydraulic) can be mixed with reduced efficiency. Document any temporary substitutions and restore proper lubricant as soon as practical.

### Vegetable Oil as Lubricant Substitute

Plant-based oils (olive, coconut, canola) provide short-term lubrication but are inferior to petroleum products. Viscosity is lower than proper engine oil; vegetable oils evaporate and degrade when heated. In extreme emergency, vegetable oil can lubricate bearings and joints for limited duration (expect additional wear).

:::tip
Rancidity develops within weeks—vegetable oil goes rancid faster than mineral oil, producing gums that can foul bearings. Use only when no petroleum-based lubricants are available, and plan to replace with proper lubricants as soon as possible.
:::

### Rendered Animal Fat

Animal fat (tallow, lard) provides short-term lubrication similar to vegetable oil, with advantages in cold weather (doesn't thicken as much in freezing temperatures). Animal fat is more stable than vegetable oil but still susceptible to rancidity and microbial growth. Useful for outdoor mechanisms (chains, hinges) in emergency situations. Contains no modern additives (detergents, anti-wear agents, rust inhibitors) that protect engines—never use in internal combustion engines. Proper storage: animal fat requires cool temperatures to prevent rancidity. Shelf life: 1-2 months unpreserved; longer if frozen.

### Proper Storage

Store all lubricants in sealed, dark containers away from light and heat. Mineral oil-based products last 5+ years sealed at room temperature. Synthetic lubricants are more stable, often lasting 10+ years sealed. Opened containers: consume within 1-2 years as oxidation occurs at the surface. Keep containers upright to prevent leakage and minimize air contact at surface. Label containers clearly with lubricant type and acquisition date. Protect from water (emulsification of oil/water mixtures reduces lubricant effectiveness).

## Fertilizer & Soil Amendments

Salvaged fertilizers and soil amendments extend agricultural productivity in survival scenarios. Understanding NPK ratios, safety concerns, and proper storage enables sustainable cultivation.

### Ammonium Nitrate: Security and Safety Concerns

Ammonium nitrate is a nitrogen-rich fertilizer with dual-use applications—it's also an explosive precursor. Law enforcement monitors large purchases of ammonium nitrate products; possession may trigger legal scrutiny in some jurisdictions. From a practical standpoint, ammonium nitrate is also an oxidizer that requires careful storage away from fuels and organic materials. Incompatibility with fuel creates spontaneous combustion risk. In survival scenarios, ammonium nitrate is valuable for nitrogen-deficient soil but requires secure, separate storage. Recommended storage: sealed containers kept completely dry, separated from all fuels by significant distance, in a secure location with controlled access. Health risks: skin contact causes irritation, inhalation of powder irritates respiratory tract. Wear dust masks when handling. Due to regulatory and safety concerns, prioritize other nitrogen sources (urea, blood meal) when available.

### Urea (Diammonide Urea)

Urea is a nitrogen fertilizer containing 46% nitrogen (higher concentration than ammonium nitrate). It's less hazardous than ammonium nitrate—not classified as oxidizer, no explosive precursor concerns. Urea dissolves readily in water and provides available nitrogen to plants. Application: dissolve in water for liquid feeding or broadcast granules before rain. Shelf life: indefinite if kept dry; clumping occurs with moisture exposure (remains effective; break up clumps before application). Storage: sealed, dry location. Environmental concern: nitrogen leaches into groundwater if over-applied—use judicious amounts.

### Potash (Potassium Sources)

Wood ash contains potassium compounds and acts as potassium fertilizer, raising soil pH (alkaline). Salvaged wood ash from fireplaces is valuable for potassium supplementation and pH adjustment (particularly for acidic soils). Wood ash also contains calcium and magnesium. Application: broadcast ash over garden area at 20-50 lbs per 1,000 sq ft (adjust based on soil test results). Do not use ash from pressure-treated wood or contaminated sources. Potassium chloride (muriate of potash) is processed fertilizer with higher concentration; same application and storage protocols apply. Shelf life: wood ash indefinite if kept dry; processed potash indefinite sealed.

### Phosphate Fertilizers

Phosphorus is essential for root development, flowering, and fruiting. Phosphate sources include bone meal (4-13% phosphorus), superphosphate (processed mineral phosphate), and rock phosphate (slow-release source). Bone meal is particularly valuable salvage from butcher scraps—it's organic, slow-release, and beneficial to long-term soil structure. Application: work into soil at planting or broadcast at 5-10 lbs per 1,000 sq ft. Storage: cool, dry location; shelf life indefinite if kept dry (bone meal can attract scavengers—store securely). Rock phosphate requires acidic soil conditions for plant availability—more useful in acidic regions.

### Lime (Calcium Carbonate)

Agricultural lime raises soil pH (makes acidic soil less acidic), provides calcium, and improves soil structure. Essential for acidic soils (pH below 6.0). Application: broadcast at 1-2 tons per acre depending on soil test recommendations (rough estimate: 40-80 lbs per 1,000 sq ft). Lime should be applied months before planting for pH effects to stabilize—don't apply immediately before planting. Quicklime (calcium oxide) is more caustic and reactive; use agricultural limestone (calcium carbonate) instead. Storage: dry location (absorbs moisture, decreasing effectiveness); shelf life indefinite sealed.

### Organic Amendments

Blood meal (12-15% nitrogen, deep red powder) is a rapid-release nitrogen source from slaughterhouse waste. Useful for leafy green production. Application: broadcast at 2-3 lbs per 100 sq ft. Storage: dry location; shelf life indefinite. Fish emulsion is liquid nitrogen/phosphorus fertilizer made from fish processing waste—highly valued as complete fertilizer with beneficial micronutrients. Apply at 1:10 dilution in water. Strong smell limits storage indoors. Shelf life: 1-2 years sealed. Bone meal was previously mentioned; it's crucial slow-release phosphorus source. Compost (well-rotted organic matter) provides balanced nutrients and soil structure—most valuable amendment overall. Salvaged compost-ready materials (vegetable scraps, leaves, grass) should be prioritized for composting rather than direct application to ensure pathogen destruction and breakdown of difficult materials.

### NPK Ratios Explained

Fertilizer packages display NPK ratios (e.g., 10-10-10 indicating 10% nitrogen, 10% phosphorus, 10% potassium by weight). Higher first number indicates nitrogen fertilizer (supports leafy growth). Higher middle number indicates phosphorus (root/flower development). Higher third number indicates potassium (overall plant health, disease resistance). Balanced fertilizers (equal NPK) support overall growth. Specialized applications: leafy vegetables benefit from high-nitrogen fertilizers (20-10-10 ratios). Fruiting crops benefit from higher phosphorus/potassium (10-20-20 ratios). Understanding ratios optimizes limited fertilizer resources in survival scenarios.

### Storage Safety: Keep Dry, Separate from Fuels

Fertilizers (particularly nitrogen-rich and oxidizer types) require storage separate from fuel (gasoline, diesel, propane). Mixed storage creates spontaneous combustion risk. Store in sealed containers in a cool, dry, well-ventilated location away from living areas. Some fertilizers absorb moisture—silica gel packets or lime added to storage containers reduce moisture. Clearly label all containers to prevent accidental use of wrong product. Ammonium nitrate requires particularly secure storage with access controls. Regularly inspect stored fertilizers for clumping, discoloration, or spoilage—discard contaminated materials.

## Storage & Compatibility

Unsafe chemical storage causes fires, toxic gas generation, container degradation, and poisoning. Proper segregation and storage protocols prevent disasters and preserve chemical utility.

### Chemical Compatibility Framework

Chemical compatibility requires understanding how substances interact. Oxidizers (bleach, hydrogen peroxide, fertilizers containing nitrates/perchlorates) must be stored separately from fuels and reducing agents (organic materials, metals, combustible substances). Acidic chemicals (hydrochloric acid, sulfuric acid if salvaged) must be segregated from bases (ammonia, lye) to prevent exothermic reactions and toxic gas generation. Reactive metals (calcium carbide, if encountered) must be stored absolutely dry and separated from all water sources and water-containing substances. Solvents must be isolated from incompatible plastics (acetone dissolves polystyrene and acetate) and kept away from reactive metals.

### Oxidizers Away from Fuels

Oxidizers provide oxygen that accelerates combustion of nearby fuels. Bleach + gasoline creates spontaneous ignition risk. Fertilizers containing nitrates + fuel oil creates explosive mixtures (similar to ANFO, ammonium nitrate/fuel oil explosives used in mining). Simple rule: oxidizers in one secure location, fuels in another, separated by maximum practical distance (minimum 50 feet for large quantities). Prevent cross-contamination by using distinct utensils, containers, and storage areas. Never mix oxidizer and fuel chemicals under any circumstances.

### Acids Away from Bases

Acids (hydrochloric, sulfuric, acetic) and bases (ammonia, lye, sodium carbonate) neutralize each other in exothermic reactions releasing heat and toxic fumes. Bleach (weak base) + ammonia generates chlorine gas (instantly lethal). Lye (strong base) + acids generates heat sufficient to cause boiling and spattering. Store all acids in one secure location, all bases in another, with clear physical separation. Label clearly to prevent accidental mixing. In emergency situations where accidental mixing occurs: evacuate immediately, don't attempt rescue until vapors dissipate, and seek medical attention for anyone exposed.

### Proper Container Types per Chemical Family

Fuel containers: use metal or heavy-duty plastic rated for fuel (no glass, no plastic grocery bags). Paint/solvent containers: original sealed containers are ideal; if transferring, use metal cans or high-grade plastic resistant to solvents. Bleach/ammonia: plastic or glass containers are acceptable for short-term storage (6-12 months); bleach eventually corrodes metal. Lye/strong bases: glass or plastic containers only—lye corrodes metal containers. Fertilizers: sealed plastic or paper bags; moisture protection is critical. Clear containers allow contents inspection; opaque containers protect light-sensitive chemicals. Ensure all containers have secure, sealed lids and are clearly labeled with chemical name and acquisition date.

### Ventilation Requirements

Volatile substances (solvents, gasoline) release vapors that accumulate in enclosed spaces, creating inhalation hazards and fire danger. Store volatile chemicals in well-ventilated areas—ideally outdoor sheds with gaps in construction allowing air circulation. Avoid sealed storage buildings without ventilation. Never store volatile chemicals in basements, attics, or enclosed spaces sharing air with living areas. Vapor concentration above certain thresholds (lower explosive limit, LEL) creates explosion risk—ventilation disperses vapors below LEL. For permanent survival storage, construct a ventilated chemical shed separate from main shelter: roof with gaps, screened windows for air circulation, earth floor for drainage, high shelving to prevent water accumulation around containers.

### Temperature Sensitivity

Temperature extremes degrade chemicals and increase safety hazards. High temperature accelerates degradation (fuel oxidation, solvent evaporation, bleach breakdown). Freezing temperatures can rupture water-based products and affect viscosity of oils. Ideally, store chemicals at 15-25°C in stable conditions. Avoid freeze-thaw cycles which promote container corrosion and seal degradation. For fuels, cooler storage (below 20°C) significantly extends usable life. Propane containers are pressure-sealed; temperature increase raises internal pressure creating rupture risk if containers are defective or improperly filled. Never expose propane cylinders to flame or extreme heat.

## Hazard Recognition & First Aid

Chemical exposure causes rapidly deteriorating conditions requiring immediate response. Recognizing exposure symptoms and providing correct first aid prevents death and permanent disability.

### Recognizing Chemical Exposure Symptoms

Respiratory irritation: coughing, wheezing, shortness of breath, chest tightness. Indicates inhalation of vapors or particulates. Immediate action: move to fresh air, lie down if severe, monitor for progression. Skin contact: redness, burning sensation, blistering, chemical burns (color change, skin loss). Severity depends on chemical concentration and exposure duration. Eye exposure: pain, redness, blurred vision, eye closing. Most serious of localized exposures—chemical burns to cornea cause permanent blindness. Ingestion/aspiration: nausea, vomiting, internal burning, breathing difficulty (if aspirated into lungs). Chemical pneumonia from aspiration develops over hours—initially mild but rapidly fatal. Systemic toxicity: dizziness, headache, confusion, loss of consciousness. Indicates toxic substance absorption into bloodstream.

### Immediate Response: Remove Contamination

For skin contact: remove contaminated clothing immediately (material holds chemical against skin), then flood affected area with copious water for 15+ minutes. Continue flushing even if pain subsides—residual chemical may cause continued damage. Don't use neutralizers (they generate heat, worsening burns). For chemical in eyes: flush with water for 15+ minutes, holding eyelids open to ensure water reaches entire eye surface. Don't rub eyes. For inhalation: move to fresh air immediately, lie down, monitor breathing. For ingestion: do NOT induce vomiting (causes additional damage to mouth and throat during expulsion). Rinse mouth with water to remove residue. Call for emergency medical assistance.

### Fresh Air and Water Flushing

Fresh air is the critical first response to vapor inhalation—moves person away from concentrated vapor source, allowing lungs to clear toxic gas. Water flushing is standard treatment for skin and eye contact—water dilutes chemical concentration and carries away residue. Use copious amounts (aim to flush for at least 15 minutes for serious exposures). Cold water has slight advantage (reduces pain/swelling for burns) but shouldn't delay flushing. If running water is unavailable, use any water source (collected rainwater, stream water, even dew from plants in extreme emergency). The quantity of water matters more than purity.

### Chemical Burns Requiring Medical Treatment

All chemical burns more than 1-2 inches in diameter require medical evaluation (infection risk). Burns involving the face, hands, genitals, or joints require medical attention (scar contracture limits function). Deep chemical burns (appearing black, white, or charred) require immediate medical intervention. Signs requiring emergency care: breathing difficulty after exposure, loss of consciousness, severe pain unrelieved by flushing, signs of systemic toxicity, or burns larger than 10% of body surface. In survival scenarios without medical facilities, chemical burns should be kept clean and infection-free. Watch for signs of infection (increasing redness, pus, fever). Severe burns risk septic shock and death—prioritize prevention through careful chemical handling.

### Carbon Monoxide Risk in Enclosed Spaces

Fuel combustion in enclosed or poorly-ventilated spaces generates carbon monoxide (CO), a toxic gas binding to hemoglobin with 200x greater affinity than oxygen. Symptoms of CO poisoning: headache, dizziness, weakness, chest pain, confusion. Victims may lose consciousness and die without realizing the hazard. CO is colorless and odorless—present without warning. Never operate internal combustion engines in enclosed spaces (homes, caves, tents, vehicles with windows closed). Never store fuels indoors where combustion might occur. Never use charcoal grills or open flames for heating in enclosed areas. Ensure exhaust systems of shelters don't route emissions into living spaces. If CO poisoning is suspected: immediately move to fresh air, lie down, monitor breathing, call for emergency assistance. Extended exposure requires high-concentration oxygen therapy (atmospheric oxygen is insufficient). Survivors of serious CO poisoning may have permanent neurological damage.

## Field Inspection & Quick Assessment

When discovering salvaged chemicals or fuels, rapid assessment determines whether the item is worth transporting or should be left behind.

### Visual Inspection Protocol

Examine container condition first: is it leaking, ruptured, or severely corroded? Discard immediately if it's actively leaking or severely compromised. Check container material: glass may be intact but is dangerous if broken during transport. Metal drums require assessment for internal/external corrosion. Plastic containers: check for cloudiness or cracks indicating degradation.

:::info-box
Quick field assessment prevents wasted effort. A salvage item that's 90% degraded or actively leaking shouldn't be transported. Make rapid judgment calls and leave poor candidates behind.
:::

Observe labeling: is any original label visible? Can you identify the contents from markings, color, or location found? Unknown contents are high-risk—only transport if the potential value justifies the unknown hazard. Contents that require special storage (compressed gases, oxidizers, highly volatile solvents) should be left unless you have dedicated transport and storage prepared.

:::warning
Never transport unknown or uncontainable items. A leaking drum of unknown chemical creates hazard during transport and disposal difficulty at destination. Leave it behind and mark the location for potential future recovery with proper equipment.
:::

### Weight & Transportability Assessment

Can you safely transport the item? A 55-gallon drum of used motor oil weighs ~200 kg. Do you have appropriate transport (cart, vehicle, multiple people)? Can you safely contain a potential leak during transport? A small container of labeled lubricant oil is transportable; a large drum of unknown chemical is not unless you have proper equipment.

### Immediate Segregation

If you transport multiple items, segregate by type: keep oxidizers away from fuels, keep acids away from bases. Transport in separate loads if space allows. This prevents accidental mixing if a container ruptures during transport.

## Chemical Degradation Identification

Chemicals degrade over time, changing composition and potentially creating new hazards. Recognizing degradation helps determine if a chemical is usable or should be discarded.

### Visual Indicators of Degradation

**Discoloration:** Most chemicals have characteristic colors. Gasoline should be clear to pale yellow; dark brown or black indicates oxidation or contamination. Lubricating oils should be amber to dark brown (used oil is darker than new, which is normal); black tar-like consistency indicates severe oxidation. Solvents should be clear; cloudy appearance indicates water contamination.

**Layering/Separation:** Ethanol-blended gasoline exposed to moisture separates into two layers: aqueous phase (water + ethanol) at bottom, pure gasoline above. This indicates fuel degradation and water absorption. Paint or coating that has separated into distinct layers (pigment settled, binder separated) is essentially unusable without significant mixing.

**Viscosity Changes:** Oils that have thickened excessively (won't pour at room temperature) have oxidized. This is normal aging for older oils; still usable for general lubrication but reduced performance. Solvents that have become thick or sticky have evaporated or polymerized—likely degraded past usefulness.

### Odor Assessment

Fuel should smell fresh and hydrocarbon-like. Sour or chemical smell indicates degradation or contamination. Solvents should smell like solvents; chemical or burnt smell indicates degradation. Unusually strong smells often indicate problem—trust your nose. Rancid smell (fatty, rotten-egg-like) indicates bacterial or mold growth in food-based items (oils, hydraulic fluids containing organics).

:::tip
Smell is one of the best chemical assessment tools, but protect yourself. Work outdoors, avoid prolonged inhalation, and trust strong instinctive reactions to dangerous smells.
:::

<section id="disposal-safety">

## Disposal of Degraded or Unusable Chemicals

Not all salvaged chemicals should be kept. Degraded or contaminated materials may be hazardous and create storage risk. Proper disposal prevents environmental contamination and reduces storage burden.

### Safe Disposal Approaches

**Evaporation:** Low-volatility items (paints, oils, viscous materials) can be spread thinly on earth and allowed to degrade naturally over months/years. This works for solvents and oils but requires ample space and time. Not suitable for reactive materials or large quantities.

**Dilution & Neutralization:** Hazardous materials diluted to harmlessness can be disposed of safely. Bleach diluted 1:10 with water, left outdoors, degrades to salt water within weeks. Acids neutralized with baking soda become harmless salts. This requires knowledge of chemistry and caution with heat generation from neutralization reactions.

**Burial:** In survival scenarios with no alternative, hazardous materials can be buried in sealed containers in a remote location far from water sources. This is not ideal but prevents immediate hazard. Mark the location for potential recovery. This approach is appropriate only in true survival situations without disposal alternatives.

:::danger
Never burn chemical waste unless absolutely certain of composition. Burning creates toxic fumes—almost never appropriate. Polyvinyl chloride or chlorine-containing plastics produce dioxins and hydrochloric acid gas. Unknown chemicals may produce explosion hazards or extreme toxicity.
:::

### Long-Term Storage of Degraded Chemicals

If you lack safe disposal options, store degraded chemicals securely and separately from usable items. Use sealed containers, label clearly as "degraded/unknown," store in remote location away from living areas. Revisit periodically to assess whether disposal options have become available.

## Troubleshooting Chemical & Fuel Issues

| Problem | Cause | Solution |
|---------|-------|----------|
| Gasoline won't start engine | Degraded fuel, water contamination, or wrong octane | Drain tank completely, flush with fresh fuel, try with fresh gasoline |
| Diesel is dark/cloudy | Microbial contamination or water absorption | Add biocide, filter through coffee filters, use only for heating |
| Paint won't brush smoothly | Over-thickened, separated, or contaminated | Add mineral spirits slowly while stirring; if unsuccessful, discard |
| Lubricant is very thick | Normal aging or extreme cold temperature | Warm to room temperature; if still thick, classify as degraded |
| Chemical smells very strong/wrong | Degradation or contamination | Move to ventilated area; do not use; consider disposal |
| Container is leaking | Corrosion, rupture, or seal failure | Transfer to new container immediately if contents valuable; otherwise prepare for disposal |
| Unknown chemical won't mix/blend | Incompatible or contaminated | Separate and store individually; do not force mixing |

## Chemical Inventory Management

Maintaining detailed inventory of salvaged chemicals prevents dangerous mistakes and optimizes resource use.

### Labeling & Documentation

Every container must be clearly labeled with:
- **Chemical name (both common and IUPAC if known)**
- **Acquisition date**
- **Original contents information (if known)**
- **Hazard classification (flammable, toxic, oxidizer, etc.)**
- **Quantity**
- **Storage location**

Use waterproof labels; marker on tape works. Photograph original labels if available. Create written inventory (notebook or digital) listing all chemicals, locations, quantities, and hazard classifications. This inventory prevents loss of information and enables safe chemical management.

### Segregation Map

Maintain a visual map or written description of storage locations showing where each chemical type is stored. This prevents accidental mixing during retrieval and enables finding items quickly. Update map whenever items are moved or new chemicals are added.

### Rotation & Monitoring

Monthly: verify all chemical containers are sealed, undamaged, and in correct locations. Check for visible degradation (cloudiness, precipitation, discoloration). Note any changes in inventory quantities (leaks, evaporation). This preventive monitoring catches problems before they become emergencies.
