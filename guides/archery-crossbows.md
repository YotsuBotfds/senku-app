## Quick Material & String Reference

Shared selection criteria for all bow types and crossbow prods. For full bow-specific wood selection and tillering, see <a href="../bow-arrow-construction.html">Bow & Arrow Construction</a>.

**Wood selection priority (all bow types and crossbow prods):**

| Priority | Wood | Draw Weight | Notes |
|----------|------|-------------|-------|
| 1 | **Yew** | 60–100+ lb | Premier bow wood; heartwood in compression, sapwood in tension; rare, slow to season (2+ years) |
| 2 | **Osage Orange** | 60–80 lb | Excellent density and durability; difficult to work; naturally rot-resistant |
| 3 | **Ash** | 40–60 lb | Best beginner wood; straight grain, clean splitting; readily available |
| 4 | **Hickory** | 50–70 lb | Extreme strength and flexibility; handles shock well; harder to work |
| 5 | **Elm** | 40–60 lb | Good flexibility for heavier draws; less common |
| 6 | **Maple** | 30–50 lb | Dense and fine-grained; good for backed bows |

Emergency substitutes: white birch, Douglas fir, red oak — require heavier staves and have shorter lifespan.

**String tuning principles (bows and crossbow prods):**

- String length = bow/prod length minus 3–4 inches
- Brace height target: 4–7" (lower = faster arrow/bolt speed, higher = smoother release)
- Strand count scales with draw weight: 8–10 strands for 30 lb, 12–14 for 50 lb, 16–18 for 75 lb, 20–30 for crossbow prods
- Wax all strings with beeswax monthly; replace when fibers separate or brace height drops more than 1" from original
- Flemish twist construction is the universal method for both bowstrings and crossbow prod strings

## 1\. Wood Selection for Bows

The foundation of bow construction lies in choosing the right wood. Different species offer varying flexibility, strength, and workability. Understanding wood selection is critical for creating safe, reliable bows.

### Optimal Wood Species

#### Yew (Taxus baccata) — The Premier Bow Wood

Yew is historically the best bow wood available and remains unmatched for traditional bow construction. Its unique combination of properties makes it ideal:

-   **Heartwood:** Dense, red-brown, extremely strong in compression, allows heavy draw weights
-   **Sapwood:** Pale, flexible, excellent for handling tension without splitting
-   **Laminate advantage:** The heartwood-sapwood combination naturally creates a composite structure. Heartwood resists compression on the back (non-bending side); sapwood handles tension on the belly (bending side)
-   **Character:** Grows slowly, producing tight growth rings and dense wood; long-lived trees provide large logs
-   **Performance:** Capable of sustained 100+ lb draw weights; excellent cast (energy return)
-   **Drawback:** Rare, expensive, and legally protected in many regions; requires 2+ years to season properly

#### Osage Orange (Maclura pomifera)

-   Dense hardwood, exceptionally strong and hard
-   Natural moisture resistance and durability
-   Capable of 60-80 lb bows if properly selected
-   Found in western North America; relatively affordable if local
-   Difficult to work due to extreme hardness; requires sharp tools
-   Can be brittle; demands careful grain selection

#### Ash (Fraxinus spp.)

-   Straight grain, consistent properties, good workability
-   Strong and relatively flexible
-   Suitable for 40-60 lb bows
-   Readily available in most climates
-   Less durable than yew; requires weather protection
-   Ideal for beginners learning bowmaking

#### Elm, Hickory, Maple

-   **Elm:** Very flexible, good for high-draw-weight bows; less common
-   **Hickory:** Extremely strong and flexible; harder to work; produces very fast bows
-   **Maple:** Dense, strong, fine grain; lighter colored; good for backed bows

### Stave Selection Criteria

Once wood species is chosen, selecting proper staves is essential. A stave is a lengthwise section of a log prepared for bow construction.

#### Requirements for Quality Staves:

-   **Straight grain:** Growth rings should run parallel along the length. Grain deviation reduces strength and increases breaking risk
-   **No defects:** Avoid knots, cracks, dead wood, rot, or insect damage. Even small imperfections can be failure points
-   **Length:** Minimum 5-6 feet for self-bows; allows room for handle (1.5"), limbs, and nocks
-   **Width:** 2-4 inches, depending on bow design; wider staves allow thicker bows requiring less draw
-   **Thickness:** 1.5-2.5 inches; provides material for roughing to proper dimensions
-   **Sap vs. heartwood:** For yew, preserve the sap-heartwood boundary; for other woods, select heartwood-dominant staves

:::warning
**Critical Inspection Rule:** Even minor defects in the stave can become catastrophic failure points under the repeated stress of shooting. If a grain deviation, knot, or crack is visible, discard that stave. A bow failure during draw can cause serious injury to the archer.
:::

### Seasoning and Preparation

#### Air Seasoning (Preferred for Traditional Methods)

-   **Duration:** 1-2 years, depending on stave thickness and local humidity
-   **Method:** Store staves in a dry location with good air circulation; stack with spacers between each stave
-   **Environment:** Keep moisture content between 10-15%; avoid rapid drying which causes checking (surface cracks)
-   **Monitoring:** Weight loss plateaus when seasoning is complete; wood becomes lighter in color

#### Heat Treatment (Faster Alternative)

-   **Process:** Subject green (freshly cut) wood to controlled heat; accelerates moisture evaporation
-   **Temperature:** 160-180°F (70-82°C) for 2-4 weeks; too much heat damages wood
-   **Advantage:** Reduces seasoning time to weeks instead of years
-   **Risk:** Requires careful monitoring; uneven drying causes warping and checking
-   **Wood bending:** Heated wood is temporarily more plastic; can be carefully steamed and bent to reduce future movement

#### Moisture Testing

Use a wood moisture meter if available. Target 8-15% moisture content for bow wood. Without a meter, wood should feel light and dry, with no fresh-cut smell.

### Log to Stave: Splitting Technique

Proper stave extraction maximizes wood quality and minimizes waste:

-   **Radial splitting:** Split along the grain from pith to bark, following the medullary rays (the grain direction)
-   **Technique:** Use a hardwood wedge and mallet; avoid splitting across the grain
-   **Advantage:** Radial staves have straight grain and are less prone to warping
-   **Tool requirement:** A fine-tooth saw, sharp hatchet, and splitting wedges (hardwood or steel)

:::info-box
**Storage Tip:** Store seasoned staves flat or vertically with full support; propping them at an angle causes permanent bending. Keep in a dry, temperature-stable location away from direct sunlight.
:::

## 2\. Self-Bow (Longbow) Construction

A self-bow is made from a single piece of wood, as opposed to a composite bow which uses multiple materials. The traditional longbow is the quintessential self-bow, capable of impressive power and distance when properly constructed.

:::info-box
**For detailed self-bow and tillering instructions** — stave splitting, floor tillering, tiller tree method, draw-weight testing, heat correction, and handle shaping — see <a href="../bow-arrow-construction.html">Bow & Arrow Construction</a>. The sections below provide supplementary reference material.
:::

### Overview of the Build Process

Creating a self-bow involves multiple stages: initial shaping, layout, careful tillering, and finishing. The entire process takes 40-80 hours for a quality bow, depending on experience and available tools.

### Step 1: Stave Preparation and Rough Shaping

#### Initial Assessment

-   Inspect the stave for grain direction, defects, and natural curve
-   Mark the back (non-bending side) and belly (bending side) based on grain orientation
-   For yew, the sapwood should become the belly and heartwood the back
-   Remove bark with a drawknife or scraper; retain sapwood

#### Roughing with Hatchet and Drawknife

-   **Hatchet:** Split the stave roughly to a 2" wide shape; work parallel to the grain to avoid splintering
-   **Drawknife:** Shave the back down to 1.25-1.5" thick; work from handle to limb tip, following grain direction
-   **Belly:** Leave slightly thicker initially (1.75-2"); will be refined during tillering
-   **Taper:** Begin a subtle taper toward the tips; the handle region should remain thicker
-   **Pace:** Remove 1/8" at a time; avoid aggressive gouging which tears grain

### Step 2: Layout and Marking

Careful layout ensures symmetrical limbs and proper weight distribution.

#### Marking the Layout

-   **Total length:** For a 64-70" bow, mark the handle in the center (6-8" long, 1.5-1.75" wide)
-   **Upper limb:** 28-32" from handle end to nock
-   **Lower limb:** 28-32" from handle end to nock (should be equal in length)
-   **Symmetry:** Measure carefully; even 1" difference between limbs causes tilting
-   **Grip area:** Mark with slightly thicker cross-section (1.5" wide, full thickness)
-   **Limb transition:** Mark where the limb begins to taper toward each tip

#### Nock Positions

-   **Location:** Position nocks at the very tip, aligned with the limb axis
-   **Nock depth:** Mark the depth (typically 0.75" deep, 0.25" wide) where the string will rest
-   **Symmetry:** Ensure both nocks align when the bow is viewed down its length

### Step 3: Initial Tapering and Shaping

Before tillering, establish the basic limb shape using drawknife and scraper.

-   **Limb width:** Begin at 1.5" wide at the handle, taper to 0.75-1" at midlimb, then to 0.5-0.75" near the tip
-   **Back shape:** The back should be nearly straight or slightly rounded, following the wood's natural contour
-   **Belly shape:** The belly should have a subtle arch or curve; this is the bending surface
-   **Edge rounding:** Round the edges slightly with scraper or file to reduce stress concentration and improve feel
-   **Asymmetry tolerance:** Perfect symmetry is impossible; accept 1-2% limb length differences

### Step 4: Tillering — The Critical Process

Tillering is the iterative process of shaping the limbs so they bend evenly throughout their length. This is where the bow gains its quality and safety. Uneven tillering causes premature failure, poor performance, and injury risk.

#### Tiller Tree Setup

A tiller tree is an essential tool for visualizing limb bend. Construct one from a wooden post with adjustable measurement points:

-   **Base:** A vertical wooden post (4x4 or 6x6), firmly braced
-   **Marking points:** Horizontal pegs or dowels inserted at regular 2" intervals along the post
-   **Clamping:** A clamp at the top secures the bow's handle to the post
-   **String attachment:** A heavy cord attached to the nock enables drawing the bow incrementally

#### Tillering Process

Tillering follows a progressive draw sequence:

1.  **First draw (shallow):** Pull the string to approximately 2-3" draw; observe which limb bends more
    -   The stiffer limb indicates uneven wood mass or grain stress
    -   Remove wood from the stiffer limb's belly and from its midpoint forward
    -   Never remove from the back unless absolutely necessary
2.  **Progressive draws:** Incrementally increase draw (4", 6", 8", etc.) and reassess bend
    -   Perform drawknife shaping between each test draw
    -   Remove 1/16"-1/8" per section, depending on degree of stiffness
    -   Check for symmetry by looking down the back of the bow
3.  **Bend distribution:** Ensure smooth, even bending throughout each limb
    -   Check specific locations: at 4", 8", 12", 16", and 20" draw distances
    -   The bow should follow a smooth curve; avoid sharp bends or flat spots
    -   Record measurements with a tiller stick or crude measuring device
4.  **Full draw testing:** Once reaching 20" draw safely, make final pulls to intended draw weight
    -   String may be released suddenly if a limb breaks; use a controlled method
    -   Do not allow untested bows to snap back under tension; hold with hand or rope
    -   Listen and feel for creaking or unusual sounds; these indicate failure risk

:::warning
**Tillering Safety:** A bow under high tension is under extreme stress. Never stand directly in front of an untested bow during draw testing. Stand to the side or slightly behind the bow. A limb failure can snap the string and send wood fragments in any direction. Always keep your face away from the potential failure plane.
:::

#### Tillering Mistakes to Avoid

-   **Over-aggressive shaving:** Remove small amounts; you cannot add wood back
-   **Shaping only one limb:** Alternate between limbs to maintain balance
-   **Ignoring the handle:** Ensure the handle transmits force evenly to both limbs
-   **Removing from the back:** This is the compression side; removing wood here reduces bow strength catastrophically
-   **Skipping intermediate checks:** Testing only at full draw misses problem areas
-   **Continuing past safety limits:** If the bow creaks, flexes oddly, or shows splintering, stop and assess

### Step 5: String Nocks

Nocks are grooves cut into the bow tips where the string will rest. Poorly cut nocks cause lateral wobble and string wear.

#### Nock Carving

-   **Width:** 0.25" wide, matching the string diameter
-   **Depth:** 0.5-0.75" deep; deep enough to prevent string slipping, shallow enough to avoid weakening the tip
-   **Shape:** Rounded V-groove; sharp angles create stress points
-   **Alignment:** The nock should be perpendicular to the limb's axis; misalignment causes the bow to twist
-   **Method:** Use a saw, rasp, or hand knife to establish the groove; round smooth with scraper and sandpaper
-   **Testing:** The string should fit snugly with minimal side-to-side movement

#### Nock Reinforcement (Optional but Recommended)

For wood that splinters easily (ash, elm), wrap the nock area with sinew, linen thread, or rawhide to prevent splitting under string tension:

-   Apply hide glue to the tip region and the final 2" of the limb
-   Wrap tightly with thread or sinew in overlapping spirals
-   Allow glue to fully cure before stringing

### Step 6: Finishing

#### Surface Preparation

-   **Scraping:** Use a sharp card scraper or spokeshave to smooth all surfaces; remove roughness without changing bow shape
-   **Sanding:** Light sanding with 120-150 grit; excessive sanding opens wood grain and absorbs finish
-   **Grain raising:** Dampen with water and dry to raise wood fibers; light sand again for smoothness

#### Finish Application

A good finish protects wood from moisture while remaining flexible:

-   **Linseed oil:** Multiple thin coats (3-5); allows natural wood character to show; rub-on application; typical finish for traditional bows
-   **Beeswax mixture:** Combine beeswax and linseed oil in a 1:3 ratio; apply thin coats; provides excellent water resistance and a satin finish
-   **Avoid:** Polyurethane, varnish, or thick modern finishes; these are too rigid and will crack with flexing
-   **Application:** Allow each coat to dry fully; typically 24-48 hours between coats; apply 3-5 coats minimum

:::tip
**Finish Maintenance:** Once dry, every few months apply a light coat of wax to maintain water resistance. A well-maintained finish can keep the bow protected for decades. Neglected finishes crack and allow moisture penetration, which damages the wood.
:::

## 3\. Flatbow Design

A flatbow is distinguished by its wider, thinner limb cross-section compared to a traditional self-bow. Flatbows are more forgiving of wood quality variations and are excellent first bows for beginners.

### Flatbow vs. Self-Bow Comparison

-   **Limb width:** Flatbow: 2-3", Self-bow: 1.5-1.75"
-   **Limb thickness:** Flatbow: 3/8-5/8", Self-bow: 0.75-1.25"
-   **Taper:** Flatbow: minimal taper along limb, Self-bow: gradual taper to tip
-   **Stress distribution:** Flatbow: stress spread over wider limb surface, Self-bow: stress concentrated on narrower limb
-   **Forgiveness:** Flatbow tolerates wood grain inconsistencies and minor flaws better
-   **Speed:** Flatbows generally produce slightly lower arrow velocity due to lower stress density
-   **Cast:** Both can achieve similar cast (energy return) if properly tillered

### Flatbow Construction

#### Design Parameters

-   **Total length:** 54-64" (shorter than self-bows)
-   **Handle:** 6-7" long, 1.5-1.75" wide, 0.5-0.75" thick
-   **Limb width:** 2.5" wide at handle, maintaining 2-2.25" width throughout most of limb, tapering to 1.5" near tip
-   **Limb thickness:** 0.5-0.625" thick throughout, with minimal taper
-   **Target draw weight:** 30-50 lbs

#### Construction Steps

1.  **Stave preparation:** Select a 4-5" wide, 1.5-2" thick stave; wider staves are ideal for flatbows
2.  **Back shaping:** Shape the back first; aim for a nearly flat or slightly crowned profile
3.  **Belly shaping:** Leave the belly relatively flat initially; the bow will develop slight curve through tillering
4.  **Limb width:** Establish the desired width using a template; mark guidelines along the back
5.  **Edge radiusing:** Round all edges moderately; sharp edges create stress points and are uncomfortable to grip
6.  **Tillering:** Tiller as described for self-bows; flatbows often tiller more easily due to their forgiving design
7.  **Draw weight management:** If the bow is too stiff, widen the limbs slightly before resizing them narrower (more material = less stiff initially)

### Advantages and Limitations

#### Advantages

-   Forgiving of wood grain variations and minor defects
-   Easier to tiller; bend distribution develops more naturally
-   Can be made from lower-quality wood than self-bows
-   Short length (54-64") makes them maneuverable in tight spaces
-   Comfortable in hand due to wider limbs
-   Good first bow for learning bowmaking

#### Limitations

-   Lower maximum draw weight compared to similar-length self-bows (30-50 lbs vs. 50-80 lbs)
-   Slightly lower arrow velocity due to geometry
-   Requires wider staves (harder to find in some regions)
-   Wider profile makes transport and storage less convenient

### Flatbow Wood Selection

Flatbows tolerate a broader range of wood species. Acceptable options include:

-   **Ash:** Best all-around choice; straight grain, forgiving, good cast
-   **Maple:** Dense, consistent, fine grain; excellent performance
-   **Elm:** Very flexible, good for heavier draws; somewhat challenging to find
-   **Hickory:** Excellent strength and speed; harder to work due to density
-   **Fruit woods (apple, cherry, pear):** Fine grain, consistent, smaller limb sections; require careful selection

:::info-box
**Beginner Recommendation:** For first-time bowmakers, ash or maple flatbows are ideal. Shoot for a 35-40 lb draw weight, 56-60" length. This weight is manageable for practice while providing enough power for small game hunting. Expect 40-60 hours of work for your first bow.
:::

## 4\. Composite Bow Basics

Composite bows combine multiple materials — wood, sinew, and horn — to achieve superior performance and design flexibility. These bows require more time and complexity to construct but offer exceptional properties.

### Materials and Their Roles

#### Wood Core (Belly)

-   Provides the primary structural component
-   Selected for compression strength and workability
-   Ash, maple, or birch are common choices
-   Requires less stiffness than self-bow wood due to reinforcement from other materials

#### Sinew Backing (Back)

-   **Purpose:** Sinew is extremely strong in tension; applied to the back (non-bending side), it resists the pulling forces generated during draw
-   **Source:** Leg tendons from deer, elk, or moose; separated and dried into sheets
-   **Preparation:** Dried sinew is scraped into thin fibers, then split into strands
-   **Application:** Glued to the bow back in overlapping layers; typically 3-6 layers for war bows
-   **Advantage:** Allows thinner, lighter wood cores while maintaining strength
-   **Mechanics:** Sinew actually absorbs and stores energy more efficiently than wood in tension

#### Horn Belly (Compression side)

-   **Purpose:** Horn is stronger in compression than wood; applied to the belly (bending side), it absorbs compressive stresses
-   **Source:** Water buffalo horn, cattle horn, or antelope horn; split into thin plates
-   **Preparation:** Raw horn is heated, flattened, and ground thin (1/8-3/16" thick)
-   **Application:** Glued directly to the wood belly; one to two layers depending on bow design
-   **Advantage:** Dramatically increases compression strength; allows very short, powerful bows
-   **Challenge:** Horn is brittle and can crack if the bow is overbent or struck

### Composite Bow Construction Overview

A basic composite bow follows this assembly:

1.  **Wood core:** Shape the wooden limbs as in a self-bow, but leaving them slightly thinner (0.5-0.75")
2.  **Horn belly application:** Glue horn plates to the belly using hide glue; clamp and allow to cure (24-48 hours)
3.  **Shaping after horn:** Carve the horn-wood assembly to final limb shape; horn is harder but workable with sharp tools
4.  **Sinew backing preparation:** Prepare sinew fibers into long strands
5.  **Sinew application:** Glue sinew to the back in overlapping layers; each layer wraps around the limb, with overlap at handle
6.  **Curing:** Composite bows require 6-12 weeks of curing as the glue fully hardens and materials bond together
7.  **Tillering:** Once cured, tiller carefully; composite bows sometimes develop different bend characteristics than self-bows

### Hide Glue — The Critical Adhesive

Hide glue is the traditional and most suitable adhesive for composite bows. Modern synthetic glues (epoxy, polyurethane) are too rigid and will crack as the bow flexes.

#### Hide Glue Preparation and Use

-   **Form:** Sold as granules or sheets; must be dissolved in water
-   **Preparation:** Soak granules in cold water for 30 minutes; warm gently to 140-150°F (60-65°C) to dissolve
-   **Consistency:** Should be smooth, creamy, free of lumps
-   **Application:** Brush onto surfaces to be joined; clamp firmly and hold for full cure
-   **Cure time:** Hide glue sets in 24-48 hours at room temperature; longer in cool conditions
-   **Temperature sensitivity:** Hide glue can soften if the bow is left in extreme heat; this is a drawback for composite bows in hot climates
-   **Reversibility:** If needed, hide glue can be softened with heat and joints disassembled — useful for repairs

:::note
**Ethical Sourcing Consideration:** Sinew and horn come from animal processing. In a survival context, use materials from game you've already harvested or obtained through hunting. Avoid harvesting animals solely for bow materials when other options are available.
:::

### Complexity and Time Investment

Composite bow construction is significantly more involved than self-bows:

-   **Material sourcing:** Finding quality sinew and horn requires hunting, trading, or purchasing from specialized suppliers
-   **Preparation:** Processing sinew and horn into usable form can take weeks
-   **Assembly:** Multiple gluing steps, each requiring curing time
-   **Total timeline:** 6+ months from material preparation to finished, tested bow
-   **Skill requirement:** More advanced than self-bow construction; requires understanding of three different materials

### Performance Advantages

-   **Power:** Composite bows can achieve 100+ lb draw weights in a 48-54" package (compare to 68-72" self-bows for similar weight)
-   **Cast (speed):** The combination of materials allows exceptional energy return; arrow velocity is 10-20% higher than equivalent draw weight self-bows
-   **Durability:** Properly made composite bows can last for decades and handle more abuse
-   **Consistency:** Once cured, composite bows are less affected by humidity and temperature changes than wood-only bows

:::warning
**Safety Caveat:** Composite bows are powerful and potentially dangerous. Never test full draw without proper safety practices. Always use a proper backing system or target, and avoid shooting anywhere near people or animals without clear line-of-sight downrange.
:::

## 5\. Bowstring Making

The bowstring is as critical as the bow itself. A poorly made string causes inconsistent arrow flight, hand shock, and potential bow failure.

### String Materials

#### Historical and Traditional Materials

-   **Linen:** Best all-around choice for traditional strings; consistent stretch characteristics, long-lasting, handles moisture reasonably well
-   **Hemp:** Similar to linen; slightly coarser texture
-   **Sinew:** Excellent tensile strength; historically used in composite bows; requires careful processing
-   **Rawhide:** Traditionally used (often in braided form); less consistent than linen but serviceable
-   **Avoid:** Cotton (stretches and wears out quickly), synthetic cord (too rigid, causes excessive hand shock)

#### Modern Materials (if available in preparedness context)

-   **Dacron:** Synthetic fiber with minimal stretch; very consistent and durable
-   **Fast-Flight:** Ultra-high-performance synthetic; minimal creep; may be too stiff for wooden bows

### Bowstring Calculation

String length is critical. A string that is too long causes low brace height and sluggish performance. A string that is too short creates excessive tension.

#### String Length Formula

String length = Bow length - 3 to 4 inches

-   **Example:** A 68" bow typically uses a 64-65" string
-   **Adjustment:** Shorter strings (bow length minus 4") create higher brace heights; longer strings (minus 3") create lower brace heights
-   **Testing:** Once strung, the bow should have 4-6" brace height; measure from the handle's deepest point to the string

### Flemish Twist Construction

The Flemish twist is the classic method for constructing a strong, durable bowstring. The resulting loop has tremendous strength and consistency.

#### Method Overview

1.  **Thread count:** Determine the number of strands needed based on bow draw weight
    -   30 lb bow: 8-10 strands
    -   50 lb bow: 12-14 strands
    -   75 lb bow: 16-18 strands
2.  **Strand length:** Each strand should be approximately 1.5x the finished string length (excess removed later)
3.  **Grouping:** Divide strands into two roughly equal bundles
4.  **Twisting:** Twist the two bundles together in a tight, consistent spiral
5.  **Loop formation:** At one end, separate several strands and wrap them around the main body to form a loop (for the upper nock); repeat at opposite end for lower nock
6.  **Serving:** Wrap the loop sections and center with thread to reinforce against fraying

#### Step-by-Step Flemish Twist (Detailed)

**Equipment needed:** Linen thread (or hemp), scissors, card or braiding board, wax

-   **Preparing strands:** Measure out equal-length pieces of thread; if using pre-twisted linen, untwist slightly to create parallel fibers
-   **Securing:** Clamp the midpoint of all strands in a vise or to a braiding board; create a center point
-   **Twisting process:** Twist the left bundle clockwise (toward you), then twist the right bundle clockwise; the bundles will naturally wrap around each other
-   **Tension:** Maintain consistent, moderate tension; too tight causes brittleness, too loose causes limpness
-   **Loop at end 1:** When approaching the first end, separate 3-4 strands from one bundle; continue twisting main body while wrapping the separated strands around it to form a loop (approximately 0.75" diameter for the nock)
-   **Loop at end 2:** Repeat at opposite end
-   **Serving:** Wrap the loop sections tightly with serving thread (typically fine linen or nylon) to prevent the loops from unraveling

### String Waxing

Wax protects the string from moisture and friction while increasing durability.

-   **Type:** Beeswax or beeswax-rosin mixture (3:1 beeswax to rosin)
-   **Application:** Rub the wax along the string while twisting it between thumb and forefinger; the friction generates heat and distributes the wax evenly
-   **Frequency:** Apply once after initial construction, then monthly during active use
-   **Benefit:** Properly waxed strings last 1-2 years with regular use; unwaxed strings deteriorate in weeks

### Serving

Serving refers to the reinforced wraps at the loops and the center of the string where the arrow nock contacts it.

#### Center Serving

-   **Purpose:** The nocking point (where the arrow attaches) experiences repeated impact from the arrow nock and finger pressure during draw
-   **Length:** Wrap approximately 1.5-2" of center serving, centered on the string's midpoint
-   **Material:** Fine thread (linen, silk, or nylon), tightly wrapped
-   **Application:** Wrap with firm, consistent tension, each wrap slightly overlapping the previous
-   **Finish:** Tie off at each end using half-hitches to prevent unwrapping

#### Loop Serving

-   **Purpose:** Reinforces the loops at both ends where the string nocks onto the bow tips
-   **Coverage:** Wrap the entire loop section and extend 0.5-1" up the main body
-   **Technique:** Wrap in tight spirals; each wrap should slightly overlap the previous

:::info-box
**String Testing:** Before using a newly made string, pull it by hand to full draw several times without the bow. This settles the fibers and can reveal weak points. The string should feel smooth and consistent; any rough spots or knots indicate potential failure points.
:::

### String Replacement and Maintenance

-   **Lifespan:** Well-maintained strings last 1-3 years with regular use
-   **Inspection:** Check for fraying, discoloration, or loose fibers; replace immediately if any section separates
-   **Unstringing:** Remove the string when not shooting; stored tension weakens wood and string over time
-   **Storage:** Keep spare strings in a dry location; wax prevents mildew and extends shelf life indefinitely

## 6\. Arrow Construction

:::info-box
**Arrow queries route here first, but deeper coverage lives in <a href="../bow-arrow-construction.html">Bow & Arrow Construction</a>.** For arrow shaft straightening, spine matching, fletching attachment, nock cutting, point hafting (stone, bone, forged), and arrow assembly testing, use that guide. The summary below covers key specifications for reference when matching bolts and arrows to a crossbow or bow.
:::

Arrows must match the bow's specifications to fly accurately and safely. Mismatched arrows cause poor accuracy, erratic flight, and potential injury.

### Arrow Shaft Selection

#### Wood Selection

-   **Straight-grained hardwoods:** Birch, poplar, cedar, or willow; must have absolutely straight grain with no knots
-   **Properties:** Light weight, consistent diameter, good penetration
-   **Processing:** Cut from a suitable tree branch or log; the shaft is then straightened, spined, and shaped
-   **Straightening:** Green wood can be steamed and bent straight; if warped after drying, heat gently and adjust

#### Shaft Dimensions

-   **Length:** 28-32" typical; should be 1-2" longer than the archer's draw length (test with arrows drawn to the corner of the mouth)
-   **Diameter:** 5/16" to 3/8" for most bows; heavier bows require thicker shafts
-   **Consistency:** Shaft diameter should be uniform along its length (within 1/32"); tapered shafts reduce accuracy
-   **Weight:** Arrows should be roughly consistent; variation between arrows causes inconsistent flight

### Spine and Spine Matching

Spine refers to the stiffness of the shaft. This is critical: arrows must have the correct spine for the bow's draw weight.

#### Understanding Spine

-   **Definition:** Spine is measured as the deflection (in inches) of a shaft suspended at its ends under a specific load
-   **Measurement standard:** Typically, a 28" shaft suspended on two points 28" apart with a 2 lb weight at the center; deflection is measured in 1/16" increments
-   **Notation:** Arrow spine is expressed as a decimal (e.g., 0.50 means 0.50" deflection) or in spine units

#### Spine Selection for Draw Weight

As a general rule, heavier bows require stiffer (higher spine number) arrows:

-   **20-30 lb bow:** 0.70-0.80 spine
-   **30-40 lb bow:** 0.50-0.65 spine
-   **40-50 lb bow:** 0.40-0.50 spine
-   **50-70 lb bow:** 0.30-0.40 spine
-   **70+ lb bow:** 0.20-0.30 spine

**Testing:** The best way to determine correct spine is to make test arrows and shoot them. Arrows that are too stiff will fly to one side; arrows that are too flexible will veer the opposite direction and may wobble dangerously in flight.

### Fletching

Fletching are the feathers (or vanes) that stabilize the arrow during flight. Proper fletching is essential for accuracy.

#### Feather Selection

-   **Source:** Turkey (best for traditional arrows; larger, more durable feathers), goose, duck, or other large birds
-   **Preparation:** Feathers are split lengthwise down the quill; the vane (the flat portion) is separated and trimmed
-   **Size:** 4-5" long vanes for hunting arrows; smaller vanes (2-3") for target arrows
-   **Hand:** Right-hand feathers come from the right wing of a bird; left-hand from the left wing. For a given arrow, all three vanes should be from the same hand

#### Fletching Configuration

-   **Number:** Traditionally three feathers per arrow, arranged 120° apart around the shaft
-   **Angle:** Feathers are typically canted at a slight angle (3-5°) to the shaft, which causes the arrow to spin during flight for stabilization
-   **Cock feather:** One feather (typically a different color or marking) is oriented perpendicular to the nocking slot; this is the "cock feather" and should be checked first
-   **Hen feathers:** The other two feathers are arranged 120° apart from the cock feather and from each other

#### Attachment Method

-   **Adhesive:** Hide glue is traditional and effective; modern archery uses fletching cement (synthetic adhesive)
-   **Process:** The quill of the feather is glued to the shaft with a thin, consistent layer of adhesive
-   **Clamping:** The feather is held at the correct angle and position using a fletching jig; left to cure for 24 hours
-   **Wrapping:** Optional: after glue cures, wrap fine thread around the quill for reinforcement, especially near the nock

:::tip
**Arrow Construction Best Practice:** Keep all components of a single arrow matched: same weight shafts, same source feathers, same point weight. This consistency dramatically improves accuracy and flight stability. Mark matched sets so they stay together throughout the arrow's life.
:::

### Arrow Nocks

The nock is the notch at the end of the arrow that fits onto the bowstring.

#### Self-Nock (Traditional)

-   **Method:** Cut a small slot directly into the shaft end using a saw or knife
-   **Dimensions:** Approximately 0.25" wide and 0.5" deep
-   **Shape:** Rounded V-groove; sharp angles weaken the nock
-   **Advantage:** Simple, no additional materials needed
-   **Disadvantage:** The slot can split under repeated stress; wood nocks are less durable than reinforced nocks

#### Reinforced Nock

-   **Material:** Horn or bone nock insert, glued into a cavity at the arrow's end
-   **Advantage:** Much more durable; lasts for hundreds of shots
-   **Preparation:** Drill a small cavity at the shaft end; fit and glue the horn nock into place
-   **Shaping:** Once glued, the slot in the nock is shaped to fit the string

### Arrow Points

Arrow points vary based on intended use and have significant impact on performance and lethality.

#### Field Points (Target)

-   **Purpose:** Target shooting practice
-   **Design:** Small, aerodynamic tip; minimal weight
-   **Advantage:** Easy to remove from targets; reusable
-   **Construction:** Can be forged from steel, cast, or carved from bone

#### Broadheads (Hunting)

-   **Purpose:** Game hunting; designed for maximum tissue damage
-   **Design:** Large, sharp blades arranged to create a wide cutting edge
-   **Types:**
    -   Two-blade: Simple, fast to make, but narrower cutting surface
    -   Three-blade: Balanced design, most common for traditional hunting
    -   Four-blade: Wider cutting surface for larger game
-   **Sharpness:** Broadheads must be extremely sharp to be effective; a dull broadhead is less lethal than a sharp field point
-   **Weight:** Heavier than field points (100-150 grains typical); requires spine adjustment
-   **Flight characteristics:** Broadheads don't fly as fast or as straight as field points due to their wider profile

#### Bodkin Points (Armor Piercing)

-   **Purpose:** Penetration of armor or thick hide
-   **Design:** Small, sharp, hardened point; no cutting blades
-   **Historical use:** Medieval crossbow bolts were often fitted with bodkin points for penetrating plate armor
-   **Effectiveness:** Extremely effective for armor penetration but cause less tissue damage than broadheads

#### Point Construction

##### Forged Steel Points

-   **Method:** Heat a steel rod or flatstock until glowing; shape with hammer on an anvil
-   **Shaft socket:** Create a socket at the base that tapers to match the arrow shaft diameter
-   **Attachment:** The socket is pushed onto the arrow shaft and glued with hide glue
-   **Advantage:** Steel points are durable and readily sharpenable

##### Flint Knapped Points

-   **Method:** Chip flint stone into a sharp point using a hammerstone or bone tool
-   **Advantage:** Extremely sharp; ancient technology requiring no metal
-   **Disadvantage:** Brittle; break with impact; require frequent replacement
-   **Attachment:** A notch or groove holds the point in place; glued with pitch or hide glue

##### Bone Points

-   **Material:** Bone from large game animals (elk, moose, deer)
-   **Preparation:** Bone is carved or filed to shape; extremely sharp edges can be achieved
-   **Durability:** Less durable than steel; can break or splinter
-   **Effectiveness:** Excellent for small to medium game

:::info-box
**Arrow Weight Consideration:** Heavier arrows (with heavier points) fly slower but retain energy better over distance. Lighter arrows fly faster but lose energy quickly. A well-matched arrow-point combination for your draw weight will provide the best balance of speed, accuracy, and penetration. Test different configurations to find optimal performance.
:::

### Arrow Assembly Checklist

-   Shaft: straight, proper diameter, proper length
-   Spine: matched to bow draw weight
-   Fletching: three feathers, 120° apart, proper cant, securely glued
-   Nock: properly sized for string, smooth, not splitting
-   Point: sharp, securely attached, proper weight for draw
-   Balance: arrows should be roughly equal in weight (within 1-2 grains)
-   Function: test by shooting into a target; check for wobble or veer

## 7\. Crossbow Construction

A crossbow is a mechanized bow, using a trigger mechanism to hold and release the bowstring. Crossbows are powerful, accurate, and relatively forgiving compared to handbows, making them effective weapons despite lower efficiency.

### Crossbow Design and Components

#### Major Components

-   **Stock (Tiller):** The wooden or wooden-reinforced frame that holds all components
-   **Prod:** The crossbow equivalent of the bow; the spring-like device that launches the bolt
-   **Trigger mechanism:** Releases the bowstring to launch the bolt
-   **String:** Heavy, short string optimized for quick-release characteristics
-   **Sights or aiming aids:** Optional but useful for accuracy
-   **Stirrup:** Attachment point for cocking device

### Stock (Tiller) Construction

#### Design Specifications

-   **Length:** 30-36" total length, with 18-24" as the main "body" (string-holder portion) and 12" for the rear stock
-   **Material:** Dense hardwood (oak, ash, or beech); must resist warping and splitting
-   **Cross-section:** Rectangular, approximately 1.5-2" wide by 1.5" thick
-   **Rear section:** Shaped for comfort when held against the shoulder; slightly enlarged for grip

#### Construction Steps

1.  **Rough stock:** Begin with a length of timber 4-6" square; saw and plane to desired shape
2.  **Channel for prod:** Route or carve a channel on the top (or front) of the stock where the prod (crossbow bow) will be mounted
3.  **String path:** Ensure the channel is straight and centered; misalignment causes the bolt to deflect
4.  **Rear grip:** Shape the rear portion for comfortable handling; add a slight curve for better grip
5.  **Sight slots or guides:** If desired, create slots for mounting sights or guides
6.  **Trigger mechanism slot:** Create a cavity or channel for the trigger mechanism assembly

### Prod (Bow) Selection and Mounting

The prod is the "spring" that launches the bolt. Multiple designs are viable:

#### Steel Leaf Spring Prod

-   **Design:** A curved piece of steel (similar to a vehicle leaf spring or flatbar)
-   **Sourcing:** Salvage from old vehicles, tools, or specialized crossbow suppliers
-   **Advantage:** Consistent, reliable, no seasoning required
-   **Preparation:** Shape and curve the steel into the desired profile
-   **Mounting:** Bolted or glued into the stock channel
-   **Effectiveness:** Capable of 100-150 lb draw weight with proper selection

#### Laminated Wooden Prod

-   **Design:** Multiple layers of different woods (or wood plus other materials) glued together to create a composite spring
-   **Layers:** A strong, stiff outer layer (ash, oak), a flexible middle layer (yew, elm), and sometimes a backing of sinew
-   **Curve:** Glued while slightly curved to establish spring shape
-   **Advantage:** Can be made from readily available materials; light weight
-   **Challenge:** Requires precise design and gluing to create even bend characteristics
-   **Effectiveness:** Capable of 75-125 lb draw weight

#### Short Heavy Bow

-   **Design:** A short (18-24"), stiff bow mounted horizontally in the stock
-   **Construction:** Similar to a flatbow, but shorter and much stiffer
-   **Advantage:** Uses proven bow-making techniques
-   **Challenge:** Achieving high draw weight in a short length is difficult

### Trigger Mechanism — Rolling Nut Design

The trigger mechanism is the most critical component. A poorly designed trigger causes misfires, safety issues, and unreliable performance. The rolling nut mechanism is the simplest effective design.

#### Rolling Nut Concept

The rolling nut is a rotating metal or wooden part that holds the bowstring. When the trigger is pressed, the nut rotates, releasing the string.

#### Components

-   **Nut:** A cylindrical piece (typically 0.75-1" diameter) with a groove on its perimeter to hold the string
-   **Shaft:** An axle through the center of the nut, mounted in the stock on two bearings or in holes
-   **Trigger lever:** A lever connected to a catch that holds the nut in place
-   **Catch:** A small projection that rests against the nut, preventing rotation until released
-   **Spring:** A spring (or elastic material) that pushes the lever back after firing, re-engaging the catch

![Archery &amp; Crossbow Construction diagram 3](../assets/svgs/archery-crossbows-3.svg)

Figure 3: Crossbow Rolling Nut Trigger Mechanism — Simple, effective design

#### Construction of Rolling Nut Mechanism

1.  **Nut fabrication:**
    -   Turn or shape a wooden or metal cylinder 0.75-1" diameter, 0.5-0.75" thick
    -   Cut a groove around the perimeter to hold the string (approximately 1/8-3/16" deep)
    -   Ensure the groove is centered and smooth
2.  **Axle and bearings:**
    -   Install a bolt or axle through the nut's center
    -   The axle is supported in the stock on two bearing points (drilled holes or metal bearing blocks)
    -   Ensure the axle rotates freely with minimal friction
3.  **Catch:**
    -   Attach a small catch (projection) that rests against the nut edge
    -   The catch must be positioned so that it prevents rotation when the string is under tension
    -   When pulled, the catch must clear the nut, allowing rotation
4.  **Trigger lever:**
    -   Construct a lever hinged in the stock behind the nut
    -   The lever's forward end is connected to the catch via a rod or linkage
    -   Pressing the lever (toward the rear of the stock) pulls the catch clear of the nut
5.  **Spring return:**
    -   Attach a spring or elastic cord to the trigger lever
    -   The spring must be strong enough to push the lever forward after firing, re-engaging the catch
    -   Proper spring tension is critical for safe operation
6.  **String attachment:**
    -   Tie the bowstring to the nut's groove; the string rests in the groove on both sides of the nut
    -   When the nut rotates, the groove rotates with it, releasing the string

### Crossbow String

-   **Material:** Heavy twisted linen or hemp, capable of handling high tensions
-   **Construction:** Similar to bowstrings but shorter (20-28") and much thicker (1/4-3/8" diameter)
-   **Strand count:** 20-30 strands for a 100 lb prod
-   **Length:** Should be short enough that the prod reaches full curve when the string is drawn
-   **Attachment:** Tied to the nut's groove; both ends should be equally tensioned

### Bolts (Crossbow Projectiles)

Crossbow bolts are shorter and heavier than arrows, optimized for the different launch mechanics of a crossbow.

#### Bolt Specifications

-   **Length:** 14-18" (shorter than arrows)
-   **Shaft diameter:** 3/8-1/2" (thicker than arrows)
-   **Weight:** 400-800 grains (much heavier than arrows)
-   **Fletching:** 2 feathers, 180° apart (less drag than 3-fletch arrows)
-   **Nock:** Square-backed nock that engages the string directly (unlike arrow nocks)
-   **Point:** Similar to arrow points but optimized for different ballistics

#### Bolt Construction

-   **Shaft:** Select wood with straight grain; wider diameter than arrows provides stiffness
-   **Nock:** Square-backed nock fabricated from horn or bone, glued firmly to the shaft end
-   **Fletching:** Two large feathers (4-5" long), placed 180° apart for balanced flight
-   **Point:** Blunt field points for target practice, broadheads for hunting; heavier than arrow points due to thicker shafts

### Cocking Mechanism

Crossbows require mechanical advantage to cock (draw) the prod. A lever or screw cocking mechanism is essential.

#### Lever Cocking Device (Goat's Foot)

-   **Design:** A lever arm with two claws that engage the string; hinged near the stock
-   **Operation:** Attach the claws to the string, then lever the arm down and back, drawing the prod and string
-   **Advantage:** Simple, portable, no additional components
-   **Disadvantage:** Requires significant strength; not suitable for very high draw weight crossbows

#### Screw Cocking Device

-   **Design:** A screw mechanism that gradually draws the prod as the screw turns
-   **Operation:** Turn the screw knob repeatedly until the string is drawn to full position
-   **Advantage:** Provides mechanical advantage; suitable for high draw weights
-   **Disadvantage:** More complex; more difficult to fabricate without tools

### Crossbow Performance and Practical Considerations

-   **Draw weight:** 100-150 lbs typical; provides significant power
-   **Bolt velocity:** 150-250 fps typical (faster than arrows from comparable draw weights due to shorter bolt)
-   **Accuracy:** Excellent; more forgiving than handbows due to mechanical trigger release
-   **Effective range:** 50-100 yards for hunting; accuracy degrades at longer ranges
-   **Penetration:** Excellent; superior to handbows for penetrating armor or thick bone
-   **Noise:** Louder than handbows; less suitable for silent hunting
-   **Complexity:** More moving parts mean more maintenance and potential points of failure

:::warning
**Safety Critical:** Crossbows are extremely dangerous. A cocked crossbow with a loaded bolt is a loaded weapon with a trigger. Never point at anyone, never leave cocked and unattended, always use proper backstop when testing. Misfires and mechanical failures can occur; treat as a live weapon at all times.
:::

## 8\. Shooting Technique

Archery is as much skill as equipment. Proper technique ensures accuracy, safety, and consistency.

### Stance

-   **Feet:** Position feet shoulder-width apart, perpendicular to the target (90° angle, with shoulders parallel to the direction of the target)
-   **Weight distribution:** Even weight on both feet; slight forward lean improves stability
-   **Knees:** Slightly bent; locked knees cause loss of balance
-   **Hips:** Aligned with the feet; avoid twisting
-   **Shoulders:** Relaxed; tension in shoulders causes tremor and misalignment

### Bow Grip

-   **Hand position:** Place the bow's handle in the webbing between thumb and forefinger, not in the palm
-   **Pressure:** Grip lightly; excessive tension transmits to the bow and causes erratic release
-   **Wrist:** Keep the wrist straight (not bent backward); avoid gripping with the wrist
-   **Finger position:** Remaining fingers should hang relaxed; they do not grip the bow

### Nocking the Arrow

-   **Procedure:** Hold the arrow horizontally with one hand while positioning it on the string with the other
-   **Nock fit:** The arrow nock should be pushed firmly onto the string, seating it against the nocking point (the center serving)
-   **Cock feather:** Ensure the cock feather (the feather perpendicular to the nocking slot) points away from the bow
-   **Shaft position:** The arrow should rest on the arrow rest or directly on the bow's handle, depending on design

### Draw Methods

#### Mediterranean Draw (Three-Finger)

-   **Description:** The most common historical draw method; the string is drawn with three fingers (index, middle, ring)
-   **Procedure:**
    -   Hook the string with the index and middle fingers above the arrow nock and the ring finger below
    -   Place the arrow across the knuckle of the middle finger
    -   Draw by moving the elbow back, not just the hand
-   **Advantages:** Consistent, forgiving, less hand shock, good for sustained shooting
-   **Disadvantages:** Slower release, more mechanical disadvantage

#### Thumb Draw (Eastern Draw)

-   **Description:** The string is drawn with the thumb (which is bent backward), while the fingers curl over the thumb
-   **Procedure:**
    -   Bend the thumb backward and hook the string
    -   Curl the fingers over the thumb to reinforce
    -   The arrow rests across the thumb knuckle
    -   Draw using the shoulder and back, not the arm
-   **Advantages:** Very fast release, high draw weight possible, efficient power transfer
-   **Disadvantages:** Steep learning curve, requires strong back muscles, more hand shock

### Draw Length and Anchor Point

-   **Draw length:** The distance the arrow is drawn back; should match the archer's physiology (typically 24-32")
-   **Anchor point:** A consistent position where the drawing hand returns to on the face for every shot
-   **Common anchor points:**
    -   Corner of the mouth (most common; provides consistent sight line)
    -   Below the chin
    -   Cheekbone (for higher precision)
-   **Consistency:** The same anchor point for every shot is critical for accuracy

### Release

-   **Clean release:** The fingers should open suddenly and completely, allowing the string to propel the arrow without interference
-   **Finger alignment:** Fingers should be aligned with the string's direction of travel; misalignment causes the string to veer sideways
-   **Thumb release:** The thumb rolls outward and back, clearing the string
-   **Follow-through:** Maintain the anchor point briefly after release; moving the hand before the arrow clears the string degrades accuracy
-   **Rhythm:** Develop a smooth, rhythmic release; rushed or hesitant release causes scatter (inconsistent grouping)

### Follow-Through and Stance Recovery

-   **Bow hand:** After release, allow the bow to move naturally in recoil; maintain a light grip
-   **Drawing hand:** The drawing hand should move slightly back and relax; this is natural recoil
-   **Eyes:** Follow the arrow to impact; watch where it hits to assess accuracy
-   **Stance:** Maintain the shooting stance briefly after release; collapse of the stance indicates loss of control

### Instinctive vs. Aimed Shooting

#### Instinctive Shooting

-   **Method:** Do not consciously aim; instead, use muscle memory and hand-eye coordination to point the bow at the target
-   **Process:** Acquire the target, draw, and release without aiming down the arrow
-   **Accuracy:** Excellent once trained; very fast; allows shooting from awkward positions
-   **Learning:** Requires significant practice (months to years) to develop consistent muscle memory
-   **Advantage:** Natural, quick, intuitive; no need for sights or reference points
-   **Disadvantage:** Difficult to learn; performance degrades under stress or fatigue

#### Aimed Shooting

-   **Method:** Use a sight or aiming system to consciously align the bow with the target
-   **Sight types:** Pinhole sight (aiming with one eye through a small hole), gap shooting (aiming based on arrow point position), or sights with pins
-   **Accuracy:** Excellent; very consistent; easier to teach and learn
-   **Learning:** Days to weeks to develop competence
-   **Advantage:** Fast to learn, very accurate, repeatable
-   **Disadvantage:** Requires a sight mechanism; slower than instinctive shooting in combat

### Range Estimation (for hunting or combat)

Accurate range estimation is critical for successful hunting. Without properly estimating distance, arrows will miss or wound, resulting in suffering of game and failure of the hunt.

#### Methods

-   **Known landmarks:** Use trees, rocks, or other features of known size to estimate distance
-   **Pacing:** Mentally pace off the distance; one pace is approximately 2.5-3 feet
-   **Bracketing:** First estimate if the target is within range (usually 20-40 yards for bows); if yes, proceed; if uncertain, assume out of range
-   **Riflescope range estimation:** If available, use optics to estimate distance
-   **Experience:** With practice, distance judgment improves significantly

#### Maximum Effective Ranges

-   **30-40 lb hunting bow:** 25-35 yards with confidence
-   **50-60 lb hunting bow:** 30-45 yards with confidence
-   **70+ lb war bow:** 50-60 yards with confidence
-   **Crossbow (100+ lb):** 40-60 yards with confidence

:::info-box
**Ethical Hunting:** Only take shots you are confident you can execute accurately. A wounded animal that escapes is inhumane and wasteful. If the range is uncertain or conditions are poor, do not shoot. It is better to let an animal pass than to wound it.
:::

## 9\. Hunting with Bow

Hunting with bow and arrow (or crossbow) is challenging, rewarding, and fundamentally different from rifle hunting. Success requires stealth, skill, and deep knowledge of animal behavior.

### Effective Range and Shot Placement

#### Effective Range

-   **Definition:** The maximum distance at which a bow can reliably kill game humanely, accounting for arrow drop, wind drift, and shooter accuracy
-   **Most self-bows:** 20-40 yards maximum; accuracy degrades significantly beyond this distance
-   **Crossbows:** 40-60 yards; higher velocity allows longer effective range
-   **Factors affecting range:** Bow power, arrow weight, weather, shooter skill, game size
-   **Conservative approach:** When in doubt, assume the animal is beyond effective range and do not shoot

#### Shot Placement

Broadhead arrows must hit a vital organ to kill quickly and humanely. The most reliable vital zones are:

-   **Heart and lungs:** Located in the forward body cavity, behind the front legs
    -   From the side: Aim directly behind the front legs, approximately 1/3 of the body height from the bottom
    -   This zone is approximately 12-18 inches across on most game
    -   An arrow through the heart-lung area causes rapid blood loss and quick death
-   **Broadside shot:** The ideal presentation
    -   Animal is facing perpendicular to the shooter, offering maximum exposure of vital organs
    -   Draw is 20-30 yards when possible
    -   Use the front leg as a reference; aim slightly forward and upward of the rear leg
-   **Quartering toward:** Animal is angled away from the shooter
    -   Aim to penetrate through the entry point and reach the vital zone
    -   More challenging; requires understanding of animal anatomy
    -   A well-placed shot can be effective, but margins for error are smaller
-   **Avoid head shots:** Too much risk of wounding without clean kill; vital areas are small and well-protected by bone
-   **Avoid rear shots:** Arrow may not reach vital organs; animal may run far before dying

:::warning
**Range Safety for Shooting:** Always ensure your shooting area has a safe backstop that will stop arrows. Never shoot toward people, buildings, or animals not intended as targets. Arrows travel long distances; know what is beyond your target. In group hunts, clearly communicate who is shooting and in which direction.
:::

### Blood Trailing and Recovery

Even with proper shot placement, animals may run before expiring. Trailing an animal by blood is essential for recovery.

#### Immediately After Shot

-   **Observe:** Watch where the animal goes; note landmarks
-   **Listen:** Harsh gasping or crashing indicates lung hit; quiet departure may indicate a marginal hit
-   **Wait:** If the hit is marginal, wait 20-30 minutes before trailing to allow the animal to bed down and die quietly
-   **If confident:** For good hits, trail immediately while blood trail is fresh and visible

#### Trailing Technique

-   **Find the first blood:** Look 10-20 feet downwind from where the animal was hit for initial blood droplets
-   **Mark the trail:** Use markers (stones, cloth strips) to mark the trail as you follow it
-   **Visual cues:** Look for:
    -   Blood droplets on vegetation or ground
    -   Disturbed vegetation or broken branches
    -   Hoofprints in mud or soft ground
    -   Drag marks indicating a wounded animal moving slowly
-   **Scent trailing:** A well-trained dog is invaluable for finding wounded animals
-   **Patience:** Follow the trail slowly and carefully; do not rush
-   **Lost trail:** If the trail is lost, mark the last known position and search in expanding circles

#### Recovery Challenges

-   **Poor blood trail:** Some wounds produce minimal external bleeding; blood may be internal
-   **Rain or water:** Washes away blood and scent; makes trailing very difficult
-   **Multiple animals:** A herd may confuse the trail; focus on the wounded animal's tracks
-   **Dark conditions:** Trailing at dusk or night is much more difficult; mark the last known position and return at dawn

### Hunting Methods and Tactics

#### Still Hunting

-   **Method:** Moving quietly through habitat, pausing to scan for game
-   **Approach:** Move slowly, cover the ground in small segments, spend more time looking than walking
-   **Wind direction:** Move with wind toward your face so game cannot scent you
-   **Advantages:** Covers large area, can find game in unexpected locations
-   **Disadvantages:** Difficult and tiring; requires high skill to avoid spooking game

#### Tree Stand Hunting

-   **Method:** Sit in a tree 15-25 feet above ground, waiting for game to approach
-   **Positioning:** Place the stand near game trails or feeding areas; game passes unaware of the elevated hunter
-   **Construction:** Build a simple stand from wood or branches; make it secure and comfortable
-   **Advantages:** Animals typically do not look up; excellent for ambush hunting
-   **Disadvantages:** Limited mobility once in the stand; requires patience
-   **Safety:** A tree stand is dangerous; always use a safety line or harness

#### Ground Blind Hunting

-   **Method:** Conceal yourself in dense brush or behind a constructed blind
-   **Construction:** Use natural materials (branches, grass, dirt) or build a simple wooden frame with vegetation covers
-   **Position:** Place near trails, food sources, or water; ensure downwind of expected approach
-   **Advantages:** More accessible than tree stands; flexible positioning
-   **Disadvantages:** Animals may notice the blind; less elevation advantage

#### Stalking

-   **Method:** Spotting a specific animal and then carefully approaching within shooting range
-   **Approach:** Use terrain to hide; move during the animal's inattentive moments
-   **Distance:** Approach to within 30-40 yards
-   **Advantages:** Exciting and engaging; opportunity to observe animal behavior
-   **Disadvantages:** Very difficult; easily detected; low success rate

### Approach and Concealment

-   **Wind direction:** Most important factor; game can scent a human from 100+ yards downwind. Always position yourself upwind of expected game approach
-   **Camouflage:** Wear neutral colors matching the environment (brown, tan, olive green); avoid bright colors
-   **Sound discipline:** Move quietly; avoid crunching leaves or twigs; speak only in whispers if necessary
-   **Field of view:** Game often detects motion in peripheral vision; move slowly and deliberately
-   **Time of day:** Dawn and dusk are peak activity times for most game; hunt during these periods if possible

### Game Species and Considerations

#### Small Game (Rabbits, Squirrels, Birds)

-   **Effective range:** 10-20 yards; blunt arrows or small broadheads
-   **Shot placement:** Head or vital organ hit required for quick, humane kill
-   **Draw weight:** 20-30 lbs sufficient

#### Medium Game (Deer, Antelope)

-   **Effective range:** 20-40 yards; quality broadheads essential
-   **Shot placement:** Behind the front legs into the heart-lung area
-   **Draw weight:** 40-60 lbs recommended (some jurisdictions require minimum 40-50 lbs)
-   **Arrow weight:** 400-600 grains for reliable penetration

#### Large Game (Elk, Moose, Bear)

-   **Effective range:** 20-35 yards; extreme precision required
-   **Shot placement:** Broadside shot into heart-lung area, aiming well back into the body
-   **Draw weight:** 60-80+ lbs required for adequate penetration
-   **Arrow weight:** 600-800 grains; heavy broadheads (125-150 grains)
-   **Legal considerations:** Many jurisdictions have minimum draw weight requirements for large game (often 50-65 lbs)

:::note
**Hunting Regulation Compliance:** Always verify local regulations before hunting. Draw weight minimums, hunting seasons, allowed methods, and licensing requirements vary significantly by region and game species. Hunting without proper licensing is illegal and unethical.
:::

## 10\. Maintenance &amp; Repair

Proper maintenance extends bow life and ensures consistent performance. Well-maintained bows can last decades; neglected bows fail prematurely.

### Regular Maintenance Schedule

#### After Each Use

-   **Unstring the bow:** Remove the string from the nocks if you won't be shooting again immediately
-   **Inspect for damage:** Check for cracks, splinters, or obvious damage
-   **Wipe down:** Remove dirt, moisture, and debris from the limbs and handle
-   **String inspection:** Check the string for broken fibers or damage; if severely damaged, replace immediately

#### Monthly (During Active Shooting Season)

-   **Deep clean:** Wipe limbs with a soft cloth; use a stiff brush to remove accumulated dirt
-   **Wax the string:** Apply fresh wax to the string to restore water resistance
-   **Finish check:** Look for worn finish; re-apply oil or wax to any exposed wood
-   **Tiller check:** String the bow (without arrows) and sight down the back; it should have the same bend pattern as when new

#### Off-Season Storage

-   **String removal:** Remove the bowstring completely during off-season storage
-   **Wax application:** Apply a protective coat of wax or oil to the entire bow
-   **Dry storage:** Store in a dry location with stable temperature; avoid basements and attics which experience humidity swings
-   **Horizontal storage:** Store the bow horizontally on a rack; avoid propping vertically which stresses the bow
-   **Protection:** Wrap in a cloth cover to prevent dust accumulation and UV exposure

### Common Issues and Fixes

#### Lifts

A lift is when the wood separates from the bow's back or along the lamination of a composite bow.

-   **Cause:** Usually stress from high draw weight, age, or impact
-   **Symptoms:** A visible separation or bulge on the back of the limb
-   **Danger:** A lifted bow is weakened and may break suddenly; it is unsafe to use
-   **Repair:** If small and not separating further, the bow may still be shootable but with reduced draw weight. If severe, the bow should be retired
-   **Prevention:** Unstring when not in use; store horizontally; avoid excessive heat or humidity

#### Cracks

-   **Types:**
    -   Surface cracks: Minor checks in the finish; generally harmless if not growing
    -   Structural cracks: Cracks running through the wood thickness; serious
-   **Repair for surface cracks:** Sand smooth and re-apply finish
-   **Repair for structural cracks:** If crack is short and not near a stress point, hide glue can be applied, the joint clamped, and allowed to cure. This may save the bow but reduces strength
-   **Prevention:** Avoid dropping the bow; protect from impact

#### Loss of Performance (Reduced Power)

-   **Cause:** Wood fatigue, age, or moisture absorption
-   **Symptoms:** Arrows fly slower or to a different point of impact
-   **Check:** Re-tiller the bow to verify limb bend is still even; if uneven, the bow has shifted and may need to be retired
-   **Remedy:** Age and fatigue are inevitable; eventually, all bows wear out

### Splice Repair for Cracked Limbs

If a limb develops a crack that doesn't penetrate fully, or for a small break, a splice repair can extend the bow's life.

#### Procedure

1.  **Sand the crack:** Clean out any splinters or debris from the crack using fine sandpaper
2.  **Prepare glue:** Mix hide glue to a smooth consistency; heat to proper temperature
3.  **Apply glue:** Brush glue into the crack, ensuring complete penetration
4.  **Clamp:** Clamp the joint firmly using C-clamps or a vise; use wooden blocks to distribute pressure and prevent damage to the limb
5.  **Cure:** Leave clamped for 24-48 hours until the glue fully hardens
6.  **Cleanup:** Remove excess glue with a chisel or scraper once partially set; sand smooth once fully cured
7.  **Finish:** Re-apply finish to the repaired area
8.  **Test:** Re-tiller and test shoot carefully before resuming normal use

### String Maintenance and Replacement

#### Condition Assessment

-   **Lifespan:** Properly maintained strings last 1-3 years with frequent use
-   **Warning signs:**
    -   Fraying or loose fibers
    -   Discoloration or darkening (indicates moisture damage)
    -   Fuzzy appearance (fibers separating)
    -   Loss of roundness (flattened or twisted appearance)
    -   Visible breaks or knots
-   **Sound test:** Snap the string with your finger; a healthy string should make a clear tone; a dull thud indicates deterioration

#### Replacement

-   **Preparation:** Have a new string ready before removing the old one
-   **Removal:** Carefully unstring the bow; save the old string for reference (useful if you need to make a replacement)
-   **Installation:** Place the new string's loops into the bow's nocks, starting with one end and working the limbs down to create bow tension
-   **Brace height:** Adjust the string position (or tie additional knots into the string) to achieve proper brace height (4-6")
-   **Testing:** Pull the bow by hand several times at low draw to settle the string fibers; then shoot test arrows

### Re-Tillering After Major Repair

If a bow has had major repair or maintenance and there is any doubt about tiller, re-tiller before resuming normal use.

-   **Process:** Use the same tiller process as initial construction: progressive draws, checking limb bend, making adjustments
-   **Caution:** Go slowly and stop immediately if you observe any unusual sounds (creaking) or limb behavior
-   **Acceptance:** A repaired bow may not perform exactly as it did before; accept reduced performance as the cost of keeping an older bow functional

### Crossbow Maintenance

#### Additional Considerations

-   **Prod (bow) care:** Similar to regular bows; oil or wax the prod regularly
-   **String care:** Crossbow strings receive higher tension; inspect more frequently (monthly if used regularly)
-   **Trigger mechanism:** Ensure the rolling nut rotates freely; apply a small amount of oil to the bearing points if needed
-   **Catch inspection:** The catch can wear with use; if engagement becomes loose, tighten or replace as needed
-   **Spring tension:** Over time, the return spring may weaken; ensure it pulls the trigger forward consistently
-   **Stock inspection:** Check wooden stocks for cracks or warping; re-finish if necessary

:::info-box
**Retirement Decision:** No bow lasts forever. As a bow ages, it becomes weaker, less efficient, and more dangerous (higher risk of catastrophic failure). Signs that a bow should be retired include: persistent loss of performance, visible cracks in the limbs, repeated failures to hold tiller, significant warping, or repeated repairs. When in doubt, retire the bow. A failed bow can cause injury to yourself or others.
:::
