# Physical Rehabilitation & Occupational Therapy

Physical rehabilitation is the systematic process of restoring function, strength, and independence after injury, illness, or disability. In resource-constrained environments without specialized equipment or professional therapists, rehabilitation relies on understanding fundamental movement principles, creative use of available materials, and consistent patient engagement.

## Reviewed Answer-Card Boundary

This is the reviewed answer-card surface for GD-650. Use it for non-diagnostic rehabilitation support intake: what changed, current pain/swelling/weakness, fall risk, activity tolerance, comfort and accessibility aids, stop-activity triggers, and a concise handoff to the clinician, physical therapist, occupational therapist, or other rehab owner.

Start by asking what injury, illness, or disability the person is recovering from; what activity they are trying to tolerate; baseline pain, swelling, weakness, balance, fatigue, and falls; what makes symptoms better or worse; what aids or environmental supports are already used; and who owns the rehab plan. A safe answer may help the user keep an activity tolerance log, note comfort and accessibility adjustments, identify red flags, stop or reduce an activity when symptoms worsen, and prepare a handoff note for the rehab owner.

Do not use this card to prescribe exercises, progressions, manipulation, gait training, return-to-work or return-to-sport clearance, fracture or neurologic diagnosis, wound care, medication or dosing, device fabrication, legal or disability determinations, or claims that an activity, aid, home setup, or device is safe. Stop routine support and hand off urgently for new or worsening severe pain, sudden swelling, warmth, redness, calf tenderness, new numbness or tingling, new or worsening weakness, instability, repeated falls, loss of bowel or bladder control, fever or infection signs, chest pain, shortness of breath, fainting, confusion, or any clinician-provided stop instruction.

<section id="principles-of-rehabilitation">

## Principles of Rehabilitation

Rehabilitation success depends on five core principles that apply regardless of available resources:

**Progressive overload:** Gradually increase the difficulty, duration, or intensity of exercises. The body adapts to demands—without progression, plateaus occur. Week 1 might involve passive range-of-motion (therapist moves the joint); Week 3 might involve active-assisted (patient initiates, therapist assists); Week 6 might involve active-resisted (patient moves against gravity or added resistance).

**Specificity:** Train the exact movement patterns and muscles needed for the patient's goals. If someone needs to walk stairs post-surgery, include stair training. If they need hand coordination for fine motor tasks, practice those patterns early.

**Consistency:** Recovery requires daily or twice-daily sessions over weeks to months. Infrequent therapy produces minimal gains. Establish a realistic schedule the patient can maintain long-term—even 15 minutes daily beats sporadic hour-long sessions.

**Early mobilization:** Begin gentle movement within 24–48 hours of injury or immobilization removal (once medical stability is confirmed). Early motion prevents stiffness, preserves muscle length, and improves circulation. Passive range-of-motion can begin while fractures are still healing.

**Pain-guided progression:** Pain is a signal. Sharp pain or pain that worsens for hours after exercise suggests over-aggression. Mild discomfort during exercise is normal and expected—distinguish between therapeutic stretch/effort and injury. The "no pain, no gain" maxim is false in rehabilitation; pain that increases swelling or function is counterproductive.

<section id="post-fracture-rehabilitation">

## Post-Fracture Rehabilitation Phases

Fracture recovery follows overlapping phases with distinct rehabilitation priorities:

### Phase 1: Immobilization (0–2 weeks, or until X-ray confirms alignment)

**Goals:** Protect the fracture, manage swelling, prevent deep vein thrombosis (DVT), begin unaffected joints.

- Elevate the limb above heart level continuously to reduce swelling.
- Perform active range-of-motion (AROM) on all non-immobilized joints: fingers, toes, shoulders, opposite arm/leg.
- Teach isometric muscle contractions (muscles tense without movement) of immobilized muscles: quadriceps sets, glute sets, calf squeezes. Hold 5 seconds, rest 5 seconds, repeat 10×, 3–4 times daily.
- Monitor for compartment syndrome: severe pain, swelling, tingling, or color changes in fingers/toes warrant emergency re-evaluation.

### Phase 2: Protected Motion (weeks 2–6 for most fractures; longer for weight-bearing bones)

**Goals:** Restore range-of-motion, prevent contractures, begin light strengthening.

Timing varies: non-weight-bearing arm fractures may progress faster than tibia fractures. Consult any available imaging or follow clinical stability (e.g., no pain with gentle movement).

**Passive range-of-motion (PROM):** Therapist or helper moves the joint through its full range, 2–3 times daily, 10 repetitions each direction. This maintains joint mobility without stress on healing bone.

**Active-assisted range-of-motion (AAROM):** Patient initiates movement; helper assists as needed. Gravity may assist (elbow flexion when arm is supported) or resist (hip abduction against gravity). Perform 2–3 times daily.

**Gentle active range-of-motion (AROM):** Patient moves the joint independently within pain-free range. Start with gravity eliminated (e.g., arm on a table sliding side-to-side) before adding gravity. 2–3 times daily, 10–15 reps.

**Isometric strengthening:** Progress to higher-intensity static holds of major muscle groups near the fracture. 10-second holds, repeat 10×, rest, repeat 2–3 sets, 1–2 times daily.

### Phase 3: Active Motion (weeks 6–12)

**Goals:** Restore full range-of-motion, strengthen across the joint, prepare for weight-bearing or functional use.

Imaging confirmation that the fracture is bridging is ideal but not always available. Clinical signs of readiness include reduced swelling, reduced pain with gentle movement, and no increase in pain with light activity.

**Active-free range-of-motion:** Patient moves the joint without assistance or resistance, through full available range. Perform 2–3 times daily, 15–20 reps each direction.

**Strengthening progressions:**
1. **Gravity-eliminated:** Slide arm on a smooth table, lie supine and move leg side-to-side, sit and kick leg forward.
2. **Gravity-resisted:** Stand and raise arm, sit and straighten leg against gravity, walk or climb stairs.
3. **Resisted:** Add bands, weights (improvised or actual), or manual resistance from a helper.

### Phase 4: Functional Training (weeks 12+)

**Goals:** Restore sport-specific, activity-specific, or work-specific movements; build endurance and confidence.

Examples:
- **After leg fracture:** Progress from walking flat ground → stairs → uneven terrain → running/jumping.
- **After arm fracture:** Progress from reaching → lifting light objects → gripping → fine motor tasks.

Introduce variability (different speeds, directions, surfaces) and multi-limb coordination.

<section id="range-of-motion-exercises">

## Range-of-Motion Exercises

Range-of-motion (ROM) is a cornerstone of rehabilitation. Full ROM preserves muscle length, reduces pain, and enables functional movement.

### Shoulder (post-fracture or injury)

**Pendulum circles (gravity-assisted):** Lean forward 90°, let arm hang, and make slow circles. 10 circles each direction, 2–3 times daily. The swinging motion, not active muscle contraction, moves the shoulder.

**Supine shoulder flexion:** Lie flat. Move arm overhead as far as comfortable, then return. If unable to move it yourself, helper gently guides. 10 reps, 2× daily.

**Shoulder abduction (gravity-eliminated):** Lie on your back, arm at your side. Slide arm out to the side (like making a snow angel) as far as possible, then slide back. 10 reps, 2× daily.

**Internal/external rotation:** Elbow bent 90°, upper arm at side, forearm across chest (internal rotation) or pointing away (external rotation). Perform 10 reps each direction, 2× daily.

### Elbow & Wrist

**Elbow flexion/extension:** Bend and straighten the elbow, moving hand toward and away from shoulder. 15 reps, 3× daily.

**Supination/pronation:** Bend elbow 90°, palm up (supine), then palm down (prone). Rotate slowly. 10 reps each direction, 3× daily.

**Wrist circles:** Move wrist in all directions—flexion, extension, radial/ulnar deviation, circumduction (circles). 10 circles each direction, 3× daily. Early-phase patients may need helper assistance.

### Hip (post-fracture, especially femur)

**Supine hip flexion:** Lie flat, slowly slide heel toward buttock, bending the knee and hip. Return. 10 reps, 3× daily. Progress to lifting the foot off the bed if tolerated.

**Hip abduction (gravity-eliminated):** Lie on your back, knees bent, feet flat. Slide one knee outward, keeping foot flat. 10 reps each leg, 3× daily.

**Hip external rotation:** Lying on back, knee bent, rotate hip so sole of foot points outward. 10 reps each direction, 2× daily.

### Knee

**Quadriceps sets:** Tighten the thigh muscle, straightening the knee. Hold 5 seconds, release. 10 reps, 3–4× daily. Effective even during immobilization.

**Hamstring sets:** Bend the knee slightly, pressing the heel into the bed. Hold 5 seconds. 10 reps, 3–4× daily.

**Knee flexion (active):** Bend knee, bringing heel toward buttock. If unable, helper assists. 10 reps, 3× daily.

### Ankle & Foot

**Ankle circles:** Move foot in all directions—plantarflexion (toes point down), dorsiflexion (toes point up), inversion (sole inward), eversion (sole outward). 10 circles each direction, 3× daily.

**Calf squeezes:** Tighten the calf muscle without movement. Hold 5 seconds. 10 reps, 3–4× daily.

<section id="contracture-prevention">

## Contracture Prevention & Management

A contracture is permanent shortening of a muscle or tendon, causing loss of range-of-motion. Contractures develop when a joint remains immobilized or under-stretched for prolonged periods. Prevention is far easier than correction.

:::warning
Contractures can develop within 1–2 weeks of immobility, especially in joints that cross multiple muscles. Once a contracture is established (stiffness present for months), reversal is slow and difficult, sometimes requiring surgical release.
:::

### Prevention Strategies

**Frequent range-of-motion:** Begin PROM or AAROM within 24–48 hours of immobilization. Perform 2–3 times daily.

**Optimal joint positioning:** When a joint is immobilized (cast, splint), position it in a functional position—not the position of comfort. For example:
- Wrist: slightly extended (20–30° dorsiflexion), not flexed or neutral, to prevent finger flexor tightness.
- Hip: neutral, slight abduction, neutral rotation—not flexed (fetal position) or adducted.
- Knee: full extension or very slight bend (0–10°), not chronically bent at 45°.
- Ankle: neutral or slight dorsiflexion (foot not plantarflexed), to prevent Achilles shortening.

**Stretch positioning:** Beyond formal exercise, position the joint to apply low-load stretch throughout the day:
- Place a rolled towel under the wrist to maintain extension while it rests.
- Use a pillow between the knees post-hip injury to prevent adduction contracture.
- Avoid prolonged sitting with knees bent—alternate with standing or lying with knee extended.

**Passive stretching:** Once acute swelling resolves (5–7 days), add gentle sustained stretching. Helper applies low-load stretch and holds 30 seconds, repeat 3× each direction, 1–2 times daily. Stretch should feel mild, not painful.

### Treating Established Contracture

If a contracture develops despite prevention:

**Low-load sustained stretch:** Use positioning, straps, or weights to apply gentle, constant tension for hours. Example: after wrist flexor contracture, position the wrist in extension with a weight (1–2 lb) draped over the hand to encourage lengthening.

**Serial stretching with splinting:** Stretch the joint 5–10 minutes to end-range, then splint in that position for 1–2 hours. Repeat 2–4 times daily. As the tissue lengthens, adjust the splint position.

**Heat before stretching:** Warm the joint with a heat pack or warm water immersion 5 minutes before stretching. Heat temporarily increases tissue elasticity.

**Progressive resistance:** As pain allows, patient applies gentle pressure against the contracture direction. Example: passive wrist extension to end-range, then patient actively tries to extend further (isometric contraction at the shortened muscle) for 5 seconds. Release and re-stretch. Repeat 10× per session.

:::tip
Contractures respond slowly—expect weeks to months of consistent stretching. Reversal rate depends on contracture age; older contractures are stiffer and progress more slowly.
:::

<section id="muscle-strengthening-progression">

## Muscle Strengthening Progressions

Strengthening rebuilds muscle mass and power lost during immobilization or illness. Progression follows a predictable sequence:

### Isometric (Static) Strengthening

**Definition:** Muscle contracts but does not lengthen or shorten; the joint angle remains fixed.

**When used:** Early phases (weeks 2–6 post-injury) when motion is not yet permitted or pain limits movement.

**Technique:** Patient tightens the muscle and holds 5–10 seconds, then releases. No equipment needed.

**Examples:**
- Quadriceps set: Tighten thigh, straighten knee (isometrically).
- Gluteal set: Tighten buttock muscles.
- Bicep set: Bend elbow 90°, pull forearm toward shoulder without moving arm.

**Progression:** Increase hold duration (from 5 to 15 seconds) and repetitions (from 10 to 20).

### Isotonic (Dynamic) Strengthening

**Definition:** Muscle contracts and changes length, moving the joint through a range.

**When used:** Intermediate phases (weeks 6–12) once pain permits active movement.

**Concentric contraction:** Muscle shortens; the joint moves against gravity or resistance. Example: lifting arm up against gravity.

**Eccentric contraction:** Muscle lengthens while contracting; the joint is controlled by the muscle as gravity or resistance moves it. Example: slowly lowering the arm down. Eccentric contraction produces more strength gain and more soreness—use cautiously early.

**Technique:** Move slowly (2 seconds up, 2 seconds down), through full available range, 10–15 reps per set, 2–3 sets.

**Resistance progression:**
1. Gravity-eliminated (arm slides on table).
2. Gravity-resisted (arm moves against gravity).
3. Manual resistance (helper applies resistance).
4. Resistance bands (color-coded for tension).
5. Weights (dumbbells, water bottles, sand bags, or improvised).

### Functional Strengthening

**Definition:** Exercises that mimic real-world movement patterns and use multiple muscle groups simultaneously.

**When used:** Late phases (12+ weeks) preparing for return to activity.

**Examples after leg fracture:**
- Sit-to-stand: Stand up from a chair without using hands (requires hip, knee, and ankle strength).
- Step-ups: Climb stairs or step over obstacles.
- Lunges: Step forward, lower the back knee toward ground, return.
- Squats: Bend knees and hips as if sitting back in a chair.

**Examples after arm fracture:**
- Carrying: Hold and carry objects (water buckets, firewood, tools).
- Reaching: Overhead reaching, reaching across body, reaching to side.
- Gripping and lifting: Progress from light to heavier objects.
- Fine motor: Threading, writing, buttoning clothes.

<section id="gait-training">

## Gait Training After Lower Extremity Injury

Walking is a complex motor task requiring strength, balance, coordination, and confidence. Gait retraining after leg injury follows a systematic progression.

### Phase 1: Weight-Bearing Status (0–4 weeks)

**Non-weight-bearing (NWB):** Patient does not place any weight on the injured leg. Use crutches or a walker. Demonstrate proper crutch technique: move crutches first, then swing the uninjured leg forward, or use a swing-through gait (move crutches, swing both legs past the crutches).

**Partial weight-bearing (PWB):** Patient places minimal weight (e.g., toes touching the ground for balance) on the injured leg. Gradually increase weight over time as healing permits.

**Weight-bearing as tolerated (WBAT):** Patient bears as much weight as pain allows. Typically progresses to full weight-bearing within 1–2 weeks.

### Phase 2: Walker or Crutches (weeks 2–6)

**Walker training:** Safer than crutches for frail or unsteady patients. Demonstrate proper technique: step into the walker with the stronger leg first, then the weaker leg, then advance the walker. Maintain good posture (no leaning forward).

**Crutch training:** Faster gait but requires upper body strength and balance. Teach three gaits:
- **Two-point gait:** Move right crutch and left leg forward together, then left crutch and right leg.
- **Three-point gait:** Move both crutches forward, then the stronger leg, then the weaker leg (good early post-op).
- **Four-point gait:** Move right crutch, left leg, left crutch, right leg (slow but very stable; good for frail patients).

### Phase 3: Cane or Independent (weeks 6–12)

Progress to a single crutch or cane as balance and strength improve. Cane is held in the opposite hand from the injured leg (right cane for left leg injury). Move cane and injured leg together, then move uninjured leg forward.

Independent walking is the goal. Practice on different surfaces (flat, grass, slight inclines) and at different speeds.

### Phase 4: Advanced Gait Training (12+ weeks)

- **Stairs:** Step over step (stronger leg leads up, weaker leg leads down).
- **Ramps and slopes:** Requires hill-climbing endurance and ankle stability.
- **Uneven terrain:** Outdoor walking, grass, gravel—improves proprioception and dynamic balance.
- **Speed and endurance:** Progress walking duration and speed to return to pre-injury function.

:::info-box
**Gait deviations to correct early:** Antalgic gait (limping to avoid pain) is normal early but can become habitual. Encourage upright, symmetrical walking even if slightly painful, to prevent compensatory injury to the other leg. Teach patient to walk with eyes forward, not watching the feet.
:::

</section>

## Upper Extremity Rehabilitation

Hand and arm injuries are common and significantly impact independence. Rehabilitation emphasizes early active motion to prevent stiffness.

### Hand Injuries & Rehabilitation

**Finger range-of-motion:** Begin within 24 hours of injury (with medical clearance). Perform active flexion and extension of each finger, making a full fist and fully opening the hand. 10 reps, 3–4× daily.

**Functional gripping:** Provide everyday objects (cloth, rope, sponge) with varying resistance. Practice grasping and releasing. Progress from soft to firm resistance.

**Fine motor tasks:** Threading, picking up small objects, writing, drawing. These restore coordination and confidence.

**Edema management:** Elevation and gentle massage above heart level to reduce swelling. Swelling limits motion and causes pain—controlling it is essential for recovery.

### Shoulder Rehabilitation (Post-Fracture or Rotator Cuff)

Early phases emphasize pendulum motion (gravity-assisted) and passive range-of-motion to prevent frozen shoulder (adhesive capsulitis), which can develop rapidly in immobilized shoulders.

**Weeks 1–4:** Passive and gravity-assisted motion only. Pendulum circles, supine shoulder flexion, side-lying shoulder abduction (gravity-eliminated).

**Weeks 4–8:** Active-assisted and active motion. Add gentle isometric strengthening.

**Weeks 8–12:** Progressive strengthening and functional activities (reaching, carrying light objects, overhead activities).

**Weeks 12+:** Sport-specific or activity-specific training (throwing, overhead lifting, etc.).

:::warning
Shoulder rehabilitation requires patience. Pain-driven limitation is common in shoulders, and aggressive therapy can worsen pain and stiffness. Progress cautiously, especially in the first 4 weeks.
:::

## Improvised Physical Therapy Equipment

Professional rehabilitation equipment is expensive and often unavailable in resource-limited settings. Effective alternatives can be created from common materials.

### Resistance Bands

**Source:** Inner tubes from bicycle tires or vehicle tires.

**Preparation:** Cut the inner tube to desired length (12–24 inches). Stretch it to test resistance. Tie multiple bands together for greater resistance.

**Use:** Anchor one end to a fixed point (doorframe, tree, beam), hold the other end, and perform resistance exercises. Examples: bicep curls, shoulder abduction, leg extension, leg press.

**Color coding (if available):** Use markers to color-code bands by resistance level (light, medium, heavy) for progression tracking.

### Weighted Objects

**Water bottles:** Fill plastic bottles with water (1 liter = 1 kg). Adjustable weight by filling or emptying.

**Sand or gravel bags:** Fill cloth bags with sand. Label weight for consistency.

**Dumbbells:** Wrap concrete blocks with rope for handles.

**Improvised barbells:** Use a broomstick or pipe with cans or water bottles attached to each end.

### Balance & Mobility Aids

**Parallel bars:** Use two sturdy benches or pipes secured at waist height (3–4 feet apart). Patient walks between, using hands for balance and support.

**Balance board:** Make a rocker from a log or PVC pipe; patient stands on it and works on stability.

**Step platform:** Stack boards or cinder blocks to desired height (4–12 inches) for step-ups and step-overs.

### Positioning & Stretching

**Positioning aids:** Rolled towels, pillows, or foam for optimal joint positioning during rest.

**Traction:** For neck or lower back: rope over a beam with gentle traction weight (5–10 lb) for 15–30 minutes daily.

**Splints:** Custom-made from malleable materials (molded plastic, cloth, tape) to maintain joint position and reduce contracture risk.

## Neurological Rehabilitation Basics

Stroke, spinal cord injury, or peripheral nerve damage causes muscle weakness, loss of sensation, spasticity, or paralysis. Neurological rehabilitation focuses on retraining neural pathways and maximizing preserved function.

### Stroke Recovery

**Timing:** Brain plasticity (reorganization) is highest in the first 3 months but continues for years. Early, intensive rehabilitation yields best outcomes.

**Weakness (hemiparesis):** The affected side (opposite hemisphere) is weaker. Rehabilitation emphasizes movement of the weak limb, resistance training, and task-specific practice.

**Spasticity:** Involuntary muscle tightness develops in some stroke survivors. Manage with frequent stretching, positioning, and gentle prolonged stretching (heat before stretching).

**Recovery strategies:**
- Constraint-induced movement therapy: Place the strong arm in a sling for hours daily, forcing use of the weak arm.
- Repetitive task training: Practice specific functional movements (grasping, reaching, walking) intensively.
- Mental practice (motor imagery): Patient visualizes performing movements, activating neural pathways without physical movement. Effective especially if physical practice is limited.

### Spinal Cord Injury (SCI)

**Paralysis level:** Determine the lowest level of normal function (incomplete vs. complete injury). Rehabilitation potential depends on preserved neural connections.

**Early mobilization:** Prevent pressure ulcers and DVT with frequent position changes (every 2 hours) and compression stockings or wrapping.

**Bowel & bladder retraining:** Establish routine times for elimination. Teach intermittent catheterization if needed (clean, non-sterile technique using boiled catheters in resource-limited settings).

**Preserved function optimization:** Work intensively with preserved muscles to maximize strength and control for mobility and self-care.

**Adaptive equipment:** Wheelchair mobility, hand controls for transfers, modified utensils for eating and grooming.

### Peripheral Nerve Injury

**Nerve regeneration timeline:** Regeneration occurs at ~1 mm/day. Recovery depends on nerve type and injury severity.

**Prevention of contracture:** Use positioning and frequent passive stretching to prevent muscles from shortening while denervated.

**Sensory retraining:** Once sensation begins returning, educate patient on protecting insensate areas from injury (burns, cuts, pressure sores).

**Functional training:** Once motor recovery begins, practice specific movements intensively to reinforce neural pathways.

<section id="occupational-therapy-adl">

## Occupational Therapy for Activities of Daily Living

Occupational therapy (OT) restores independence in self-care, home management, and meaningful activities. OT adapts the environment or teaches compensatory techniques to enable function despite disability.

### Adaptive Techniques for One-Handed Function

After arm amputation or loss of use, a single arm can perform most tasks with training and adaptive equipment.

**Dressing:** Use loops on zippers, Velcro closures instead of buttons, elastic waistbands to reduce fastening needs. Front-opening shirts are easier than pullover tops.

**Grooming:** Secure a brush or comb in a vise or clamp; brush hair by moving the head rather than the hand. Electric razors easier than manual ones.

**Eating:** High-rim dishes prevent food sliding away. A plate guard (curved edge) helps push food onto fork with one hand. Nonskid placemats secure dishes. Weighted utensils for tremor.

**Bathing:** Install grab bars for safety. Use a shower seat to reduce balance demands. Soap on a rope. Washcloth secured to a handle.

**Toileting:** Raised toilet seat reduces hip flexion demand. Grab bars for balance. Bidet or spray bottle if toilet paper use is difficult.

### Adaptive Equipment on a Budget

- **Reachers:** Cut a long branch, attach tongs or pincers to the end for grasping items out of reach.
- **Built-up handles:** Wrap tape or cloth around thin utensil handles to increase diameter for weak grip.
- **Nonskid surfaces:** Cut rubber from inner tubes, glue to dishes or jar lids for grip.
- **Dressing aids:** Long-handled shoehorn (mop handle + spoon glued to the end).

### Cognitive Rehabilitation

After head injury or stroke affecting cognition:

**Memory aids:** Write schedules, use daily checklists, set routines. Environmental cues (labels, alarms) support memory.

**Executive function:** Break complex tasks into small steps. Use step-by-step checklists.

**Attention:** Minimize distractions. Practice focused tasks for short intervals, gradually increasing duration.

**Problem-solving:** Practice solving real-life problems (e.g., "Your stove is broken—what would you do for meals?"). Discuss options and decision-making.

<section id="pain-management-rehabilitation">

## Pain Management During Rehabilitation

Pain is expected during rehabilitation but can limit exercise tolerance and slow recovery. Effective pain management balances comfort with activity.

### Pain Assessment

Use a 0–10 numeric scale: 0 = no pain, 10 = worst possible pain. Ask: "How much pain do you feel right now?" Establish a target pain level for exercise (e.g., "We aim for 3/10 during therapy—if you reach 5/10, we'll back off").

Record pain levels before, during, and after exercise to identify what aggravates pain and what eases it.

### Non-Pharmacological Pain Control

**Positioning:** Place pillows to support limbs, reduce joint stress, and distribute weight evenly. Proper positioning reduces background pain and exercise-related pain.

**Heat and cold:**
- **Cold:** First 48 hours after injury; reduces swelling and numbs pain. Apply ice (or cold water) for 15–20 minutes, repeat every 2–3 hours.
- **Heat:** After acute swelling resolves; increases blood flow, relaxes muscles. Apply heat for 15–20 minutes before exercise to increase tissue elasticity and reduce stiffness.

**Massage:** Gentle soft-tissue massage increases blood flow, reduces muscle tension, and modulates pain. Avoid deep pressure over fracture sites.

**Distraction:** Music, conversation, television, or purposeful activity reduces pain perception.

**Relaxation:** Teach deep breathing (slow, full breaths) and progressive muscle relaxation (tensing and releasing muscles) to reduce pain-related muscle guarding.

**Elevation:** Keep swollen limbs elevated above heart level to reduce swelling-related pain.

### Pharmacological Pain Management

If available, use analgesics strategically:

- **Before exercise:** Administer pain medication 30–45 minutes before therapy to enable fuller participation.
- **Timing:** Use long-acting medications (if available) for continuous comfort rather than as-needed dosing.
- **Escalation:** Start with the lowest effective dose; increase only if pain limits function.

<section id="psychological-aspects">

## Psychological Aspects of Disability and Recovery

Injury and disability trigger emotional responses: fear, anger, grief, depression. Psychological well-being is as important as physical recovery for optimal outcomes.

### Adjustment to Disability

**Acute phase (days–weeks):** Shock, denial, and grief are normal. Validate feelings without reinforcing helplessness. Provide honest, optimistic information about recovery.

**Intermediate phase (weeks–months):** Depression and anxiety are common. Encourage participation in therapy and social activities. Connect patients with others in similar situations for perspective and hope.

**Chronic phase (months+):** Identity shifts as patients adjust to new abilities. Support exploration of alternative goals and meanings.

### Preventing Learned Helplessness

Avoid over-protection. Encourage patients to attempt self-care and mobility tasks, even if slower or messier than before. Success—even partial—is powerfully motivating.

Break large goals into achievable micro-goals. Celebrating small wins (e.g., first time standing independently) maintains motivation.

### Addressing Fear and Anxiety

Fear of re-injury is rational post-injury but can limit recovery if it causes avoidance of beneficial movement. Gradually expose patients to movement through graded activity. For example, after leg fracture, start with weight-bearing on a smooth floor holding two rails; progress to one rail; progress to no rails; progress to uneven surfaces.

<section id="documentation-tracking">

## Documentation and Progress Tracking

Systematic documentation enables objective assessment of improvement and informs treatment adjustments.

### Baseline Assessment (Week 1)

Record initial status:
- **Range-of-motion:** Measure in degrees if a goniometer is available (simple angle-measuring tool). Otherwise, describe: "Knee bends to 45°" or "Full finger flexion but limited extension."
- **Strength:** Rate 0–5 scale: 0 = no movement, 1 = visible twitch only, 2 = movement with gravity eliminated, 3 = movement against gravity, 4 = movement against gravity with some resistance, 5 = normal strength.
- **Functional ability:** "Unable to walk," "Walks with walker," "Walks without aid but slowly," "Walks normally."
- **Pain:** 0–10 scale at rest and with activity.
- **Swelling:** Measure limb circumference (e.g., 3 inches above knee), or describe: "Mild," "Moderate," "Severe."

### Weekly Progress Notes

Every 7 days, re-assess and record:
- Range-of-motion (same measurement method).
- Strength (same scale).
- Functional improvements (e.g., "Now walks 30 feet with cane without pain").
- Pain changes.
- Swelling changes.
- Adherence to home program.
- Modifications made to the exercise program.

### Example Progress Note

*Week 4 (Right ankle fracture):*
- ROM: Dorsiflexion improved to 15° (was 5°); plantarflexion full.
- Strength: Dorsiflexors 3/5 (was 2/5); plantarflexors 4/5.
- Function: Walks 50 feet with crutches and partial weight-bearing; pain 2/10 (was 6/10 at week 1).
- Swelling: Reduced by 1 inch circumference (ankle now 9 inches vs. 10 inches week 1).
- Adherence: Patient reports doing exercises 2× daily, 5 days/week.
- Plan: Advance to full weight-bearing, begin balance training, transition to cane next week.

This documentation enables objective tracking and reinforces progress to the patient, boosting motivation.

</section>

<section id="long-term-disability-adaptation">

## Long-Term Disability Adaptation

Some injuries result in permanent disability despite optimal rehabilitation. Adaptation focuses on maximizing remaining function and quality of life.

### Environmental Modifications

Remove barriers and hazards:
- Ramps or widened doorways for wheelchair access.
- Grab bars in bathrooms, hallways, stairs.
- Lowered shelving and counters for wheelchair users.
- Improved lighting for those with vision loss.
- Accessible storage and organization for those with limited mobility.

### Role and Identity Shifts

Help patients redefine identity beyond pre-injury abilities. A farmer with leg amputation may shift to supervising farm work. A musician with hand injury may teach. Explore alternative meaningful activities.

### Social Reintegration

Isolation is common post-severe injury. Facilitate:
- Family education (how to help without over-helping).
- Peer support (connecting with others managing similar disabilities).
- Community participation (adapted recreation, employment, volunteering).
- Transportation solutions (accessible vehicles or alternatives).

### Ongoing Therapy

Long-term therapy maintains function and addresses new problems. Spasticity worsens over time in some conditions; contractures can return without continued stretching; strength naturally declines without resistance training. Establish a sustainable long-term routine (e.g., 30 minutes stretching + strengthening 5× weekly) and re-assess quarterly.

</section>

<section id="when-to-push-and-when-to-rest">

## When to Push vs. When to Rest

Rehabilitation requires balancing activity with rest. Excessive activity causes pain, swelling, and re-injury. Excessive rest causes weakness and stiffness. Clinical judgment is essential.

### Signs to Push Harder

- Pain decreases over weeks; stiffness is the limiting factor.
- Patient completes prescribed exercises easily; no swelling increase afterward.
- Strength and range-of-motion plateau for 2+ weeks despite consistent therapy.
- Patient is psychologically ready for increased challenge.

### Signs to Back Off

- Pain increases during or worsens for hours after exercise.
- Swelling increases markedly (limb circumference increases >1 cm per day).
- Patient demonstrates signs of re-injury (sharp pain, instability, bruising).
- Fever or signs of infection appear.
- Patient is experiencing emotional distress or depression limiting motivation.

:::warning
Red flag: Sudden increase in swelling, warmth, redness, or calf tenderness suggests deep vein thrombosis (DVT). Stop exercise and seek medical evaluation immediately—DVT is potentially life-threatening.
:::

## Summary

Physical rehabilitation in resource-limited environments relies on understanding fundamental principles of movement, creative use of available materials, and patient engagement. Progressive challenge, consistent execution, pain-guided progression, and psychological support enable recovery despite constraints. Even without specialized equipment or professional therapists, systematic rehabilitation restores function and independence for most patients.
