:::danger
**CRITICAL EXPERIENCE REQUIREMENT:** Building entry and salvage operations are inherently hazardous. Structural collapse, falls, toxic exposures, and other hazards kill experienced salvagers regularly. This guide is NOT sufficient training to safely enter potentially unstable buildings. You must have:

- **Prior experience:** Minimum 20-50 hours of supervised building entry with experienced personnel
- **Structural assessment training:** Ability to identify major hazard categories and make safe/unsafe decisions
- **First aid certification:** Ability to stabilize injured personnel and initiate rescue
- **Team-based operations:** Never work alone; minimum 3-person team required with designated safety officer
- **Acceptance of risk:** Buildings marked unsafe should NOT be entered. No salvage is worth a fatality.

Deaths in building entry occur suddenly and without warning. Delayed collapses kill people hours after initial entry. If in doubt, evacuate immediately and do not return.
:::

## Prerequisites and Risk Assessment

### Required Prior Experience

Building entry should not be attempted without foundational knowledge and experience:

**Minimum training requirements:**

- **20-50 hours minimum experience** in building entry under experienced supervision (not learning on the job)
- **Structural assessment basics** learned through hands-on training, not just reading (visual assessment alone is insufficient)
- **First aid and rescue training** (knowledge of extraction procedures, stabilization, emergency response)
- **Team decision-making protocols** (how to communicate hazards, abort decisions, emergency procedures)

**Recommended prerequisite guides:**

- <a href="../search-rescue.html">Search & Rescue</a> (contains team-based building entry protocols)
- <a href="../first-aid.html">First Aid</a> (necessary for treating injuries from structural failure)
- <a href="../family-emergency-plan.html">Family Emergency Plan</a> (incident command for building emergencies)

### Situations Where Entry Should NOT Be Attempted

The following structural conditions indicate buildings are unsafe. Do NOT enter under any circumstances:

**Absolute contraindications:**

- **Visible collapse or severe structural damage:** Sagging roofs, separated walls, large cracks (>1/2 inch width), floors that appear to sag >2-3 inches
- **Active hazards:** Fire ongoing or recent fire damage, gas smell (methane, natural gas, hydrogen sulfide), water damage indicating structural saturation
- **Post-earthquake or major disaster:** Assume structures are compromised. Wait for professional assessment before entry.
- **Foundation failure:** Severe foundation cracks, building settling on one side, visible gaps between foundation and walls
- **Heavy snow/ice load:** Winter conditions add weight; do not enter structures with existing damage under heavy snow
- **Evidence of asbestos or hazardous materials:** Friable asbestos (deteriorating), chemical spills, pest control treatments visible (pesticide residues)
- **Building lean >1 inch per 10 feet height:** Example: A 30-foot building leaning 3+ inches is unsafe

**Conditional contraindications (requires expert assessment):**

- **Moderate foundation cracking:** Stair-step cracks, horizontal cracks, cracks >1/4 inch width (consult with structural engineer if possible)
- **Previous major fire:** Assess burn patterns; heavily burned structural members may have severely reduced capacity
- **Partial collapse:** One section of building collapsed; will remaining structure remain stable? Unknown.

### Structural Failure Warning Signs

Recognize these indicators of imminent or progressive failure:

**Audible/visible warning signs (EVACUATE IMMEDIATELY):**

- **Sudden cracking sounds** (pop, snap, or groan from structural members)
- **Dust falling from ceiling or upper walls** (indicates shearing or settling)
- **Doors/windows that suddenly jam or won't close** (indicates frame distortion)
- **Floor tilting or sagging visibly as you walk** (weight redistribution, member failure)
- **Water pouring from walls** (structural saturation, weakening)
- **Objects falling** (gravity is pulling down; ceiling may follow)

**Visual warning signs (VACATE BUILDING):**

- **Horizontal cracks in walls** (indicate lateral stress or water pressure; more dangerous than vertical cracks)
- **Cracks wider than at initial entry** (structure is actively failing; dynamic, not static)
- **Buckling or bowing of walls** (lateral force is pushing; wall failure imminent)
- **Gaps widening between walls and floors** (frame is separating; structural integrity compromised)
- **Diagonal cracks (45-degree angle)** spreading across large wall areas (shearing failure beginning)

**Smell/sensory warning signs (LEAVE IMMEDIATELY):**

- **Unusual smells** (burning, chemical, or rotten smell indicates hazardous conditions)
- **Excessive dust or mold spores** (respiratory hazard; structure may be deteriorating rapidly)
- **Visible insect activity or rodent nests** (indicates water damage or decay creating weak points)

### Buddy System Requirements

Never enter potentially unstable buildings without minimum 3-person team:

**Roles:**

- **Structural assessment lead:** Most experienced person. Makes go/no-go decision before entry. Supervises all decisions during operation.
- **Primary entry/search team:** Two experienced personnel who enter and conduct search. Stay together; maintain visual/verbal contact.
- **External safety officer:** Stationed outside with clear sightlines. Monitors team via communication. Initiates rescue if needed.

**Responsibilities:**

- Assessment lead: Responsible for structural safety decision—if hazard level exceeds acceptable risk, refuses entry
- Search team: Report hazards, abide by assessment lead's decisions, maintain communication
- Safety officer: Watches for external threats and building hazard progression, initiates extraction if needed

**Communication protocols:**

- Check-in every 15 minutes minimum (if no communication response, assume emergency—initiate rescue)
- Hand signals for hazard communication (see scavenging-logistics guide for details)
- Whistle code if radio fails (see scavenging-logistics guide for details)

### Emergency Extraction Procedures

Plan extraction before entering. Identify escape routes and practice extraction:

**Extraction planning:**

- Identify primary exit (entrance used) and secondary exit (alternate route if primary blocked)
- Locate stairs, windows, or egress routes in multi-story buildings
- In basement or deep spaces, identify vertical rescue routes (windows that allow reaching exit)
- Establish rally point (200+ meters from building for regrouping if separated)

**Extraction triggers:**

- Any of the warning signs listed above (audible cracks, visual distortion, sensory indicators)
- Structural assessment lead orders evacuation (for any reason—decision is final)
- No check-in response (assume team is in distress; initiate rescue)
- External safety officer observes external hazard (approaching storm, fire, hostile activity)

**If trapped:**

- Move to highest possible floor (more visible for rescue)
- Move to exterior (window area) where rescuers can reach
- Do not jump from height (injury from fall likely worse than structural collapse risk)
- Signal continuously (yell, flashlight, bright cloth) to aid rescue location

## Load-Bearing Wall Identification

Understanding load paths—how weight transfers through a building's structure—is essential. Removing or damaging a load-bearing wall can trigger progressive collapse that may not be immediately obvious but becomes catastrophic hours or days later.

### Exterior Walls: Almost Always Load-Bearing

Exterior walls support the weight of the roof, floors above, and the wall itself. In residential construction, exterior walls are typically load-bearing. Never cut large openings in exterior walls or remove exterior structural members without expert assessment.

### Interior Walls: Variable Load-Bearing Status

Interior walls may or may not carry structural load. Use these identification methods:

-   **Perpendicular to Floor Joists:** If an interior wall runs perpendicular to the floor joists (the parallel structural members supporting floors), the wall is likely load-bearing. Running parallel to joists, it's likely non-load-bearing.
-   **Thickness Comparison:** Load-bearing walls are typically thicker (2x6 or larger framing) than non-load-bearing partition walls (2x4 framing). Compare thickness to other walls in the building.
-   **Direct Above/Below Alignment:** If an interior wall on one floor aligns directly above a beam or wall on the floor below, it's likely load-bearing. Misalignment suggests it's a partition wall.
-   **Basement Evidence:** If you can access the basement, check if the wall sits on a continuous footing or beam. Footings and beams indicate load-bearing walls above.
-   **Basement Beams:** The largest horizontal member in the basement typically runs under load-bearing walls above. All walls directly above basement beams are load-bearing.

### Commercial and Steel-Frame Buildings

In commercial buildings with steel frames:

-   **Steel Frame Buildings:** Interior walls may be non-load-bearing curtain walls because the steel frame carries all structural load. Interior masonry walls are often non-load-bearing. However, verify by checking for columns and visible support systems.
-   **Masonry Load-Bearing Buildings:** Exterior masonry walls (thick brick or stone) carry structural load. Interior masonry walls may also be load-bearing. When in doubt, treat masonry walls as structural.
-   **Column Locations:** Visible interior columns indicate a structural frame. Walls perpendicular to column rows may be non-load-bearing, but walls aligned with columns are likely load-bearing.

### Why Load-Bearing Status Matters

Damage to load-bearing elements can cause:

-   Immediate collapse if damage is extensive
-   Progressive collapse if damage is partial (structural members fail over time as wood rots, connections weaken, or stress exceeds reduced capacity)
-   Cascading failures where one collapsed element shifts load to other members, causing secondary collapses
-   Delayed collapse: visible damage today may not cause failure for days or weeks, meaning danger is invisible to the untrained eye

:::warning
Conservative approach: Assume all walls carry load unless you have positive evidence otherwise. Avoid any activity that could damage walls, cut openings, or shift load distribution. Many salvage operations have ended in fatalities due to underestimating load-bearing wall importance.
:::

## Structural Damage Assessment

Before entering any building, conduct an exterior survey to identify visible structural damage. This takes 5-10 minutes and prevents entry into buildings near failure.

### Foundation Cracks and Settlement

The foundation is the critical structural base. Foundation damage indicates serious problems:

-   **Vertical Cracks:** Minor. Settlement or minor movement. Acceptable if not wider than 1/4 inch.
-   **Horizontal Cracks:** Serious. Indicates water pressure or lateral stress. Building is likely unsafe. Avoid entry.
-   **Stair-Step Cracks:** Moderate to serious. Indicates differential settlement (one part of the foundation settling more than another). May indicate structural instability.
-   **Wide Cracks (>1/2 inch):** Serious structural concern. Building likely has ongoing movement. Avoid entry.
-   **Spalling and Deterioration:** Concrete or brick foundation actively deteriorating. Water infiltration is occurring. Building is destabilizing.

:::tip
Use a credit card or coin to measure crack width. If the crack is wider than the card, it's serious. Multiple large cracks indicate systematic structural problems, not localized settlement.
:::

### Wall Plumb and Alignment

Buildings should have vertical walls. Leaning or bowed walls indicate structural failure:

-   **Obvious Lean:** If you can see the wall is visibly leaning away from plumb (use a plumb bob or drop a water bottle on a flat surface—water finds level), the building is unsafe. Do not enter.
-   **Subtle Lean:** Stand perpendicular to a wall and sight along it. Uniform straightness is good. If the wall appears to bow or curve, there's movement or deformation. Assess severity before deciding to enter.
-   **Buckled Siding:** Rippled or corrugated exterior indicates wall deflection. May be cosmetic or may indicate structural movement beneath.
-   **Separated Corners:** If you can see daylight between walls at corners, the building is pulling apart. Serious structural failure. Avoid entry.

### Roof Line Assessment

The roof should be straight and level (or follow a consistent pitch on gabled roofs):

-   **Sagging Roof:** A roof that sags in the center is carrying more load than its structure can support, or interior support members have failed. Sagging roofs indicate imminent or advanced structural failure. Do not enter; risk of collapse is high.
-   **Missing Sections:** Obvious missing roof sections indicate either storm damage or severe deterioration. Entry is dangerous.
-   **Roof Pitch Change:** A gabled roof should have consistent pitch on each side. If the pitch changes (steeper on one side), there's uneven loading or structural failure.

### Earthquake Damage Indicators

In earthquake-prone regions, look for:

-   Diagonal cracks (45-degree angle) from corners of windows/doors—shear stress
-   Separation of roof from walls (shift visible at edges)
-   Collapsed stories (partial collapse crushing a floor below)
-   Columns that are cracked or bent
-   Visible ground rupture or settlement near the building

### Fire Damage Assessment

Fire-damaged buildings have reduced structural capacity:

-   **Charred Wood:** Wood that has charred (turned black/dark brown) loses 50% or more of its load-carrying capacity. The outer layer is charcoal; internal structure may be partially compromised.
-   **Spalling Concrete:** Concrete exposed to intense heat spalls (chunks break off). The interior remains but surface protection is lost, allowing water infiltration and further deterioration.
-   **Deformed Steel:** Steel exposed to fire may bend or warp. Once warped, steel has lost its original load-carrying geometry. Reject buildings with obviously deformed steel frames.
-   **Burn Line Height:** If fire damage is concentrated at upper levels, the upper structure is compromised. Lower floors may be safe but structural connections between floors are at risk.

:::danger
Fire-damaged buildings are significantly more dangerous than water-damaged buildings because thermal damage weakens materials permanently. Even structures that appear intact may fail suddenly when stressed by vibration or additional load.
:::

### Water Damage and Rot

Prolonged water exposure degrades structural materials:

-   **Water Stains:** Discoloration on exterior indicates prolonged water exposure. Check interior at the same location for rot.
-   **Visible Rot:** Wood with black or brown discoloration, spongy texture, or crumbling appearance is rotted. Structural capacity is reduced significantly.
-   **Soft Spots:** If you can press a pry bar or screwdriver into wood with minimal effort, the wood is rotted and compromised.
-   **Mold Growth:** Visible mold indicates sustained moisture. Structural members in moldy areas are likely compromised.

## Floor & Roof Integrity Testing

Before putting full weight on a floor, test its integrity. Upper floors are particularly dangerous because longer spans between supports mean more flex and more stress concentration.

### Testing Floor Integrity

Use a systematic approach when entering unfamiliar buildings:

-   **Start at Edges:** Floors typically support load from exterior walls inward. Floor edges near walls are usually strongest. Begin testing at the building perimeter, not in the middle of large rooms.
-   **Probe Before Stepping:** Before committing weight to a floor, use a long pole or stick to probe the surface. Listen for cracking, creaking, or collapse indicators. Step on solid parts of the pole; if it breaks through, the floor is compromised.
-   **Weight Distribution:** Step with your weight spread on the widest base possible. Concentrated heel impact (from boots) creates point loading; distribute weight with flat foot contact.
-   **Listen and Feel:** Movement or bouncing indicates possible structural compromise. A slight springy feel is normal in some wood frame buildings, but pronounced flex or bouncing is a warning sign.
-   **Progressive Entry:** Step slowly, testing each new area before moving further. Don't run or move suddenly.

### Water Damage and Floors

Water dramatically weakens floor structures:

-   **Soggy Floors:** Wood exposed to standing water for extended periods (days to weeks) loses load-carrying capacity. A soggy floor may look intact but crumble under stress.
-   **Visible Water Stains or Discoloration:** Indicate past water exposure. The wood may have partially recovered if it dried, but structural strength remains reduced.
-   **Basement/Lower Floors:** Most vulnerable to water damage. Basements and ground floors near exterior walls or in flood-prone areas should be treated as potentially compromised.

### Upper Floor Risk Escalation

Risk increases with elevation:

-   **Ground Floor:** Lowest fall distance if collapse occurs. Acceptable risk if structure is sound.
-   **Second Floor:** 10-15 feet fall distance. Injuries from falls are common; death less likely but possible.
-   **Third+ Floors:** 20+ feet fall distance. Injuries from such falls are severe (broken legs, spinal injuries, internal bleeding). Death becomes more likely. Additionally, upper floors are more likely to have water infiltration from roof leaks, compromising structural integrity.

Strategic approach: Prioritize extraction from ground floors and lower levels. Upper floors should be accessed only for high-value salvage or when lower floors are fully depleted.

### Roof Integrity Testing

Roofs require careful assessment because of falling risk and potential for sudden failure:

-   **Never Walk on Pitched Roofs:** Even in good condition, pitched roofs are slippery. Falls from pitched roofs are routine fatal injuries. Do not attempt to access pitched roof interiors from above.
-   **Flat Roof Assessment:** If a flat roof appears intact, you may carefully inspect it from the interior (attic) if the attic structure is sound. Avoid walking on the roof surface itself; one step through deteriorated or hidden weak spots is a fall.
-   **Sagging Roof Interior:** If visible from interior, sagging indicates load exceeding structural capacity. Risk of partial collapse is significant. Limit time spent under sagging roofs.
-   **Visible Roof Damage:** Missing shingles, holes, or obvious deterioration indicate water infiltration and potential structural rot in roof members.

## Hazmat Recognition

Some buildings contain materials that are not just hazardous to touch but become dangerous when disturbed through scavenging activity. Recognizing these hazards allows you to avoid or safely manage them.

### Asbestos: The Silent Killer

Asbestos is a naturally occurring mineral that was widely used in building materials until the 1980s. It's not dangerous when undisturbed, but becomes extremely hazardous when fibers are released into the air.

-   **Common Locations:** Pipe insulation (1920-1980 buildings), loose-fill attic insulation (beige or gray fluffy material), floor tiles (9"x9" tiles are highly suspicious), roofing, siding, joint compounds, and acoustic ceiling tiles.
-   **Visual Identification:** Asbestos insulation appears as a fibrous, loose material or wrapped pipe covering. Floor tiles from the 1960s-1970s may contain asbestos. If unsure, assume pre-1985 insulation products may contain asbestos.
-   **Hazard When Disturbed:** Sanding, breaking, drilling, or otherwise disturbing asbestos releases microscopic fibers into the air. Inhaled fibers accumulate in lungs and cause mesothelioma (cancer) or asbestosis (progressive lung disease) years later. No safe exposure level exists.

:::danger
Asbestos exposure is permanent and dose-accumulative. Even brief exposure to disturbed asbestos creates lasting lung damage risk. Mesothelioma can develop 20-50 years after exposure. Never disturb suspected asbestos material. This is not negotiable.
:::

-   **Entry Protocol:** If you suspect asbestos, avoid disturbing the material. Do not insulate, cut, or remove items with suspected asbestos. Simply leaving asbestos in place is the safest approach. If absolute extraction is necessary, encapsulate (seal) it before moving it.

### Lead Paint

Lead-based paint was used in buildings constructed before 1978 (federal ban date). Lead is toxic, especially to children.

-   **Visual Identification:** Lead paint looks identical to other paint. Assume all pre-1978 buildings have lead paint, especially on exterior walls and window frames.
-   **Hazard Mechanism:** Lead paint is safe when painted over or intact. It becomes dangerous when sanded (dust), burned (fumes), or chipped and ingested (children are most at risk).

:::warning
Lead accumulates in the body and causes neurological damage, kidney damage, and reproductive harm. Children exposed to lead paint dust have measurably reduced IQ and increased behavioral problems. Pregnant women must avoid lead exposure entirely due to fetal developmental damage risk.
:::

-   **Entry Protocol:** Do not sand, grind, or burn surfaces with suspected lead paint. Do not scrape paint into areas where it can be inhaled. Wipe down contaminated surfaces with damp cloth (doesn't create dust). Wear gloves when handling lead paint areas. Wash hands before eating.

### Gas Leaks

Natural gas is odorless but companies add mercaptan (rotten egg smell) for detection:

-   **Detection Signs:** Smell (mercaptan additive smells like rotten eggs), hissing sound at rupture points, dead vegetation near gas lines, or discoloration around gas pipes.

:::danger
Natural gas is extremely explosive when mixed with air in proper proportions. A single spark or ignition source can cause a devastating explosion that levels a building and kills everyone inside and nearby. If you detect a gas smell, assume immediate danger.
:::

-   **Entry Protocol:** If you smell gas, do not enter the building. Do not use flames, create sparks, or operate electrical equipment (light switches included). Exit immediately and wait for the smell to dissipate. If gas odor is strong or persistent, the building has a dangerous leak; avoid until professional response occurs.

### Sewage and Biological Hazards

Sewage backup or sanitary waste creates biological hazard:

-   **Identification:** Foul sewage smell, standing water in basements with black or brown discoloration, or visible human waste.
-   **Hazard:** Pathogenic bacteria and viruses cause serious infection. Contact with contaminated surfaces or inhalation of aerosols is dangerous.

:::info-box
Sewage contains hundreds of pathogenic species: E. coli, hepatitis A virus, salmonella, cryptosporidium, norovirus, and others. A single contamination event can cause severe illness lasting weeks or months. Some pathogens are fatal if untreated.
:::

-   **Entry Protocol:** Avoid basements with sewage backup. If you must enter areas with sewage, wear respiratory protection (N95 mask minimum, preferably P100), full-body coverage (long sleeves, pants, boots), and double gloves. Wash thoroughly after exposure. Assume cuts or open wounds are infected.

### Carbon Monoxide and Other Gases

Invisible, colorless gases can accumulate in confined spaces:

-   **Carbon Monoxide (CO):** From incomplete combustion (fires, generators, vehicle exhaust). Causes headaches, dizziness, then unconsciousness and death.
-   **Hydrogen Sulfide (H2S):** From decomposition or industrial processes. Smells like rotten eggs (similar to mercaptan). Causes respiratory irritation, then unconsciousness and death at high concentrations.
-   **Entry Protocol:** If you notice any unusual smell (especially rotten eggs, ammonia, or chemical odors), ventilate the space before entering. Do not enter enclosed spaces (basements, sealed rooms) without ventilation. If you become dizzy, nauseous, or lose consciousness, leave immediately.

<section id="entry-exit-protocols">

## Entry & Exit Protocols

Systematic entry and exit procedures prevent being trapped and ensure rapid evacuation if problems develop.

### Exit Planning Before Entry

Never enter a building without knowing how you'll leave:

-   **Primary Exit:** The entrance you used. Verify it's not blocked or jam-prone before fully entering.
-   **Secondary Exit:** An alternate route (different door, window, or passage) if primary exit is blocked. Mentally map this before entering.
-   **Locked Doors:** Identify any doors that might slam shut and lock behind you (self-closing doors). Prop them open with tools or rocks if you'll be moving past them.
-   **Two-Exit Rule:** Only enter buildings where you can identify at least two separate exits. If only one exit exists, do not enter.

### Propping and Securing Doors

Buildings shift and settle; doors can jam unexpectedly:

-   Prop the entry door open with a solid object (wood block, large rock, or metal wedge). Ensure the prop won't slide out if the door moves.
-   For doors you'll pass through on the way out, also prop them open if they're self-closing.
-   Check door clearance—ensure the prop doesn't prevent the door from opening fully for emergency exit.
-   If you can't secure a door safely, don't use that route.

### Never Enter Alone

Minimum team size is 3. Never send a single person into a structure. If that person becomes injured or trapped, they have no way to get help.

:::warning
Solo salvagers die regularly in building collapses, falls, and other accidents. By the time a missing person is discovered, rescue is impossible if collapse has occurred or if they're severely injured and unable to call for help. The minimal extra resource cost of a second team member is worth the life-saving potential.
:::

### Time Limits and Check-in Schedule

Set a specific duration for the building search:

-   30-60 minutes is typical for residential buildings. Shorter for large commercial buildings or buildings with questionable integrity.
-   The person outside (security/lookout) enforces the time limit. When time is up, everyone exits, regardless of ongoing search productivity.
-   Fatigue increases accident risk. Better to exit with 80% of salvage after 45 minutes than risk injury pushing to 90 minutes when exhausted.

### Emergency Signaling

Carry a way to signal distress:

-   Whistle (loudest, travels farthest). Three sharp blasts is standard distress signal.
-   Flashlight (visible signal at night or in dark buildings).
-   Radio or two-way communication (if available).
-   Voice (last resort; identify yourself to prevent friendly fire).

### Announcing Yourself

Other people may be present in the building (homeless, other scavengers, or hostile occupants):

-   Upon entry, announce yourself loudly: "Building entry, scavenging team, please identify yourselves." This prevents surprising armed occupants and gives warning if others are present.
-   If someone responds, assess their intent. If they're friendly or non-threatening, negotiate or leave. If they're hostile, exit immediately using your planned secondary exit.

### Movement Patterns for Safety

How you move affects your safety:

-   **Stay Near Walls:** Move along walls near exterior support structure. Interior center of large rooms puts you far from structural support if floors collapse.
-   **Avoid Center of Rooms:** If floor integrity is questionable, being in the center of a room places you over the weakest point of the floor span.
-   **Move Deliberately:** Slow, controlled movement is safer than running. You can assess conditions as you go.
-   **Test Each Step:** Probe ahead with your foot or stick before placing full weight.

## Emergency Procedures

Despite precautions, emergencies happen. Know your response.

### Partial Collapse While Inside

If the building begins to collapse or part of it collapses while you're inside:

-   **Immediately Move to Doorway or Structural Corner:** Don't run randomly. Doors and corners have inherent structural strength; buildings often leave these standing even during partial collapse.
-   **Protect Your Head:** Raise arms over head and crouch. Most fatal injuries are from head trauma.
-   **Wait Before Moving:** If the collapse stops, wait 30-60 seconds before moving. Secondary collapses are common after initial failure—don't rush into unstable areas.
-   **Exit Carefully:** Once movement has stopped, carefully move toward exits, testing each step. If primary exit is blocked, use secondary exit. Don't stay in the building.

### Dust from Collapse

Dust from a collapse creates zero visibility and respiratory hazard:

-   **Immediate Response:** Cover your mouth and nose (use fabric, hand, or respirator if available). Prevent inhalation of dust.
-   **Sight Loss:** If visibility is zero, stay in place briefly to allow dust to settle. Moving blindly increases risk of running into obstacles or stepping through floor gaps.
-   **Breathing:** Shallow breathing through a cloth filter until dust settles. Deep breaths pull more dust into lungs.

### Trapped Teammate

If a team member is trapped by debris:

-   **Don't Rush to Rescue:** Assess the situation first. Can you reach them safely? Is the area around them stable, or will your approach cause additional collapse?
-   **Assess Stability:** If the area is unstable, attempting rescue may trap both of you. Sometimes the better choice is to mark their location, exit the building safely, and call for outside help.
-   **Remove Debris Carefully:** If you can reach them, work carefully to remove debris. Sudden shifting can cause secondary collapse or further injury to the trapped person.

:::info-box
Rescue decisions under pressure are often wrong. The guard positioned outside serves as the voice of reason. Tell them to abort if entry becomes dangerous, and listen to their judgment even if you think you can rescue the trapped person. A rescue that traps more people is a failure, not a success.
:::

-   **Provide Signal to External Team:** If you must exit to get help, mark the trapped person's location (chalk mark, visible object, or verbal description to waiting team). Get the external team to call for help if possible (emergency services if operational, or other community members).

### Crush Injuries

People trapped under debris or in collapsed spaces suffer crush injuries:

-   **Immediate Field Treatment:** Once the person is freed, assess for bleeding. Apply direct pressure to control bleeding. Check responsiveness and breathing. Position person on their side if unconscious (prevents airway obstruction from blood/vomit).
-   **Crush Syndrome:** Crushed limbs release toxins when pressure is released. The person may feel fine initially, then collapse from kidney failure or cardiac arrest. All crush injury victims require evacuation for professional medical care, even if they seem okay.

:::danger
Crush syndrome can cause sudden cardiac arrest hours after apparent recovery. Do not let a crush injury victim lie down or stop moving without fluids. Keep them hydrated and moving gently. Kidney failure from crush toxins can develop over 24-48 hours. Professional medical intervention may be the only thing that saves their life.
:::

-   **Evacuation Priority:** Any serious collapse or injury makes continued scavenging impossible. Evacuate the injured person and abort the operation. Get them to your community's medical personnel immediately.

<section id="tools-safe-entry">

## Tools for Safe Entry

Carrying appropriate tools increases safety and effectiveness. Balance is necessary—more tools increase capability but also increase fatigue.

### Essential Tools

-   **Pry Bar (16-24 inches):** For forcing doors and windows. Also useful for clearing debris. Weight: 3-5 lbs. Mandatory.
-   **Flashlight:** LED flashlight (rechargeable or battery) for visibility in dark buildings. Carry extra batteries or carry multiple lights. Weight: 0.5-1 lb including batteries. Mandatory.
-   **Dust Mask/Respirator:** N95 masks for basic protection; P100 cartridge respirators for heavy dust or suspected hazmat. Weight: negligible. Mandatory.
-   **Work Gloves:** Leather or heavy fabric for hand protection. Weight: 0.25 lbs. Mandatory.
-   **Hard Hat or Helmet:** For head protection. Climbing helmets (lighter) or construction hard hats (heavier, more impact resistant). Weight: 1-2 lbs. Highly recommended for upper floors or buildings with overhead hazard.

### Supporting Tools

-   **Rope (1/2" diameter, 50 feet):** For rappelling, belay (safety tether), or retrieving items from upper floors. Weight: 3-4 lbs. Recommended for multi-story buildings.
-   **Pole or Stick (8-10 feet):** For floor probing before stepping, for extending reach to high areas, and for moving items safely from distance. Weight: 2-5 lbs depending on material. Highly recommended.
-   **Chalk or Spray Paint:** For marking hazards, marking cleared areas, or leaving signs for team members. Weight: negligible. Recommended.

### Tool Load Management

Tools add weight; prioritize utility:

-   Pry bar and flashlight are non-negotiable. Everything else is secondary.
-   Rope is essential if you're entering multi-story buildings; less so if you're focusing on ground floor.
-   Hard hat/helmet weighs 1-2 lbs but prevents serious head injury. Don't skip this.
-   Total tool load should not exceed 15-20 lbs for a single person. If your tools weigh more, you're reducing your carrying capacity for salvage.

</section>

### Recommendation Summary

**Prioritize:** Wood frame residential and single-story steel frame buildings. Rewards are reasonable, and structural risks are manageable with proper assessment.

**Proceed With Caution:** Masonry commercial buildings (3-10 stories). Higher rewards but higher fall risk. Limit work to lower 2-3 floors unless high-priority salvage is identified.

**Avoid:** Mobile homes (low reward, high risk), parking garages (minimal salvage), and multi-story office buildings (extreme fall risk). These aren't worth the injuries they cause.

## Troubleshooting & Common Mistakes

### Problem: Cannot Determine if Wall is Load-Bearing

**Issue:** You're uncertain whether a wall carries structural load and don't have time for detailed investigation.

**Solution:** Treat it as load-bearing and avoid any action that could damage it. Conservative approach is always correct. Better to miss salvage than to risk collapse.

### Problem: Questionable Floor Integrity

**Issue:** Floor feels slightly bouncy or has minor soft spots. You're unsure if it's safe to proceed.

**Solution:** Probe extensively with a pole before stepping. Test from wall edges (strongest areas) first. If floor is genuinely compromised, locate an alternate route. Do not force weight onto questionable areas.

### Problem: Ceiling Appears Damaged But Salvage is Valuable

**Issue:** Ceiling has water damage or sagging but the floor below contains valuable salvage. Risk vs. reward calculation is unclear.

**Solution:** Assume ceiling collapse is possible. Minimize time spent underneath damaged ceilings. Work quickly and exit immediately if you hear creaking or see movement. No salvage is worth head trauma or crush injury.

### Problem: Access is Difficult; Tempted to Cut Wall or Pry Doorway

**Issue:** You want to open a new passage or enlarge an existing one to speed up work.

**Solution:** Do not cut or damage walls, especially if unsure of load-bearing status. Cutting load-bearing elements can trigger immediate collapse. Find an alternate route, even if it takes longer.

### Problem: Found Possible Hazmat; Salvage Nearby is Valuable

**Issue:** You spot asbestos insulation or lead paint in an area, but good salvage is accessible without disturbing it.

**Solution:** Leave the area and do not salvage anything in that zone. Hazmat contamination can spread via dust during handling, and you cannot guarantee you won't accidentally disturb it. Loss of salvage is better than hazmat exposure.

</section>

<section id="common-fatal-mistakes">

## Common Fatal Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Overconfidence After Safe Entry

**What happens:** First building entry went smoothly, so team assumes all buildings are similar. They skip pre-entry assessment and enter a more dangerous structure.

**Consequence:** Building collapse injuries or deaths. Each building must be individually assessed.

### Mistake 2: Staying Too Long in Damaged Building

**What happens:** Salvage is productive, so team ignores time limit. They continue past the point of fatigue.

**Consequence:** Fatigue causes missteps, missed hazards, falls, or trips through floor gaps. Injuries from tired mistakes are common.

### Mistake 3: Entering Without Identifying Exit Routes

**What happens:** Team focuses on salvage and forgets to locate secondary exit. Primary exit becomes blocked by collapse.

**Consequence:** Entrapment and death if rescue cannot reach them quickly.

### Mistake 4: Ignoring Structural Warning Signs

**What happens:** Team sees foundation cracks, roof sagging, or wall lean but decides structure "looks okay enough." They enter anyway.

**Consequence:** Building fails during entry or salvage operations. Collapse injuries are often fatal.

### Mistake 5: Solo Salvage Attempts

**What happens:** Single person enters structure "just to do a quick check" without a team.

**Consequence:** If injury occurs, no one is available to help. Death from injuries that would be survivable with immediate aid (falls, crushed limbs, asphyxiation).
