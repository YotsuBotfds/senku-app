<section id="overview">

## Overview

Food salvage represents the highest-priority resource category during infrastructure collapse. Unlike manufactured goods that can be fabricated or replaced, food is immediately consumable and directly sustains life. Salvaged food supplies buying time for agriculture establishment, hunting operations, or gradual resource stabilization. Understanding shelf life, spoilage indicators, and safety assessment prevents food-borne illness while maximizing caloric extraction from available sources.

:::info-box
Core principle: Any food from unknown source requires assessment before consumption. Botulism, salmonella, and other pathogens are life-threatening. When in doubt about safety, reject the food. Your salvage will be large enough that eliminating borderline items doesn't cause starvation.
:::


Preservation is not a fix for spoiled food. If the item is already suspect, use food-spoilage-assessment.md first and keep it out of the preservation lane.

### Key Assessment Categories

This guide covers five major salvage food categories: (1) Canned goods (most stable); (2) Dry goods (durable and compact); (3) Frozen foods (time-limited); (4) Condiments and specialized items (high value per weight); (5) Pet food and supplements (emergency resources). Each category has distinct assessment criteria and shelf life parameters.

<section id="location-scouting">

## Location Scouting

High-yield food locations prioritize density, accessibility, and preservation conditions.

### Residential Kitchens

Single-family homes contain 30-60 days of food (based on family size). Average food yield: 50-100 lbs of consumable items per residence. Key locations: kitchen pantry (highest concentration), freezer (time-sensitive), cupboards, storage closets. Time per location: 30-45 minutes. Advantage: less competition than commercial locations, items often undisturbed. Disadvantage: items may have been consumed or deteriorated before you arrive.

### Commercial Food Stores

Grocery stores, convenience stores, and specialty food shops contain vast quantities. A single grocery store may hold 100,000+ lbs of consumable food. However, access is restricted and other scavengers likely compete. Focus on areas cleared of obvious spoilage: sealed shelf items (canned goods, boxed foods, condiments), freezer sections (if power available or items still frozen), stockroom areas (least picked-over). Dumpsters behind stores contain rejected or expired items; some remain safe and valuable despite expiration dates.

### Wholesale Clubs

Costco, Sam's Club, and similar bulk retailers contain enormous quantities of food. Member access areas limit scavenger competition. Focus on sealed, packaged items. Time yield is excellent (high-volume containers). Transportation is critical—weight and volume are substantial.

### Restaurants and Commercial Kitchens

Closed restaurants contain prepared foods (highly perishable), bulk dry ingredients (durable), canned goods, oils, and condiments. Freezers may contain substantial meat if power recently lost. Pantries contain specialty ingredients and spices. Advantage: bulk quantities of items typically difficult to salvage elsewhere. Disadvantage: perishable items dominate; assessment and immediate processing required.

### Warehouses and Food Storage Facilities

Commercial storage facilities hold food destined for retail. Conditions are typically excellent (climate controlled, protected from light). Single warehouses contain 10,000+ lbs of shelf-stable food. Access is difficult without keys; requires breaking and entering.

### Pantries and Emergency Shelters

Government emergency shelters, schools with pantry programs, and religious institutions maintain food caches. These locations may contain pre-packaged emergency rations, MREs, and bulk shelf-stable foods. Access depends on political/social situation; may be protected or community resources. Salvage ethically—these may sustain vulnerable populations.

### Farms and Agricultural Sites

Grain storage, root vegetable cellars, and farm buildings contain seasonal food stores. Yields vary dramatically by season and crop type. Pre-harvest farms offer minimal food; post-harvest contains maximum. Assessment difficulty is high; mold, insect damage, and grain quality require specific knowledge. See Agricultural Salvage guide for detailed procedures.

</section>

## Tools Required

Food salvage requires minimal equipment but specific safety items are critical.

### Essential Tools

- Utility knife (cut open boxes, cans when needed)
- Flashlight (inspect dark cupboards, assess can interiors)
- Thick gloves (protection from sharp edges, glass, contamination)
- Containers (transport food, prevent mixing)
- Hygiene supplies (hand sanitizer, disinfectant wipes)

### Optional Equipment

- Respirator/dust mask (for moldy areas, grain storage)
- Food thermometer (assess freezer temperatures)
- Notebook (record locations, conditions, dates)
- Scale or volume measurements (quantify salvage)

<section id="identification">

## Identification & Selection Criteria

Determining which foods to prioritize during salvage operations maximizes caloric intake while minimizing safety risk.

### Priority Tier System

**Tier 1 (Immediate grab):** Sealed, undamaged canned goods; frozen foods if freezer functional or items still fully frozen; high-calorie condiments (oils, honey); medications and vitamins. Time sensitivity: hours to days for frozen items.

**Tier 2 (Next priority):** Sealed dry goods (pasta, rice, beans, flour, grains); boxed prepared meals; MREs; sealed pet food. Time sensitivity: weeks to months depending on type.

**Tier 3 (Third pass):** Opened containers requiring processing; bulk materials; items requiring immediate assessment. Time sensitivity: varies.

**Tier 4 (Final pass):** Fresh or perishable items requiring immediate consumption; items with extensive damage requiring cleaning. Time sensitivity: hours.

<section id="canned-goods">

## Canned Goods Assessment

Canned goods represent some of the most valuable salvage items—commercially canned food can remain safe and nutritious for decades if the seal remains intact. Your first assessment step is determining whether a can is still sound.

### Visual Inspection Protocol

Begin by examining the entire can surface under good light. Look for bulging on either end—this indicates bacterial gas production inside, making the contents unsafe regardless of appearance or smell. Never open a bulging or leaking can; discard it immediately. The contents may be pressurized or contaminated and likely contain pathogens.

Rust requires nuanced assessment. Surface rust (orange discoloration on the exterior) is primarily cosmetic and does not compromise safety. However, deep rust that creates pits in the metal can eventually create holes. Run your finger over rusty areas; if it flakes away easily or you feel sharp edges, the integrity is compromised. Seam rust—rust appearing along the sealed edges of the can—is more concerning than body rust, as seams are the critical pressure-holding component.

Dents demand careful evaluation. Small dents on flat surfaces (the sides) are generally acceptable if the dent is minor and doesn't create sharp edges that could cut your hand. The critical concern is whether the dent compromised the seal or created a structural weakness. Seam dents (along the top or bottom rim where the can was sealed) are more dangerous than body dents, as they may have compromised the seal itself. Deep dents that create sharp angles or affect the structural integrity should be rejected.

### Can Integrity Testing

After visual inspection, press firmly on the ends of the can (top and bottom). They should be rigid and not give under pressure. If an end flexes inward or outward when pressed, the seal has likely failed and pressure has been lost. Discard such cans. The flexibility indicates the can is no longer holding a protective seal.

Shake the can gently near your ear. You should hear the expected contents (liquid sloshing, beans rattling). If you hear unusual sounds—excessive liquid where there shouldn't be, metal-on-metal sounds suggesting interior corrosion—the can's interior may be compromised.

### Acidic vs. Low-Acid Foods

Acidic foods (tomatoes, citrus, pickled items, vinegar-based products) remain safe far longer than low-acid foods because the acidity inhibits botulism. Acidic canned goods often remain safe for 12-18 months or longer. Low-acid foods (vegetables, meats, soups) degrade faster; bacteria and toxins develop more readily. For low-acid foods, prioritize freshness: consume those over 5 years old first, and monitor for any off-odors when opening.

### Date Code Interpretation

Most commercial cans display Julian codes (three-digit date codes where 001 = January 1st, 100 = April 9th). Smaller manufacturers use simple month/year stamps. These are production dates, not expiration dates. Commercially canned goods remain safe well beyond these dates if the seal is intact. The "best by" date reflects peak nutrition and flavor, not safety. A can from 2010 is safe to eat if the container is sound, though flavors may be muted and some nutrients (especially vitamin C) will have degraded.

### Home-Canned Goods

Salvaged home-canned goods require extra caution. Home canning has stricter safety margins than commercial canning because home processes are less controlled. Botulism risk exists with improper sealing or processing. Inspect the seal carefully: the lid should be slightly concave (curved inward), indicating a proper seal. If the lid flexes or pops when pressed, the seal has failed. Discard any home-canned good from an unknown source unless you're certain the person followed proper procedures (pressure-canning for low-acid foods, boiling water bath for acidic foods). When in doubt, reject home-canned goods—botulism is not worth the risk.

<section id="dry-goods">

## Dry Goods Viability

Dry goods form the backbone of salvaged food supplies because they store for extended periods with minimal care. Understanding how each category behaves allows you to maximize your salvage value.

### Flour and Grain Products

Flour under normal conditions (room temperature, exposed to air) remains viable for 6-12 months. However, properly sealed flour lasts years—some salvagers have successfully used 10-year-old flour from sealed containers. The limiting factor is rancidity; flour contains oils that oxidize over time. Store flour in airtight containers or freezer bags to dramatically extend shelf life. Whole wheat flour goes rancid faster than white flour due to the bran's higher oil content. Signs of spoilage: rancid or musty odor, discoloration, clumping due to moisture absorption.

### Rice Longevity

White rice is nearly indefinite in storage if kept dry and sealed. Archaeological evidence suggests properly stored rice remains edible after 50+ years. Brown rice, conversely, goes rancid within 6-12 months due to the oil-rich bran layer. Inspect rice for moisture clumping, visible mold, or musty odors. Small insects (rice weevils) are common in salvaged grain; they're not harmful but indicate inadequate storage. Sift these out or use the rice in cooked dishes where the insects won't be noticed. Proper storage: airtight containers, cool and dry environment.

### Pasta and Noodles

Dried pasta lasts for years if kept dry and protected from insects. The primary concern is moisture absorption and insect infestation. Sealed pasta packages remain viable for 5+ years easily. Look for clumping, discoloration, or insects. Expired pasta cooks fine; it simply may have absorbed moisture and become slightly brittle. Use it.

### Sugar and Salt

Sugar lasts indefinitely if kept dry. Clumping occurs with moisture exposure but doesn't affect safety or functionality—break up clumps or dissolve in liquid. Salt lasts indefinitely under virtually any conditions. It's one of the most shelf-stable foods available. Iodized salt may lose iodine potency over many years, but the salt itself remains safe and effective as a preservative.

### Baking Soda and Baking Powder

Baking soda (sodium bicarbonate) is indefinite in storage but loses potency if exposed to moisture or acidic vapors. Test a small amount: mix with vinegar or lemon juice. A vigorous fizz reaction indicates full potency. No reaction suggests degradation; discard. Baking powder degrades faster, especially the double-acting variety. Test similarly by mixing with warm water—look for vigorous fizzing. Expired baking powder may still work but produce less rise.

### Dried Beans and Legumes

Dried beans remain technically edible for years but become increasingly difficult to rehydrate and cook. Storage time is critical; beans older than 2-3 years may require extended cooking times (several hours vs. 1-2 hours). They won't become unsafe, but they become less practical. Store in airtight containers to prevent insect infestation. Pressure cooking is your ally with older beans—it breaks down the hardened outer layer more effectively than boiling.

### Oats and Cereals

Oats in sealed containers last 2-3 years easily. Opened containers degrade faster. Instant oats contain added oils and degrade quicker than steel-cut or rolled oats. Look for rancid smell (a key indicator of oil oxidation), clumping, or insects. Unopened sealed cereal boxes remain viable well past their printed dates if kept dry, though the cereal becomes stale and nutrient-poor.

## Frozen Food Timeline

Frozen food represents an emergency resource with a finite shelf life once power is lost. Understanding the timeline allows you to prioritize consumption and prevent waste.

### Freezer Stability Without Power

A full freezer maintains safe temperatures for approximately 48 hours if unopened. A half-full freezer maintains safe conditions for about 24 hours. The insulation of the freezer itself protects the contents. Once the freezer temperature reaches 40°F (4°C)—the danger zone boundary—bacterial growth accelerates rapidly. During the 48-hour window, inspect frozen items for signs of thawing: ice crystals, weeping liquid, unusual softness. Items that thaw will need immediate attention.

### Post-Thaw Safety Rules

Once thawed, meat becomes unsafe after two hours at room temperature (above 40°F / 4°C). Poultry and ground meats are more vulnerable; whole cuts may last slightly longer. Vegetables and fruits can thaw for longer periods without safety risk but degrade in quality. Bread, baked goods, and prepared meals also tolerate longer thaw times. Never leave thawed meat at room temperature expecting to refreeze it later—this creates ideal conditions for bacterial multiplication.

### Refreezing Rules

Thawed items can be refrozen if they still contain ice crystals (indicating incomplete thawing) or if they were thawed in the refrigerator. If meat thawed at room temperature for one hour, refreezing is safe but not ideal—thaw it again and cook immediately. The quality degrades with each freeze-thaw cycle, but safety isn't compromised if thawing occurred safely. If you must refreeze, do so as quickly as possible.

### Identifying Thaw-and-Refreeze Damage

Look for evidence that food was thawed and refrozen before you acquired it: large ice crystals throughout the package (indicating ice formation after partial thaw), freezer burn (gray or white discoloration), or liquid pooled at the bottom of the packaging. These indicators suggest multiple temperature cycles. Such items are safe but low quality. Use them in cooked dishes where quality degradation won't be noticed (soups, stews, ground meat preparations).

### Freeze-Dried vs. Frozen

Freeze-dried foods (common in camping and emergency supplies) differ fundamentally from frozen foods. Freeze-dried items have had water removed via sublimation, leaving shelf-stable products that don't require refrigeration. These last years if stored in sealed containers. Reconstitute with water before eating. Value freeze-dried foods highly in salvage—they combine shelf stability with reasonable nutrition and flavor.

<section id="condiments-spices">

## Condiment & Spice Value

Condiments and spices offer disproportionate value in survival situations, far exceeding their caloric content. They affect morale, mask unpalatable foods, and provide important micronutrients.

### Spices for Nutrition and Preservation

Many spices contain surprising micronutrients. Paprika provides vitamin C (crucial in survival situations where fresh vegetables are scarce). Oregano, thyme, and sage possess antimicrobial properties that can extend food shelf life naturally. Turmeric contains curcumin, a potent anti-inflammatory compound. Garlic and onion powders offer both flavor and documented antimicrobial benefits. In survival contexts, these aren't mere flavorings—they're functional medicines and nutrition supplements.

Salvage spices aggressively. They store indefinitely (though potency decreases over time), take minimal space, and have high morale impact. A can of excellent spice might make the difference between eating and actually enjoying food when calories are scarce.

### Vinegar and Preservation

Vinegar (any type: apple cider, white, rice, balsamic) lasts indefinitely and serves multiple purposes. It's a flavor amplifier, a natural preservative, a cleaning agent, and a mild antimicrobial. Salvage all vinegar. Even weak vinegar is useful. Store in sealed containers to prevent evaporation.

### Honey

Honey is indefinite in storage. Crystallization is completely normal and doesn't indicate spoilage or degradation. Simply warm the jar (don't microwave; use warm water) to liquefy it. Honey provides energy, acts as a mild antimicrobial, and can be used medicinally for cough suppression. Archaeological evidence confirms honey viability after thousands of years. Prioritize salvaging honey.

### Oils and Fat Degradation

Cooking oils go rancid over time, especially at room temperature or in light. Olive oil, canola oil, and other unsaturated oils degrade faster than saturated fats (lard, coconut oil). Rancid oil tastes bitter and unpleasant and can cause digestive upset. Smell is your primary indicator—rancid oil smells unpleasantly sharp or off. Discard rancid oils. Unopened, high-quality oil lasts 1-2 years; opened bottles degrade faster. Store in cool, dark places in sealed containers.

### Soy Sauce and Fish Sauce

These fermented products have very long shelf lives—years even when opened. The high salt content and fermentation process preserve them. They may separate or develop a white film (salt crystals), which is cosmetic. If unopened, they're essentially indefinite. These are invaluable salvage items for flavor impact on bland emergency foods.

</section>

## Pet Food as Emergency Calories

In prolonged survival scenarios, pet food represents a nutritionally complete caloric source. This is uncomfortable to consider but essential knowledge for survival planning.

### Dry Kibble as Nutrition

Dog and cat kibble are nutritionally complete for the animals they're formulated for. Dry kibble contains 300-450 calories per cup and includes proteins, fats, carbohydrates, and vitamin/mineral supplementation. It's palatable if you can overcome the psychological barrier. Most dry kibble is made from grains, meat by-products (rendered meat), and synthetic vitamins. In an emergency, it sustains life effectively.

Caloric density varies by brand: premium kibbles often have higher meat content and thus higher calories, while economy brands have more grain filler. Budget dry food may be 200-250 calories per cup; premium brands reach 450+. Assess based on ingredient lists: more meat = more calories and protein.

### Food Safety Considerations

Pet food operates under different regulatory standards than human food. Contamination risks are lower than some human food products but higher quality control than others. Pet food manufacturers have experienced recalls for salmonella and other contaminants, though incidents are relatively rare. The primary concern isn't food-borne pathogens but the lower-standard ingredients: rendered meat from unknown sources, fillers, and synthetic supplements designed for animal metabolism rather than human preference.

Cook dry kibble if possible (simmer in water to soften and reduce any contamination risk). Eat it dry if necessary. Stomach upset is possible if not accustomed to it, but humans digest it safely. Cats have more strict nutritional requirements than dogs; cat food is acceptable for humans, but dog food is generally preferred due to lower protein requirements that make it slightly more palatable.

### Wet Pet Food Assessment

Canned wet pet food is assessed like any canned goods: check for bulging, rust, dent damage, and seal integrity. Wet pet food provides similar caloric density per serving as dry kibble but with higher water content. It's more palatable to most humans than dry kibble and easier to eat if dental problems exist. Apply the same shelf-life rules: well over 5 years if sealed properly, but consume older stock first.

### Alternative Uses

If you're unable to overcome the psychological barrier to consuming pet food directly, use it as livestock feed (for chickens, pigs, or goats if you're raising animals) or as fish feed in aquaculture systems. You convert pet food calories to animal calories, then consume the animals—a more psychologically acceptable pathway.

## Supplement & Vitamin Recovery

Salvaged supplements and vitamins offer valuable micronutrients, especially in long-term survival where diet becomes monotonous and limited.

### Multivitamin Degradation

Multivitamins lose potency over time but rarely become harmful. The active compounds degrade through oxidation and moisture exposure, resulting in lower efficacy, not increased toxicity. A multivitamin from 10 years ago might be 30-50% potency instead of 100%, but you'll still absorb some benefit. Take expired multivitamins liberally; they won't hurt and will help. Store in cool, dry containers and away from light to minimize degradation.

### Vitamin C Instability

Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) is the most unstable vitamin supplement. It oxidizes rapidly when exposed to air, light, or moisture. A sealed bottle of vitamin C from 3-5 years ago might be 20% potency. However, salvage it anyway—even reduced potency helps in a scenario where fresh citrus is unavailable. Vitamin C deficiency leads to scurvy, a serious problem in long-term survival. Any amount of vitamin C is better than none.

### Fish Oil and Omega-3 Capsules

Fish oil capsules contain unsaturated fats that go rancid. Rancid fish oil tastes fishy and unpleasant and can cause nausea or digestive upset. Open a capsule—if the oil smells strongly fishy or off, discard it. Fresh fish oil has a fishy smell but not an unpleasant one; rancid oil is unambiguous. Salvage only fresh-smelling fish oil. Oxidized fish oil may not provide the omega-3 benefits, but it won't poison you.

### Iron Supplements

Iron supplements are highly valuable in survival situations. Iron deficiency is common when diet is restricted, and iron supplementation prevents anemia and maintains energy. Iron supplements don't degrade like other vitamins. Salvage all iron supplements. Check for any visible rust or white crystalline deposits (indicating exposure to moisture), but generally they remain viable indefinitely. Iron supplements can cause constipation and GI upset, especially on an empty stomach or if large doses are taken; take with food and increase gradually.

### Electrolyte Powders

Powdered electrolyte supplements (sodium, potassium, magnesium) remain viable for years if kept sealed and dry. Caking or clumping indicates moisture exposure but doesn't make them unusable—dissolve and drink. Electrolyte powders are extremely valuable in survival situations, especially if dehydration is a risk (hot climates, illness, or extreme exertion). Salvage all electrolyte products.

<section id="mre-rations">

## MRE & Emergency Ration ID

Military and civilian emergency rations represent engineered survival food with documented shelf life and caloric content.

### Military MRE Identification

Meals, Ready to Eat (MREs) are U.S. military rations recognized by their tan/brown vacuum-sealed pouches. Each MRE contains an entrée, side dish, dessert, crackers, spread, and accessories (salt, pepper, gum, instant coffee, sugar). MREs are manufactured by multiple contractors but share similar packaging. Military MREs are designed for consumption without cooking, though heating improves palatability (included flameless heater or hot water).

MREs have a printed production date and shelf life. Most are designed for 3-year shelf life at ambient temperature (70°F / 21°C), with extended life at cooler temperatures. An MRE from 2015 stored in cool conditions remains safe; it may taste less fresh, but it's nutritionally complete and safe. Never discard an MRE based solely on date; assess the storage conditions.

### Civilian Emergency Rations

Civilian emergency rations include freeze-dried meal packages (Mountain House, Backpacker's Pantry, etc.), emergency food bars (high-calorie dense bars designed for 20+ year shelf life), and civilian MRE equivalents sold by private manufacturers. These vary widely in quality and taste. Freeze-dried meals require water to reconstitute; emergency bars are shelf-stable and ready to eat. Both are valuable salvage. Emergency bars often taste waxy or overly sweet but provide 200-400 calories per bar and remain viable for decades if stored cool and dry.

### Ship's Stores and Other Rations

Salvaging from vessels or maritime sources may yield ship's rations, canned provisions designed for long storage on ships. These follow similar shelf-life rules as standard canned goods but may be unfamiliar brands or unusual products. Assess using the standard canned goods evaluation: check for bulging, rust, dent damage, and seal integrity.

### MRE Component Salvaging

Individual MRE components can be separated and used independently. The included caffeine tablets, sugar packets, and salt are valuable. The main entrée can be consumed cold, though it's more pleasant heated. MRE crackers, often hard and bland, can be softened in water or soup. Even the packaging is useful—MRE pouches can serve as cooking vessels for boiling water. Don't waste any component; salvage everything.

<section id="hazard-identification">

## Hazard Identification

Recognizing contamination and spoilage indicators prevents food-borne illness.

### Botulism Risk Factors

Botulism is caused by Clostridium botulinum toxin produced under anaerobic (no-oxygen) conditions. The toxin is deadly at microgram quantities and destroys the nervous system. Home-canned low-acid foods (meats, vegetables) without pressure-canning are primary sources. Commercial canning is controlled and safe. Botulism indicators: bulging cans (gas production), hissing when opened (explosive pressure), off-odors (though toxin may be odorless), visible mold growth. Any suspicious signs = rejection. Cooking to 212°F for 10 minutes inactivates botulism toxin but doesn't eliminate the risk if you can't verify proper heating. When in doubt, don't taste—reject home-canned goods from unknown sources.

:::danger
Botulism is 100% fatal if untreated and very difficult to treat even with medical care. Never taste questionable canned goods to assess safety. Visual and smell assessment only. When in doubt, reject.
:::

### Pathogens in Raw and Undercooked Food

Salmonella (poultry, eggs, reptiles), E. coli (beef, contaminated vegetables), Listeria (dairy, ready-to-eat meats), and Campylobacter (poultry) all cause severe food poisoning. Raw foods must be cooked to specific temperatures: poultry 165°F, beef 160°F, fish 145°F. Avoid raw salvaged foods unless you're certain of cooking capability. Frozen raw foods are only safe if still frozen or thawed in refrigeration; room-temperature thawed meat becomes unsafe within 2 hours.

### Mold and Mycotoxins

Mold on food indicates fungal contamination and possible mycotoxin production (fungal toxins). Some molds are non-toxic; many are poisonous. Never assume moldy food is safe—you cannot visually distinguish safe molds from toxic ones. Reject all visibly moldy foods. Exception: hard cheeses can sometimes be salvaged by cutting away mold. Do not scrape or skim mold, lid growth, rim growth, or surface film off jams, jellies, sugary preserves, or fruit spreads; discard them.

### Insect and Pest Contamination

Living insects in food indicate infestation. Dead insects indicate historical infestation. Insect feces appear as small dark specks in grain or flour. Light infestation (few insects) can be sifted or picked out; heavy infestation means food is waste. Rodent contamination (droppings, chewing damage) requires immediate rejection—rodent feces transmit hantavirus and other serious pathogens.

### Rancidity in Fats and Oils

Rancid oils and fats smell unpleasant (sharp, chemical, off-smell) and taste bitter. Rancidity indicates fat oxidation and potential formation of harmful compounds. Rancid oil can cause digestive upset. When in doubt, smell is the indicator—rancid smells obviously wrong. Reject.

### Freezer Burn

Freezer burn appears as gray or white discoloration on frozen items and indicates ice crystal formation from thawing and refreezing cycles. Freezer-burned items are safe to eat but reduced quality. Use in cooked dishes where quality degradation isn't noticeable (soups, stews, ground meat dishes).

<section id="troubleshooting">

## Troubleshooting

### Problem: Uncertainty About Canned Good Safety

Solution: Use the conservative rule: if bulging or leaking, reject unopened. If seams show rust, reject. If you opened it and it hisses (pressure release), reject. If smell is off, reject without taste-testing. If unsure, reject. Uncertain cans are not worth risking botulism.

### Problem: Dry Goods Have Insect Infestation

Solution: (1) Light infestation—sift through cloth or screen to remove insects; (2) Heavy infestation—freeze items for 2+ weeks (if freezer available) to kill insects and eggs, or seal in airtight container for 1-2 months to suffocate insects; (3) As last resort, use infested items in cooking where insects are unnoticeable (soups, stews). Never eat heavy infestation raw.

### Problem: Frozen Food is Still Partially Thawed

Solution: If ice crystals remain and thawing occurred in cool conditions (below 40°F), refreezing is safe. If thawed at room temperature for more than 2 hours, discard meat; vegetables tolerate longer thaw times. If food is still frozen but partially soft, it's safe to refreeze.

### Problem: Food Smells Off But You're Not Sure

Solution: Never risk it. Smell is your best indicator of spoilage. Reject any food that smells wrong, including food that changed smell after drying or repackaging. Do not taste-test to confirm. Survival food comes from large salvage operations; eliminating questionable items doesn't create scarcity.

### Problem: Can is Rusty But Still Sealed

Solution: Surface rust on outside is cosmetic and acceptable. Seam rust (rust along sealed edges) is more concerning. If seam is compromised (rust pit visible), reject. If seam rust is superficial, the can is likely acceptable. Press ends to confirm rigidity. If flexible, reject.

<section id="common-mistakes">

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Prioritizing Bulk Over Safety

Salvaging 100 lbs of questionable food and risking contamination is worse than salvaging 50 lbs of confirmed safe food. Quality over quantity. Reject marginal items.

### Mistake 2: Assuming Expiration Dates Are Safety Limits

Commercial canned goods are often safe years past printed dates. Expiration dates reflect peak quality, not safety. Exception: home-canned goods should be treated as time-critical (botulism risk). Never eat home-canned goods older than 2-3 years regardless of appearance.

### Mistake 3: Mixing Unknown-Source Foods

Store newly salvaged food separately from stored inventory until assessed. Contamination (mold spores, insects, pathogens) spreads from unknown-source foods to stored items.

### Mistake 4: Trusting Appearance Over Smell

A can can look perfect and contain botulism toxin. When you open it, smell is your final indicator. Strange smell = rejection. Trust your nose above all visual indicators.

### Mistake 5: Cooking Questionable Food to Eliminate Risk

Cooking does not eliminate botulism toxin (heat-stable); cooking may reduce other pathogens but not guarantee safety. Never cook your way to safety with questionable food. Reject first.

<section id="safety">

## Safety Considerations

### Handling Unknown-Source Foods

Wear gloves when handling salvaged food. Wash hands thoroughly after contact. Food-borne pathogens are invisible and asymptomatic in the early stages of infection. Treat all salvage as potentially contaminated until proven safe through careful assessment.

:::warning
Food poisoning from salmonella or other pathogens causes severe dehydration in survival situations where medical care is limited. Prevention through careful assessment is critical.
:::

### Safe Food Preparation from Salvage

Cook salvaged meat to proper internal temperatures (use meat thermometer if available). Bring broth-based soups to rolling boil before serving. Avoid eating raw vegetables unless you're certain of water source safety (salvaged vegetables may have been watered with contaminated water). Store leftover cooked food in cool conditions (<40°F) and consume within 2 hours at room temperature, up to 3-4 days in cool storage.

### Freezer Loss Protocol

If power loss to freezer occurs: (1) Don't open freezer—leave closed to maintain temperature as long as possible; (2) Full freezer maintains safe temps ~48 hours; half-full lasts ~24 hours; (3) Once temperature rises above 40°F, meat becomes unsafe; (4) Begin consuming frozen items in priority order (meats first, vegetables last); (5) Don't refreeze thawed meat unless still partially frozen or thawed in refrigeration.
