## Overview: Importance & Yield

Falconry and raptor husbandry represent a centuries-proven method for sustainable protein procurement and pest control in low-tech environments. Unlike agriculture requiring land preparation and seasonal cycles, falconry provides immediate returns: a trained raptor begins productive hunting within 6-12 weeks of initial training and sustains itself on 40-60 grams of daily food consumption.

**Caloric Yield:** A single trained raptor achieves a 30-40% success rate, averaging 1-2 kills per hunting session in good habitat (0-1 kills in marginal habitat), 3-5 sessions per week. Average prey (rabbit, pigeon, grouse) weighs 150-300g with approximately 250-400 calories per specimen. This yields approximately 750-4,000 calories weekly per bird (depending on habitat quality and success consistency), or 107-570 calories daily. For a household of 5-8 people requiring 10,000-12,000 combined daily calories, three trained raptors supply 10-25% of protein needs with zero ammunition expenditure.

**Difficulty Level:** Advanced—requires 4-6 months to achieve reliable hunting, 2+ years to master multiple species. Falconry demands consistent daily attention, precise weight management, detailed record-keeping, and willingness to recover from training setbacks. Unlike passive food preservation, falconry is an active skill requiring continuous engagement.

**Historical Context**

Falconry is one of humanity's oldest sustained hunting partnerships, documented for over 4,000 years across Europe, the Middle East, Central Asia, and China. Falconry represents the pinnacle of animal training and the foundation for human-predator collaboration. In post-collapse scenarios, this knowledge becomes practical survival craft: raptors require minimal resource investment compared to firearm ammunition, reproduce independently, and can sustain hunters through seasons when other game is scarce.

Medieval and Renaissance falconry supported entire noble households, with elaborate hierarchies determining which raptors belonged to which social classes. This wasn't pomp—it was resource management. A single trained hawk could supply 300-500 meals per year in optimal conditions. Understanding historical training methods reveals principles underlying modern falconry: consistency, patience, respect for animal natural drives, and precise weight management.

The cultural knowledge of falconry survived because it works. The same Manning and recall techniques used by 12th-century Arab falconers remain standard today because they align with raptor neurobiology and instinct. In a world without modern supply chains, this ancient craft becomes essential survival knowledge.

## Climate & Environmental Requirements

Raptors thrive across diverse climates but performance and survival probability vary significantly by species and geography.

**Temperature Tolerances:**
- Harris's Hawks: 5°C to 40°C (42°F to 104°F). Tolerate heat well; can hunt successfully in afternoon temperatures 35-40°C if water provided. Cold tolerance adequate for northern climates; below 5°C requires insulated roosting shelter.
- Red-Tailed Hawks: -10°C to 38°C (14°F to 100°F). Excellent cold tolerance; molt provides natural insulation. In extreme cold (below -10°C), energy expenditure increases 30-50% requiring additional feeding.
- Goshawks: -15°C to 32°C (5°F to 90°F). Cold specialists adapted for boreal forest. Poor heat tolerance; above 32°C causes lethargy and reduced hunting drive.
- Peregrine Falcons: 0°C to 35°C (32°F to 95°F). Open-country specialists; perform best in cool seasons. Heat and humidity compromise diving ability.

**Humidity & Altitude:**
Humidity 20-70% optimal. Above 75% increases fungal disease risk (aspergillosis). Below 20% causes respiratory distress. Altitude up to 2,000 meters acceptable; above 2,500 meters reduces oxygen availability and hunting performance.

**Wind & Weather:**
Moderate winds (5-20 km/h) improve hunting success; strong winds (above 25 km/h) make flight dangerous and recovery risky. Avoid hunting 24 hours before/after heavy rain—wet plumage impairs aerodynamics. Thunderstorm lightning risk is severe; do not fly birds during electrical storms.

**Habitat Specifics:**
- Grassland/savanna: Harris's Hawks, Red-tails, Peregrines. Requires 5-20 hectares per bird for sustainable hunting.
- Forest/woodland: Goshawks (dense coniferous), Red-tails (mixed). Requires 50-100 hectares per bird—lower prey density requires larger territories.
- Urban/suburban: Red-tails adapt well to city peripheries. Requires 10-15 hectares; prey (pigeons, rats, squirrels) often abundant.
- Wetland/marsh: Marsh hawks (if available). Requires open water and tall vegetation; 10-30 hectares.

:::info-box
**Key Fact:** Raptors require territory with 15-40% vegetation cover for stalking; too dense (>80%) prevents effective hunting, too open (<10%) offers no escape cover. Survey your hunting area's vegetation structure before committing to a species.
:::

## Falconry as Food Procurement & Acquisition

Birds of prey represent a supplemental but reliable food source in survival situations. A trained raptor can secure 2-5 pieces of quarry per hunting session, with minimal ammunition expenditure compared to firearms. This makes falconry particularly valuable when ammunition is scarce or rationed. The training investment pays dividends over months or years of consistent hunting.

In post-collapse scenarios, a single trained raptor becomes equivalent to one consistent hunter. Unlike dogs, hawks don't require large quantities of food—they consume 40-60 grams per day, roughly equivalent to one small kill. This makes them sustainable on the biomass of a moderate hunting territory. Three to four trained raptors can provide year-round protein supplementation for a household of 6-8 people when supplemented with other hunting methods.

Falconry's advantage over trapping is selectivity: a hawk hunts live prey matching its preference, whereas snares catch indiscriminately. Falconry also provides psychological benefit—the partnership with a raptor creates a reliable routine and reinforces hunting skill development in younger handlers.

**Acquisition Methods:**
In modern times, falconry requires permits and breeder connections. In post-collapse scenarios without institutional oversight, three acquisition paths exist: trapping passage birds (young, migrating raptors in late summer/early fall), capturing breeding adults (difficult and dangerous), or breeding captive pairs (long-term strategy). Passage bird capture is most practical for beginners.

## Raptor Species Selection Guide

**Use this decision tree to choose the best species for your goals and environment.**

### Purpose-Based Selection

**If you want to hunt small game (sparrows, mice, voles):**
→ **Kestrel** (smallest falcon, 85-115g)
- Difficulty: Beginner-friendly
- Space required: 5-10 hectares grassland
- Training time: 8-12 weeks
- Kills/week: 4-6 small prey
- Notes: Docile, excellent for first-time handlers, some loss to predators due to size

**If you want to hunt rabbits and medium game:**
→ **Harris's Hawk or Red-Tailed Hawk**
- Harris's: Beginner-Intermediate difficulty, 700-1200g, 6-10 weeks training
- Red-Tail: Intermediate difficulty, 900-1500g, 10-16 weeks training
- Habitat: Grassland to mixed woodland
- Space: 10-20 hectares
- Kills/week: 6-15 medium-sized prey (rabbits, squirrels, small birds)

**If you want to hunt waterfowl (ducks, geese):**
→ **Peregrine Falcon** or **Marsh Hawk** (if available)
- Difficulty: Advanced
- Space required: Open wetlands, 15-30 hectares
- Training time: 14-20 weeks
- Kills/week: 4-8 waterfowl
- Notes: Fast aerial hunter, requires open water and wetland habitat

**If you want to hunt in dense forest:**
→ **Northern Goshawk** (dense woods specialist)
- Difficulty: Advanced
- Space required: 50-100 hectares of dense forest
- Training time: 16-24 weeks
- Temperament: Aggressive, demanding, less forgiving of handler errors
- Kills/week: 5-10 medium prey (grouse, squirrels)
- Notes: NOT recommended for beginners; requires experienced falconer

### Environment-Based Selection

**Grassland/Open Terrain (<10% trees):**
- Best choice: Harris's Hawk or Peregrine Falcon
- Why: Open country hunters; don't need perching between hunts
- Training: 6-20 weeks depending on species

**Mixed Terrain (30-50% trees, meadows, scrub):**
- Best choice: Red-Tailed Hawk (versatile) or Harris's Hawk (social)
- Why: Equally comfortable hunting from perches or open ground
- Training: 8-16 weeks

**Dense Forest (>70% trees):**
- Best choice: Northern Goshawk (difficult but designed for forest)
- Why: Short-winged, highly maneuverable in tight spaces
- Training: 16-24 weeks (expert-level)
- WARNING: Not recommended unless you already have falconry experience

**Wetland/Marsh:**
- Best choice: Peregrine Falcon or Marsh Hawk
- Why: Specialized for hunting over water
- Training: 14-20 weeks (advanced)

### Experience Level & Difficulty

**Beginner (first raptor):**
- Recommended: Harris's Hawk or Kestrel
- Why: Forgiving temperament, quick training, good food drive, tolerate handler errors
- Timeline: 6-10 weeks to first hunt
- Space minimum: 5-15 hectares
- Expected kills: 1,500-3,000 calories/week from hunting

**Intermediate (2+ years experience):**
- Options: Red-Tailed Hawk, Merlin, American Kestrel variants
- Why: More challenging but still forgiving; better performance
- Timeline: 10-16 weeks to proficiency
- Space: 15-30 hectares
- Expected kills: 3,000-5,000 calories/week

**Advanced (5+ years experience):**
- Options: Goshawk, Peregrine Falcon, hybrid combinations
- Why: Maximum hunting performance; require constant attention
- Timeline: 14-24+ weeks to true proficiency
- Space: 20-100+ hectares (species-dependent)
- Expected kills: 4,000-7,500 calories/week

## Trapping Wild Raptors

The bal-chatri trap (a small pigeon crate with snares) and bow nets are effective for capturing passage juveniles—young birds migrating for the first time, which have not yet learned survival skills. Initial capture requires quiet handling with a hood applied immediately to calm the bird. Keep stress minimal during the first handling to avoid injury from panic.

Passage birds (late summer to early fall) are ideal first hawks, as they lack ingrained wild behaviors and adapt more readily to captivity and training than adult birds. Targeting juveniles has a secondary benefit: their natural instinct to remain with parental figures (as they would in the wild) transfers to the handler.

![Bal-Chatri trap diagram showing wooden frame, pigeon bait, snare loops, and net covering](../assets/svgs/falconry-raptors-2.svg)

**Bal-Chatri Construction:** Build a wooden frame 12-18 inches on each side with 1-inch mesh. Place a live pigeon in the center (confined but visible). Arrange 6-8 snare loops around perimeter using strong nylon cord (6-8 lb test). Cover top with net secured at corners. The live pigeon provides movement and visual stimulus; a raptor striking it becomes entangled in snares while the net prevents escape.

**Bow Nets:** More effective for larger hawks. Set multiple bow nets (3-5) along open corridors where raptors hunt. Place live bait (pigeons or rabbits) visible beneath each net. When a raptor lands on the net frame to grab bait, the wooden springs collapse the net over the bird. Less traumatic than snares if monitored every 15 minutes.

**Post-Capture Handling:** Immediately upon capture, apply a blind hood (even a makeshift cloth covering) to calm the bird. Keep the bird in a dimly lit, quiet space for the first 4-6 hours. Handle gently but firmly to prevent injury from struggling. Offer water within 2 hours but delay food until the bird has calmed significantly.

## Growth Stages & Training Timeline

**Weeks 1-3: Capture & Initial Adaptation (Manning Phase)**
Immediately upon capture, hood the bird and place in quiet, dimly lit enclosure (6+ foot dimensions minimum). Handler provides no handling—observe at distance only. Offer food (pre-killed prey) twice daily at consistent times. By day 3-4, bird should begin eating. Allow 3-4 days of no disturbance before introducing glove. Weeks 2-3, place gloved hand near perch, gradually move closer. By week 3, bird tolerates glove presence without panic (ruffled feathers and striking indicate insufficient manning—extend this phase by 1-2 weeks).

**Weeks 4-6: Weight Management & Glove Training**
Establish precise weight target (85-95% natural flying weight). Weigh daily at dawn using gram scale. Chart weight trends. Begin 15-minute glove sessions daily—place bird on glove for quiet sitting, no reward expected. Gradually increase session duration to 30 minutes. By week 6, bird should sit calmly on glove for 45+ minutes without bating. This phase is critical—inadequate manning causes recall failures later.

**Weeks 7-10: Recall Indoors (Pre-Flight Training)**
Introduce whistle signal (consistent 2-3 second tone). Toss small meat piece (5-10g) while sounding whistle. Bird eats reward, creates association: whistle = food. Repeat 10-15 times per session, twice daily. Extend distance from 1 meter to 2 meters to 3 meters over 2-week period. Success rate should reach 95%+ (bird responds on first whistle) before outdoor training.

**Weeks 11-16: Outdoor Recall & Creance Flying**
Transition to outdoor training area (open field, 50+ meter diameter). Introduce creance (20-30 meter light cord attached to jesses). Release bird in open area, allow 15-20 feet of flight. Sound whistle, toss meat reward. Catch bird as it lands, reward again. Repeat 5-10 times per session. Gradually extend creance length to full 30 meters. By week 14-16, bird should return reliably from 30 meters at whistle. Success rate should be 90%+ before removing leash.

**Weeks 17-24: Free Flight Hunting (Operational Phase)**
Remove creance and transition to free hunting flight. Initial flights: fly bird in area with minimal wild prey (or time flights for low prey activity like early morning). Allow 20-30 minute flights. Handler should remain visible and nearby. Reward all returns with food. Gradually increase flight duration to 60-90 minutes as bird demonstrates consistent return. By week 20-24, bird should hunt independently while returning reliably when signaled.

:::info-box
**Key Timeline:** Full training cycle from capture to reliable free-flight hunting requires 16-24 weeks of consistent daily work (never skip more than 1 day per week). Rushing any phase causes recall failures or escape risk. Budget minimum 1-2 hours daily for training, feeding, equipment maintenance, and log keeping.
:::

## Training Fundamentals & Methods

**Manning:** Allow 1-3 weeks for the bird to become accustomed to human presence. Start with quiet observation and minimal interaction. Successful Manning means the bird perches calmly on the glove for 15+ minutes without stress behaviors (bating, ruffling, tail flaring). Daily 30-minute sessions with no attempts to grab the bird accelerates Manning. Soft speech and slow movements encourage calmness.

**Weight Management:** Maintain the bird at 85-95% of its natural free-feeding weight. This is critical—overweight birds lack motivation, underweight birds become dangerous and stressed. Weigh daily at the same time (early morning is standard). Chart weight trends. A Harris's Hawk should stabilize at 900-950g for training; a Red-tail at 1050-1100g. Small daily adjustments (5-10g) prevent sudden drops or climbs that stress the bird.

**Recall Training:** Start at close range (1 meter) with food rewards, gradually extending to 100+ meters. Use a whistle or verbal cue consistently. The bird must associate the signal with reward reliably. Begin indoors or in a confined space. Toss a small piece of meat while sounding the whistle. Reward the bird immediately upon approach. Extend distance by 2-3 meters per successful session over 2-3 weeks. Never attempt outdoor free flight recall until the bird responds reliably indoors at 10+ meters.

**Quarry Introduction:** Introduce live prey only after solid recall and basic obedience. Start with easy prey like pigeons or rabbits in controlled settings. Use a freshly caught pigeon tethered by a 6-foot cord in an open area. Allow the bird to hunt and strike while you remain nearby. This "creance flying" (flying on a long cord) lets the bird experience hunting while remaining under control. Progress to longer cords (15-20 feet) over 3-4 weeks before attempting free flight hunting.

:::tip
**Tip:** Keep a detailed training log. Record date, weight, distance of recall, any refusals, weather conditions, and quarry type. Over months, patterns emerge showing which conditions your bird hunts best in and how weather affects performance.
:::

## Water & Nutrient Management

**Daily Water Requirements:**
Raptors require fresh water daily for drinking and bathing. Provide a shallow dish (3-5 cm depth) with 500-1000 mL capacity. Change water twice daily to prevent contamination. In hot weather (above 30°C), increase frequency to three changes daily or provide larger volume. Dehydration causes lethargy, reduced appetite, and poor hunting performance. Always offer water before morning feeding.

**Feeding Schedule:**
Raptors consume one meal daily in natural wild state; captive birds can tolerate daily feeding. Optimal practice: offer single feeding late afternoon (14:00-16:00), allowing overnight fasting which sharpens hunting appetite. Daily intake varies by species:

- Harris's Hawk: 40-60g (1-2 small prey items)
- Red-Tail: 60-90g (1-1.5 larger prey items)
- Goshawk: 50-80g (smaller, higher metabolism)
- Peregrine Falcon: 50-75g (specialized bird prey)

Provide whole prey (bones, organs, skin intact). Never feed muscle-only meat—lacks essential minerals and fat-soluble vitamins. Vary prey species weekly: pigeons, sparrows, rabbits, mice, grouse. Each species offers different nutrient profiles.

**Molting & Seasonal Adjustments:**
During molt (6-10 weeks annually), increase daily calories by 15-20% to support feather regrowth. A molting Harris's Hawk requires 50-75g instead of 40-60g. Provide organ-rich prey (livers, hearts) more frequently. Winter feeding: increase by 10% in temperatures below 0°C to compensate for energy loss through cold exposure. Monitor weight weekly during seasonal transitions.

:::warning
**Warning:** Overfeeding causes obesity, reduces hunting motivation, and shortens lifespan. Underfeeding causes stress, compromises immunity, and increases injury risk. Weekly weight should vary by no more than 5-10g. Erratic fluctuations indicate feeding inconsistency—stabilize immediately.
:::

<section id="equipment">

## Equipment (Field-Craftable)

**Glove:** Double-layer leather, fitted to protect the forearm from talons. Essential for safe handling. Fashion from stiff leather (deer hide, cow leather, or vegetable-tanned) reinforced with canvas or burlap on the inner surface. The glove should reach mid-forearm (8-10 inches long) and fit snugly—a loose glove shifts during landing and can cause talon injuries. Include a removable wrist strap to prevent the bird from slipping off.

![Falconry equipment layout showing glove, jesses, hood, perch, and bells with labels](../assets/svgs/falconry-raptors-3.svg)

**Jesses:** Leather leg strips attached to the bird's legs, allowing you to tether and control the bird during training and rest periods. Cut from 1/4-inch leather in strips 6-8 inches long and 1/2 inch wide. Create a small loop at one end (slit-sewn or riveted) to secure to the bird's leg. The opposite end slides through a flight-line knot. Quality jesses prevent leg injury—ensure no sharp edges or rough seams.

**Hood:** Covers the bird's eyes to keep it calm and focused. Can be crafted from soft leather with proper ventilation holes. A well-fitted hood prevents the bird from bating during transport and rest periods. Include two small air holes near the nostrils and cover the eyes snugly but not painfully. The hood should come off cleanly in one smooth motion—a poorly fitted hood causes stress. Practice hood application 20+ times before hunting.

**Perch:** Bow-shaped perch for hawks; block perch for falcons. Should allow comfortable standing without stress to feet. A properly sized bow perch has a 6-8 inch diameter. Sand the wood smooth to prevent splinters. Attach to a stable base or secure mounting point. Some handlers use a portable perch with a ground stake for field use—this prevents the bird from sitting on the ground or moving out of sight.

**Bells:** Attached to the bird's tail or legs for tracking in dense vegetation. Audible from 100+ meters away. Use small falconry bells (1-2 oz) rather than large bells which restrict movement. Attach with leather glue and thread to the outermost tail feathers or via small leather anklets on the legs. Test bells for consistent ringing before attachment.

**Leash System:** Construct a light but strong leash from paracord or braided leather. Attach via a falconer's knot at one end (allows quick detachment) and a swivel connector at the bird's end (prevents tangling). A 6-foot leash suffices for training; longer lines (15-20 feet) allow greater movement during creance flying.

## Pest & Disease Management

**Fungal Infections (Aspergillosis):**
Most serious health threat. Caused by fungal spores from moldy food or bedding. Symptoms: wheezing, labored breathing, reduced flight endurance, lethargy. Incubation: 2-4 weeks from exposure. Prevention: store fresh food at <10°C, replace bedding weekly, ensure good ventilation (minimum 4 air changes per enclosure per day). Treatment: if available, systemic antifungal agents (itraconazole 5-10 mg/kg). Prognosis: 40-60% survival with early treatment; untreated is fatal within 4-8 weeks.

**Parasitic Infections:**
External parasites (mites, lice) visible as tiny insects or feather degradation. Treat with food-grade diatomaceous earth dusted on perch (bird grooms and ingests), or by isolation and manual removal. Internal parasites (worms) indicated by weight loss despite feeding or loose stools. Prevention: rotate prey sources, avoid feeding dead rodents of unknown origin, maintain sanitation. Treatment: vermifuges (if available) or dietary change to proven parasite-free prey for 4 weeks.

**Bacterial Infections (Bumblefoot):**
Swelling and infection on foot pads, typically from unsanitary perches or puncture wounds. Early signs: redness, mild swelling, bird favoring one foot. Treatment: clean perch thoroughly with bleach solution, disinfect foot (iodine or alcohol if available), provide soft padded perch for 5-7 days. Apply topical antibiotics if available. Prevention: sand perches smooth, inspect feet daily, replace perch bedding weekly.

**Trichomonosis:**
Parasitic infection causing yellow plaques in throat, difficulty swallowing, reduced appetite. Most common in birds sharing water with wild pigeons. Prevention: provide individual water dishes, avoid contact with wild bird populations. Treatment: metronidazole (if available, 50 mg/kg) or deprivation diet (fast bird for 24 hours to disrupt parasite). Prognosis: 70-80% survival with treatment.

**Preventive Practices:**
- Daily health inspection: eyes bright? Posture upright? Appetite normal? Droppings white/brown, not liquid? Feathers smooth, not ruffled?
- Weekly perch/enclosure cleaning with hot water (no chemicals near food areas)
- Monthly weight trending (plot on graph to detect slow decline)
- Quarterly prey source rotation to prevent pathogen accumulation
- Isolation protocol: any sick bird immediately separated to prevent herd transmission

:::info-box
**Critical Symptom Recognition:** A healthy raptor is alert, responsive, maintains smooth feather posture, and eats vigorously. Any deviation—lethargy, ruffled feathers, reduced appetite, or abnormal droppings—requires immediate isolation and assessment. Early intervention prevents 70% of health crises.
:::

<section id="hunting-practice">

## Hunting Practice & Habitat Deployment

Successful hunting requires matching habitat to species capability. Scout terrain before hunts. Work with one hunter flushing game while the other manages the bird. Share quarry—never let the bird gorge alone, as this promotes independence from training.

Daily limits: Hunt 2-3 hours per session, 3-5 days per week. More frequent hunting can exhaust the bird and damage your training investment. Allow rest days for recovery and bonding. Monitor the bird's physical condition—ruffled feathers, drooping posture, or reduced appetite signal fatigue.

Record successful flights and quarry in a log. This data helps refine your technique and understand seasonal patterns in your hunting area. Track: date, time, location, weather, wind direction, prey species, success/failure, and behavioral notes.

**Habitat Matching:** Harris's Hawks excel in open grassland with scattered brush. Deploy them in prairie or savanna environments. Red-tails work across forest edges and open fields—train them for mixed terrain. Goshawks require dense forest with clearings for approach angles. Peregrines demand open sky and high-flying opportunities.

**Pre-Hunt Protocol:** Arrive at hunting grounds 30+ minutes before peak activity. Establish the bird on a portable perch in a sheltered spot. Scout for fresh sign (droppings, feathers, recent kills) indicating active prey. Begin with the bird on the glove, offering opportunities for short flights (5-10 minutes) to warm muscles before extended hunting.

:::warning
**Warning:** Never hunt with an undertrained bird or in populated areas. A bird that escapes can become a genuine wild predator and poses risk to small pets and poultry. Always maintain secure control via jesses or leash during approach to hunting grounds. Free flight should only occur in remote areas where an escaped bird cannot threaten domestic animals or humans.
:::

</section>

<section id="harvest-storage">

## Harvest & Storage of Quarry

**Signs of Hunting Success:**
- Bird stoops (dives) with speed and accuracy
- Makes audible strike (slap of talons on prey)
- Secures prey, pins wings, subdues target
- Calls out (prey acquired signal)
- Carries quarry to consumption area

Failed hunts show: aborted stoops, hesitant pursuit, prey escapes despite pursuit. Log success rate (kills/flights attempted). Target 30-50% success rate for experienced hunters.

**Prey Preparation:**
Raptors eat entire small prey (whole). For larger prey (rabbits 500g+), remove fur/skin at first catch site to avoid contamination. Bird consumes organs, head, legs, and soft tissue. Discard large bones, pelt. Transport fresh kill in canvas bag or burlap (not plastic—retains moisture and causes spoilage). Keep kill temperature <15°C until consumption.

**Human Consumption:**
If using raptor-caught game for household food, ensure proper field dressing: eviscerate within 2 hours of kill, remove organs, cool meat rapidly. Fresh rabbit or grouse from raptor kill is high-quality game meat. Skin and butcher cleanly. Safe consumption requires proper storage and cooking. Cook to internal temperature 165°F (74°C) to eliminate pathogens.

**Storage Conditions:**
- Fresh whole prey: <10°C, maximum 24 hours
- Refrigerated (if available): 3-5 days
- Frozen: 3-6 months at -15°C or colder
- Salted/dried: 1-3 months at <15°C, dry environment
- Cured meat: 2-6 months depending on salt ratio and humidity

:::tip
**Tip:** In post-collapse scenarios, freeze or salt excess kills immediately. A single hunting session might yield 2-5 kills—immediate preservation prevents spoilage and provides emergency food reserves. Salt-cure technique: salt-coat entire prey, wrap in cloth, hang in cool, dark, dry location. Check weekly for mold; if present, increase salt or reduce humidity exposure.
:::

**Per-Bird Output:**

- Red-Tailed Hawk: 2-3 kills per session, 3-4 sessions/week = 6-12 kills/week, or 300-600 kills/year
- Goshawk: 2-4 kills per session, 2-3 sessions/week = 4-12 kills/week, or 200-600 kills/year

- One Harris's Hawk: 80-200 kg annual harvest = 320,000-800,000 calories/year

- Summer (molt): -80% yield; hunting impossible during feather molt

- Winter: +20% yield; cold concentrates prey but reduces activity

Plan food procurement accounting for seasonal valleys. Maintain 25-30% raptor surplus capacity.

<section id="breeding-nesting">

## Breeding & Nesting

Captive breeding of raptors requires significant infrastructure, knowledge, and legal permits in most regions. However, in post-collapse scenarios where institutional oversight disappears, breeding becomes the path to raptor self-sufficiency—no longer dependent on trapping wild birds.

**Breeding Basics:** Raptors reach sexual maturity at 2-3 years old. Establish a compatible pair (pairing two birds requires careful observation—aggression is common). Provide a large flight enclosure (40+ feet long) with minimal human disturbance. Construct a breeding box mounted 8-12 feet high with a sloped roof to shed rain and a side entrance hole 3-4 inches in diameter.

**Nest Construction:** Stock the breeding box with sticks, bark, and dried vegetation. The birds will arrange nesting material themselves. Goshawks prefer stick nests; Harris's Hawks often use abandoned raptor or corvid nests. Red-tails build substantial stick nests 3-4 feet in diameter.

**Egg Incubation:** Raptors lay 2-4 eggs per clutch, spaced 2-3 days apart. Incubation takes 28-35 days depending on species. In wild settings, the female incubates while the male brings food. In captivity, do not interfere unless the bird abandons the clutch. If intervention is necessary, maintain eggs at 99.5°F (37.5°C) with 40-50% humidity using an artificial incubator. Rotate eggs 5-7 times daily. Eggs hatch over 2-3 days.

**Chick Rearing:** Newly hatched chicks ("eyasses") are vulnerable. Provide optimal nutrition to the breeding pair so they can feed chicks adequately. Fresh, whole prey (day-old chicks, small birds, or lean meat with bones and organs) supports chick growth. Chicks fledge (first flight) at 6-10 weeks depending on species. Provide climbing branches and flight space to encourage muscle development.

**Fledgling Management:** Allow fledged chicks to remain with parents for 2-4 weeks post-fledging while they learn hunting skills. Then separate for independent training and imprinting. A single breeding pair produces 2-4 trained raptors per year—enough to sustain a small falconry program indefinitely.

:::tip
**Tip:** Maintain detailed records of breeding pairs: parentage, genetic line, clutch size, hatch dates, fledging dates, and any anomalies. Over generations, this data allows selective breeding for temperament and hunting ability, improving your raptor stock.
:::

</section>

<section id="health-veterinary">

## Health & Veterinary Care

**Common Raptor Diseases:** Aspergillosis (fungal respiratory infection) causes wheezing and reduced performance. Prevent via clean housing and fresh air. Trichomonosis (parasitic infection) presents as yellow plaques in the throat. Treat with metronidazole (if available) or prevent via food hygiene. Bumblefoot (bacterial foot infection) develops from unsanitary perches or sharp objects. Prevent by maintaining pristine perch surfaces and daily foot inspection.

**Injury Assessment and Treatment:** Minor cuts and abrasions heal quickly if kept clean. For wing injuries, immobilize via wrapping with medical tape (apply loosely to avoid restricting breathing). Fractures require splinting: create a rigid support along the wing or leg using aluminum foil and tape, then rest the bird for 3-4 weeks. A bird with a fractured wing cannot hunt—assess whether treatment is feasible given available resources.

**Nutritional Requirements:** Raptors require whole prey (small birds, mice, rabbits, insects) containing bones, organs, and skin. Muscle meat alone causes nutritional deficiency. Calculate daily intake: Harris's Hawks consume 40-50g per day; larger species consume 60-100g daily. Vary prey species to ensure nutritional diversity. Offer "casting" (regurgitated pellets containing indigestible material like bones and fur)—normal, not pathological.

**Parasite Control:** External parasites (mites, lice) are visible on the bird's body. Treat by dusting with food-grade diatomaceous earth or applying topical parasiticides (if available). Internal parasites (worms) are harder to detect—signs include weight loss despite adequate feeding and loose stools. Treat with vermifuges if available, or prevent via careful food hygiene and rotating parasite-free prey sources.

**Molt Management:** Raptors molt annually, shedding and regrowing all feathers over 4-6 months. During molt, the bird is weakened and cannot fly effectively for hunting. Provide enhanced nutrition, stress reduction, and rest. Molting birds require 15-20% more calories than normal. Hunting during molt is impossible—plan rest periods accordingly and avoid molt season for critical food procurement.

**Stress Indicators:** Watch for ruffled feathers (persistent), reduced appetite, or behavioral changes (excessive bating, lack of movement). These signal stress or illness. Respond by reducing handling, increasing rest time, and consulting your training log for recent changes. Most stress-related issues resolve within 3-7 days of reduced disturbance.

<section id="seasonal-considerations">

## Seasonal Considerations

**Spring (March-May):** Raptors enter breeding season. Wild raptors begin courtship and nesting. Captive raptors may show reduced hunting interest due to hormonal changes. Use spring for facility maintenance, equipment repairs, and training new birds. If breeding is planned, provide appropriate nesting structures and reduce handling stress.

**Summer (June-August):** Peak molt season. Hunting becomes impractical as the bird sheds feathers and regrows plumage over 6-8 weeks. Provide enhanced nutrition and rest. Young-of-year raptors fledge; wild population pressure decreases. Use summer for chick rearing if breeding is occurring. Focus on gentle handling and acclimatization rather than hunting.

**Fall (September-November):** Optimal hunting season. Passage birds (young raptors in migration) are easiest to trap. Raptors complete molt and are in peak physical condition. Weather is mild (avoiding extreme heat and cold). Hunting opportunities peak—implement aggressive food procurement. Training new birds should commence in late August to build proficiency before winter.

**Winter (December-February):** Harsh conditions challenge raptors. Cold reduces prey activity and availability. Provide shelter (roosting boxes with insulation). Heating is often unnecessary for healthy birds, but wind protection is critical. Hunting continues but requires more effort for reduced rewards. Monitor weight carefully—underfeeding during cold causes hypothermia; overfeeding is difficult in scarcity.

**Molt Cycle Specifics:** Primary flight feathers molt first, reducing flying ability over 6-8 weeks. Secondary feathers follow. Tail feathers drop last. The molt creates a window of 2-3 months where the bird cannot hunt effectively. Track molt onset in your log—typically 2-3 weeks post-summer solstice. Plan food procurement tasks to use birds before molt begins.

## Scaling for Community

**Small Group (5-10 people):** Train 2-3 raptors. Assign one handler per bird to ensure consistency and bonding. A single Harris's Hawk and Red-tail team can sustain nutritional needs of a small household via 2-3 hunting sessions weekly. Establish rotating hunt days to share labor.

**Medium Community (50-150 people):** Establish a formalized falconry program with 8-12 trained raptors. Recruit 4-6 dedicated handlers and provide apprenticeship training (8-12 weeks per new handler). Build a central raptor facility with 4-6 flight enclosures (30 feet long minimum), perching stations, weathering areas, and breeding boxes. Rotate birds among handlers to prevent dependency on a single trainer. Implement food procurement targets: aim for 15-20 kills per week to supply protein for 50-100 people (3-4 oz per person weekly).

**Large Community (200-500 people):** Scale to 20-30 raptors across multiple species. Establish a formal training program and mentorship hierarchy: master falconer (1-2 people with 5+ years experience), certified handlers (6-8 people with 2+ years), and apprentices (8-10 new handlers rotating annually). Build infrastructure: two dedicated facility locations with environmental controls (predator-proof enclosures, weathering sheds, food storage, medical supplies). Implement selective breeding program—maintain 6-8 breeding pairs across species to achieve genetic sustainability and reduce dependence on trapping. Document all breeding records and chick training outcomes.

**Organizational Structure:** Establish clear protocols: daily health checks, weight monitoring, feeding schedules, hunting rotations, and apprenticeship standards. Designate roles: master falconer (policy and advanced training), head handler (daily operations), apprentices (chores and basic training), and a record keeper (logs and breeding genetics). Require weekly community council meetings to address issues, share data, and coordinate hunting schedules.

At average prey weight of 150-200g (rabbit/pigeon), that's 4.5-25 kg per month per bird. A 12-bird program: 54-300 kg monthly = 2-10 kg per person monthly for 50 people (exceeds minimum protein needs if supplemented with other hunting/farming). Large communities should aim for 25-30 raptors to ensure redundancy and growth.

<section id="failure-modes">

## Failure Modes & Troubleshooting

**Bird Refuses Recall:** Most common failure. Causes: overfeeding (bird sated and unmotivated), undertraining (insufficient repetitions), or prey distraction (wild prey nearby diverting attention). Solution: reduce daily food intake by 15-20g, return to close-range recall training (1-3 meters), and move to area with fewer prey distractions. Track weight obsessively—slight underfed state (92-94% flying weight) dramatically improves recall. Time cost: 2-4 weeks to rebuild reliability.

**Bird Becomes Independent/Bolts:** Trained bird ignores handler and hunts alone, or flies away mid-session. Causes: over-reward during initial training (bird learned it doesn't need handler), or escape opportunity (unhooded bird in unfamiliar area). Prevention: never reward successful quarry capture without handler involvement. Always share kill with bird during hunting. Recovery: if bird bolts, immediate search within 500 meters—many birds return to roosting sites. Set out live bait (pigeons) to lure back. Success rate: 30-50% recovery within 48 hours. If not recovered, bird is lost.

**Injury During Training:** Handler talon strike, bird tangled in leash, or collision with obstacles. Causes: improper glove fit, inadequate flight space, or swift bird movement. Prevention: wear proper double-layer glove, inspect equipment before each use, train in open spaces. Treatment: disinfect puncture wounds immediately (talon strikes harbor bacteria), assess movement for fractures, rest the bird. Minor injuries (surface cuts) heal within 5-7 days. Fractures require 3-4 weeks immobilization.

**Escape From Facility:** Bird flies out of enclosure during feeding or handling. Causes: gate left open, enclosure damage, or poor door protocol. Prevention: implement two-door access on all enclosures (prevents single-door escape), inspect netting weekly for holes, require handlers to verify door closure before leaving. Recovery: same as "Bolts" above—search immediately, set bait, listen for bells. Containment facility escape is more recoverable than free-flight escape.

**Aggression Toward Handler:** Bird bites, strikes, or displays persistent aggressive behavior. Causes: improper Manning (insufficient habituation), rough handling, or poor weight management. Solution: return to basic Manning—30+ minutes daily of quiet presence without handling. Do not attempt hunting until bird tolerates handling calmly. Gentle weight reduction (88-92% target) increases docility. Severe aggression may require retiring the bird from program (assign to breeding only). Time cost: 4-8 weeks.

**Poor Hunting Success:** Bird trained correctly but consistently fails to catch prey. Causes: species-habitat mismatch, poor prey selection, or low bird physical condition. Diagnosis: observe multiple hunts—does the bird pursue? Strike accurately? Lack aerobic fitness? Solution: match species to habitat (Goshawks in forest, Harris's Hawks in open ground), improve prey availability (stock area with easier targets), increase conditioning flights (daily 15-20 minute flights to build muscle), or reduce daily food 5-10g to sharpen hunger drive. Time cost: 3-8 weeks improvement.

**Molt-Related Performance Drop:** Bird loses feathers and stops hunting. This is not a failure—it is normal biology. Prevention: plan seasonal rotations so molting birds rest while fresh birds hunt. Maintain 25%+ spare birds beyond immediate needs to cover molt season. Provide enhanced nutrition during molt (15-20% extra calories). Monitor molt timeline (typically 6-10 weeks) and adjust hunting schedule accordingly.

**Nutritional Deficiency:** Bird loses weight despite adequate feeding, shows lethargy, or displays brittle feathers. Causes: inadequate nutrient diversity or parasites. Solution: vary prey species (alternate pigeons, rabbits, small birds, insects), ensure whole-prey feeding (not muscle-only meat), and treat for parasites if indicated (loose stools, excessive scratching). Add organ-rich prey (birds' organs are more nutrient-dense than muscle). Recovery: 2-4 weeks with corrected diet.

:::warning
**Warning:** Never attempt to force-feed a struggling bird or restrain it excessively. Stress-induced death ("capture myopathy") can occur within hours in severely stressed raptors. If a bird appears panicked or unresponsive during handling, immediately place it in a quiet, dark space and minimize intervention. Most recover within 1-2 hours of undisturbed rest.
:::

<section id="common-mistakes">

## Common Mistakes & Consequences

**Mistake 1: Insufficient Manning (Early Introduction to Glove)**
Consequence: Bird never fully accepts handler, maintains wild fear response, becomes unpredictable during flights, high escape risk. Prevents reliable recall training.
Prevention: Allocate minimum 3-4 weeks for Manning phase. If bird shows stress after 3 weeks (persistent bating, ruffled feathers), extend by additional 2 weeks. Never rush to handling.

**Mistake 2: Erratic Feeding Schedule**
Consequence: Bird weight fluctuates 20-30g daily, creating unpredictable hunting motivation. Inconsistent recall. Increased stress and health issues.
Prevention: Weigh daily same time (dawn), feed same time (late afternoon), adjust by 5g maximum per day based on trend. Maintain detailed weight log.

**Mistake 3: Single-Prey Diet**
Consequence: Nutritional deficiency despite adequate calories. Brittle feathers, low immunity, reduced hunting success. Bird becomes sick or fails to molt properly.
Prevention: Rotate prey weekly (Monday: pigeon, Tuesday: rabbit, Wednesday: sparrow, etc.). Ensure whole prey only.

**Mistake 4: Over-Reward During Hunting**
Consequence: Bird learns that successful kill doesn't require handler. Becomes independent, ignores recall signal, escapes.
Prevention: Handler always assists in final kill phase. Always share meal—never let bird gorge alone. Bring secondary food piece to offer during hunt to maintain handler association.

**Mistake 5: Training in Populated Areas**
Consequence: Escaped bird becomes wild predator, kills domestic poultry, threatens human safety. Community conflict. Loss of bird.
Prevention: Free flight only in remote areas 5+ km from settlement. Even fully trained birds can bolt unexpectedly.

**Mistake 6: Inadequate Creance Flying Duration**
Consequence: Transitioning to free flight before bird demonstrates consistent 90%+ recall success. Bird escapes on first free flight.
Prevention: Minimum 2-3 weeks of creance flying (20-30 meter cord) with 95%+ success rate before removing leash.

**Mistake 7: Ignoring Health Warnings**
Consequence: Minor illness becomes severe within days. Aspergillosis, parasites, or infections progress to death.
Prevention: Daily health check 5 minutes (eyes, posture, appetite, feathers, droppings). Log any abnormalities. Treat immediately.

:::info-box
**Pattern Recognition:** 80% of failures stem from rushing training phases, inadequate record-keeping, or inconsistent handling. The bird's success is directly proportional to handler discipline and attention to detail. Invest time upfront—shortcuts cost months of lost training.
:::

<section id="climate-adaptations">

## Climate Adaptations & Regional Modifications

**Arid/Desert Regions (Annual rainfall <250 mm, temperature 35-45°C):**
- Water: Critical limiting factor. Provide larger water dishes (1-2 liters). Change 3-4 times daily. Scout hunting area for water sources (small mammals congregate near water).
- Species choice: Harris's Hawks excel in arid environments; Peregrines also suitable.
- Hunting: Shift to dawn/dusk when prey active. Avoid midday (35+ °C) unless necessary.
- Molt: Timing may shift 2-4 weeks earlier due to heat stress. Monitor closely.
- Housing: Provide shade structures (thatch, cloth), ensure airflow. Insulated roosts prevent overheating.

**Tropical Regions (Annual rainfall >1500 mm, high humidity 70-90%, temperature 25-35°C):**
- Disease: Aspergillosis and parasites flourish in moisture. Increase bedding changes to twice weekly. Ensure maximum ventilation.
- Prey: Often abundant but diverse. Monitor bird's success rate; may take time to identify preferred quarry.
- Species choice: Red-tails and Harris's Hawks adapt; Goshawks unsuitable (too humid).
- Food spoilage: Faster in heat/humidity. Use kills within 12 hours. Freeze or salt immediately.
- Water: Less critical (high humidity), but still provide fresh daily.

**Cold/Boreal Regions (Winter temperature <-10°C, snow cover 4-6 months):**
- Housing: Insulated roosting shelter (straw bedding, windbreak, not sealed). Raptors generate sufficient body heat if protected from wind.
- Feeding: Increase daily intake 10-15% to compensate for cold expenditure. Provide high-fat prey (whole prey with skin/organs).
- Water: Ensure liquid water always available. If frozen, break ice 2-3 times daily or provide slightly warm water (30-35°C).
- Molt: Delayed or compressed due to cold stress. Plan molt anticipation 2-4 weeks later than temperate zones.
- Hunting: Possible year-round but reduced success in heavy snow. Scout for prey concentration areas.
- Species: Goshawks and Red-tails excel in cold. Harris's Hawks tolerate but require extra food.

**Temperate/Mediterranean (Temperature -5 to 30°C, seasonal variation):**
- Ideal conditions for most species. Seasonal planning critical:
  - Spring: Allow breeding rest, maintenance work
  - Summer: Molt period, limited hunting
  - Fall: Peak hunting, aggressive prey procurement
  - Winter: Hunting possible with increased feeding

<section id="specific-species-advanced">

## Specific Species Troubleshooting

**Harris's Hawks - Dependency on Handler:** Solution: rotate handlers (introduces new humans into training). Ensure bird receives food from multiple people. Never let single handler become exclusive caretaker.

**Red-Tails - Unpredictable Bolting:** Cause: high prey drive + early independence from training. Prevention: never allow sustained successful hunts without handler involvement (share every kill). Return to close-range recall training regularly (monthly). Solution: Revert to creance flying (long cord) for 2-4 weeks to rebuild reliability.

**Goshawks - Extreme Aggression:** Often indicates mishandling or improper weight. Solution: immediate return to Manning (hands-off presence only) for 4 weeks. Increase weight to 92-94% target to calm bird. Gentle handling with gloved hand during feeding. If aggression persists, retire bird to breeding program.

**Peregrines - Excessive Height Seeking:** Normal for species (they hunt from altitude). Solution: train in areas with low vegetation (prairies, tundra) rather than mountains. Provide lower roost perches. Accept that Peregrines require different management (more time, more distance) than other species.

| Small birds | 30-80g | Medium | Harris's Hawks | Quick kills, sporadic |

:::tip
**Hunting Efficiency:** A Harris's Hawk averages 2-4 kills per hunting session (2-3 hours). A Red-tail averages 1-2 kills per session. Consistency improves with experience and bird condition. Track individual bird success rates—some birds are naturally better hunters than others (genetic + experience).

## Advanced Breeding & Genetics

### Selective Breeding Program

Establishing a breeding program for raptors requires long-term commitment and genetic knowledge. Key traits to select:

**Temperament:** Docile birds recover faster from training errors and bond more reliably with handlers. Selecting for calm temperament over 3-4 generations produces significantly easier-to-train lines.

**Hunting Drive:** Some birds naturally hunt more aggressively than others. Selecting breeding pairs that demonstrate high prey drive produces offspring with stronger hunting instinct.

**Size:** Larger birds require more food but can hunt larger prey. Smaller birds are nimbler in dense forest. Select for size appropriate to your hunting environment and food budget.

**Health Traits:** Birds with minimal disease susceptibility, strong immune response, and robust physiology are preferable. Track health records; avoid breeding birds with chronic issues.

### Record Keeping for Breeding

Maintain detailed pedigree records:
- **Parentage:** Parents' IDs, breeding year, clutch size
- **Hatch Date:** Exact date (affects age calculation)
- **Fledge Date:** When first flight occurred (6-10 weeks post-hatch typically)
- **Imprinting Success:** How quickly bird bonded with handler
- **Training Timeline:** When recalled at 1m, 5m, 10m, 50m, 100m
- **Hunting Success:** Kill count per session, prey types hunted successfully
- **Health:** Any injuries, illnesses, treatments given
- **Molt Timeline:** When molt began, when completed
- **Genetic Traits:** Eye color, plumage patterns, size at maturity

Over 5-10 years, this data reveals breeding correlations: "Offspring of Female A consistently train faster than offspring of Female B" or "Male C's genes produce larger birds suitable for rabbit hunting."

### Genetic Diversity Maintenance

In closed captive populations, genetic diversity erodes (inbreeding depression). Strategies:

**Population Management:** Maintain 6+ breeding pairs. Rotate genetics: avoid breeding siblings (if possible). Document all genetic lineages.

**Outcrossing:** Periodically introduce new birds from wild population (if legal/ethical). This introduces genetic diversity but risks introducing inferior temperament genes.

**Cryopreservation:** Advanced technique—freeze semen for future use. Allows genetic preservation without maintaining large live population. Expensive, requires expertise.

**Trade Network:** Establish relationships with other breeding falconers (if your post-collapse community extends to other groups). Exchange genetic material (eggs, chicks) to diversify breeding stock.

## Integrating Falconry into Community Food Systems

### Seasonal Hunting Plans

**Spring (March-May):** Breeding season for wild raptors. Hunting reduced. Use for facility maintenance, equipment repair, training new birds. Breeding birds require minimal disturbance.

**Summer (June-August):** Peak molt season (6-8 weeks). Hunt with non-molting birds. Provide enhanced nutrition to molting birds. Young fledged birds (from breeding program) available for training—start with 2-3 new birds this season.

**Fall (September-November):** Best hunting season. Wild juvenile raptors (passage birds) available for trapping. Establish new birds in September/early October for full season hunting. All birds in peak condition. Implement aggressive food procurement—3-5 hunts weekly with all available birds.

**Winter (December-February):** Harsh conditions reduce prey activity. Cold-stressed birds require shelter and extra food. Hunting yields diminish 20-40% compared to fall. Maintain hunting but accept reduced returns. Use winter for detailed record keeping and maintenance.

### Food Procurement Targets

**Small Household (5-10 people):** 1-2 trained raptors
- Weekly target: 4-8 kills = 0.5-1.5 kg meat
- Monthly yield: 16-32 kills = 2-6 kg meat
- Annual yield: 200-380 kills = 30-75 kg meat
- Nutritional contribution: 5-15% of household protein needs (supplemented by other hunting, farming)

**Medium Community (50-150 people):** 8-12 trained raptors
- Weekly target: 40-80 kills = 6-15 kg meat
- Monthly yield: 160-320 kills = 24-60 kg meat
- Annual yield: 2000-3800 kills = 300-750 kg meat
- Nutritional contribution: 1-5 kg per person monthly (4-20 oz weekly per person)

**Large Community (200-500 people):** 20-30 trained raptors
- Weekly target: 100-200 kills = 15-40 kg meat
- Monthly yield: 400-800 kills = 60-160 kg meat
- Annual yield: 5000-9500 kills = 750-1875 kg meat
- Nutritional contribution: 0.5-2 kg per person monthly (feasible to supply 25% of community protein needs)

### Skills Development & Mentorship

**Apprenticeship Model:** Master falconer trains 2-4 apprentices over 2-year periods. Each apprentice takes 1-2 birds, with master providing oversight and corrections. Timeline: 6 months to handle bird competently, 1 year to hunt reliably, 2 years to make independent decisions.

**Certification Levels:**
1. **Apprentice (Year 1):** Handle birds, execute training under supervision, record data
2. **Handler (Year 2+):** Train new birds independently, make tactical decisions, troubleshoot problems
3. **Master (5+ years):** Establish breeding program, mentor apprentices, strategic planning for program

**Knowledge Transfer:** Document all procedures in community records: training techniques, prey identification, seasonal scheduling, problem-solving. Prevents knowledge loss if key person becomes unavailable.
