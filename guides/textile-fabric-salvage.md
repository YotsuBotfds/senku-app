## Overview

Textile salvage represents one of the most valuable and overlooked resource categories in post-collapse scenarios. Fabric and clothing provide insulation, protection, shelter materials, and raw material for tool production—essential components of survival infrastructure. Unlike single-use items, textiles are durable, multifunctional, and degrade slowly when properly stored. A single salvaged wool sweater can be repurposed into insulation, patches, cordage, or emergency shelter material. This guide covers systematic assessment, location scouting, extraction, processing, and preservation of textile materials.

## Clothing Recovery & Repurposing

Salvaged clothing is perhaps the most immediately useful textile resource. In survival scenarios, the focus is on function and material properties rather than fit or fashion.

### Priority Materials: Wool, Denim, Synthetics, and Leather

Wool is the highest priority insulation material—it retains warmth even when wet, a critical survival advantage. Wet wool maintains 80% of its insulating properties due to the dense fiber structure trapping air. Cold weather survival demands wool clothing; prioritize wool sweaters, socks, long underwear, and blankets. Durable denim provides excellent abrasion resistance and protection—valuable for work garments. Jeans can be repurposed for leg protection, patches, cordage strips, and tool sheaths. Synthetic fibers (polyester, nylon, acrylic) are rot-resistant compared to natural fibers—they last longer in damp conditions and resist mold. Synthetic fleece provides lightweight insulation; synthetic underwear wicks moisture better than cotton. Leather (leather jackets, coats, shoes) provides durable external protection and weather resistance. Prioritize salvage in this order: wool > denim > other synthetics > cotton (heavy when wet, slow to dry) > leather (valuable for specialized applications).

### Sizing Flexibility in Survival Context

Clothing fit matters far less in survival scenarios than in normal life. An oversized jacket can be layered or adjusted; undersized clothing can be layered underneath. Prioritize material quality and insulation value over perfect fit. A wool sweater three sizes too large provides the same insulation as proper-fitting garment and can be belted or tied to prevent wind loss. Pants can be cuffed, folded, or tied at the ankles. Multiple small garments can be combined for layering, which provides superior insulation to single thick garment. The key principle: material > fit in survival scenarios. Don't discard otherwise useful clothing due to sizing—repurpose or adapt.

### Bulk Clothing Sources

Laundromats contain abandoned clothing left behind—useful source of recovered textiles. Retail clothing stores typically discard damaged merchandise to back rooms—look for donation bins or dumpsters (with permission). Donation centers (Goodwill, Salvation Army, etc.) have facilities storing bulk clothing—organized locations make efficient collection possible. Residential sources include clotheslines (worn but unwashed garments), closets (in abandoned homes), and laundry facilities (apartment complexes). Hospitals and medical facilities discard protective garments (surgical gowns, isolation gowns) which are lightweight, often synthetic, and useful for layering or weather protection. Focus collection efforts on buildings likely to contain bulk clothing rather than scattered residential sources.

### Washing and Decontamination

Any salvaged clothing from unknown sources requires washing before use (contamination risk including bacterial pathogens, mold spores, body lice). Without electricity, wash in collected water heated over fire (hot water kills most pathogens; cold water with mechanical agitation removes surface contamination). Boiling if possible (10+ minutes at rolling boil) eliminates most biological hazards. Hang dry in sunlight—UV light provides additional disinfection. For items with strong odors or visible contamination, soak in bleach solution (1 part bleach to 10 parts water) for 30 minutes, then rinse thoroughly. Alternatively, air exposure in direct sunlight for several days reduces pathogens naturally (slower process but effective for items that shouldn't be bleached). Once washed and dried, clothing is safe for storage and use.

## Upholstery & Curtain Fabric

Heavy upholstery and curtain fabrics provide shelter materials, insulation, and ground cover—often overlooked in favor of clothing but highly valuable for infrastructure.

### Heavy Fabrics for Shelter and Ground Cover

Upholstery fabric is typically heavy, closely-woven material (often weighing 2-4 oz per square yard) designed for durability. Salvaged upholstery can be repurposed as shelter fabric (tent material, weather covering), ground barriers (preventing moisture wicking from soil into shelter), and insulation layers. Curtain fabric provides similar utility—heavy velvet or brocade curtains offer insulation and weather protection. These materials are often overlooked because they lack the immediate portability of clothing but are invaluable for building shelter infrastructure. Collect upholstery from furniture (couches, chairs, car interiors) and curtains from windows during salvage operations.

### Automotive Upholstery

Vehicle seats and trim are constructed from durable synthetic upholstery specifically engineered for abuse resistance. Car interior fabrics are typically polyester or nylon blends with tight weaves that resist tearing and mold. Salvage car seats by carefully separating covering fabric from foam padding. The covering fabric is high-quality, weather-resistant material—far superior to typical household upholstery. Car door panels often feature padded fabric suitable for insulation or protective layers. Total weight of fabric per vehicle is substantial (10-20 lbs of salvageable upholstery). Automotive sources should be prioritized when available.

### Mattress Components and Repurposing

Mattress springs are valuable metal salvage (discussed in separate guides), but mattress construction also includes ticking (outer covering), batting, and filling materials. Ticking is heavy fabric designed to contain fill without tearing—useful for bags, ground cover, and shelter material. Batting (compressed fiber layer) provides insulation properties; salvage and repurpose as padding or insulation layer in shelter. Foam layers (if not deteriorated) can be cut and repurposed for padding or insulation. Cotton filling (horsehair, straw in vintage mattresses) has limited utility in survival context but can be used for insulation or fire starting material. Disassemble mattresses carefully to preserve large intact fabric sections rather than shredding them into small pieces.

### Carpet as Insulation and Cordage Source

Carpet provides two major survival utilities: insulation and cordage source. As insulation, carpet pieces laid on ground under shelter prevent heat loss through contact with soil (ground conducting heat away from body). Carpets are typically 1-2 inches thick with backing layers providing air-trapping properties essential for insulation. Cutting carpet into manageable pieces (2-3 feet square) allows flexible insulation arrangement. Carpet can also be unraveled to produce cordage—carpet backing is frequently woven polypropylene capable of producing strips suitable for binding and tying (not load-bearing applications but functional for general use). Synthetic carpet (nylon, polyester, polypropylene) is rot-resistant and survives damp environments indefinitely; wool carpet is superior insulation but more susceptible to mold in damp conditions. Target nylon or polyester carpet for maximum utility in wet climates.

## Cordage from Fabric Strips

Converting fabric into cordage extends limited cordage supplies and utilizes otherwise low-value textile scraps. Understanding cutting, twisting, and braiding techniques maximizes strength. For techniques on constructing garments from salvaged fabric, see <a href="../sewing-garment-construction.html">Sewing & Garment Construction</a>.

### Continuous Strip Cutting Technique

Produce cordage-grade strips by cutting spiral rings from fabric. Start at an outer edge of the fabric and cut in continuous circle approximately 1-1.5 inches wide, spiraling toward the center. A 36x36 inch piece of cotton or denim produces 200+ feet of continuous strip. The key is maintaining consistent width and uninterrupted length—even tears require tying to continue. Once you have a continuous strip, gently stretch it (tension doesn't break the material if done gradually) to encourage curling and tightening. The stretching process partially converts flat strips into semi-rounded cordage, improving grip and knot-holding properties.

### Twisting for Single-Strand Cordage

Single fabric strips twisted along their length create rope-like cordage. Secure one end and rotate the opposite end with consistent tension, allowing the material to naturally spiral and tighten. Twisted cordage is stronger than flat strips due to fiber compression and tension distribution. Load capacity of twisted single-ply fabric cordage: denim twist handles 50-100 lbs tension; thinner cotton 20-50 lbs. Three-ply (three twisted strands further twisted together) multiplies strength approximately 2-3x, producing durable cordage capable of 100-300 lbs tension depending on material. Twisted cordage is more secure than flat strips for load-bearing applications.

### Braiding for Multi-Strand Cordage

Three-strand braiding interweaves strips for superior strength and flexibility compared to twisting. Create three strips of similar length (2-4 feet for initial braid), then interweave them in standard three-strand braid pattern. The completed braid is then stretched and can be further twisted into higher-ply cordage. Braided cordage is superior for applications requiring both strength and flexibility (rope for climbing, rigging pulleys, structural support). Four-strand and more complex braiding patterns increase strength but require more material and practice. Braided cordage made from denim strips handles 200+ lbs tension with proper construction.

### T-Shirt Yarn Production

Cotton t-shirts produce excellent yarn through cutting and processing. Cut t-shirts (excluding seams and collars) into 1-inch wide continuous strips by cutting spirally from the hem toward the shoulders. The stretch property of t-shirt knit causes the cut strips to naturally curl and form yarn-like material. No further processing needed for basic cordage. T-shirt yarn is weaker than twisted or braided fabric cordage (handles approximately 20-40 lbs tension) but is easily produced with minimal tools and provides soft, flexible cordage suitable for bundling, general binding, and non-load-bearing applications. Multiple t-shirt yarns can be braided for increased strength.

### Denim Strips for Heavy-Duty Cordage

Denim jeans produce the strongest fabric cordage due to denim's dense weave and inherent durability. Cut denim into 1.5-2 inch strips and twist or braid as described previously. Denim cordage is stiffer and less flexible than fabric strips from thinner materials, but superior in strength (300+ lbs capacity for 3-ply braided denim cordage). Use denim cordage for structural applications, load-bearing rigging, and applications requiring durability over flexibility. Jeans seams are already heavily stitched—cutting along seams preserves the reinforced edges. Twin leg seams can be harvested individually as pre-made cordage (handling 100+ lbs each).

### Load Capacity Estimates and Testing

Fabric cordage load capacity depends on material type, weave density, and construction method. Use these rough guidelines: single-ply flat strips (denim) = 30-50 lbs; twisted single-ply = 50-100 lbs; three-ply twisted = 100-300 lbs; three-strand braided = 150-250 lbs; denim three-ply braided = 300+ lbs. Always test homemade cordage before trusting it with critical loads. Testing procedure: attach cordage to fixed point, add weight gradually (buckets of water work well), and increase load until failure point. Record failure load and material combination for future reference. Never exceed 50% of failure load for safety-critical applications (climbing, rigging, load suspension). Mark tested cordage with tape or dye indicating safe load limit to prevent accidental overload.

## Natural vs Synthetic Fiber ID

Fiber type determines durability, heat properties, and survival utility. The burn test provides reliable identification when visual inspection is inconclusive.

### Burn Test Methodology

Perform burn test in controlled environment away from shelter and flammables. Cut small sample (1-2 inch square) from garment. Light the sample corner with flame and observe behavior. Cotton chars black and burns readily (typical paper smell); flame spreads quickly along fibers and sample remains ash (completely consumed). Polyester melts into hard plastic beads (chemical smell, not paper); melting pulls threads into ball and produces pungent synthetic odor. Wool burns reluctantly (smells like burning hair due to protein composition); curls into ball, produces acrid smell; doesn't melt but chars. Nylon melts like polyester (producing hard plastic) but slower; smell is slightly different (more chemical, less acrid than polyester). Blends behave unpredictably—sample may partially melt and partially burn. Document observations: speed of burn, odor, residue consistency (ash vs. plastic ball), and flame spread rate.

### Why Fiber Type Matters for Survival

Natural fibers (cotton, wool, linen): excellent insulation when dry but absorb water and lose insulation properties (cotton becomes heavy and cold when wet; wool retains warmth). Natural fibers are flammable—keep away from fire. Cotton and linen rot in damp environments; wool resists rot. Synthetic fibers (polyester, nylon, acrylic): rot-resistant and hydrophobic (water-resistant); poor insulation compared to wool but lightweight and dry quickly. Synthetic clothing is ideal outer layer protection. Acrylic fleece provides good insulation and dries rapidly—excellent survival garment. Critical difference: synthetic melting in fire creates severe burns (molten plastic adheres to skin); natural fibers burn but don't melt. For clothing worn near fire, natural fibers are safer (though burn risk remains). For water-exposed situations, synthetics are superior. Ideal survival wardrobe combines natural and synthetic layers (synthetic outer for water resistance, natural inner for insulation).

### Fiber Behavior and Blended Materials

Blended fabrics contain both natural and synthetic fibers (e.g., 60% cotton/40% polyester). Blends offer compromise properties (dries faster than pure cotton, provides more insulation than pure polyester). Blends are common in modern clothing. When identifying blends, the dominant fiber (percentage-wise) determines primary properties—60% cotton blend behaves mostly like cotton (water-absorbent, somewhat heavy when wet) while 60% polyester blend behaves mostly like polyester (quick-drying, rot-resistant). Burn test on blends shows intermediate behavior—may partially melt and partially ash depending on blend ratio. 100% natural fiber fabric is increasingly rare in modern clothing; expect most salvaged garments to be blends. Accept blends as useful despite intermediate properties—survival prioritizes having clothing over optimal fiber composition.

## Leather Salvage

Leather is a specialized and valuable material for survival applications. Understanding condition assessment and reconditioning extends the utility of salvaged leather goods.

### Leather Sources and Priority Items

Leather shoes are the highest priority—feet protection is critical for survival. Salvage any leather footwear regardless of apparent condition; leather can be reconditioned. Leather belts provide raw material for straps, sheaths, and hinges. Leather furniture (couches, chairs) provides bulk material but requires significant effort to harvest. Leather jackets and coats offer weather protection and bulk raw material. Car interiors often contain leather seats and trim (high-quality leather optimized for durability and stain resistance). Leather handbags and luggage provide smaller pieces of pre-worked leather in various finishes. During scavenging, collect any leather items—the material is durable and valuable for purposes beyond clothing (sheaths, straps, hinges, gaskets, bellows).

### Assessing Leather Condition

Check for cracking: light surface cracks are normal and repairable; deep cracks that penetrate through the leather thickness indicate degradation beyond practical repair. Cracking occurs when leather loses moisture and becomes brittle. Assess by flexing the item—healthy leather flexes without cracking; degraded leather cracks audibly and visibly. Rot manifests as soft spots, dark discoloration, and foul smell (decay smell, not normal leather smell). Rotted leather cannot be reconditioned—discard. Mold appears as fuzzy growth or dark spots on surface; mold can be removed by brushing or surface cleaning, but indicates moisture exposure risk. Check for mold particularly on stored leather goods. Color change alone doesn't indicate damage; darkening is normal as leather ages. Leather should feel supple when flexed; stiff, brittle feel indicates drying. Assess repairability: cracked but dry leather is repairable; rotted or heavily molded leather is waste.

### Reconditioning Dried Leather

Dried leather becomes brittle and loses flexibility. Reconditioning restores suppleness through application of oil-based treatments. Neatsfoot oil is the traditional choice—derived from animal bone marrow, it conditions leather without darkening dramatically. Apply neatsfoot oil by hand, working it into the leather in circular motions. Allow to soak for 30 minutes, then wipe off excess. Leather should feel noticeably more flexible after single application. Repeat applications may be needed for severely dried leather. Alternative: rendered animal fat (tallow or lard) provides similar conditioning effect; apply same way as neatsfoot oil. Modern leather dressing products work well if available but are not survival-critical. Test reconditioning on small area first—over-application makes leather greasy and weakens it for structural applications. Properly reconditioned leather lasts indefinitely with occasional maintenance oiling.

### Leather Survival Applications

Straps: leather cut into strips replaces rope for non-load-bearing applications (bundling, securing items, binding). Leather is more durable than fabric cordage for extended use. Sheaths: leather is ideal material for tool sheaths (knife, axe), providing protection and accessibility. Leather naturally molds to tool shape with use. Hinges: leather can serve as flexible hinge material for containers and boxes (more durable than fabric, non-metallic). Gaskets and seals: leather works as waterproof seal material in some applications. Footwear repair: leather patches repair damaged shoe uppers; soles are more difficult to repair but possible with dedicated effort. Bellows: leather is essential material for forging bellows (pumping air for fire). Leather's durability makes it superior to fabric for these specialized applications. Prioritize storing reconditioned leather for specialized uses rather than general cordage where fabric substitutes work acceptably.

## Bedding & Insulation Materials

Bedding items provide the highest insulation value per weight among textile salvage. Prioritize bedding materials for shelter and sleep system applications.

### Down and Feather Fill Insulation

Down (soft fluffy inner feathers) and feather (stiffer outer feathers) provide exceptional insulation by trapping air in three-dimensional structures. Down is lighter and more compressible than synthetic insulation but requires dry conditions—moisture destroys loft (insulating air pockets collapse). Salvage down items: down comforters, down pillows, down jackets, down sleeping bags. Extract fill by carefully opening seams and separating material. Down is extremely valuable insulation material—prioritize protection from moisture through waterproof storage. Weight comparison: down provides equivalent insulation to synthetic materials at 30% of the weight—critical advantage for portable survival systems. Down's weakness is moisture vulnerability; synthetic alternatives are preferable in damp climates. Store down sealed in waterproof containers. Inspect for dust mites and mildew before use—salvaged down may require cleaning (gentle washing, thorough drying).

### Sleeping Bags as High-Priority Salvage

Sleeping bags are pre-engineered insulation systems—highest priority textile salvage items. Assess temperature rating (indicated on bag) for climate compatibility. Winter sleeping bags (rated -10 to -30°F) are extremely valuable; summer bags (rated 20-40°F) less so but still useful. Inspect for tears or holes (allow fill to shift and escape, reducing insulation). Small holes can be patched; extensive damage may not be repairable. Check for water damage (damp spots, mildew smell, dark staining) indicating previous exposure; sleeping bag can still function if thoroughly dried. Loft (thickness) is visual indicator of insulation value—compressed fill indicates age and reduced performance. Sleeping bags remain functional even with reduced loft; they're not useless at reduced performance level. Salvaged sleeping bag is worth multiple blankets as insulation source. Prioritize sleeping bags over loose blankets in salvage operations.

### Blanket Types and Survival Value

Wool blankets are superior insulation—wool retains warmth when wet and resists rot. Older wool blankets (sometimes from military or institutional sources) are extremely durable and valuable. Wool blankets are heavy (2-4 lbs) but provide excellent insulation. Synthetic fleece blankets provide good insulation, dry quickly, and are lightweight. Fleece is not as warm as wool but superior in damp conditions. Cotton blankets are heavy when wet, slow to dry, and lose insulation value when damp—least valuable for survival. Down-filled quilts are essentially blanket-form down sleeping systems—high insulation value per weight. Assess blanket material by burn test if uncertain. Prioritize: wool > synthetic fleece > down quilts > cotton. Weight and portability matter—multiple thin insulation layers provide better temperature regulation than single heavy layer. Store dry blankets in waterproof storage.

### Quilts and Patching Material

Quilts provide bulk insulation material and valuable patching fabric. Disassemble quilts by carefully separating layers: outer fabric, batting layer (insulation), backing fabric. Each layer has separate utility. Outer and backing fabric are usable as shelter material, garment patches, or cordage source. Batting provides insulation layer—can be repurposed as padding or shelter insulation. Quilts are also sources of pre-printed patterns and durable fabric suitable for patches on other items. Salvage quilts from beds, furniture, and storage. The effort required to disassemble and organize quilt materials is justified by utility value—particularly for large quilts which produce significant insulation quantity.

## Location Scouting

Identifying high-yield textile sources maximizes salvage efficiency. Different location types offer distinct materials and accessibility challenges.

### Residential Locations

Single-family homes contain distributed textile resources: bedroom closets (clothing, sheets, blankets), bathrooms (towels), attics (stored seasonal items, old blankets), basements (stored clothing, boxes of fabric). Average residential location yields 50-100 lbs of salvageable textiles. Time per location: 30-60 minutes. Access is often easier than commercial locations; items may be undisturbed and well-preserved if location has remained sealed.

### Laundromats and Dry Cleaners

Laundromats contain lost or abandoned clothing in lost-and-found areas or left in machines. Dry cleaners have unclaimed garments stored for months or years. Both locations contain already-processed (clean or cleaned) textiles requiring no decontamination. Garment density is high—a single unclaimed rack at dry cleaner contains 50-100 garments. These are among the best sources due to condition and accessibility.

### Retail Clothing Stores and Warehouses

Retail stores have damaged merchandise relegated to back rooms or dumpsters. Many retailers discard seasonal stock rather than storing it. Warehouse conditions preserve textiles well. Garment volume is enormous—a single retail store dumpster may contain 200+ undamaged garments. Breaking into restricted areas carries legal and safety risks; focus on accessible dumpsters and donation areas.

### Donation Centers and Thrift Stores

Goodwill, Salvation Army, and similar centers maintain bulk clothing in storage facilities. Facilities are organized; locating specific material types is feasible. However, these represent community resources and should be respected as such—salvage during genuine scarcity, not opportunistically. Access requires either permission or risk of legal issues.

### Hospitals and Medical Facilities

Medical facilities discard protective garments (isolation gowns, surgical gowns) and uniforms. These are usually synthetic, durable, and well-preserved. Gowns typically weigh 2-4 oz and are individually packaged. Hospitals may also have damaged bed linens and mattresses discarded in marked dumpsters. Safety hazard: ensure no bloodborne pathogen contamination before handling. Wear gloves during collection.

### Automotive Sources

Vehicle seats, door panels, and trim contain substantial upholstery. Average car interior yields 15-25 lbs of usable upholstery fabric. Extraction requires time but tools are minimal (utility knife, pliers). Salvage yards often allow dismantling in exchange for materials.

### Hotels and Hospitality Facilities

Hotels continuously discard worn linens, damaged furniture upholstery, and guest-left items. Dumpster areas often contain minimal damage items discarded due to minor stains or small holes. Volume per location can be significant (hotel may discard 50-100 lbs of textiles weekly).

## Tools Required

Textile salvage requires minimal specialized equipment. Most operations use household tools.

### Essential Tools

- Sharp utility knife or scissors (for cutting fabric, seams)
- Pliers (for removing fasteners, separating upholstery from frames)
- Hammer (for separating components)
- Gloves (protect hands from contamination, sharp edges)
- Bags or containers (transport, sorting)

### Optional Equipment

- Respirator or dust mask (for moldy items, dusty environments)
- Flashlight (for dark attics, basements)
- Measuring tape (assess fabric yardage)
- Documentation notebook (record location yields, conditions)

Textile salvage is one of the lowest-tool-requirement salvage categories. Prioritize personal protective equipment over specialized tools.

<section id="extraction-procedures">

## Extraction Procedures

Removing textiles from locations and structures requires specific techniques to preserve material integrity.

### Clothing and Garment Extraction

Remove garments by hand. If items are layered or stacked, remove individually to prevent damage. Separate by material type immediately (wool separate from synthetics, leather separate from fabric). Fold garments to minimize wrinkles and creasing that may cause permanent damage. Don't force garments through small spaces—enlarge openings or remove items via alternative routes.

### Upholstery Removal from Furniture

Upholstery removal takes time but yields substantial fabric. Procedure: (1) Examine stapling pattern—identify where fabric is attached to frame; (2) Using pliers or utility knife, carefully remove staples, working from one corner around the furniture piece; (3) Gently pull fabric away from frame as staples release; (4) If fabric is glued, carefully slice through glue line to separate; (5) Roll harvested fabric to prevent tangling. A typical couch yields 10-15 lbs of usable upholstery from the seating surface alone.

### Curtain and Drape Removal

Curtain rods hold fabric via various mechanisms: rod pockets (fabric sewn to enclose rod), hooks, or clips. Identify attachment type and release accordingly. Rod pocket curtains require sliding rod through the pocket or carefully slicing stitches to release. Cloth typically remains undamaged. Fold curtains as they come down to preserve length and minimize handling. Bundle by location to avoid mixing different curtain types accidentally.

### Mattress Disassembly

Mattress construction: outer ticking (fabric), batting layer, springs (metal), and filling. To harvest: (1) Cut through ticking along seams or edges; (2) Carefully remove batting, separating from springs; (3) Separate foam layers (if present) from springs; (4) Save ticking, batting, and foam separately. Mattress springs are valuable metal salvage (separate guide). Average queen mattress yields 8-12 lbs of fabric and batting.

### Bedding Extraction

Comforters, pillows, and quilts are removed intact. Inspect for down leakage before moving—bag leaking items separately to prevent down dispersal. Sleeping bags should be rolled from foot to head (prevents zippers from catching). Blankets fold compactly and stack efficiently.

<section id="processing-cleaning">

## Processing & Cleaning

Salvaged textiles require assessment and cleaning before storage or use.

### Sorting by Material Type

Sort immediately upon returning from salvage location. Categories: (1) Wool items; (2) Synthetic blends; (3) Pure synthetics; (4) Cotton items; (5) Leather items; (6) Upholstery/bulk fabric; (7) Bedding/insulation materials. Sorting by material simplifies cleaning, storage, and future selection for specific applications.

### Initial Inspection and Damage Assessment

Inspect each item for: visible damage (tears, stains, burn holes), odors (musty/mold, chemical, ammonia/rodent), insects or contamination. Document condition. Reject items with strong odors or active infestation. Set aside questionable items for separate assessment after other items are processed.

### Cleaning Procedures

For items from unknown sources, washing is mandatory. Three-tier approach: (1) **Light contamination** (surface dust, minor odors): Soak in cool water 30 minutes, gentle hand-wash, hang-dry in sun; (2) **Moderate contamination** (visible stains, persistent odors): Soak in hot water (boiling if heat-safe) 30 minutes, hand-wash with soap, rinse thoroughly, sun-dry; (3) **Heavy contamination** (rodent evidence, potential mold): Treat with dilute bleach solution (1:10 bleach:water) for 30 minutes, thorough rinsing (multiple changes of water), extended sun-drying (several days). Never dry-clean unknown salvage items—water cleaning is preferable.

:::warning
Never mix unknown salvage textiles with stored clean items until thoroughly washed and dried. Contamination (mold spores, insects, pathogens) spreads to clean inventory.
:::

### Sun Drying for Disinfection

Sun exposure provides natural disinfection via UV light. Hang items in full sunlight for 8-12 hours minimum for light disinfection, 2-3 days for thorough disinfection. UV kills most pathogens. Air circulation is critical—never pile wet textiles together (promotes mold). Spread items individually or layer loosely.

<section id="storage-preservation">

## Storage & Preservation

Proper storage extends textile viability and prevents degradation.

### Storage Conditions

Optimal conditions: cool (50-70°F), dry (below 50% humidity), dark (prevents UV damage), ventilated (prevents mold). Container options: sealed plastic bins (protects from insects, moisture), breathable cotton bags (allows air circulation but less pest-proof), hanging storage on rods (excellent air circulation, takes significant space). Never use plastic bags for long-term storage—moisture accumulates and promotes mold.

### Moisture Control

Include desiccant packets (silica gel, calcium chloride) in sealed containers. Replace desiccants when saturated. Alternatively, rotate items annually—remove from storage, air-dry, repack. Sign of moisture problem: musty smell when opening storage containers. Response: remove all items, air-dry completely, inspect for mold, then repack with fresh desiccants.

### Pest Prevention

Store in sealed containers if possible. Inspect regularly (monthly minimum) for signs of insect activity or rodents. If insects are detected, freeze items for 2+ weeks (if freezer space available) or seal in plastic for extended period (weeks to months). Establish air circulation and ventilation to discourage pest nesting.

### Documentation System

Record stored textiles: (1) Material type (wool, synthetic, cotton, etc.); (2) Item description (sweater, jeans, upholstery yardage); (3) Condition (excellent, good with small hole, stained, etc.); (4) Quantity (single item or yardage); (5) Date stored. Simple spreadsheet or notebook sufficient. Review inventory when planning projects—knowing what you have stored prevents accidental repurchasing.

<section id="hazard-identification">

## Hazard Identification

Certain textiles and materials carry specific hazards requiring precautions.

### Asbestos in Vintage Materials

Vintage insulation textiles (1950s-1970s blankets, batting, upholstery) may contain asbestos fibers. Asbestos was commonly used in fireproofing treatments. Don't shake, brush, or disturb suspected asbestos material—fibers become airborne and cause lung damage when inhaled. If you suspect asbestos: (1) Don't wash it; (2) Seal in plastic bags; (3) Label clearly; (4) Store separately; (5) Don't repurpose. Asbestos identification: very soft, fibrous material, often white or light gray, brittle. When in doubt about vintage insulation, treat as potentially asbestos-containing and isolate.

:::danger
Asbestos inhalation causes mesothelioma (incurable cancer) with 10-50 year latency period. Handle suspected asbestos with extreme caution: do not disturb, do not shake, do not wash. Isolation and sealed storage is safest approach.
:::

### Chemical Contamination

Textiles stored near cleaning chemicals, pesticides, fuel, or solvents absorb chemical vapors. If salvaged items smell chemical-like or were stored adjacent to chemical containers, reject them. Chemical odors are indicator of absorption; chemicals absorbed into fibers don't wash out easily. Risk is not just contamination but potential burning or damage if contaminated textiles contact skin or are worn.

### Mold and Biological Hazard

Extensive mold growth indicates mycotoxin production (fungal toxins). Moldy textiles should be handled with respiratory protection. Isolate before cleaning to prevent spore dispersal. See Cleaning Procedures section for mold remediation. Never wear heavily moldy clothing; risk of respiratory infection is significant.

<section id="troubleshooting">

## Troubleshooting

Common challenges and solutions during textile salvage operations.

**Problem: Strong musty odor in stored textiles**
Solution: Textiles absorbed moisture during storage. Unpack completely, air-dry in sun (UV helps eliminate odor-causing bacteria), repack with fresh desiccants in sealed containers. If odor persists, wash items in water with 1 tsp baking soda per gallon, then sun-dry.

**Problem: Holes or weak spots in insulation items (sleeping bags, jackets)**
Solution: For small holes (less than 1 inch), apply fabric patch from inside using patch adhesive or needle-and-thread. For larger holes, assess if item remains functional (does insulation stay in place?). Sleeping bags with holes can be sealed using fabric tape as temporary fix or patched permanently.

**Problem: Excessive wrinkling or creasing in stored fabric**
Solution: Crease from extended storage can become permanent. Gently dampening fabric and hanging in moderate heat (sun, near fire but not touching) for 24-48 hours may relax fibers. Don't force creases out violently; patience prevents damage.

**Problem: Sticky or stiff feeling in salvaged items**
Solution: Chemical residue or incomplete rinsing. Soak in fresh water (3-4 changes), gently agitate, rinse until water runs clear. Air-dry completely. If stickiness persists, item may have been treated with sizing (fabric stiffener) or stored with silicone products—may require multiple washing cycles.

**Problem: Down leaking from comforter or jacket**
Solution: Minor leakage is common from older items. Contain leakage by placing item in sealed bag when not in use. For more than token leakage, reinforce stitching around seams using needle and thread (waterproof thread if available). Consider downgrading item to indoor/storage-only use if leakage is extensive.

<section id="common-mistakes">

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Salvaging Water-Damaged Items Without Drying Immediately

Water-damaged fabrics develop mold within 48-72 hours if not dried. Response: immediately separate, spread out, and air-dry. Delay causes mold colonization which creates health hazards and ruins items.

### Mistake 2: Mixing Unknown Salvage with Stored Clean Items

Contamination (mold spores, insects, pathogens) spreads from newly salvaged items to clean inventory. Response: maintain separate staging area for salvage items until they're washed, dried, and inspected. Only mix with clean inventory after full processing.

### Mistake 3: Over-Packing Storage Containers

Tightly packed fabrics compress insulation materials and promote mold by preventing air circulation. Response: pack loosely, distribute weight, and ensure air can circulate within containers. Leave 20-30% airspace in sealed containers.

### Mistake 4: Ignoring Odors as Indicator of Problems

Strong chemical, ammonia, or rot smells indicate contamination. Response: trust your nose—if it smells bad, it's problematic. Never assume washing will remove chemical or severe contamination odors.

### Mistake 5: Storing Without Desiccants in Humid Climates

Humidity in storage areas promotes mold even in sealed containers. Response: use desiccant packets and monitor humidity with hygrometer. Replace desiccants monthly in humid climates.

<section id="safety">

## Safety Considerations

### Infection Risk from Unknown Sources

Salvaged clothing may carry bacteria, viruses, fungi, or parasites from previous owners. Always wear gloves when handling unknown salvage. Wash items thoroughly before wearing. Never put unknown salvage directly on skin without washing first.

:::warning
Body lice infestations are possible in salvaged clothing. Ensure thorough hot-water washing or heat treatment (drying at high heat for 30+ minutes) before storing with other items.
:::

### Sharp Objects in Salvaged Items

Needles, pins, broken zippers, or hooks hidden in pockets or seams create injury hazards. Always feel pockets thoroughly before handling. Shake items carefully to dislodge loose objects. Wear gloves to protect hands.

### Structural Hazards During Location Salvage

When salvaging from buildings, assess structural integrity first. Avoid floors with visible sagging, walls with major cracks, or roofs showing visible degradation. Never salvage alone—always have a partner and establish communication signals. Set time limits (30-60 minutes) and stick to them. If building feels unsafe, leave immediately.
