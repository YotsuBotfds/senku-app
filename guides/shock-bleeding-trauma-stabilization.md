## Overview

Uncontrolled bleeding and shock kill faster than almost any other wilderness injury. This guide covers field assessment, hemorrhage control without medical equipment, and shock stabilization for non-trained survivors. The goal is to slow or stop bleeding long enough to buy time for evacuation or self-rescue.

:::danger
A person can lose 1 unit of blood (approximately 500 mL / 1 pint) every 5-10 minutes from a major arterial wound. Massive hemorrhage can be fatal in 3-5 minutes. Do not delay hemorrhage control to gather materials.
:::

## Understanding Blood Loss Categories

### Class 1 Hemorrhage (0–15% blood volume lost)
- **Volume lost:** 500 mL or less
- **Heart rate:** Normal or slightly elevated (< 100 bpm)
- **Blood pressure:** Normal
- **Respiratory rate:** Normal
- **Skin:** Warm, normal color
- **Mental status:** Calm
- **Action:** Monitor, continue activity cautiously if minor cut

### Class 2 Hemorrhage (15–30% blood volume lost)
- **Volume lost:** 500–1000 mL
- **Heart rate:** 100–120 bpm (anxious, rapid)
- **Blood pressure:** Normal (systolic ≥ 100 mmHg)
- **Respiratory rate:** 20–30 breaths/min
- **Skin:** Cool, pale, sweaty
- **Mental status:** Anxious, restless
- **Action:** Direct pressure, elevation, monitor closely. Begin evacuation planning.

### Class 3 Hemorrhage (30–40% blood volume lost)
- **Volume lost:** 1500–2000 mL
- **Heart rate:** > 120 bpm (very rapid, weak)
- **Blood pressure:** Systolic 90–100 mmHg (drop of 20–30 mmHg from baseline)
- **Respiratory rate:** 30–40 breaths/min
- **Skin:** Cool, pale, clammy, possibly cyanotic (blue-tinged) lips
- **Mental status:** Confused, agitated, or withdrawn
- **Action:** Tourniquet or aggressive packing required. Shock protocol. Immediate evacuation critical.

### Class 4 Hemorrhage (> 40% blood volume lost)
- **Volume lost:** > 2000 mL
- **Heart rate:** > 140 bpm (barely palpable)
- **Blood pressure:** < 90 mmHg systolic (profound shock)
- **Respiratory rate:** > 40 breaths/min (gasping)
- **Skin:** Ashen, clammy
- **Mental status:** Lethargic, unconscious, unresponsive
- **Action:** Tourniquet, aggressive resuscitation, shock protocol. Death is likely without immediate advanced medical care.

:::info-box
**Estimating Blood Loss Without Measurement:**
A tablespoon of blood (15 mL) appears dramatic on cloth. A soaked 4×4 inch gauze pad represents ~10–15 mL. Two large handfuls of saturated cloth = ~100 mL. If you soak through a cloth larger than your hand in less than 2 minutes, assume Class 2+ hemorrhage.
:::

## Hemorrhage Control Decision Tree

### Step 1: Identify Bleeding Source

**Arterial bleeding (high priority):**
- Bright red blood
- Spurts in rhythm with heartbeat
- Difficult to stop with pressure
- Sources: wounds to neck/groin/upper arm/axilla, open fractures of femur

**Venous bleeding (moderate priority):**
- Dark red blood
- Steady flow (not spurting)
- Responds quickly to direct pressure
- Sources: deep cuts, lacerations, punctures

**Capillary oozing (low priority):**
- Dark red, steady slow ooze
- Stops with gentle pressure
- Sources: abrasions, minor cuts

### Step 2: Apply Direct Pressure

1. **Do not remove embedded objects.** If an object (knife, glass, stake) is embedded, stabilize it in place with bandaging. Removal may cause massive bleeding.

2. **Use direct pressure immediately.** Place clean cloth directly on wound. If unavailable, use your hand (infection risk is lower than death from hemorrhage).

3. **Apply firm, consistent pressure.** Press perpendicular to wound surface. Do not probe or check if bleeding has stopped.

4. **Maintain pressure for 3–5 minutes minimum.** In cold conditions or with coagulopathy, pressure may need to extend 10+ minutes.

5. **If bleeding soaks through cloth, do not remove the cloth.** Layer new cloth on top. Removal disrupts clot formation.

:::warning
Do not apply tourniquet before attempting direct pressure. Direct pressure controls bleeding in ~90% of extremity wounds and preserves limb viability.
:::

## Elevation and Pressure Points

### Use Elevation for Limb Wounds

If bleeding is from a hand, forearm, foot, or lower leg:
- Raise the injured limb above the level of the heart
- Maintain direct pressure while elevated
- Elevation reduces bleeding pressure due to gravity

Do NOT elevate if:
- Bone fracture is suspected (risk of fat embolism or further injury)
- Patient is in shock (keep limbs at or below heart level)

### Apply Pressure Points for Proximal Control

If direct pressure alone fails to control bleeding from a limb wound, apply pressure to the artery above the wound:

**Upper extremity (axillary artery):**
- Press the armpit (axilla) against the chest wall
- Use your fist or thumb to compress against bone
- Maintain while continuing direct pressure on the wound

**Lower extremity (femoral artery in groin):**
- Locate the groin fold (where thigh meets abdomen)
- Press hard into the fold with your fist or heel of hand
- May require 20+ lbs of pressure; expect discomfort
- Maintain for 2–3 minutes while assessing wound

**Arm (brachial artery):**
- Inner surface of upper arm, above the elbow
- Press against the bone with thumb or fingers
- Hold while continuing direct pressure on wound

## Tourniquet Application

:::danger
Tourniquets are a last resort for life-threatening limb hemorrhage. Incorrect placement or duration can cause permanent nerve damage or tissue death. Only apply if direct pressure fails after 3 minutes and bleeding is Class 3+.
:::

### When to Apply a Tourniquet

- **Arterial spurting that does not slow with 3 minutes of direct pressure**
- **Inability to apply or maintain direct pressure (e.g., multiple injuries, terrain hazard)**
- **Crush injuries with active hemorrhage**
- **Partial or complete amputation**
- **Uncontrolled bleeding in a limb that threatens life**

### Tourniquet Placement

**Location principle:** Place tourniquet 2–3 inches ABOVE the wound, on the limb itself (not on a joint). On the thigh, this is typically mid-thigh. On the calf, it is above the knee.

**Improvised tourniquet from available materials:**

1. **Belt method:** Use a belt, rope, or fabric strip 1–2 inches wide.
   - Wrap around limb twice
   - Tie a half-knot, leaving ends loose
   - Insert a stick, pen, or bone into the half-knot
   - Twist the stick to tighten until bleeding stops
   - Secure the stick with cloth or cord to prevent unwinding

2. **Cravat/cloth method:** Tear cloth into a 2-inch-wide strip.
   - Wrap tightly around limb 2–3 times
   - Tie a square knot on top of the limb (not underneath)
   - Insert a stick through the knot
   - Twist until bleeding stops completely
   - Secure stick with additional wraps

3. **Rope or paracord method:**
   - Wrap rope tightly 3–4 times around limb
   - Tie with a square knot
   - Use a tourniquet windlass: insert a stick or bone through a loop at the knot
   - Twist until bleeding completely stops
   - Prevent rotation by securing the stick to the limb with cord

### Tourniquet Tightening Sequence

1. **Initial tightening:** Compress until arterial bleeding stops visibly.
2. **Over-tightening:** Continue twisting/tightening slightly more (~10% additional pressure) to ensure veins are also occluded and to prevent gradual loosening.
3. **Check distal pulse:** If trained, check for the absence of a pulse below the tourniquet. Presence of pulse = insufficient tightness.
4. **Mark the time:** Use cloth or skin marker to write the time the tourniquet was applied. This is critical for hospital care.

:::warning
A tourniquet should cause pain and discomfort. If the patient cannot feel the tourniquet or if pain decreases, it may be loosening and require re-tightening.
:::

## Wound Packing for Deep Hemorrhage

Deep wounds that cannot be controlled by direct pressure (e.g., deep lacerations, penetrating injuries, crush injuries) require wound packing.

### Packing Materials

**Preferred (if available):**
- Clean cloth torn into 1–2 inch strips
- Gauze or sterile cloth
- T-shirt material, cut lengthwise

**Improvised:**
- Boiled cloth (cooled), torn into strips
- Dried plant fiber bundled tightly
- Lint from dried plant material (cattail, milkweed) if cloth unavailable

### Packing Technique

1. **Prepare materials.** Tear cloth into strips 1–2 inches wide and 2–3 feet long.

2. **Assess wound depth.** Use your finger (gloved or clean) to probe the wound gently and determine depth and internal bleeding source.

3. **Start packing.** Place the first strip of cloth into the deepest part of the wound, pushing firmly to apply pressure to bleeding vessels.

4. **Continue layering.** Add strips one at a time, pushing each into the wound and working toward the surface. Do not leave air pockets.

5. **Maintain pressure.** Apply direct pressure with your hand on top of the packing for 3–5 minutes while packing settles.

6. **Cover and secure.** Once packing is in place and bleeding has slowed, cover with dry cloth and secure with bandaging (do not remove packing).

7. **Monitor for re-bleeding.** Check every 15–30 minutes. If bleeding returns, apply additional direct pressure. Do not remove original packing.

:::info-box
**Packing Duration:** Packed wounds may require 24–48 hours of packing before clotting is sufficient to unpack safely. If evacuation is possible, evacuate with packing in place.
:::

## Hemostatic Agents from Natural Materials

When cloth alone is insufficient, natural hemostatic agents can augment clotting.

### Honey

**Properties:**
- Viscous, promotes platelet adhesion
- Provides mild antimicrobial effect
- Available in wild from bee colonies (extract before injury if possible)

**Use:**
- Apply thick layer to wound after direct pressure
- Can be combined with cloth packing for deep wounds
- Store dried honey or honeycomb in emergency kit

**Caution:** Do not use honey in infants (botulism risk) or on wounds that will be sutured in hospital (may impede closure).

### Salt

**Properties:**
- Osmotic agent; draws fluid to wound surface
- Promotes hemostasis through osmotic pressure
- Widely available

**Use:**
- Mix salt with boiled water to create paste (3 parts salt : 1 part water)
- Apply to wound surface and cover with cloth packing
- Can be combined with honey for synergistic effect

**Caution:** Excessive salt causes pain and tissue damage. Use sparingly.

### Charcoal

**Properties:**
- Absorbs fluid and bacteria
- Provides structural support for clot formation
- Can be obtained by burning wood and crushing charred pieces

**Use:**
- Crush charcoal fine and mix with water to paste
- Apply thin layer to wound surface
- Cover with cloth packing
- Charcoal alone is not sufficient for major hemorrhage

### Plantain (Plantago major/minor)

**Properties:**
- Contains compounds that promote clotting
- Historically used for wound hemostasis
- Leaves are astringent and slightly antimicrobial

**Use:**
- Crush fresh leaves to release juices
- Apply directly to wound or saturate cloth with crushed material
- Bind in place with cloth wrap
- Can be dried and rehydrated for storage

## Tension Pneumothorax Recognition and Treatment

A tension pneumothorax (collapsed lung due to air in chest cavity) is a life-threatening condition that requires immediate intervention even without medical equipment.

### Recognition Criteria

**Look for:**
- Sudden dyspnea (difficulty breathing) after chest trauma or penetrating wound
- Severe anxiety, panic
- Cyanosis (blue lips, face, fingernails)
- Distended neck veins (veins bulging on sides of neck)
- Unequal breath sounds (one side silent, other side normal)
- Severe hypotension (very low blood pressure, weak pulse)
- Tracheal deviation (windpipe pushed to one side, visible as asymmetry in neck)

**Trigger mechanism:**
- Stab or gunshot wound to chest
- Crushing injury to chest
- Fractured ribs with lung laceration
- Severe blunt force trauma to thorax

:::danger
Tension pneumothorax can kill in minutes. If you strongly suspect this diagnosis after trauma, proceed with needle decompression immediately. Do not wait for hospital care if evacuation will take > 30 minutes.
:::

### Emergency Needle Decompression (Needle Thoracostomy)

If pneumothorax symptoms are severe and worsening:

1. **Locate the 2nd intercostal space.** Find the hollow space between the 2nd and 3rd ribs at the midclavicular line (imagine a vertical line from the center of the collarbone).

2. **Prepare puncture site.** If possible, clean the skin with soap, water, or alcohol. If not available, proceed without cleaning.

3. **Insert needle.** Use the largest needle available (18-gauge or larger; a large sewing needle, medical needle, or even a sharp stick/bone). Push firmly through skin and into the intercostal space at a 90-degree angle. You will feel a "pop" as the needle enters the pleural space.

4. **Listen/feel for air release.** If this is truly a tension pneumothorax, air will rush out audibly or you will feel air flow against your hand.

5. **Stabilize the needle.** Once inserted, tape the needle in place to prevent dislodgment. Do not remove it.

6. **Monitor patient.** Breathing should improve immediately. If no improvement, reassess diagnosis.

:::warning
This procedure is used only as a last resort when a patient is actively dying from inability to breathe and you believe the diagnosis is tension pneumotharax. Incorrect insertion can damage the heart, lungs, or major vessels. Only attempt if evacuation will take > 30 minutes and patient is deteriorating.
:::

## Crush Injuries and Crush Syndrome

Large crushing injuries (trapped under debris, logs, rocks) can cause traumatic crush syndrome: shock from release of cellular contents when the person is freed.

### Recognition

- Heavy weight on limb or torso for extended period (> 4 hours)
- Limb appears swollen, discolored, or has obvious compartment syndrome (skin tight, shiny, very painful to passive movement)
- Upon release: shock, hypothermia, hyperkalemia (low heartbeat, cardiac arrhythmia)

### Field Management

1. **Do NOT remove the weight quickly.** If possible, gradually reduce pressure or pack ice around the limb before full removal (if time permits).

2. **Upon release, expect shock.** Follow shock management protocol (see below).

3. **Aggressive fluid replacement if available.** Crush victims need 2–3 times normal fluid intake to prevent kidney failure. If you have water, encourage drinking if patient is conscious and able to swallow.

4. **Expect rhabdomyolysis complications.** Dark brown urine, weakness, and cardiac arrhythmias are signs of muscle breakdown. Evacuation is urgent.

5. **Splint the limb.** Immobilize the crushed limb to reduce further tissue damage and swelling.

## Shock Management Protocol

Shock is the body's failure to perfuse tissues adequately. Hemorrhagic shock is the most common type in wilderness trauma.

### Shock Positioning

1. **Raise lower extremities.** If no spinal injury is suspected, elevate legs 12 inches above heart level using backpack, log, or rock.

2. **Keep patient warm.** Provide blanket, sleeping bag, or body heat. Hypothermia worsens shock.

3. **Keep patient calm.** Reassure and minimize anxiety. Do not leave patient alone.

4. **Monitor vitals continuously.** Check heart rate, breathing rate, and skin color every 5–10 minutes.

### Fluid Replacement (if resources available)

- Offer water or electrolyte solution by mouth if patient is conscious and able to swallow
- Do NOT give fluids if patient is unconscious or vomiting (aspiration risk)
- Small frequent sips are better than large amounts

### Monitoring for Shock Improvement or Deterioration

**Improving shock:**
- Pulse slows toward 90–100 bpm
- Blood pressure rises
- Skin becomes warmer, less clammy
- Patient becomes more alert

**Deteriorating shock:**
- Pulse increases further (> 130 bpm)
- Blood pressure drops further (below 80 mmHg systolic or unmeasurable)
- Skin becomes more pale, colder
- Patient becomes lethargic, loses consciousness
- Breathing becomes slow or irregular

If shock is deteriorating despite intervention, prepare for evacuation or recognize that survival is unlikely without advanced medical care.

## Amputation Decision Framework

If a limb cannot be saved and is threatening the patient's life (severe infection, tissue necrosis spreading proximally, overwhelming odor and pain), amputation may be the only survival option.

### Indicators for Amputation

- **Spreading gangrene or necrosis** moving up the limb toward the torso
- **Systemic sepsis** from limb infection (high fever, chills, confusion, rapid heartbeat)
- **Uncontrolled odor** indicating massive tissue death
- **Intolerable pain** not relieved by available methods
- **Patient at imminent risk of death** from sepsis or toxicity
- **Evacuation impossible** for weeks or months

### Contraindications (Do Not Amputate If)

- **Patient is in shock** (too dangerous without anesthesia/skill)
- **Amputation site is contaminated** with grossly dirty material (risk of catastrophic infection)
- **You lack any sharp tools** (improvising a saw from flint is slower and riskier)
- **Evacuation is possible** within days or weeks (tolerate the limb even if severely injured)

:::danger
Field amputation without anesthesia is one of the most traumatic procedures possible. Only consider if the patient consents, understands the risk, and is in imminent danger of death from sepsis or shock. If possible, sedate the patient with any available anesthetic, alcohol, or opium prior to amputation.
:::

### Field Amputation Procedure (Outline)

If amputation is the only survival option:

1. **Anesthetize if possible.** Use alcohol, opium, willow bark, or any available analgesic.

2. **Sterilize tools.** Sharpen knife/saw in flame or boil in hot water.

3. **Place tourniquet proximal to amputation site.** Tighten well to minimize bleeding.

4. **Make skin incision** with sharp knife several inches proximal to the necrotic/infected area, cutting skin and muscle layer.

5. **Saw through bone** using a sharp saw, knife, or improvised bone saw. Saw perpendicular to the bone axis.

6. **Control bleeding** from remaining limb stump by applying tourniquet, pressure, or cautery (heating with flame).

7. **Treat stump** with honey, salt paste, or cloth wrapping. Do not seal the wound; allow drainage.

8. **Manage shock** aggressively. Expect severe shock from pain and blood loss.

9. **Provide antibiotics if available.** If you have any antimicrobial herbs or stored antibiotics, use immediately to prevent stump infection.

10. **Evacuate immediately.** Stump amputation is not the end of treatment; infection and shock management follow.

## Summary: Decision Tree for Limb Hemorrhage

1. **Direct pressure 3–5 minutes?** → If bleeding stops, pack wound and elevate.
2. **Bleeding continues after pressure?** → Apply pressure point or elevation above wound.
3. **Bleeding still uncontrolled?** → Assess wound packing; apply packing and direct pressure 5–10 minutes.
4. **Still bleeding (Class 3+ hemorrhage)?** → Apply tourniquet 2–3 inches above wound; tighten until completely stopped; mark time.
5. **Tourniquet in place, continue monitoring.** → Check for compartment syndrome (increasing pain, numbness, coldness). Maintain for min. 2 hours to allow underlying clotting.

**Always evacuate if:** Class 2+ hemorrhage, tourniquet applied, ongoing shock, or signs of compartment syndrome.
