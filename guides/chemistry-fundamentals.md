Chemistry is the study of substances and how they interact. You don't need a lab to understand chemistry fundamentals. This guide covers the basic concepts that explain why things react, burn, dissolve, or change. Understanding these principles makes every chemistry experiment safer and more predictable.

If your question starts with "what is this?", "what spilled?", "what raw material makes X?", "what feedstock do I need?", or "what precursor starts the route to X?", use the routing below first. Unknown material should go to safety triage, and raw-material or starting-material questions should come here before you detour into household-chemical, toxicology, or lab-safety guides.

## Safety-first routing for unknown substances, spills, and precursor questions

**"I found a spilled or unknown substance" — scene safety before identification or cleanup.** Stop all nearby activity. Isolate the area, keep bystanders back, ensure ventilation, and do not touch, step in, or mix any unknown materials. **Never mix, rinse, or neutralize unknowns** — adding water, bleach, vinegar, or any other chemical to an unknown may produce heat, toxic gas, or violent reaction. Containment and isolation come first; treatment comes after identification or professional guidance.

**Active household chemical exposures route out of this guide immediately.** If the prompt sounds like "mixed bleach and ammonia," "bleach and vinegar fumes," "drain cleaner splash," "strong cleaner smell and now coughing," "child swallowed cleaner," "detergent pod burst," or "unknown under-sink liquid on skin or in the eye," treat it as a poisoning or corrosive-exposure problem, not a cleanup problem. Stop the task, isolate the area, move to fresh air if fumes are present, remove contaminated clothing, and flush skin or eyes with running water. **Do not mix cleaners and do not try to neutralize exposure with another product.** Call poison control or emergency services right away. Use [`toxicology-poisoning-response.md`](toxicology-poisoning-response.md) for inhalation, skin, and eye exposures, and [`unknown-ingestion-child-poisoning-triage.md`](unknown-ingestion-child-poisoning-triage.md) for swallowed or mouth-contact exposures.

**Household-level unknown substances and spills route to poisoning and chemical-safety guides before general chemistry advice.** If the question involves household cleaners, bleach, ammonia, mixed-cleaner fumes, drain cleaner, detergent pods, under-sink chemicals, or any unknown substance in a home or domestic context, do not stay in a generic chemistry explanation. For active symptoms or exposure, call poison control or emergency services and route first to [`toxicology-poisoning-response.md`](toxicology-poisoning-response.md) or [`unknown-ingestion-child-poisoning-triage.md`](unknown-ingestion-child-poisoning-triage.md). ### Symptom-first recognition: exposure events

- **Breathing difficulty, dizziness, or sharp chemical smell:** evacuate area, move to fresh air, ventilate. Do not re-enter without respiratory protection. If smoke or fire gases are involved, see [`smoke-inhalation-carbon-monoxide-fire-gas-exposure.md`](smoke-inhalation-carbon-monoxide-fire-gas-exposure.md).
- **Chemical on skin:** flush with running water for 15+ minutes, remove contaminated clothing and jewelry, and seek medical/poison control guidance for any burn, persistent pain, or large exposure. Do not apply baking soda, vinegar, or any other neutralizer to skin.
- **Chemical in eyes:** flush with clean water 15+ minutes holding eyelids open. Seek medical attention.
- **Reaction overheating or producing unexpected gas:** back away, do not seal the container, ventilate, do not add more reactants.
- **Ingestion of unknown substance:** do not induce vomiting. Call emergency services or poison control immediately.

## Atoms and Molecules

### Elements

An **element** is a pure substance made of only one type of atom. There are about 118 known elements. Each element has a single-letter or two-letter symbol:

- **H** = Hydrogen (lightest, most abundant element in universe)
- **C** = Carbon (basis of all life; found in coal, oil, diamonds)
- **O** = Oxygen (makes up 21% of air; essential for combustion and respiration)
- **N** = Nitrogen (78% of air; needed for plant growth)
- **S** = Sulfur (yellow; makes rotten-egg smell)
- **Fe** = Iron (magnetic metal; rusts)
- **Cu** = Copper (reddish metal; electrical conductor)
- **Na** = Sodium (silver metal; reacts violently with water)
- **Cl** = Chlorine (yellow-green gas; used to disinfect water)

![Periodic table showing civilization-critical elements including metals, nonmetals, and noble gases with atomic symbols and atomic masses](../assets/svgs/chemistry-fundamentals-1.svg)

### Atoms

An **atom** is the smallest unit of an element. Atoms are made of:
- **Nucleus:** Contains protons (positive charge) and neutrons (no charge)
- **Electron cloud:** Contains electrons (negative charge) orbiting the nucleus

**Key concept:** Electrons can be transferred or shared between atoms. This is where chemistry happens.

### Molecules

A **molecule** is two or more atoms bonded together. Examples:

- **Hâ‚‚** = Hydrogen molecule (two hydrogen atoms bonded)
- **Oâ‚‚** = Oxygen molecule (two oxygen atoms bonded)
- **Hâ‚‚O** = Water (two hydrogen atoms + one oxygen atom)
- **COâ‚‚** = Carbon dioxide (one carbon atom + two oxygen atoms)
- **NaCl** = Salt (one sodium atom + one chlorine atom)

**Subscripts (small numbers)** indicate how many atoms of each element. Hâ‚‚O means two hydrogens and one oxygen. COâ‚‚ means one carbon and two oxygens.

### Compounds

A **compound** is a substance made of two or more different elements chemically bonded. When elements combine into compounds, they create new substances with completely different properties.

**Example:**
- Sodium (Na) = soft, silver metal that reacts explosively with water
- Chlorine (Cl) = poisonous yellow-green gas
- NaCl = sodium chloride = salt = white solid that's safe to eat and use

This dramatic change in properties shows the power of chemical bonding.

## Chemical Reactions

A **chemical reaction** is a rearrangement of atoms and molecules. Atoms break apart from old molecules and form new ones.

### The Reaction Arrow

Example: `2Hâ‚‚ + Oâ‚‚ â†’ 2Hâ‚‚O`

**Combination (Synthesis):** Two substances combine to form one:
- `2Hâ‚‚ + Oâ‚‚ â†’ 2Hâ‚‚O` (hydrogen and oxygen make water)
- `C + Oâ‚‚ â†’ COâ‚‚` (carbon and oxygen make carbon dioxide)

**Decomposition:** One substance breaks into two or more:

- `2CaCOâ‚ƒ â†’ 2CaO + COâ‚‚` (limestone breaks down into calcium oxide and carbon dioxide)

- `CHâ‚„ + 2Oâ‚‚ â†’ COâ‚‚ + 2Hâ‚‚O` (methane burns in oxygen)

**Redox (Oxidation-Reduction):** Electrons are transferred from one substance to another (see section below).

### Energy in Reactions

**Exothermic reactions** release energy (heat, light):
- Burning (combustion) releases heat and light
- Many other reactions release heat
- These are usually dramatic and visible

**Endothermic reactions** absorb energy from surroundings:
- Some dissolving (sugar in water absorbs heat; solution feels cold)
- Some decomposition reactions
- These are less dramatic but important

**Practical note:** If a reaction gets unexpectedly hot, it's exothermic. Watch for runaway reactions where heat produces more reaction, producing more heat.

## Oxidation and Reduction (Redox)

**Oxidation** originally meant "combining with oxygen," but it now means "losing electrons."

**Reduction** means "gaining electrons" (the opposite of oxidation).

These always happen together in a **redox reaction:** one substance is oxidized (loses electrons) and another is reduced (gains electrons).

### Common Examples

**Rusting (Oxidation of Iron):**
- Iron metal is oxidized (loses electrons) to iron ions
- Oxygen is reduced (gains electrons) to oxide ions
- Result: Iron oxide (rust), which is brittle and falls off

**Burning Wood:**
- Carbon in wood is oxidized (loses electrons)
- Oxygen in air is reduced (gains electrons)
- Result: Carbon dioxide and water vapor (plus heat and light)

**Tarnishing of Silver:**
- Silver metal is oxidized (loses electrons)
- Sulfur compounds in air are reduced (gain electrons)
- Result: Silver sulfide (black tarnish)

### Prevention and Control

**Preventing oxidation:**
- Remove oxygen (store in airtight containers)
- Remove moisture (use desiccants)
- Use protective coatings (paint, oil, wax)
- Use sacrifice metals (zinc coating on steel; zinc oxidizes instead of steel)

**Accelerating oxidation (controlled burning):**
- Increase heat (higher temperature burns faster)
- Increase oxygen supply (blow air into fire)
- Use dry fuel (moisture slows combustion)

## Acids and Bases

### Acids

An **acid** is a substance that:
- Tastes sour (though never taste unknown substances)
- Turns litmus paper red
- Reacts with metals, releasing hydrogen gas
- Reacts with bases to form salt and water

**Common acids:**
- Vinegar (acetic acid) - weak, safe for food
- Lemon juice (citric acid) - weak, safe for food
- Battery acid (sulfuric acid) - strong, VERY corrosive
- Stomach acid (hydrochloric acid) - strong, in your body naturally
- Acetone or mineral spirits - acidic, flammable

**Industrial sulfuric acid production (contact process, battery acid salvage, how to make acid, H2SO4):** see [`sulfuric-acid.md`](sulfuric-acid.md) for the full production guide covering making acid, battery acid reconcentration, and industrial chemistry acid production.

**Industrial chemistry — strong acids and industrial precursors:** For sulfuric acid (H2SO4) production, battery acid salvage, acid concentration, and all industrial-scale acid chemistry, see [`sulfuric-acid.md`](sulfuric-acid.md).

**pH scale for acids:** 0â€“6 (lower numbers = more acidic = stronger)

**Handling:** Dilute acids are often safe; concentrated acids require care. Always dilute acids by adding acid to water (never water to acid, which splatters).

### Bases (Alkalis)

A **base** is a substance that:
- Tastes bitter (though never taste unknown substances)
- Turns litmus paper blue
- Feels slippery (soap is a base)
- Reacts with acids to form salt and water

**Common bases:**
- Baking soda (sodium bicarbonate) - weak, safe
- Ammonia solutions - medium strength, pungent smell
- Lye/caustic soda (sodium hydroxide) - strong, VERY caustic
- Lime (calcium hydroxide) - medium strength, caustic

**pH scale for bases:** 8â€“14 (higher numbers = more basic = stronger)

**Handling:** Bases are less famous for causing acid burns, but they're just as dangerous. They cause "base burns" that are sometimes worse than acid burns because they penetrate deeper.

:::warning
**Acid and Base Safety:**
- Never taste unknown substances
- Always dilute acids with water (add acid to water, never the reverse)
- Wear gloves and eye protection when handling concentrated solutions
- Acids and bases neutralize each other but release HEAT. Mix slowly
- Never pour bases into acids; mix slowly with constant cooling
- Have vinegar (weak acid) or baking soda solution (weak base) nearby to neutralize accidental spills
:::

## States of Matter

Matter exists in four states:

### Solid

- Has a fixed shape and volume
- Molecules are tightly packed and vibrating in place
- Examples: Metal, wood, ice

### Liquid

- Has a fixed volume but takes the shape of its container
- Molecules are close together but can slide past each other
- Examples: Water, oil, mercury

### Gas

- Has no fixed shape or volume
- Molecules are far apart and move freely
- Examples: Air, oxygen, carbon dioxide, water vapor

### Plasma

- Ionized gas (electrons stripped from atoms)
- Occurs at very high temperatures or in lightning
- Not relevant to most practical chemistry

### State Changes

- **Melting:** Solid â†’ Liquid (add heat)
- **Freezing:** Liquid â†’ Solid (remove heat)
- **Evaporation:** Liquid â†’ Gas (add heat)
- **Condensation:** Gas â†’ Liquid (remove heat)
- **Sublimation:** Solid â†’ Gas (add heat directly; skip liquid phase)

**Practical example:** Water boils at 100Â°C at sea level. If you're at high altitude, boiling point is lower (less air pressure). If you pressurize the container, boiling point is higher.

## Solutions and Solubility

A **solution** is a homogeneous mixture of two substances:
- **Solvent:** The substance doing the dissolving (usually liquid, usually water)
- **Solute:** The substance being dissolved (can be solid, liquid, or gas)

Example: Salt water. Water is the solvent; salt is the solute.

### Solubility

**Solubility** is how much of a substance can dissolve in a solvent at a given temperature.

Some substances dissolve easily; others don't:

| Substance | Soluble in Water? |
|-----------|----------|
| Salt (NaCl) | Yes, very much |
| Sugar | Yes, very much |
| Oil | No (insoluble) |
| Alcohol | Yes, mix in any proportion |
| Sand | No (insoluble) |
| Carbon dioxide | Yes, but gas escapes (carbonated water) |

**Practical rule:** "Like dissolves like." Polar solvents (like water) dissolve polar solutes (like salt, sugar). Non-polar solvents (like mineral oil) dissolve non-polar substances (like wax, grease).

### Saturated Solutions

A **saturated solution** contains as much dissolved solute as possible at that temperature. Adding more solute won't dissolve; it settles to the bottom.

**Example:** Dissolve salt in water. Add more salt. At room temperature, you can dissolve about 36 grams of salt per 100 mL of water. Beyond that, excess salt won't dissolve.

**Temperature effect:** Solubility often increases with temperature. Hot water dissolves more salt than cold water. This is why you heat water to dissolve sugar in tea.

## Chemical Safety Fundamentals

### The Four Rules

1. **Know what you're working with.** Read labels. Understand properties. Never assume.

2. **Use appropriate protection.** Wear gloves, eye protection, and proper clothing based on hazard level.

3. **Work in ventilated spaces.** Many chemicals produce dangerous fumes. Work outdoors or in well-ventilated areas.

4. **Have a plan for emergencies.** Know where safety equipment is. Know how to handle spills. Keep water and, for hard-surface spills only, any neutralizing agents recommended by the chemical's guidance nearby.

### Hazard Symbols

Chemical labels show hazard information:

**Corrosive:** Acids and bases that damage skin or materials
**Flammable:** Substances that catch fire easily
**Toxic:** Substances that poison you
**Irritant:** Substances that cause discomfort but not permanent damage
**Reactive:** Substances that react violently with other substances

### Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

**Always use:**
- Safety glasses or face shield (protects eyes from splashes)
- Gloves (protects hands; choose type based on chemical)
- Long sleeves and pants (protects skin)
- Closed-toe shoes (protects feet)

**Sometimes use:**
- Apron (protects clothing)
- Respiratory protection (for toxic fumes; requires proper fitting)
- Heat-resistant gloves (for hot substances)

### Emergency Response

**In case of chemical splash:**
1. Flush with water immediately for at least 15 minutes`r`n2. Remove contaminated clothing and jewelry as soon as it is safe to do so`r`n3. For skin contact, do not use baking soda, vinegar, or other neutralizers on the body`r`n4. Seek medical attention or poison control guidance if severe, persistent, or if the substance is unknown

**In case of chemical inhalation:**
1. Move to fresh air immediately
2. Rest and seek medical attention if symptoms persist

**In case of ingestion:**
1. Do NOT induce vomiting
2. Call poison control immediately
3. Follow their instructions

:::warning
**Chemical Safety Priority:** Prevention is always better than treatment. Respect chemicals. Assume they're dangerous until proven otherwise. Many severe injuries happen because people are careless with "common" chemicals like bleach, drain cleaner, or paint stripper.
:::

## Practical Chemistry at Home

You can observe and perform basic chemistry with safe, household substances:

### Simple Distillation

**Distillation** is a technique to separate and purify liquids by evaporating and condensing them. This diagram shows a basic distillation apparatus:

![Diagram of a simple distillation apparatus showing a heat source, thermometer, condenser tube, and collection vessel for pure distillate](../assets/svgs/chemistry-fundamentals-3.svg)

**How it works:**
1. Heat the mixture containing a liquid you want to separate
2. The more volatile (lower boiling point) component evaporates first
3. Vapor rises through the thermometer
4. Cold water flowing through the condenser cools the vapor back to liquid
5. Pure distillate collects in the receiving flask

This works because different liquids have different boiling points. For example, you can separate salt water by distilling itâ€”pure water evaporates, leaving salt behind.

### Creating a Simple Battery (Voltaic Cell)

![Voltaic cell diagram showing copper and zinc electrodes in acidic solution with electron and ion flow, demonstrating electrical generation](../assets/svgs/chemistry-fundamentals-4.svg)

You can create a simple battery using copper and zinc strips in acidic solution (salt water with vinegar):

1. Place a zinc strip (anode, negative) and copper strip (cathode, positive) in dilute acid
2. Electrons flow from zinc to copper (external circuit)
3. Ions flow through the solution to complete the circuit
4. Voltage output: Single cell â‰ˆ 0.5â€“1.0 V
5. Stack multiple cells in series for higher voltage

This is the principle behind early batteries and demonstrates redox reactions in action.

### Acid-Base Reactions

**Vinegar + Baking Soda:**
1. Pour vinegar into a container
2. Add baking soda
3. Observe: Vigorous reaction, fizzing, bubble production
4. Why: Acid (vinegar) + base (baking soda) â†’ salt (sodium acetate) + water + carbon dioxide (bubbles)

### Oxidation

**Rusting:**
1. Place steel wool in water (humid environment)
2. Watch over several days
3. Steel wool turns brown/rust-colored
4. Why: Iron oxidizes in the presence of water and oxygen

**Burning:**
1. Light wood or charcoal in air
2. Observe heat, light, smoke
3. Why: Carbon and hydrogen in fuel oxidize in oxygen; releases energy as heat and light

### Crystallization

**Salt Crystals:**
1. Dissolve salt in hot water (saturated solution)
2. Pour into a shallow container
3. As water evaporates, salt crystals form
4. Why: As solvent evaporates, solute can't stay dissolved; forms crystals

### Dissolving

**Oil and Water:**
1. Pour water in a clear container
2. Add oil
3. Notice they don't mix (separate layers)
4. Add a drop of dish soap
5. Notice they now mix
6. Why: Soap is an emulsifier; its molecules have one end that likes oil and one end that likes water

## Key Takeaways for Beginners

1. **Chemistry is rearrangement:** Atoms and molecules break apart and recombine in chemical reactions.

2. **Observe carefully:** Heat, color change, gas production, and solid formation tell you a reaction is happening.

3. **Energy matters:** Many reactions release heat (exothermic); some absorb it (endothermic). Understand which you're doing.

4. **Oxidation and reduction happen together:** One substance loses electrons; another gains them. Combustion is a type of oxidation.

5. **Acids and bases are opposites:** They neutralize each other, but carefullyâ€”the reaction releases heat.

6. **Safety is always first:** Respect chemicals. Use protection. Have a plan for accidents.

7. **Practice with safe materials:** Start with household substances you understand before moving to more dangerous chemicals.

## Practical Synthesis Procedures: Making Useful Chemicals

Once you understand basic chemistry, you can synthesize useful compounds from available materials. These procedures are safe, use common materials, and produce items you'll actually use.

### Making Sodium Carbonate (Washing Soda) from Wood Ash

Sodium carbonate is a powerful degreaser and water softener, essential for soap-making and laundry.

**Starting material:** Hardwood ash (oak, maple, hickoryâ€”not softwood)

**Process:**

1. Collect clean wood ash (avoid ash from pressure-treated wood or coal)
2. Place ash in a container and pour water through itâ€”this is "lixiviation" (making lye water)
3. The water extracts soluble alkaline compounds from the ash
4. Catch the liquid in a container below
5. Test the liquid with a simple soap test: if it forms a slick coating, you have lye (sodium hydroxide)
6. Boil the lye water vigorously for several hours
7. The boiling causes carbon dioxide in the air to react with the sodium hydroxide, converting it to sodium carbonate
8. Continue boiling until the liquid is completely evaporated
9. White crystals of sodium carbonate remain

**Practical use:** Add 1â€“2 teaspoons to laundry water to soften hard water and boost detergent effectiveness. Use in soap-making as an alternative to commercial lye.

**Time commitment:** 2â€“3 hours of active boiling, plus ash collection and preparation

### Making Lime Water (Calcium Hydroxide) from Limestone

Lime water is useful for water purification, mortar-making, and as a weak base for chemistry experiments.

**Starting material:** Limestone (calcium carbonate) or crushed seashells

**Process:**

1. Heat limestone to high temperature (800Â°C+) in a forge or strong fire. The heat drives off carbon dioxide gas.
2. The remaining white powder is quicklime (calcium oxide)â€”very caustic and reactive
3. Carefully add water to the cooled quicklime. The reaction is exothermic (releases heat) and caustic smoke formsâ€”work outdoors
4. Mix slowly: quicklime + water â†’ calcium hydroxide (slaked lime) + heat
5. Allow solids to settle, then pour the clear liquid (lime water) into a clean container
6. Strain through cloth to remove particles
7. Store in sealed containers; it will absorb COâ‚‚ from air and gradually become less effective

**Practical uses:**
- Water purification: Lime water neutralizes acidity and helps precipitate iron and other contaminants
- Mortar: Mix slaked lime with sand for basic masonry mortar
- pH adjustment: Raise the pH of acidic solutions

:::warning
**Quicklime Safety:**
- Quicklime is extremely causticâ€”it burns skin instantly
- Never touch quicklime with bare hands
- Wear gloves, long sleeves, and eye protection
- The reaction with water produces steam and caustic vapor
- Work outdoors away from people and flammable materials
- Keep water away from quicklime until you're ready for the reaction
:::

### Making Charcoal (Activated Carbon) from Wood

Charcoal is essential for forges, water purification, and many industrial processes.

**Basic charcoal-making:**

1. Build a hot fire in an enclosed space (pit, metal barrel, or enclosed forge)
2. Add cut wood pieces as the fire burns
3. The goal is to heat the wood without letting it fully combustâ€”partial burning under low-oxygen conditions
4. Stack wood loosely to allow some air flow but prevent complete combustion
5. Cover with ashes or dirt to reduce oxygen
6. After hours of careful burning, you have charcoalâ€”light, mostly black, and breaks easily
7. Cool completely before handling

**Activated charcoal (better filtering ability):**

1. Make regular charcoal as above
2. Soak the cooled charcoal in salt water solution (dissolve salt in water)
3. Drain and dry thoroughly
4. Heat again in a closed container with steam (place wet cloth in container, heat until steam forms)
5. The steam creates tiny pores in the charcoal, dramatically increasing its absorptive capacity
6. Cool and store in airtight containers

**Practical use:** Activated charcoal filters water, removes odors, and purifies many liquids. A pound of activated charcoal can purify hundreds of gallons of water.

### Making Soap from Animal Fats and Wood Ash Lye

Soap is made from fat (lye + heat). This is one of chemistry's oldest and most practical reactions.

**Materials needed:**
- Rendered animal fat (lard, tallowâ€”see the Animal Husbandry guide for rendering)
- Wood ash lye (made as described in sodium carbonate section above)
- Salt (optional, for finishing)
- Heat source and stirring equipment

**Process:**

1. **Prepare lye:** Make wood ash lye water as described above. Test its strength by floating an eggâ€”if the egg floats just at the surface, it's the right concentration. Lye that's too weak won't saponify; too strong is dangerous and produces rough soap.

2. **Heat the fat:** Gently warm the rendered fat to body temperature (~37Â°C). Too hot and the saponification reaction races out of control; too cold and the reaction stalls.

3. **Mix fat and lye:** Slowly pour lye water into the fat while stirring constantly. Do this very slowlyâ€”the reaction is exothermic and can boil over if rushed.

4. **Saponification:** Continue stirring for 30â€“60 minutes. The mixture goes through stages:
   - Thin and separated
   - Gradually thickens
   - Looks like thin pudding (trace)
   - Becomes stiff and uniform (saponified)

5. **Test readiness:** When the mixture reaches trace (leaves a slight impression when you drip from the spoon), saponification is happening. Continue until fully uniform.

6. **Pouring:** Pour the soap into shallow molds lined with cloth. It will harden as it cools.

7. **Cutting:** After 24 hours, cut into bars or chunks.

8. **Curing:** Store the soap in a dry place for 2â€“4 weeks. During this time, remaining lye is neutralized and the soap hardens further.

**Result:** Functional, albeit rough, soap. Improves with aging.

:::warning
**Soap-Making Safety:**
- Lye is causticâ€”never let it splash on skin
- Wear gloves, long sleeves, and eye protection
- Work outdoors or in very well-ventilated areas
- Fumes are sharp and irritating
- Have vinegar nearby to neutralize accidental splashes
- Never pour lye and fat together rapidlyâ€”the reaction is violent
:::

### Making Vinegar from Alcohol or Fruit Juice

Vinegar is acetic acidâ€”useful for cleaning, food preservation, and many chemical processes.

**Fastest method (starting from alcohol):**

1. Pour diluted alcohol (beer, wine, or diluted spirits) into a wide, shallow container
2. Cover with cloth (allows air in, keeps debris out)
3. Leave at room temperature in a warm place
4. Bacteria in the air (Acetobacter) convert alcohol to acetic acid
5. After 2â€“4 weeks, you smell vinegar (acetic acid). Taste-test: it should taste sharp and acidic
6. Bottle and store

**Method from fruit juice (slower but works with just fruit):**

1. Crush or juice fruit (apples, grapes, berries)
2. Place juice in a container with cloth cover
3. The natural yeast and bacteria ferment the juice to alcohol, then to acetic acid
4. Takes 6â€“12 weeks
5. Result: Cider vinegar or wine vinegar

**Uses:** Food preservation (pickling), cleaning, cooking, chemical reactions, and water treatment.

## Basic Analytical Chemistry: Testing and Identification

Analytical chemistry is the art of identifying what's in a substance. You don't need a labâ€”simple tests can tell you a lot about unknown materials.

### Testing for pH (Acidity/Basicity)

**Litmus paper test:**
- Dip red litmus paper in solution: turns blue if basic, stays red if acidic
- Dip blue litmus paper in solution: turns red if acidic, stays blue if basic
- Compare to a color chart if available

**Practical alternatives (no litmus paper):**
- Red cabbage juice: Changes color depending on pH (red in acid, blue in base, purple in neutral)
- Turmeric: Turns yellow in acid, reddish in base
- Beet juice: Similar color changes to cabbage

**pH scale applications:**
- Very acidic (pH 1â€“3): Strong acids (sulfuric, hydrochloric)
- Acidic (pH 4â€“6): Weak acids, vinegar, lemon juice
- Neutral (pH 7): Pure water
- Basic (pH 8â€“10): Weak bases, baking soda, ammonia
- Very basic (pH 11â€“14): Strong bases (lye, drain cleaner)

### Metal Identification Tests

**Iron and steel (ferrous metals):**
- Magnet test: Strongly attracted
- Spark test: Bright, branching sparks
- Heating: Glows red, then orange under forge heat

**Copper:**
- Appearance: Reddish metal
- Magnet test: Not attracted
- Heating: Turns black on surface from oxidation (copper oxide)
- Flame test: Produces blue-green flame (if placed in hot fire)

**Zinc:**
- Appearance: Silver-white metal
- Magnet test: Very weakly attracted
- Heating: Oxidizes to white powder (zinc oxide)
- Flame test: Produces blue-green flame

**Lead (WARNING: toxic):**
- Appearance: Heavy, dull gray metal
- Weight: Extremely heavy for its size
- Magnet test: Not attracted
- Melting point: Low (~327Â°C)â€”melts easily in a hot forge
- Toxicity: Dust and fumes are poisonousâ€”avoid sanding or heating

**Aluminum:**
- Appearance: Silver, very light
- Weight: Very light for its size
- Magnet test: Not attracted
- Heating: Oxidizes rapidly to white powder; very difficult to work with in forge (melts at ~660Â°C)

### Water Quality Tests

**Clarity:** Pour water in a clear glass. Hold to light. Cloudy water contains particles (sediment, algae, bacteria).

**Smell:** Fresh, clean water has no smell. Musty or rotten smell indicates organic contamination or decomposition.

**Taste (only after other tests):** Salty taste indicates mineral contamination or salt intrusion. Metallic taste suggests metal ions or contamination.

**Hardness test (soap scum test):**
1. Add a few drops of soap to a glass of water
2. Shake vigorously
3. If it froths and remains foamy, water is soft
4. If bubbles disappear quickly and a scum layer forms, water is hard (contains calcium/magnesium)
5. More scum = harder water

**Iron test:**
1. Leave water in a clear glass for several hours
2. If it develops a brown/reddish tint or coating, iron is present
3. Iron-rich water stains and is unsuitable for many uses

**Alkalinity test:**
- Use pH testing method above
- Most natural waters are pH 6â€“8
- Very acidic water (pH < 5) is corrosive and may leach metals from pipes
- Very alkaline water (pH > 8) forms mineral deposits

## Safety Procedures for Chemical Handling

Beyond the Four Rules mentioned earlier, here are specific procedures for safe chemical work at home or in field conditions.

### Pre-Experiment Planning

**Before starting any procedure:**

1. **Identify all substances:** Know the exact name and properties of every chemical you'll use. Never assume.

2. **Understand reactions:** Know what products will form. Research exothermic reactions (releases heat), toxic gas production, or violent reactions.

3. **Plan waste disposal:** Decide how to safely dispose of products before you make them. Spilling a surprise chemical is an emergency.

4. **Gather safety equipment:**
   - Gloves (appropriate type for the chemical)
   - Eye protection (safety glasses minimum; face shield for splashing risks)
   - Apron or protective clothing
   - Heat-resistant gloves if heating is involved
   - Vinegar or baking soda (for acid/base neutralization)
   - Water (for dilution and rinsing)
   - First aid kit

5. **Plan your work area:** Use a heat-resistant surface, away from flammables, with good ventilation.

### Scale and Concentration

**Start small:** A 10-mL test is safer than a 1-L batch. Mistakes in small batches are learning experiences; large mistakes are disasters.

**Dilute when possible:** Diluted acids and bases are safer than concentrated versions. A reaction that might boil over at full strength is manageable when diluted.

**Add acid to water, never the reverse:** When diluting strong acids, always pour acid slowly into water. The reverse (water into acid) causes violent boiling and splattering.

### Temperature Control During Reactions

**Exothermic reactions release heat and can runaway:**

1. Start with ice baths or cool water to absorb generated heat
2. Mix reactants slowly
3. Stir constantly to distribute heat evenly
4. Never seal containersâ€”pressure builds as heat increases
5. If the reaction gets too vigorous, add more cool water or ice to dilute and cool
6. Remove heat source if temperature becomes uncontrollable

**Endothermic reactions absorb heat:**

1. Apply gentle heat and let the reaction proceed slowly
2. Overheating can cause explosions or dangerous byproduct release
3. Use a thermometer to monitor temperature if available

### Ventilation and Inhalation Safety

**Toxic or irritating gases:** Hydrogen sulfide, chlorine, ammonia, carbon monoxideâ€”these kill or damage lungs.

**Adequate ventilation means:**
- Working outdoors in open air (safest)
- Working in a room with open windows and doors
- Using a fan to push fumes away from you
- Never working in enclosed spaces without exhaust

**If you feel dizzy, light-headed, or smell something sharp:** Stop immediately. Go to fresh air. Rest for several minutes.

For field protocols when smoke, carbon monoxide, or fire-gas exposure has already occurred, see [`smoke-inhalation-carbon-monoxide-fire-gas-exposure.md`](smoke-inhalation-carbon-monoxide-fire-gas-exposure.md).

### Spill and Accident Response

**Minor acid/base spill:**

1. Immediately dilute with water to reduce concentration
2. For acids on skin: Rinse with running water for at least 15 minutes, remove contaminated clothing/jewelry, and seek medical or poison control guidance.
3. For bases on skin: Rinse with running water for at least 15 minutes, remove contaminated clothing/jewelry, and seek medical or poison control guidance.
4. Never use baking soda, vinegar, or any other neutralizer directly on skin or eyes-always use water first and escalate when needed

**Chemical in eyes:**

1. Rinse with clean water for at least 15 minutes
2. Hold eyelid open and flush water into the eye
3. Seek medical attention

**Spill on large areas:**

1. Leave the area
2. Open all windows and doors for ventilation
3. If safe, on hard-surface spills only, use the appropriate spill-control material from the chemical's guidance; never apply neutralizers to skin or eyes
4. Call for help; don't try to heroically clean it up
5. If it's a strong base, let water do the dilution while you call for help

**Ingestion:**

1. Do NOT induce vomiting
2. Call poison control (in most countries, there's a national poison control number)
3. Follow their instructions exactly
4. Have the substance name and concentration available

:::warning
**Prevention is always better than treatment. Respect chemicals. The safest experiment is one you never have an accident with.**
:::
