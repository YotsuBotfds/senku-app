When expert anesthesia is available, use it. This guide covers field techniques for life-threatening situations where conventional help is unavailable.

<section id="overview">

## Overview: Airway Emergency Recognition

**The "Cannot Intubate, Cannot Oxygenate" Crisis** is the most feared scenario in emergency medicine. In austere settings, you may lack anesthesia backup, airway equipment, or rapid access to operating theaters. Early recognition and decisive action save lives.

### Why Airway Emergencies Are Lethal

- Brain damage from hypoxia occurs in **4–6 minutes** without effective ventilation
- Cardiac arrest from hypoxia within **10–15 minutes**
- Once the airway is lost, options narrow rapidly
- Prevention (early intubation before crisis) is far superior to rescue (emergency cricothyrotomy)

### The ABCDE Hierarchy

1. **A (Airway) → B (Breathing) → C (Circulation)** — If the airway is not patent, nothing else matters
2. Establish airway patency FIRST, even before starting chest compressions
3. In trauma: assume cervical spine injury; use inline stabilization during intubation

## Respiratory Distress: Recognition & Severity Stratification

### Signs of Mild Respiratory Distress

- Respiratory rate 20–30/min
- Able to speak full sentences
- No accessory muscle use
- Oxygen saturation (if pulse ox available) >90%
- Able to lie flat

**Management:** Supplemental O₂ (if available), monitor closely, assess cause

### Signs of Moderate Respiratory Distress

- Respiratory rate 30–40/min
- Speaks in short phrases; cannot complete sentences
- Accessory muscle use (intercostal, supraclavicular retractions)
- Oxygen saturation 85–90%
- Prefers upright position

**Management:** Aggressive respiratory support, prepare for escalation

### Signs of Severe Respiratory Distress / Imminent Airway Failure

:::warning
**These are EMERGENCY indicators. Act immediately.**

- Respiratory rate >40/min OR <10/min (both are ominous)
- Inability to speak (stridor or silent); cyanosis
- Profound accessory muscle use; extreme retractions
- Altered mental status, confusion, combativeness (sign of hypoxia)
- Tripoding, inability to maintain position
- Oxygen saturation <80% (if available)
- Gasping, agonal respirations
:::

**Immediate actions:**
1. Position patient upright (sitting forward if possible)
2. Begin rescue breaths or BVM ventilation
3. Prepare for intubation or surgical airway
4. Secure IV access
5. Call for help; prepare for evacuation

### Specific Causes & Recognition Patterns

| Cause | Key Features | Immediate Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Stridor (upper airway obstruction)** | Noisy breathing; may be inspiratory (laryngeal) or biphasic (tracheal); history of throat pain, swelling, or choking | Position upright; avoid agitation; prepare for emergency airway |
| **Wheezing (lower airway obstruction)** | Expiratory wheeze; history of asthma, anaphylaxis, or aspiration | Bronchodilators if available (salbutamol); epinephrine if anaphylaxis |
| **Pulmonary edema (wet lungs)** | Crackles on auscultation; pink/frothy sputum; history of heart failure or fluid overload | Elevate head; diuretics if available; PEEP if intubating |
| **Pneumothorax (collapsed lung)** | Unilateral decreased breath sounds; hyperresonance to percussion; history of trauma or sudden onset | Needle decompression if tension; chest tube if available |
| **Flail chest (broken ribs)** | Paradoxical breathing (inward movement during inspiration); multiple rib fractures | Pain control; positive pressure ventilation if severe |
| **Tension pneumothorax** | Hypotension + distended neck veins + unilateral breath sounds + shock | **Needle decompression FIRST; ask questions later** |

## Bag-Valve-Mask (BVM) Ventilation: The First Rescue Technique

BVM ventilation keeps a patient oxygenated while you prepare for intubation or surgical airway. It is a **CRITICAL skill** in austere settings.

### Equipment & Setup

**Minimum:** Bag (500–1500 mL), mask (appropriate size), oxygen source (if available)
**Ideal:** Oxygen source, PEEP valve, nasopharyngeal airway, oral airway

### One-Rescuer BVM Technique

**Position:**
1. Patient supine, head extended back slightly (unless cervical spine injury suspected; then use inline neutral position)
2. Rescuer at patient's head

**Steps:**
1. **Select mask size:** Should cover nose and mouth; bridge of nose to above eyebrows
2. **Head positioning:** "Sniffing position" — align external auditory meatus with sternal notch (extends atlantoaxial joint)
3. **Mask placement:** Press mask firmly over nose and mouth; create airtight seal with thumb and index finger on mask, remaining fingers under mandible pulling jaw forward
4. **Open airway:** Place oral airway (if responsive enough to tolerate) BEFORE bagging
5. **Squeeze bag:** Once per 5–6 seconds (10–12 breaths per minute); watch for chest rise
6. **Ventilation goal:** Slow, steady breaths that cause visible chest rise; too fast causes gastric inflation

### Two-Rescuer BVM (Superior Technique)

**Recommended if available:**
- **Rescuer 1 (at head):** Both hands on mask, double-handed seal, jaw thrust
- **Rescuer 2 (opposite side):** Squeezes bag at correct rate
- Better seal, better minute ventilation, less gastric inflation

### Common BVM Mistakes (& How to Avoid Them)

| Mistake | Consequence | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| **Mask too large or small** | Poor seal; air leaks | Use correct size; should contact cheek and forehead fully |
| **One-handed seal (pulling jaw forward insufficient)** | Airway collapses; no ventilation | Use two-handed seal; pull jaw up and forward |
| **Bagging too fast** | Gastric inflation → aspiration risk | Slow down; target 1 breath per 5–6 seconds |
| **Not opening airway first** | Tongue falls back; obstruction | Place oral/nasal airway before bagging |
| **Applying too much pressure** | Gastric inflation; aspiration | Gentle steady pressure; watch for chest rise as endpoint |

### Nasopharyngeal vs. Oral Airway Placement

**Nasopharyngeal airway (better for patients with intact gag reflex):**
- Size: Roughly equal to patient's finger diameter; length = distance from naris to earlobe
- Lubricate with saline or water-soluble jelly
- Insert along floor of nasal cavity; gentle rotation if resistance
- Advantage: Better tolerated; maintains airway without inducing vomiting

**Oral airway (use if patient cannot protect own airway or unconscious):**
- Size: Measured from corner of mouth to angle of jaw
- Insert gently; if patient gags, attempt different size
- Secure with tape or hold in place; risk of vomiting if partially inserted

## Field Intubation: Rapid Sequence Induction (RSI)

Intubation secures the airway and protects lungs from aspiration. **This is a high-risk procedure in austere settings.** Never attempt unless trained and necessary.

### Indications for Intubation

- **Apnea or agonal respirations** (no spontaneous ventilation)
- **Inability to protect airway** (coma, Glasgow Coma Scale <8)
- **Severe respiratory distress despite supplemental oxygen**
- **Severe head trauma with imminent airway loss**
- **Anticipated need for transport** (prevents aspiration during evacuation)

### Contraindications (Consider Alternatives)

- Partial airway obstruction that patient is compensating for (intubation may complete obstruction)
- Known/suspected difficult airway with no backup plan
- Hemodynamic instability (may crash during RSI drugs)

<div class="airway-algorithm">

### Rapid Sequence Intubation (RSI) Algorithm

**Pre-intubation checklist:**
1. Do you have a tube size? Do you have a backup tube?
2. Can you ventilate by bag-valve-mask? (If NO → do NOT induce; provide BVM until you have a plan)
3. Do you have working suction?
4. Have you positioned the patient optimally?
5. Is cervical spine precautions in place (if trauma)?

**Steps:**
1. **Preoxygenation (3–5 min):** Bag-valve-mask with 100% O₂ if available (or high-flow O₂); goal SpO₂ >95%
2. **Prepare drugs:** Have sedative and paralytic drawn up (if using muscle relaxants)
3. **Apply cricoid pressure:** Assistant presses cricoid cartilage backward; prevents aspiration during induction
4. **Give induction drug:** Ketamine 1–2 mg/kg IV (preferred in trauma/austere settings; preserves airway reflexes)
   - Alternative: Etomidate 0.2–0.3 mg/kg (less airway protection)
5. **Give paralytic (if available):** Succinylcholine 1–1.5 mg/kg IV (short-acting) OR Rocuronium 1.2 mg/kg (longer-acting)
   - Wait 30–60 seconds for paralysis
6. **Perform laryngoscopy:** Open mouth; visualize vocal cords
   - Straight blade (beveled spatula): Lifts epiglottis directly; easier if epiglottis difficult to see
   - Curved blade: Passes tip under epiglottis; may be easier with swelling
7. **Insert tube:** Endotracheal tube (ETT) between vocal cords; 20–23 cm at teeth (males typically need 23 cm)
8. **Confirm placement:**
   - Bilateral breath sounds on auscultation (key check)
   - Condensation in tube on exhalation
   - Chest rise with ventilation
   - If available: capnography color change or end-tidal CO₂ waveform
9. **Secure tube:** Tape or tie to teeth/face; note centimeter mark at lips (if tube slips, it usually advances into right mainstem)
10. **Post-intubation:** Start sedation + analgesia if continuing to ventilate manually

</div>

<div class="tube-sizes">

### Endotracheal Tube Sizing (Estimates for Austere Settings)

**Internal Diameter by Age:**
- Neonates: 3.0–3.5 mm
- Infants (6–12 months): 3.5–4.0 mm
- Toddlers (1–2 years): 4.0–4.5 mm
- Preschool (2–4 years): 4.5–5.0 mm
- School-age (4–8 years): 5.0–6.0 mm
- Adolescents: 6.0–7.0 mm
- Adults: 7.0–8.5 mm (males typically 8–8.5, females 7.0–7.5)

**Depth of insertion (from lips):**
- Adults: 20–23 cm (typically 21 cm for average adult)
- Use formula: **Depth (cm) = 10 + (ETT size in mm)** as rough guide

**Nasotracheal intubation (if cannot open mouth):**
- ETT 0.5 mm smaller than oral tube
- Insert through nostril; guide with finger in mouth; advance into trachea
- Higher rate of tube obstruction; more difficult; requires practice

</div>

### Difficult Airway Recognition & Rescue Techniques

**LEMON criteria for predicting difficult intubation:**
- **L**ook externally: Facial trauma, burns, obesity, facial hair preventing seal
- **E**valuate 3-3-2 rule: Can fit 3 fingers between incisors? 3 fingers from chin to hyoid bone? 2 fingers from hyoid to thyroid notch?
- **M**allampati score: Grade of visualization of soft palate when patient says "Ahh"
  - Grade I (easy): Soft palate, fauces, uvula, pillars visible
  - Grade IV (difficult): Only hard palate visible
- **O**bstruction: Stridor, drooling, difficulty swallowing?
- **N**eck mobility: Can patient extend head (sniffing position)? Cervical collar limits extension

**Rescue maneuvers if intubation fails:**
1. **Reposition:** Head extension + jaw thrust may be all that's needed
2. **Smaller ETT:** May pass through narrower opening
3. **Different blade:** Straight vs. curved; different brand if available
4. **Optimize visualization:** Remove foreign material (vomitus, blood); suction; better light
5. **Consider surgical airway:** If >2 failed attempts, move to cricothyrotomy

## Needle & Surgical Cricothyrotomy: The Emergency Airway

When intubation fails and the patient is apneic or in severe distress, **emergency cricothyrotomy is the only option** to establish an airway.

### Anatomy: Finding the Cricothyroid Membrane

**Landmarks:**
1. Palpate thyroid cartilage (laryngeal prominence / "Adam's apple")
2. Feel the depression below the thyroid cartilage — that's the **cricothyroid membrane**
3. It's typically 1–1.5 cm tall; feels like a small notch between cartilage edges
4. Mark with finger; ensure you're in midline (critical for needle placement)

### Needle Cricothyrotomy (Emergent; Temporary)

**Indications:** Apneic patient, unable to intubate, need to buy time for surgical airway or evacuation

**Equipment:**
- Large-bore needle: 14- or 16-gauge IV catheter (blue or green)
- 3 mL syringe with saline (to test for air aspiration)
- Oxygen tubing (can be connected to 15 mm adapter)

<div class="cricothyrotomy-steps">

**Needle Cricothyrotomy Procedure:**

1. **Patient positioning:** Supine, neck extended ("sniffing position") if no cervical injury
2. **Prep:** Clean skin with alcohol if available; may use iodine if available
3. **Palpate landmarks:** Find cricothyroid membrane; mark midline with finger
4. **Stabilize larynx:** Non-dominant hand stabilizes thyroid cartilage (prevents movement)
5. **Needle insertion:** 14-gauge IV catheter, bevel up, at 45° angle pointing inferiorly
   - Insert through skin; pierce cricothyroid membrane; enter trachea
6. **Confirm placement:** Aspirate with 3 mL syringe; if air comes back, needle is in trachea (may see air bubbles in saline)
7. **Advance catheter:** Holding needle, advance plastic catheter over needle into trachea; withdraw needle
8. **Secure catheter:** Tape to neck; can connect to oxygen tubing
9. **Ventilate:** Connect oxygen source (if available) or attach BVM to catheter hub

**Important:** Needle cricothyrotomy provides oxygenation but NOT effective ventilation (no CO₂ removal). This buys time until surgical airway or intubation is performed. Typically lasts 30–45 minutes before hypercapnia develops.

</div>

### Surgical Cricothyrotomy (Definitive; Lasting)

**Indications:** Failed intubation, failed needle cric, need for definitive airway, expecting prolonged ventilation

**Equipment:**
- Scalpel (or sharp knife)
- Retractor or spreader (or small forceps to hold open)
- ETT (7.0–7.5 mm for adults)
- Sutures or tape for securing

**Surgical Cricothyrotomy Procedure:**

1. **Patient positioning:** Supine, neck extended
2. **Prep:** Clean skin with alcohol/iodine if available
3. **Palpate landmarks:** Thyroid cartilage, cricothyroid membrane
4. **Incisions:**
   - **Vertical skin incision:** 1.5–2 cm vertical incision through skin overlying membrane (faster, less bleeding)
   - **Stabilize with fingers:** Non-dominant hand holds thyroid cartilage steady
5. **Membrane incision:** Horizontal incision through cricothyroid membrane (0.5–1 cm)
   - Use knife tip; deliberate controlled motion; avoid trachea below
6. **Spread opening:** Insert finger or retractor; widen opening
7. **Insert tube:** ETT (7.0–7.5 mm) inserted into trachea; advance 1–2 cm
8. **Confirm placement:** Bilateral breath sounds, chest rise, capnography if available
9. **Secure:** Sutures through tube wings, tied around neck; or tape to neck
10. **Post-procedure:** Anesthesia/sedation if continuing ventilation

### Complications & Troubleshooting

| Problem | Cause | Solution |
|---|---|---|
| **No air on aspiration** | Needle not in trachea (in soft tissue) | Withdraw; reposition; try again with fresh needle |
| **Massive bleeding** | Thyroid injury or large vessel | Stop; apply pressure; surgical airway may need delayed; consider evacuation |
| **Tube won't pass through hole** | Hole too small; swelling | Enlarge incision horizontally; may need to wait for swelling to reduce |
| **Tube in right mainstem (asymmetric breath sounds)** | Tube advanced too far | Withdraw tube 1–2 cm; reassess breath sounds |
| **No breath sounds on either side** | Tube in false passage or esophagus | Remove tube; confirm with chest rise, capnography; reintubate |

:::warning
**Do not hesitate to perform cricothyrotomy if the patient is apneic or severely distressed after failed intubation attempts.** Delay is deadly. A cricothyrotomy done at the scene beats waiting for help that never arrives.
:::

## Tension Pneumothorax: Recognition & Decompression

**Tension pneumothorax is a KILL-YOU-IN-MINUTES emergency.** The pressure inside the chest collapses the lung, compresses the heart, and prevents venous return. Patients decompensate suddenly and die without immediate intervention.

<div class="tension-pneumo">

### Classic Presentation of Tension Pneumothorax

- **Hypotension** (systolic <90)
- **Distended neck veins (JVD)** — only present if patient not hypovolemic
- **Unilateral absent breath sounds** + hyperresonance on percussion
- **Tracheal deviation** (toward opposite side) — late sign; often absent
- **History:** Trauma, rapid onset, OR ventilated patient with sudden decompensation

**Key point:** Do NOT wait for tracheal deviation or imaging. If you suspect it clinically, **decompress immediately.**

</div>

### Needle Decompression (Immediate Rescue)

**Equipment:** 14–16 gauge IV catheter, syringe (optional)

**Steps:**
1. **No time for prep.** If patient is in shock, decompress first
2. **Site:** 2nd intercostal space, midclavicular line (or 4th intercostal space, anterior axillary line)
   - Find the rib; insert needle ABOVE the rib (avoid neurovascular bundle below)
3. **Insert catheter:** Bevel up, 90° angle, perpendicular to chest wall
4. **Advance:** Should feel "pop" or air rush when entering pleural space
5. **Aspirate:** May hear air escaping or see condensation
6. **Leave catheter in place:** Remove needle; leave plastic catheter; may tape open to allow air egress
7. **Assess:** Reassess vitals immediately; continue resuscitation

**Goal:** Convert tension pneumo to simple pneumo; allows time for chest tube placement or evacuation

### Chest Tube Placement (Definitive)

**Indications:** Persistent pneumothorax, hemothorax, or after needle decompression

**Equipment:**
- Scalpel
- Chest tube (28–36 French for adults; 18–20 French for children)
- Clamp for tube
- Sutures
- Bottle or flutter valve for water seal (improvise if needed)

**Standard site:** 4–5th intercostal space, anterior axillary line (just anterior to latissimus dorsi muscle)

**Procedure (abbreviated):**
1. Local anesthesia if time permits (1% lidocaine with epinephrine)
2. **Incision:** Small (1–2 cm) horizontal incision above the rib (6th space)
3. **Blunt dissection:** Use finger or clamp to penetrate pleura bluntly
4. **Tube insertion:** Guide chest tube over your finger into pleural space
5. **Secure:** Pursestring suture around tube; additional sutures; secure with tape
6. **Check placement:** Breath sounds bilaterally, chest rise, tube fogging with respiration
7. **Water seal:** Connect to bottle with underwater seal OR flutter valve (improvised: thin IV bag with slit-valve)

### Simple Pneumothorax (No Tension)

- Breath sounds decreased but present; no hypotension; no JVD
- **Observation:** Many small pneumothoraces reabsorb in 5–7 days
- **If symptomatic:** Needle aspiration or small chest tube
- **Avoid aggressive bagging** (increases air leak)

## Aspiration & Airway Foreign Bodies: Recognition & Removal

### Foreign Body in Airway

**Presentation:**
- Sudden-onset stridor or wheezing during eating/play
- Cyanosis if complete obstruction
- Coughing, gagging, drooling
- Unilateral findings if asymmetrically lodged

**Immediate action:**
1. **Do not panic patient** (agitation increases work of breathing)
2. **Observe** to determine if partial or complete obstruction
3. **Partial obstruction:** Encourage coughing; prepare for intubation if worsens
4. **Complete obstruction:** Back blows + chest thrusts (pediatric) or abdominal thrusts (Heimlich; adult); do NOT do blind finger sweeps (may lodge deeper)
5. **If object visible:** Carefully remove with forceps if trained
6. **If object not visible:** Intubate around object if possible; proceed to OR for bronchoscopy if available

### Aspiration Pneumonia (Post-Aspiration)

**Risk factors:**
- Loss of consciousness, altered mental status
- Vomiting during anesthesia
- Swallowing dysfunction
- Nasal/oral gastric tubes

**Prevention:**
- NPO before anesthesia (6 hrs for food; 2 hrs for clear liquids)
- Cricoid pressure during intubation
- Suction before extubation

**Treatment if aspiration occurs:**
1. **Immediate:** Suction trachea vigorously; do NOT give fluids
2. **X-ray if available:** Assess for infiltrates
3. **Antibiotics:** Broad-spectrum (cover anaerobes) if risk high: Clindamycin 600 mg IV or Ceftriaxone + Metronidazole
4. **Ventilatory support:** May need intubation + positive pressure if large aspiration
5. **Avoid steroids** (no benefit; risk of secondary infection)

## ARDS (Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome): Management in Austere Settings

ARDS is severe acute lung inflammation causing refractory hypoxemia. Mortality is high even in ICU settings; in austere settings, supportive care focuses on oxygenation and preventing further harm.

### ARDS Triggers (In Austere Settings)

- Sepsis (most common)
- Pneumonia
- Aspiration
- Trauma (fat embolism, pulmonary contusion)
- Pancreatitis

### Recognition

- Acute onset of respiratory distress (hours to days from trigger)
- Bilateral infiltrates on imaging (or auscultation: crackles)
- PaO₂/FiO₂ ratio <300 (or SpO₂ <90% on high-flow O₂ if no pulse ox)
- NOT explained by left heart failure

### Management Principles

:::tip
**ARDS is a ventilation challenge, not just an oxygenation problem.** High oxygen helps temporarily; lung-protective ventilation prevents further injury.
:::

1. **Lung-protective ventilation (if intubated):**
   - **Low tidal volumes:** 6–8 mL/kg of PREDICTED body weight (not actual weight)
     - Calculate: Males: 50 + 2.3 × (height in inches). Females: 45.5 + 2.3 × (height in inches)
   - **Low pressures:** Plateau pressure <30 cm H₂O (if you can measure; otherwise aim for gentle breaths)
   - **PEEP (positive end-expiratory pressure):** 5–10 cm H₂O (if available); prevents alveolar collapse

2. **Treat underlying cause:**
   - Antibiotics if sepsis suspected
   - Drainage if pneumonia/abscess present

3. **Fluid management:**
   - Restrictive fluids (avoid overload)
   - Maintain urine output 0.5 mL/kg/hr
   - If hypotensive, use vasopressors (norepinephrine if available) rather than aggressive fluids

4. **Positioning:**
   - Prone positioning (if resources allow) improves oxygenation in moderate-severe ARDS
   - Upright positioning if patient semi-conscious

5. **Supportive care:**
   - Analgesia, sedation (allows lung-protective ventilation)
   - Stress ulcer prophylaxis (famotidine or ranitidine)
   - VTE prophylaxis (mechanical if anticoagulation contraindicated)

### Prognosis in Austere Settings

- ARDS mortality is **30–40% even in modern ICUs**
- In austere settings without advanced support: **>50%**
- Focus on preventing secondary infections and organ failure
- Consider palliative care early if no improvement after 5–7 days

## Austere Airway Emergency Kit

**Essential equipment:**
- BVM (multiple sizes)
- Oral airways (multiple sizes)
- Nasopharyngeal airways (multiple sizes)
- ETT sizes 6.0–9.0 mm, with 1 backup of each
- Laryngoscope + backup blade (straight & curved if possible)
- Stylet (can improvise: coat-hanger wire or plastic-coated wire)
- 14–16 gauge needles × 2–3 (for cricothyrotomy/decompression)
- Scalpel or sharp knife
- Retractors or spreaders
- Sutures (4-0 or 5-0 nylon/silk)
- Tape for securing tubes
- Lubricant (saline-soaked gauze acceptable)
- Suction (manual or electric)

**Medications:**
- Ketamine 100 mg/mL (induction agent; preserves airway reflexes)
- Succinylcholine 20 mg/mL (rapid-acting paralytic)
- Rocuronium 10 mg/mL (longer-acting paralytic)
- Midazolam 5 mg/mL (sedation/anxiolysis)
- Morphine or other analgesic

**Pre-intubation checklist:**
- Do I have ETT sizes in both directions?
- Can I ventilate this patient by BVM? (If no → do NOT paralyze)
- Is suction working?
- Is oxygen available?
- Are my drugs drawn up?
- Do I have a backup plan if intubation fails?
