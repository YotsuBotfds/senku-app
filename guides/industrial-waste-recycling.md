## Overview

In a circular economy, waste is feedstock. Pickling acid from metal processing becomes a processing chemical. Wood ash becomes potash (plant nutrient). Steel slag becomes concrete aggregate. This guide maps industrial waste streams—what you generate, where it goes, how to capture value—into a closed-loop system.

The bridge connects **industrial processes** → **waste streams identified** → **collection & storage** → **reprocessing methods** → **feedback into production**.

## Routing and Handoff Rules

Use this guide only for waste that is already separated at the source, clearly labeled, and traceable back to one process.

- Keep acids, bases, ash, slag, brines, and organic scraps in separate containers from the start. Do not pool "general waste" and sort later.
- If the source is unknown, the container is unlabeled, or the stream contains mixed oils, solvents, paint, plating sludge, or other unknown chemicals, stop here and hand off to a broader chemistry or wastewater guide before any recovery step.
- If a batch contains visible heavy-metal contamination, pressure-treated wood, burned plastics, or other non-target material, do not attempt reuse in food, soil, or fertilizer pathways.
- Reuse only when the waste stream is consistent enough that one recovery method can be applied cleanly. If it needs repeated washing, repeated neutralization, or repeated screening to become usable, the batch is too mixed for this guide.

## Major Industrial Waste Streams and Recovery

### **Stream 1: Pickling Acid (from Metalworking)**

**Source:** Metal surface preparation. Before welding or coating, remove rust/scale with acid (sulfuric or hydrochloric).

**Waste volume:** ~5–10% of acid input becomes spent (saturated with iron/contaminants)

**Chemistry:**
```
Spent pickling acid (acidic + iron salt solution) → Reuse in 3 ways
```

**Recovery Option A: Regenerate the acid**

1. Filter to remove large particles
2. Heat to 80–90°C to boil off water (concentrates acid)
3. Cool; ferrous sulfate (FeSO₄) crystallizes and settles
4. Decant the acid back into working tank
5. Crystalline ferrous sulfate → fertilizer or iron supplement

**Yield:** 70–80% of acid recovered; byproduct is usable fertilizer

**Recovery Option B: Neutralize for agricultural use**

1. Slowly add lime (calcium oxide) or limestone to spent acid
2. Exothermic reaction; stir constantly, add lime slowly
3. Neutralization endpoint: pH 6–7 (test with indicator paper)
4. Filter to remove gypsum/calcium sulfate precipitate
5. Result: Iron-rich, acidic water (now safe to use for soil amendment in low-pH soils)

**Recovery Option C: Extract ferrous sulfate (iron fertilizer)**

1. Cool spent acid slowly (controlled crystallization)
2. Ferrous sulfate crystallizes: FeSO₄·7H₂O (blue-green crystals)
3. Filter and dry
4. Use as iron supplement for agriculture or animal feed

:::warning
**Safety:** Sulfuric acid is caustic. Never add water to acid (add acid to water). Wear gloves + goggles. Ventilate when heating. If you burn yourself, flush with water for 15 minutes.
:::

### **Stream 2: Wood Ash (from Heating/Cooking)**

**Source:** Any fire-based heating or cooking (furnace, kiln, stove)

**Waste volume:** ~5–10% of wood mass burned becomes ash

**High-value products:**

**Product A: Potash (K₂CO₃) — Plant nutrient**

1. Collect ash (not charcoal; wait for coals to fully burn)
2. Sift to remove charcoal/large pieces (use for soil amendment as-is)
3. Leach ash with water: 1 kg ash : 3–4 liters water, stir daily for 3–5 days
4. Strain through cloth; ash residue stays behind (use for soil)
5. Evaporate water by boiling (slow, or let sit in sun for weeks)
6. Crystalline potassium carbonate (potash) remains
7. Dry and store in sealed container

**Yield:** ~2–3% of ash mass becomes potash; rest is insoluble residue (soil amendment)

**Product B: Lye (KOH) — Soap/detergent base**

1. Follow potash extraction (leaching step)
2. Boil the filtered liquid to concentrate
3. Result is potassium hydroxide (lye/potash lye)
4. Use directly for soap-making (mix with fats) or cleaning

**Product C: Lime water (for food/agriculture)**

1. If you have limestone/chalk, burn it to make quicklime (CaO)
2. Add water slowly (exothermic; add lime to water, never reverse)
3. Slaked lime (Ca(OH)₂) remains; filter through cloth
4. Clear liquid is lime water; use for water disinfection or agriculture
5. Residue (calcium hydroxide) = whitewash or mortar additive

:::tip
**Pro workflow:** Collect ash → extract potash (3 months) → use residue for soil amendment → save time by running multiple batches in parallel.
:::

### **Stream 3: Metal Slag (from Smelting/Casting)**

**Source:** Byproduct when smelting ore or melting/casting metals

**Waste volume:** ~10–30% of smelted metal becomes slag

**Products:**

**Product A: Aggregate (for concrete/mortar)**

1. Collect slag (cooled and solidified)
2. Break into 1–3 cm chunks (hammer or drop from height)
3. Rinse to remove dust
4. Dry completely
5. Mix with lime mortar (3:1 sand:slag) or concrete (1:2:3 cement:sand:slag)

**Quality:** Slag aggregate is durable, slightly reactive (self-cementing in alkaline conditions)

**Product B: Soil amendment**

1. If slag contains calcium/magnesium silicates, it improves soil structure
2. Break into fine pieces (< 5 mm)
3. Mix into acidic soils (2–5% by mass)
4. Increases pH and provides minerals

**Product C: Road base**

1. Compacted slag layer under paths/roads
2. Self-cementing in wet conditions; hardens over time
3. Better drainage than fine clay

:::warning
**Caution:** Some slag (especially from chromium or lead smelting) is toxic. Test first on small batch. If in doubt, bury safely away from water/crops.
:::

### **Stream 4: Calcium Carbonate Residue (from Lime Production)**

**Source:** When you burn limestone to make quicklime, impurities remain

**Waste volume:** ~5–15% of limestone becomes residue

**Products:**

**Product A: Fine aggregate for mortar/plaster**

1. Grind residue to powder (hand mill or mortar/pestle)
2. Mix with slaked lime (Ca(OH)₂) and sand for mortar
3. Result: whitewash, plaster, or mortar with improved workability

**Product B: Soil pH amendment**

1. Calcium carbonate naturally raises soil pH
2. Spread on acidic fields (2–5 tons/hectare)
3. Let weather for 1 season before planting

---

### **Stream 5: Vegetable Processing Waste (if farming/pickling)**

**Source:** Vegetable scraps, brining liquid, seeds

**Products:**

**Product A: Compost (from solid waste)**

1. Layer vegetable scraps with brown material (leaves, straw, cardboard)
2. Inoculate with finished compost or soil
3. Keep moist; turn every 2 weeks
4. Ready in 3–6 months
5. Result: soil amendment + nutrient recycling

**Product B: Salt water reclamation (from brine)**

1. If brine is uncontaminated (vegetables + salt + water only)
2. Boil to evaporate water; collect salt crystals
3. Dry and store for next pickling season or food use
4. Or: acidify leftover brine, reduce volume, reuse for next batch

**Product C: Seeds for next crop**

1. If fermenting vegetables for food, save seeds from best plants
2. Dry completely and store cool/dark
3. Next year's planting material

## Closed-Loop Diagram (Integration)

```
Metal Processing    →  Pickling acid waste  →  Recover acid + make fertilizer
                    →  Metal scraps         →  Recast into new items

Heating/Cooking     →  Wood ash             →  Extract potash (fertilizer)
                    →  Ash residue          →  Soil amendment

Smelting/Casting    →  Slag (cooled)        →  Crush and use as aggregate
                    →  Dust                 →  Road base or refine

Farming/Food        →  Vegetable scraps     →  Compost or animal feed
                    →  Brining liquid       →  Recover salt
                    →  Seeds               →  Next crop

Building/Lime       →  Limestone residue    →  Fine aggregate for mortar
                    →  Slaked lime liquid   →  Soil pH adjustment

(All) → Byproducts  →  Back into production
```

## Safety Rules for Industrial Recycling

:::warning
**Mandatory precautions:**

1. **Never burn or smelt unknown materials** — Unknown metals + heat = toxic fumes (cadmium, lead, mercury)
2. **Acid/base handling** — Always add acid to water, not reverse. Wear gloves + goggles
3. **Ventilation** — Lime slaking and acid heating must occur outdoors or in well-ventilated space
4. **Testing first** — New recovery process = small test batch on scrap material
5. **Labeling** — All containers clearly marked (acid, base, potash, etc.) with contents + date
6. **Disposal of non-recoverable waste** — Bury heavy metals away from water. Do not burn.
:::
