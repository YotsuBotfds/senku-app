## Overview {#overview}

:::info-box
**Start here if the paper exists and you need usable printed output fast.**

- Use this guide for block printing, carved stamps, simple type, screen printing, posters, bulletin runs, and repeatable community notices.
- Use <a href=\In a post-disruption world, the ability to produce written communication at scale becomes a critical infrastructure. Mass-produced printed materials serve as the backbone for community coordination, education, emergency announcements, and the preservation of knowledge. Where digital systems fail, the humble printing press becomes an indispensable tool for rebuilding.

The history of printing spans millennia. Block printing emerged in China around 200 CE, allowing images and text to be reproduced by carving into wood or stone. The innovation reached its peak when Johannes Gutenberg invented the movable-type printing press in 1440 CE, revolutionizing information distribution across Europe and eventually the world. Today, when electricity and industrial manufacturing are unavailable, these same techniques remain viable and teachable.

This guide covers three primary manual printing methods, each suited to different needs:

- **Block Printing** — The simplest method, best for small runs of posters, illustrations, or stamps. Requires minimal equipment and can be executed with hand carving alone.
- **Movable Type** — The most versatile for text-heavy production. Allows rearrangement of individual letters, enabling rapid changes between print jobs.
- **Screen Printing** — Ideal for fabric, large posters, and color prints. Offers excellent coverage and durability on varied substrates.

:::info-box
**Comparative Output Rates (hand-operated):**
| Method | Setup Time | Production Rate | Best For |
|--------|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| Block Printing | 2–8 hours | 100–300 prints/day | Posters, art, small runs |
| Movable Type | 1–3 hours | 200–500 prints/day | Notices, forms, announcements |
| Screen Printing | 2–6 hours | 150–400 prints/day | Fabric, color, large areas |

All rates assume a single operator and hand-powered press. Rates improve with crew training and optimized workflow.

## Block Printing Basics {#block-printing-basics}

Block printing is the most accessible printing method for resource-limited communities. The entire process—from carving to final print—requires no metal casting, no complex machinery, and minimal materials beyond the surface itself.

### Material Selection

**Softwoods** (pine, birch, linden) are ideal for beginners. They carve easily, are forgiving of mistakes, and widely available from scrap lumber or fallen trees. The downside: they wear quickly under pressure, limiting print runs to a few hundred impressions.

**Hardwoods** (cherry, maple, elm) hold fine detail and last for thousands of impressions, but they require sharper tools and significantly more effort to carve. Reserve these for designs intended for long-term production.

**Linoleum blocks** are modern alternatives requiring no wood but offering similar carving properties to softwoods. If available in salvage or stockpiles, they're superior: no grain direction issues, uniform density, and excellent line quality.

**Linocut depth:** Typically 3/16" to 1/4" thickness is sufficient. The surface you're printing from needs to be at least 1/8" above the background (unprintable areas).

### Essential Tools

The basic block printing toolkit requires only five items:

1. **V-gouge** — Creates fine lines and detail work. Available in sizes from 45° to 90° angles; 45° is most versatile.
2. **U-gouge** — Clears larger areas and backgrounds. Common sizes: 2mm, 4mm, 6mm. The wider the gouge, the faster the clearing.
3. **Flat chisel** — Straight-edged tool for sharp edges and alignment correction.
4. **Carving knife** — General-purpose cutting and shallow detail work.
5. **Sharpening stone** — Absolutely essential. Dull tools cause slip-ups, hand injuries, and poor print quality. Maintain tools after every 15–30 minutes of carving.

Additional helpful items: wooden mallet (for controlled gouge strikes), cutting mat (to protect work surface), ruler, and carbon paper for design transfer.

### Carving Technique

The cardinal rule: **design in reverse**. Any text or directional imagery must be carved backwards or flipped before transfer to the block. This is the source of most beginner frustration; use carbon paper to transfer a mirror-image design directly.

**Step-by-step carving:**

1. **Transfer design** — Use carbon paper to press the mirror-image sketch onto the block surface. Press firmly so the entire outline is visible.
2. **Outline first** — Using a V-gouge, carefully follow the outline of all positive (printable) areas. This establishes boundaries and prevents accidental gouging into keepers.
3. **Clear backgrounds** — Methodically remove all non-printing area using U-gouges. Work away from the outline toward the edges. Clear in one direction to maintain consistent depth.
4. **Detail work** — Refine lines and add texture using V-gouges and knives. Avoid undercuts (angles that slope away from the print surface), which trap ink and cause ghosting.
5. **Test print** — Ink the block lightly and pull a single test print on scrap paper. Look for unexpected ink transfer, uneven depth, or areas needing refinement.

:::tip
**Registration marks:** Before finalizing your block, carve two small reference marks (tiny triangles or dots) on opposite corners of the block edge. These are invisible on final prints but help you align multiple blocks for color work or ensure consistent positioning across a print run.
:::

## Ink Formulation {#ink-formulation}

The quality of your print depends entirely on ink consistency, pigment concentration, and oil viscosity. Three primary inks are achievable in resource-limited settings:

### Lampblack Ink (Carbon-Based)

The most forgiving and historically reliable ink, made from soot.

**Recipe:**
- Lampblack or carbon powder (soot collected from oil lamps or candles): 1 part
- Linseed oil: 3 parts
- Rubbing agent (crushed charcoal or bone meal): 1 part

**Procedure:**
1. Heat linseed oil gently (not smoking—aim for 140–160°F/60–70°C).
2. Add lampblack powder slowly while stirring constantly to break lumps.
3. Grind the mixture on a stone or in a mortar for 5–10 minutes until completely uniform.
4. Add rubbing agent for better texture and reduce glossiness.
5. Store in a sealed container; stir before each use.

This ink dries in 12–24 hours and is washable with soap and water.

### Iron Gall Ink (Historical Permanence)

An iron-tannic acid ink used for centuries, offering superior permanence—prints remain legible for 500+ years.

**Recipe:**
- Oak galls (insect-infested oak twigs), ground fine: 10 parts
- Ferrous sulfate (copperas): 2 parts
- Gum arabic powder: 1 part
- Water: 20 parts

**Procedure:**
1. Boil oak galls in water for 30 minutes; strain through cloth.
2. Dissolve ferrous sulfate in the strained liquid.
3. Add gum arabic and simmer for 10 minutes, stirring constantly.
4. Cool and filter through fine cloth. The liquid should be dark brown-black.
5. Test consistency on scrap paper; if too thin, simmer to reduce; if too thick, add water.

:::warning
**Iron Gall Ink Hazard:** Ferrous sulfate is corrosive and can damage paper over decades, particularly acidic wood-pulp paper. Iron gall ink is best suited for archival printing on alkaline (acid-free) paper made from cotton or other plant fiber. Test on expendable stock first. The ink is also mildly toxic—avoid skin contact and never ingest. Wear gloves when handling ferrous sulfate.
:::

### Plant and Berry Inks (Color)

For colored prints, use natural pigments. These are weaker than lampblack but sufficient for posters and visual communication.

- **Red:** Madder root (3 parts root powder to 1 part linseed oil)
- **Yellow:** Turmeric or weld (2 parts powder + 3 parts oil)
- **Blue:** Indigo (1 part indigo + 4 parts oil; requires extended grinding)
- **Brown:** Walnut hulls dried and powdered (2 parts + 3 parts oil)

Grind all plant inks thoroughly until uniform. Plant-based inks fade faster than carbon inks but are suitable for temporary notices and ephemeral prints.

## Paper Selection {#paper-selection}

This section is about choosing stock for printing, not making that stock from scratch. If the actual bottleneck is pulp prep, de-inking, or sheet formation, hand off to <a href=\The substrate must be chosen carefully. Poor paper choice results in disappointing prints: ink spread, uneven transfer, or rapid deterioration.

### Suitable Papers

**Salvaged printer paper** — Most accessible in immediate post-disruption scenarios. Standard office paper (20 lb) is adequate but not ideal; 28–32 lb cardstock or text-weight paper is better for block printing. Printer paper is acid-free (usually), so it holds detail well.

**Handmade paper** — If you have access to a <a href="../paper-recycling-processing.html">paper recycling operation</a>, produce custom stock. Handmade papers offer superior ink absorption and permanence. 60–80 GSM weight is suitable for block printing.

**Newsprint** — Absorbent and fast-drying, but prone to yellowing and brittleness. Reserve for temporary notices.

**Prepared paper** — Papers can be sized (coated with gelatin or starch) to reduce ink absorption and preserve sharp detail. Unsized paper drinks ink immediately, losing edge definition; properly sized paper holds the impression.

### Sizing with Gelatin Solution

1. Dissolve 1 ounce gelatin (unflavored, powdered) in 1 quart of hot water (160–180°F/70–80°C).
2. Let it cool to 100–110°F (38–43°C).
3. Dip entire sheets into the solution or brush it on evenly. Let drain.
4. Hang to dry, 2–4 hours. The paper should feel slightly stiff.

Gelatin sizing reduces ink spread and improves detail retention.

### Dampening Technique

Slightly damp paper accepts ink more evenly than bone-dry paper. Use a spray bottle to lightly dampen your paper stack 30 minutes before printing. The paper should feel cool and slightly flexible, never wet. Test on scrap first.

### Grain Direction Testing

Paper has a grain direction that affects how ink transfers. To determine grain:

1. Dampen a strip of paper along one edge.
2. The grain direction is the way the paper curls (due to fiber alignment).
3. Grain parallel to your press's motion ensures smoother, more consistent pressure.

## Movable Type {#movable-type}

Movable type enables rapid layout changes and text-heavy production. This method requires metal casting or hand-carved hardwood type.

### Type Casting (Metal)

:::warning
**Lead Toxicity:** Lead-based metal alloys are traditional but hazardous. Exposure to lead dust and fumes during casting causes neurological damage, particularly in children and pregnant individuals. If casting is absolutely necessary, use extreme precautions: cast outdoors, wear a respirator with P100 filters, avoid creating dust, and never allow children in the casting area. Alternatively, consider hardwood type (see below) or pre-cast salvaged type if available.
:::

**Traditional type metal** is an alloy of lead (85%), tin (10%), and antimony (5%), melting at approximately 250°C (482°F). This is lower than pure lead (327°C) and allows easier hand-casting.

**Simplified hand-mold casting (if proceeding despite hazards):**

1. **Punch** — Carve a hardwood or brass punch with a single letter in reverse (mirror image). The punch face should be at least 0.25" tall.
2. **Matrix** — Strike the punch into a soft metal plate (copper or lead). The resulting indentation is the matrix.
3. **Mold** — A hand mold consists of two L-shaped metal plates forming an adjustable rectangular cavity. The matrix sits at the bottom.
4. **Casting** — Heat metal to 250°C (480–500°F) in a crucible. Pour carefully into the mold with the matrix in place. Let cool 1–2 minutes before removing the cast type.
5. **Finishing** — File the bottom flat and trim excess metal. The type should be exactly 0.918" tall (standard height to paper).

Each letter requires its own punch and matrix. Casting 26 letters plus numbers and punctuation is labor-intensive.

### Hardwood Type (Simpler Alternative)

Hand-carve individual letters from hardwood (cherry, maple, or boxwood) using the same block-printing technique. Each piece is 0.918" tall and approximately 0.5–1" wide depending on letter width.

**Advantages:**
- No metal casting equipment or hazardous materials
- Hand-carved letters develop character and personality
- Salvageable from scrap hardwood
- Simpler training for community members

**Disadvantages:**
- More carving labor per character
- Wood type wears faster than metal (maybe 5,000–10,000 impressions per character)
- Grain variation can affect printing consistency

### Composing with Type

**Type case** — A wooden frame with compartments holding each character. Traditional cases have 89 compartments, with frequently used letters (e, t, a, o, i, n) in accessible positions.

**Composing stick** — A metal or wooden frame, typically 2–3 inches wide, that holds a line of type as you assemble it. The stick has an adjustable stop to control line length.

**Composing procedure:**

1. Set the composing stick to your desired line length.
2. Pick letters from the type case one at a time, placing them left-to-right in the stick (note: type is always in reverse, so you work right-to-left).
3. Add spaces (blank metal pieces) between words.
4. Once the line fills to the set width, tie it with twine to prevent falling apart.
5. Place the line onto a galley (a flat metal or wooden tray) and continue composing subsequent lines.

**Justification** — Ensuring each line is exactly the same width requires adjusting word and letter spacing. Use thin metal spacing pieces (points) to fill gaps. Proper justification is an art that takes practice.

## Press Construction {#press-construction}

A hand-operated screw press is the simplest reliable printing mechanism. It consists of five components:

**1. Frame** — A sturdy wooden or metal structure, typically 2–3 feet tall and 2 feet wide. The frame must resist downward pressure without flexing.

**2. Bed** — A flat surface (metal plate or thick hardwood) where the paper and type rest. The bed must be perfectly level.

**3. Platen** — An upper flat plate (usually hardwood or metal) that descends to apply pressure. It should be slightly larger than the largest item you'll print.

**4. Screw mechanism** — A large threaded rod passing through the frame. As you turn the handle, the screw lowers the platen onto the bed. A typical press has 8–12 turns per print cycle.

**5. Tympan** — A protective sheet (thin leather or cloth) placed between the platen and paper to distribute pressure evenly and protect the platen from ink damage.

### Ink Distribution (Brayer/Roller)

A brayer (hand roller) applies ink evenly to type or block surfaces.

**Traditional brayer construction:**

- Core: A 1.5–2" diameter wooden dowel or turned hardwood roller, 6–8 inches long.
- Surface: Wrapped with soft leather (chamois or split leather from tanning) secured with leather straps or rivets.

**Alternative:** A gelatin-glycerin compound provides a firmer, more uniform surface:
- Gelatin: 3 parts
- Glycerin: 2 parts
- Water: 1 part

Dissolve gelatin in hot water, add glycerin, and allow to set in a cylindrical mold. Once hardened, wrap the cylinder with leather for additional durability.

### Ink Slab Technique

Before rolling ink onto type or blocks, distribute it evenly on a flat surface (ink slab):

:::tip
**Ink slab procedure:**
1. Place a small amount of ink (roughly the size of a hazelnut) on a flat, smooth surface: a marble tile, polished steel plate, or glass.
2. Using the brayer, roll the ink back and forth across the slab for 2–3 minutes, distributing it in a thin, even layer.
3. The ink should look glossy and uniform, without streaks or lumps.
4. Roll the brayer across the full slab 3–4 times to charge it evenly.
5. Then apply the brayer to your type or block with a gentle, even pressure.
:::

Proper ink slab work results in uniform color across every print. Skip this step, and you'll have darker and lighter areas, uneven coverage, and inconsistent quality.

## Screen Printing {#screen-printing}

Screen printing (also called silk-screen or serigraphy) is ideal for bold designs, color prints, and fabric applications. The method uses a stretched mesh screen and a squeegee to force ink through a stencil onto the substrate below.

### Frame Construction

A screen frame is a simple wooden structure:

1. **Four wooden strips** — Create a rectangle from 1x2 or 1x3 lumber. For small prints, 12"x16" is manageable; larger screens (24"x36") require more pressure but allow bigger designs.
2. **Corner reinforcement** — Brace the corners with steel angle iron or wooden cross-braces to prevent racking (twisted deformation).
3. **Mesh attachment** — Stretch mesh fabric (traditionally silk, but modern nylon or polyester works) across the frame and staple it down, maintaining even tension. The mesh should be taut like a drum.
4. **Mesh choice** — Mesh is rated by thread count: 77-110 mesh is suitable for most prints; higher thread counts (200+) are for fine detail but harder to push ink through; lower counts (30-60) are for coarse, fast printing.

### Stencil Creation Methods

**Paper stencil (simplest):**
1. Cut your design from kraft paper or thin cardboard using a knife.
2. Place the stencil against the underside of the stretched mesh.
3. Apply temporary adhesive (spray glue) to hold it in place during printing.
4. The cut-out areas allow ink to pass; the remaining paper blocks it.
5. Limitation: Paper degrades after 50–100 prints; re-cut for longer runs.

**Photo emulsion method (more durable):**
1. Coat the entire mesh with light-sensitive emulsion (if available in salvage stockpiles).
2. Dry in darkness.
3. Create a film positive (transparent image) of your design.
4. Expose the coated mesh to bright light through the film (sunlight works, 10–30 minutes depending on intensity).
5. Rinse with water; the unexposed (covered) areas wash away, creating a stencil.
6. This method is durable for 500+ impressions but requires photo emulsion supplies.

**Drawing fluid method (chemical-free):**
1. Draw or paint your design onto the mesh using water-resistant ink or waterproof paste (such as asphaltum or bitumen-based ink).
2. Once dry, coat the entire screen with water-soluble glue (such as gum arabic or cornstarch slurry).
3. Once the glue dries, carefully wash away the draw-ink with mineral spirits or water (depending on what you used).
4. The areas where you drew ink are now open (the emulsion didn't set there); the rest is blocked.
5. This method is creative and avoids chemicals but is slower and less precise.

### Printing Procedure

1. **Setup** — Place paper or fabric under the screen. Apply temporary adhesive or tape to hold it in place.
2. **Ink loading** — Place ink along one edge of the screen's top side (inside the frame).
3. **Squeegee angle** — Hold the squeegee at 45° to the mesh, pressing firmly as you drag it across the screen. The ink is forced through the mesh and stencil onto the substrate.
4. **One stroke** — A single squeegee stroke from top to bottom is usually sufficient. Over-stroking can cause ink bleeding.
5. **Repeat** — Lift the screen, remove the printed item, register new substrate, and repeat. Each print takes 30–60 seconds.

### Multi-Color Registration

For multi-color prints:

1. **Separate stencils** — Create one screen per color.
2. **Registration guides** — Use tape or wooden blocks to mark exact positioning on the print surface. Align each screen to the same guides.
3. **Ink sequence** — Apply darkest colors first, then lighter colors (lighter ink shows better on top of darker).
4. **Drying** — Allow 5–10 minutes between color passes for the ink to set. This prevents smudging.

## Community Applications {#community-applications}

Printing capacity directly enables community survival and organization:

**Emergency notices** — Disaster alerts, shelter locations, water distribution schedules, and evacuation routes must be distributed immediately. A single printing session can produce hundreds of notices for rapid distribution.

**Educational materials** — Create textbooks, instruction manuals, safety guides, and skill-sharing pamphlets. <a href="../written-communication-protocols.html">Written communication protocols</a> benefit enormously from printed reference cards available to every household.

**Community bulletins** — <a href="../community-bulletin-notice-systems.html">Community bulletin boards</a> are more effective with printed notices than handwritten announcements. Bulletins can detail upcoming events, resource availability, and administrative decisions with clarity and permanence.

**Administrative forms** — Tax collection, work rosters, supply inventory, and medical records all require standardized printed forms. Printing allows rapid duplication of forms as needed.

**Maps** — Printed maps of your region showing water sources, hazards, safe routes, and resource locations are invaluable for community members unfamiliar with the territory. Map printing is often overlooked but critical for new arrivals.

**News distribution** — <a href="../news-distribution-networks.html">News distribution networks</a> benefit from printed daily or weekly bulletins. A small printing operation can sustain a community newsletter, keeping residents informed without electricity.

### Workflow Optimization

Establish a printing schedule: dedicate specific days to community printing. A single trained operator, working 4 hours per day, can produce 500–1,000 printed items. Batch similar jobs together (all one-color posters in one session, all multi-color prints in another) to minimize setup time between jobs.

### Troubleshooting Guide

| **Problem** | **Cause** | **Solution** |
|-------------|----------|------------|
| Uneven ink transfer (light and dark areas in one print) | Ink slab work insufficient; ink not evenly distributed on brayer | Spend 3–5 minutes rolling ink on slab before each print run. Ensure brayer is charged uniformly. |
| Blurred or smudged prints | Excessive ink or paper movement during printing | Reduce ink quantity slightly. Secure paper firmly with tape. Ensure press platen is parallel to bed. |
| Chipping or splintering block/type during printing | Block or type fibers are raised; wood grain splitting under pressure | Sand or plane the carving surface smooth before printing. Seal endgrain with wax or oil. Consider using harder wood. |
| Uneven impression from movable type | Type pieces are different heights (usually 0.918" ± 1/64") | Measure and sand down oversized pieces. Use a planer for bulk adjustment. Replace worn type. |
| Ink drying too fast (clogging screen or block) | Ink formula is too thick; ambient temperature is high | Add 1–2% linseed oil to ink and stir. Work in shade or indoors. Cover ink when not in use. |
| Mirror-image errors (text printed backwards) | Designer forgot to reverse text before carving or composing | For block printing, always transfer a mirror-image sketch. For movable type, compose right-to-left. Test print on scrap before full run. |
| Screen printing: ink bleeds under stencil (design outline is fuzzy) | Mesh is clogged with dried ink from previous run; stencil has gaps | Clean mesh thoroughly with appropriate solvent between jobs. Ensure stencil edges are sealed tightly to mesh. |
| Inconsistent pressure (some parts of large print are light) | Press platen is not parallel to bed; tympan is wrinkled | Check platen alignment with a straightedge. Replace worn or wrinkled tympan. Use shims if needed. |
| Paper sticks to inked surface (difficult removal) | Ink formula too sticky; paper sizing inadequate | Reduce oil content slightly in next batch. Dampen paper more thoroughly before printing. Dust printed sheet lightly with powder to reduce adhesion. |
