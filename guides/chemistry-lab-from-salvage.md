## Overview

In survival or isolated conditions, chemical analysis becomes essential—testing water quality, identifying minerals, determining soil composition, or producing needed compounds. A functional chemistry lab can be built entirely from salvaged materials. Glass bottles become flasks and condensers; household chemicals provide reagents; improvised equipment handles distillation, filtration, and titration. This guide covers constructing and operating a working chemistry lab from salvage, emphasizing practical techniques that require no specialized glassware, minimal cost, and careful safety practices.

Chemical work without proper equipment is inherently risky. This guide assumes the operator understands basic chemistry safety and has access to protective gear (goggles, gloves, apron). Proceed only with full awareness of the hazards involved.

**Household-level unknown substances and spills route to [`chemical-safety.md`](chemical-safety.md) first.** This guide covers building and operating a salvage lab for water testing, mineral identification, and compound production. For household chemical emergencies — unknown substances, spilled cleaners, mixed-cleaner fumes, bleach or ammonia spills, ingestion of household products — route to [`chemical-safety.md`](chemical-safety.md) for structured hazard triage and do-not-mix rules. For unknown ingestion (especially children), route to [`unknown-ingestion-child-poisoning-triage.md`](unknown-ingestion-child-poisoning-triage.md). For toxic exposure treatment, route to [`toxicology-poisoning-response.md`](toxicology-poisoning-response.md).

If you encounter an unknown substance during salvage lab work: isolate, do not touch or inhale, ventilate, contain with sand or earth, and do not mix or neutralize. See [`chemistry-fundamentals.md`](chemistry-fundamentals.md) for detailed scene-safety triage steps.

## Improvised Glassware from Salvage

Glass bottles, jars, and containers are the foundation of improvised chemistry. The goal is to assemble a minimal set of functional equipment that handles heat, chemical exposure, and repeated use.

### Safe Glass Selection

Not all glass is suitable for chemistry work. Glass cookware (Pyrex, borosilicate) is heat-resistant and chemical-stable. Standard bottles and jars may shatter under heat or be attacked by strong chemicals.

**Best salvage glass:**
- Pyrex or borosilicate cooking containers (oven-safe marking visible)
- Laboratory glassware from old unused equipment (old school labs, medical facilities)
- Wine bottles and beer bottles (heat to ~100°C safely, good for room-temperature work)
- Automotive fluid bottles (previously containing oil, antifreeze—verify empty and clean)

**Glass to avoid:**
- Window panes (not heat-safe; high thermal shock risk)
- Ceramic-coated glass (coating flakes into solutions)
- Dark/colored decorative glass (purity uncertain; may contain lead pigments)

### Minimum Glassware Set

**Beakers:** Wide-mouth mason jars (1 L, 2 L) serve well. Mark volume lines with a permanent marker for graduated measurement.

**Flasks:** Narrow-mouth bottles (wine bottles, laboratory-grade if available) for liquids and storage. Wide-mouth jars for mixing.

**Test tubes:** Small bottles, shot glasses, or rolled thin glass sealed at one end. Handle gently to prevent cracking.

**Funnels:** Cut the bottom from a small plastic bottle, creating a conical funnel. For glass funnels, stack concentric bottles or hand-shape glass tubing.

**Stirring rods:** Glass tubing (if available), wooden dowels, or hardened bamboo. Avoid metal unless inert (stainless steel is acceptable; iron rusts in aqueous solutions).

### Sealing and Connecting Glassware

**Glass-to-glass connections:** Grind the edges smooth with sandpaper, then wrap with a small amount of clay to create a seal. Replace clay frequently to prevent drying and cracking.

**Glass-to-rubber tubing:** Stretch a rubber tube over the glass outlet and secure with wire or cord tied tightly.

**Glass-to-cork:** Cork seals work for most liquids. Wrap the cork with rubber or cloth to ensure a tight fit. Test seals by inverting the container.

:::warning
Never heat sealed glass containers—pressure builds and glass ruptures violently. All containers being heated must have open tops or vents allowing vapor to escape.
:::

## Reagent Sourcing from Household and Industrial Sources

Modern household and industrial products contain most common laboratory chemicals. Sourcing reagents from these materials avoids the need for chemical suppliers while requiring careful separation and purification.

### Common Household Reagent Sources

| Chemical | Source | Extraction Method |
|----------|--------|-------------------|
| Sodium chloride | Table salt | Dissolve and recrystallize if impurities present |
| Calcium carbonate | Antacids, chalk, eggshells | Grind chalk; burn eggshells to ash |
| Acetic acid | Vinegar (4–5%) | Use directly; distill if concentration needed |
| Hydrogen peroxide | Hair bleach, wound disinfectant | Use directly (check concentration) |
| Sulfuric acid | Battery acid from car batteries | Use directly (highly dangerous—handle with extreme care) |
| Hydrochloric acid | Drain cleaner, metal etchant | Use directly (caustic—handle carefully) |
| Sodium hydroxide (lye) | Drain cleaner (older formulations) | Use directly (caustic—avoid skin contact) |
| Sodium bicarbonate | Baking soda | Use directly (food-grade is pure) |
| Sodium nitrate | Fertilizer (some formulations) | Obtain from agricultural suppliers; verify purity |
| Copper sulfate | Fungicide (some old formulations), electronics etching supplies | Available from salvaged electronics or agricultural stores |
| Iron oxide | Rust from corroded metal, or from roasted iron filings | Collect rust, grind to powder |
| Iodine | Iodized salt, some disinfectants | Extract from iodized salt by chemical precipitation (advanced) |
| Lime (calcium oxide) | Burnt limestone (kilns), or slaked lime (garden lime) | Burn limestone rocks at high heat; or obtain garden lime (calcium hydroxide) |

### Purification Basics

**Recrystallization:** Dissolve impure salt in hot water, let cool, and collect crystals that form. Repeat until pure.

**Distillation:** Heat a solution to boil off the volatile component, then condense the vapor. Used to concentrate liquids or separate liquid from non-volatile impurities.

**Filtering:** Pass a liquid through cloth, sand, or paper to remove solid particles.

:::info-box
**Purity assumption:** Salvaged chemicals from common sources are acceptable for most field chemistry. For precise analytical work, additional purification steps are needed. For water testing, soil analysis, and qualitative identification, salvaged reagents are adequate.
:::

## Distillation Apparatus and Technique

Distillation separates liquids based on boiling point or concentrates solutions by evaporating water. A functional distillation setup requires minimal equipment: heat source, boiling vessel, condenser, and collection vessel.

### Simple Distillation Assembly

**Heat source:** Campfire, camp stove, or alcohol lamp (open flame is acceptable for this application).

**Boiling vessel:** Heavy-walled glass bottle or metal pot. Avoid thin glass—thermal shock shatters it.

**Condenser (reflux coil):** A tube through which cooling water flows, condensing hot vapors back to liquid. Improvised from coiled copper or aluminum tubing, or even a rolled-up metal sheet placed in ice/cool water.

**Outlet tube:** Glass or rubber tubing leading from the top of the boiling vessel into the condenser and then to the collection vessel.

**Collection vessel:** Any heat-safe bottle or jar placed at the condenser outlet.

### Assembly Steps

1. **Fill boiling vessel:** Add liquid to be distilled (80% full to prevent splattering).
2. **Insert outlet tube:** Place the tube 1–2 cm above the liquid surface (allows vapor, not liquid, to enter tube).
3. **Connect to condenser:** Attach outlet tube to the condenser inlet.
4. **Attach collection vessel:** Place collection vessel at the condenser outlet.
5. **Start cooling water:** If using a coiled tube condenser, start cool water or ice circulation before heating.
6. **Begin heating slowly:** Increase heat gradually so vapor rises without violent boiling or splattering.
7. **Collect condensate:** Distillate (condensed liquid) drips into the collection vessel.

:::warning
Distillation of unknown liquids is hazardous—some liquids produce toxic fumes or explosive vapors when heated. Never distill unidentified liquids or mixtures from unknown sources. Distill only water, dilute acids, or other well-understood materials.
:::

### Reflux Condenser Design

**Tube-in-tube condenser:**
- Outer tube: metal tubing or glass ~2 cm diameter
- Inner tube: smaller diameter, carries hot vapor
- Cooling water inlet at bottom of outer tube, outlet at top
- Vapor enters the inner tube, cools as water surrounds it, and condenses back to liquid

**Coil condenser:**
- Copper or aluminum tubing coiled into a spiral (10+ coils)
- Vapor passes through the coil
- Cooling water flows in the opposite direction around the coil, extracting heat
- Condensed liquid collects at the coil outlet

Coil condensers are simpler to construct but less efficient. Tube-in-tube is more effective but requires careful assembly to avoid leaks.

## Filtration Setup and Techniques

Filtration removes solid particles from liquids using a porous medium. A basic filtering funnel is easy to improvise; advanced setups use multiple stages for fine separation.

### Single-Stage Filtration

**Materials needed:**
- Funnel (improvised from cut plastic bottle or stacked glass)
- Filter medium: cloth, coffee filters, or paper towel
- Receiving vessel
- Liquid to be filtered

**Setup:**
1. Place funnel over receiving vessel
2. Fit filter medium into funnel (fold cloth or stack filters)
3. Pour liquid slowly into funnel
4. Collect filtrate (liquid) in receiving vessel
5. Dispose of filter residue (solid trapped in filter)

**Filter media ranking (best to worst):**
- 100% cotton cloth (re-usable, wash after use)
- Coffee filters (disposable, fine pore size)
- Paper towel (somewhat fine, single-use)
- Cloth napkins (coarse, re-usable)
- Moss or plant fibers (very coarse, emergency use only)

### Multi-Stage Filtration (Sand Filters)

For heavily contaminated or turbid water, use a sand-and-gravel filter:

1. **Layer 1 (bottom):** Coarse gravel, 5 cm depth (supports sand, allows drainage)
2. **Layer 2:** Sand (fine, clean sand), 15–20 cm depth (primary filter, removes particles and some contaminants)
3. **Layer 3:** Charcoal chunks, 5 cm depth (removes colors, odors, and some organic compounds; optional but effective)
4. **Layer 4 (top):** Cloth filter to catch large debris before sand contact

Water poured into the top slowly percolates through layers, emerging clear at the bottom outlet.

**Effectiveness:** Sand filters remove suspended particles and some colors/odors, but do not remove dissolved salts or biological contaminants (bacteria, viruses). Filtered water should still be boiled for consumption.

:::info-box
**Filter cleaning:** After filtration, rinse cloth filters thoroughly with clean water and allow to air dry. Sand filters can be reused indefinitely; periodically "backflush" (pour water up through the filter) to clean layers. Charcoal loses effectiveness after 1–2 months of heavy use; replace if water smell/color returns.
:::

## pH Testing with Natural Indicators

pH indicates acidity/alkalinity on a scale of 0–14 (7 = neutral, <7 = acidic, >7 = alkaline). Natural pH indicators are extracts from plants and minerals that change color at different pH values.

### Natural pH Indicators

| Indicator | Acidic Color | Neutral Color | Alkaline Color | Source |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|----------------|--------|
| Red cabbage juice | Pink | Purple | Blue-green | Chop red cabbage, boil, filter |
| Turmeric | Yellow | Yellow | Orange-brown | Turmeric powder mixed with water |
| Beet juice | Pink | Pink | Yellow | Grate beets, squeeze juice |
| Tree bark extract | Red-brown | Brown | Yellow | Boil oak or birch bark |
| Blueberry | Red | Purple | Blue | Crush blueberries, filter juice |

### Making Red Cabbage Indicator

1. Chop red cabbage into small pieces
2. Boil in water (1 part cabbage to 3 parts water) for 10–15 minutes until water is deeply colored
3. Strain, collecting the purple liquid
4. Cool and store in a glass bottle (lasts 1–2 weeks; refrigerate if possible)
5. Use to test pH by mixing a few mL of indicator with the solution being tested

**Color interpretation:**
- Deep pink/red: pH 1–3 (strongly acidic)
- Light pink: pH 4–5 (weakly acidic)
- Purple: pH 7 (neutral)
- Blue: pH 8–9 (weakly alkaline)
- Green-blue: pH 10–12 (strongly alkaline)

### Quick Field pH Test

When indicator is unavailable, use physical sensation and taste (carefully):
- **Strongly acidic:** Burns tongue, stings nose, sharp sour taste (avoid consuming)
- **Weakly acidic:** Sour taste without burn
- **Neutral:** No taste
- **Weakly alkaline:** Slight slippery feeling on skin, bitter taste
- **Strongly alkaline:** Intense slipperiness, caustic burn sensation (stop immediately and rinse)

:::warning
Never taste unknown solutions. This method is only for preliminary assessment of known solutions (e.g., stream water, soil extract). For any hazardous-looking liquid, use indicator test instead of taste.
:::

## Titration Without Burettes

Titration measures the concentration of a solute by adding a reagent of known concentration until a reaction endpoint is reached. Without graduated burettes, titration can be performed using drop-count or volume estimation.

### Drop-Count Titration

**Setup:**
1. Measure a fixed volume of test solution (e.g., 25 mL) into a flask
2. Add indicator to show when reaction is complete (e.g., pH indicator changes color)
3. Using a dropper, add reagent drop by drop until color change occurs
4. Count the total drops needed
5. Repeat 3 times to get an average
6. Calculate concentration based on drop count

**Reagent volume from drop count:**
- Average drops counted: 40 drops
- Volume per drop: 0.05 mL (standard dropper)
- Total volume: 40 × 0.05 = 2 mL of reagent used

**Calculation example:**
If 2 mL of 0.1 M reagent is needed to reach endpoint in 25 mL of test solution:
- Moles of reagent = 2 mL × 0.1 M / 1000 = 0.0002 mol
- Molarity of test solution = 0.0002 mol / (25 mL / 1000) = 0.008 M

**Accuracy limits:** Drop-count titration is accurate to ±10%, acceptable for field assessment but not precision analysis.

### Volume Estimation Titration

If dropper volume is unknown, estimate volume using small containers and time:

1. Measure a known volume of reagent (e.g., 10 mL) into a burette-like container (graduated cylinder or marked syringe)
2. Allow drops to fall from a dropper for a fixed time (e.g., 30 seconds)
3. Count drops and measure volume expelled
4. Calculate drops per mL: (10 mL) / (number of drops in 30 sec × 2)
5. Use this to estimate future drop volumes

### Acid-Base Titration (Acidity Determination)

Common field application: determining water acidity.

**Setup:**
1. Pipette 25 mL of water sample into a flask
2. Add 3 drops of methyl orange indicator (or red cabbage juice indicator) to turn sample color
3. Add 0.1 M sodium hydroxide solution drop by drop from a dropper
4. Note the drop count when color change occurs (yellow to orange-red)
5. Use formula: Acidity = (mL NaOH × 0.1 M) / (mL sample / 1000)

This measures titratable acidity and is useful for assessing drinking water quality or soil pH.

## Safety Equipment and Procedures

Chemistry work without professional equipment requires careful attention to safety. Improvise protective equipment and establish safe practices.

### Minimum Safety Equipment

**Goggles or face shield:** Protects eyes from chemical splashes. Plastic safety goggles from hardware stores work; improvise from clear polycarbonate sheet secured to head with elastic if commercial goggles unavailable.

**Gloves:** Nitrile or latex gloves; multiple pairs to replace as they degrade. Improvise from plastic bags if necessary (less durable but functional).

**Apron:** Cloth or plastic apron protects clothing and skin from splashes.

**Heat-resistant gloves:** For handling hot glassware; oven mitts or leather work gloves work.

**Acid/base neutralizers:** Keep baking soda (for acids) and vinegar (for bases) nearby to neutralize spills immediately.

**First aid supplies:** Eyewash, bandages, antibiotic ointment, pain relief.

### Safe Practices

1. **Work in ventilation:** Perform chemistry outdoors or in well-ventilated spaces to minimize inhalation exposure.

2. **Know your reagents:** Before handling any chemical, understand its hazards. Assume all salvaged chemicals are hazardous until proven otherwise.

3. **Never eat or drink during work:** Contamination risk is high in improvised labs.

4. **Work slowly and deliberately:** Rushed chemistry leads to accidents. Allow time for reactions to proceed.

5. **Heat slowly:** Rapid heating causes violent boiling and splashing. Increase heat gradually.

6. **Never seal containers during heating:** Pressure builds and glass ruptures violently. Use open tops or vent tubes.

7. **Dispose safely:** Never pour chemicals down drains unless explicitly safe to do so. Contain liquid waste in sealed bottles. Solid waste (filter residues, used reagents) should be dried, labeled, and stored safely or buried far from water sources.

<div class="lab-warning">
<strong>CRITICAL HAZARDS:</strong>
<ul>
<li>Strong acids (sulfuric, hydrochloric) cause severe burns. Wear gloves and goggles. Dilute acid by adding acid to water (never water to acid—violent reaction).</li>
<li>Strong bases (sodium hydroxide) are caustic. Wear gloves and goggles. Assume all drain cleaners are caustic unless verified otherwise.</li>
<li>Hydrogen peroxide above 10% concentration can burn skin and decompose explosively when concentrated. Use at 3% (wound disinfectant level) for lab work.</li>
<li>Distillation of unknown liquids is inherently dangerous—unknown vapors may be toxic or flammable.</li>
<li>Sealed containers under heat rupture violently, projecting glass fragments and hot liquid.</li>
</ul>
</div>

### Spill Response

**Acid spill:**
1. Put on gloves and goggles immediately
2. Spread baking soda on spill to neutralize (sodium bicarbonate reacts with acid to form non-toxic salt)
3. Wipe up residue with cloth and dispose in sealed container
4. Rinse area with water

**Base spill:**
1. Put on gloves and goggles
2. Spray with dilute vinegar or weak acid to neutralize
3. Wipe up and dispose safely
4. Rinse thoroughly with water

**Unknown chemical spill:**
1. Evacuate area
2. Do not attempt to clean unless you know the chemical
3. Ventilate the space
4. Return only when fumes have cleared

## Practical Chemistry Applications

### Water Quality Testing

Improvised chemistry lab is ideal for:
- **Hardness:** Titrate with EDTA (complex cation), or observe soap behavior (soft water foams; hard water forms scum)
- **Acidity:** Use pH indicator or acid-base titration
- **Iron:** Add KSCN solution (forms blood-red complex) or tannin (forms blue-black complex) to detect iron
- **Chlorine:** Use starch solution (turns blue in presence of chlorine); useful for evaluating treated water

### Soil Analysis

- **pH:** Extract soil with water, test with indicator
- **Organic matter:** Ignite soil sample; compare ash weight to original (% loss = approximate organic matter)
- **Nutrient potential:** Dissolve soil in dilute acid, test filtrate for nitrogen, phosphorus (requires more advanced reagents)

### Mineral Identification

- **Acid test:** Drop dilute HCl on minerals; carbonates (calcite, dolomite) effervesce (bubble)
- **Density:** Use water displacement; compare sample volume to weight
- **Flame color:** Heat mineral in flame; observe color (copper compounds turn green, lithium red, potassium violet)

### Compound Synthesis (Advanced)

With clean reagents and careful technique, produce:
- **Sulfuric acid:** Distill from lead-acid battery acid (verify purity and dilution)

:::danger
Hydrogen peroxide concentration above 30% is extremely hazardous and prone to explosive decomposition. Do not attempt home concentration above 30%. Concentrated H2O2 (>30%) can ignite organic materials and decompose violently with trace contaminants or metal catalysts. Use commercially sourced H2O2 for any application requiring concentrations above 30%, or accept the 3% level from wound disinfectants for lab work.
:::

- **Hydrogen peroxide:** Concentrate from 3% wound disinfectant by careful heating and condensation (maximum 30% recommended; higher concentrations are unstable and dangerous)
- **Salts:** Neutralize acids with bases and recrystallize (e.g., sodium sulfate from sulfuric acid + sodium bicarbonate)

<section id="disposal">

## Safe Waste Disposal

Chemistry work produces chemical waste that must be stored and disposed carefully.

**Liquid waste containers:**
- Use thick glass or plastic bottles with secure caps
- Label clearly with contents and date
- Store in secondary containment (another jar or tray) to catch leaks
- Keep away from heat and incompatible materials

**Acid waste:** Keep separate from base waste (neutralization is hazardous if mixed at once). Dilute heavily with water before disposal.

**Base waste:** Keep separate from acid waste. Allow to cool before capping.

**Mixed or unknown waste:** Do not mix different waste streams. Store separately and dispose cautiously—once mixed, reactions may be violent.

**Final disposal methods:**
- **Dilute acid/base:** Neutralize with opposite reagent (acid + base → salt + water), then pour into soil far from water sources
- **Organic waste:** Dry completely, then safely burn or bury
- **Heavy metal waste:** Stabilize with lime or cement, then bury in sealed container far from water

:::info-box
Never dump chemical waste into waterways or wells—contamination is long-lasting and affects entire communities. Offsite deep burial (>1 meter down, >100 meters from water sources) is preferred.
:::
