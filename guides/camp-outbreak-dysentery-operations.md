:::danger
**Medical Disclaimer:** A camp-wide diarrhea or dysentery cluster can become a mass-casualty event through dehydration, contaminated water, and caregiver spread. The first goal is not naming the bug. The first goal is breaking the transmission loop while keeping people hydrated.
:::

<section id="overview">

## Overview

This guide is for the moment when a camp or settlement starts asking:

- "Why do several people suddenly have diarrhea?"
- "How do we stop dysentery from spreading through camp?"
- "What should we do first in a camp outbreak?"

It is an operational quickstart, not a deep epidemiology text.

</section>

<section id="first-hour">

## First-Hour Actions

1. **Assume a real cluster until proven otherwise** when multiple households or tents develop diarrhea within the same day or two.
2. **Stop sick people from food, water, and childcare duties immediately.**
3. **Create sick, care, and clean zones before moving anyone around.**
4. **Open an ORS station now**, before you finish investigation.
5. **Check the water source, latrines, and kitchen in that order.**
6. **Assign one person to case tracking** so rumors do not replace counts.
7. **Escalate early** if the cluster is growing fast, if dehydration is appearing in children or elders, or if the source is unclear after the first pass.

</section>

<section id="isolation">

## Isolation and Movement Control

Treat the camp as a transmission-control problem first.

Create three basic zones:

- **Sick zone** for symptomatic patients
- **Care zone** for staff, ORS, cleaning supplies
- **Clean zone** for food prep, water storage, and sleeping areas not yet affected

Rules:

- Sick patients do not handle communal food.
- Caregivers wash hands after every contact.
- Shared cups, bowls, and bedding stay inside the sick zone until cleaned.
- If space is tight, cohort people with the same syndrome rather than mixing with the general camp population.
- Do not rotate people back into water, food, or childcare work until the cluster is clearly controlled.

</section>

<section id="water-source-checks">

## Water Source Checks

If water is suspect, treat it as an active transmission route until it is cleared or replaced.

Look for recent changes:

- new water source
- flooded wellhead
- cracked storage lid
- dirty collection buckets
- animal access
- upstream latrine or waste runoff

Immediate actions:

- stop using the suspect source for drinking until treated
- switch to the safest backup source
- intensify boiling, chlorination, filtration, or all three
- label containers clearly as raw or treated water
- if the camp has only one plausible source and illness is rising fast, hand off to `water-purification` and `epidemic-pandemic-response` while continuing local containment

</section>

<section id="latrine-inspection">

## Latrine Inspection

Inspect latrines the same day. Outbreaks spread when the latrine system fails quietly.

Check for:

- overflow
- flies
- no handwashing station
- poor separation from living or cooking areas
- runoff toward water collection
- children defecating outside the designated latrine area

Corrective actions:

- add ash, lime, or cover material
- post handwashing monitor at busy times
- move or expand the latrine if it is too close to water or traffic
- assign cleaning duty by shift, not "whenever someone notices"
- if overflow, runoff, or missing handwashing is obvious, escalate sanitation repair the same shift instead of waiting for the next routine round

</section>

<section id="ors-workflow">

## ORS Workflow

Oral rehydration solution is the center of early survival.

### ORS station setup

- one mixing table
- one clean-water source
- one recorder
- one person watching for vomiting, weakness, and mental-status change

### Homemade ORS if packets are unavailable

Use the simplest safe household formula:

- 1 liter clean water
- 6 level teaspoons sugar
- 1/2 level teaspoon salt

Stir until fully dissolved. Use exact, level measures if possible. A stronger salt mix can worsen dehydration instead of fixing it.

Operational notes:

- mix fresh batches daily
- keep the container covered and clearly marked
- continue breastfeeding infants and young children
- do not force oral fluids into a confused, choking, or unconscious patient

### Basic workflow

- small frequent sips
- more after every loose stool
- if vomiting occurs, pause briefly and restart slowly
- keep a visible queue so weak patients are not missed
- if the queue is filling faster than you can rehydrate people, escalate and pair this guide with `epidemic-pandemic-response`

Escalate beyond oral hydration if the patient has:

- confusion
- inability to drink
- repeated vomiting preventing intake
- very little urine
- shock signs
- blood in stool with worsening weakness

</section>

<section id="handwashing-enforcement">

## Handwashing Enforcement

Do not leave handwashing as a suggestion during a diarrhea outbreak.

Enforce handwashing:

- after toilet use
- after cleaning a sick person
- before food handling
- before feeding children

Soap is ideal. If supplies are limited, place handwashing points where behavior happens, not where it is convenient to store buckets.

</section>

<section id="food-service-restrictions">

## Food-Service Restrictions

During an active dysentery or diarrhea outbreak:

- remove sick workers from cooking duty
- simplify menus
- stop communal raw-food handling
- serve food hot and soon after cooking
- stop high-risk leftovers unless safe reheating is guaranteed
- restrict shared dip bowls, ladles, and drinking containers
- if the kitchen cannot separate clean and sick workflows, pause service and hand off to `community-kitchen-mess-hall-operations`

If one kitchen crew is ill, create a replacement crew rather than "working through it."

For the ordinary group-meal side of service flow, leftovers, and cleanup boundaries, cross-check `community-kitchen-mess-hall-operations.md`.

</section>

<section id="case-tracking">

## Case Tracking

Track at minimum:

- name or tent/household
- age
- time symptoms began
- stool frequency
- blood in stool yes/no
- vomiting yes/no
- able to drink yes/no
- urine today yes/no
- outcome: improving, worse, evacuated, dead

Even a paper list is enough to answer the operational questions:

- Is this spreading?
- Is one water point involved?
- Which households need follow-up?
- Are children and elders getting hit hardest?
- If the line list shows spread across multiple zones or a widening time window, hand off to `public-health-disease-surveillance` and `epidemic-pandemic-response`.

</section>

<section id="red-flags">

## Red Flags

Urgent escalation, higher-level medical response, or evacuation is favored for:

- blood in stool plus weakness
- little or no urine
- confusion or lethargy
- severe abdominal pain
- repeated vomiting
- shock signs
- infants, frail elders, and pregnant patients with rapid dehydration
- do not wait for stool testing, perfect diagnosis, or a completed investigation if these are present

</section>

<section id="summary">

## Summary

1. Contain first: separate sick people from food, water, and childcare roles.
2. Sanitize second: check water, latrines, and kitchens in that order.
3. Rehydrate early: open ORS before the investigation is finished.
4. Restrict food service and enforce handwashing without delay.
5. Track cases in writing so the response stays tied to reality.
