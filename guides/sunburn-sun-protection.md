## Overview

Sunburn is a burn from ultraviolet light. It can happen on a cloudy day, near water, on sand, at high altitude, or when a person is outside longer than expected. The skin may not hurt right away. By the time the redness and pain appear, the damage has already happened.

This guide is written for questions like:

- "How do I keep from getting sunburned when I work outside?"
- "What should I do for a mild sunburn?"
- "When is a sunburn serious?"
- "How do I protect my lips, eyes, and face from the sun?"

The safest approach is simple:

1. Use shade whenever you can.
2. Cover skin with clothing, a hat, and sunglasses.
3. Avoid the strongest sun when possible.
4. Drink enough fluids for the heat and work level.
5. Treat sunburn early and watch for heat illness.

Sunscreen can help when you have it, but it should be treated as one layer of protection, not the only layer. Do not rely on homemade sunscreen or oils as a substitute for proper shade and clothing.

## Sunburn Versus Heat Illness

Sunburn and heat illness are different problems, but they often happen together.

Sunburn is damage to the skin from ultraviolet light.
Heat illness is the body getting too hot and not cooling well enough.

The overlap matters because a person who is sunburned may also be:

- Dehydrated
- Overheated
- Sweating poorly
- More likely to get headache, weakness, or nausea

If someone is sunburned and also dizzy, confused, cramping, faint, or vomiting, think about heat illness too.

## Best Sun Protection: Shade, Clothing, Timing

The best sun protection is not a cream. It is a combination of **shade, clothing, and timing**.

### Shade

Use the strongest shade available:

- Roof overhangs
- Trees with dense cover
- Tarps
- A lean-to
- Screened porch
- Vehicle shade if safe and ventilated

Shade helps most when it blocks direct sun, but reflected light from water, sand, snow, pale ground, or walls can still burn skin.

### Clothing

Clothing is one of the best sun barriers if it is chosen well.

Look for:

- Long sleeves and long pants
- Tight weave fabric
- Darker or denser fabric if available
- Loose fit so air can move
- A wide-brim hat
- A cloth or flap that covers the neck and ears

If you can hold a shirt or scarf up to the light and see a lot of light through it, it may not block much sun. A tighter weave usually protects better.

### Timing

The sun is strongest in the middle of the day. If you can, plan the longest outdoor work:

- Earlier in the morning
- Later in the afternoon
- In shade during the hottest hours

If you must work through the middle of the day:

- Take shade breaks
- Rotate tasks
- Cover skin before you start
- Do not wait until you feel burned

### Water, Snow, Sand, and Altitude

Sun exposure is worse:

- Near water
- On snow
- On sand or pale rock
- At higher altitude

If you are around any of those conditions, assume you need more protection than usual.

## Hydration Overlap

Sun and heat dry people out faster, especially during hard labor. Even if the skin problem is the main concern, hydration still matters.

Practical rules:

- Drink before you feel thirsty if you are working in the heat.
- Keep water close at hand.
- Use electrolytes if sweating heavily for hours.
- Do not rely on sugary drinks alone.

If you are sunburned and also working hard, you may need more fluids than usual. Watch for:

- Dry mouth
- Dark urine
- Headache
- Weakness
- Dizziness
- Muscle cramps

These signs can mean dehydration or heat illness, even if the skin burn itself seems mild.

## How to Protect Lips and Eyes

### Lips

Lips burn easily. They need direct protection.

Best options:

- Hat brim that shades the face
- Lip balm with SPF if available
- Cloth face cover or neck flap in strong sun

Do not use food oils, butter, or homemade mixtures as if they were real sun protection. They do not reliably protect against UV damage.

### Eyes

Sun can irritate the eyes and damage vision over time. Bright sun, glare, and reflected light are especially hard on the eyes.

Protection:

- Wear sunglasses if available
- Use wraparound styles if possible
- Use a brimmed hat to block overhead light
- In snow, water, or sand, treat eye protection as important, not optional

If the eyes are painful, very red, tearing a lot, light-sensitive, or vision is changed after sun exposure, treat that as more than ordinary sun discomfort.

## Mild Sunburn Care

If the sunburn is mild and the person is otherwise well, the goal is to cool the skin, reduce irritation, and prevent more damage.

### First steps

1. Get out of the sun.
2. Move to shade or indoors.
3. Cool the skin with cool water or a cool damp cloth.
4. Drink fluids.
5. Wear loose clothing that does not rub the burn.

### What helps

- Cool baths or showers
- Cool compresses
- Bland moisturizer after the skin is cooled
- Aloe gel if the skin tolerates it and it is not heavily scented
- Pain relief if available and safe for the person
- Light itch control measures such as cool cloths, gentle moisturizer, and loose clothing while the skin heals

### What to avoid

- Ice directly on skin
- Butter, grease, or oil
- Scrubbing
- Harsh soap on burned skin
- Popping blisters
- More sun exposure while the skin is healing

If the burn is on the face, neck, shoulders, or the tops of the feet, expect it to hurt more than the size might suggest. Those areas are often exposed longer and have thinner skin.

## Blisters

Blisters mean the burn is deeper than simple redness. Treat blistering sunburn more carefully.

Do not pop blisters if you can avoid it. A blister roof helps protect the skin underneath.

Protect blistered skin by:

- Keeping it clean
- Avoiding friction
- Covering it with a clean, non-stick dressing if needed
- Watching for redness, pus, smell, or increasing pain

Blistered skin should be kept out of the sun until it heals.

Seek medical care if possible if:

- Blisters cover a large area
- Blisters are on the face, hands, genitals, or near the eyes
- The person has trouble drinking because of pain or nausea
- The skin looks infected

## After-Sun Skin Care

As the burn starts to settle:

- Continue gentle moisturizing if the skin is dry
- Wear loose clothing
- Keep the skin out of strong sun until healed
- Do not peel loose skin by force
- Expect the skin to stay sensitive for several days
- Mild itching and later peeling can happen during healing

Normal healing is gradual improvement. Worsening redness, swelling, pain, or drainage is not "just peeling" and needs more caution.

If the skin starts to peel, that does not mean it is time to get more sun. It means the skin is healing and still fragile.

## When Sunburn Is an Emergency

Get urgent help if any of these are present:

- Confusion, collapse, fainting, or severe weakness
- High fever or hot dry skin
- Repeated vomiting
- Trouble breathing
- Severe swelling of the face, lips, or tongue
- Signs of heat stroke
- Sunburn with major blistering over a large area
- White, gray, leathery, or charred skin
- Severe pain that is not manageable at home
- Eye pain, vision change, or a chemical-like eye injury from a sunscreen or product

:::danger
Do not confuse a large blistering burn with simple redness. Large blistering burns, burns on sensitive areas, and any sun injury with confusion or collapse can become dangerous quickly.
:::

## Practical Checklist for Outdoor Work

Before going outside:

- Put on a hat
- Cover shoulders, back, and arms if possible
- Bring water
- Bring shade if you will be away from shelter
- Bring sunglasses if available
- Put lip protection on if you have it

While outside:

- Stay in shade when you can
- Reapply clothing or barriers if they shift
- Drink regularly
- Watch for headache, dizziness, or nausea

After work:

- Check face, ears, shoulders, neck, and feet for redness
- Cool any hot skin early
- Change out of sweaty clothing
- Drink more fluids if you were sweating hard

## Bottom Line

The most reliable sun protection is **shade, clothing, and timing**. Sunscreen helps when available, but it should not be treated as the main defense. Mild sunburn usually improves with cooling, hydration, and rest out of the sun. Blistering sunburn, eye symptoms, confusion, vomiting, or signs of heat illness are not routine and need more caution.
