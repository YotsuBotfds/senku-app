:::warning
**Disclaimer:** This guide teaches vital sign assessment for austere settings where professional monitoring equipment may be unavailable. Vital signs are tools for recognizing patient deterioration and guiding treatment decisions. They are NOT diagnostic tests; they must be interpreted in clinical context. When professional medical care and equipment are available, use them. This guide is for field assessment in resource-limited settings.
:::

<section id="overview">

## Overview: Why Vital Signs Matter

Vital signs (pulse, respiration, temperature, blood pressure) are the body's basic life-support indicators. Measuring them regularly reveals whether a patient is stable or deteriorating. In austere settings without monitors and lab tests, vital signs are your primary assessment tools.

**Key principle:** A single vital sign measurement is less useful than TRENDS. Taking vitals every 15–60 minutes and comparing shows you if a patient is improving or declining.

## Vital-Sign Observation, Intake, and Handoff Boundary

This is the reviewed answer-card surface for GD-734. Use it for recording observed or already-measured vital signs, tracking whether the person is stable or worsening, and preparing a clear handoff to a clinician or emergency responder.

**Record what is available:** time, temperature, pulse rate and quality, respiration rate and effort, blood pressure if measured, oxygen saturation if a pulse oximeter reading is available, mental status, skin color/temperature/moisture, and any notes about pain, distress, exertion, fever, injury, or measurement conditions.

**Handle uncertainty plainly:** If a reading may be unreliable, say why: no equipment, poor cuff fit, movement, cold fingers, weak pulse, difficult counting, recent exertion, anxiety, or an unclear pulse-ox signal. Repeat the observation if practical, record the uncertainty, and do not invent a diagnosis from one number.

**Trend and handoff:** Compare readings over time. Worsening pulse, respirations, blood pressure, oxygen saturation, mental status, skin signs, or overall appearance should be documented with times and handed off urgently.

**Emergency handoff triggers:** severe or worsening breathing trouble, inability to speak full sentences, stridor, respiratory rate below 8 or above 30, shock signs, BP below 90 with symptoms, pulse above 120 with deterioration, heart rate below 40 unless known trained-athlete baseline, progressive confusion, unresponsiveness, unequal pupils, severe fever with confusion or stiff neck, blue or gray color, or any vital sign trending toward danger values.

**Hard stops for answers:** Do not diagnose the condition, choose treatment, give medication or dosing advice, give resuscitation protocols, instruct oxygen therapy, repair or calibrate medical devices, interpret labs or imaging, clear someone for work, transport, or activity, make legal claims, or certify that a person or setting is safe.

</section>

<section id="pulse">

## Pulse: Heart Rate & Quality

The pulse represents the heartbeat and indicates cardiovascular function. Check pulse by feeling an artery near the skin surface.

### Normal Pulse Rates (At Rest)

| Population | Rate (bpm) | Note |
|-----------|---------|------|
| **Adult** | 60–100 | Well-trained athletes: 40–60 |
| **Child (6–12 yrs)** | 70–110 | - |
| **Child (3–6 yrs)** | 80–120 | - |
| **Infant (1–3 yrs)** | 100–160 | - |
| **Newborn** | 120–160 | - |

**Abnormal rates:**
- **Tachycardia:** Heart rate >100 bpm (stress, fear, fever, pain, shock, stimulants)
- **Bradycardia:** Heart rate <60 bpm (athlete's fitness, hypothermia, increased intracranial pressure, certain medications)

### Finding & Assessing the Pulse

**Radial pulse (most common):**

1. Place fingers (index and middle) on patient's inner wrist, thumb side
2. Feel for the radial artery (runs along thumb side of wrist)
3. Apply gentle pressure; avoid pressing too hard (occludes artery)
4. Count beats for 60 seconds (or count 15 seconds × 4 for quick assessment)

**Alternative pulse locations:**

- **Carotid pulse:** Side of neck, between trachea and sternocleidomastoid muscle; easier to find in shock; **never compress both sides simultaneously** (cuts off brain blood supply)
- **Femoral pulse:** Groin, where leg meets body; useful in shock
- **Pedal pulse:** Top of foot (dorsalis pedis); indicates lower limb circulation

### Pulse Quality Assessment

Beyond just counting beats, assess quality:

**Strength/amplitude:**
- **Bounding:** Very forceful, easy to feel (fear, exertion, fever, aortic regurgitation)
- **Strong:** Normal, easily felt
- **Weak:** Barely palpable (shock, low blood pressure, poor circulation)
- **Thready:** Very weak, barely perceptible (severe shock)

**Regularity:**
- **Regular:** Consistent intervals between beats (normal)
- **Irregular:** Irregular intervals; may skip beats (arrhythmia)
  - If very irregular with rates >100 bpm: Possible atrial fibrillation (seek medical evaluation)

:::danger
**Absent central pulses (carotid or femoral)** indicate cardiac arrest or severe shock. This is a life-threatening emergency requiring immediate resuscitation (CPR if trained) and evacuation.
:::

<section id="respiration">

## Respiration: Breathing Rate & Pattern

Respiration rate reflects oxygenation and ventilation. Assess by watching chest rise and fall.

### Normal Respiratory Rates (At Rest)

| Population | Rate (breaths/min) |
|-----------|------------|
| **Adult** | 12–20 |
| **Child (6–12 yrs)** | 18–25 |
| **Child (3–6 yrs)** | 22–30 |
| **Infant** | 30–60 |

### Assessment Technique

1. Position patient sitting or lying comfortably
2. Observe chest rise and fall (or place hand on chest)
3. Count breaths for 60 seconds
4. **Crucial:** Don't tell patient you're counting respiration; they may unconsciously change breathing. Observe while appearing to assess pulse (count respirations while "counting" pulse)

### Respiration Abnormalities

**Tachypnea (fast breathing):**
- Rate >20 bpm in adults
- Causes: Fever (1°C fever increases respiration ~4 breaths/min), pain, anxiety, shock, lung disease, metabolic acidosis
- Clinical significance: Often first sign of deterioration in shock or sepsis

**Bradypnea (slow breathing):**
- Rate <12 bpm in adults
- Causes: CNS depression (head injury, stroke, medication overdose), hypothermia, fatigue
- Danger: Inadequate oxygen delivery; worsens if continues

**Dyspnea (difficulty breathing):**
- Subjective complaint of breathlessness
- Patient states "can't catch breath" or feels short of breath
- Causes: Respiratory/cardiac disease, anxiety, shock, pain
- Red flag: Dyspnea at rest indicates serious pathology

**Stridor:**
- High-pitched breathing sound (usually on inhalation)
- Indicates airway obstruction (swelling, foreign object)
- Emergency: Airway obstruction; requires immediate intervention

### Breathing Pattern Assessment

- **Regular:** Consistent depth and rate (normal)
- **Irregular:** Variable depth or rate; concerning for CNS injury or fatigue
- **Cheyne-Stokes:** Progressively deeper breathing → pause → shallow breathing cycle; seen in severe illness
- **Pursed-lip breathing:** Patient breathing through pursed lips; sign of respiratory distress (trying to keep small airways open)

:::danger
**Respiratory red flags:**
- Stridor (airway obstruction)
- Severe dyspnea at rest (respiratory failure imminent)
- Severe bradypnea (<8 breaths/min; inadequate ventilation)
- Inability to speak full sentences (severe respiratory distress)

Any of these require immediate airway support and evacuation.
:::

</section>

## Temperature: Body Heat Regulation

Temperature reflects metabolic rate and immune response to infection.

### Normal Temperature Ranges

| Location | Range |
|----------|-------|
| **Oral (mouth)** | 36.5–37.5°C (97.7–99.5°F) |
| **Rectal** | 37.5–38°C (99.5–100.4°F) |
| **Axillary (armpit)** | 36–37.5°C (96.8–99.5°F) |

**Note:** Oral temperature is standard. Rectal is more accurate but less convenient. Axillary is least accurate. Temperature varies by time of day (lower morning, higher evening by ~0.5°C).

### Assessing Temperature Without a Thermometer

If no thermometer available:

**Tactile assessment:**
1. Place back of hand on patient's forehead, then on your own forehead, then back to patient's
2. Feel for relative warmth (patient hotter, equal, or cooler than you)
3. Compare to patient's baseline
4. Not quantitative but can detect significant fever

**Subjective signs of fever:**
- Flushed, red face (especially cheeks)
- Glazed eyes
- Complaint of feeling hot or chills
- Sweating (as fever breaks)
- Shivering (generating body heat when fever rising)

### Fever vs. Hypothermia

- **Fever:** Core temperature >38°C (100.4°F); indicates infection or heat stress
- **Hypothermia:** Core temperature <35°C (95°F); indicates cold exposure or systemic illness

**Mild hypothermia (35–32°C / 95–90°F):** Shivering, confusion, decreased coordination
**Severe hypothermia (<32°C / <90°F):** No shivering, decreased consciousness, cardiac arrhythmias, appears dead

:::danger
**Severe hypothermia:** Patient may appear dead (no pulse, not breathing) but can be resuscitated if warmed slowly. Do not assume death until patient is warm and pulseless. Gentle rewarming (external heat, warm drinks if conscious) is critical.
:::

## Blood Pressure: Circulation Adequacy

Blood pressure indicates how effectively the heart pumps blood and how resistant blood vessels are. Useful for detecting shock and hypertension.

### Normal Blood Pressure

- **Adult:** <120/80 mmHg is optimal; <130/80 considered normal
- **Elevated:** Systolic 120–139 or diastolic 80–89
- **Stage 1 hypertension:** Systolic 130–139 or diastolic 80–89
- **Stage 2 hypertension:** Systolic ≥140 or diastolic ≥90
- **Hypotension (low BP):** <90/60 mmHg; concerning if patient symptomatic

**Children:** Normal BP is lower; varies by age and size

### Assessment Without Equipment (Qualitative)

If no blood pressure cuff available:

**Radial pulse presence:**
- Radial pulse palpable at rest: BP likely ≥80 systolic
- Radial pulse absent but carotid present: BP likely 50–80 systolic
- No pulses palpable: BP likely <50 (profound shock)

**Capillary refill:**
1. Press fingernail bed until white (apply pressure for 2 seconds)
2. Release and observe color return
3. Normal: Immediate color return (<2 seconds = normal)
4. Delayed (>2 seconds): Suggests poor circulation/shock

**Skin quality:**
- Warm, pink: Good circulation
- Cool, pale: Poor circulation (shock)
- Blue lips/fingertips: Severe hypoxia

**Orthostatic vital signs (if patient able to stand):**
1. Measure BP and HR lying down; rest 3 minutes
2. Measure BP and HR sitting; rest 3 minutes
3. Measure BP and HR standing
4. Significant drop when standing (systolic >20 mmHg drop or HR increase >20 bpm) suggests dehydration or blood loss

### Blood Pressure Measurement (With Cuff If Available)

**Manual measurement:**
1. Patient seated, arm at heart level
2. Inflate cuff until radial pulse disappears
3. Place stethoscope in antecubital fossa (inner elbow)
4. Slowly deflate cuff; note pressure when first sound heard (systolic)
5. Continue deflating; note pressure when sound disappears (diastolic)

**Record as:** Systolic/Diastolic (e.g., 120/80 mmHg)

### Abnormal Blood Pressures

**Hypertension (high BP):**
- Causes: Stress, pain, kidney disease, salt excess
- Usually asymptomatic but increases stroke/heart attack risk

**Hypotension (low BP):**
- Causes: Shock (blood loss, sepsis, heart failure), dehydration, medication, severe illness
- **Symptomatic hypotension** (with dizziness, weakness, confusion) is dangerous

:::danger
**Hypotension with symptoms (dizziness, weakness, confusion, rapid weak pulse, cool clammy skin) indicates shock—a medical emergency.** Requires fluid resuscitation, elevating legs, keeping warm, and evacuation if available.
:::

## Rapid Patient Assessment

When first assessing a patient, use this systematic approach:

### Scene Safety & Primary Assessment (ABCDE)

**A - Airway:** Is airway open? Can patient speak clearly?
- If not: Open airway (head-tilt, chin-lift), clear obstructions, consider airway support

**B - Breathing:** Is patient breathing? Rate normal? Effort normal?
- If not breathing: Begin rescue breathing if trained
- If stridor or severe distress: Treat airway emergency

**C - Circulation:** Is patient conscious? Pulse present? Bleeding?
- If no pulse: Begin CPR if trained
- If major bleeding: Apply direct pressure
- If appears in shock: Elevate legs, keep warm

**D - Disability:** Level of consciousness? Neurologic deficits?
- Alert, verbal, pain-responsive, unresponsive (AVPU scale)
- Move all limbs equally? Pupils equal and reactive?

**E - Exposure:** Examine for hidden injuries; prevent heat loss

### Secondary Assessment (Head-to-Toe)

1. **Head:** Deformity? Bleeding from ears/nose (CSF)? Scalp wounds?
2. **Neck:** Stiffness? Deformity? Tracheal deviation? Jugular venous distension?
3. **Chest:** Symmetry? Breath sounds equal? Rib tenderness? Crepitus?
4. **Abdomen:** Distension? Tenderness? Rigidity? Rebound? Bowel sounds?
5. **Pelvis:** Stability? Pain?
6. **Extremities:** Deformity? Swelling? Distal pulses? Color/temperature? Sensation?
7. **Back (log-roll if spine clearance uncertain):** Deformity? Tenderness? Wound?

### Vital Signs

Take and record:
- Pulse (rate, strength, regularity)
- Respiration (rate, effort, pattern)
- Temperature (if possible)
- Blood pressure (if equipment available)

### Trending Vital Signs

In austere settings, **trends matter more than single measurements**:

- Take vitals every 15–60 minutes (frequency depends on illness severity)
- Record each measurement with time
- Compare to previous measurements:
  - **Improving:** Vital signs normalizing; patient responding to treatment
  - **Stable:** Consistent vitals; patient status unchanged
  - **Deteriorating:** Vital signs worsening (increasing HR, decreasing BP, increasing RR); indicates patient is decompensating

Example trend indicating shock development:
- T0: HR 85, RR 16, BP 120/75, alert
- T1 (15 min): HR 95, RR 18, BP 118/75, alert
- T2 (30 min): HR 110, RR 22, BP 110/72, slightly confused
- T3 (45 min): HR 125, RR 28, BP 95/60, confused, cool skin

This trend shows **progressive shock** requiring immediate intervention (fluids, evacuation).

<section id="consciousness-assessment">

## Level of Consciousness: AVPU Scale

Rapid assessment of neurologic function:

**A - Alert:** Patient is awake and oriented to person, place, time
**V - Verbal:** Patient responds to voice (may be confused)
**P - Pain:** Patient responds only to painful stimulus (sternal rub, pinch)
**U - Unresponsive:** Patient does not respond to any stimulus

**Clinical significance:**
- **Alert or Verbal with appropriate response:** Good neurologic status
- **Pain-responsive or decreased verbal response:** Possible head injury, stroke, intoxication, severe illness
- **Unresponsive:** Severe CNS injury or systemic illness; medical emergency

:::danger
**Progressive decrease in consciousness** (Alert → Verbal → Pain → Unresponsive) indicates brain injury, increased intracranial pressure, or systemic deterioration. This is a medical emergency.
:::

</section>

<section id="skin-assessment">

## Skin Assessment: Perfusion & Hydration

Skin reflects circulation, hydration, and systemic illness.

### Skin Color

- **Pink:** Normal, good oxygenation
- **Pallor (pale):** Anemia, shock, fear, hypothermia
- **Flushing (red):** Fever, heat, embarrassment, heat stroke
- **Cyanosis (blue):** Severe hypoxia (lips, fingertips, tongue are key sites)
- **Jaundice (yellow):** Liver disease or hemolysis
- **Mottled (blotchy):** Shock, poor perfusion

### Skin Moisture

- **Dry:** Normal or dehydration
- **Diaphoretic (wet with sweat):** Shock, fear, heat, sympathetic activation
- **Clammy (cool and damp):** Shock, heart attack

### Skin Temperature

- **Warm:** Normal perfusion
- **Cool (extremities):** Poor peripheral circulation (shock)
- **Hot:** Fever, heat stroke

### Skin Turgor (Hydration Assessment)

1. Pinch skin on forearm
2. Release and observe how quickly skin returns to normal
3. **Normal (<2 seconds):** Good hydration
4. **Slow (>2 seconds):** Dehydration

**Note:** Less reliable in elderly (skin naturally loose) and children (more elastic)

### Wounds & Infection Signs

Assess any wounds for:
- Redness (erythema)
- Warmth
- Swelling (edema)
- Pus or drainage
- Red streaks extending from wound (lymphangitis—spreading infection)

## Pupil Response: Neurologic Function

Pupil response indicates brainstem function.

### Normal Pupils

- **Size:** 3–5 mm diameter at rest
- **Shape:** Round
- **Response:** Constrict (get smaller) with light; dilate with darkness
- **Equality:** Both sides equal in size and response

### Abnormal Pupil Findings

**Dilated pupils (mydriasis):**
- Causes: Brain injury, hemorrhage, catecholamine release (fear, stimulants), drugs
- Concern: Increased intracranial pressure

**Constricted pupils (miosis):**
- Causes: Opioid drugs, brainstem injury, pontine hemorrhage
- Concern: Severe brain injury, drug overdose

**Unequal pupils:**
- Concerning sign; possible increased ICP, hemorrhage, mass effect
- Emergency: Requires evacuation

**Fixed pupils (no response to light):**
- Sign of death or severe brain injury
- Either side fixed alone suggests herniation; both fixed suggests brain death

### Pupil Assessment Technique

1. Darken environment if possible
2. Hold penlight 15 cm from eye
3. Shine light into pupil; observe constriction
4. Move light away; observe dilation
5. Compare both eyes for size and speed of response

:::tip
**Pupil light reflex:** Test in both eyes. A normal response is constriction to light; dilation when light removed. Any asymmetry or lack of response is abnormal.
:::

<section id="when-to-seek-help">

## When to Seek Professional Medical Care

Based on vital signs and assessment findings, evacuate if:

- **Abnormal airway/breathing:** Stridor, severe dyspnea, respiratory rate <8 or >30, unable to speak
- **Shock:** HR >120, BP <90, cool clammy skin, altered mental status
- **Altered consciousness:** Unresponsive, progressive confusion, unequal pupils
- **Severe bradycardia:** HR <40 (unless trained athlete)
- **Fever with severe symptoms:** High fever with confusion, severe headache, stiff neck
- **Any vital sign trending toward danger values** despite treatment

Professional medical evaluation is superior to field assessment when available.

</section>

## Sample Vital Signs Log

Keep written record of vitals for trending:

| Time | HR | RR | Temp | BP | Mental Status | Skin | Notes |
|------|----|----|------|----|----|------|-------|
| 10:00 | 88 | 16 | 37°C | 120/78 | Alert | Warm, pink | Baseline |
| 10:15 | 92 | 18 | 38°C | 118/76 | Alert | Warm, pink | Slight fever developing |
| 10:30 | 98 | 20 | 38.5°C | 115/74 | Alert | Warm, flushed | Fever increasing |
| 10:45 | 105 | 24 | 39°C | 110/72 | Drowsy | Warm, diaphoretic | Deteriorating—fever continues |

**Trend:** HR and RR increasing; BP decreasing; altered mental status appearing = **Possible sepsis. Evacuate.**
