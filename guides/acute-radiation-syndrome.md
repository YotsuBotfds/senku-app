# Acute Radiation Syndrome Clinical Management

Acute radiation syndrome (ARS) is systemic illness caused by exposure to high doses of ionizing radiation over a short period. Unlike external trauma, ARS is invisible—damage occurs at the cellular level in rapidly dividing tissues (bone marrow, GI tract, skin). Successful management requires rapid clinical assessment, aggressive supportive care, and realistic triage when resources are scarce.

<section id="radiation-physics-for-clinicians">

## Radiation Physics Basics for Clinicians

Understanding radiation terminology enables communication with specialists and rational treatment decisions.

### Dose Units

**Gray (Gy):** Measure of radiation absorbed by tissue. 1 Gy = 1 joule of energy absorbed per kilogram of tissue. This is the physical dose, independent of biological effect.

**Sievert (Sv):** Measure of biological effect of radiation (dose equivalent). Accounts for the fact that different types of radiation cause different biological damage at the same physical dose. For acute whole-body exposure to gamma rays or X-rays, 1 Gy ≈ 1 Sv.

**Lethal dose thresholds:**
- 0.5–1.0 Sv: Some symptoms; recovery likely with supportive care.
- 1.0–6.0 Sv: Serious illness (ARS); outcome depends on dose and care availability.
- 6.0–8.0 Sv: Probably lethal even with intensive care (CNS syndrome).
- >8.0 Sv: Nearly universally fatal (CNS syndrome, death within hours to days).

### Types of Exposure

**External exposure:** Radiation source is outside the body. Dose depends on distance, shielding, and duration of exposure. Stopping the exposure stops dose delivery. Most ARS cases result from external exposure.

**Internal contamination:** Radioactive material is ingested, inhaled, or absorbed through skin. Continued internal dose delivery even after the source is no longer external. Decontamination and chelation therapy may reduce dose.

**Combined external + internal:** Most dangerous. Requires dose estimation from both sources.

<section id="exposure-decontamination">

## Exposure Pathways and Initial Decontamination

Early actions after suspected radiation exposure reduce dose and contamination.

### Exposure Pathways

**Direct radiation:** Gamma rays or neutrons from the source penetrate tissue directly. Distance is the primary protection: dose decreases as the square of distance (2× distance = 1/4 the dose).

**Radioactive particles/fallout:** Inhalation or ingestion of airborne or surface-deposited particles. Primary route of internal contamination.

**Contaminated food/water:** Consumption of contaminated food or water in days to weeks after exposure. Iodine-131 concentrates in thyroid; cesium-137 in muscles; strontium-90 in bones.

### Initial Response and Decontamination

**Immediate (first minutes after exposure):**
- **STOP the exposure:** Move away from the source; increase distance or barriers.
- **Remove outer clothing:** Takes off ~90% of surface contamination. Double-bag clothing; do not incinerate (airborne particles).
- **Wash with water:** Soap and water removes surface contamination. Wash face, hands, hair. Use lukewarm water to avoid opening pores. Do not scrub (can drive particles deeper into skin).
- **Isolate contaminated items:** Place in leak-proof bags; do not touch bare-handed.

**First hours:**
- **Thyroid blocking (if iodine-131 exposure is suspected):** Potassium iodide (KI) 130 mg (adults) or age-adjusted dose (see below) taken within hours of exposure blocks thyroid uptake of iodine-131, reducing thyroid dose by 90%. Timing is critical; efficacy drops dramatically after 4 hours.

:::info-box
**Potassium Iodide (KI) Dosing by Age:**
- Adults and adolescents (≥12 years): 130 mg
- Children (3–11 years): 65 mg
- Children (1 month–2 years): 32.5 mg
- Infants (<1 month): 16.25 mg
- Pregnant women: 130 mg (protects mother and fetus)
- Breastfeeding women: 130 mg (limits iodine-131 in breast milk)

**Mechanism:** KI floods the thyroid with stable iodine, preventing uptake of radioactive iodine-131. Must be given before or shortly after exposure.

**Side effects:** Rash, GI upset, rarely severe allergic reaction. Benefits for thyroid cancer prevention outweigh risks in exposure scenario.

**Shelf life:** Liquid formulations degrade after ~1 year; tablets last longer if stored in cool, dry conditions. Check for discoloration or odor before use.
:::

**Continued decontamination (first 24 hours):**
- Repeat showering if contamination suspected.
- Monitor urine and feces for radioactivity (if equipment available). Encourage voiding to excrete internal contamination.
- Activate charcoal (if available) binds some isotopes and reduces GI absorption: 1–2 g every 4–6 hours for 24 hours.

</section>

## Acute Radiation Syndrome Phases

ARS progresses through overlapping phases with distinct clinical features and management priorities.

### Phase 1: Prodromal Phase (First Hours to 2 Days)

The immune system senses radiation damage; inflammatory mediators flood the system.

**Timing:** Earlier onset = higher dose.
- <1 hour: very high dose (>6 Sv) — CNS syndrome likely.
- 1–4 hours: high dose (4–6 Sv) — severe ARS, GI syndrome likely.
- 4–24 hours: moderate dose (1–4 Sv) — hematopoietic syndrome.
- >24 hours: lower dose (<1 Sv) — mild or no symptoms.

**Clinical features:**
- Nausea and vomiting (nearly universal above 1 Sv): starts within minutes to hours, lasts 24–48 hours.
- Diarrhea (often bloody).
- Abdominal pain and cramping.
- Headache.
- Fever (temperature may spike to 101–103°F, then drop).
- Fatigue and malaise.
- Erythema (skin reddening) if local burns occurred.

**Prodromal syndrome:** Combination of symptoms that correlates with dose:
- 0.5–1.0 Sv: mild nausea, possibly one episode of vomiting.
- 1.0–2.0 Sv: moderate nausea, vomiting, some diarrhea.
- 2.0–4.0 Sv: severe nausea, repeated vomiting (multiple episodes), bloody diarrhea, fever.
- 4.0–6.0 Sv: severe vomiting within 30 minutes, severe diarrhea, shock-like symptoms.

**Management during prodromal phase:**
- **Antiemetics:** Prevent dehydration from vomiting. Metoclopramide 10 mg IV/IM every 6 hours, or ondansetron 4–8 mg IV every 8 hours (if available). If not available, ginger tea, acupressure on wrist (PC6 point), or positioning help some patients.
- **Fluids:** IV crystalloid solution (0.9% saline or lactated Ringer's) 500–1000 mL/hour initially, titrated to maintain urine output. Oral fluids if patient can tolerate.
- **Supportive care:** Rest, dark quiet room, reassurance.
- **Initial workup:** CBC (complete blood count) with differential, BMP (electrolytes, kidney function), urinalysis, chest X-ray (baseline).

**Dosimetry during prodromal phase:**

Use symptom onset and severity to estimate dose:

| Dose Estimate | Symptom Onset | Vomiting Severity | Other Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0.5–1.0 Sv | >24 hours | Mild or absent | Mild nausea, no diarrhea |
| 1.0–2.0 Sv | 6–24 hours | Moderate (1–2 episodes) | Nausea, mild diarrhea |
| 2.0–4.0 Sv | 1–6 hours | Severe (3+ episodes) | Bloody diarrhea, fever, cramps |
| 4.0–6.0 Sv | <1 hour | Severe, immediate | Profound shock-like symptoms, seizures possible |
| >6.0 Sv | Minutes | Severe, immediate | Altered mental status, hemorrhage, respiratory distress |

### Phase 2: Latent Phase (1–3 Weeks)

Symptoms resolve; patient feels relatively well. Internally, bone marrow is decimated; lymphocytes plummet.

**Clinical features:**
- Apparent recovery: nausea gone, appetite returns.
- Mild fever may persist.
- Fatigue (often attributed to stress, not recognized as significant).
- Skin changes may develop: erythema darkens, potential bullae/blistering at >10 Sv local exposure.

**Deceptive period:** Patient and family may believe recovery is occurring. In reality, critical damage is progressing unseen. Opportunistic infections are incubating.

**Management:**
- Reassess CBC: lymphocyte depletion kinetics (below) refine dose estimate.
- Nutritional support: encourage eating despite anorexia.
- Psychosocial support: prepare families for possible deterioration.
- Infection prevention: hand hygiene, clean environment, safe food/water.

### Phase 3: Manifest Illness Phase (2–6 Weeks)

Bone marrow and GI damage manifests as system failures.

**Hematopoietic syndrome (dose 1–6 Sv):**
- **Anemia:** Low red blood cell count → fatigue, shortness of breath, pallor.
- **Thrombocytopenia:** Low platelet count → easy bruising, bleeding (gums, nose, GI, urinary, CNS).
- **Neutropenia:** Low white blood cell count → infections (bacterial, fungal, viral).

**Gastrointestinal syndrome (dose 4–8 Sv):**
- Severe diarrhea (often bloody and protein-rich → dehydration and albumin loss).
- Abdominal pain and cramping.
- Nausea and vomiting resume.
- Potential GI perforation → peritonitis, sepsis.

**CNS syndrome (dose >6 Sv):**
- Altered mental status: confusion, disorientation, delirium.
- Seizures.
- Loss of consciousness.
- Death within hours to days due to brain edema and cardiovascular collapse.

**Cutaneous radiation injury (localized high dose):**
- Beta burns: superficial skin burns from radioactive particles (usually not lethal).
- Gamma burns: deep burns from external gamma radiation (risk of necrosis, infection, significant scarring).

**Management of manifest illness phase:** See specific syndrome sections below.

### Phase 4: Recovery Phase (After 4–6 Weeks, if Surviving)

Bone marrow begins regenerating. Blood counts slowly recover. Infection risk gradually decreases.

**Duration:** Months to years depending on dose and residual marrow function.

**Long-term complications:**
- Secondary cancers (leukemia, solid tumors) increase lifetime risk.
- Cataracts (opacity of lens) develop months to years post-exposure.
- Chromosome aberrations and genetic effects (if reproductive cells affected).
- Fibrosis in organs exposed to high dose (lungs, liver).

## Lymphocyte Depletion Kinetics as Dose Proxy

Lymphocyte counts are exquisitely radiosensitive. Serial CBC provides real-time biological dosimetry, refining initial estimates.

### Dose Estimation from Lymphocyte Depletion

**Biological dosimetry principle:** Lymphocyte count falls proportionally to dose delivered. By comparing pre-exposure baseline (assumed normal ~1500–3000 cells/μL) to post-exposure count, dose can be estimated.

**Timeline and counts by dose:**

| Time Post-Exposure | 1–2 Sv | 2–4 Sv | 4–6 Sv | >6 Sv |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 hour | 1000–1200 | 500–1000 | 200–500 | <100 |
| 6 hours | 800–1200 | 300–800 | 100–300 | <50 |
| 24 hours | 500–1000 | 200–600 | 50–200 | Near 0 |
| 48 hours | 300–700 | 100–300 | <50 | 0 |
| 7 days | 100–300 | 50–100 | ~0 | — |

**Interpretation:**
- If 6-hour CBC shows 400 lymphocytes/μL, estimate is 2–4 Sv (hematopoietic syndrome).
- If 24-hour CBC shows 50 lymphocytes/μL, estimate is 4–6 Sv (GI syndrome likely).
- If 48-hour count is near zero, prognosis is lethal (>6 Sv, CNS syndrome).

**Serial measurements:** Repeat CBC every 6–12 hours for first 2 days, then daily. Falling counts confirm severe exposure; stabilization or rising counts suggest lower dose or measurement error.

:::warning
Lymphocyte count is the most practical real-time dosimetry in resource-limited settings without sophisticated radiation detection. However, lymphocytes are also acutely affected by stress, infection, and other conditions. Always interpret in context of clinical presentation and timeline of symptom onset.
:::

### Threshold Calculations

If baseline count is known (e.g., pre-exposure CBC on file):

**Absolute lymphocyte count reduction = (baseline count – current count) / baseline count**

Examples:
- Baseline 2000, current 500: reduction = 75% → estimated dose 2–3 Sv.
- Baseline 2000, current 50: reduction = 97.5% → estimated dose 4–6 Sv.

</section>

<section id="triage-categories">

## Triage Categories and Prognosis

In mass casualty scenarios, rapid triage determines who receives intensive care and who receives comfort care. Triage is based on dose estimation (from symptom timing and severity) and available resources.

### Traditional Triage Categories

**Green (Walking wounded):** Dose <1 Sv, mild or no ARS symptoms. Supportive care, observation. Most will recover. Route to self-care or minimal treatment area.

**Yellow (Delayed/non-critical):** Dose 1–4 Sv, moderate ARS, hematopoietic syndrome expected. Significant mortality but salvageable with intensive supportive care if resources available. Requires transfusion, antibiotics, possibly bone marrow support.

**Red (Immediate/critical):** Dose 4–6 Sv, severe ARS with hematopoietic and GI symptoms. High mortality even with intensive care. Mortality rate 50–90% depending on available care. Requires ICU-level supportive care and transfusion.

**Black (Expectant/morgue):** Dose >6 Sv, CNS syndrome, severe shock, or rapid deterioration indicating lethal dose. Death expected within hours to days. Palliative/comfort care only; may not transport to hospital. Family presence and comfort measures prioritized.

### Resource-Limited Triage Refinement

In scenarios where ICU beds and blood transfusions are severely limited:

**Scarce critical resource allocation (e.g., only 10 ICU beds available):**

Prioritize based on combination of factors:

**Likelihood of survival:** Lower doses (1–3 Sv) have better prognosis than 4–6 Sv.

**Age and baseline health:** Younger patients and those without serious comorbidities recover better. Age >60 and pre-existing organ disease reduce recovery likelihood.

**Responsiveness to treatment:** Early in the clinical course, patients who respond to supportive care (improving vital signs, stable or rising counts) should be prioritized over those deteriorating.

**Infection control:** Patients with controlled or minimal infection have better outcomes than those with sepsis or pneumonia.

**Realistic survival probability:** For 4–6 Sv dose with limited ICU, survival is ~20–30% even with full support. For >6 Sv, survival is ~0% — palliative care is appropriate.

:::info-box
**Triage Example: Mass Exposure Scenario**

100 people exposed to fallout; dose estimates distributed:
- 30 people: <1 Sv (green)
- 50 people: 1–4 Sv (yellow)
- 15 people: 4–6 Sv (red)
- 5 people: >6 Sv (black)

With 20 hospital beds available:
- Green (30): sent to shelters, self-care
- Yellow (50): 20 admitted to hospital, 30 treated as outpatients with family support
- Red (15): 0–5 admitted (if prognosis thought favorable); others managed as outpatient with comfort measures
- Black (5): comfort care, family presence, avoid transport/medical interventions

Goal: Maximize lives saved and quality of remaining life.
:::

</section>

<section id="hematopoietic-syndrome">

## Hematopoietic Syndrome Management

Hematopoietic syndrome (dose 1–6 Sv) results in inadequate blood cell production, manifesting as anemia, bleeding, and infection.

### Clinical Progression

**Week 1–2 (prodromal/latent):**
- CBC shows falling counts but patient feels relatively well.
- Lymphocytes drop first (most radiosensitive).
- Platelets and RBCs fall more slowly.

**Week 2–4 (nadir):**
- Severe anemia (Hgb <7 g/dL): fatigue, dyspnea, tachycardia.
- Thrombocytopenia (platelets <20,000/μL): spontaneous bleeding, petechiae (small red dots on skin), ecchymoses (large bruises), mucosal bleeding, potentially catastrophic CNS or GI hemorrhage.
- Severe neutropenia (WBC <500/μL): infections—bacterial (pneumonia, UTI, bacteremia), fungal (mouth/esophageal candidiasis), viral reactivation (CMV, herpes).

**Week 4–8 (recovery phase):**
- Bone marrow regeneration: counts slowly climb.
- Infection risk decreases as immune function recovers.

### Anemia Management

**RBC transfusions:**
- Goal: maintain Hgb >7 g/dL in stable patients, >8–9 g/dL in symptomatic patients.
- 1 unit packed RBCs raises Hgb ~1 g/dL in adults.
- Transfuse when symptomatic (dyspnea, severe fatigue, chest pain) or Hgb <7 g/dL.
- Risk: transfusion-related acute lung injury (TRALI), fluid overload. Transfuse slowly (over 2–4 hours).

**If transfusions unavailable:**
- Rest and minimize exertion.
- Elevate head of bed to ease dyspnea.
- Oxygen if available.
- Iron supplementation (100–200 mg/day elemental iron) supports RBC recovery.
- Folate and B12 (if deficient) support marrow function.

### Thrombocytopenia and Bleeding Management

**Platelet transfusions:**
- Goal: maintain platelets >20,000/μL in stable patients, >50,000/μL if active bleeding or planned surgery.
- 1 unit platelets (5–6 units of apheresis product) raises count ~30,000–50,000/μL.
- Transfuse when count <20,000/μL or if spontaneous bleeding occurs.
- Alloimmunization risk: repeated transfusions may cause platelets to become ineffective (refractoriness). Consider HLA-matched or crossmatched platelet transfusions if available.

**Bleeding precautions (if platelets <20,000/μL and transfusions unavailable):**
- Bed rest to reduce physical trauma.
- Soft toothbrush; avoid dental trauma.
- No NSAID or aspirin (increase bleeding).
- Stool softeners (colace 100 mg 2× daily) to prevent straining.
- Avoid IM injections; use IV or oral medications.
- Prophylactic antifibrinolytic (tranexamic acid 15 mg/kg IV 3× daily) if catastrophic bleeding risk (not standard care, experimental).

**Management of active bleeding:**
- Minor: apply pressure; elevate affected area; ice if applicable.
- GI: NPO; IV fluids; blood products; consider platelet transfusion.
- CNS (headache, confusion, seizure): MRI or CT if available; massive transfusion protocol; neurosurgical consultation.
- Pulmonary (hemoptysis): oxygen; upright positioning; blood products; consider endotracheal intubation if massive bleeding.

### Infection Management

Severe neutropenia (WBC <500/μL) creates profound immunosuppression.

**Infection prevention:**
- Strict hand hygiene (most important intervention).
- Protect from visitors and caregivers with symptoms.
- Safe food: cook thoroughly, avoid raw/unpasteurized foods and unwashed vegetables (contain bacteria and fungi).
- Clean environment; daily cleaning of room and equipment.
- Respiratory protection: N95 mask for patient in shared spaces (reduces inhalation risk); visitors wear masks near patient.

**Antimicrobial prophylaxis (if available):**
- Fluoroquinolone (e.g., levofloxacin 500 mg daily) reduces bacterial infections during severe neutropenia.
- Antifungal prophylaxis (fluconazole 200 mg daily) if anticipated neutropenia >7 days.
- TMP-SMX (trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole) daily reduces fungal/PCP risk but is less commonly used in acute scenario.

**Fever management in neutropenic patient:**
- Temperature >38.3°C (101°F) warrants urgent evaluation and empiric antibiotics (do not wait for cultures).
- Blood cultures (if possible), urinalysis, chest X-ray.
- Empiric broad-spectrum antibiotics: combination such as ceftazidime (2 g IV Q8H) + gentamicin (5 mg/kg IV daily) or fluoroquinolone if cephalosporins unavailable.
- Anti-fungal coverage if fever persists >48 hours despite antibiotics: add fluconazole 400 mg IV daily.
- Remove indwelling catheters (central lines, foley) if infected.

**Commonly encountered infections:**
- **Oral candidiasis (thrush):** White patches in mouth/throat. Treat with nystatin rinse 100,000 units 4–5× daily or oral fluconazole 100 mg daily.
- **Bacterial pneumonia:** Cough, fever, infiltrate on chest X-ray. Treat with empiric broad-spectrum antibiotics covering respiratory pathogens.
- **Bacteremia/sepsis:** Fever, hypotension, altered mental status. Life-threatening; requires aggressive IV fluid resuscitation, antibiotics, and supportive care.

### Hematologic Growth Factors

**G-CSF (Granulocyte Colony-Stimulating Factor):**
- Stimulates neutrophil production.
- Dose: 5 μg/kg IV or SC daily; escalate up to 10–20 μg/kg if inadequate response.
- If available (costly): can accelerate neutrophil recovery by 3–5 days.
- Not standard in resource-limited scenarios due to cost.

**GM-CSF (Granulocyte-Macrophage CSF):** Similar to G-CSF; promotes neutrophil and macrophage recovery. If available, use same dosing as G-CSF.

**Thrombopoietin (TPO) agonists:** Stimulate platelet production. Expensive; not standard in radiation scenarios.

**In resource-limited settings:** Growth factors often unavailable. Focus on supportive care, infection prevention, and transfusions.

## Gastrointestinal Syndrome Management

GI syndrome (dose 4–8 Sv) results from destruction of the intestinal lining, leading to severe diarrhea, electrolyte loss, malnutrition, and opportunistic infection.

### Pathophysiology

High-dose radiation destroys epithelial cells lining the GI tract. The intestinal barrier breaks down; bacteria translocate into the bloodstream. Villous atrophy prevents nutrient absorption. Massive fluid and protein loss occurs.

### Fluid and Electrolyte Management

**Severity:** Diarrhea may be 10–30+ liters per day in severe cases—comparable to cholera. Dehydration and electrolyte derangement are rapid.

**IV rehydration:**
- **Isotonic crystalloid:** 0.9% saline or lactated Ringer's.
- **Initial bolus:** 1–2 liters over 1–2 hours if hypotensive.
- **Maintenance + replacement:** Estimate ongoing losses (500 mL per diarrheal stool, plus baseline urine and insensible losses). Typically 2–3 liters/hour initially, titrated based on urine output (goal 0.5–1 mL/kg/hour) and vital signs.
- **Potassium supplementation:** Add 20 mEq/liter to IV fluids once urine output documented (avoid hyperkalemia if renal function compromised).

**Oral rehydration (if GI tolerates):**
- WHO oral rehydration solution (ORS): 1 liter water + 2.6 g NaCl + 1.5 g KCl + 20 g glucose (or 75 mL 5% dextrose).
- Alternative: broth, diluted juice, rice water.
- Avoid hyperosmolar solutions (apple juice, sports drinks) which worsen diarrhea.

**Monitoring:**
- Daily weights (1 kg = ~1 liter fluid loss or gain).
- Urine output and electrolytes.
- Vital signs: tachycardia and hypotension indicate ongoing fluid deficit.

### Anti-Diarrheal Medications

**Antiperistaltic agents (opioids):**
- Loperamide (Imodium) 4 mg initial, then 2 mg after each stool (max 16 mg/day). Slows GI transit; reduces fluid loss.
- Morphine: analgesic effect; also reduces GI motility.
- Caution: risks bacterial translocation by reducing GI clearance; use cautiously.

**If opioids unavailable:** Bismuth subsalicylate (Pepto-Bismol), tannin-containing preparations (black tea with honey), or plain supportive care.

### Nutritional Support

**Early challenge:** Anorexia, severe diarrhea, and inability to absorb oral intake.

**Approach:**
- NPO (nothing by mouth) during acute phase if severe diarrhea/vomiting.
- Parenteral nutrition (PN): IV nutrition bypassing the GI tract.
  - If PN available: initiate early to sustain nutrition and minimize wasting.
  - Formulation: dextrose, amino acids, lipids, electrolytes, vitamins.
  - Requires central line; risk of infection.
- If PN unavailable: enteral nutrition (feeds through nasogastric tube) or elemental diet (small molecular weight, easier absorption) if any GI tolerance.
- As tolerance improves (usually week 2–3): transition to soft, bland diet (rice, broth, bananas, crackers).
- Supplemental vitamins: B vitamins support recovery; vitamin A and C are antioxidants.

### GI Infection Prevention

**Bacterial translocation:** Breakdown of the GI barrier allows bacteria to enter bloodstream.

**Prevention strategies:**
- Selective decontamination of the digestive tract (SDD): non-absorbable antibiotics (polymyxin E, neomycin, amphotericin B) to suppress GI flora. Reduces but doesn't eliminate translocation. If available and tolerated: give via nasogastric tube.
- Proton pump inhibitor (omeprazole 20 mg 2× daily) or H2-blocker (famotidine 20 mg 2× daily) to reduce gastric acid and promote healing.
- Prophylactic systemic antibiotics: fluoroquinolone ± clindamycin covers common GI bacteria.

**Management of GI infection:**
- Fever + diarrhea suggests bacterial translocation/sepsis.
- Blood cultures if possible.
- Empiric broad-spectrum antibiotics covering GI gram-negatives (see Hematopoietic Syndrome section).

### GI Perforation

Risk increases in second and third week as necrotic tissue sloughs.

**Signs:** Sudden severe abdominal pain, peritoneal signs (rigidity, guarding, rebound), fever, sepsis.

**Management:**
- NPO; nasogastric suction.
- IV fluids and antibiotics.
- Surgical consultation (if surgical capability available): repair of perforation may be attempted if patient is stable enough; mortality is high even with surgery in context of severe ARS.
- If surgery not available: supportive/palliative care with focus on comfort.

<section id="cns-syndrome">

## CNS Syndrome

CNS (central nervous system) syndrome occurs at very high doses (>6 Sv) and is characterized by cerebral edema, cardiovascular collapse, and death within hours to days. There is no effective treatment; management is supportive and palliative.

### Clinical Features

**Prodromal phase (minutes to hours):**
- Extreme nausea/vomiting.
- Diarrhea, sometimes bloody.
- Disorientation or restlessness.
- Headache.

**Manifest illness phase (hours to days):**
- Altered consciousness: confusion, delirium, obtundation.
- Seizures (often multiple).
- Loss of pupillary light reflex; dilated pupils.
- Respiratory depression or irregular breathing (Cheyne-Stokes pattern).
- Hypotension refractory to fluids.
- Bradycardia (slow heart rate).
- Death typically within 24–48 hours of symptom onset.

### Management (Supportive/Palliative)

**Goals:** Comfort, dignity, family presence.

**Symptom management:**
- Anticonvulsants: phenytoin 500 mg IV load, then 100 mg IV Q6H for seizure prophylaxis; lorazepam 2–4 mg IV for acute seizures.
- Sedation: morphine or benzodiazepines for comfort if patient is distressed.
- Oxygen: ease dyspnea (though supplemental oxygen unlikely to improve outcome).
- Head of bed elevated 30° to ease cerebral edema.
- Catheterization to monitor urine output and ease care.

**Realistic counseling to family:**
"Your loved one has received a very high dose of radiation. The brain is being affected. He is becoming unresponsive and experiencing seizures. Despite everything we do, his body is shutting down. Our focus now is on keeping him comfortable, not on trying to reverse what's happening. We don't expect him to recover from this."

**Time to death:** Typically 24–48 hours from symptom onset. Organize family presence, religious rites, farewells.

<section id="cutaneous-radiation-injury">

## Cutaneous Radiation Injury (Radiation Burns)

Localized high radiation doses cause thermal-like burns to skin and underlying tissue.

### Dose-Effect Relationships for Skin

**<6 Sv (local dose):** Erythema (redness); may progress to desquamation (skin peeling). Usually heals within 2–3 weeks without scars.

**6–10 Sv:** Blistering (bullae); ulceration. Significant pain. Heals with scars; may require skin grafting.

**10–20 Sv:** Necrosis of skin and underlying tissue (fat, muscle, bone). Severe pain. Requires surgical debridement and reconstruction.

**>20 Sv:** Full-thickness necrosis; tissue death extends deep. Risk of amputation.

### Management of Radiation Burns

**Early care (hours after exposure):**
- Clean with soap and water.
- Remove contaminated clothing.
- Pain management: analgesics (especially if high-dose local burns).
- Elevation to reduce swelling.

**Ongoing care:**

**For mild erythema/desquamation:**
- Topical antibiotic ointment (bacitracin, silver sulfadiazine) to prevent infection.
- Loose, clean dressings; change daily or as soiled.
- Maintain hygiene; avoid further trauma.
- Expected to heal within 2–3 weeks.

**For significant blistering/ulceration:**
- Keep clean and uninfected: daily wound washing, topical antibiotics.
- Debride dead tissue gently as it separates.
- If available: specialized burn dressings (silver-impregnated, hydrogel).
- Pain control with opioids.
- Monitor for infection (wound warming, increasing drainage, fever).
- Once clean: consider skin grafting if available and patient stable enough to undergo surgery.

**For deep necrosis:**
- Surgical debridement of dead tissue (autolytic debridement—allowing natural separation—is slower but may be only option without surgical capability).
- Prevent infection: keep dry and covered.
- Significant reconstructive surgery likely needed; may involve amputation if limb viability is lost.

:::warning
Radiation burns are painful and become infected easily. In resource-limited settings without surgery, wound care focus is on infection prevention and pain management. Expectations for recovery may need to be recalibrated toward functional loss and adaptive technology (prosthetics, assistive devices).
:::

### Psychological Impact of Visible Scarring

Visible disfiguring scars have profound psychological impact. Counseling and supportive care are important, especially for young patients. Community education to reduce stigma is beneficial.

<section id="combined-injury">

## Combined Injury Management

Mass casualty scenarios often involve simultaneous radiation exposure, blast trauma, thermal burns, and crush injuries.

### Prioritization Challenges

**Traditional trauma triage** (salvage highest acuity first) conflicts with **radiation triage** (salvage survivable doses first).

In high-dose exposure scenarios:
- A patient with severe burns plus 6 Sv radiation exposure has poor prognosis from radiation alone; extensive burn surgery is futile.
- Redirect resources to moderate-dose exposure patients without extensive trauma.

### Management Principles

**Injury assessment:**
- Radiation dose estimate (as above).
- Trauma severity (ABCDE assessment, vital signs, CT if available).
- Burn severity (% body surface area, depth).

**Treatment plan:**
- Address life threats first (airway, bleeding, major burns).
- If patient salvageable from trauma (e.g., controlled bleeding, stable vital signs) AND radiation dose is survivable (1–4 Sv), proceed with trauma care.
- If radiation dose is lethal (>6 Sv) or patient has overwhelming trauma (e.g., 80% burns, massive hemorrhage), transition to comfort care regardless of trauma severity.

**Example scenarios:**

**Scenario 1:** 35-year-old with estimated 2 Sv exposure (moderate ARS expected) plus crush injury to leg requiring amputation.
- Prognosis from radiation: ~70% survival with supportive care.
- Prognosis from trauma: amputation likely, but compatible with life.
- **Plan:** Stabilize, perform amputation, provide ARS supportive care. Reasonable prognosis.

**Scenario 2:** 72-year-old with estimated 8 Sv exposure (lethal CNS syndrome) plus 60% burns.
- Prognosis from radiation: <1% survival.
- Burn care is futile in context of lethal radiation dose.
- **Plan:** Comfort care, pain relief, family presence. Do not perform burn surgery or aggressive resuscitation.

</section>

<section id="internal-contamination">

## Internal Contamination Treatment

Internal contamination (ingestion, inhalation, or absorption of radioactive material) delivers continued internal dose. Reduction of uptake and acceleration of excretion minimize dose.

### Isotope-Specific Treatments

**Iodine-131 (radioactive iodine):**
- **Potassium Iodide (KI):** See earlier section. Blocks thyroid uptake. Dose: 130 mg adults, repeated daily for 10 days if exposure is ongoing.
- **Perchlorate:** Blocks thyroid uptake; alternative to KI. Dose: 750 mg BID for 10 days (if available).

**Cesium-137 (radioactive cesium):**
- **Prussian Blue:** Binds cesium in GI tract, enhances fecal excretion. Reduces biologic half-life from ~100 days to ~30 days.
- Dose: 1 g (10 mL of 100 mg/mL suspension) PO 3× daily for 30 days.
- Mechanism: Not absorbed; passes through GI tract; traps cesium in lumen for excretion in stool.
- If Prussian Blue unavailable: No effective alternative; supportive care and time are only options.

**Strontium-90 (radioactive strontium):**
- **Barium sulfate and alginates:** Non-absorbable agents that compete with strontium for intestinal absorption; reduce uptake.
- Dose: limited standardization; empirically 500 mg–1 g BID for 1–3 weeks.
- **Calcium supplementation:** High calcium intake competes with strontium for bone uptake; may reduce bone deposition. Dose: 1–2 g elemental calcium daily during exposure period.

**Plutonium, americium, curium (transuranics):**
- **DTPA (diethylenetriaminepentacetic acid):** Chelates transuranics, accelerates urinary excretion.
- IV or inhaled formulations. Dose varies; typical IV dose: 1000 mg daily in 3 divided doses.
- Complex logistics; requires specialist consultation.
- May not be available in resource-limited setting.

**If Prussian Blue and DTPA unavailable:**
- Activated charcoal (1–2 g QID) binds some isotopes; limited efficacy.
- Encourage voiding and bowel evacuation to reduce absorptive time.
- Oral fluid intake to dilute concentrated radioactivity.

### Activated Charcoal vs. Other Adsorbents

**Activated charcoal:**
- Binds some isotopes and organic compounds.
- Dose: 1–2 g every 4–6 hours.
- May reduce GI absorption if given immediately after contamination.
- Not superior to Prussian Blue for specific isotope treatment; use as general GI decontamination measure.

**Bentonite/kaolin:** Alternative clay-based absorbers. Variable efficacy; limited data in radiation contamination.

</section>

## Long-Term Monitoring and Cancer Screening

Radiation survivors face increased lifetime cancer risk. Survivors of high-dose whole-body exposure (>1 Sv) require ongoing monitoring.

### Cancer Risk by Dose and Age

**Dose-risk relationship:** Risk increases with dose; younger age at exposure correlates with higher lifetime risk.

**Approximate excess cancer risk:**
- 1–2 Sv: ~1–5% lifetime excess risk above background.
- 2–4 Sv: ~5–15% lifetime excess risk.
- 4–6 Sv: Higher risk, but most don't survive to develop cancer.

**Most common cancers post-exposure:**
- Leukemia (acute myeloid, acute lymphocytic): appears 2–10 years post-exposure; ~10–15% of cancer deaths.
- Solid cancers (breast, lung, thyroid, GI): appear 10–40 years post-exposure; ~85% of radiation-related cancer deaths.

### Screening and Surveillance

**Thyroid (especially if iodine-131 exposure):**
- Baseline thyroid ultrasound and TSH level.
- Annual palpation and TSH; thyroid ultrasound every 2–5 years.
- Monitor for hypothyroidism (common after radiation); replacement with levothyroxine if needed.

**Leukemia:**
- CBC annually for first 10 years.
- No screening tool prevents leukemia; monitor for symptoms (easy bruising, infections, fatigue).

**Solid cancers:**
- Encourage smoking cessation (multiplicative risk of smoking + radiation).
- Regular screening appropriate for age: mammography, colonoscopy, lung cancer screening (if applicable).
- Early reporting of new symptoms.

**Eye (cataracts):**
- Baseline ophthalmology exam.
- Annual examination post-exposure; cataract surgery if vision threatened.

### Genetic Counseling

Concerns about heritable effects (passing mutations to offspring) are common.

**Reassurance:** Radiation doses causing significant somatic cancer risk do not reliably cause heritable genetic damage—the reproductive tissues are more resistant than rapidly dividing somatic tissues. Risk of birth defects in offspring is <1% above background for doses <1 Sv.

However, counseling should be offered; genetic testing may be helpful.

</section>

<section id="palliative-radiation-casualties">

## Palliative Care for Radiation Casualties

For patients with lethal-dose exposures (>6 Sv CNS syndrome, or devastating combined injuries incompatible with survival), shift focus to comfort and dignity.

**Goals of care conversation:**
"Based on the radiation dose your loved one received, we do not expect them to survive. Our focus is on keeping them comfortable and allowing family time together. We will not pursue aggressive interventions like resuscitation, breathing machines, or intensive testing. Instead, we'll manage pain, keep them calm, and support your family."

**Comfort measures:**
- Pain management: morphine as needed for pain and dyspnea.
- Anxiolytics: benzodiazepines for anxiety and restlessness.
- Anticonvulsants if seizure risk.
- Positioning and hygiene for comfort.
- Family presence encouraged; create environment for farewells.

**See Palliative & End-of-Life Care guide for detailed symptom management and family support approaches.**

</section>

<section id="mass-casualty-triage">

## Mass Casualty Triage Frameworks

Large-scale radiation events (nuclear accident, attack) involve hundreds to thousands of exposed people with varying doses. Triage must be rapid and rational.

### Simple Field Triage

**Assess by symptom timing (first hour post-exposure):**

1. **Asymptomatic or mild nausea:** Likely <1 Sv. Assess later; most can self-manage.
2. **Moderate nausea, vomiting within 1 hour:** Likely 1–2 Sv. Transport to medical facility.
3. **Severe nausea/vomiting within 30 minutes; diarrhea:** Likely 2–4 Sv. Urgent facility transport.
4. **Extreme nausea/vomiting <10 minutes; shock-like symptoms; disorientation:** Likely >4 Sv. Comfort care; transport if family wishes, otherwise manage in place.

### Hospital Triage by Available Resources

**If resources ABUNDANT (ICU beds, blood products, antibiotics plentiful):**
- Green/Yellow/Red patients admitted; best supportive care offered.
- Black patients offered comfort care (can occur at hospital or home).

**If resources SEVERELY LIMITED (few ICU beds, limited transfusions, antibiotics scarce):**
- Green: self-care, outpatient monitoring.
- Yellow: limited inpatient care if prognosis favorable (lower dose, younger, no comorbidities); others outpatient with family support.
- Red (4–6 Sv): palliative care; minimal hospitalization unless family and patient strongly prefer.
- Black: definitely palliative; no hospitalization.

### Communication and Documentation

**Clear communication** with patients, families, and medical team prevents confusion and reduces conflict.

**Example documentation:**

*Mass exposure event: estimated 150 people exposed.*

*Triage Decisions:*
- *Patient A (age 42, estimated 2 Sv): Moderate dose, survivable with care. Admitted for hematopoietic ARS supportive care—transfusions, antibiotics, ICU monitoring.*
- *Patient B (age 68, estimated 5 Sv, pre-existing COPD): Severe dose, poor expected prognosis given age and comorbidity. Offered outpatient comfort care with family support; declining hospitalization.*
- *Patient C (age 55, estimated 8 Sv): Lethal dose. Discussed CNS syndrome prognosis with family; offered comfort measures at home; called hospice for support.*

**Accountability:** Document the reasoning, prognostic estimates, and conversations so decisions are transparent and defensible.

</section>

## Summary

Acute radiation syndrome clinical management in resource-limited environments requires rapid dose estimation from symptom timing and lymphocyte kinetics, practical triage decisions balancing prognosis with available resources, and aggressive supportive care for salvageable patients. Hematopoietic syndrome is managed with transfusions, antibiotics, and infection prevention. Gastrointestinal syndrome requires massive fluid replacement and nutritional support. CNS syndrome is managed palliatively. Realistic communication with patients and families about lethal exposures enables dignity and comfort in the final hours. Long-term cancer screening of survivors is important. When overwhelming numbers of severely exposed people exceed available resources, rational triage frameworks allocate limited resources to those with the best chance of survival, while ensuring comfort and respect for those with lethal exposures.
