## Overview

Nutrition is survival. In a post-collapse or low-resource environment, the difference between health and disease often comes down to consistent access to a variety of foods containing adequate calories, protein, vitamins, and minerals. Deficiency diseases—once thought defeated by modern supply chains—can emerge rapidly when populations lose access to diverse food sources.

This guide equips you with:

- Caloric and macro/micronutrient requirements by age, sex, and activity level
- Recognition and prevention of the six major deficiency diseases
- Practical food sources for every key nutrient
- Strategies for community nutrition planning and detection of early deficiency signs

Canonical routing note: use `nutritional-planning-deficiency-prevention` for calorie planning, macro/micronutrient tables, and mixed-basis comparisons. Use `pharmacy-compounding` for the shared ORS recipe wording.

Deficiency diseases develop over weeks to months of inadequate intake. Early recognition and intervention—long before clinical symptoms emerge—is crucial.

## Macronutrients: Protein, Fat, Carbohydrates & Fiber

### Protein

Adults require **0.8 g per kg of body weight per day** (0.36 g/lb). During illness, heavy labor, or growth (childhood/pregnancy/lactation), increase to **1.2–2.0 g/kg/day**.

**Sources in survival settings:**
- Legumes (dried beans, lentils, peas): 15–25 g protein per cooked cup; also rich in fiber and iron
- Whole grains (wheat, oats, barley): 10–15 g protein per cooked cup; combine with legumes for complete amino acid profile
- Eggs: 6–8 g per egg; excellent complete protein and rich in micronutrients
- Fish (fresh or preserved): 20–30 g per 100 g; rich in omega-3 fats and micronutrients
- Meat (domestic or wild): 20–30 g per 100 g; source of iron, B vitamins, and micronutrients
- Insects (crickets, locusts, mealworms): 10–20 g per 100 g; underutilized protein source
- Nuts and seeds: 5–10 g per ounce; also rich in healthy fats and minerals

:::tip
Combine grains with legumes in a single meal to create a complete protein (all 9 essential amino acids). Examples: rice + beans, corn + beans, wheat bread + lentil soup. This is crucial when animal protein is limited.
:::

### Fat

Adults require **20–35% of total calories from fat**. In survival settings with limited resources, achieving adequate fat becomes critical for hormone production, vitamin absorption, and satiety.

**Practical sources:**
- Cooking oils from pressed seeds (flax, sunflower, coconut): 120 kcal per tablespoon; store carefully (dark, cool conditions) to prevent oxidation
- Animal fats (lard, tallow, butter, ghee): 115–120 kcal per tablespoon; stable when stored cool; rendered animal fat can last months
- Nuts and seeds: 160–200 kcal per ounce; also provide protein and micronutrients
- Fatty fish: 200–350 kcal per 100 g; excellent source of omega-3 fats (anti-inflammatory)
- Avocados (in appropriate climates): 240 kcal per fruit; excellent source of heart-healthy monounsaturated fat
- Egg yolks: 55 kcal and 5 g fat per egg; also rich in choline and lutein

### Carbohydrates

Adults require **45–65% of total calories from carbohydrate**. In survival settings, carbs are often the easiest macronutrient to produce consistently via grains and root vegetables.

**Practical sources:**
- Staple grains (wheat, rice, oats, barley): 130–150 kcal per cooked cup; store as whole grain (longer shelf life) and mill/cook as needed
- Starchy roots (potatoes, sweet potatoes, cassava, taro): 80–120 kcal per 100 g raw; calorie-dense when grown in volume
- Legumes: 120–140 kcal per cooked cup; also significant protein and fiber
- Honey or tree sap (if available): 60 kcal per tablespoon; concentrated energy and long shelf life

### Fiber

Adults require **25–35 g dietary fiber per day**. Adequate fiber is critical for gut health, regular digestion, and prevention of constipation—especially important in survival settings where medical care is limited.

**Practical sources:**
- Whole grains: 3–8 g per cooked cup (higher in grain than in refined grain products)
- Legumes: 6–8 g per cooked cup
- Vegetables (especially leafy greens, root vegetables with skin): 2–4 g per cooked cup
- Fruits: 2–4 g per fruit (higher with skin intact)
- Seeds: 2–3 g per tablespoon

:::info-box
Fiber intake must increase gradually as the digestive system adapts. Sudden large increases cause bloating and gas. If transitioning a community from refined grains to whole grains, phase in over 2–4 weeks.
:::

## Essential Vitamins & Food Sources

### Vitamin A (Retinol and Carotenoids)

**Daily requirement:** 700–900 μg for adults (higher during pregnancy/lactation)

**Food sources:**
- Orange/red vegetables (carrots, sweet potatoes, pumpkin): 5000–12000 μg per 100 g raw
- Dark leafy greens (kale, spinach, collards): 5000–8000 μg per 100 g raw
- Animal liver (beef, chicken, pork): 5000–30000 μg per 100 g; excellent absorption (retinol form)
- Fish (especially fatty fish like mackerel): 1000–4000 μg per 100 g
- Eggs: 500 μg per large egg

**Deficiency disease:** See Vitamin A Deficiency section below.

### B Vitamins (B1, B2, B3, B5, B6, B12, Folate)

**Daily requirements:** Vary by specific B vitamin; generally 1.1–2.4 mg per day (adults)

**Food sources:**
- Whole grains: 0.3–0.6 mg B1 per cooked cup; 0.2–0.5 mg B2; 3–5 mg niacin
- Legumes: 0.3–0.5 mg B1; 0.1–0.2 mg B2; 1–2 mg niacin; 150–250 μg folate
- Meat/poultry: 0.5–1.0 mg B1; 0.2–0.3 mg B2; 5–8 mg niacin; 2–10 μg B12
- Liver: 0.3–0.5 mg B1; 3–4 mg B2; 10–15 mg niacin; 50–100 μg B12; 500–1000 μg folate
- Leafy greens (folate source): 150–300 μg per cooked cup
- Fish (especially mackerel, salmon): 0.2 mg B1; 0.3 mg B2; 6–10 mg niacin; 1–5 μg B12

**Deficiency diseases:** See Beriberi, Pellagra sections below.

### Vitamin C (Ascorbic Acid)

**Daily requirement:** 75–90 mg for adults (increases to 120–130 mg during lactation)

**Food sources:**
- Citrus fruits (if available): 30–50 mg per fruit or 100 g juice
- Berries: 30–150 mg per 100 g (rose hips are extremely high: 1000+ mg per 100 g)
- Leafy greens (raw): 20–50 mg per 100 g (decreases with cooking: 5–15 mg)
- Cruciferous vegetables (cabbage, broccoli): 30–90 mg per 100 g raw; 15–30 mg cooked
- Root vegetables with skin (potatoes): 10–20 mg per 100 g; higher in sprouted potatoes
- Fermented vegetables (sauerkraut, kimchi): retain 50% of original vitamin C content

**Deficiency disease:** See Scurvy section below.

:::warning
Vitamin C is rapidly destroyed by heat, light, and oxidation. To preserve it: ferment vegetables, store cut produce in water under cool conditions, eat some raw vegetables daily, or supplement with rose hips/wild berries. Boiling water leaches vitamin C; steaming preserves 30–50% more.
:::

### Vitamin D

**Daily requirement:** 600–800 IU (15–20 μg) for adults

**Food sources:**
- Fatty fish (mackerel, salmon, herring): 200–800 IU per 100 g
- Fish liver oil (if available): 4000–10000 IU per tablespoon
- Egg yolks: 20–25 IU per large egg
- Fortified dairy (in pre-collapse settings): 100 IU per cup

**Primary synthesis:** Skin exposure to sunlight (UVB rays) generates vitamin D; 20–30 minutes of midday sun 3× per week is typically sufficient for adults in moderate climates. In winter or high latitudes, synthesis is greatly reduced.

**Deficiency disease:** See Rickets section below.

### Vitamin E

**Daily requirement:** 15 mg alpha-tocopherol for adults

**Food sources:**
- Nuts and seeds: 5–15 mg per ounce
- Plant oils (sunflower, safflower): 6–8 mg per tablespoon
- Whole grains: 1–3 mg per cooked cup
- Leafy greens: 2–5 mg per 100 g raw
- Avocados: 2 mg per fruit

### Vitamin K

**Daily requirement:** 90–120 μg for adults

**Food sources:**
- Leafy greens (kale, spinach, collards): 200–1000 μg per 100 g raw
- Cruciferous vegetables (broccoli, cabbage): 100–200 μg per 100 g
- Fermented vegetables (sauerkraut): 70–150 μg per 100 g
- Legumes: 20–50 μg per cooked cup

## Essential Minerals & Food Sources

### Iron

**Daily requirement:** 8 mg for adult men; 18 mg for women 19–50 years; 8 mg for women 51+

**Comparison note:** Food tables in this guide and in `nutritional-planning-deficiency-prevention` use mixed bases such as `per 100 g`, `per cooked cup`, and `per serving`. Compare like with like before treating range differences as meaningful disagreement.

**Food sources:**
- Red meat: 2–4 mg per 100 g (heme iron—highly absorbable, 15–35%)
- Poultry: 1–2 mg per 100 g (heme iron)
- Fish: 1–2 mg per 100 g (heme iron)
- Legumes (cooked): 2–6 mg per cooked cup (non-heme iron—less absorbable, 2–20%; improve absorption by pairing with vitamin C)
- Whole grains: 2–3 mg per cooked cup (non-heme iron)
- Leafy greens (raw spinach): 2–3 mg per 100 g (non-heme iron; oxalates reduce absorption)
- Seeds (pumpkin, sunflower): 3–5 mg per ounce (non-heme iron)

**Absorption tip:** Pair non-heme iron sources (grains, legumes, vegetables) with vitamin C sources (citrus, berries, fermented vegetables) in the same meal to increase bioavailability by 3–4×.

**Deficiency disease:** See Anemia section below.

### Calcium

**Daily requirement:** 1000–1200 mg for adults

**Comparison note:** Calcium values across the nutrition guides are meant as practical food-table ranges, not exact lab constants. Check whether an entry is listed per `100 g`, per `cooked cup`, or per `serving` before comparing values across guides.

**Food sources:**
- Dairy products (if available): 200–300 mg per serving
- Leafy greens (especially collards, kale, bok choy): 100–300 mg per cooked cup (lower in spinach and chard due to oxalates)
- Legumes: 50–150 mg per cooked cup
- Nuts and seeds (especially sesame): 100–200 mg per ounce
- Small fish with edible bones (sardines, anchovies): 200–400 mg per 100 g
- Fortified grains (pre-collapse): 100–300 mg per serving

:::info-box
In survival settings, prioritize dark leafy greens for calcium. Combine with vitamin D exposure (sunlight) and adequate vitamin K to support bone health.
:::

### Iodine

**Daily requirement:** 150 μg for adults

**Food sources:**
- Seaweed/kelp (dried): 100–1000+ μg per gram (highly variable by species and water source)
- Fish and seafood: 50–150 μg per 100 g
- Eggs: 20–30 μg per large egg
- Dairy (if available): 40–50 μg per cup
- Salt fish (traditional preparations): 30–100 μg per 100 g

**Critical note:** Iodine deficiency causes goiter and cretinism (irreversible brain damage in infants/children). In areas far from the ocean, iodine is scarce in soil and food. See Goiter section below.

### Zinc

**Daily requirement:** 8–11 mg for adults

**Food sources:**
- Meat, poultry, fish: 2–6 mg per 100 g (highest in oysters/shellfish: 10–50 mg per 100 g)
- Legumes: 1–2 mg per cooked cup (less bioavailable due to phytates)
- Nuts and seeds: 1–3 mg per ounce
- Whole grains: 1–2 mg per cooked cup
- Dairy: 0.5–1.0 mg per serving

:::warning
Zinc bioavailability is reduced by phytates (found in whole grains and legumes) and increased by pairing with animal protein. Communities relying heavily on plant-based diets must increase total zinc intake to compensate for lower absorption.
:::

### Magnesium

**Daily requirement:** 310–420 mg for adults

**Food sources:**
- Leafy greens: 80–160 mg per cooked cup
- Nuts and seeds: 150–250 mg per ounce
- Whole grains: 80–150 mg per cooked cup
- Legumes: 40–80 mg per cooked cup
- Leafy vegetables (spinach particularly high): 150 mg per cooked cup

### Potassium

**Daily requirement:** 2600–3400 mg for adults

**Food sources:**
- Potatoes (with skin): 500–700 mg per medium potato
- Sweet potatoes: 400–600 mg per medium potato
- Legumes: 300–500 mg per cooked cup
- Leafy greens: 300–400 mg per cooked cup
- Bananas (if available): 400–450 mg per fruit
- Root vegetables (beets, carrots): 200–400 mg per 100 g

## Deficiency Diseases: Recognition, Prevention & Treatment

### Scurvy (Vitamin C Deficiency)

**Onset:** 1–3 months of inadequate vitamin C intake

**Timeline note:** The companion nutrition-planning guide frames severe scurvy in weeks rather than months. These are the same general window; the exact timing depends on starting vitamin C stores and how close intake is to zero.

**Early symptoms:**
- Fatigue and weakness
- Irritability and mood changes
- Joint and muscle aches
- Poor wound healing

**Advanced symptoms:**
- Bleeding gums and tooth loss
- Rash (especially on extremities)
- Anemia-like symptoms (pale, shortness of breath)
- Opening of old wounds or surgical sites
- Severe edema (swelling)

**Prevention:**
- Daily vitamin C intake of 75–90 mg (adult requirement)
- Include fresh or fermented vegetables and fruits
- Preserve rose hips, berries, or citrus as dried products or ferments
- When fresh produce is unavailable, fermented vegetables retain 50%+ of original vitamin C

**Treatment:**
- Immediate increase of vitamin C-rich foods; symptoms begin reversing within 1 week
- Prioritize leafy greens, fermented vegetables, wild berries, rose hips
- If no fresh foods available, prepare reconstituted dried fruit or fermented extracts
- Severe cases may benefit from animal liver (also rich in vitamin A) and fish

:::warning
Scurvy is rapidly fatal if untreated in advanced stages. A single individual without access to vitamin C will show severe symptoms within 3 months and can deteriorate to death within 6 months if untreated. In community planning, ensure diverse preserved and fresh sources are available year-round.
:::

### Beriberi (Thiamine/Vitamin B1 Deficiency)

**Onset:** 2–3 weeks of inadequate B1 intake (acute); or 2–3 months (chronic)

**Early symptoms:**
- Fatigue, weakness, irritability
- Headaches and poor concentration
- Pins and needles (paresthesia) in extremities
- Constipation or diarrhea

**Advanced symptoms:**
- Wet beriberi: Edema (swelling), heart problems, high heart rate, shortness of breath
- Dry beriberi: Severe muscle weakness, foot drop, difficulty walking, loss of reflexes
- Wernicke encephalopathy: Confusion, loss of memory, vision problems (medical emergency)

**Prevention:**
- Daily B1 intake of 1.1–1.2 mg (adult requirement)
- Whole grains are excellent sources (0.3–0.6 mg per cooked cup)
- Include legumes, seeds, nuts, meat/poultry, and fish
- Critically important if diet relies heavily on polished/refined grains (white rice, white flour) which have B1 removed during processing

**Treatment:**
- Prioritize whole grain consumption (wheat, oats, barley, brown rice)
- Include legumes, animal proteins, and seeds
- Response is rapid: symptoms begin improving within 3–5 days of adequate intake
- Severe cases benefit from liver (high in B1 and other B vitamins)

:::warning
Beriberi is especially dangerous in populations relying on polished white rice without dietary diversity. In post-collapse settings transitioning to subsistence agriculture, ensure whole grains are prioritized, not polished grains. The disease can progress from fatigue to severe heart failure within weeks if untreated.
:::

### Pellagra (Niacin/Vitamin B3 Deficiency)

**Onset:** 2–4 months of inadequate niacin intake

**The "Four Ds" classic symptoms:**
- **Dermatitis:** Scaly, itchy rash on sun-exposed areas (hands, face, neck)
- **Diarrhea:** Persistent diarrhea and poor nutrient absorption
- **Dementia:** Mental confusion, memory loss, mood changes, depression
- **Death:** If advanced and untreated

**Additional symptoms:**
- Glossitis (swollen tongue), mouth sores
- Burning sensation in mouth and hands
- Weakness, anemia
- Poor wound healing

**Prevention:**
- Daily niacin intake of 14–16 mg (adult requirement)
- Whole grains provide 3–5 mg per cooked cup
- Legumes: 1–2 mg per cooked cup
- Meat/poultry: 5–8 mg per 100 g
- Fish: 6–10 mg per 100 g
- Note: Niacin in grains is partly bound (unavailable); combine with legumes and diverse protein sources

**Treatment:**
- Prioritize diverse protein sources: meat, fish, legumes, whole grains
- Liver is particularly high in niacin (10–15 mg per 100 g)
- Response is slow (2–4 weeks for full resolution)
- Severe dermatitis may permanently scar; early intervention is critical

:::tip
Pellagra was once endemic in populations subsisting primarily on polished corn without dietary diversity. The key prevention is dietary diversity—never rely on a single grain source. Ensure legumes and animal protein are always part of the food plan.
:::

### Anemia (Iron Deficiency)

**Onset:** 3–6 months of inadequate iron intake or blood loss

**Early symptoms:**
- Fatigue, weakness, shortness of breath with activity
- Pale skin and mucous membranes
- Frequent infections (iron is needed for immune function)
- Poor concentration, dizziness

**Advanced symptoms:**
- Severe shortness of breath at rest
- Rapid or irregular heartbeat
- Swollen ankles and feet (edema)
- Difficulty performing physical labor

**Prevention:**
- Women 19–50: 18 mg daily; adult men: 8 mg daily
- Pair heme iron sources (meat, fish, poultry) with vitamin C sources to improve non-heme iron absorption
- Legumes + vitamin C = much better iron absorption than legumes alone
- Support livestock (chickens, goats, fish ponds) for consistent animal protein
- Include leafy greens, seeds, and whole grains as secondary sources

**Treatment:**
- Increase red meat, poultry, and fish consumption
- Pair every legume-based meal with vitamin C source (fermented vegetables, berries, citrus if available)
- Liver is particularly high in iron (5–10 mg per 100 g) and highly absorbable
- Avoid excess tea/coffee during or immediately after iron-rich meals (they reduce absorption)
- Pregnant and lactating women need extra attention: 27 mg daily during pregnancy, then 9–10 mg while lactating

:::warning
Iron-deficiency anemia in women of childbearing age and in pregnant women can lead to complications during pregnancy and labor, including premature birth and postpartum hemorrhage. Community planning must prioritize animal protein and diverse plant sources for women's health.
:::

### Goiter & Iodine Deficiency Disorders

**Onset:** 3–6 months of inadequate iodine intake; cretinism develops in utero during maternal iodine deficiency

**Early symptoms:**
- Thyroid enlargement (swelling in the neck—the "goiter")
- Fatigue, weakness, cold intolerance
- Dry skin, weight gain
- Slowed metabolism, slow speech

**Cretinism (in infants born to iodine-deficient mothers):**
- Severe intellectual disability
- Growth retardation
- Deaf-mutism
- Spasticity and poor coordination
- Often permanent even after iodine intervention

**Prevention:**
- Daily iodine intake of 150 μg (adult requirement); 220 μg during pregnancy; 290 μg during lactation
- Seaweed/kelp is an excellent source if available (100–1000+ μg per gram, but highly variable)
- Fish and shellfish: 50–150 μg per 100 g
- In inland areas far from the ocean, iodine is scarce—consider preserving seaweed, investing in salt fish trade, or supplementing
- Eggs: 20–30 μg per large egg (good secondary source)

**Treatment:**
- Increase iodine-rich foods (seaweed, fish, shellfish)
- Pregnant and lactating women require increased intake (see above)
- Small goiters may shrink over weeks; large goiters are slow to regress
- Cretinism is NOT reversible once developed—prevention during pregnancy is essential

:::warning
Iodine deficiency is the leading preventable cause of intellectual disability worldwide. In post-collapse inland communities, this must be a priority. Plan now to secure seaweed/kelp, preserve fish, or establish trade routes with coastal areas. Without iodine, every pregnancy in an iodine-deficient area risks a baby with permanent brain damage.
:::

### Rickets (Vitamin D & Calcium Deficiency)

**Onset:** 3–6 months of inadequate vitamin D and/or calcium intake (childhood)

**Symptoms in children:**
- Soft, weak bones
- Delayed bone development and walking
- Delayed tooth eruption or poor dental development
- Bowing of legs (especially when weight-bearing)
- Rachitic rosary (beading of rib cage)
- Frontal bossing (prominent forehead)
- Seizures or spasms (from low calcium)
- Susceptibility to respiratory infections

**Symptoms in adults (osteomalacia—"adult rickets"):**
- Severe bone pain, muscle weakness
- Increased fracture risk
- Difficulty walking or climbing stairs

**Prevention:**
- Vitamin D: 15–20 μg daily (or 20–30 minutes of midday sun exposure 3× per week on exposed skin)
- Calcium: 1000–1200 mg daily
- In northern latitudes or winter, vitamin D synthesis is greatly reduced; prioritize dietary sources (fatty fish, egg yolks) and sun exposure when available
- Pair calcium intake with adequate vitamin K (leafy greens) and phosphorus (meat, grains, legumes)

**Treatment:**
- Increase fatty fish consumption (mackerel, salmon): 200–800 IU vitamin D per 100 g
- Include egg yolks regularly (20–25 IU per egg)
- Prioritize dark leafy greens for calcium and vitamin K
- Maximize sun exposure (face, arms, legs) during summer months to build vitamin D stores
- Response is slow (bones remodel over months to years)

:::tip
In northern latitudes, dried or fermented seaweed is an excellent source of both iodine AND vitamin D (if it was exposed to sunlight during drying). Communities should preserve these dual-benefit foods year-round.
:::

## Special Nutritional Needs: Pregnancy, Lactation, Children & Elderly

### Pregnancy

Pregnant women have elevated requirements for nearly all nutrients. See <a href="../pregnancy-nutrition-maternal.html">Pregnancy & Maternal Nutrition</a> for detailed guidance, including:
- **Extra calories:** +300–500 kcal/day in second and third trimester
- **Extra protein:** +25 g/day above non-pregnant requirement
- **Extra iron:** 27 mg/day (vs. 18 mg for non-pregnant women) to support expanded blood volume and fetal needs
- **Extra calcium:** 1000 mg/day (same as non-pregnant adult women, but intake is critical for fetal skeletal development)
- **Extra folate:** 600 μg/day (vs. 400 μg) to prevent neural tube defects
- **Extra iodine:** 220 μg/day (critical for fetal brain development)

### Lactation

Lactating women produce 700–1000 mL of milk daily. Milk composition remains stable regardless of maternal diet (body stores are depleted instead), so lactating women must:
- **Add 500 kcal/day** above non-pregnant baseline (not quite as high as pregnancy, but sustained for entire lactation period)
- **Increase protein** to 71 g/day (from 46 g baseline for adult women)
- **Increase calcium** to 1000 mg/day (same as non-pregnant, but depletion accelerates without replacement)
- **Increase iodine** to 290 μg/day
- **Ensure diverse diet** so milk contains full spectrum of vitamins and minerals

:::warning
In communities with food scarcity, prioritize lactating women after young children. Maternal nutrition directly determines infant nutrition (via breast milk), so it is a community health investment.
:::

**Key points for child nutrition:**
- Iron and zinc are particularly critical during rapid growth periods (toddlerhood, adolescence)
- Adolescent girls have elevated iron needs due to menstruation
- Calcium and vitamin D are essential for peak bone mass development
- Protein-calorie malnutrition in childhood causes stunting (short height) and impaired cognitive development; effects may be partially irreversible
- Breastfeeding to 2 years (with complementary foods after 6 months) is optimal when possible

:::warning
Stunting (short stature due to chronic malnutrition in childhood) affects an estimated 150 million children globally and is partially irreversible. Community food planning must prioritize child nutrition as an investment in the future.
:::

### Elderly Adults (65+ Years)

Elderly adults often have:
- **Reduced caloric needs** (20–30% lower than young adults), but micronutrient needs remain equal or increase
- **Reduced stomach acid**, impairing absorption of vitamin B12, calcium, iron
- **Increased need for vitamin B12:** 2.4 μg/day (same as younger adults), but absorption is worse; fortified foods or supplements are more reliably absorbed
- **Increased vitamin D needs:** Some sources recommend 800–1000 IU/day (vs. 600–800 standard) due to reduced skin synthesis and intestinal absorption
- **Increased calcium needs:** 1200 mg/day (vs. 1000 mg for non-pregnant women younger than 50)
- **Difficulty chewing:** Soft foods, soups, and well-cooked foods are more practical; ensure nutrition is maintained even if texture changes are needed

## Food Combining for Complete Nutrition

Complete protein (containing all 9 essential amino acids) is easier to achieve when combining plant and animal sources. In plant-only diets, combining legumes + grains creates completeness.

### Complete Protein Combinations

**Legumes + grains:**
- Rice + beans (any combination: black beans, pinto beans, kidney beans)
- Wheat bread + lentil soup
- Corn + beans (creates a particularly well-balanced amino acid profile)
- Oats + chickpeas
- Barley + split peas

**Effect:** A serving of cooked legumes (1 cup) combined with whole grains in the same meal provides approximately 15–20 g of complete protein (equivalent to 2.5–3 oz of meat)

### Iron Absorption Enhancement

**Pair non-heme iron sources with vitamin C:**
- Beans + tomato-based sauce or fermented vegetable condiment
- Whole grain bread + sauerkraut or fermented cabbage
- Lentil soup + citrus juice (if available) or wild berry compote
- Leafy greens cooked with meat broth (fat and protein also enhance iron absorption)

**Effect:** This pairing increases non-heme iron bioavailability by 3–4×

### Calcium + Vitamin K + Vitamin D

- Dark leafy greens (kale, collards, bok choy) provide both calcium and vitamin K
- Pair with small amounts of fatty fish (vitamin D) or maximize sun exposure
- Add legumes for additional calcium and minerals

### Zinc Bioavailability in Plant-Heavy Diets

- Combine legumes with animal protein (even small amounts): zinc absorption improves dramatically
- Sprout legumes/grains before cooking: reduces phytates and increases mineral bioavailability
- Ferment grains/legumes: fermentation also reduces phytate content

:::tip
In post-collapse settings with limited animal protein, fermentation and sprouting of staple grains and legumes dramatically improve nutrient bioavailability. These ancient techniques are not luxuries—they are essential for meeting nutrient needs on plant-based diets.
:::

## Detecting Deficiencies Without Lab Tests

In low-resource settings without access to medical testing, careful observation of community members can reveal emerging deficiency diseases before they progress to advanced/fatal stages.

### Signs to Monitor

**Scurvy (vitamin C deficiency):**
- Bleeding gums or spontaneous bleeding
- Slow wound healing
- Rash, especially on legs
- Fatigue disproportionate to workload

**Beriberi (thiamine deficiency):**
- Pins-and-needles in hands/feet
- Swelling in legs and feet (wet beriberi) or muscle weakness (dry beriberi)
- Unexplained tachycardia (fast heartbeat)
- Memory problems or confusion

**Pellagra (niacin deficiency):**
- Symmetrical rash on sun-exposed areas (hands, face, neck, upper chest, legs)
- Diarrhea
- Mood changes, irritability, memory problems
- Swollen, bright red tongue

**Anemia (iron deficiency):**
- Pale mucous membranes (inside mouth, eyelids)
- Shortness of breath with normal activity
- Frequent infections
- Fatigue out of proportion to workload

**Goiter/iodine deficiency:**
- Visible thyroid enlargement in the neck
- Fatigue, weight gain despite adequate intake
- In pregnant women: any goiter warrants immediate attention

**Rickets (vitamin D/calcium deficiency in children):**
- Bowing of legs once child begins weight-bearing (around 1 year old)
- Delayed walking or crawling
- Delayed tooth eruption
- Poor growth compared to peers

### Early Intervention Strategy

Early detection and dietary intervention prevent progression to severe symptoms. Establish community protocols:

1. **Monthly spot checks:** A community health worker observes signs in vulnerable groups (young children, pregnant women, elderly)
2. **Rapid dietary intervention:** If signs appear, immediately increase relevant nutrient sources
3. **Document response:** Most deficiencies show dramatic improvement within 1–4 weeks of adequate intake
4. **Adjust community food plan:** If multiple people show the same deficiency, it indicates a gap in the food production/distribution system

:::info-box
A single case of symptomatic deficiency disease in a community indicates systemic food production or distribution failure. The response is not just individual treatment but community-level planning adjustment.
:::

## Community Nutrition Planning

### Annual Food Production Targets

To meet the nutrition requirements of a community, plan crop production based on:

1. **Total caloric requirement:** (number of people) × (average caloric requirement including all ages/activity levels)
2. **Macronutrient balance:** Aim for 50–60% calories from carbohydrates (grains, root crops), 20–30% from fat (oils, nuts, fatty animals), 10–20% from protein (legumes, meat/fish)
3. **Micronutrient diversity:** Ensure year-round access to:
   - **Vitamin A:** Orange/red vegetables, dark leafy greens (preserved or fresh)
   - **B vitamins:** Whole grains, legumes, animal products
   - **Vitamin C:** Fermented vegetables, preserved berries, dried rose hips
   - **Vitamin D:** Fatty fish (fresh or preserved), eggs, sun exposure
   - **Iron:** Animal protein (primary), legumes, leafy greens with vitamin C enhancement
   - **Calcium:** Leafy greens, legumes, dairy (if livestock available)
   - **Iodine:** Seaweed, fish, eggs, salt fish (if available/traded)
   - **Zinc:** Animal protein, legumes, nuts/seeds

### Sample Community Nutrition Plan (per capita, annual)

For a family/small community, these are realistic annual production/acquisition targets:

| Food Category | Amount/Person/Year | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Staple grain (wheat, rice, oats) | 150–200 kg | Whole grain preferred; store in cool, dry conditions |
| Legumes (beans, lentils, peas) | 30–40 kg | Stored dried; combine with grains for complete protein |
| Root vegetables (potatoes, sweet potatoes) | 50–100 kg | Store in cool conditions; support vitamin A via orange varieties |
| Leafy greens (fresh/dried/fermented) | 10–15 kg | Dried or fermented for year-round access; vitamin A + calcium |
| Oilseeds/oils | 5–8 kg | Pressed oils or stored seeds; essential for fat, vitamin E |
| Animal products (meat, fish, eggs, dairy) | 30–50 kg | Fresh/preserved; critical for complete protein, heme iron, B vitamins |
| Preserved vegetables/fruits (fermented, dried) | 20–30 kg | Vitamin C from rose hips, berries; fermented goods for gut health |
| Sea vegetables (seaweed, kelp) | 1–2 kg | If coastal/trade access available; dual iodine + vitamin D |

### Seasonal Preservation & Distribution

| Season | Priority Actions |
|---|---|
| **Spring/Early Summer** | Begin fermentation of new vegetables; dry excess herbs; raise young livestock |
| **Mid-Summer** | Preserve berries (dry, ferment); maximize leafy green production |
| **Late Summer/Fall** | Harvest and store grains and legumes; preserve root vegetables; prepare seaweed/dried fish for winter |
| **Winter** | Rely on stored crops, preserved vegetables, and preserved animal products; monitor for deficiency signs; adjust diet if shortages apparent |
| **Year-round** | Maintain modest livestock (chickens, goats) for eggs/milk; preserve/ferment to ensure year-round diversity |

### Red Flags Requiring Action

- **Single-food diet:** If 80%+ of calories come from one source (e.g., white rice), deficiency diseases will emerge within 3–6 months. Immediately diversify with legumes, vegetables, and animal protein.
- **Absence of animal products:** Communities without eggs, dairy, or meat face challenges meeting protein, heme iron, B12, and zinc needs. Prioritize small livestock (chickens, goats, rabbits) and fish if possible.
- **No year-round vitamin C source:** Scurvy will emerge in winter if no fermented vegetables or dried berries are preserved. Establish preservation system immediately.
- **No leafy greens in winter:** Calcium and folate intake drops sharply. Plan to dry, ferment, or cold-store greens for winter.
- **Geographic isolation from iodine source:** Goiter will become endemic in inland communities without seaweed access or salt fish trade. This is a critical planning priority.

:::warning
Community nutrition failure is not a natural disaster—it is a planning failure. Every deficiency disease visible in a population represents a gap in the food production, preservation, or distribution system. Intervene at the community level, not just individual level.
:::

<section id="conclusion">

## Conclusion

Nutrition is the foundation of survival. A well-fed community has energy for labor, resilience against infection, and the capacity for growth. A malnourished community becomes vulnerable to disease, loses productive capacity, and faces generational damage (stunted children, brain-damaged infants born to iodine-deficient mothers).

The good news: deficiency diseases are entirely preventable with modest dietary diversity and planning. Use this guide to:

1. **Understand requirements** for your community by age, sex, and activity level
2. **Plan food production** to meet these requirements year-round, with emphasis on diversification
3. **Recognize early signs** of deficiency so intervention happens before severe disease emerges
4. **Preserve and ferment** to extend seasonal foods into winter months
5. **Combine foods strategically** (grains + legumes, non-heme iron + vitamin C) to maximize nutrient bioavailability

Start with the most critical nutrients in your context:
- **Inland communities:** Prioritize iodine source (seaweed, fish, or trade)
- **Communities relying on grains:** Ensure legumes and vegetables diversify the diet
- **Isolated winter regions:** Establish fermented vegetable and dried fruit systems now
- **Communities with limited animal protein:** Invest in sprouting/fermentation techniques to improve plant-based nutrient bioavailability

Your community's future depends on the nutritional decisions you make today.
