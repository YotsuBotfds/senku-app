Water is one of the most reliable and accessible sources of mechanical power. A river or stream near your location provides continuous, predictable energy. This guide covers how to assess water resources, build simple water wheels, and convert water power into usable mechanical or electrical power.

<section id="water-power-basics">

## Why Water Power?

Water power has advantages:
- **Continuous:** Unlike wind and sun, running water provides power day and night
- **Predictable:** Flow varies seasonally but is relatively stable
- **Powerful:** Water is heavy; small flows contain significant energy
- **Historical:** Watermills powered civilizations for thousands of years
- **Scalable:** From tiny micro-hydro (a few watts) to massive dams (megawatts)

The only requirement: moving water with significant elevation change.

### Energy in Falling Water

Power depends on two factors:

**Flow rate (volume of water per time)**
- Measured in gallons per minute (GPM), liters per second, or cubic meters per second
- More water = more power

**Height drop (elevation change)**
- Measured in feet or meters
- More height = exponentially more power
- Doubling height doubles power; doubling flow doubles power

**Formula:**
Power (watts) = Flow (kg/s) × Gravity (9.8 m/s²) × Height (m)

**Example:** 100 liters/sec dropping 5 meters
- Power = 100 kg/s × 9.8 × 5 = 4,900 watts (almost 5 kilowatts)

<section id="assessing-your-water">

## Assessing Your Water Resource

Before building anything, understand what you have.

### Finding Your Site

**Look for:**
- Running water (stream, creek, river, or artificial channel like canal or spillway)
- Consistent flow year-round (or at least during seasons you need power)
- Elevation change (even small drops provide power)
- No hazards (flooding, dangerous currents, contamination)

**Avoid:**
- Highly contaminated water (pollutants damage equipment)
- Unpredictable floods
- Water already allocated for other use (irrigation, human consumption)
- Seasonal drying (unless you store water)

### Measuring Flow Rate

**Simple method (volume and time):**
1. Place a container of known volume (1-gallon bucket, for example) in the water
2. Time how long it takes to fill (use stopwatch)
3. Calculate: Flow = Volume / Time
4. Example: 5-gallon bucket fills in 4 seconds = 1.25 gallons/second = 450 gallons/minute

**Float method (for larger streams):**
1. Mark two points on the stream 10 meters apart
2. Drop a floating stick at the first point
3. Time how long it takes to reach the second point
4. Measure the width and average depth of the stream
5. Cross-sectional area = width × average depth
6. Flow = Cross-sectional area × distance / time

**More accurate method:**
- Use a bucket and stopwatch at multiple points across the stream
- Average results for better accuracy

### Measuring Height Drop

**Simple method (spirit level and measuring stick):**
1. At upstream point, hold measuring stick level (horizontal, using level)
2. Measure down to water surface
3. Walk downstream 10-20 meters
4. Measure down from level to water surface
5. Difference is elevation drop
6. Scale up for full fall distance

**Practical: You need height drop along the reach where you'll place your wheel**

For a waterwheel at a narrowing or falls, measure vertical drop directly (easier).

### Calculate Available Power

**Using the formula:**

Power (watts) = 9.8 × flow (kg/s) × height (m)

Example: 500 liters/minute drop 2 meters
- Convert: 500 L/min = 8.33 L/sec = 8.33 kg/sec
- Power = 9.8 × 8.33 × 2 = 163 watts

This is theoretical maximum. Expect to capture 30-60% of this with a simple waterwheel.

<section id="water-wheel-types">

## Types of Water Wheels

### Overshot Wheel (Highest Efficiency)

**Best for:** Moderate to high head (height drop), moderate flow

**Design:**
- Water enters at the top of the wheel
- Buckets along the rim catch water
- Water's weight drives wheel downward
- Exit at bottom

**Efficiency:** 60-80% (very good)

**Power formula:** Uses both kinetic energy (flowing water) and potential energy (water weight in buckets)

**Disadvantages:** Needs precise height; requires more infrastructure to direct water to top

### Breast Wheel (Moderate Efficiency)

**Best for:** Moderate head and flow (most common historically)

**Design:**
- Water enters at side of wheel (at level of axle, roughly)
- Buckets catch water and fill
- Combination of weight and flow drives wheel

**Efficiency:** 50-70%

**Advantages:** Simpler to set up than overshot; works with moderate head

### Undershot Wheel (Lower Efficiency)

**Best for:** Low head, high flow (watermill in large river)

**Design:**
- Water passes under the wheel
- Paddles or buckets are pushed by moving water
- Relies on kinetic energy of current, not water weight

**Efficiency:** 20-40%

**Advantages:** Works with low elevation drop; simple construction

### Pelton Wheel (High Speed, High Efficiency)

**Best for:** Very high head (mountain streams), small flow

**Design:**
- Jet of water hits cup-shaped buckets
- Buckets deflect water, extracting energy
- Very efficient at converting high-velocity water

**Efficiency:** 80-90%

**Disadvantages:** Requires high head; more complex construction; best paired with generator

### Turgo Wheel (Variation)

Similar to Pelton but flow can be higher; buckets are simpler.

**Efficiency:** 70-85%

### Crossflow Turbine (Modern, Efficient)

**Design:**
- Water passes through channel, hitting buckets arranged in a cylinder
- Water passes through twice (increases energy extraction)

**Efficiency:** 70-80%

**Advantages:** Works across range of heads and flows; relatively simple; becoming more common

</section>

<section id="building-simple-wheel">

## Building a Simple Undershot Waterwheel

**This is the easiest wheel for beginners.**

**Materials:**
- Wood (for axle and frame)
- Metal axle (iron rod, minimum 1 inch diameter for any serious power)
- Wooden paddles or curved sheet metal
- Bearings or wooden bearings
- Frame for mounting
- Tools (saw, drill, wrench, etc.)

### Design

**Wheel size:** 3-6 feet diameter (larger captures more water)

**Axle:** Iron rod, through the center

**Paddles:** 8-12 curved or flat paddles around the wheel circumference

**Frame:** Sturdy wooden structure to hold axle and position wheel in water

### Construction Steps

1. **Make the axle:**
   - Iron rod 6-8 feet long (extends beyond wheel for belt/pulley)
   - Centered in the wheel structure

2. **Build the wheel:**
   - Create circular frame from wood (two wooden rings)
   - Space apart to width of paddles (12-18 inches)
   - Connect with wooden spokes or metal brackets

3. **Attach paddles:**
   - Mount 8-12 curved paddles around circumference
   - Slight curve (concave upstream) catches water better
   - Space evenly around wheel

4. **Create bearings:**
   - Mount axle on bearings that allow free spinning
   - Wooden journal bearings (simplest): Axle rests in wooden block with greased hole
   - Metal ball bearings (better): Reduce friction significantly
   - Bearings should be solid and support axle weight

5. **Mount in water:**
   - Position frame so paddles dip into moving water
   - Wheel axis should be horizontal
   - Paddles should enter water at bottom, exit near side
   - Depth of paddle dipping: 6-12 inches

6. **Test and adjust:**
   - Check for smooth rotation
   - Listen for grinding (indicates misalignment)
   - Measure RPM (revolutions per minute) under load
   - Adjust paddle angle slightly if needed for efficiency

</section>

<section id="powertransmission">

## Transmitting Power from Wheel to Use

Once the wheel is spinning, you need to use that power.

### Direct Drive

**Simple:** Attach device directly to axle
- Example: Waterwheel axle drives grinding mill directly
- Advantage: No efficiency loss to transmission
- Disadvantage: Can't change speed; wheel must spin at right speed for device

### Pulley and Belt

**System:** Pulley on wheel axle, belt drives pulley on output

**Advantages:**
- Can change speed (different size pulleys)
- Can transmit power some distance
- Simple and proven technology

**Efficiency:** ~85-95% (good)

**Design:**
- Wheel pulley (large) = slow speed, high torque
- Output pulley (smaller) = higher speed, lower torque
- Ratio = Wheel pulley diameter / Output pulley diameter
- Example: 24-inch wheel pulley / 8-inch output = 3:1 speed increase

### Gears

**System:** Gears on wheel axle mesh with gears on output shaft

**Advantages:**
- Can change speed and direction
- Compact; no slipping
- Most efficient

**Disadvantages:**
- More complex; harder to make
- Requires precise machining

**Efficiency:** ~95-98% (excellent)

### Generator Connection

**For electrical power:**
- Connect wheel to generator shaft (via pulleys, gears, or direct)
- Generator converts mechanical rotation to electricity
- See related guides on generators

</section>

<section id="practical-systems">

## Practical Water Power Systems

### Micro-Hydro (Small Home System)

**Scale:** 100 watts to 5 kilowatts

**Requirements:**
- Stream with flow (even small)
- Elevation drop (can be small)
- Location (close to where power is needed)

**Typical setup:**
1. Small waterwheel or Turgo turbine (1-2 feet diameter)
2. Drives small generator or transmission system
3. Charges batteries or powers direct loads
4. Minimal environmental impact

**Cost:** Very low if DIY

### Run-of-River System

**Scale:** Few kilowatts to hundreds of kilowatts

**Requirements:**
- Substantial stream
- Significant elevation drop
- Channel to direct water

**Typical setup:**
1. Channel or pipe directs water from upstream to wheel/turbine
2. Water returns to stream downstream
3. Minimal damming; uses natural flow

**Advantage:** More power than micro-hydro; less environmental impact than dam

### Waterwheel Mill

**Scale:** 5-50 kilowatts

**Requirements:**
- Good-sized stream
- Elevation drop
- Mechanical use (grinding, sawing, etc.)

**Historical system:**
- Waterwheel drives gears
- Gears drive mill mechanism (grinding stones, saw blade, etc.)
- Power is used directly (no electricity generation)

**Advantage:** Mechanical power is reliable; no electrical system needed

<section id="efficiency-and-power">

## Expected Power Output

**Reality check:** Your actual power will be less than theoretical maximum.

### Losses

1. **Waterwheel efficiency:** 40-80% depending on design
2. **Transmission loss:** 5-15% in belt/pulley or gear systems
3. **Generator loss:** 10-20% converting mechanical to electrical (if using generator)

### Example Calculation

Stream: 100 liters/min, 3-meter drop

Theoretical power: 9.8 × 1.67 kg/s × 3 m = 49 watts

Using undershot wheel (40% efficient) → 20 watts
Transmission loss (10%) → 18 watts
Generator loss (15%) → 15 watts actual electrical power

This small system powers a few LED lights or charges small batteries.

### Increasing Output

**Options:**
- Find location with more flow
- Find location with more elevation drop
- Use more efficient wheel design
- Reduce transmission losses (good bearings, tight belts)
- Improve generator efficiency

**Most effective:** Find a better location (more flow or drop)

</section>

<section id="safety">

## Water Power Safety

:::warning
**Fast-Moving Water:** Water currents are deceptive. Moving water is powerful and can sweep you away.
:::

:::warning
**Moving Machinery:** Waterwheels and belts are dangerous. Keep hair, loose clothing, and body parts clear of moving parts.
:::

:::warning
**Structural Collapse:** Channel, dams, or wheels under stress can fail suddenly. Build solidly; maintain regularly.
:::

**Practices:**
- Warn others of moving machinery
- Don't enter fast water without safety equipment
- Inspect structures regularly
- Drain system before maintenance
- Use guards over belts and gears
- Secure axles and bearings properly

</section>

<section id="environmental-considerations">

## Environmental Considerations

**Before building:**

- Check local regulations (many jurisdictions regulate water use)
- Ensure adequate flow for downstream users (wildlife, people)
- Avoid blocking fish migration if applicable
- Minimize impact on ecosystem

**Responsible design:**
- Run-of-river system better than dam
- Keep water in stream below your use
- Don't over-extract water from fragile systems

</section>

## Next Steps

After understanding water power:

1. **Assess your water:** Measure flow and drop at a promising location
2. **Build simple wheel:** Start with small test wheel
3. **Generate electricity:** Add generator to wheel
4. **Scale up:** If successful, build larger system
5. **Combine sources:** Add wind or solar for reliability

Water power is among the most reliable and proven energy sources. A well-designed water system provides power for decades with minimal maintenance.
