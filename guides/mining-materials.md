## 1\. Ore Identification

Identifying ores in the field is essential for establishing a material processing supply chain. Each ore has distinctive visual characteristics, hardness, and streak color that aid identification.

### Gold and Precious Metals

**Gold (Au):** Yellow metallic, density ~19.3 g/cm³. Found in placer deposits (stream beds, alluvial fans) and in quartz veins. Extremely malleable and soft (2.5-3 hardness). Panning is the most primitive recovery method.

**Silver (Ag):** White metallic, tarnishes dark. Often occurs with copper and lead ores. Rarely found native but fairly common in sulfide deposits.

:::info-box
**Mohs Hardness Scale for Ore Identification:** The Mohs scale ranges 1-10. Field testing: fingernail (~2.5), copper penny (~3), steel knife (~5.5), glass (~5.5), quartz (~7). Magnetite (5.5-6.5) resists knife scratching and is magnetic. Hematite (5-6) is harder than galena (2.5-2.75, very soft, easily scratched). Cassiterite (6-7) is among the hardest common ores. Hardness helps distinguish similar-looking ores: malachite (green, soft 3.5-4) vs. azurite (blue, also 3.5-4 but different color) vs. harder green minerals like olivine (7-7.5, not ore mineral).
:::

### Ore Color Reference Guide

Visual identification using color swatches:

![⛏ Mining &amp; Materials Processing diagram 1](../assets/svgs/mining-materials-1.svg)

### Field Testing Techniques

-   **Streak Test:** Rub ore on unglazed ceramic plate. Color of streak is diagnostic.
-   **Hardness Test:** Use Mohs hardness scale. Fingernail ~2.5, copper coin ~3, steel knife ~5.5.
-   **Density Test:** Gold and lead are heavy; copper ores medium-heavy; iron ores medium.
-   **Magnetic Test:** Magnetite is strongly magnetic; hematite weakly so.
-   **Acid Test:** Copper carbonates (malachite, azurite) fizz in dilute HCl. Galena tarnishes in acid.

:::tip
**Tip:** In early civilization rebuilding, focus on bog iron and surface copper deposits. These require minimal depth mining and yield high-quality ore with less processing.
:::

:::info-box
**Specific Gravity Reference for Ores:** Specific gravity (density ratio to water) helps field identification. Light ores (SG 2.0-3.0): bauxite 2.5, sulfur 2.0. Medium (3.0-5.0): magnetite 5.2, hematite 5.3, copper ores 3.5-4.2. Heavy (5.0+): cassiterite 7.0, galena 7.4, sphalerite 3.9-4.1. Pure metals: copper 8.9, iron 7.9, tin 7.3, lead 11.3, zinc 7.1. A small sample feels much heavier than a rock of the same size if it contains heavy metal ore. Density testing by water displacement gives precise identification: weigh sample in air, then weigh suspended in water; SG = weight in air / (weight in air - weight in water).
:::

## 2\. Prospecting & Mining Basics

Before building furnaces and smelters, you must locate ore deposits and extract raw ore from the earth. Prospecting is the art of finding ore; mining is the systematic extraction.

### Surface Indicators of Ore Deposits

-   **Color Staining:** Iron ores cause reddish-brown staining in soil and stream water. Copper deposits create green (malachite) or blue (azurite) discoloration.
-   **Gossan (Iron Hat):** Oxidized zone above sulfide ore bodies, appears as rust-colored, porous rock.
-   **Stream Concentrations:** Heavy minerals (cassiterite, magnetite) accumulate in stream beds and terraces.
-   **Outcrops:** Exposed rock faces where ore veins are visible. High-altitude cuts and cliff faces are valuable.
-   **Vegetation Changes:** Toxic metals create stunted vegetation or bare zones above deposits.
-   **Geothermal Features:** Hot springs, geysers indicate deep thermal activity and possible mineral veins.

### Panning for Gold and Heavy Minerals

The simplest ore recovery method, requiring only a pan and water:

1.  Fill pan 3/4 full with gravel and sediment from stream bed
2.  Submerge in water and shake to break up clay
3.  Wash away lighter material by tilting pan forward
4.  Repeat in circular motion to concentrate heavy minerals to pan bottom
5.  Repeat until black sand (magnetite, cassiterite) and gold visible
6.  Collect using tweezers or small magnet (for magnetite)

:::tip
**Panning Hotspots:** Inside bends of rivers, behind large rocks, and at the base of waterfalls where water slows and deposits heavy materials.
:::

### Basic Shaft Mining

Vertical or near-vertical access to ore bodies:

-   **Shaft Sinking:** Dig straight down following ore vein
-   **Shoring:** Support walls with timber to prevent collapse. Use logs (4-6 inches diameter) in horizontal/vertical bracing patterns.
-   **Drainage:** Dig sump at bottom to collect water; use bucket chain or simple pump
-   **Ventilation:** Critical! Dead air becomes toxic. Use chimney effect with separate intake/exhaust shafts or bellows-driven air tubes.
-   **Lighting:** Torches, candles, or oil lamps. Test air quality with flame—flame shrinks if CO₂/CO present.
-   **Depth Limits:** Without mechanical pumps, rarely exceed 30-50 meters before water becomes unmanageable

### Open-Pit & Trench Mining

Surface mining for shallow, widespread deposits:

-   Remove overburden (top soil/rock) to expose ore
-   Extract ore in horizontal layers or benches
-   Best for bog iron, laterite bauxite, placer deposits
-   Requires less ventilation and shoring than deep mines
-   Risk: Slope collapse. Limit angles to ~45° for stable soil

### Tool Technique: Pick and Shovel

**Pick:** For breaking hard ore and rock. Swing with full body weight, aim strike perpendicular to grain. Dulls quickly—regular maintenance required.

**Shovel:** For moving broken ore and overburden. Shorter handle for deeper work, longer handle for ground level. Keep blade sharp for clay/wet soil.

**Efficiency Tip:** A team of 3-4 miners working in shifts can extract 2-5 tons of ore daily, depending on hardness and depth.

### Mine Safety

:::warning
**Critical Hazards:**

-   **Cave-in:** Proper shoring prevents most collapses. Never underestimate need for support.
-   **Bad Air:** CO, CO₂, CH₄ (methane), H₂S accumulate in mines. Constant ventilation essential. Canary test: single bird in cage enters shaft first. If it stops singing, evacuate immediately.
-   **Gas Detection:** Before deep mining, light a match/candle at shaft bottom. Flame behavior indicates gas type (blue flame = CO, shrinking flame = CO₂, extinguishes = O₂ depletion).
-   **Water Ingress:** Can be sudden. Have drainage system before deep mining.
-   **Falls:** Use ropes and proper ladder/shaft design. Accidents are common in early mining.
:::

## 3\. Charcoal Production

Charcoal is the fuel for all metalworking. It burns hotter and cleaner than wood, essential for smelting and forging. Early civilizations produced charcoal in earth mounds or simple kilns.

### Earth-Mound Charcoal Kiln

The simplest and most accessible method for pre-industrial societies:

![⛏ Mining &amp; Materials Processing diagram 2](../assets/svgs/mining-materials-2.svg)

### Construction Steps

1.  **Site Preparation:** Clear level ground. Dig small depression (20-30 cm) to catch liquid tar runoff.
2.  **Wood Selection:** Use hardwoods (oak, birch, maple, walnut). Avoid softwoods (pine, fir—too resinous). Green wood acceptable; seasoned wood preferred.
3.  **Stack Construction:** Stack wood vertically in cone shape, ~3-4 m diameter base, ~2-2.5 m height. Leave central channel for ignition.
4.  **Thatching Layer:** Cover entire stack with dry grass, leaves, or straw (~10 cm layer).
5.  **Soil Seal:** Cover everything with 10-15 cm layer of earth/clay mix. Compact firmly. This controls air flow and contains heat.
6.  **Air Holes:** Poke 4-6 small holes (4-5 cm diameter) in soil around base, alternating sides. These allow oxygen in and control burn rate.
7.  **Ignition:** Light central channel using dry kindling pushed through top or ignition tube.
8.  **Monitoring:** Observe smoke color. White/gray = too much air (close holes). Black = incomplete combustion. Optimal: thin, steady gray smoke.
9.  **Burn Time:** 3-7 days depending on size and wood type. Keep holes partially sealed with clay.
10.  **Cooling:** Once smoke ceases, wait 1-2 days before opening. Charcoal can spontaneously ignite if exposed to air while hot.
11.  **Extraction:** Carefully remove soil, collect charcoal. Yield: ~25-30% of original wood weight.

### Metal Drum Method

For smaller, more controlled production:

-   Pack wood vertically in 55-gallon metal drum
-   Seal with metal lid with small hole for vapor escape
-   Place drum in fire pit surrounded by coals
-   Burn 6-8 hours until vapor becomes thin
-   Remove from fire, allow to cool overnight
-   Yield: ~20-25% of wood weight, but higher quality (more consistent)

### Retort Method (Advanced)

Cast iron or steel pressure vessel allows recovery of wood distillation products (tar, acetic acid) while making charcoal:

-   Sealed retort filled with wood
-   Heated externally to ~400-450°C
-   Vapors condense in cooled pipes → tar and acids collected
-   Charcoal remains in retort
-   Yield: 30% charcoal + ~5% tar + pyroligneous acid

### Charcoal Quality Testing

:::tip
**Good Charcoal:** Black, lightweight, brittle, burns hot without smoking, ~80% carbon content. Test by striking with steel—should break into pieces but not powder.
:::

:::info-box
**Ore Mineral Content and Extraction Yields:** Not all ore is equal—pure ore has higher metal content. Magnetite (Fe₃O₄) is 72.4% iron by weight; hematite (Fe₂O₃) is 69.9% iron; limonite varies 30-60% iron. Malachite (Cu₂CO₃(OH)₂) contains 57.5% copper; azurite (Cu₃(CO₃)₂(OH)₂) contains 55.2%. Cassiterite (SnO₂) is 78.8% tin. These theoretical maxima are rarely achieved in smelting due to slag inclusion and losses. Bloomery iron smelting recovers 70-85% of ore's iron content. Copper furnaces 75-90%. Practical yield from raw ore is 40-70% (accounting for mining waste and rock included with ore). Maximize yield by hand-sorting ore, crushing to uniform size, and roasting before smelting to drive off volatiles.
:::

## 4\. Iron Smelting - Bloomery Furnace

The bloomery is the oldest and simplest iron smelting furnace, used for 2000+ years before blast furnaces. It directly reduces iron ore to soft iron metal without melting, producing a "bloom" that is then hammered into workable form.

### Bloomery Furnace Diagram

![⛏ Mining &amp; Materials Processing diagram 3](../assets/svgs/mining-materials-3.svg)

### Bloomery Construction Steps

1.  **Foundation:** Choose level ground. Dig foundation pit ~40 cm deep, 1 m diameter. This serves as bloom collection chamber.
2.  **Base Layer:** Create ceramic or clay-lined basin at bottom. This catches molten slag and protects earth.
3.  **Furnace Walls:** Build from clay, stone, or ceramic. Stack in circular pattern, ~40-50 cm internal diameter. Walls should be ~20 cm thick. Use clay as mortar.
4.  **Tuyere Installation:** At ~20 cm height from base, insert horizontal clay pipe (4-6 cm diameter) angled slightly upward. This is where bellows air enters.
5.  **Slag Tap Hole:** Near base, insert small ceramic pipe or simply leave hole for slag runoff.
6.  **Top Opening:** Leave top ~15 cm open for charging and exhaust gases.
7.  **Drying:** Allow clay to dry for 3-7 days before use. Premature heating causes cracking.

### Bellows Design for Bloomery

**Simple Bellows (Skin Bellows):** Two wooden frames (40 x 30 cm) with animal hide (goat, sheep) stretched and glued to frame edges. Pivot pin at one end connects to tuyere. Operator pushes down on one handle, raising pulls up the other. Produces ~50-100 liters of air per stroke.

**Double Bellows:** Two chambers with one-way valves. One fills while other pushes, creating more continuous air flow. Much more efficient than single bellows.

**Bellows Requirements:** ~200-300 liters of air per minute needed. Single operator can sustain 1 bellows; 2+ operators needed for continuous high-output smelting.

### Charging and Operating the Furnace

#### Fuel and Material Ratios

-   **Iron Ore:** 5-10 kg per smelt cycle
-   **Charcoal:** 8-15 kg per cycle (roughly 2:1 charcoal:ore by weight)
-   **Flux (optional):** 0.5-1 kg limestone or shell (helps slag flow)

#### Charging Sequence

1.  Start with hot coals or wood fire to preheat furnace (30-60 minutes)
2.  Add layer of charcoal (~5 cm)
3.  Add layer of roasted ore (~2 cm)
4.  Add layer of charcoal (~5 cm)
5.  Alternate layers until charged. Top layer charcoal.
6.  Begin bellows operation at controlled rate to avoid thermal shock

#### Monitoring the Smelt

**Duration:** 4-8 hours depending on ore quality and charcoal. Look for:

-   Orange-white glow inside furnace (proper temperature)
-   Steady flow of molten slag from tap hole
-   Charcoal consumption and level decline
-   Top temperature indicators: iron ore changes color from red/brown to black as it reduces

### Extracting the Bloom

**Process:**

1.  After 4-8 hours, reduce bellows operation to allow furnace to cool slightly (~800-900°C)
2.  Carefully dig/dig out bloom from base with iron tongs
3.  Place hot bloom on flat anvil or iron plate
4.  **CRITICAL:** Bloom is extremely hot and contains liquid slag. Work quickly but carefully.
5.  Strike bloom repeatedly with heavy hammer to squeeze out slag (process called "shingling")
6.  Fold and reheat in forge to consolidate
7.  Continue hammering until bloom is solid and slag eliminated (~2-4 hours work)

## 5\. Steelmaking

Iron from a bloomery is soft and relatively pure. Steel is iron with precisely controlled carbon content (0.2-2.1%), making it harder and stronger. Civilizations developed several methods to add carbon to wrought iron.

### Carbon Content Classifications

<table><thead><tr><th scope="col">Material</th><th scope="col">Carbon Content</th><th scope="col">Properties</th><th scope="col">Uses</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td><strong>Wrought Iron</strong></td><td>&lt;0.08%</td><td>Very soft, malleable, high melting point (~1540°C)</td><td>Chains, nails, general metalwork</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Low Carbon Steel</strong></td><td>0.2-0.4%</td><td>Tough, slightly springy, hardens with quench</td><td>Tools, structural members</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Medium Carbon Steel</strong></td><td>0.4-0.8%</td><td>Good hardness and toughness balance, holds edge</td><td>Swords, axes, knives, springs</td></tr><tr><td><strong>High Carbon Steel</strong></td><td>0.8-2.1%</td><td>Very hard, brittle if not tempered, excellent edge</td><td>Files, saws, fine tools, weapons</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Cast Iron</strong></td><td>&gt;2.1%</td><td>Very hard, brittle, melts at ~1150°C</td><td>Engine blocks, cookware, sculpture</td></tr></tbody></table>

### Blister Steel (Cementation)

Ancient method (Roman era onward) to convert wrought iron to steel by soaking in carbon source:

#### Process:

1.  **Prepare Iron:** Clean wrought iron bars or plates (thickness 12-25 mm)
2.  **Carbon Source:** Bone charcoal, charcoal powder, or crushed charcoal
3.  **Pack in Box:** Layer wrought iron alternately with carbon powder in sealed clay/ceramic box
4.  **Heat:** Place in forge or furnace and heat to ~900-1000°C for 6-12 hours
5.  **Cool:** Allow slow cooling (don't quench immediately)
6.  **Result:** Surface of iron carburizes to 0.5-1.5% carbon, inner remains soft. "Blister" appearance from gas bubbles.
7.  **Hammer:** Hot-forge the blister steel to consolidate and distribute carbon

**Yield:** ~70% of original iron weight becomes usable steel (some material scales off, some remains wrought iron core)

**Time:** 12-24 hours per batch

**Advantage:** Simple, requires no new furnace, only heat source

### Crucible Steel (Advanced)

Higher quality method developed in India and Middle East, produces homogeneous steel:

1.  **Crucible:** High-temperature clay pot (25-50 cm tall, thick walls)
2.  **Charge:** Wrought iron pieces + charcoal + optional fluxes (lime, glass)
3.  **Heat:** Heat crucible in furnace to ~1500°C for 2-3 hours until all melts
4.  **Pour:** Carefully pour molten steel into mold to create ingot
5.  **Cool:** Slow cooling in sand
6.  **Forge:** Hot-forge ingot to desired shape. Can be repeatedly reheated and forged.

**Yield:** ~80-90% of charged material becomes usable steel

**Quality:** Very high, homogeneous carbon distribution, less slag inclusion

**Challenge:** Requires very hot furnace and sophisticated crucibles

### Heat Treatment: Quenching and Tempering

Steel properties depend on cooling rate after heating:

#### Quenching

Heat steel to critical temperature (~750-850°C depending on carbon %), then plunge into cold liquid (water, oil, or brine):

-   **Effect:** Rapid cooling locks in hard martensite structure
-   **Result:** Very hard but brittle—may crack or shatter
-   **Water Quench:** Fastest cooling, maximum hardness, maximum brittleness, risk of distortion
-   **Oil Quench:** Slower than water, less risk of cracking, good for complex shapes
-   **Brine Quench:** Salt water, intermediate speed, used for high carbon steel

#### Tempering

After quenching, reheat to moderate temperature (150-600°C) then cool slowly:

-   **Low Temp (150-250°C):** Minimal softening, high hardness, brittle. For springs, chisels.
-   **Medium Temp (300-450°C):** Good balance of hardness and toughness. For tools, weapons.
-   **High Temp (500-600°C):** Very tough, less brittle, suitable for shock-loaded applications.

:::tip
**Temperature Color Guide (by oxide layer during tempering):**

-   Pale Yellow: ~150-200°C (brittle, springs)
-   Straw Yellow: ~200-250°C (springs, fine tools)
-   Brown: ~250-300°C (general tools)
-   Purple: ~300-350°C (balanced)
-   Blue: ~350-400°C (shock-resistant)

Watch the color change as steel heats and choose the right moment to quench in water.
:::

## 6\. Copper Smelting

Copper smelts at lower temperature than iron (~1100°C vs 1540°C). Oxidized ores (malachite, azurite) are easier to reduce than iron sulfides. Copper was one of the first metals smelted by humans.

### Simple Copper Smelting Furnace

![⛏ Mining &amp; Materials Processing diagram 4](../assets/svgs/mining-materials-4.svg)

### Copper Smelting Process

#### Ore Preparation

-   **Malachite (Cu₂CO₃(OH)₂):** Green ore, relatively pure. Roast at 400-500°C to drive off water and CO₂, converting to CuO.
-   **Azurite (Cu₃(CO₃)₂(OH)₂):** Blue ore, similar treatment to malachite.
-   **Chalcopyrite (CuFeS₂):** More complex. Roast at 600-700°C to partially oxidize sulfide to oxide.
-   **Crush to powder:** Fine powder (< 5 mm) aids reduction.

#### Smelting Steps

1.  **Preheat:** Heat furnace with charcoal-only charge for 30-60 minutes
2.  **Add ore:** Introduce roasted ore powder mixed with charcoal in small batches
3.  **Maintain temperature:** Keep at ~1100-1200°C. Bellows operation crucial for heat.
4.  **Monitor slag:** Slag should flow freely. If too thick, add lime flux.
5.  **Duration:** 2-4 hours depending on ore quantity
6.  **Collect copper:** As molten copper accumulates at bottom, carefully pour into mold or ladle

### Casting Copper

**Mold Materials:** Sand, clay, or stone. Heat mold to prevent thermal shock and cracking.

**Sand Mold:** Compact slightly damp sand around wooden pattern, remove pattern, pour molten copper. Simple, reusable.

**Clay Mold:** Hand-shape clay into desired form, fire to harden. More precise shapes possible.

**Open Ingot Cast:** Pour into simple rectangular stone mold. Results in ingot that can be further forged.

### Bronze Making (Copper + Tin Alloy)

Bronze is superior to pure copper—harder, better casting properties, lower melting point. Requires access to tin ore (cassiterite).

#### Bronze Composition

<table><thead><tr><th scope="col">Type</th><th scope="col">Copper %</th><th scope="col">Tin %</th><th scope="col">Properties</th><th scope="col">Uses</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td><strong>Arsenical Bronze</strong></td><td>90-98%</td><td>0% (arsenic 2-10%)</td><td>Harder than copper, lighter color</td><td>Early weapons (Bronze Age)</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Low Tin Bronze</strong></td><td>95%</td><td>5%</td><td>Soft, good casting flow</td><td>Sculptures, decorative items</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Standard Bronze</strong></td><td>88%</td><td>12%</td><td>Hard, excellent casting, good ductility</td><td>Tools, weapons, bells, currency</td></tr><tr><td><strong>High Tin Bronze</strong></td><td>75%</td><td>25%</td><td>Very hard, brittle, silvery</td><td>Speculum (mirror bronze), bells</td></tr></tbody></table>

#### Bronze Smelting

1.  **Smelt tin ore first:** Cassiterite reduces easily at ~1200°C. Requires 2-3 kg charcoal per kg ore. Yield ~70%.
2.  **Separate copper:** If using mixed ore, may be more efficient to smelt copper and tin separately.
3.  **Mix in crucible:** Melt copper in crucible, then add calculated amount of tin. Stir with iron rod.
4.  **Timing:** Add tin last so it doesn't oxidize during copper heating. Tin melts at 232°C; copper at 1085°C. Both melt together into bronze.
5.  **Pour into mold:** Bronze flows well into detailed molds. Excellent for decorative work and statues.

:::tip
**Bronze Recipe (88/12):** For 100 kg bronze, melt 88 kg copper, add 12 kg tin. Melting point: ~1000°C. Stir constantly. Temperature control is critical—too hot and tin volatilizes, too cool and alloy won't flow.
:::

### Brass Making (Copper + Zinc)

Zinc is rare in pure form but exists in ores (sphalerite). Brass is made by heating copper with zinc-containing ore or zinc metal:

**Composition:** Typically 70% copper, 30% zinc. Melting point ~900°C.

**Properties:** Golden color, harder than pure copper, excellent corrosion resistance.

**Production:** Heat copper to ~1000°C, add zinc ore or zinc metal, stir. Zinc vaporizes easily—work quickly and in well-ventilated area (zinc oxide fumes are toxic).

**For Weapons:** Medium carbon steel (0.5-0.8% C). Quench in water, temper to purple (balance hardness/toughness).

## 8\. Cement & Lime Production

Lime and cement are crucial for building. Limestone (calcium carbonate) is heated to produce quicklime, which can be slaked (hydrated) into slaked lime. Portland cement is a modern formulation, but lime mortar works adequately.

### Lime Kiln Construction

![⛏ Mining &amp; Materials Processing diagram 5](../assets/svgs/mining-materials-5.svg)

### Lime Production Process

#### Step 1: Limestone Preparation

-   Quarry limestone rock (calcium carbonate, CaCO₃)
-   Break into chunks ~10-15 cm diameter
-   Remove clay and soil (makes quicklime weak)
-   High-purity limestone (chalk or marble) produces best quicklime

#### Step 2: Kiln Charging and Heating

1.  **Build kiln:** Stone or clay cylinder, 2-4 meters tall, 1-2 meter diameter. Leave ash pit at base with grate.
2.  **Grate placement:** Horizontal bars at bottom allow ash/quicklime to fall through but support fuel.
3.  **Fuel layer:** Charcoal at bottom, 1-2 meters thick
4.  **Limestone charge:** Load prepared limestone on top of fuel, 2-3 meters height
5.  **Ignition:** Light charcoal from bottom. Maintain continuous burn.
6.  **Duration:** 3-10 days depending on kiln size and limestone hardness
7.  **Temperature:** 900-1000°C required to fully decompose limestone

#### Step 3: Cooling and Collection

-   Once lime has been thoroughly heated (visible by color change to white and light texture), stop adding fuel
-   Allow slow cooling (2-3 days). Hot quicklime is dangerous—will burn skin.
-   Carefully remove cooled quicklime from ash pit
-   Store in dry location immediately. Quicklime is hygroscopic—absorbs water from air and becomes slaked

### Quicklime to Slaked Lime

**Chemical Reaction:** CaO (quicklime) + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ (slaked lime)

#### Slaking Process

1.  Place quicklime in pit or trough
2.  Add water slowly (exothermic reaction—generates heat!)
3.  Stir and add more water as quicklime hydrates
4.  Can produce steam—work carefully
5.  Final product is slaked lime paste
6.  Age for several weeks ("sweetening") before use in mortar improves quality

:::warning
**Safety:** Quicklime + water produces intense heat and steam. Never pour water on quicklime—add quicklime to water instead. Wear protection for skin and eyes.
:::

### Lime Mortar and Plaster

#### Basic Lime Mortar Recipe

<table><thead><tr><th scope="col">Component</th><th scope="col">Ratio</th><th scope="col">Notes</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Slaked Lime</td><td>1 part</td><td>By volume, aged paste</td></tr><tr><td>Sand</td><td>2.5-3 parts</td><td>Clean, coarse sand preferred. Sharp sand (angular) better than rounded.</td></tr><tr><td>Water</td><td>Add as needed</td><td>Mix to thick paste consistency</td></tr><tr><td>Additives (optional)</td><td>5-10%</td><td>Marble dust, crushed tile, animal hair for strength</td></tr></tbody></table>

#### Mortar Properties

-   **Setting time:** Several weeks to months depending on thickness and humidity
-   **Sets by carbonation:** Ca(OH)₂ + CO₂ → CaCO₃ (limestone again, permanently hard)
-   **Appearance:** Light gray to off-white
-   **Strength:** Lower than modern Portland cement, but adequate for most masonry

### Hydraulic Lime

Limestone with clay content (typically 15-30% clay) produces hydraulic lime that sets faster than pure lime:

-   **Composition:** Natural limestone-clay mix, common in some regions
-   **Heating:** Same process as pure limestone kiln
-   **Setting:** Sets both by carbonation AND by hydration of silicates—faster than pure lime
-   **Use:** Can be used for underwater or damp environments where pure lime fails

### Concrete Formulas

#### Simple Lime-Based Concrete

-   1 part slaked lime
-   3 parts coarse sand
-   3 parts gravel (5-10 cm diameter)
-   Mix with water to thick consistency
-   Place in forms, compact by hammering
-   Cure time: 4-8 weeks before use

:::tip
**Concrete Durability Tip:** Add crushed brick, tile, or volcanic ash (if available in region). The Romans added volcanic ash ("pozzolana") which made their concrete extremely durable, some lasting 2000+ years.
:::

<section id="salt-extraction">

## 9\. Salt Extraction

Salt is critical for food preservation (pickling, curing meat) and as a chemical raw material. Multiple sources: seawater, rock salt deposits, brine springs.

### Solar Evaporation from Seawater

Simplest method for coastal civilizations:

#### Process

1.  **Pond Construction:** Create shallow pond (15-30 cm deep) by building dikes. Slope floor gradually to collection sump.
2.  **Water Inlet:** Allow seawater to flow in, cap inlet during evaporation
3.  **Evaporation:** Sun evaporates water. In hot, dry climate, takes 3-6 months to concentrate salt.
4.  **Harvesting:** Once water is saturated (brine), carefully rake crystallized salt from pond bottom
5.  **Collection:** Gather salt, allow to drain and dry for 1-2 weeks
6.  **Washing (optional):** Rinse salt in fresh water if needed to remove impurities

**Yield:** ~1 kg salt per 30 kg seawater (~3% salt by weight). A 1-hectare pond can produce 1-2 tons salt per year in Mediterranean climate.

### Rock Salt Mining

Halite deposits (evaporite rock) exist globally:

-   **Detection:** Look for white, salty-tasting rock layers
-   **Extraction:** Same technique as other mining—shaft or open pit
-   **Processing:** Crush large crystals, dissolve in water if purity needed, re-evaporate
-   **Advantage:** No weather dependence, can mine year-round

### Brine Spring Extraction

Underground saltwater springs in some regions:

-   **Identification:** Salty springs often have white salt crusts around them
-   **Collection:** Dig shallow wells or pond to collect brine
-   **Evaporation:** Heat brine or let sun evaporate (both work)
-   **Boiling:** Direct heating can produce salt faster but consumes more fuel than solar method

### Salt Purification

Raw salt often contains magnesium salts, calcium sulfate, clay impurities:

#### Purification Steps

1.  **Dissolve in water:** Saturated salt solution. Most impurities remain suspended or sink.
2.  **Filter:** Pass through cloth or sand to remove solids
3.  **Boil:** Heat to evaporate water, leaving pure salt crystals
4.  **Dry:** Spread on cloth to dry completely before storage

:::tip
**Economic Importance:** Salt was so valuable in ancient times it was used as currency (Roman soldiers' "salarium" = salt payment). Control of salt sources meant economic power.
:::

<section id="sulfur-saltpeter">

## 10\. Sulfur & Saltpeter

Combined with charcoal, these two substances create blackpowder-blasting. Individually, sulfur is used for bleaching, fungicides, and chemistry. Saltpeter (potassium nitrate) is essential for food preservation and explosives.

### Sulfur (S)

#### Native Sulfur Sources

-   **Volcanic regions:** Hot springs, fumaroles, lava fields contain elemental sulfur deposits
-   **Appearance:** Bright yellow, soft, characteristic rotten egg smell
-   **Extraction:** Simply collect exposed sulfur deposits and crush
-   **Purification:** Melt in water (melting point 115°C), allow to cool. Impurities settle; sulfur solidifies on top of water layer. Collect and dry.

#### Sulfur from Pyrite (FeS₂)

More complex extraction for regions without native sulfur:

1.  Roast pyrite ore at 600-700°C
2.  Sulfur dioxide gas (SO₂) is driven off
3.  Collect in water or cool surface to condense
4.  Further processing (beyond early civilization scope) needed to convert SO₂ to elemental S

### Saltpeter (Potassium Nitrate, KNO₃)

#### Natural Deposits

-   **Cave deposits:** Found in soil of caves, especially those inhabited by animals (bird guano)
-   **Soil deposits:** Arid regions, especially where large herbivore herds grazed historically
-   **Appearance:** White crystalline deposits, bitter taste, hygroscopic (absorbs water)
-   **Extraction:** Collect soil containing saltpeter, leach with water, evaporate to get crystals

#### Saltpeter Production from Soil/Manure

Most practical method for non-volcanic regions:

1.  **Material Collection:** Animal manure (especially bird droppings), aged urine, soil from stable floors, straw heaps
2.  **Leaching:**
    -   Layer materials: wood ash (calcium hydroxide, Ca(OH)₂), manure, soil, wood ash
    -   Build layers in pit or wooden box with drainage hole at bottom
    -   Pour water slowly through layers (like a filter)
    -   Collect runoff—contains potassium nitrate
3.  **Boiling:** Boil collected liquid to concentrate nitrate salts
4.  **Crystallization:** As liquid cools, saltpeter crystallizes. Less soluble than common salt in cold water.
5.  **Collection:** Filter crystals, dry thoroughly
6.  **Yield:** Labor-intensive but yields 5-10 kg saltpeter per 1000 kg manure

**Saltpeter vs. Common Salt:** Critical difference—saltpeter has lower solubility in cold water than hot. When hot saltpeter solution cools, it crystallizes and can be collected. Salt is opposite (more soluble when hot). This allows separation of these chemically similar materials.

### Gunpowder Formulation

<table><thead><tr><th scope="col">Component</th><th scope="col">Percentage</th><th scope="col">Material</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Saltpeter (oxidizer)</td><td>75%</td><td>KNO₃, potassium nitrate</td></tr><tr><td>Charcoal (fuel)</td><td>15%</td><td>Finely ground charcoal</td></tr><tr><td>Sulfur (accelerant)</td><td>10%</td><td>Elemental sulfur powder</td></tr></tbody></table>

#### Gunpowder Mixing

-   **Grinding:** Each component ground separately to fine powder
-   **Mixing:** Combine in correct proportions. Traditional method: pound together in mortar with pestle.
-   **Safety:** Extremely dangerous. Friction, sparks, or heat ignites powder. Work in open air, use non-sparking tools (wood or bronze, never steel striking steel).
-   **Storage:** Keep components separate until ready to use. Gunpowder degrades over time, especially if exposed to humidity.
-   **Applications:** Mining (blasting), military (cannons, early firearms), fireworks

:::warning
**CRITICAL SAFETY WARNING:** Do not attempt to make blackpowder-blasting without understanding explosion mechanics. Uncontrolled explosive materials cause severe injuries and death. Only manufacture in appropriate facilities with proper ventilation, non-flammable surroundings, and trained personnel.
:::

## 11\. Clay, Sand & Stone

These abundant materials are foundations of civilization—clay for pottery and bricks, sand for glass and mortar, stone for building and tools.

### Clay

#### Identification and Testing

-   **Appearance:** Fine-grained, often colored red/brown/gray/white
-   **Feel:** Smooth, sticky when wet, hardens when dry
-   **Plasticity Test:** Roll wet clay into ball. If it holds shape and flexes without cracking, good plasticity
-   **Location:** Valley floors, river beds, eroded hillsides, old lake beds

#### Clay Properties and Uses

<table><thead><tr><th scope="col">Clay Type</th><th scope="col">Color</th><th scope="col">Firing Temp</th><th scope="col">Use</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td><strong>Earthenware</strong></td><td>Red-brown</td><td>1000-1100°C</td><td>Pottery, bricks, tiles</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Stoneware</strong></td><td>Gray-brown</td><td>1200-1300°C</td><td>Durable pottery, large vessels</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Kaolin (China Clay)</strong></td><td>White</td><td>1200-1450°C</td><td>Fine porcelain (requires high tech)</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Ball Clay</strong></td><td>Dark gray</td><td>1200-1250°C</td><td>Fine ceramics, high plasticity</td></tr></tbody></table>

#### Brick Making

1.  **Extraction:** Dig clay deposit, remove overburden
2.  **Preparation:** Knead clay thoroughly to remove air pockets. Add water as needed for workability.
3.  **Molding:** Press into wooden mold (25x12x6 cm standard), strike off excess
4.  **Drying:** Air-dry for 1-2 weeks. Must dry slowly to avoid cracking.
5.  **Firing:** Stack in kiln, fire to 900-1100°C for 3-7 days
6.  **Yield:** ~500-1000 bricks per kiln batch, depending on size

### Sand

#### Types and Sources

-   **River Sand:** Smooth, rounded grains, good for mortar and concrete
-   **Beach Sand:** Often contaminated with salt—unsuitable for building without washing
-   **Desert Sand:** Fine-grained, weak mortar properties—coarser sand preferred
-   **Sharp Sand:** Angular grains provide better mechanical grip in mortar/concrete

#### Sand for Glass Making

-   **Composition:** High silica content (SiO₂) essential. Ideally >95% silica.
-   **Purity:** Iron oxides create green/brown tint. White sand preferred.
-   **Extraction:** Beach deposits or specialized sand mines
-   **Melting:** Mix with flux (sodium carbonate/soda) and lime. Heat to ~1700°C. Requires very hot furnace.

### Stone

#### Stone Types and Properties

<table><thead><tr><th scope="col">Stone Type</th><th scope="col">Composition</th><th scope="col">Hardness</th><th scope="col">Workability</th><th scope="col">Uses</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td><strong>Granite</strong></td><td>Igneous (feldspar, quartz, mica)</td><td>6-7</td><td>Hard, difficult to shape</td><td>Building foundations, monuments, tools</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Limestone</strong></td><td>Sedimentary (calcium carbonate)</td><td>3-4</td><td>Soft, easy to shape</td><td>Building blocks, decorative carving</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Sandstone</strong></td><td>Sedimentary (cemented quartz)</td><td>6-7</td><td>Medium, varies by cementation</td><td>Building blocks, paving</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Slate</strong></td><td>Metamorphic (layered)</td><td>4-5</td><td>Splits easily along layers</td><td>Roofing tiles, paving, writing surfaces</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Basalt</strong></td><td>Igneous (dark, dense)</td><td>6-7</td><td>Very hard</td><td>Paving, tools, sculptures</td></tr></tbody></table>

#### Quarrying and Shaping

-   **Location:** Exposed outcrop or hillside
-   **Extraction:** Use picks and chisels to break along natural fractures
-   **Shaping:** Dress stones with hammer and chisel. Softer stones easier to shape.
-   **Transport:** Heavy blocks require levers, rollers, and ramps
-   **Labor-intensive:** One mason can shape ~2-4 building stones per day

:::tip
**Stone Selection for Building:** Granite is durable but hard to work. Limestone is easier but weathers over centuries. Sandstone is a compromise. Choose based on local availability and intended building lifespan.

## 12\. Materials Technology Tree

This flowchart shows the logical progression of material development from raw earth materials to advanced metallurgy. Early civilizations followed roughly this sequence.

![⛏ Mining &amp; Materials Processing diagram 6](../assets/svgs/mining-materials-6.svg)

### Development Priorities

**Year 1-2 (Survival):** Clay for pottery, stone tools, basic shelter materials. Focus on water containers and food storage.

**Year 2-3 (Foundational):** Charcoal production and lime kilns. These enable all subsequent development. Start bog iron collection if available.

**Year 3-5 (Bronze Age):** Build bloomery for iron, copper furnace for copper. Iron tools vastly improve efficiency of all other work.

**Year 5-10 (Iron Age Transition):** Develop steelmaking via cementation or crucible. Better tools, weapons, and infrastructure possible.

**Year 10+ (Industrial Foundation):** Gunpowder for mining, large-scale construction with reinforced concrete, advanced metalworking with precision tools and machinery.

### Critical Bottlenecks

-   **Charcoal Supply:** Most limiting resource. Requires enormous quantities of wood. Establish sustainable forestry early.
-   **Metallurgical Knowledge:** Trial and error is expensive (wasted ore, time, fuel). Written records of successful recipes invaluable.
-   **Ore Availability:** Lack of accessible ore deposits means slow progress. Geographic luck matters.
-   **Labor and Specialization:** Mining, smelting, blacksmithing require trained individuals. Cannot build all simultaneously with limited population.
-   **Fuel Source Transition:** Wood-based society will eventually deplete forests. Coal mining or long-distance transport needed for continued growth.
