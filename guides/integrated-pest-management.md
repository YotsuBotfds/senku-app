## Overview

Integrated Pest Management (IPM) is a data-driven approach to controlling crop pests using monitoring, action thresholds, beneficial insects, physical barriers, and pesticides (botanical, as a last resort) only when needed. IPM aims to maintain pest populations below economic damage thresholds while minimizing chemical input and environmental impact.

Unlike spray-at-first-sight chemical approaches, IPM requires regular field scouting, understanding pest life cycles, and deploying multiple control tactics simultaneously. This guide covers monitoring and action thresholds, beneficial insect habitat creation, trap crop deployment, biological control agents, physical barriers, botanical pesticide preparation, and record-keeping for multi-season management.

</section>

<section id="monitoring-thresholds">

## Monitoring, Scouting, and Action Thresholds

Pest populations fluctuate. IPM aims to control pests before they cause economic damage—not to achieve zero pests.

**Economic Threshold (ET):** The pest population level at which control costs less than crop damage. For example, a 10% loss of leafy greens from aphids (worth $100/acre) justifies control costs up to $100/acre. But a 5% loss (worth $50/acre) does not justify the same $100 control cost.

**Action Threshold (AT):** A practical number based on ET, set 10–30% below ET to allow time for control deployment before ET is reached. If ET is 10% plant damage and AT is 7%, you take action at 7% damage to prevent reaching 10%.

**Scouting protocol:**

1. **Frequency**: Scout crops every 3–7 days during the growing season (more frequently during high-risk periods like fruit set or flowering).
2. **Coverage**: Walk the entire field or garden. Use a zigzag pattern (not a circle) to sample different areas uniformly.
3. **Sample spots**: In a 1-acre field, inspect at least 10 plants/spots. In a home garden (1,000 sq ft), inspect 3–5 representative plants in different areas.
4. **Count method**: For aphids, mites, whiteflies (small insects): Count on 5 leaves per plant (top, bottom, and middle leaves), record the average count per leaf. For larger insects (beetles, caterpillars): Count visible individuals on the plant and surrounding area.
5. **Record data**: Write down the date, pest species, count/percentage affected, weather conditions, and any natural enemies observed. Over weeks, this data reveals trends (is the population growing, stable, or declining?).

**Action thresholds for common pests (temperate crops):**

| **Pest** | **Crop** | **AT (Action Threshold)** | **ET (Economic Threshold)** |
|----------|---------|--------------------------|---------------------------|
| Aphid | Leafy greens | 5–10 per leaf (average) | 15–20 per leaf (visible yellowing) |
| Aphid | Fruiting crops (tomato, pepper) | 2–5 per leaf | 10–15 per leaf |
| Spider mite | Most crops | 2–5 per leaf (early signs: stippling) | 10+ per leaf (severe yellowing) |
| Whitefly | Tomato, pepper, eggplant | 10–15 per leaf | 25+ per leaf (fruit drop) |
| Caterpillar (cabbage looper) | Brassica | 5% leaf damage | 10% leaf damage |
| Caterpillar (tomato hornworm) | Tomato | 1–2 visible individuals per 10 plants | 5+ individuals per 10 plants |
| Japanese beetle | Beans, grapes | 5% leaf damage | 15% leaf damage |

**At-threshold actions:**
- Deploy beneficial insects (see section below)
- Apply physical barriers (netting, row covers)
- Apply botanical pesticides if the above don't work within 5–7 days

:::info-box
Action thresholds are not absolutes. They vary by crop value, market standards (cosmetic damage tolerance), and regional pest pressure. A premium-market vegetable tolerates less damage than a commodity crop. Adjust thresholds based on your situation.
:::

<section id="beneficial-habitat">

## Beneficial Insect Habitat Creation

Beneficial insects—parasitic wasps, hoverflies, ladybugs, ground beetles, spiders—feed on pest insects. Providing habitat (flowering plants, shelter, water) encourages populations that provide free, continuous pest control.

**Nectar and pollen flowers (attract and feed beneficial adults):**

| **Flower** | **Bloom Period** | **Beneficial Insects Attracted** |
|-----------|-----------------|--------------------------------|
| Alyssum (sweet) | Continuous if deadheaded | Hoverflies, small wasps |
| Yarrow | Summer | Parasitic wasps, ladybugs, hoverflies |
| Dill | Spring/early summer | Parasitic wasps, ladybugs |
| Fennel | Summer | Parasitic wasps, hoverflies |
| Cilantro (allow to flower) | Late spring | Parasitic wasps, hoverflies |
| Marigold | Summer, continuous | Hoverflies, ladybugs |
| Borage | Early summer to frost | Bumblebees, hoverflies |
| Calendula | Summer | Hoverflies, ladybugs |
| Buckwheat | Summer (as cover crop) | Parasitic wasps, hoverflies, bees |
| Phacelia | Spring/summer | Parasitic wasps, hoverflies |
| Cosmos | Mid-summer to frost | Hoverflies, parasitic wasps |

**Deployment:**
- Plant beneficial flowers around crop perimeters and in corners of gardens.
- Allow some vegetable crops to flower (lettuce bolt, cilantro flower, dill left un-harvested) for beneficial insects.
- Space flowers 10–20 feet apart (within sight of pests for easy predation).

**Shelter (for overwintering and daytime refuge):**
- Leave dead plant stems standing through winter (parasitic wasps lay eggs in plant pith; dead stems protect pupae).
- Create a "bug hotel": Stack logs, bundle twigs, fill gaps with straw or leaves. Beetles and ground beetles shelter inside.
- Maintain mulch (2–3 inches) under trees and shrubs (ground beetles burrow in mulch).
- Avoid tilling or removing leaf litter; these actions expose and kill ground beetles and their pupae.

**Water source:**
- Provide shallow water (saucer with pebbles or gravel, filled to just below pebble height). Insects drink from pebbles, not open water (prevents drowning).
- Place 1–2 water sources per 1000 sq ft of garden.

:::tip
The most effective beneficial habitat is one integrated into the crop area itself (not isolated to a distant corner). Beneficial insects must encounter pests within a few minutes of emergence. A gap of more than 30 feet from flowers to crop means slower predation.
:::

<section id="trap-crops">

## Trap Crop Selection and Deployment

Trap crops (also called sacrificial or lure crops) attract pests away from the main crop, concentrating pests in an area where they can be easily removed or killed.

**Common trap crop deployments:**

| **Main Crop** | **Pest** | **Trap Crop** | **Deployment** |
|---------------|---------|---------------|---------------|
| Squash, cucumber | Squash bug, cucumber beetle | Nasturtium | Plant around the perimeter; insects lay eggs in nasturtium; remove vines before eggs hatch (or allow as food sacrifice) |
| Cabbage, broccoli | Cabbage moth | Mustard greens, turnip | Plant 3–6 feet from main crop; moths prefer trap crop; hand-remove infested leaves |
| Bean | Bean beetle | Nasturtium, marigold | Beetles attract to orange/yellow flowers; inspect trap plants daily; remove beetles |
| Tomato, pepper | Aphid | Nasturtium | Plant nearby; aphids concentrate on nasturtium; strong water spray dislodges them (they die or move to nasturtium) |
| Lettuce | Slugs | Wetted burlap, cardboard | Place moist burlap under or near lettuce; slugs hide beneath; overturn and remove daily |

**Trap crop management:**
1. **Placement**: Position trap crops upwind (if prevalent wind) or at edges where pests encounter them before the main crop.
2. **Monitoring**: Scout trap crops every 2–3 days. If pests are abundant, manually remove (by hand-picking, water spray, pruning infested leaves).
3. **Timing**: Trap crops are most effective when planted slightly before the main crop (so they're more attractive when pests arrive) or when pest populations are low (before they establish on the main crop).
4. **Sacrifice strategy**: Some trap crops are planted as a sacrifice—they concentrate pests, which are then removed (sometimes the whole trap plant is removed and burned/buried). This is acceptable if the trap crop is undesirable or less valuable.

:::info-box
Trap crops work best for mobile, actively-seeking pests (squash bugs, moths). For sedentary pests (mites, whiteflies), trap crops are less effective; use beneficial insects instead.
:::

</section>

<section id="biological-control">

## Biological Control Agents

Biological control uses parasitic insects and pathogens to kill pests. Some agents are naturally present in gardens; others can be purchased and released.

**Parasitic wasps (parasitoids):**
These wasps lay eggs inside pest insects. The wasp larva develops inside the host, killing it. Parasitoids are specific to certain pests.

| **Parasitoid** | **Hosts** | **Release Timing** | **Cost** |
|----------------|---------|-------------------|---------|
| Trichogramma (tiny wasp) | Lepidoptera eggs (moths, caterpillars) | When first moths appear; repeat weekly for 3–4 weeks | $10–20 per 1000 parasitoids |
| Aphidius (small wasp) | Aphids | When aphids first appear; repeat weekly if infestation persists | $15–30 per 2000 wasps |
| Encarsia formosa | Whitefly nymphs | When whiteflies appear; repeat weekly | $15–40 per 1000 parasitoids |
| Phytoseiulus persimilis (mite) | Spider mites | When mites appear | $20–50 per 1000 mites |

**Predatory insects:**

| **Predator** | **Prey** | **Notes** | **Cost** |
|------------|--------|----------|---------|
| Ladybug (Hippodamia) | Aphids, scale insects, mites | Release 500–1000 per 1000 sq ft; many fly away immediately. Plant native shrubs to encourage wild populations. | $10–20 per 500 |
| Green lacewing | Aphids, whitefly, caterpillar eggs | Larvae are voracious; adults need nectar/pollen. Plant beneficial flowers. | $15–30 per 1000 eggs |
| Ground beetles | Slugs, snails, root maggots | Promote by maintaining mulch and avoiding tilling. Purchase only if native species are absent. | $5–15 per 1000 |

**Microbial agents:**

| **Agent** | **Pest** | **Application** | **Notes** |
|----------|---------|-----------------|----------|
| Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt) | Lepidoptera larvae (caterpillars) | Spray on foliage; pest must ingest it | Organic-approved; kills target pests in 2–3 days; do not spray when beneficial wasps are active (parasitoids may ingest Bt) |
| Beauveria bassiana (fungal) | Whitefly, spider mites, beetles | Spray diluted as directed | Works at 60–80°F; requires high humidity (mist after application); 5–7 days to kill pest |
| Isaria fumosorosea (fungal) | Whitefly | Spray or drench as directed | Similar to Beauveria; moderate effectiveness |

**Release protocols:**
1. **Purchase from certified suppliers** only (ensures viability and species purity).
2. **Release early**: Deploy biological control agents at low pest populations (10–20% of AT). If the pest population is already at 50% damage, predators cannot catch up.
3. **Repeat releases**: Single releases rarely establish permanent populations. Release weekly for 3–4 weeks, or release larger numbers initially (500–1000 per 1000 sq ft).
4. **Avoid insecticides**: Pesticides (even botanical) kill beneficial insects. If applying Bt or botanical pesticide, do so only at night (when beneficial insects are less active) or stop releases 2 weeks before and resume 2 weeks after.
5. **Habitat support**: Plant beneficial flowers simultaneously with releases. Predators need pollen and nectar to survive.

## Physical Barriers and Exclusion

Physical barriers prevent pests from reaching crops without harming plants.

**Row covers (lightweight, floating):**
- **Material**: Nonwoven polypropylene (0.5–1.5 oz/yd²).
- **Application**: Drape over crops immediately after planting, bury edges in soil or weight with boards. Pests cannot penetrate.
- **Crops**: Effective for brassica (cabbage moth), cucumber/melon (beetles), squash (early growth).
- **Removal timing**: Remove when plants flower (pollinators must access flowers) or when pests are no longer a risk (typically 4–6 weeks after planting).
- **Cost**: $0.10–0.30 per sq ft; reusable for 3–5 seasons.

**Insect netting (fine mesh, not row cover):**
- **Material**: Polyethylene or nylon mesh, 1/8-inch or smaller holes.
- **Application**: Similar to row covers; drape and bury edges.
- **Crops**: Effective for high-value crops (berries, early vegetables) where pest pressure is intense.
- **Longevity**: Reusable; lasts 5–10 years if stored carefully.
- **Cost**: $0.50–1.00 per sq ft.

**Floating barriers (lightweight structures):**
- **Collars around seedlings**: Cardboard tubes (2–3 inches diameter, 2 inches tall) placed around transplants prevent cutworm damage (cutworms cannot cross the barrier).
- **Copper tape on containers**: Slugs and snails avoid copper. Apply 1-inch-wide copper tape around the rim of raised beds. Cost: $0.05 per linear foot.

**Trunk wraps and banding:**
- **Codling moth (fruit trees)**: Wrap tree trunk with burlap or corrugated cardboard (3–4 inches wide) in late summer. Codling moth larvae crawl down the trunk to pupate in the soil; they hide under the wrap. Remove wrap in fall and destroy larvae.

**Sticky traps and barriers:**
- **Sticky traps**: Yellow or blue boards coated with a sticky substance, hung at canopy height. Catch flying insects (whitefly, aphid fliers, some beetles).
  - Yellow: Attracts whitefly, aphid fliers, small beetles.
  - Blue: Attracts thrips, some beetles.
  - Cost: $2–5 per trap; reusable with fresh coating.
- **Sticky bands (tree bands)**: Applied around tree trunks to prevent crawling pests (ants, flightless beetles). Cost: $0.10–0.30 per linear foot.

:::warning
Sticky traps also catch beneficial insects. Minimize trap numbers (use only as monitoring tools or in high-pest-pressure situations) and remove traps when beneficial insects are active.
:::

## Botanical Pesticide Preparation and Use

Botanical pesticides (derived from plants) are organic-approved and less toxic than synthetic chemicals, but they require proper preparation and application timing.

**Neem oil (from neem tree seeds):**
- **Pests**: Aphids, spider mites, whitefly, Japanese beetle, some caterpillars.
- **Preparation**: Purchase concentrated neem oil. Dilute per label (typically 1–2% concentration): Mix 1 tablespoon neem oil + 1 tablespoon mild liquid soap (soap is an emulsifier) per gallon of water. Shake well before each use.
- **Application**: Spray in early morning or late evening (heat reduces efficacy). Spray thoroughly, covering tops and undersides of leaves. Repeat every 7 days for 2–3 applications.
- **Efficacy**: Neem disrupts insect hormone systems; kills pests over 3–5 days. Does not kill on contact; requires ingestion or absorption. Moderately effective.

**Pyrethrum (from pyrethrum daisy flowers):**
- **Pests**: Soft-bodied insects (aphids, whitefly), beetles, caterpillars.
- **Preparation**: Purchase food-grade pyrethrin (extract of pyrethrum flowers) or pyrethrum spray concentrates. Dilute per label (typically 0.5–1% pyrethrin).
- **Application**: Spray in late evening (breaks down rapidly in sunlight). Repeat every 3–5 days as needed.
- **Efficacy**: Pyrethrin paralyzes insects; kills on contact within hours. Fast-acting but requires thorough coverage. Breaks down quickly (low persistence—safe for beneficial insects if applied at night when they're inactive).

**Rotenone (from derris/cube root):**
- **Pests**: Beetles, caterpillars, aphids, some spider mites. Particularly effective on Japanese beetles.
- **Preparation**: Purchase rotenone dust or spray concentrate. Mix per label (typically 0.5–1% rotenone in spray form).
- **Application**: Spray in early morning or late evening. Can be dusted directly on plants (rotenone dust). Repeat every 5–7 days.
- **Efficacy**: Rotenone is a neurotoxin; kills insects over 12–24 hours. Moderate persistence (2–3 days). More toxic than neem or pyrethrum; use as a last resort.
- **Caution**: Rotenone is mildly toxic to mammals; avoid inhalation and skin contact. Some regions restrict rotenone use; check local regulations.

**Sulfur powder (fungal and mite control):**
- **Pests**: Spider mites (and mite eggs), powdery mildew (fungal disease, not a pest insect).
- **Preparation**: Purchase wettable sulfur powder or dusting sulfur. Mix wettable sulfur per label (typically 2–4% sulfur) in water to form a suspension.
- **Application**: Spray or dust on foliage. Apply when temperature is below 85°F (above 85°F, sulfur may burn foliage). Do not apply within 2 weeks of oil sprays (combination may burn leaves).
- **Efficacy**: Sulfur kills mites and mite eggs on contact; very effective. Also prevents fungal diseases. Safe for beneficial insects.

**Oil sprays (horticultural oils: neem, mineral, or paraffinic oil):**
- **Pests**: Soft-scale insects, spider mites (egg and adult), aphids.
- **Preparation**: Purchase horticultural oil concentrate (1–3% mineral or paraffinic oil). Mix per label; typically 1–2% final concentration.
- **Application**: Spray thoroughly, covering all foliage. Apply in early spring (dormant oil, before buds break) or in late fall (post-harvest, cleaner application). Summer applications risk leaf burn if temperature exceeds 85°F.
- **Efficacy**: Oil coats insect bodies, blocking respiration; kills within 2–3 days. Very effective but requires thorough coverage.

**Soap spray (potassium fatty acid salts):**
- **Pests**: Soft-bodied insects (aphids, whitefly, spider mites).
- **Preparation**: Purchase insecticidal soap concentrate. Mix per label (typically 1–2% soap).
- **Application**: Spray thoroughly. Repeat every 5–7 days. Soap must make direct contact with pests (no residual activity).
- **Efficacy**: Soap disrupts insect cell membranes; kills on contact within hours. Very safe, low toxicity to mammals and beneficial insects.

**Application guidelines:**
1. **Scout before spraying**: Confirm pest population is above AT before applying any pesticide.
2. **Timing**: Apply early morning or late evening to avoid sun degradation and harm to beneficial insects.
3. **Coverage**: Spray thoroughly, especially undersides of leaves (where pests hide).
4. **Repeat schedule**: Most botanical pesticides persist 5–10 days. Repeat weekly for 2–3 applications if the pest population is still above AT.
5. **Stop spraying when beneficial insects increase**: Once parasitic wasps, ladybugs, or predatory mites are abundant, reduce spraying frequency. Let natural enemies manage pests.

<section id="record-keeping">

## Record-Keeping for Multi-Season Management

IPM success depends on tracking pest pressures and control tactics over multiple seasons. Records reveal patterns and improve decision-making.

**Scouting record template:**

```
Date: [Date]
Weather: [Temperature, wind, recent rain]
Field/Garden: [Location ID]
Pest Species: [e.g., Aphid (Aphis gossypii)]
Plant Stage: [Seedling, flowering, fruit set, etc.]
Sample Size: [Number of plants scouted]
Count/Severity: [e.g., 3 aphids/leaf (average), 5% leaf damage]
Action Taken: [Released wasps, applied neem, installed row cover, etc.]
Notes: [Beneficial insects observed, weather forecast, next action planned]
```

**Multi-year trend tracking:**

Keep records in a spreadsheet or notebook over years. Over time, patterns emerge:
- Which pests appear at which plant growth stages and calendar dates?
- Which control tactics are most cost-effective?
- Are pest populations increasing year-over-year (signal of control failure)?
- Which crops are most affected by each pest?

**Example trend (aphids on tomato over 4 years):**

```
Year 1: Aphids detected July 15 at 2 per leaf. Released parasitic wasps; aphids declined to <1 per leaf by August 1.
Year 2: Aphids detected July 20 at 4 per leaf. Released same wasps; declined by August 5.
Year 3: Aphids detected July 10 at 10 per leaf. Released wasps + applied neem; took 2 weeks to control.
Year 4: Aphids detected June 25 at 8 per leaf. Trend suggests earlier appearance and higher initial populations.
Action: For Year 5, release parasitic wasps in mid-June (2 weeks earlier) and plant beneficial flowers earlier.
```

:::info-box
Good record-keeping transforms IPM from reactive (spray when you see pests) to proactive (plan controls based on historical patterns). Invest 15 minutes per week in scouting and recording; the insights pay for themselves in reduced pesticide use and better harvests.
:::

## Regional Pest Threshold Variants

Action thresholds for pests vary significantly by climate region. Temperature, humidity, and growing season length affect pest population dynamics, lifecycles, and economic impact. Understanding regional variants allows IPM decisions to be adapted to local conditions.

### Temperate Climate Zone Thresholds

**Characteristics:** 4 distinct seasons; cold winters limit pest overwintering; moderate to cool growing season (4-6 months frost-free)

**Environmental factors affecting thresholds:**
- Winter cold kills many overwintering pests (reduced pest carryover year-to-year)
- Spring population buildup is slower; pests reach peak populations mid-late summer
- Fall freeze kills active pests; late-season damage is self-limiting

**Typical temperate thresholds (baseline reference):**

| **Pest** | **Crop** | **AT (Temperate)** | **Peak Activity** | **Notes** |
|----------|---------|-------------------|-------------------|----------|
| Aphid | Leafy greens | 5-10 per leaf | July-August | Lower early season; populations build slowly |
| Spider mite | General | 2-5 per leaf | August-September | Heat/drought promotes; rarely problematic in cool springs |
| Cabbage moth | Brassica | 5% leaf damage | June-July (generation 1) | Multiple generations; repeat scouting critical |
| Japanese beetle | Beans | 5% leaf damage | July (peak) | Adults active only 4-6 weeks; severe damage concentrated |

### Subtropical Climate Zone Thresholds

**Characteristics:** Long frost-free season (8-10 months); warm year-round; minimal winter pest mortality; high humidity

**Environmental factors affecting thresholds:**
- Year-round pest reproduction; 3-4 generations possible for many pests
- Winter cold does not interrupt pest populations
- High humidity favors fungal pathogens and soft-bodied insect pests
- Growing season extends through winter; winter crops equally vulnerable to pests

**Adjusted thresholds for subtropical regions:**

| **Pest** | **Crop** | **AT (Subtropical)** | **Peak Activity** | **Notes** |
|----------|---------|----------------------|-------------------|----------|
| Aphid | Leafy greens | 3-5 per leaf | Year-round; peaks spring/fall | Threshold LOWER because populations never crash; continuous control needed |
| Spider mite | General | 1-2 per leaf | Year-round; peaks dry season | Threshold LOWER; outbreaks common in low-humidity periods |
| Whitefly | Solanums | 5-10 per leaf | Year-round | Multiple generations overlapping; early intervention critical |
| Scale insect | Perennial crops | 2-5 per shoot | Year-round | Overwinters actively; no seasonal relief |
| Caterpillar (lepidoptera) | Brassica | 3-5% leaf damage | Spring/fall (cooler months) | Summer heat provides natural control; thresholds higher summer |

**Key difference:** Subtropical thresholds are LOWER across the board because pest populations never decline seasonally. A pest that would naturally crash in winter must be managed aggressively year-round.

### Tropical Climate Zone Thresholds

**Characteristics:** No true winter; warm/hot year-round; high humidity; 2+ rainy seasons; year-round growth

**Environmental factors affecting thresholds:**
- Year-round reproduction with 4-6 generations annually for many pests
- Humidity favors fungal/microbial biocontrol agents BUT also favors fungal plant diseases
- Alternating wet/dry seasons; pest populations fluctuate with rainfall
- Biodiversity is high; many natural enemies present but also many pests

**Adjusted thresholds for tropical regions:**

| **Pest** | **Crop** | **AT (Tropical)** | **Peak Activity** | **Notes** |
|----------|---------|-------------------|-------------------|----------|
| Aphid | General | 2-3 per leaf | Dry season (lower humidity suppresses) | Threshold VERY LOW; pest pressure constant |
| Spider mite | General | <1 per leaf | Dry season | Threshold LOWEST of all regions; dry conditions explosive populations |
| Whitefly | Solanums | 3-5 per leaf | Year-round | Very low thresholds; rapid population growth in heat |
| Leaf-cutter ant | Perennial crops | 1-2% defoliation | Wet season | Threshold MUCH LOWER; major pest in tropics; early intervention prevents colony establishment |
| Fruit fly | Fruiting crops | 0 (zero tolerance) | Year-round; peaks wet season | Economic threshold is zero; any infestation reduces export marketability |
| Fungal disease (fungal, not insect) | Cacao, coffee | Preventive spray | Wet season | High humidity makes fungal diseases more damaging than insects; preventive approach standard |

**Key difference:** Tropical thresholds are lowest because pest populations are never suppressed by cold; humidity supports both pests AND biocontrol agents; management is proactive rather than reactive.

### Arid/Semi-Arid Climate Zone Thresholds

**Characteristics:** Low rainfall; high temperatures; low humidity; limited growing season (irrigation-dependent); extreme seasonal temperature variation

**Environmental factors affecting thresholds:**
- Low humidity favors spider mites, whiteflies, aphids (low natural mortality)
- High temperatures speed insect development (shorter generation time)
- Irrigation creates localized high-humidity pockets (fungal disease risk)
- Water scarcity limits effectiveness of water-based cooling controls (spray, sponging)
- Winter dormancy provides some pest-free period (unlike tropics/subtropics)

**Adjusted thresholds for arid regions:**

| **Pest** | **Crop** | **AT (Arid)** | **Peak Activity** | **Notes** |
|----------|---------|---------------|-------------------|----------|
| Spider mite | All crops | 1-2 per leaf | Early summer (dry, hot) | Very low threshold; explosive population growth in heat/drought |
| Whitefly | Solanums, cucurbits | 3-5 per leaf | Summer (hottest months) | Threshold LOW; high temperature accelerates reproduction |
| Aphid | General | 5-10 per leaf | Spring/fall | Threshold HIGHER than humid regions (lower pest pressure in extreme heat) |
| Scale insect | Perennial crops | 2-4 per shoot | Spring/early summer | Dry conditions favors some scales; limited water for organic control methods |
| Thrips | Peppers, onions | 5-10 per flower | Summer | Threshold MODERATE; heat accelerates reproduction |

**Key difference:** Arid thresholds reflect the dominance of spider mites and whiteflies (dry-favoring pests); water-based controls are less effective (water scarcity); integrated approach must rely on beneficial insects and resistant varieties rather than spray-based tactics.

### Using Regional Thresholds: Decision Logic

When adjusting thresholds for your region:

1. **Identify your climate zone** (or nearest analog) from descriptions above
2. **Note major differences from temperate baseline:**
   - Subtropical: Thresholds LOWER (year-round pest pressure)
   - Tropical: Thresholds MUCH LOWER (constant reproduction, high humidity)
   - Arid: Thresholds VARIABLE (mites/whiteflies low, others moderate)
3. **For unknown pests:** Start with temperate thresholds, then adjust downward if pest pressure develops quickly (sign of favorable climate) or upward if populations naturally collapse (sign of unfavorable climate)
4. **Record local pest history:** Over 2-3 seasons, document when each pest appears, peak populations, natural decline timing. This empirical data becomes more valuable than any published threshold
5. **Consult local growers:** Experienced farmers in your region have refined thresholds through generations of trial. Their guidance is invaluable

:::tip
**Regional Calibration Rule:** If your region's climate is between two zones (e.g., warm-temperate-transitioning-to-subtropical), use thresholds from the warmer zone and adjust upward slightly. Over-caution is safer than under-control when pest pressure is unknown.
:::

### Climate Change Impact on Regional Thresholds

Climate change is shifting pest ranges and lifecycles. Regions experiencing:
- **Warming trends:** Use subtropical/tropical thresholds (pests extending range northward)
- **Increased humidity:** Lower thresholds (favors fungal pests, soft-bodied insects)
- **Increased rainfall variability:** Prepare for both wet-season (fungal disease) and dry-season (spider mite/whitefly) surges
- **Extended growing seasons:** Anticipate additional pest generations; plan year-round management

Adaptive IPM requires updating thresholds every few years as regional climate patterns shift.

<section id="seasonal-plan">

## Seasonal IPM Plan Example (Temperate Vegetable Garden)

**Spring (April–May):**
- Plant beneficial flowers (alyssum, calendula, dill, phacelia) throughout the garden.
- Install row covers on brassicas and cucurbit seedlings immediately after planting.
- Scout weekly for cabbage moth eggs and aphids.
- If aphids appear, release parasitic wasps (Aphidius).

**Early summer (June–July):**
- Remove row covers when plants flower.
- Release whitefly parasitoids (Encarsia) if whiteflies appear on tomato or pepper.
- Deploy sticky traps to monitor flying insects (whitefly, aphids, beetles).
- Scout for spider mites on beans and squash; if AT is reached (2–5 per leaf), apply sulfur or release predatory mites.
- Deploy trap crops (nasturtium near squash, mustard greens near cabbage).

**Mid-summer (July–August):**
- Maintain beneficial habitat (deadhead flowers for continuous bloom; maintain mulch).
- If trap crops are heavily infested, remove and destroy to reduce pest populations.
- Scout for whitefly on solanums (tomato, pepper, eggplant). If AT is reached (10–15 per leaf), apply oil spray or neem, followed by parasitoid release 1 week later.
- Maintain sticky traps as monitoring tools; replace when saturated.

**Fall (September–October):**
- Plant cover crops (if not extending tomato/pepper harvest).
- Scout for late-season caterpillar and beetle activity; apply Bt if caterpillars appear.
- Harvest and remove crop residue promptly (prevents pest overwintering habitat).
- Plant beneficial flowers that bloom into fall (cosmos, calendula).

**Winter (November–February):**
- Plan next year's IPM strategy based on records.
- Clean and store row covers, netting, and stakes.
- Maintain bug hotels and beneficial insect habitat.
