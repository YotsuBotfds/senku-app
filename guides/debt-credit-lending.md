<section id="credit-necessity">

## Why Credit Matters in Rebuilding Economies

Credit is not a luxury — it is a necessity for economic recovery. Without access to credit, a society cannot:

- **Finance seasonal gaps** — Farmers need seed before harvest; without credit, they cannot plant
- **Start enterprises** — A blacksmith needs materials before revenue; without credit, no craft economy develops
- **Recover from disruption** — A trader loses goods to disaster; without credit, recovery stalls
- **Invest in improvements** — Building a kiln, constructing a well, or training apprentices requires capital upfront

In post-crisis settings, the first communities to develop functional credit systems rebuild fastest. Conversely, communities that only allow barter (direct exchange) see economic stagnation because timing mismatches and indivisible goods create deadlock.

:::tip
Credit does not require money. Grain can be loaned and repaid in grain. Tools can be loaned and returned. Labor can be advanced and repaid later.
:::

</section>

<section id="collateral-based-lending">

## Collateral-Based Lending

Collateral reduces lender risk by providing something of value if the borrower defaults.

### Acceptable Collateral

| Type | Value Stability | Liquidity | Best Use |
|------|-----------------|-----------|----------|
| Land / Property | High | Low (months to sell) | Long-term loans |
| Livestock | Medium | Medium (weeks to sell) | Medium-term loans |
| Grain / Stored Goods | Medium | High (quick sale) | Short-term loans |
| Tools / Equipment | Low | Low | Secured against tools themselves |
| Jewelry / Valuables | High | High | Emergency credit |
| Debt Certificates | Variable | Low | Pledged against future income |

### Loan-to-Value (LTV) Ratio

Lend conservatively against collateral value. If an item is worth 100 kg grain, lend maximum 70–80 kg grain. The buffer protects against:

- **Market price drops** — Collateral value falls unexpectedly
- **Deterioration** — Livestock dies; grain moldy; tools rust
- **Liquidity cost** — Selling collateral quickly often requires 10–20% discount

### Collateral Documentation

Create a written record (in simple form):

**COLLATERAL PLEDGE**
- Borrower: [Name], [Community]
- Lender: [Name], [Community]
- Collateral: [Description, quantity, condition assessment]
- Collateral stored at: [Location]
- Collateral value: [Amount and measurement]
- Loan amount: [Amount]
- Repayment terms: [Date, quantity, form]
- Default terms: [If unpaid by X date, collateral may be sold or transferred]
- Witness signatures: [Two neutral parties]

Store collateral safely. Conduct monthly inspections (for livestock/goods) and document condition changes.

<section id="community-credit-unions">

## Community Credit Unions

A credit union pools resources from multiple lenders, reducing individual risk and centralizing management.

### Structure

A small credit union operates with:

1. **Pool master** — One trusted person managing all records, storing collateral, tracking repayments
2. **Oversight committee** — 3–5 community members reviewing large loans and disputes
3. **Members** — Individuals who both contribute to and borrow from the pool

### Operation Model

**Deposits:** Members contribute surplus resources (grain, metal, goods, or time in maintenance). These become loanable capital.

**Lending:** When members request loans, the committee evaluates collateral, purpose, and repayment ability. Loans come from the pool, not individual lenders.

**Repayment:** Members repay loans to the pool. Interest or dividends are distributed to depositors.

**Failure Management:** If a borrower defaults, the collateral is sold. If the pool runs short, members may share the loss proportionally, or deposits are frozen until recovery.

### Interest/Dividend Rates

For a small community:
- **Deposit dividend:** 5–10% annually (borrowers' interest covers this)
- **Borrow rate:** 10–20% annually (varies by loan duration and default risk)

These rates seem high to modern ears but reflect genuine risk in pre-industrial settings.

:::warning
Prevent corruption by rotating the pool master every 2–3 years and requiring the committee to witness all major transactions.
:::

<section id="interest-rate-setting">

## Setting Interest Rates

Interest compensates the lender for:

1. **Opportunity cost** — Capital lent cannot be used elsewhere
2. **Inflation** — Goods/money may lose value during the loan period
3. **Default risk** — The loan might not be repaid
4. **Administrative cost** — Record-keeping, collateral storage, monitoring

### Risk-Based Rates

Base rates on loan characteristics:

| Loan Type | Duration | Collateral | Interest Rate |
|-----------|----------|-----------|---|
| Secured, seasonal (3 months) | Short | 100%+ LTV | 8–10% |
| Secured, medium-term (12 months) | Medium | 70% LTV | 12–15% |
| Secured, long-term (3+ years) | Long | 50% LTV | 15–20% |
| Unsecured (character/signature) | Any | None | 25–50% or refused |

**Default risk premiums:** If a borrower has previously defaulted, charge 5–10% higher rates (or require better collateral).

### Inflation Adjustment

If goods are losing value rapidly (grain depreciates 2% monthly in humid storage), increase interest rates to compensate. This is not greed; it reflects real cost.

<section id="loan-documentation">

## Loan Documentation

Proper written terms prevent disputes and enable enforcement.

### Essential Loan Agreement

Use a simple template:

**LOAN AGREEMENT**

- **Date:** [Date issued]
- **Borrower:** [Name, seal/mark]
- **Lender:** [Name, seal/mark]
- **Principal amount:** [Quantity, measurement]
- **Form of principal:** [Grain, metal, tools, etc.]
- **Purpose (if relevant):** [Spring planting, tool purchase, etc.]
- **Collateral:** [Description, value, location]
- **Term:** [Date due]
- **Interest:** [Amount and percentage]
- **Repayment form:** [Same as principal, or equivalent value?]
- **Penalty for late payment:** [Additional interest, collateral forfeiture, other]
- **Default conditions:** [When is loan considered defaulted?]
- **Witness:** [Two independent signatures]

Both borrower and lender retain copies. The pool master or community clerk keeps a third copy in the archive.

:::tip
If borrowers cannot write, use marks or seals (an impressed pattern unique to each person) instead of signatures.
:::

</section>

<section id="default-collection">

## Default and Collection

When a borrower fails to repay, the process is:

1. **Formal notice** — Lender notifies borrower in writing (or publicly) that payment is overdue
2. **Cure period** — Give 14–30 days to catch up before enforcement (allows for harvest delays, illness)
3. **Collateral seizure** — If loan is not repaid, the agreed collateral is claimed and stored
4. **Public sale** — Collateral is sold at market; proceeds cover the loan and costs
5. **Surplus/deficit** — If sale exceeds debt, surplus is returned to borrower. If shortfall, borrower may owe the difference

### Preventing Abuse

- Require two witnesses for collateral seizure (prevents lender theft)
- Conduct sale publicly with multiple bidders (ensures fair market price)
- Document all sale proceeds and disbursements
- Allow dispute resolution (borrower can contest if terms were violated)

<section id="grain-banks">

## Grain Banks and Seed Lending

In agricultural societies, grain banks are institutions providing both food security and agricultural credit.

### Function

A community collects surplus grain during good harvests and stores it in a secure facility (granary). During poor harvests or off-season, community members can:

1. **Borrow grain to eat** — Repaid in grain after the next harvest, with 10–15% interest
2. **Borrow seed for planting** — Repaid in grain at harvest, with 20–30% interest (higher because seed loss is a risk)

### Operation

- **Year 1 (good harvest):** Community contributes 15% of surplus grain to the bank. Total: 500 kg
- **Year 2 (poor harvest):** Farmer borrows 100 kg seed grain, agrees to repay 120 kg after harvest
- **Year 2 (post-harvest):** Farmer repays 120 kg; bank total is now 480 kg (used 100, gained 120, minus some loss to pests/spoilage)
- **Year 3+:** Bank grows or stabilizes depending on harvest variability

### Rules to Prevent Collapse

- **Minimum reserve ratio:** Never lend more than 60% of stored grain (keep 40% emergency reserves)
- **Loan limits:** No individual can borrow more than 25% of current inventory
- **Defaults:** Develop a mercy rule for true crop failure; strict enforcement for negligence or fraud

:::danger
Grain banks are vulnerable to:
- **Theft** — Secure the granary structure and limit access
- **Corruption** — Rotate bank managers; require committee witnesses
- **Pests/spoilage** — Monitor storage conditions monthly
- **Over-lending** — Enforce reserve ratios strictly
:::

</section>

<section id="tool-lending">

## Tool Lending Libraries

Specialized tools (anvil, weaving loom, printing press, cheese mold) are expensive and often underused. A tool library allows community members to borrow at low cost.

### Model

- **Ownership:** Community or cooperative holds tools
- **Membership:** Anyone can join (fee: small annual contribution, e.g., 5 kg grain)
- **Borrowing:** Check out tool for 7–14 days, free or at very low cost (1 kg grain per month)
- **Return:** Tool must be returned clean and in good condition
- **Damage:** If borrower breaks tool, they pay repair/replacement cost

### Benefits

- Reduces capital burden on individuals
- Increases tool utilization (one anvil serves 10 blacksmiths instead of each owning one)
- Encourages new crafts (people try activities without owning expensive tools)

### Management

Maintain a logbook:

| Date Out | Borrower | Tool | Date In | Condition | Notes |
|----------|----------|------|---------|-----------|-------|
| 2026-02-01 | Kasia | Grain mill | 2026-02-08 | Good | Returns clean |
| 2026-02-08 | Marcus | Grain mill | 2026-02-15 | Worn | Needs oiling |

Perform maintenance monthly. Keep spare parts on hand for wear items.

<section id="microcredit">

## Microcredit Principles

Microcredit extends small loans to individuals too poor to access traditional collateral, used to start or expand microenterprises.

### Eligibility

- Limited collateral (often just a personal asset or group guarantee)
- Very small loan amounts (10–100 kg equivalent value)
- Clear income-generating purpose (trader, crafter, smallholder)

### Loan Guarantee

Instead of collateral, borrowers form groups of 5–10 peers. Each member guarantees the others' loans:

- If one member defaults, the group must cover the debt
- This social pressure creates accountability without collateral
- Peer lending also allows group members to discuss business challenges

### Typical Microcredit Loan

| Feature | Amount |
|---------|--------|
| Loan size | 20–50 kg grain equivalent |
| Term | 3–12 months |
| Interest rate | 20–30% |
| Repayment | Weekly or monthly small amounts |
| Group size | 5 members |
| Group guarantee | If one defaults, others owe |

:::tip
Microcredit's strength is not cost but accessibility. Interest rates are high because default risk is high, but borrowers can access capital they could not obtain otherwise.
:::

</section>

<section id="usury-prevention">

## Usury Prevention

Historically, many societies imposed limits on interest rates to prevent exploitation of the poor. Consider similar protections.

### Usury Limits

Define a maximum interest rate (e.g., 20% annually). Loans exceeding this are illegal or are socially sanctioned against. Rationale:

- Prevents debt spirals (when interest compounds beyond borrower's ability to repay)
- Protects vulnerable groups (poor, inexperienced, desperate)
- Maintains community stability (rampant debt allows lenders to accumulate excessive wealth)

### Enforcement

- Lender community reputation: violators are marked, denied trades, socially ostracized
- Religious/ethical codes: many traditions forbid excessive interest
- Legal nullification: courts declare excessive-interest loans void; borrowers owe only principal

### Exceptions

Allow higher rates in specific cases:

- **Commercial loans** (merchant to merchant, where both are sophisticated): 25–30%
- **High-risk ventures** (new technology, untested market): 25–35%
- **Unsecured loans** (no collateral): up to 50%

</section>

<section id="debt-forgiveness">

## Debt Forgiveness Mechanisms

Complete debt cancellation is rare but used for:

- **Jubilee cycles** — Every 7–50 years, all debts are cancelled to reset and prevent permanent underclass
- **Hardship release** — Individuals facing genuine crop failure or disaster get partial or full forgiveness
- **Death of debtor** — Heirs inherit assets but not debt obligations (or inherit limited obligation)

### When to Forgive

| Situation | Action | Rationale |
|-----------|--------|-----------|
| Borrower repeatedly defaults through negligence | Enforce collection | Accountability |
| Borrower defaults due to crop failure/disaster | Partial forgiveness | Beyond control |
| Borrower dies; heirs are poor | Full forgiveness | No means to pay |
| Community-wide crisis (plague, war) | Mass forgiveness | Systemic reset |
| Debt obligation exceeds borrower's lifetime income | Partial forgiveness | Unrepayable |

Forgiveness is typically a community decision, not a lender's individual choice.

:::warning
Excessive forgiveness undermines the credit system: lenders stop issuing loans if they expect non-repayment. Balance compassion with system sustainability.
:::

</section>

## Credit Scoring Without Computers

Assessing borrower creditworthiness traditionally relied on reputation and character.

### Reputation Factors

- **Repayment history:** Has this person repaid previous loans on time?
- **Assets:** Does borrower own land, livestock, or tools? (indicates stability)
- **Family/community ties:** Is borrower rooted in the community or likely to flee?
- **Occupation stability:** Does job provide reliable income?
- **Moral character:** Is borrower known to be honest, hardworking, or dissolute?

### Formal Credit Record

Maintain a written record for each person:

**BORROWER PROFILE**
- Name: [Name]
- Address/Community: [Location]
- Occupation: [Trade]
- Family: [Spouse, children, dependents]
- Assets: [Land (acres), livestock (number), tools, valuables]
- Loan history:
  - Loan 1: Date, amount, term, result (repaid on time, late, or defaulted)
  - Loan 2: [Details]
- Risk rating: Low / Medium / High
- Notes: [Character observations, warnings, recommendations]

### Default History Weight

- **0 defaults in 5 years:** Good credit, lend freely
- **1 default (recovered):** Medium credit, lend with collateral
- **1 default (unresolved):** Poor credit, lend only for vital needs with strong collateral
- **2+ defaults:** No unsecured lending; collateral must cover 150%+ of loan value
