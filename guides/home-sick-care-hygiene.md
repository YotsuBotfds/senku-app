## Overview

When one person in the home is sick, the goal is not to turn the whole house into a clinic. The goal is to reduce the amount of germ spread between the sick person, the caregiver, and the rest of the family.

This guide focuses on practical home steps that answer the most common questions:

- How do I keep the rest of the family from getting sick?
- How do I clean up vomit safely?
- Should I wash the sick person's clothes separately?
- How do I handle bedding, dishes, trash, and shared rooms?

Use this guide for routine home illness care, especially for flu-like illness, stomach bugs, vomiting, diarrhea, and respiratory infections. If the sick person has trouble breathing, confusion, severe dehydration, blood in vomit or stool, coffee-ground vomit, black tarry stool, cannot keep fluids down, has a hard or swollen belly, keeps guarding the belly, will not walk upright because of belly pain, has vomiting with no stool or gas, or has fever with one very tender belly spot, seek medical help and route to acute-abdominal-emergencies rather than routine home-care hygiene.

:::info
**The core idea:** isolate the mess, not the person. Keep body fluids, dirty laundry, and shared surfaces under control, and the rest of the household has a much better chance of staying well.
:::

## The First 10 Minutes

If someone starts vomiting, has diarrhea, or is coughing heavily, do these first:

1. Put the sick person in one room if possible.
2. Open a window or improve airflow.
3. Set out a trash bag, tissues or paper towels, water, soap, and a clean towel.
4. Keep one cup, one plate, and one spoon for the sick person if they are eating or drinking.
5. Wash your hands before and after helping them.

If you only remember one rule, remember this: do not touch your face until you have washed your hands after contact with the sick person or anything they touched.

If the "stomach bug" picture starts looking wrong because vomit looks like coffee grounds, there is blood in vomit or stool, the stool is black and tarry, the belly is getting hard, swollen, or very tender, the person will not stand or walk upright, or vomiting is paired with no stool or gas, stop treating it as routine home sick care and switch to urgent abdominal assessment.

## How To Keep the Rest of the Family from Getting Sick

The biggest risk is not the room itself. It is shared hands, shared surfaces, and shared body fluids.

### Reduce Contact

- Keep the sick person in one room if possible.
- Limit visitors to the room.
- Have one main caregiver if the household can manage it.
- Avoid hugs, kisses, and close face-to-face contact while the person is actively coughing or vomiting.
- If the sick person must move through the home, have them wear a mask if they can tolerate one and if the illness is respiratory.

### Separate High-Risk Items

- Use one towel for the sick person.
- Use one cup and one set of utensils for the sick person.
- Keep their toothbrush away from everyone else's.
- Do not share pillows, blankets, or water bottles.

### Clean Hands at the Right Moments

Wash hands:

- before and after helping the sick person
- after contact with vomit, stool, mucus, or dirty laundry
- before preparing food
- before eating or drinking
- after using the bathroom
- after removing gloves
- after cleaning surfaces
- after taking out trash

Alcohol sanitizer is useful when hands are not visibly dirty, but soap and water are better after vomiting, diarrhea, or visible contamination.

## Room Setup

The ideal sick-room setup is simple and boring:

- one bed or couch
- one trash bag or lined bin
- tissues or paper towels
- hand soap
- a thermometer
- a dedicated drinking cup
- a laundry hamper or bag
- a fan or open window for ventilation

### Ventilation

Fresh air helps reduce airborne spread, especially for coughs and colds.

- Open windows if weather and safety allow.
- Use a fan to move air out of the room, not from the sick person toward the rest of the house.
- If possible, keep the door closed while still allowing some airflow.
- If the person is coughing, sneezing, or breathing heavily through the mouth, air out the room regularly.

Ventilation does not replace cleaning, but it helps.

## Bedding and Laundry

### Should I Wash the Sick Person's Clothes Separately?

Usually, yes for handling, and often yes for the wash load if there is vomiting, diarrhea, blood, heavy mucus, or any other body fluid contamination.

### Best Practice

- Put dirty clothing and bedding straight into a hamper, bin, or bag.
- Do not shake sheets or clothes, because shaking spreads droplets and debris into the air.
- Keep soiled laundry away from clean laundry.
- Wash contaminated items as soon as practical.

### Washing Steps

1. Wear gloves if the laundry is visibly soiled.
2. Carry items carefully to the washer or bag them until washing time.
3. Use detergent and the warmest water the fabric can safely tolerate.
4. Dry completely if possible.
5. Wash your hands after handling the laundry.

If the item is heavily contaminated and cannot be cleaned well, it may be safer to discard it than to keep trying to rescue it.

### Bedding Changes

- Change sheets and pillowcases when visibly soiled.
- Change towels often, especially if the person is vomiting, sweating, or having diarrhea.
- If the illness is contagious and there is a lot of body fluid exposure, change bedding daily or sooner.

Keep clean bedding separate from the sick person's laundry until it is washed.

## Dishes and Cups

You do not always need a separate dishwashing system, but you do need clear separation while the person is sick.

> For a fuller wash-rinse-sanitize workflow, including how to handle dishes after vomiting, diarrhea, or raw-food contamination, cross-check `dishwashing-kitchen-cleanup-without-running-water.md`.

### Safe Approach

- Give the sick person their own cup, plate, and utensils if possible.
- Wash dishes in hot soapy water or a dishwasher.
- Do not leave used cups or bowls sitting around the room.
- Do not share straws, bottles, or utensils.

If the person is only mildly ill and able to eat normally, regular dishwashing is usually enough. If they are vomiting or have diarrhea, keep their items together until washing so they do not spread contamination around the kitchen.

## Vomit Cleanup

Vomit cleanup is one of the highest-risk moments for household spread because it often splashes and contaminates hands, clothing, and nearby surfaces.

### What To Wear

- Gloves if available
- A mask if the person is actively vomiting or if there is a strong splash risk
- Clothes you can wash afterward if the spill is large

### Cleanup Steps

1. Keep other people away.
2. Put on gloves.
3. Use paper towels, disposable cloths, or old rags to cover and absorb the spill.
4. Pick up solids first, then wipe the area.
5. Bag the waste immediately.
6. Clean the area with soap and water.
7. Disinfect hard, non-porous surfaces if the cleaner or household bleach product is appropriate for that surface.
8. Wash hands after removing gloves.

### What To Do With Soft or Porous Items

- Mattresses, rugs, and upholstered furniture are harder to disinfect.
- If contamination is light, clean thoroughly and dry well.
- If contamination is heavy or keeps soaking in, removal or disposal may be the safer option.

### Important Cleanup Habits

- Do not vacuum fresh vomit.
- Do not sweep dry powder through the air.
- Do not mix cleaning chemicals.
- Do not carry contaminated cloths around the house before bagging them.

If you can smell the spill, assume some contamination is still present and clean again.

## Waste Handling

### Trash

- Use a lined trash can or trash bag in the sick room.
- Empty it often if it contains tissues, wipes, or vomit cleanup waste.
- Tie bags closed before carrying them through the house.
- Wash hands after taking out trash.

### Dirty Tissues and Wipes

Put used tissues, paper towels, and cleanup cloths directly into the waste bag. Do not set them on counters, beds, or floors.

### Bathroom Waste

If the sick person is using a bathroom, clean the toilet handle, seat, and sink regularly. Flush with the lid down if there is one. Keep a separate cleaning cloth or disposable wipe set for the bathroom.

## Caregiver Protection

Caregivers are often the next people to get sick because they are closest to the source.

### Protect Yourself

- Wash hands after every close contact.
- Avoid touching your eyes, nose, and mouth.
- Wear gloves for vomit, stool, or blood cleanup.
- Change clothes if they are visibly contaminated.
- Rest and drink water so you do not become run down.

### Do Not Share Contaminated Air or Surfaces

- Keep the sick room ventilated.
- Clean door handles, faucets, remotes, and phones if the sick person used them.
- If the illness is respiratory, keep distance when possible.

### Watch Your Own Symptoms

If you start getting fever, vomiting, diarrhea, cough, or severe fatigue, assume you may be infected too and adjust caregiving so the household does not keep passing germs back and forth.

## Daily Routine

A simple routine works better than a perfect one that nobody follows.

### Morning

- Wash hands.
- Check the sick person's temperature and hydration.
- Change bedding if needed.
- Empty trash.
- Open windows briefly if weather allows.

### During the Day

- Give fluids in clean cups.
- Wash hands before and after each care task.
- Clean shared surfaces.
- Keep laundry and dishes contained.

### Evening

- Collect dirty laundry and trash.
- Clean any spill zones.
- Reset the room with fresh tissues, soap, water, and a clean cup.
- Wash hands before leaving the room for the night.

## When To Get Help

Get medical help promptly if the sick person:

- cannot keep fluids down
- is confused, very sleepy, or hard to wake
- has trouble breathing
- has blood in vomit or stool
- has severe belly pain, a hard or swollen belly, guarding, or pain that localizes to one spot
- will not stand or walk upright because of belly pain
- is vomiting and not passing stool or gas
- shows signs of dehydration such as very little urine, dry mouth, or dizziness
- worsens instead of improving over time

Those belly-pain patterns are not routine "stomach bug" hygiene questions. Treat them as possible surgical-abdomen or obstruction complaints and route to acute-abdominal-emergencies while arranging urgent medical help.

If multiple family members start getting sick quickly, treat it as a household spread problem and tighten cleaning, handwashing, and room separation immediately.

## Quick Answers

**How do I keep the rest of the family from getting sick?**  
Separate the sick person, wash hands at the right moments, keep laundry and dishes contained, clean body-fluid spills fast, and ventilate the room.

**How do I clean up vomit safely?**  
Wear gloves, cover the spill, bag the waste, clean with soap and water, then disinfect hard surfaces if appropriate. Wash hands afterward.

**Should I wash the sick person's clothes separately?**  
Yes, handle them separately and wash them in a dedicated load if they are contaminated with vomit, stool, blood, or heavy mucus.

**Do dishes need to be separate?**  
Dedicated cups and utensils help, but regular hot-soapy dishwashing is usually enough if items are not shared before washing.

**What matters most?**  
Hands, body fluids, ventilation, and not mixing dirty items with clean ones.
