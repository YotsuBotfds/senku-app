## Overview

Canals and waterways have been the backbone of commerce and settlement for millennia. Whether built for transportation, irrigation, or drainage, canal systems require careful surveying, engineering, and construction. This guide covers the practical methods for planning and building canal networks, including the critical components like locks and gates that allow vessels to navigate changes in elevation.

Unlike rivers, canals can be precisely engineered to slope uniformly and pass through difficult terrain via aqueducts and embankments. With proper design and maintenance, a well-built canal system can operate indefinitely, moving goods and people far more efficiently than roads.

</section>

<section id="canal-route-surveying">

## Canal Route Surveying & Design

Before any earth is moved, the entire canal route must be surveyed and graded. Begin by establishing a baseline along the proposed route using surveying instruments—a level, transit, and measuring chain. Mark reference points every 100 meters, recording the elevation at each point.

**Surveying Tools and Methods:**

- **Level:** A simple spirit level mounted on a tripod allows you to sight horizontal lines. Establish a "backsight" on a known reference point, then rotate the instrument to take "foresight" readings ahead. The difference in rod readings equals the elevation change.
- **Transit (or Theodolite):** Records both horizontal angles and vertical angles. Essential for determining the canal's alignment through valleys and around obstacles.
- **Measuring Chain:** A 20-meter or 30-meter surveying chain, marked in meters, measures horizontal distances along the canal centerline.

Record all survey data in a field book, drawing cross-sections every 200–500 meters depending on terrain complexity. The survey defines:

1. **Water surface elevation** along the entire canal (the "grade line")
2. **Bottom elevation** of the canal channel
3. **Natural ground elevation** to determine cut/fill quantities
4. **Obstacles** requiring aqueducts or changes in alignment

The canal's water surface slope should be minimal—typically 0.0005 to 0.001 (vertical drop of 5 cm per kilometer or less). Too steep and water velocity accelerates, eroding the canal bed; too flat and sediment accumulates.

</section>

<section id="canal-cross-section">

## Cross-Section Design & Channel Shape

Canal cross-sections are most commonly **trapezoidal** in soil or clay, or **rectangular** when lined with stone or concrete. The choice depends on soil stability, water depth, and durability requirements.

**Trapezoidal Section (Earthen Canals):**

- **Bottom width:** 2–4 meters for transport canals, 1–2 meters for irrigation canals
- **Water depth:** 1.0–1.5 meters for transport, 0.6–1.0 meters for irrigation
- **Side slopes:** 1.5H:1V to 2H:1V (rise 1 meter for every 1.5–2 meters of horizontal distance) depending on soil stability
- **Freeboard** (height above water): 0.3–0.5 meters to prevent overflow and wave action damage
- **Towpath:** 3–4 meters wide, typically on one or both sides, raised 0.3 meters above the water level

The trapezoidal shape distributes water pressure uniformly and is stable in most soil types. Clay and silt soils allow steeper slopes (1.5H:1V); sandy soils require gentler slopes (2H:1V or flatter).

**Rectangular Section (Lined Canals):**

- **Bottom width:** 3–5 meters
- **Water depth:** 1.0–2.0 meters
- **Vertical or near-vertical walls** if concrete or stone-lined
- **Towpath:** Built outside the canal on solid ground or as a cantilever structure

Rectangular sections are more compact and allow deeper water in the same width, but require stronger structural support and regular maintenance of the lining.

</section>

<section id="canal-lining">

## Lining Materials & Construction

Unlined (earthen) canals lose water to seepage and require continuous maintenance to prevent erosion. Lining reduces seepage loss and extends the canal's lifespan significantly.

**Clay Lining:**

Traditional and effective. Compact wet clay to 30 cm thickness, achieving a permeability less than 1 cm/day. This requires careful site selection—clay must be available nearby or imported. Layer clay in 10 cm lifts, rolling and flooding each layer to achieve proper compaction. Total thickness should be 30–60 cm.

**Concrete Lining:**

Produces a durable, low-maintenance canal. Pour concrete (1 part cement : 2 parts sand : 4 parts gravel, by volume) to 10–15 cm thickness over a compacted sand subbase. Reinforce with wire mesh or rebar every 50–100 cm to control shrinkage cracking. Cure for 14 days before filling. Concrete linings typically last 30–50 years with maintenance.

**Stone and Brick Lining:**

Stack dressed stone (ashlar) or brick in lime mortar. Requires skilled masons but uses local materials well. A double-thickness brick wall with lime mortar joints provides good durability. Thickness: 30–45 cm minimum.

All linings require expansion joints every 5–10 meters to accommodate settlement and thermal movement. Damage repairs are easier on concrete than clay, making it preferable for permanent infrastructure.

</section>

<section id="lock-design">

## Lock Chamber Design & Operation

Locks allow vessels to move between two water levels. A typical lock consists of:

1. **Upper and lower approach channels** (still water, free of current)
2. **Lock chamber** (the box where the vessel floats)
3. **Upper and lower gates**
4. **Filling and emptying valves** (ports in the gates or walls)

**Lock Dimensions:**

A practical transport lock accommodates vessels approximately 20 meters long, 4 meters wide, and 1.5 meters draft:

- **Chamber length:** 25 meters (20 m vessel + 5 m clearance for boats approaching)
- **Chamber width:** 6 meters (4 m vessel + 1 m clearance each side)
- **Bottom elevation:** Designed to maintain at least 0.5 meters water depth over the lock sill when empty
- **Lift (height difference):** 1.5–3.0 meters typical; locks handle larger lifts but require more time to fill

**Lock Operation (Pound Lock System):**

1. Vessel enters from lower level, gates close behind it
2. Filling valves slowly open; water rises from the upper reservoir into the chamber (typically 15–30 minutes depending on valve size)
3. When water levels equalize, upper gates open
4. Vessel proceeds to upper level; upper gates close
5. Lower valves empty the chamber, lowering water for the next downbound vessel

A single lock filling gate valve (sluice) 0.3 m × 0.3 m can fill a 25 m × 6 m chamber in about 20 minutes.

</section>

<section id="lock-gates">

## Gate Construction

Lock gates must be strong, watertight, and durable.

**Miter Gates (Most Common):**

Two gates meet at an angle (approximately 120°) in the center of the lock opening. The water pressure forces them together, creating a watertight seal. Miter gates are most effective where the water level difference is moderate (up to 2–3 meters).

Construction: Build each gate from timber (oak or cedar preferred) or steel plate. A wooden miter gate suitable for a 6-meter wide opening, 2.5-meter lift:

- **Main frame:** Four beams forming a rectangle (6 m wide, 3 m tall)
- **Panel backing:** 100 mm × 50 mm timbers spaced 0.5 m apart, supporting the facing
- **Facing:** 50 mm thick planks, perpendicular to the backing beams, creating a watertight surface
- **Hinges:** Large iron strap hinges (100 mm × 100 mm, bolted through the frame) support each gate on a pivoting post
- **Sealing:** A rubber or felt seal along the miter joint prevents leakage

**Lift Gates:**

Vertical gates suspended by cables or chains. Used for larger lifts or where space is constrained. A simple lift gate:

- **Frame:** Steel or timber, 6 m wide × water depth + 1 m tall
- **Facing:** Planks or steel plate, fully watertight
- **Hoist:** Block and tackle, or a hand-powered windlass with a 50:1 mechanical advantage ratio
- **Safety dogs:** Latches that engage under the gate frame to hold it in raised position, preventing failure of the hoist mechanism

Lift gates require a lock chamber extended upward or a separate hoist structure above the lock.

:::warning
Gate construction and installation require precise tolerances. Misaligned gates leak, allowing water to escape uncontrolled. All gate pivots must be truly vertical and smooth. Test gate operation with sandbags or water pressure simulations before allowing vessel traffic.
:::

</section>

<section id="water-supply">

## Water Supply & Feeder Systems

Canals lose water to evaporation and seepage. In dry climates, the loss can exceed 50% of the water intake. Feeder channels from nearby rivers or springs replenish the canal.

**Feeder Design:**

- **Intake structure:** A low weir or dam in the water source, fitted with a sluice gate and trash rack (to catch branches and debris)
- **Feeder channel:** Lower grade line than the main canal (to minimize water loss) but sufficient slope for flow. Typically 0.0001 to 0.0005.
- **Settling basin:** A wide, slow section where suspended sediment settles before entering the main canal. 1–2 meter deep, 50–100 meters long for a typical canal.
- **Capacity:** Must equal maximum evaporation + seepage loss + traffic usage. Estimate seepage at 1–5 cm per day through unlined sections; 0.1–1 cm per day through lined sections.

**Level Maintenance:**

Overflow weirs at the high point of the canal automatically spill excess water back to the water source. This maintains a constant water level despite variations in feeder flow. A simple overflow weir:

- **Crest elevation:** Set 0.3–0.5 meters below the canal banks (freeboard)
- **Crest width:** 1–3 meters, depending on expected overflow rate
- **Spillway:** Leads back to the water source via a channel or aqueduct

</section>

<section id="sediment-management">

## Sediment Management & Dredging

Suspended sediment settles in still or slow-moving water within the canal, gradually filling the bottom. Regular dredging maintains navigable depth.

**Monitoring Sediment:**

- **Settling basins:** Designed to catch most sediment before it enters the main canal. Dredge annually or as needed.
- **Main canal:** Inspect every 2–3 years. Measure bottom depth at regular intervals (every 500 meters).
- **Maintenance dredging:** When depth decreases by more than 10–15 cm, dredging is economical.

**Dredging Methods:**

1. **Hand dredging:** Manual labor with shovels and buckets. Practical for small canals or isolated sections. Excavate sediment and pile it on the towpath; spread it as top dressing on land or stockpile it.

2. **Dredging boats:** A flat-bottomed barge fitted with a dragline (bucket on a rope and pulley) or a dipper. One operator and 3–4 laborers can dredge 50–100 cubic meters per day.

3. **Stone/brick removal:** Periodically remove any cobbles or debris that accumulates; these can damage vessels or lock gates.

Sediment dredged from clay canals can be used for canal bank repair or distributed on farmland as fertilizer (if the water source is not polluted).

</section>

<section id="aqueducts">

## Aqueduct Construction

Where a canal must cross a valley or a river, an aqueduct (a raised channel) conveys water across the gap.

**Simple Stone Aqueduct (Small Span, 10–20 meters):**

- **Piers:** Build stone or brick supports 2–3 meters apart. Each pier has a rectangular opening ("arch") 2–3 meters wide.
- **Arches:** Simple barrel vaults (semicircular or flat) spanning the opening. Roman-style stone arches are most durable.
- **Channel bed:** On top of the arches, build a concrete or stone-lined channel identical to the rest of the canal.
- **Total height above ground:** 3–8 meters depending on valley depth.

**Timber Aqueduct (Quick Construction):**

- **Supports:** Timber frame (heavy beams, braced with diagonals) resting on stone or concrete piers.
- **Channel:** Wooden trough, 1–2 meters deep, lined internally with clay, concrete, or sealed planks.
- **Maintenance:** Timber aqueducts require regular inspection; wood decays, loosens, and requires replacement every 15–25 years.

Construction of a 20-meter span aqueduct requires:

1. **Layout and leveling:** Ensure the channel bed is truly horizontal along the aqueduct
2. **Pier foundation:** Excavate to solid ground, build piers 2–3 meters apart
3. **Arch centering:** Support the arch with a temporary timber framework (removed after the stone or mortar sets)
4. **Channel lining:** Install clay, concrete, or sealed wood on top
5. **Testing:** Fill slowly, monitor for leaks and settlement

</section>

<section id="towpaths">

## Towpath Design & Maintenance

Towpaths allow draft animals (horses, mules, oxen) or humans to pull boats with ropes (towing).

**Towpath Specifications:**

- **Width:** 3–4 meters minimum, allowing two horses to pass side-by-side
- **Surface:** Compacted earth in temperate climates; stone/brick paving if heavy traffic
- **Elevation:** Raised 0.3–0.5 meters above normal water level to prevent flooding
- **Slope:** Gentle slope away from the canal for drainage
- **Vegetation:** Keep clear of trees and brush to prevent roots damaging the canal bank and snags interfering with tow ropes

**Maintenance:**

- **Vegetation control:** Scythe or cut brush annually to maintain a clear, 5-meter-wide corridor
- **Drainage:** Dig shallow ditches parallel to the towpath to carry rain runoff away from the canal bank
- **Compaction:** Re-roll or re-compact the towpath surface every 2–3 years in high-traffic areas
- **Repairs:** Fill ruts and depressions immediately; these accelerate erosion

Towpaths can also serve foot traffic and light vehicle traffic, making them valuable for settlement access and maintenance.

</section>

<section id="flow-control">

## Flow Control Structures

Weirs, sluices, and other structures regulate water flow within the canal system.

**Weir (Overflow Dam):**

A low dam that raises water level and directs excess flow downstream. A simple weir for a 6-meter wide canal:

- **Crest elevation:** 0.5–1.0 meter above downstream water level
- **Crest length:** 6–10 meters
- **Construction:** Stone dam with a concrete spillway face, or entirely concrete
- **Purpose:** Maintains a pond upstream (useful for feeder intakes, sediment settling) and generates head for hydropower if desired

**Sluice Gate (Discharge Control):**

A sliding gate that opens or closes to regulate flow. Built into a lock gate, feeder intake, or standalone structure.

- **Gate size:** 0.3 m × 0.3 m to 1.0 m × 1.5 m depending on flow requirements
- **Mechanism:** Handwheel with screw, lever, or chain hoist
- **Operation:** Slow adjustment prevents sudden water level surges

**Flash Lock (Historic Design):**

An older, simpler lock system—essentially a large sluice gate. When closed, it creates a pond. When opened, it releases a surge of water that floats boats over shallows downstream. Dangerous (sudden currents, boats capsized) but requires less maintenance than pound locks. Rarely built for new construction.

</section>

<section id="ice-management">

## Ice Management & Winter Operations

In freezing climates, ice forms on canals, blocking navigation and damaging structures.

**Prevention & Mitigation:**

- **Flowing water:** Keep feeder channels flowing to prevent ice formation in critical areas
- **Channel design:** Steeper banks (more vertical) reduce ice expansion pressure on towpath and structures
- **Breaking:** Manually chop ice in navigation channels every 1–2 weeks as needed; remove broken ice to prevent jams at locks
- **Drainage:** Lower canal water level in severe winter to reduce ice expansion force against lock gates and canal banks

**Lock Protection:**

- **Grease gates:** Apply lard or tallow to gate pivots and hinges to prevent freezing and ice accumulation
- **Drainage:** Use sluices to keep lock gates from freezing in place
- **Raised gates:** Partially raise lift gates to relieve ice pressure

**Seasonal Closure:**

In very cold climates, many canals are seasonally closed (drained or allowed to freeze solid), reopened in spring. Lock gates may be removed in winter and reinstalled in spring.

</section>

<section id="canal-maintenance">

## Maintenance Schedules & Long-Term Stewardship

Regular maintenance extends canal life and keeps it operational.

**Annual (Spring & Fall):**

1. Inspect banks for erosion, cracks, slumping
2. Clear vegetation from towpath and canal margins
3. Remove fallen debris from the canal
4. Drain, inspect, and grease all gate mechanisms
5. Check embankment slopes for seepage or springs
6. Drain and inspect sluices and valves

**Every 2–3 Years:**

1. Dredge settling basins and main canal if sediment depth exceeds tolerance
2. Repair any eroded or failed sections of bank
3. Re-compact towpath, fill ruts
4. Inspect all stone/brick structures for deterioration
5. Test water level gauges and measurement points

**Every 5–10 Years:**

1. Inspect lock gate structural integrity; repair or replace as needed
2. Inspect all aqueducts for settlement, cracking, or water loss
3. Inspect concrete linings for cracking, spalling
4. Major bank repair or relining if deterioration is significant
5. Drain completely and inspect bottom for damage

**Record Keeping:**

Maintain a log of all maintenance, dredging, and repairs. Note dates, locations, work done, and materials used. This history helps predict future maintenance needs and identify chronic problem areas.

</section>

## Summary

Canals are one of humanity's greatest engineering achievements. Unlike rivers, they can be precisely controlled and maintained, providing reliable transport and water management indefinitely. Success depends on careful initial surveying and design—poor surveys lead to non-functional or dangerous canals. Locks are the critical component enabling navigation across elevation changes; they are complex but proven technology. Regular maintenance, particularly sediment management and vegetation control, is essential for long-term operation. With competent construction and committed stewardship, a canal system built today can serve communities for centuries.
