## Welcome to the Compendium Roadmap

This page is the master guide. It shows you the complete sequence of technologies humanity needs to redevelop, from the first day to modern times. Each technology is connected to the others—some require previous discoveries, some enable the next generation.

**The core principle:** You cannot build a steam engine without metallurgy. You cannot have metallurgy without charcoal. You cannot smelt metal without pottery (for crucibles). Every step matters.

Use this roadmap to navigate the offline compendium. Each technology links to detailed guides. Follow the dependencies. Build in order.

:::info-box
**Era Milestones by Technology Class:** Era 0 (Days 1-30): Fire/Shelter/Water. Era 1 (Months 1-6): Stone/Pottery/Fiber. Era 2 (Months 6-2yr): Agriculture/Animals/Weaving. Era 3 (Years 2-5): Copper/Tin/Bronze. Era 4 (Years 3-7): Iron/Steel/Smithing. Era 5 (Years 5-15): Water Wheels/Glass/Paper. Era 6 (Years 10-25): Optics/Chemistry/Steam. Era 7 (Years 20-50): Rail/Steel/Oil. Era 8 (Years 30-75): Electricity/Radio. Era 9 (Years 50-100+): Computing. Each era requires all previous technologies as foundation. Attempting to jump eras fails catastrophically (e.g., steam without metallurgy is impossible).
:::

</section>

## Era 0: Immediate Survival

Day 1 – 30

The first month. You have nothing but knowledge and your hands. Your goal: stay alive. No tools. No shelter. Just knowledge.

**Core Technologies:**  
• Fire (ignition, maintenance, heat control)  
• Shelter (natural caves, lean-tos, thermal insulation)  
• Water (sourcing, purification, collection)  
• Friction tools (digging sticks, pressure tools)  
• Basic traps and snares (twine from plant fiber)  
• Emergency food (edible plants, insects, basic hunting)

**What You Need:** Nothing. Only knowledge of survival techniques. This is where the compendium begins.

[→ Survival Basics](survival-basics.html)

**Critical success factor:** Fire. Everything else depends on it. You must master fire in the first 3 days or you will not survive to Era 1.

:::tip
**Era 0 Survival Heuristic:** In your first month, prioritize fire → shelter → water in that order. Fire takes 1-3 days if you know friction methods or have flint. Shelter takes 2-7 days (natural cave or lean-to). Water takes 1 day (find nearby stream). Do not attempt pottery or tools until fire is reliable and shelter is weatherproof. Most common failure: spending Day 1 on tools when fire-building should take priority.
:::

</section>

## Era 1: Stone Age Settlement

Month 1 – 6

You have fire. Now build with it. Develop hand tools, basic crafts, and the first technologies that compound.

**Core Technologies:**  
• Stone tool refinement (flakes, blades, scrapers)  
• Pottery (clay vessels, firing, kilns)  
• Cordage and fiber (plant twine, nets, baskets)  
• Bone tools (needles, hooks, harpoons)  
• Bow and arrow (wooden frames, fletching)  
• Hide tanning (alkaline processing, leather)  
• Early agriculture (seed saving, basic planting)

**Key Dependency Chain:**  
Fire → Charcoal (burn wood, select for carbon) → Pottery (clay + heat) → Kilns (controlled clay chambers)

Settled agriculture → Food surplus → Labor specialization → Crafts develop → Trade begins

## Era 3: Bronze Age

Year 2 – 5

The first true metallurgical age. Copper and tin create bronze—stronger than stone, more valuable than gold.

**Core Technologies:**  
• Charcoal kilns (controlled high-temperature burns)  
• Ore identification (malachite, copper, tin sources)  
• Copper smelting (ore + charcoal + heat)  
• Tin smelting (cassiterite reduction)  
• Bronze alloys (90% copper, 10% tin ratio)  
• Metal casting (molds, pouring, cooling)  
• Smithing basics (shaping hot metal)

**Critical Dependency:**  
Pottery (crucibles) + Charcoal + Ore + Heat = Metal  
  
**More detailed:** You need pottery crucibles to hold molten metal. You need charcoal hot enough to smelt (1100°C+). You need ore deposits. You need a furnace that reaches and maintains this temperature.

## Era 4: Iron Age

Year 3 – 7

Bronze is good. Iron is everywhere. The challenge: iron requires hotter furnaces and better technology to work with it.

**Core Technologies:**  
• High-temperature furnaces (bloomery, shaft furnace)  
• Bellows (forced air, temperature control)  
• Iron smelting (ore + charcoal, 1200°C+)  
• Steel production (iron + carbon control)  
• Blacksmithing (hammering, quenching, tempering)  
• Advanced tools (axes, plows, chisels)  
• Tool standardization

**Prerequisite:** Bronze Age technology. You need bronze tools to build iron furnaces. You need blacksmiths who understand metallurgy from working with bronze.  
  
**Temperature barrier:** Iron smelting requires 1200°C+. This requires: good furnace design, bellows to force air, and sustained heat. A bloomery furnace is the practical approach.

**Historical note:** Iron has higher melting point than copper (1538°C vs 1085°C), but lower melting point than smelting temperature. This creates iron "blooms"—spongy iron that must be hammered repeatedly to remove slag. Labor-intensive but possible.

## ⚡ Critical Dependency Chains

These are the five most important sequences in the roadmap. They unlock other technologies and must be completed in order. Failure or delay in any one cascades through the entire civilization.

:::warning
**Common Era Progression Errors:** Attempting smelting (Era 3) before mastering pottery (Era 1) → crucibles fail, metal smelts fail. Building water wheels (Era 5) before iron tools (Era 4) → cannot fabricate wheel parts. Jumping to steam engines (Era 6) before understanding gearing → pressure systems fail catastrophically. Each era's technology is foundational for the next. Skipping any piece cascades: one missing prerequisite delays the entire civilization by 2-5 years minimum.
:::

### 1\. The Foundation Chain: Fire → Metal → Tools

FIRE (Day 1) → Charcoal (Month 1) → Pottery Kilns (Month 3) → Smelting (Year 2) → Bronze (Year 3) → Iron (Year 4) → Steel (Year 5) → Everything else

**Explanation:** Fire is the foundation of all technology. It creates charcoal for fuel. Charcoal fires pottery, which makes crucibles for smelting. Smelting creates metals. Metals create tools. Tools build everything else. This sequence cannot be skipped or rushed.

### 2\. The Knowledge Chain: Clay → Paper → Printing → Literacy → Innovation

Clay (Month 1) → Pottery (Month 3) → Paper (Year 8) → Printing Press (Year 10) → Literacy spreads (Year 12) → Scientific revolution (Year 15)

**Explanation:** Pottery teaches you how to work with clay and fire. Paper production follows the same principles. The printing press multiplies knowledge. Literacy accelerates innovation exponentially. Without this chain, progress stalls after Year 10.

### 3\. The Medicine Chain: Glass → Lenses → Microscope → Germ Theory → Medicine

Sand (available) → Glass furnace (Year 8) → Lens grinding (Year 10) → Microscope assembly (Year 12) → Bacteria observation (Year 14) → Germ theory (Year 16) → Antiseptics, antibiotics (Year 20+)

**Explanation:** Glass is common but requires high temperature furnaces. Lenses require precision glass grinding. Microscopes require mathematics and optics. Once you observe bacteria, germ theory follows naturally. This unlocks medicine, reducing mortality by 60%. This is the single biggest quality-of-life improvement possible.

### 4\. The Power Chain: Magnets + Copper → Generator → Electricity → Everything Modern

Copper wire (Year 5) + Magnets (Year 4, from iron) → Generator (Year 30) → Electricity distribution (Year 35) → Motors (Year 38) → Electronics (Year 45) → Computing (Year 60)

**Explanation:** Electricity is the universal power carrier. One generator can replace 100 workers. It enables radio, motors, refrigeration, and eventually computing. This chain takes longest but has highest payoff. Modern civilization is impossible without this.

### 5\. The Extraction Chain: Mining → Refining → Industrial Scale Production

Gunpowder (Year 12) → Mining at scale (Year 15) → Ore refining (Year 18) → Industrial production (Year 25) → Railroad transport (Year 30) → Global supply chains (Year 40)

**Explanation:** Gunpowder enables precision mining. Mining at scale extracts ore faster. Refining converts raw ore to usable metal. Industrial production means you can make 1000 items per day instead of 1 per day. This drives everything from Year 15 onward.

#### Five Critical Chains Visualization

![⚡ The Master Technology Roadmap diagram 7](../assets/svgs/technology-roadmap-7.svg)

:::tip
**Prioritization Rule of Thumb:** When in doubt, build what enables the most dependent technologies. Fire enables everything (foundational). Pottery enables smelting. Metallurgy enables machines. Glass enables medicine. Electricity enables computing. Within each era, identify the single technology that unblocks the most subsequent techs and attack that first. This greedy approach minimizes delays and maximizes compound progress.
:::

## Population Milestones

Each technology milestone unlocks the ability to support more people. Here's what you can achieve at each population level:

### 10 People

**Year 0:** Immediate survival. One person gathering, one hunting, one making fire. Everyone else on rotating shifts. No specialization.

Skills needed: Fire, water, shelter, basic hunting.

#### 50 People

**Year 1-2:** Stone Age villages. Specialization begins: 10% crafters, 20% hunters, 70% gathering/farming. pottery makers emerge.

Skills needed: Tool crafting, pottery, basic agriculture.

#### 200 People

**Year 2-4:** Agricultural settlements. 30% farmers, 20% crafters, 10% hunters. First specialists: potters, weavers, metalworkers learning bronze.

Skills needed: Organized farming, metallurgy basics.

#### 1,000 People

**Year 4-8:** Bronze Age city-state. 50% agriculture, 30% crafts, 10% defense. Specialized roles: smiths, priesthoods, merchants, soldiers.

Skills needed: Iron tools, complex organization, trade networks.

#### 5,000 People

**Year 8-15:** Medieval town. 40% agriculture, 40% crafts/trade, 10% defense, 10% clergy/administration. Water mills emerge—30% less farm labor needed.

Skills needed: Water wheels, advanced construction, glass making.

#### 50,000+ People

**Year 20+:** Industrial city. 10% agriculture, 60% manufacturing, 15% service/trade, 10% administration. Steam power: 1 factory worker = 50 farm workers.

Skills needed: Steam engines, steel production, electricity, printing.

:::note
**Key insight:** Population growth is logarithmic, not linear. You can feed 10x more people with 3x more technology. By Year 15, one settlement can feed more people than 100 stone-age villages. This is where civilization scales.
:::

<section id="climate-roadmaps">

## Climate-Specific Roadmaps

The master roadmap assumes temperate climates. But your location changes everything. Here are region-specific variants that account for local advantages, constraints, and seasonal rhythms. The core technology sequence stays the same—but priorities shift based on what nature provides and what nature demands.

### Tropical/Equatorial Climates

**Defining traits:** Year-round growing season, abundant water (but humidity kills stored goods), high disease burden (malaria, dengue, parasites), fast decomposition of materials, no heating needed.

**Unique Advantages:**  
• Continuous crop production (two growing seasons per year in many regions)  
• Bamboo as primary building/tool material (grows faster than wood, incredibly strong)  
• Natural rubber from rubber trees (available, not requiring synthesis)  
• Fish and aquatic protein year-round  
• Fruit and nut abundance (coconut, mango, etc.)  
• Natural fibers (jute, hemp, sisal)

**Unique Challenges:**  
• Disease pressure is extreme (malaria, dengue, parasites, fungi)  
• Monsoon seasons bring flooding, soil erosion, and agricultural disruption  
• Metal and tools rust/corrode rapidly in humidity  
• Food preservation is hardest here (fermentation and drying work, but mold is aggressive)  
• Leather rots; need alternatives for waterproofing  
• High crop failure risk if monsoon timing shifts

**Priority Sequence for Tropical Settings:**  
  
1\. **Disease prevention (Year 0-5):** Malaria netting and dengue management must be Priority #1. Build elevated housing to avoid mosquitoes. Start germ theory work earlier if possible.  
  
2\. **Food preservation (Year 1-3):** Master fermentation (salt pickling, koji, tempeh). Smoking works but is labor-intensive. Salt preservation is critical.  
  
3\. **Monsoon agriculture (Year 2-5):** Rice cultivation for flooded paddies. Develop terraced mountain farms to redirect water rather than fighting it.  
  
4\. **Bamboo engineering (Year 3-8):** Don't skip to iron tools immediately. Bamboo is better than iron for many tropical applications—it doesn't corrode, it's lighter, it's naturally available.  
  
5\. **Waterproofing (Year 4-10):** Natural latex from rubber trees, palm oil products, wax treatments. These are more reliable than imported materials.

:::note
**Key insight:** Tropical regions can skip some phases of the roadmap. You don't need extensive food storage (continuous harvest). You don't need heavy winter clothing (cooling matters more). You should accelerate germ theory and disease prevention. Expect to reach similar population levels 15-20% faster due to continuous agriculture, but disease mortality may offset this unless medicine advances quickly.
:::

### Temperate Climates (Europe, Eastern US, East Asia)

**Defining traits:** Four distinct seasons, moderate precipitation, rich forest biomes, soil recovery periods, winter dormancy forces planning.

**Unique Advantages:**  
• Abundant timber and building materials  
• Natural iron ore deposits (often in soil and shallow deposits)  
• Cold storage for winter (preservation without active technology)  
• Distinct seasons train planning and foresight  
• Animal furs naturally available (beaver, fox, rabbit)  
• Leather stays stable and ages well  
• Spring water sources are dependable  
• Low disease pressure (plague cycles, but manageable)

**Unique Challenges:**  
• Winter requires fuel (heating), food storage, and animal care  
• Growing season is only 5-7 months  
• Spring flooding can destroy crops and villages  
• Freezing cycles damage roads, buildings, and infrastructure  
• Shorter daylight in winter reduces work productivity

**Priority Sequence for Temperate Settings:**  
  
1\. **Winter preparation (Year 0-2):** Food storage, firewood cutting, shelter insulation are non-negotiable. Build root cellars for stable storage.  
  
2\. **Four-field crop rotation (Year 2-4):** Don't use three-field rotation—go straight to four-field: grain, legumes, other crops, fallow. This works better in temperate climates and regenerates soil faster.  
  
3\. **Animal husbandry for winter (Year 2-4):** Cattle, sheep, pigs. They eat summer crops and stored hay. They provide winter protein.  
  
4\. **Wool and leather industries (Year 3-7):** Sheep for wool (winter clothing), cattle hides for leather (waterproofing, tools).  
  
5\. **Root cellar technology (Year 2-3):** Underground storage keeps food at constant cool temperature. This is more valuable than any technology in temperate zones.

:::note
**Key insight:** Temperate zones mature slower but more stably. You have time to think, plan, and iterate. The roadmap's timeline is calibrated for temperate zones—expect to track it closely. Winter planning teaches systems thinking. By Year 15, temperate settlements often exceed tropical ones in output due to superior infrastructure and planning culture.
:::

### Arid/Desert Climates

**Defining traits:** Water is THE constraint, abundant solar energy, thermal variations (hot days, cold nights), minimal disease pressure, low vegetation but hardy crops survive.

**Unique Advantages:**  
• Abundant sun (unlimited fuel for heating/drying)  
• Minimal disease pressure (dry kills microbes)  
• Date palms provide food, fuel, and materials year-round  
• Natural salt available (evaporation ponds)  
• Adobe/rammed earth construction is ideal (thermally massive)  
• Stone abundant for building  
• Clear skies enable astronomy and timekeeping  
• Natural evaporative cooling works well

**Unique Challenges:**  
• Water is limiting—irrigation is essential  
• Aquifer depletion is a long-term risk  
• Dust storms damage crops and equipment  
• Soil salinity increases with irrigation (requires management)  
• Limited biological materials (fewer trees)  
• Extreme temperature swings stress materials

**Priority Sequence for Desert Settings:**  
  
1\. **Irrigation technology (Year 0-3):** Channels, dams, wells, qanat systems (underground tunnels). Without this, settlement is impossible. Prioritize water engineering over metallurgy if necessary.  
  
2\. **Drought-resistant crops (Year 1-2):** Sorghum, millet, barley, chickpeas—not wheat. These survive with half the water.  
  
3\. **Date palm cultivation (Year 1-3):** Date palms need minimal water once established. Provide calories, fiber, wood, and shade.  
  
4\. **Adobe/rammed earth construction (Year 2-4):** Thermal mass building keeps interior cool during hot days, warm during cold nights. Preferable to stone in deserts.  
  
5\. **Salt preservation and mining (Year 3-5):** Evaporation ponds produce salt. This preserves food and enables trade with other regions.

:::note
**Key insight:** Desert settlements are fragile if water fails. Technology advances slowly until irrigation stabilizes. But once stable, deserts can be incredibly productive (Nile valley, Middle East, North Africa). The roadmap timeline stretches: expect Year 3 technology by Year 5-6 if water is uncertain. Prioritize geological surveys to find aquifers.
:::

### Arctic/Subarctic Climates

**Defining traits:** Extremely short growing season (6-8 weeks), hunting/fishing primary food, permafrost and frozen ground, long winter darkness, human morale is critical.

**Unique Advantages:**  
• Freezing is free preservation (food frozen, stays good for months)  
• Animal abundance (whales, seals, fish, game)  
• Fur and blubber available (insulation and caloric density)  
• Minimal disease pressure (cold kills pathogens)  
• Mineral deposits often accessible (bedrock exposed)  
• Ice as construction material (igloos, ice architecture)  
• Natural ice roads in winter (transport is free)

**Unique Challenges:**  
• Agriculture is impossible (growing season too short)  
• Hunting provides 90%+ of calories (supply is variable)  
• Darkness for 3-6 months affects morale and productivity  
• Extreme cold breaks materials (wood becomes brittle)  
• Metal tools require special handling (warmth makes them brittle in cold)  
• Nutrition is harder (vitamin C from organ meats and pine needle tea)  
• Isolation is extreme (trade networks are seasonal)

**Priority Sequence for Arctic Settings:**  
  
1\. **Hunting and fishing (Year 0-ongoing):** This is not a Year 1 tech—it's baseline survival. Master whale and seal hunting first. Fish traps. Trap game.  
  
2\. **Dog sled transport (Year 1-2):** Dogs are crucial. They hunt, they travel, they provide warmth. Invest in breeding and training immediately.  
  
3\. **Sod house construction (Year 1-2):** Not metal or wood—sod and turf. Whale bones as frames. This is better insulation than any Arctic alternative.  
  
4\. **Food preservation by freezing (Year 0-1):** Trivial in Arctic. Use it. Build ice cellars. Store everything frozen.  
  
5\. **Vitamin C sources (Year 0-1):** Organ meats contain vitamin C (don't cook them to death). Pine needle tea is backup. Prevent scurvy obsessively.  
  
6\. **Morale technology (Year 2-10):** Art, music, storytelling, social gatherings. Long darkness breaks minds. Community is survival.

:::note
**Key insight:** Arctic settlements have fundamentally different constraints. Agriculture phase doesn't happen. Jump straight from hunting/gathering to permanent settlements (Year 2-3 in temperate = permanent settlement here from Year 1). Expect technology advancement to be slower overall, but hunting pressure drives innovation in weapons and boats faster. Population is lower but denser (concentrated hunting grounds). By Year 30, a single Arctic settlement may have fewer people than a temperate town, but also requires far fewer resources.
:::

### Coastal Regions

**Defining traits:** Fish and shellfish as primary protein, salt extraction effortless, maritime trade routes, storm and tsunami risk.

**Unique Advantages:**  
• Fish protein abundant and reliable (less variable than hunting)  
• Salt extraction trivial (evaporation ponds, easy trade item)  
• Boat-building becomes primary technology (wood, sealskin, reeds)  
• Tidal power potential (tidal mills work well)  
• Seaweed as food and fertilizer (iodine, nutrients)  
• Shellfish beds provide long-term protein security  
• Coral and shell provide lime for mortar (important for construction)  
• Natural harbors encourage trade

**Unique Challenges:**  
• Storms damage boats and settlements  
• Tsunamis and storm surge are unpredictable  
• Ocean storms trap ships (seasonal navigation only)  
• Overfishing depletes stocks if not managed  
• Salt and humidity corrode tools and buildings  
• Disease risks from crowded ports and trade

**Priority Sequence for Coastal Settings:**  
  
1\. **Boat building (Year 0-2):** Better boats → better fishing → better trade. This is your highway. Master wooden boat construction, then later sailing.  
  
2\. **Fish preservation (Year 1-2):** Salting and smoking. Dried fish trades globally and keeps for months.  
  
3\. **Navigation (Year 2-10):** Stars, currents, landmarks. Build coastal charts. Enable longer voyages and trade routes.  
  
4\. **Storm-resistant architecture (Year 2-5):** Elevated foundations, storm cellars, reinforced roofs. Normal buildings blow away in hurricanes.  
  
5\. **Salt production (Year 1-3):** Evaporation ponds or salt pans. Trade salt inland (it's valuable and stable).

:::note
**Key insight:** Coastal settlements can skip agriculture entirely and trade fish for grain. Population scales on trade volume, not local production. Boat technology becomes as important as metallurgy. By Year 10, a coastal settlement may be wealthier than an inland one of the same size due to trade. But vulnerability to storms means boom-bust cycles.
:::

### Mountain/Highland Regions

**Defining traits:** Altitude affects cooking and weather, isolation enforces self-sufficiency, water abundance from streams, terrace farming possible, mineral deposits nearby.

**Unique Advantages:**  
• Terraced agriculture supports population on steep slopes  
• Water from mountain streams (abundant, reliable, clean)  
• Hydropower potential (streams drive mills)  
• Mineral deposits often nearby (ore, stone, gems)  
• Timber abundant  
• Natural fortification (difficult to attack)  
• Animal husbandry (goats, sheep thrive on steep terrain)  
• Cool climate year-round (easier food preservation)

**Unique Challenges:**  
• Altitude affects boiling point (cooking takes longer, preservation is different)  
• Winter snow can isolate for months  
• Terrain makes transport hard (no roads for carts)  
• Avalanches and landslides risk  
• Soil erosion on slopes  
• Settlement is spread out (hard to form large cities)  
• Trade with lowlands requires passes (seasonal)

**Priority Sequence for Mountain Settings:**  
  
1\. **Terracing (Year 0-3):** Not optional. Build terraces immediately. They're labor-intensive but they're your foundation for agriculture.  
  
2\. **Livestock for mountain (Year 1-3):** Goats and sheep. Cattle work on lower slopes. These animals thrive where crops struggle.  
  
3\. **Water engineering (Year 1-4):** Channels to divert streams to terraces. Hydropower mills (Year 4-6).  
  
4\. **Winter survival (Year 2-4):** Food storage, hay storage, animal shelter. Mountain winters are harsh.  
  
5\. **Mining (Year 3-8):** Locate and mine iron, copper, tin. Mountains often have deposits. This enables metallurgy faster than lowlands.

:::note
**Key insight:** Mountain settlements are inherently small and isolated. But they're also naturally defended and resource-rich. Expect slower technology advance (isolation), but also expect better-quality products (time, attention) and specialized crafts. By Year 20, a mountain settlement may be poorer than lowland equivalents but more self-sufficient and more technically skilled.
:::

## Seasonal Priority Calendar

Don't treat every day the same. Nature has rhythms. Work with them, not against them. Here's what you should focus on each season, universally applicable across climates (with local adjustments).

### Seasonal Work Wheel (Temperate & Tropical)

![⚡ The Master Technology Roadmap diagram 8](../assets/svgs/technology-roadmap-8.svg)

**Spring (Vernal Equinox → Summer Solstice):**  
• Planting season—all hands to fields  
• Repair tools before hard work begins  
• Animals birth (calves, lambs, piglets)—protect them  
• Repair buildings and structures (easier before summer heat)  
• Build/improve irrigation before growing season  
• Gather spring plants (wild greens, herbs)

**Summer (Summer Solstice → Autumnal Equinox):**  
• Maintaining crops—constant weeding and care  
• Early harvests (berries, early vegetables)  
• Construction of buildings/mills (long daylight hours)  
• Preserving early harvest (drying, fermenting, smoking)  
• Animal care (shearing sheep, managing herds)  
• Long-term projects (mining, road building, infrastructure)

**Autumn (Autumnal Equinox → Winter Solstice):**  
• Main harvest—everything must come in  
• Intensive food preservation (massive effort)  
• Firewood cutting and stacking  
• Winterizing buildings (insulation, repairs)  
• Preparing animals for winter (feeding, shelter)  
• Root cellar stocking

**Winter (Winter Solstice → Vernal Equinox):**  
• Tool making and maintenance (crafts indoors)  
• Textile work (spinning, weaving, sewing)  
• Education and knowledge work (reading, apprenticeships)  
• Planning next year's infrastructure  
• Design new technologies (low-energy thinking work)  
• Storytelling and community building

:::note
**Optimization insight:** High-intensity labor (harvest, construction) happens when your environment supports it. Low-intensity skilled labor (crafting, design) happens during constraint periods (winter, wet seasons). This is how pre-industrial societies scaled. They weren't inefficient—they optimized for human capacity and environmental rhythm.
:::

</section>

<section id="hemisphere-notes">

## ⚡ Hemisphere Notes: Southern Hemisphere Variants

If you're in the Southern Hemisphere, the roadmap still applies. But seasonal timing flips. Here are the key adjustments.

**Seasonal Flip:**  
• Spring happens in September (not March)  
• Summer is December-February (not June-August)  
• Autumn is March-May  
• Winter is June-August (COLD if you're south of 40°S)  
• This affects all planting/harvest schedules. Update your calendar.

**Navigation Changes:**  
• **Polaris is invisible.** You cannot navigate by the North Star.  
• **Southern Cross (Crux) is your guide.** Four bright stars form a cross. The long axis points toward the South Celestial Pole.  
• **Altitude of Southern Cross tells you latitude.** Use it like Polaris in the North.  
• Trade winds blow east-to-west differently (east coast vs. west coast have opposite patterns)

**Agricultural Differences:**  
• Winter wheat planted in April/May (before Southern winter)  
• Corn/maize planted in September/October (Southern spring)  
• Cooler growing season than Northern Hemisphere equivalents  
• If you're below 40°S, growing season is similar to temperate zones but shorter  
• If you're above 40°S, you have tropical/subtropical growing conditions year-round

:::note
**Adjustment timeline:** Take the main roadmap. Shift all dates by 6 months. Everything else (technology sequence, dependencies, population milestones) is identical. The only real difference is that you cannot use Polaris for navigation—use the Southern Cross instead.
:::

</section>

<section id="critical-resources">

:::info-box
**Critical Resource Thresholds and Bottlenecks:** Tin is rarer than copper—Era 3 (Bronze Age) stalls without tin deposits or trade. Iron ore is abundant everywhere—Era 4 (Iron Age) accelerates once furnace technology achieves 1200°C. Forest depletion is the constraint for charcoal-dependent civilizations—unsustainable logging causes metallurgical collapse by Year 20. Coal availability extends metallurgical capacity indefinitely. Saltpeter (for blackpowder-blasting) is geographically concentrated—Era 6 (Renaissance) depends on mining or trade for this single ingredient. Oil availability is irregular—Era 7 (Industrial Revolution) accelerates dramatically in oil-rich regions, plateaus elsewhere. Copper wire availability is THE constraint for Era 8 (Electrical Age)—without sufficient copper, power distribution is impossible. These single-resource bottlenecks have crashed civilizations. Identify your region's limiting resources at Year 0 and plan extraction/trade networks early.
:::

</section>

## First Year Timeline by Climate

Year 1 is critical. You establish patterns that echo for decades. Here's a detailed month-by-month breakdown for temperate and tropical climates. Use this as a template for your climate.

### Temperate Climate (Europe, Eastern North America, East Asia) First Year

**Month 1 (Spring, March-April in Northern Hemisphere):**  
• Days are getting longer. Plant early crops (peas, beans, radishes).  
• Build/repair tools and shelter before the rush.  
• Scout for good farmland—high ground, water access, south-facing slopes.  
• Master fire maintenance (keep hearth alive during damp season).  
• Trap game and fish as weather permits.  
• Gather spring plants (nettle, sorrel, wild onions).

**Month 2-3 (Late Spring, April-May):**  
• Plant main grain crops (barley, rye, wheat depending on region).  
• Plant root crops (turnips, parsnips, beets).  
• Build permanent shelter if not done. Walls, roof, insulation.  
• Create pottery kiln (experiment with clay). This is the bottleneck—start now.  
• Herd any domesticated animals into fenced areas.  
• Create 4-5 stone tools variants (blades, scrapers, axes).

**Month 4-5 (Early Summer, May-June):**  
• Weed fields constantly (this month is pure labor).  
• Fire pottery kiln multiple times. Accept failures. You're learning.  
• Harvest early spring vegetables if growth is fast.  
• Fish heavily. Dry/salt catch for storage.  
• Build storage containers from woven fiber/bark.  
• Extend shelter (storage areas, workshop for pottery).

**Month 6-7 (Mid Summer, June-July):**  
• Harvesting early crops (late peas, early beans, greens).  
• Preserve heavily (drying, fermenting, salting).  
• Build/improve irrigation if needed (channels to fields).  
• Create bone tools (needles, hooks, harpoons) from hunting debris.  
• Start textile work (spindle and thread from plant fiber).  
• Begin construction of mill-race or water channel (long-term project).

**Month 8-9 (Late Summer, July-August):**  
• Grain crops ripen. Prepare harvest tools.  
• Haying season—cut and dry grass for winter animal feed.  
• Preserve meat heavily (smoking, salting). Hunt for winter.  
• Build root cellar or improve storage (this is critical).  
• Create sealed pottery vessels for grain storage.  
• Patch shelter before autumn storms.

**Month 9-10 (Autumn, September-October):**  
• MAIN HARVEST. Everything comes in now.  
• Thrashing grain, processing vegetables, preserving food (90% of effort here).  
• Gather firewood obsessively (need 5-10 cords for winter).  
• Gather kindling and tinder for winter fire-starting.  
• Prepare animal shelter for winter (bedding, weatherproofing).  
• Process leather from hunting (tanning baths take weeks).

**Month 10-12 (Late Autumn & Winter, October-December):**  
• Winter preparation: insulate shelter, patch roof, stock food.  
• Deep storage organization: root cellar, grain silo, smoke house.  
• Tool making from bone, wood, stone (winter crafts).  
• Weaving from prepared fiber (slow work, no rush).  
• Plan Year 2: what worked? What failed? What's next?  
• Community gathering during long nights (morale is survival).

:::note
**Temperate Year 1 Summary:** By December, you have: shelter, crops stored, firewood stacked, animals sheltered, pottery vessels, stone tools, bone tools, some textiles started. You're ready for winter. This is not luxury—it's survival. If you miss the harvest window, people starve. If you don't prepare shelter, people freeze. The entire year is a sprint toward November.
:::

### Tropical Climate (Southeast Asia, Congo Basin, Amazon) First Year

**Month 1-3 (Dry Season, varies by region):**  
• Plant crops: rice in paddies, root vegetables (yam, taro, cassava).  
• Build shelter elevated above ground (mosquito prevention and monsoon prep).  
• Scout water sources and irrigation sites.  
• Fish heavily (dry season = good catches). Smoke and salt heavily.  
• Gather fruit (coconut, mango, papaya depending on season).  
• Experiment with pottery—humidity is lower, kiln fires better.

**Month 4-6 (Monsoon/Early Wet Season):**  
• Monsoon rains arrive—flooding is normal.  
• Rice paddies flood intentionally. Manage water levels.  
• Build raised platforms and elevated storage (flood preparation).  
• Create bamboo structures (faster to harvest than wood).  
• Preserve food aggressively against mold/rot.  
• Hunt before wet season makes travel hard.  
• Maintain pottery production despite humidity.

**Month 7-9 (Full Wet Season/Height of Monsoon):**  
• Wet season farming: weed paddies, protect crops from rot.  
• Fish from flooded areas (abundance).  
• Fermentation work: miso, tempeh, kimchi (wet season preservation).  
• Textile work indoors (wet is better for fiber).  
• Build/improve structure (repairs from last monsoon).  
• Disease pressure is highest—manage malaria prevention (elevation, mosquito netting).

**Month 10 (Monsoon Tail/Early Dry):**  
• Rains slow down. Early harvest of first rice crop possible.  
• Begin drying and sun-preservation of stored food.  
• Plant second crop in paddies (double-cropping is possible).  
• Repair structures damaged in monsoon.  
• Gather wild fruit (mango peak, coconut harvest).

**Month 11-12 (Dry Season Return):**  
• Harvest second crop and store heavily.  
• Gather dried goods before next monsoon.  
• Preserve food: smoking, salting, drying (take advantage of low humidity).  
• Hunt in dry season (easier movement).  
• Plan irrigation for next dry season planting.  
• Tool making during dry weather.

:::note
**Tropical Year 1 Summary:** By month 12, you have: two harvests if lucky, continuous fishing, preserved foods (fermented and dried), mosquito-resistant shelter, bamboo structures, pottery production established. You don't face winter starvation risk, but you face rot and disease risk. Preservation is more about fighting mold than fighting cold. Year 2 is about doubling crops and establishing year-round fishing/gathering.
:::

## The Complete Picture

You now have the entire roadmap. From Day 1 (fire and shelter) to Year 100+ (digital computing). Follow the dependencies. Don't skip steps. Each era builds on the previous one.

**The five critical chains** (fire→metal, clay→literacy, glass→medicine, copper→electricity, mining→industry) are your backbone. Master these first.

**Population scaling** happens when you complete each era. 10 people → 50 → 200 → 1000 → 5000+ as technology advances. By Year 50, you can support cities.
