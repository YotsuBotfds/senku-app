## Routing Note: Immediate Safety First

If you hear coercive control, stalking or phone tracking, a partner blocking the exit or taking the phone, suicide threats being used to control the person, a child saying the home is unsafe, sexual assault with injuries needing urgent help, or the exact phrase "the abuser is in the house right now," do not keep screening here. First action: get the person to immediate physical safety if it can be done safely, and hand off at once to emergency medical, domestic violence, sexual assault, or child-protection response. If sexual assault injuries are present, treat that as urgent medical care first.

If the complaint is "they have barely slept for days," "they seem invincible," "they will not stop talking," or they are suddenly grandiose, reckless, or impossible to slow down, keep them in the acute crisis lane here rather than the everyday anxiety/self-care lane. Treat that pattern as possible acute manic activation and escalate early, especially if judgment is worsening or reality-testing is slipping.

## 2. PTSD & Trauma Recognition

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder is a normal response to abnormal events. Not everyone exposed to trauma develops PTSD, but most will have some trauma symptoms. PTSD is treatable, and peer support and grounding techniques are effective tools for non-therapists.

### PTSD Symptoms (DSM-5 Criteria)

#### Intrusive Memories

-   Flashbacks (feeling as if reliving the event)
-   Nightmares about the event
-   Intrusive thoughts or images
-   Physical reactions to reminders (panic, sweating, racing heart)

#### Avoidance

-   Avoiding places, people, or conversations associated with the trauma
-   Emotional numbing or dissociation
-   Not allowing oneself to think about the event

#### Hypervigilance and Arousal

-   Exaggerated startle response
-   Scanning the environment for threats
-   Difficulty concentrating
-   Sleep disturbance
-   Irritability or aggression

#### Negative Mood and Cognition

-   Negative beliefs about self or world ("I'm broken," "no one can be trusted")
-   Blame of self or others for the trauma
-   Persistent fear, anger, guilt, or shame
-   Loss of interest in activities
-   Emotional detachment from loved ones

### Grounding Techniques for Flashbacks

#### The 5-4-3-2-1 Senses Technique

When experiencing a flashback or panic attack, anchor yourself in the present moment by engaging all five senses:

1.  **Identify 5 things you can SEE:** A wall, a person's face, the sky, a tool, clothing.
2.  **Identify 4 things you can TOUCH:** Feel the ground, your clothing, the air temperature, an object in your hand.
3.  **Identify 3 things you can HEAR:** Wind, voices, animals, water, silence.
4.  **Identify 2 things you can SMELL:** Food, earth, smoke, another person.
5.  **Identify 1 thing you can TASTE:** Salt, sweetness, bitterness, your mouth.

**Why it works:** The brain can only fully process present sensory information. Engaging all senses forces a shift from memory (past trauma) to perception (now).

#### Box Breathing (4-4-4-4)

![Mental Health, Counseling & Community Wellness diagram 2](../assets/svgs/mental-health-counseling-2.svg)

**Box Breathing Instructions:**

1.  Slowly inhale through your nose for 4 counts.
2.  Hold your breath for 4 counts.
3.  Slowly exhale through your mouth for 4 counts.
4.  Hold empty for 4 counts.
5.  Repeat 4-5 times. Do this whenever you notice panic or hypervigilance.

**Why it works:** Panic is driven by rapid breathing. Slowing the breath signals the nervous system that danger has passed. Holding breath slightly increases CO2, which calms the amygdala.

#### Safe Place Visualization

1.  Close eyes. Imagine a place where you felt completely safe—real or imagined. (A childhood home, a forest clearing, a beach.)
2.  Engage all senses: What do you see? Hear? Feel? Smell? Taste?
3.  Notice your body in this place. Are you sitting? Standing? Lying down?
4.  Stay here for 2-5 minutes. When the flashback has passed, slowly return to the present moment.
5.  Practice this daily when calm so you can access it quickly during crisis.

### PTSD Symptom Clusters: Recognition Framework

The DSM-5 recognizes four key symptom clusters. Understanding these helps you recognize when someone is at risk and what specific interventions may help.

:::info-box
### The Four PTSD Symptom Clusters

**Cluster 1: Re-Experiencing (Intrusion)**
- Flashbacks, nightmares, intrusive images, panic reactions to reminders
- **Body system involved:** Amygdala (fear center) keeps replaying the threat
- **Intervention:** Grounding, sensory awareness, safe place visualization
- **Field recognition:** Person suddenly dissociates, sees something that "takes them back," or is triggered by sensory reminder

**Cluster 2: Avoidance**
- Emotional numbing, avoiding places/people, not thinking about event, dissociation
- **Body system involved:** Prefrontal cortex shutdown; person is in "freeze" mode
- **Intervention:** Gradual exposure to safe reminders, rebuilding sense of safety
- **Field recognition:** Person withdraws from community, won't discuss event, seems emotionally flat

**Cluster 3: Hyperarousal (Arousal/Reactivity)**
- Exaggerated startle, hypervigilance, sleep issues, irritability, recklessness
- **Body system involved:** Sympathetic nervous system locked in "on" position
- **Intervention:** Box breathing, progressive muscle relaxation, physical activity
- **Field recognition:** Person is jumpy, always scanning, irritable, aggressive over minor things

**Cluster 4: Negative Cognitions and Mood**
- Self-blame, negative beliefs about self/world, persistent fear/anger/guilt/shame, social withdrawal
- **Body system involved:** Prefrontal cortex (reasoning) disconnected from limbic system (emotion)
- **Intervention:** Narrative processing, meaning-making, peer support, identifying strengths
- **Field recognition:** Person expresses hopelessness ("I'm broken," "I can't trust anyone"), blames self, lost interest in activities
:::

### Field Assessment Screening for PTSD

Use these questions to assess if someone may have PTSD. Positive answers suggest PTSD symptoms are present and intervention may be needed.

:::tip
### Simple PTSD Screening (4-Question Tool)

1. **Have you experienced a traumatic event involving actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence?** (Direct trauma or witnessed/heard about it)
2. **Do you have unwanted memories, dreams, or flashbacks about the event?** (Re-experiencing)
3. **Do you avoid reminders of the event—places, people, activities, or thoughts?** (Avoidance)
4. **Have you noticed you're more on-edge, startled easily, or having trouble sleeping?** (Hyperarousal)

**Interpretation:**
- 4 "Yes" answers = High likelihood of PTSD; recommend regular support and monitoring
- 3 "Yes" answers = Moderate likelihood; may benefit from structured intervention
- 2 "Yes" answers = Some symptoms; provide grounding skills and peer support
- 1 or 0 "Yes" answers = Unlikely PTSD, but normal trauma response; monitor over time

**Note:** This is a screening tool, not a diagnosis. Professional assessment is preferred if available, but in field settings, this simple tool guides intervention decisions.
:::

## 3. Depression, Anxiety & Mood Disorders

Beyond general depression and anxiety, it is important to distinguish between depression and normal grief, understand the spectrum of anxiety disorders, and recognize when these conditions require intervention.

### Distinguishing Depression from Grief and Situational Distress

Three similar-appearing conditions require different management approaches.

<div class="mental-section">

#### Normal Grief

**Trigger:** Loss (death, separation, major life change).

**Characteristics:**
- **Timeline:** Waves of intense sadness, gradually lessening over weeks-to-months
- **Functional:** Grief person still eats, sleeps, works (reduced efficiency but not absent)
- **Triggers:** Memories, anniversaries, reminders prompt intensified sadness
- **Emotion range:** Sadness predominates, but moments of normalcy, laughter, connection

**Healthy process:** Allow feeling, talk about loss, connect with others, create rituals (memorial, ceremony).

**When grief becomes concern:** If person still completely non-functional after 6+ months, or expresses suicidal ideation, shift to depression protocol.

#### Situational Distress (Adjustment Disorder)

**Trigger:** Stressor (job loss, move, illness, conflict).

**Characteristics:**
- **Timeline:** 3–6 months duration from stressor onset
- **Severity:** Distressing but proportional to stressor
- **Functional:** Reduced but some function maintained
- **Symptoms:** Anxiety, sadness, irritability, but no guilt/worthlessness/anhedonia

**Management:**
- **Problem-solving:** Address underlying stressor (find job, resolve conflict, adapt to change)
- **Support:** Talk with trusted people; validation of difficulties
- **Time:** Usually resolves as person adjusts
- **Watch for:** Progression to depression if stressor unresolved or person isolates

#### Clinical Depression (Major Depressive Disorder)

**Definition:** Persistent depressed mood AND loss of interest/pleasure in activities, lasting >2 weeks, causing functional impairment.

**Diagnostic criteria (must have 5+ symptoms):**
- Depressed mood most of the day
- Loss of interest/pleasure (anhedonia) in activities
- Significant weight change or appetite change
- Sleep disturbance (insomnia or hypersomnia)
- Fatigue or loss of energy
- Feelings of worthlessness or guilt
- Difficulty concentrating
- Psychomotor changes (slowed or agitated movement)
- Recurrent thoughts of death or suicide

**Duration:** >2 weeks; causes functional impairment.

**Characteristics distinct from grief:**
- **Anhedonia:** Nothing brings joy; numbness predominates
- **Guilt:** Self-blame, shame ("I'm worthless," "I'm a burden")
- **Suicidal ideation:** Thoughts that death is preferable
- **Functional collapse:** Unable to care for self (hygiene, eating, basic tasks)

</div>

### Anxiety Disorder Types and Recognition

Anxiety ranges from adaptive (protective) to pathological (disabling).

#### Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)

**Characteristics:**
- Persistent worry about multiple domains (health, finances, relationships, work)
- Difficulty controlling worry
- Duration >6 months
- Accompanied by physical symptoms (tension, restlessness, difficulty sleeping)

**Severity:** Mild (bothersome) to severe (debilitating; inability to work/function).

**Management:** Relaxation techniques, cognitive restructuring (challenging worry thoughts), problem-solving for legitimate concerns, exercise.

#### Panic Disorder

**Presentation:** Sudden, intense fear with physical symptoms (see panic attack section below).

**Frequency:** Attacks recur unpredictably; person develops anxiety ABOUT anxiety (anticipatory dread).

**Complication:** Often leads to agoraphobia (avoidance of situations where panic might occur), progressively restricting life.

**Management:** Breathing techniques, exposure therapy (gradually re-entering feared situations), acceptance of anxiety (not fighting it).

#### Social Anxiety Disorder

**Trigger:** Social situations (public speaking, eating in front of others, crowds).

**Symptoms:** Intense fear, blushing, sweating, trembling, worry about judgment.

**Avoidance:** Person increasingly avoids social situations, leading to isolation and depression.

**Management:** Graduated exposure (start small, build tolerance), social support, acceptance.

#### Specific Phobia

**Trigger:** Single object or situation (heights, animals, flying, enclosed spaces).

**Severity:** Can range from minor (mild avoidance) to severe (total functional impairment).

**Management:** Exposure therapy (graded, starting with less anxiety-provoking version of trigger), relaxation techniques.

### Panic Attack: Recognition and Management

Panic attack is sudden onset of intense fear with physical symptoms, often seeming to come "out of nowhere."

#### Symptoms (Peak within 10 minutes)

<div class="crisis-protocol">

| Physical | Cognitive | Behavioral |
|----------|-----------|-----------|
| Chest pain/pressure | Fear of dying | Pacing, restlessness |
| Shortness of breath | Fear of losing control | Escape/avoidance |
| Heart racing (tachycardia) | Fear of fainting | Clinging to safe person |
| Dizziness, lightheadedness | Feeling detached (derealization) | Calling for help |
| Sweating | Feeling unreal (depersonalization) | |
| Trembling, shaking | Catastrophic thoughts | |
| Nausea | | |
| Numbness/tingling | | |

**Key feature:** Symptoms feel life-threatening (dying, losing mind, losing control) but person is physically safe.

</div>

#### Self-Management Techniques

**During panic attack (when it's happening):**

1. **Name it:** "This is a panic attack. It's not dangerous. It will pass in 10–20 minutes."
2. **Breathing:** Slow, deep breathing (in for 4 count, hold 2, out for 6)
   - Hyperventilation (rapid breathing) causes dizziness; slow breathing reverses it
3. **Ground yourself:** 5 senses technique
   - **5 things seen:** Name 5 objects you see in detail
   - **4 things touched:** Feel 4 textures (fabric, wall, skin)
   - **3 things heard:** Identify 3 sounds
   - **2 things smelled:** Notice 2 scents
   - **1 thing tasted:** Identify a taste
4. **Movement:** Gentle movement (walking, stretching) or exertion (pushups, running) can discharge the adrenaline
5. **Positive self-talk:** "I am safe. My body is responding to perceived threat, not real threat. This will pass."

**After panic attack:**
- Rest; do not judge self ("Why did I panic?")
- Normal activity; don't avoid due to embarrassment
- Avoid caffeine, alcohol (can trigger more panic)

#### Preventing Future Attacks

- **Identify triggers:** Certain situations, thoughts, or physical sensations that precede panic
- **Gradual exposure:** Re-enter feared situations slowly (exposure therapy). Each time, anxiety lessens.
- **Acceptance:** Accept that anxiety will come; don't fight it (resistance strengthens anxiety)

## 4. Addiction & Withdrawal Recognition

In collapse contexts, substance use (and withdrawal) will be widespread. People will use substances to numb pain, manage anxiety, stay alert, or simply maintain habit. Helpers must understand withdrawal syndromes—some are medically dangerous, others are miserable but survivable. The goal is harm reduction and community-based recovery.

### Alcohol Withdrawal (Medically Dangerous)

:::danger
**Alcohol withdrawal can be FATAL without medical intervention.** Seizures and delirium tremens (DTs) occur 24-72 hours after last drink. Death rate without treatment is 5-15% in severe cases. Do not attempt cold turkey without constant supervision and support.
:::

#### Timeline and Symptoms

-   **6-24 hours:** Tremors, sweating, anxiety, headache, nausea.
-   **24-48 hours:** Hallucinations (seeing/hearing things), paranoia.
-   **24-72 hours:** SEIZURES (life-threatening). This is the most dangerous period.
-   **48-96 hours:** Delirium tremens (DTs)—confusion, hallucinations, extreme agitation, hyperthermia. Mortality rate 5-15% without treatment.

#### Management Without Medical Help

If benzodiazepines are available (from medical supplies, natural sources like valerian, or prescription hoards):

-   **Gradual taper:** Never abrupt cessation. Reduce alcohol consumption by 10-20% every 1-2 days.
-   **Benzodiazepine equivalents:** If available, benzodiazepines can substitute for alcohol and be tapered safely. Example: diazepam 5mg ≈ 1 drink. Taper by 10% every 3-7 days.
-   **Supportive care:** Constant supervision during withdrawal (seizure/fall risk). Thiamine (vitamin B1), magnesium, hydration. Cool environment (DTs involve hyperthermia). Reassurance and presence.

#### High-Risk Signs Requiring Immediate Help

-   Seizures or convulsions.
-   Extreme confusion or disorientation.
-   Dangerous behavior (violence, self-harm).
-   Uncontrollable tremors or muscle rigidity.
-   Temperature above 103°F (39.4°C).

### Benzodiazepine Withdrawal (Medically Dangerous)

Similar timeline and dangers as alcohol. Seizures, DTs, and death are possible. Tapered discontinuation over weeks is essential. Do not attempt to manage severe benzodiazepine withdrawal without medical consultation if possible.

### Nicotine Withdrawal (Unpleasant, Not Dangerous)

#### Timeline and Symptoms

-   **Peak:** Days 1-3. Intense irritability, anxiety, inability to concentrate, insomnia, strong cravings.
-   **Subsiding:** Weeks 2-4. Cravings decrease, mood stabilizes, sleep improves.
-   **Maintenance:** Months 1-3. Occasional cravings triggered by stress or specific situations.

#### Management

-   **Gradual reduction:** Cut cigarettes/tobacco by 25% every 1-2 weeks.
-   **Distraction:** Physical activity, work, time with others. Nicotine withdrawal creates boredom and anxiety; activity helps both.
-   **Herbal support:** Mugwort tea, oat straw, or valerian can calm anxiety.
-   **Social support:** Tell others you're quitting. Ask them to help distract you during cravings.
-   **Reassurance:** It's temporary. The worst is done by day 7. Cravings become manageable within 2-4 weeks.

### Opioid Withdrawal (Miserable, Survivable)

#### Timeline and Symptoms

-   **6-12 hours:** Anxiety, restlessness, muscle aches, sweating.
-   **24-48 hours:** Peak symptoms. Severe body aches, insomnia, nausea, diarrhea, dilated pupils, rapid heartbeat. Feels like severe flu.
-   **Days 5-10:** Symptoms decline, but insomnia and achiness persist.
-   **Weeks 2-4:** Psychological cravings without physical symptoms.

#### Management

-   **Comfort measures:** Warm baths, heating pads, massage. Hydration and electrolyte support (salt, potassium).
-   **Sleep support:** Valerian, passionflower, or melatonin if available. Sleep deprivation worsens symptoms.
-   **Medical support (if available):** Methadone or buprenorphine can ease withdrawal and prevent relapse. Tapering over weeks is safer than cold turkey.
-   **Antidiarrheal:** If available, loperamide (Imodium) provides some relief.
-   **Constant presence:** Do not leave the person alone. This is when relapse risk is highest. They need activity, distraction, reassurance, and monitoring.
-   **Physical activity:** Walking, labor, or exercise reduces cravings and improves mood.

## 5. Children's Mental Health Crisis Recognition

Children are resilient but not invulnerable. Their mental health depends on stable caregivers, predictability, understanding, and appropriate honesty about the situation. Regression is normal; shielding them completely from reality is harmful.

### Normal Regression and Stress Responses

Under stress, children often revert to earlier behaviors:

-   **Bedwetting (enuresis):** Common in anxious or traumatized children who had previously developed bladder control.
-   **Thumb sucking, baby talk:** Regression to soothing behaviors.
-   **Clinginess:** Separation anxiety increases. Child becomes attached to caregiver.
-   **Sleep problems:** Nightmares, difficulty falling asleep, sleeping in caregiver's bed.
-   **Eating changes:** Picky eating, overeating, or refusing food.

**Response:** These are normal, temporary adaptations to stress. Do not shame the child. Increase reassurance and physical comfort. Regression usually resolves once the child feels secure again.

### Children's Grief by Age

**Ages 2-5 (Preschool):** Limited understanding of permanence. Expect regression, clinginess, repeated questions about death. Use concrete, simple language. Avoid euphemisms ("they're sleeping," "they went away"). Say: "Grandpa's body stopped working. He died. You can remember him and look at his picture." Maintain routines and reassure they will be cared for.

**Ages 6-11 (School-age):** Beginning to understand death is permanent but may believe it only happens to old people. May have magical thinking ("if I hadn't been bad, they wouldn't have died"). Reassure: death is not punishment. May want factual details. Allow questions and answer honestly. May want to help with memorial or continue the deceased's traditions.

**Ages 12+ (Adolescent):** Cognitively understand death's finality but may intellectualize to avoid emotion. May appear "fine" while struggling privately. May feel ashamed of grief. Include in decision-making and rituals. Provide peer support. Allow various expressions: talking, physical activity, creative work, silence.

### When to Seek Help

-   Self-harm or harm to others.
-   Complete withdrawal from peers and activities.
-   Suicidal ideation.
-   Inability to function in school or responsibilities.
-   Symptoms that worsen or persist beyond a few months.

## 6. Severe Mental Illness: Psychosis, Mania & Severe Depression

Severe mental illness (SMI) encompasses conditions where reality testing is compromised, mood is severely dysregulated, or functioning is severely impaired. While professional psychiatric care is ideal, austere environments require community members to recognize and manage these crises.

### Acute Mania or Activated Bipolar State

Sometimes the immediate complaint is not sadness or fear but a person who has become dangerously "up." This can sound like "they have not slept in days but they are still wired," "they think they are unstoppable," or "they are talking so fast nobody can interrupt."

**Complaint-first red flags:**
- Very little or no sleep for 2 or more days without seeming tired
- Talking nonstop, pressured speech, or jumping rapidly between ideas
- Feeling invincible, specially chosen, unusually powerful, or grandiose
- Reckless spending, driving, sex, fights, travel, or other high-risk behavior
- Agitation, irritability, or escalating conflict when others try to slow them down
- Sliding into paranoia, hallucinations, or severe confusion

**Routing rule:** This is not routine anxiety, motivation, or just "a lot of energy." If someone has gone days with almost no sleep and is also grandiose, reckless, invincible-seeming, or talking nonstop, treat it as an acute mental health crisis and seek urgent evaluation.

### Acute Psychosis (Losing Touch with Reality)

**What is psychosis?** Psychosis is a break from shared reality. The person's internal experience (thoughts, perceptions) does not match external reality, and they believe their experience is real.

**Presentation types:**

**Paranoid psychosis:**
- Fixed false beliefs (delusions) that others are plotting, poisoning, tracking, or persecuting
- May escalate to violence (defending against perceived threat)
- Example: "They're putting poison in the water. I'm not drinking."
- Often lucid in other areas; can discuss nondelusional topics coherently

**Bizarre/disorganized psychosis:**
- Incoherent speech (word salad: sentences don't connect logically)
- Disorganized behavior (strange movements, inappropriate affect, bizarre appearance)
- Delusions may be bizarre (impossible, like "aliens implanted device in brain")
- Significant functional impairment

**Hallucinatory psychosis:**
- Perceptions without external stimulus: hears voices, sees things others don't
- Often voices are critical, commanding, or threatening
- May respond to internal stimuli (talking to unseen person, reacting to internal commands)
- Can be terrifying to the afflicted person

**Common triggers in collapse scenarios:**
- Abrupt medication cessation (psychiatric medications create dependency; stopping causes relapse in 1-4 weeks)
- Sleep deprivation, including days of little or no sleep during acute manic activation
- High fever or medical illness (delirium mimics psychosis; resolve fever and it clears)
- Trauma or extreme stress (stress-induced brief psychosis)
- Substance use (stimulants, hallucinogens, marijuana in vulnerable individuals)

**Recognition checklist:**
- Beliefs/perceptions that others don't share and don't make logical sense
- Person cannot be reasoned out of belief ("But I saw it with my own eyes")
- Behavior is significantly unusual or dangerous
- Onset relatively acute (days to weeks, not years of gradual change)
- May have clear timeline ("started after the event" or "started when I ran out of pills")

**Immediate safety assessment:**
- **Is the person a danger to self?** ("Voices telling me to jump")
- **Is the person a danger to others?** ("They're going to kill us; we need to kill them first")
- **Is the person unable to care for self?** (Too disorganized to eat, drink, stay warm)
- If any "yes," this is a mental health emergency requiring higher level of care/restraint/safety monitoring

### Management of Acute Psychosis

:::warning
Psychosis is neurobiological; it cannot be argued away or reasoned with. Attempting to convince someone their delusion is false often worsens paranoia ("You're in on it; you're part of the conspiracy"). Validation paired with reality does not mean agreeing with the delusion.
:::

**Do:**
- **Remain calm and non-threatening.** Slow movements, low voice, open body posture
- **Validate emotion without validating delusion.** "I can see you're frightened. That must feel terrifying." (NOT "Yes, they're poisoning the water.")
- **Establish safety first.** Remove potential weapons; ensure person is in safe location; have staff nearby
- **Avoid confrontation.** Do not argue about the false belief. Redirect to factual, simple topics.
- **Maintain routine.** Predictable meals, sleep, activity reduce anxiety
- **Simplify environment.** Reduce stimulation (noise, lights, crowds)
- **Offer help.** "I'd like to help. What do you need right now?"
- **Consider medical causes.** High fever? Recent medication changes? Withdrawal? Blood sugar extremes? Hypoxia? Rule out delirium first.

**Don't:**
- Argue about the delusion ("That's not real!")
- Play along with the delusion ("Yes, they're poisoning the water")
- Leave the person alone if there's imminent danger
- Attempt physical restraint unless trained and necessary for safety
- Dismiss the distress ("It's just in your head")

**Expected course:**
- Acute psychosis often peaks within 24-72 hours
- With antipsychotic medication: gradual improvement over days-weeks
- Without medication: may persist for weeks to months, especially if untreated bipolar or schizophrenia
- Reorientation: gently provide reality: "It's Tuesday morning. You're safe. The water is fine."

### Severe Depression with Suicidal Ideation

**Severe depression signs:**
- No interest in anything (anhedonia persists despite positive events)
- Inability to get out of bed for days
- Thoughts like "I'm worthless" or "Everyone would be better if I died"
- Sleep: either sleeping all day or severe insomnia
- Appetite: significant loss or overeating
- Duration: most of the day, most days, for weeks

:::danger
**Take ALL suicidal ideation seriously. Ask directly:** "Are you thinking about killing yourself?" Direct questions do NOT increase suicide risk; they open communication. Untreated suicidal ideation has a 5-10% fatality rate over 10 years. This is preventable with intervention.
:::

**Suicidal ideation levels:**

**Passive ideation ("I wish I were dead but won't act"):**
- No plan, no intent, no access to means
- Desire to escape pain but no will to act
- Risk: Low in immediate term; monitor for progression

**Active ideation without plan ("I want to kill myself"):**
- Desire + thought, but no specific method or timeline
- Risk: Moderate; high if access to lethal means exists

**Active ideation with plan and means ("I will hang myself with rope I've hidden"):**
- Specific method, timeline, and means
- Risk: Very high; imminent danger

**Imminent suicide risk (action):**
- Writing goodbye notes
- Giving away possessions
- Sudden calmness after depression (decision to act creates relief)
- Actively obtaining means (medication hoarding, weapon access)
- Risk: Extreme; intervention needed immediately

**Immediate intervention for active suicidal ideation:**

- **Remove access to means:** Secure medications, weapons, ropes, harmful objects
- **Assign 1:1 monitoring:** Someone is with person at all times (no bathroom door locks, no private access to means)
- **Medical stabilization:** Low glucose, infections, medications (withdrawal) can worsen depression and increase risk; treat medical issues
- **Establish safety plan:** "If you feel like you're about to hurt yourself, you'll come to me/ring bell/tell a staff member"
- **Antidepressant + anti-anxiety if available:** SSRIs (sertraline 50 mg daily, escitalopram 10 mg daily); lorazepam for acute anxiety
- **Do NOT be alone:** Isolation worsens suicidal thinking
- **Validate without agreeing:** "I hear you're in terrible pain. I'm glad you told me. We'll get through this together."
- **Follow-up:** Daily brief check-ins; assess for mood improvement over 1-2 weeks; if no improvement and ongoing risk, may need restraint or separation

**High flags requiring escalation:**
- Active psychosis (false beliefs justifying suicide)
- Substance intoxication + suicidality (impulsivity increases)
- Recent loss or humiliation (acute grief + depression)
- Older age male (highest completed suicide rate)
- Access to lethal means they've acquired
- Prior suicide attempt (increases risk of completion)

## 7. Quick Decision Tree: When to Escalate Care

:::info-box
**Use this flowchart to quickly assess whether peer support is appropriate or if professional/emergency intervention is needed.**

**START: Person in psychological distress**

↓ **Are they in immediate physical danger or danger to others?**
- YES → STOP all support. Emergency response. Call for help / Medical evacuation.
- NO → Continue.

↓ **Are they actively suicidal, homicidal, or experiencing overdose/severe intoxication?**
- YES → STOP all support. Emergency response. Constant supervision. Seek medical help.
- NO → Continue.

↓ **Are they experiencing acute psychosis, severe delusions, hallucinations they believe are real, or days of little/no sleep with grandiose, invincible, reckless, or talking-nonstop behavior?**
- YES → Approach cautiously. Reassurance without validating delusions or grandiose beliefs. "I see you're frightened. Let me help you stay safe." Seek urgent medical evaluation.
- NO → Continue.

↓ **Are they experiencing grief, loss, anxiety, panic, mild-moderate depression, or trauma symptoms?**
- YES → Peer support is appropriate. Use grounding, active listening, behavioral activation, social connection.
- NO → Unknown condition.

↓ **Have they responded to 1-2 weeks of consistent peer support?**
- YES → Continue support. Check in regularly. Encourage group participation.
- NO → Escalate. Seek professional evaluation. Condition may require treatment beyond peer support.

↓ **END: Person has appropriate level of support.**
:::
