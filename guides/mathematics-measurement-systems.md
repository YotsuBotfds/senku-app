## Overview

Modern civilization depends on precision measurement and calculation, yet these capabilities existed long before electronic devices. Roman engineers built aqueducts spanning 50+ miles with error margins of inches. Mayan mathematicians tracked celestial cycles accurate to the day over centuries. Egyptian surveyors divided land and designed pyramids using only ropes, stakes, and geometric principles.

Post-collapse, accurate measurement and calculation remain essential for construction, land management, resource allocation, and navigation. This guide covers practical mathematics (arithmetic, geometry, ratios), measurement techniques using non-mechanical tools, and systems for recording and transferring quantitative knowledge. The goal is competence sufficient for everyday survival tasks without calculators or precise instruments.

## Mathematical Foundations for Survival

### Arithmetic Without Pen and Paper

Before writing, civilizations tracked quantities using tally marks, knotted cords (quipu), and counting boards (abacus).

**Tally systems:**

The simplest method: mark one line per unit. Group marks in fives (cross-hatching) for quick scanning. A tally of 47 items: 9 groups of five plus 2 single marks.

**Quipu (Inca knotted-cord system):**

Quipu record quantities by tying knots in cords. Each cord represents a category (e.g., grain stores, population); the number of knots on each cord encodes the quantity.

1. **Cord preparation**: Use twisted plant fiber (cotton, alpaca wool). Main cord runs horizontally.
2. **Pendant cords**: Tie secondary cords to the main cord, leaving 3–4 inches of loose cord.
3. **Knot system**: Tie knots in the pendant cords, spacing them about 1 inch apart. Each knot position represents a decimal place (units, tens, hundreds).
4. **Recording**: For the number 347, tie 7 knots in the units position, 4 knots in the tens position, 3 knots in the hundreds position.
5. **Storage and recall**: Quipu hang vertically. Reading top-to-bottom reproduces the original quantity. A collection of quipu records an entire census or inventory.

**Abacus (counting frame):**

An abacus speeds mental arithmetic by representing numbers physically.

1. **Construction**: Build a wooden frame with vertical rods. String beads or stone pieces on each rod.
2. **Bead arrangement**: Place 10 beads per rod (or 5 beads if using a bisected frame). Group beads above and below a center bar.
3. **Operations**:
   - **Addition**: Represent the first number by pushing beads upward. Represent the second by pushing additional beads upward. Count the total.
   - **Subtraction**: Represent the minuend; remove beads for the subtrahend; count remaining.
   - **Multiplication**: Use repeated addition or physical grouping of beads (treating rows as units, tens, hundreds).

A skilled abacus user performs arithmetic as fast as someone using pen and paper.

:::tip
Practice mental arithmetic daily. Memorize multiplication tables (1–12 × 1–12) and simple division facts. In survival situations, quick mental calculation is more reliable than written work that could be lost or misread.
:::

### Geometry and Proportions

Geometric relationships enable construction, land division, and resource estimation without measurement tools.

**The right angle (Pythagorean triple):**

Ancient builders used a 3-4-5 right triangle rope to establish right angles.

1. **Rope preparation**: Tie a rope into a closed loop with 12 equal segments. Mark the loop at the 3rd, 7th (3+4), and 12th knots.
2. **Placement**: Peg one knot at the desired corner. Stretch the side of length 3 along one direction; stretch the side of length 4 perpendicular. The knots form a 90° angle.
3. **Scaling**: For a larger right angle, scale all sides proportionally (e.g., 6-8-10 or 9-12-15).

**Similar triangles:**

If two triangles have the same angles, their sides are proportional. Use this to measure heights and distances.

**Example: Measuring a tree height**

1. Stand a known distance from the tree (e.g., 50 feet).
2. Hold a stick at arm's length; angle it to align with the top of the tree from your eye to the stick's end.
3. Without moving the stick, turn it horizontal. Mark where the stick points (a stake or person).
4. Measure the distance from you to the mark.
5. **Calculation**: If you're 50 feet from the tree and the stick points to a mark 30 feet away, the tree is approximately (50 ft ÷ 30 ft) × stick length = 1.67 × stick length.

**Ratios and scaling:**

Proportional relationships allow upscaling or downscaling recipes, construction plans, and agricultural yields.

- **Recipe scaling**: If a bread recipe calls for 2 cups flour and 1 cup water (2:1 ratio), doubling requires 4 cups flour and 2 cups water. Halving requires 1 cup flour and 0.5 cup water.
- **Construction scaling**: Blueprint proportions (1 inch = 10 feet) are ratio-based. A wall 30 feet long appears as 3 inches on a 1:10 scale plan.

## Measurement Techniques Without Instruments

### Linear Measurement

**Body-based units:**

Before standardized measures, humans used body proportions.

- **Cubit**: Distance from elbow to fingertip (~18 inches). Used in ancient Egypt and Mesopotamia for construction.
- **Pace**: A single step (~2.5 feet). Used by Roman soldiers and land surveyors to measure distances.
- **Fathom**: Arm span (~6 feet). Used for measuring water depth.
- **Span**: Distance from thumb to little finger when hand is spread (~8 inches).

For consistent measurement, establish a local standard: "Our pace is 30 inches. Our cubit is 18 inches." Record these standards; train community members to replicate them.

**Rope and chain measurement:**

1. **Rope preparation**: Take a rope or cord and mark it at known intervals using dyed wool threads or knots. Intervals of 1 cubit, 1 pace, or 1 fathom are practical.
2. **Calibration**: Lay the marked rope on flat ground alongside a reference distance (e.g., a building whose length is known). Adjust marks if necessary.
3. **Field use**: Stretch the marked rope along the distance to be measured. Count intervals and fractional lengths.

**Wheel circumference measurement:**

If a wheel of known diameter is available:

1. Mark a point on the wheel and the ground.
2. Roll the wheel forward one complete rotation until the marked point touches ground again.
3. Measure the distance traveled (the circumference).
4. For future measurements, roll the wheel the desired distance and count rotations. Distance = rotations × circumference.

:::info-box
Consistency matters more than absolute accuracy. If all measurements in a community use the same marked rope or wheel, internal consistency is maintained even if the absolute standard drifts over time.
:::

### Area Measurement

**Counting unit squares:**

The simplest method: divide the area into unit squares (using a marked rope as the unit).

1. Establish a unit square—e.g., a 1-pace × 1-pace area.
2. Lay out the area to be measured using ropes. Mark the boundaries.
3. Count how many unit squares fit within the boundary. For partial squares at the edges, estimate the fraction (half square, quarter square, etc.).
4. Record the total.

**Trapezoid and triangle areas:**

For non-rectangular land parcels:

- **Triangle**: Measure base and height (perpendicular distance from base to opposite corner). Area ≈ (base × height) ÷ 2.
- **Trapezoid**: Measure the two parallel sides and the perpendicular distance between them. Area ≈ ((side1 + side2) ÷ 2) × height.

**Surveying irregular boundaries:**

1. **Triangulation**: Divide the area into triangles by running straight lines (ropes) from one corner to all other corners.
2. **Measurement**: Measure the base and height of each triangle.
3. **Summation**: Sum the areas of all triangles.

### Volume and Capacity

**Cubic measurement:**

For soil, grain, or water storage:

1. Establish a unit cube (e.g., 1 pace × 1 pace × 1 pace).
2. Measure the length, width, and height of the container or area in paces.
3. Volume ≈ length × width × height (in cubic paces).

**Liquid capacity:**

A standard vessel (clay jug, wooden barrel) becomes the unit of measure.

1. Fill a standard vessel to the brim with water. Mark the vessel at this level—this is 1 unit.
2. Use this vessel to measure and transfer liquids.
3. For smaller quantities, divide the vessel: halfmarks and quarter-marks allow fractions.

**Dry goods (grain, flour):**

Use a standard basket or container. Measure quantities in baskets. For partial baskets, use a smaller standard (e.g., a hand-sized bowl = 1/4 basket).

:::warning
Container measurements vary with material density. A basket of flour weighs more than a basket of salt due to density differences. For precision, record both volume (baskets) and estimated weight when possible.
:::

## Practical Calculations for Survival

### Proportional Allocation

Distribute resources based on need or contribution.

**Example: Grain distribution to a community of 50 people, with 500 units of grain to be stored for a 6-month winter.**

- Ration per person per month: 500 units ÷ 50 people ÷ 6 months = 1.67 units/person/month (or 50 units/person/month to avoid fractions, approximately 8.3 units/month total available).
- Per day per person: 8.3 units/person/month ÷ 30 days/month ≈ 0.28 units/person/day.

Adjustment: If some people work harder (builders, hunters) and others don't (elderly, children), adjust proportions accordingly. A builder might receive 1.5× ration; a child might receive 0.7× ration. Verify the total still equals 500 units.

### Material Estimation

**Construction material quantities:**

For a stone wall:

1. Determine the length, height, and thickness (e.g., 100 feet long, 8 feet high, 3 feet thick).
2. Volume = 100 × 8 × 3 = 2,400 cubic feet.
3. Estimate stone density: A cubic foot of stone (depending on type) weighs 100–150 pounds. A reasonable estimate is 125 pounds/cubic foot.
4. Total stone needed ≈ 2,400 × 125 = 300,000 pounds (or 150 tons).
5. Labor estimate: Experienced stoneworkers place ~1 cubic foot per person-day. Estimate 2,400 person-days of labor.

**Crop yield estimation:**

If previous years' records exist:

1. Known yield: 50 acres produced 1,200 bushels last year.
2. Yield per acre: 1,200 ÷ 50 = 24 bushels/acre.
3. For a field of 75 acres this year (with similar conditions): 75 × 24 = 1,800 bushels expected.

Adjust for known variations (drought, flood, improved soil) by increasing or decreasing the estimate proportionally.

### Scaling Recipes and Medicines

**Bread recipe scaling:**

Base recipe (makes 1 loaf):
- 2 cups flour
- 1 cup water
- 1 teaspoon salt
- 1/2 teaspoon yeast or sourdough starter

For 10 loaves, multiply all ingredients by 10:
- 20 cups flour
- 10 cups water
- 10 teaspoons salt
- 5 teaspoons yeast

**Medicine dosage scaling:**

If a medicine recipe calls for 1 handful of willow bark per 2 cups of water, and you need to prepare a batch for 20 people, each receiving 1 cup:

- Standard batch (1 cup per person × 2 people) = 2 cups water → requires 1 handful bark.
- For 20 people (20 cups water) → requires (20 ÷ 2) × 1 = 10 handfuls bark.

:::tip
Document all recipes and calculations. A written record (carved into wood or on preserved paper) allows reproducibility and avoids errors that arise from relying on memory.
:::

## Recording and Transmitting Quantitative Knowledge

### Written Notation Systems

**Tally marks:**

Simple, requires no ink. Each item = one vertical line; every fifth item is marked with a diagonal cross through four previous lines. Easy to count and audit.

**Roman numerals:**

I = 1, V = 5, X = 10, L = 50, C = 100, D = 500, M = 1,000.

Examples: VII = 7, XIV = 14, MCCCXLII = 1,342.

Roman numerals are less efficient than place-value systems (e.g., 1,342 in Roman requires 7 characters; in Arabic numerals, only 4). However, they require no zero symbol and are resistant to misinterpretation when handwritten.

**Place-value notation (Hindu-Arabic system):**

Requires a zero symbol and ten digit characters (0–9). Numbers are written positionally: 347 means 3 hundreds, 4 tens, and 7 ones.

Advantages: Compact, efficient for large numbers, and suited to calculation (especially with an abacus or written algorithms).

### Geometric Notation for Measurement

Surveying plans use geometric symbols to record field measurements:

- **Rectangle**: Property boundaries.
- **Triangle**: Triangulation points used for surveying.
- **Arrow or line with length**: Distance and direction.
- **Circle or cross**: Reference point or landmark.

Document all plans with written notes explaining symbols and scales.

### Oral Transmission of Calculation Methods

**Memorization techniques:**

1. **Mnemonic rhyme**: Encode sequences in verse. Example, for a market price list: "Two fishes fetch five coppers, three loaves cost seven." The rhythm aids memory.
2. **Knuckle mnemonics**: Use knuckles and finger segments to encode multiplication facts. (Specific hand positions correspond to products.)
3. **Chaining**: Link each fact to the next in a story. Example: "One times two is a pair holding hands (2); two times two is four hands clapping (4)," etc.

**Teaching by example:**

Demonstrate calculations repeatedly using examples drawn from the student's experience (local prices, field sizes, harvest yields). Repetition with meaningful context accelerates learning.

## Estimation and Approximation

### Percentage Estimation

**Quick percentage calculations:**

- **10% of a number**: Divide by 10.
- **5% of a number**: Divide by 20 (or take 10% and halve it).
- **1% of a number**: Divide by 100.

Example: Find 15% of 240.
- 10% of 240 = 24.
- 5% of 240 = 12.
- 15% = 24 + 12 = 36.

### Rounding and Limits

When precision is unnecessary, rounding simplifies calculation and communication.

**Rules for rounding:**

- If the digit to be dropped is less than 5, round down.
- If the digit to be dropped is 5 or greater, round up.

Example: 347 rounded to the nearest 10 = 350 (because the ones digit is 7, ≥ 5).

**Uses:**

- Population estimates: A town has "about 5,000 people" (actual: 4,876).
- Crop yields: "This field produced roughly 1,200 bushels" (actual: 1,187).

Rounding reduces calculation burden while maintaining practical accuracy.

### Comparative Estimation

When absolute precision is unknown, comparison provides useful data.

Example: Comparing two fields for productivity.

- Field A: 50 acres, yielded 1,000 bushels last year.
- Field B: 60 acres, yielded 1,100 bushels last year.

Yield per acre:
- Field A: 1,000 ÷ 50 = 20 bushels/acre.
- Field B: 1,100 ÷ 60 ≈ 18 bushels/acre.

Conclusion: Field A is more productive despite being smaller.

## Advanced Geometry for Engineering

### Circles and Circumference

If a circular structure must be constructed (round hut, water tank, defensive fort):

1. **Establishing the center**: Drive a stake into the ground at the proposed center.
2. **Creating the circle**: Attach a rope of the desired radius to the center stake. Walk around with the rope taut, marking the circumference with stones or stakes.
3. **Measuring circumference**: Once the circle is marked, a rope can walk the perimeter and be measured to get the circumference. Alternatively, use the formula: Circumference ≈ 3.14 × diameter (or 3 × diameter for approximation).

### Arches and Vault Geometry

**Circular arch:**

An arch transfers weight outward and downward, allowing spans wider than a straight beam could support.

1. **Radius**: All stones in the arch are positioned so they would fall radially toward a common center point.
2. **Wedge shape**: Each stone (called a voussoir) is shaped like a wedge—thicker on the outer edge, tapered on the inner edge—so it fits snugly with neighbors.
3. **Rise and span**: The arch rises above its base (rise) and spans a horizontal distance (span). Rise is determined by the radius and span.

**Simple calculation for a semicircular arch:**

- If the span is 20 feet, the radius is 10 feet, and the rise is 10 feet (a perfect semicircle).
- If a flatter arch is desired, the radius is larger, and the rise is smaller. For example, a radius of 20 feet with a 20-foot span gives a rise of about 8 feet (a gentler curve).

Construct an arch template (a wooden frame shaped to the desired curve) to guide stone placement.

### Leveling and Grading

**Water level method (ancient technique):**

Water always seeks the same level. Use this to determine if surfaces are level or to establish a consistent slope.

1. **Construction**: Join two clear tubes with water-filled flexible tubing. Add a dye to the water for visibility.
2. **Operation**: Place one tube vertically at a reference point; the water rises to a certain height. Place the second tube at another point; the water rises to the same height regardless of distance or intervening terrain.
3. **Application**: Mark the height on both tubes; the marked points are at the same elevation (level). Use this to level building foundations or establish irrigation channels with a precise slope.

**Slope measurement:**

To establish a 1% slope (0.5 inches of drop per 50 feet):

1. Measure out a distance (e.g., 50 feet).
2. Determine the target elevation drop (0.5 inches for a 1% slope).
3. Place a level at the starting point; measure down 0.5 inches at the endpoint. Use the water level method to verify.

## Record Keeping and Verification

### Census and Inventory

**Population recording:**

A tally system for a community of 200 people.

1. Create a master list on preserved material (papyrus, cloth, carved wood). List family names or household identifiers.
2. Use hash marks to count: 5 marks per family member, grouped in fives.
3. Total population = sum of all marks.

**Inventory of stored goods:**

For grain stores:

- **Location**: Describe the storage structure (e.g., "North granary, room 3").
- **Quantity**: Record in standard units (e.g., 50 bushels).
- **Date**: When stored and expected consumption period.
- **Inspector signature or mark**: A trusted person verifies the count and marks the record.

**Audit procedures:**

Periodically (monthly or quarterly), physically recount to ensure records are accurate. Discrepancies indicate theft, spoilage, or miscounting. Investigate.

### Preserving Numerical Records

**Durable materials:**

- **Clay tablets**: Incise marks into clay with a stylus; fire to harden. Extremely durable; lasts thousands of years.
- **Carved stone**: Chisel or incise into stone. Lasts indefinitely but is labor-intensive.
- **Preserved wood**: Incise or paint onto wood treated with preservatives (ash, smoke, wax). Can last centuries.
- **Paper or cloth with ink**: Use waterproof ink (iron gall ink) on preserved paper or cloth. Store in a dry location.

**Redundancy:**

Keep multiple copies in different locations. If a fire destroys a storehouse, the records in another location survive.

## Practical Application: Planning a Construction Project

A community decides to build a granary. The measurement and calculation process:

1. **Site preparation**: Survey the area using the 3-4-5 rope method to establish right angles. Mark the corners with stakes. Measure the desired dimensions using marked ropes (e.g., 30 feet × 40 feet).

2. **Material estimation**: Volume = 30 × 40 × 8 (height) = 9,600 cubic feet. Stone volume needed (3-foot walls): approximately 30 × 2 (walls on two sides, 3-foot thickness) × 8 = 480 cubic feet of stone. Total weight (at 125 lb/cubic foot) ≈ 60,000 pounds.

3. **Labor planning**: Skilled masons place 1 cubic foot per day. Labor estimate: 480 person-days. With 10 masons, the project takes ~48 days (accounting for rest days, weather, and inefficiencies, plan 2–3 months).

4. **Workforce allocation**: 10 masons + 20 laborers to haul stone = 30 people. Food requirement for 3 months: 30 people × 30 days/month × 3 months × standard ration/day. Verify grain stores are sufficient.

5. **Record keeping**: Document all material quantities, labor hours, and dates in a written record. Upon completion, verify dimensions against the original plan.

## Conclusion

Mathematics and measurement are foundational to survival and prosperity. Without electronic calculators, civilizations relied on body-based units, marked ropes, geometric principles, and systematic recording. The techniques in this guide—tally systems, proportional reasoning, unit squares for area, and written records—enable accurate planning and resource management in resource-limited contexts.

Accuracy comes from standardization and practice. Establish community standards for units (pace, cubit, hand-basket), train members to replicate them, and maintain written records. Geometric relationships (right angles, similar triangles, arch curves) solve construction problems without precise instruments. The combination of arithmetic, geometry, and careful documentation enables ambitious projects—terraced fields, durable structures, and equitable resource distribution—that ensure community survival and prosperity.
