:::danger
Cooking fires can injure you quickly and quietly. Open fires belong outdoors or in a proper cookstove with a working flue and good ventilation. If smoke collects where people breathe, stop and fix the setup before continuing. Never leave a cooking fire unattended.
:::

## Overview

Daily fire management is the set of small habits that keep cooking safe, efficient, and repeatable. The goal is not a dramatic blaze. The goal is a fire that starts easily, cooks steadily, uses the least fuel that still gets the job done, and dies down without leaving dangerous embers behind.

This guide assumes a simple outdoor cooking fire, fire pit, brazier, or properly vented cookstove. If you are cooking indoors, cross-check `cookstoves-indoor-heating-safety.md` first.

This guide covers *the fire under the pot*: fuel choice, lighting, heat control, smoke, ash, and coal banking. It is not about recipes or what to cook.

## Choosing Fuel

The best cooking fuel is dry, predictable, and easy to split or arrange into the size you need. For daily cooking, fuel choice matters as much as fire skill.

### Fuel comparison

| Fuel | Strengths | Weaknesses | Best Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dry hardwood splits | Hot, steady, good coals, less smoke than wet wood | Takes time to gather and dry | Most daily cooking |
| Small dry sticks | Lights fast, easy to adjust | Burns quickly, needs frequent feeding | Starting fires, quick boils |
| Charcoal | Clean, steady heat, very little smoke | Needs a hot start, can waste heat if overused, dangerous in enclosed spaces | Simmering, frying, stable cooking |
| Softwood | Easy to light, often available | More resin, more smoke, shorter burn | Kindling, not main fuel if avoidable |
| Green or wet wood | Can be all that is available in an emergency | Smoky, weak, wastes fuel | Emergency only |
| Trash, painted wood, treated lumber | None for cooking | Toxic fumes, unpredictable smoke | Do not use |

### Good fuel habits

- Keep fuel dry and under cover.
- Split wood smaller than you think you need.
- Separate kindling, main fuel, and backup fuel before lighting.
- Use the driest material first, then move to larger pieces.
- Save charcoal for the part of cooking that needs steady heat.
- Do not use charcoal in a closed shelter, tent, vehicle, or poorly vented room.

### Fuel sizing

For beginner cooking fires, fuel size matters more than fuel quantity.

- tinder: dry grass, bark fibers, shavings, paper, or similar fine material
- kindling: pencil-thin to finger-thin sticks
- main fuel: thumb-thick to wrist-thick pieces
- long-burn fuel: thicker split wood or charcoal bed

If you only remember one thing, remember this: a cooking fire starts small and grows only as needed.

## Lighting The Cooking Fire

The easiest way to fail at cooking fire management is to try to cook before the fire is ready. A good cooking fire has a clear sequence: tinder catches, kindling builds, main fuel joins in, and the fire settles into a usable bed of heat.

### Simple lighting sequence

1. Build the fire in a clear, non-flammable spot.
2. Put tinder in the center with air around it.
3. Lean fine kindling over the tinder like a small teepee or loose cone.
4. Light the tinder in one or two places.
5. Add more kindling only after the first layer is burning well.
6. Wait for a bright flame and a few small coals before adding larger fuel.

Do not use gasoline, kerosene, solvents, lamp fuel, or other volatile liquids to force a cooking fire to start.

### What a good start looks like

- Flames are bright, not lazy.
- Smoke is light and brief, not thick and gray.
- Kindling catches without repeated relighting.
- The fire grows from the center outward.

### What a bad start looks like

- Smoke rolls out but the wood does not catch.
- The fire keeps dying when you add larger pieces.
- Flames stay low and weak, with lots of gray or steam-like smoke.
- The fuel hisses, sputters, or steams because it is wet.

### How to fix a weak start

- Add more dry kindling, not more large wood.
- Open the fuel pile so air can reach the flame.
- Move damp fuel aside and use dry pieces first.
- Do not bury the flame under a heavy log too early.

### Fire layout for cooking

For most daily cooking, arrange the fire so you can control where the heat goes.

- center fire: good for a pot directly over the hottest point
- side fire: good for moving pots closer or farther from heat
- two-zone fire: one hot zone for boiling, one cooler zone for holding or simmering

The more often you cook, the more useful it becomes to think in zones instead of treating the fire as one single block of heat.

## Controlling Heat For Boiling, Simmering, And Frying

Cooking is mostly heat control. If the food is too cold, it does not cook. If it is too hot, it burns, sticks, or wastes fuel.

### Heat cues

| Cooking goal | What you want to see | What to do |
|---|---|---|
| Boiling | Strong rolling bubbles, steam, water moving vigorously | Add more heat or move the pot closer to the hottest part |
| Simmering | Small bubbles and gentle movement | Reduce fuel, shift pot to the edge, or add a lid |
| Frying | Fat shimmers, food sizzles, but does not scorch immediately | Use a stable medium-high heat and preheat first |
| Holding warm | A few coals and low flame, not a full blaze | Pull the pot farther from the center or reduce fuel |

### Boiling

Boiling is for water-heavy tasks such as soups, pasta, blanching, and the first stage of beans or root vegetables.

To boil well:

- use enough heat to keep the pot active
- center the pot over the hottest coals or flame
- keep the lid on when possible
- stir thick foods so they do not stick

If the pot is barely moving, you are not really boiling yet.

### Simmering

Simmering saves fuel and improves texture for rice, beans, stews, sauces, and broths.

To simmer well:

- let the pot come up to heat first, then reduce the fire
- use a lid
- move the pot partly off the main heat
- keep only a few coals or a low flame under the pot

If simmering turns into a hard boil, you are wasting fuel and often making food worse.

### Frying

Frying needs stable heat, not a huge fire. Most beginner frying mistakes come from overheating the pan or adding food before the pan is ready.

To fry well:

- preheat the pan before adding fat
- wait until the fat shimmers, not smokes heavily
- keep food in a single layer if possible
- do not crowd the pan
- adjust heat early, not after the food burns

If the pan smokes heavily, the fire is too hot or the fat is breaking down.

### Pot position matters

You can often control heat without changing fuel at all.

- move the pot closer to the center for more heat
- move it to the edge for less heat
- raise it on stones or a grate for gentler heat
- add a lid to trap heat and reduce fuel use

The pot position is usually the fastest control you have.

## Reducing Smoke

Smoke is often a sign of poor fuel, poor airflow, or a fire that is too cool. A cooking fire should not smoke heavily for long.

### Common smoke causes

- wet or green wood
- fuel pieces that are too large for the fire stage
- a fire packed too tightly
- too little air under or through the fuel
- trying to cook before the fire is hot enough

### Smoke reduction steps

1. Use dry fuel first.
2. Split fuel smaller.
3. Keep air spaces in the fuel pile.
4. Let the fire get hot before putting the pot on.
5. Stir or rearrange fuel so flames can reach fresh surfaces.
6. Clear excess ash if it is choking airflow.

### What not to do

- Do not smother a cooking fire with too much fuel at once.
- Do not keep adding green wood because the fire is already smoky.
- Do not trap smoke under the pot and call it normal.
- Do not cook over an oxygen-starved fire if you can fix the draft instead.

### Practical smoke rule

If the smoke is thick and gray, the fire is usually too cool or too wet. If the fire is bright and active, smoke usually drops quickly after the fire is established.

## Ash, Ember, And Cleanup Safety

Ash and embers stay dangerous after the flames look gone. Many fires restart because someone assumed gray ash meant cold ash.

### Safe ash habits

- Treat all ash as hot until proven otherwise.
- Stir ash only with a metal tool or a stick that will not burn through quickly.
- Keep ash away from dry grass, paper, cloth, and compost until fully cold.
- Use a metal container, bare soil, or another non-flammable surface for cooling ash.

### How to check if ash is still hot

- Hold your hand near it first, not over it.
- If heat radiates strongly, it is not safe.
- If you see glowing red points, it is still active.
- If you hear crackling or see steam after adding a little water, it was not fully cold.

### Ember disposal

The safest method is simple:

1. Spread embers thinly.
2. Wet them thoroughly if water is available.
3. Stir.
4. Wet again.
5. Leave them in a non-flammable place until completely cold.

If you cannot wet them, isolate them on bare mineral soil or in a secure fire pit and wait.

### Ash cleanup

- Remove excess ash when it begins to block airflow.
- Do not throw hot ash into a trash pile, leaf pile, or wood storage area.
- Do not dump ash near tents, walls, or dry fuel.
- Keep a metal shovel or scoop for cleanup if you cook daily.

Ash can be useful later, but only after it is truly cold and only when the site and task make sense.

## Banking Coals For The Next Meal

Banking coals means reducing a fire to a small, controlled bed that can be brought back to cooking heat later. This saves time and fuel, but only if done carefully.

### When banking coals helps

- breakfast after a supper fire
- reheating water or soup soon after a meal
- long cooking days with several meal periods
- cold weather where relighting is difficult

### How to bank coals safely

1. Stop adding new fuel when you have enough coals for the next task.
2. Push coals together into one compact area.
3. Remove large ash clumps that block airflow.
4. Cover lightly with ash only if you want to slow the burn, not extinguish it.
5. Leave enough air for the coals to stay alive if you plan to relight soon.

### Relighting from banked coals

- Clear away some ash from the top.
- Add a few dry sticks or small pieces first.
- Let the coals brighten before adding larger fuel.
- Do not bury a weak coal bed under heavy logs.

### When not to bank coals

- in windy conditions that make stray embers likely
- near dry grass, leaves, or wood piles
- when you cannot supervise the fire later
- when the site is not secure enough for hot ash to remain overnight

If you are unsure, fully extinguish the fire instead of trying to save it.

## Common Mistakes

Most cooking fire problems come from a few repeat errors.

### 1. Using fuel that is too wet

Wet fuel wastes heat on drying itself. The result is smoke, weak flame, and slow cooking.

Fix: use dry fuel first and save damp wood for emergency backup only.

### 2. Building the fire too large

A large fire is harder to control, harder to clean up, and often more smoky than a smaller one.

Fix: start small and add only what the cooking task needs.

### 3. Cooking before the fire is ready

Food on a weak fire usually sticks, scorches, or cooks unevenly.

Fix: wait for a real bed of heat before putting the pot or pan on.

### 4. Packing the fuel too tightly

If air cannot reach the fire, combustion gets dirty and weak.

Fix: leave gaps for airflow.

### 5. Ignoring ash buildup

Too much ash blocks air from reaching the coals.

Fix: clear ash during long cooking sessions.

### 6. Leaving the fire to "sort itself out"

Cooking fires do not become safe on their own.

Fix: watch the fire through the cooking cycle and shut it down deliberately.

### 7. Thinking gray ash is cold ash

It is often still hot enough to burn or restart a fire.

Fix: test, cool, and isolate ash before disposal.

### 8. Letting smoke into living space

Smoke is not a normal cooking side effect in a closed room. It is a setup problem.

Fix: improve the fire setup or move the cooking outdoors.

### 9. Treating accelerants as a shortcut

Liquid fuels can flare suddenly, spread fire where you did not expect it, and cause bad burns.

Fix: build a better tinder and kindling stage instead of reaching for accelerants.

## Quick Reference

- Dry fuel cooks better than wet fuel.
- Small fuel pieces start easier than large ones.
- Boiling needs more heat than simmering.
- Frying needs a stable pan temperature, not a giant flame.
- Smoke usually means poor airflow, wet fuel, or a fire that is not hot enough.
- Ash can stay hot long after flames are gone.
- Bank coals only when the site is safe and you can supervise them.
- If smoke is in the breathing zone, stop and fix the fire.
