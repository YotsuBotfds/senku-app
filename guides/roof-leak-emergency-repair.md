:::danger
**Fall risk, electrical shock, and collapse hazard:** A wet roof is slick, ladders shift easily, and leaking water can energize lights, outlets, or metal fixtures. If you see sagging drywall, cracking sounds, or water near electrical fixtures, shut off power to that area and stay out of the space until it is safe. Do not work on a roof in lightning, high wind, ice, or any situation where you cannot maintain stable footing.
:::

If your roof is leaking right now, the goal is not perfection. The goal is to keep water out, protect the structure below, and buy time for a proper repair. A fast temporary fix is often the difference between a wet spot and a ruined ceiling, soaked insulation, and hidden rot. If the complaint is really a door, window, wall, or drainage problem, hand off to [Simple Home Repairs](simple-home-repairs.html) instead of forcing a roof diagnosis.

:::info
**First-check: roof leak failure modes**
Before tracing the stain, rule out these common causes in order:

1. **Active dripping, soaked materials, or a wet ceiling?** -> contain the water inside first, then trace the source
2. **Wet ceiling near a pipe, vent, skylight, or chimney?** -> check flashing and boot seals first, because penetrations fail before the shingle field
3. **Only leaks during wind-driven rain?** -> side lap, wall intersection, or failed flashing, not a simple hole in the shingles
4. **Only leaks after long steady rain?** -> saturated valley, underlayment failure, or broad shingle defect
5. **Bulging or sagging ceiling?** -> stop, shut off power below, drain trapped water before it collapses
6. **Water near lights, outlets, or metal fixtures?** -> electrical hazard first, patch second
7. **Leak source is below a bathroom, tank, or pump line instead of the roof?** -> hand off to the plumbing, storage, or hand-pump guide before patching the roof

If none of those match, the entry point is likely a nail pop, exposed fastener, or a small puncture uphill from the visible drip.
:::

<section id="immediate-actions">

## If Your Roof Is Leaking Right Now

Start with damage control inside, then trace the leak source, then apply the best temporary weatherproofing you can do safely.

:::warning
If water is near a breaker box, light, outlet, or metal fixture, shut off power to the affected area if you can do so safely and stay out of that space until it is safe.
:::

1. Move furniture, electronics, papers, and stored items away from the drip.
2. Catch the water in a bucket, tote, or plastic bin.
3. If the ceiling is bulging, put a second container under it and be ready to control the release.
4. Turn off power to any light, fan, or outlet that is getting wet.
5. Use towels, plastic sheeting, or a tarp to protect floors and walls.
6. Get eyes on the attic or roof only if conditions are safe enough to do so.

If you are checking what to look at first, start with the active drip, any wet ceiling or insulation, and any power or fixture hazard before you chase the stain itself.

The first question is not "where is the stain?" It is "where is the water entering?" Water often travels several feet from the actual opening before it shows up inside.

:::tip
If you can reach the attic safely, the best clue is usually the highest wet point, not the lowest drip. Follow the water uphill to the first sign of damp wood, insulation, or sheathing.
:::

</section>

<section id="find-entry-point">

## Finding Where Water Is Getting In

Roof leaks usually show up in one room and start somewhere else. The entry point is commonly uphill from the stain, and often at a flashing joint or penetration rather than in the middle of a shingle field.

### Most common entry points

| Likely source | Typical clue | Why it leaks |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Plumbing vent boot | Leak near a pipe, cracked rubber, rusted clamp | Rubber boots split with age and UV exposure |
| Chimney flashing | Water on nearby ceiling after wind-driven rain | Counterflashing or step flashing fails at joints |
| Skylight | Drips from corners or frame | Seal failure, clogged weep paths, bad flashing |
| Roof valley | Leak appears after heavy rain or snowmelt | High water flow concentrates there |
| Ridge or hip cap | Wet attic ridge boards | Missing cap seal, lifted shingles, wind-driven rain |
| Nail pops or exposed fasteners | Small but persistent leak | Fastener backs out and opens a path |
| Roof-to-wall transition | Stain below a wall line or dormer | Step flashing or siding integration fails |
| Damaged shingles or membrane | Visible tear, missing tab, puncture | Direct opening to underlayment or deck |
| Condensation | Damp attic with no obvious roof defect | Moist indoor air condenses on cold roof surfaces |

### How to trace the leak

1. Go into the attic with a bright flashlight during or shortly after rainfall.
2. Look for the first wet wood, dark staining, drips, and compressed insulation.
3. Trace from the wettest area toward the roof deck and then uphill.
4. Check penetrations first: vents, pipes, chimneys, skylights, dormers, and wall intersections.
5. Look for rusty nail tips, mold streaks, and water trails on rafters or trusses.
6. If the stain is far from the opening, remember that water can run along framing members before dropping.

If the roof is dry, use a garden hose test only with a helper inside watching for the first drip. Test one small roof zone at a time and start low, then work uphill. That prevents false conclusions from water running from a tested area into an untested one.

### What the symptom usually means

- Leak only during wind-driven rain: flashing, side laps, or a wall intersection is likely.
- Leak after long, steady rain: underlayment failure, a saturated valley, or a broad shingle defect is likely.
- Leak after snow or ice: ice damming, backed-up gutters, or refreezing at eaves is likely.
- Leak only in humid weather, with no rain: condensation or a ventilation problem is likely.

:::warning
Do not assume every ceiling stain is a roof leak. Plumbing, HVAC condensate, and bathroom overflow can mimic roof damage. Verify the source before cutting the ceiling or sealing the roof.
:::

<section id="emergency-patching">

## How to Patch a Roof in the Rain

In active rain, the most reliable emergency repair is usually a tarp. Most sealants and adhesives do not bond well to wet, dirty, or moving surfaces. If you cannot dry and clean the repair area, use a cover first and patch later.

### Emergency tarp method

Use a tarp when you need immediate, rainproof coverage over a damaged section, missing shingles, torn membrane, or failed flashing area.

1. Choose a tarp large enough to cover the damaged area and extend well past it on all sides.
2. Run the tarp from above the leak down past the lower edge so water can shed off the tarp, not behind it.
3. If possible, place the upper edge near or over the ridge so water cannot run under the cover.
4. Smooth out folds that could trap water or catch wind.
5. Secure the tarp with boards or battens along the edges, not just loose rocks or a few staples.
6. Anchor the tarp so wind cannot flap it into a sail and tear it free.

A good rule is to overlap the damaged area generously. Small tarps and tiny overlaps fail fast in wind-driven rain.

### Fast tarp anchoring options

- **Board-and-screw method:** Sandwich the tarp edge between two strips of wood and fasten to a solid edge or framing member.
- **Ridge-over method:** Drape the tarp over the ridge so both sides shed water away from the damaged zone.
- **Edge clamp method:** Clamp the tarp to eaves, fascia, or other structural edges where it will not be cut by sharp metal.

### Small patches when a tarp is not practical

For a small puncture, split seam, or isolated nail-hole leak, a self-adhering roofing patch or flashing tape can work if the surface can be wiped as dry and clean as possible. Press firmly from the center outward to avoid trapped channels.

Use wet-surface patch products only as a temporary measure, and only when the product is rated for damp conditions. They are not a substitute for proper flashing or underlayment repair.

### What not to rely on in the rain

- Standard caulk on a soaking wet surface
- Loose plastic held by a few bricks
- Duct tape as a lasting roof repair
- Patching over rotten decking or crumbling shingles
- Walking on a steep or slick roof just to reach a minor leak

:::tip
If the roof surface is too slick to stand on confidently, stop there and use a ground-based or ladder-based tarp strategy instead. A fast, imperfect tarp is better than a fall.
:::

<section id="interior-damage">

## Interior Damage Control

Interior protection keeps a roof leak from becoming a whole-house cleanup.

### Immediate interior steps

- Put a container under every active drip.
- Move items out of the wet zone.
- Use plastic sheeting to protect flooring, furniture, and stored materials.
- Add towels around the bucket to catch splash.
- If the ceiling is bulging, do not ignore it; water is pooling above the finish layer.
- If water is near wiring or fixtures, shut off power to that circuit or the main if needed.

If drywall is sagging from trapped water, controlled drainage can reduce the chance of a larger collapse. Only do this if you are certain there is no hidden wiring or fixture in the area. Make a small opening at the lowest point so the water releases into a bucket instead of bursting unpredictably.

### Dry-out and cleanup

1. Remove wet rugs, cardboard, and paper as soon as practical.
2. Open windows only if outside air is drier than inside air.
3. Run fans and a dehumidifier to drive moisture out of the room.
4. Pull soaked insulation if it can be removed safely, because wet insulation loses performance and can hide mold.
5. Check framing for soft spots, swelling, staining, and persistent dampness over the next day or two.

### Mold and hidden damage

Water trapped inside a ceiling or wall cavity can produce mold, odor, and rot even if the surface looks dry. A dry stain is not proof that the assembly is sound. Inspect the cavity, not just the paint.

:::warning
Do not close up a leak with fresh drywall, trim, or paint until the source is fixed and the cavity has dried. Trapping moisture creates a larger repair later.
:::

<section id="temporary-vs-durable">

## Temporary Fixes Versus Durable Repairs

Temporary fixes are for surviving the storm. Durable repairs are for restoring the roof so it sheds water for years.

| Repair type | Best use | Limit |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Tarp cover | Active rain, storm damage, missing sections | Temporary, can blow loose if poorly anchored |
| Roofing tape or patch strip | Small holes, seam splits, emergency sealing | Works best on clean, dry surfaces |
| Wet-surface mastic | Short-term sealing when surfaces cannot fully dry | Not a substitute for flashing or membrane repair |
| Replace a few shingles | Localized shingle damage in workable weather | Must match the surrounding system and sealing |
| Flashing repair | Leaks at chimneys, walls, vents, skylights | Often requires partial removal of surrounding roofing |
| Deck or underlayment replacement | Rot, widespread leaks, structural damage | Needs more time and materials, but actually solves the cause |

### What makes a repair durable

A repair is durable when it addresses the water path, not just the symptom. That usually means:

- Replacing damaged shingles, membrane, or panels
- Rebuilding or re-bending flashing
- Sealing penetrations correctly
- Replacing rotten decking or underlayment
- Confirming the roof drains properly with no ponding or backflow

If a repair still depends on a thick bead of sealant to stay dry, it is probably a temporary fix, not a finished repair.

### When a full repair is the smarter choice

Choose a larger repair when you see:

- Multiple leak points in the same roof area
- Soft or spongy decking
- Repeated leaks after previous patching
- Widespread shingle cracking or granule loss
- Failed flashing around several penetrations
- Sagging structure or long-term rot

<section id="safety">

## Ladder and Wet Roof Safety

The fastest way to turn a leak into an emergency is to fall while trying to fix it.

### Ladder basics

- Set the ladder on firm, level ground.
- Keep three points of contact when climbing.
- Extend the ladder above the roof edge so you have a stable handhold when stepping on and off.
- Do not overreach sideways.
- Keep the ladder out of gutters that can crush or slip.
- Have another person steady the ladder if possible.

### Wet-roof basics

- Do not walk on a wet, steep, icy, mossy, or debris-covered roof unless you absolutely must.
- Watch for standing water on low-slope roofs.
- Assume every roof edge is slippery when wet.
- Stay clear of overhead power lines and damaged service drops.
- Stop if wind gusts, lightning, or darkness reduce your margin for error.

### Best practice

If the leak can be controlled from the ground, attic, or eaves, do that first. Climbing onto the roof should be the last option, not the first reflex.

<section id="after-storm">

## After the Storm

Once the weather clears, replace temporary measures with proper materials and inspect the whole area around the leak, not just the visible hole.

1. Remove the tarp or temporary patch carefully.
2. Inspect the roof deck, underlayment, flashing, and fasteners.
3. Replace any damaged roofing material.
4. Rework the flashing so water is directed over, not behind, the layers.
5. Dry the attic and interior completely before closing everything up.
6. Watch the area during the next rain to confirm the leak is actually fixed.

The most common mistake is patching only the visible drip point and ignoring the larger failure around it. Roof leaks are often systems failures, not single-hole failures.

:::tip
If the same area leaks again after a patch, expand the inspection zone. Look one bay farther uphill and one component farther outward than before.
:::

</section>

When someone says, "my roof is leaking right now," the right response is: protect the interior, find the true entry point, tarp or patch only as safely as possible, and plan a real repair as soon as the weather allows. The safer the temporary fix, the more time it buys for the permanent one.
