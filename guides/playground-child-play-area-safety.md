:::danger
A safe play area is not "a place where children are left alone." It is a place where a caregiver can see the children, the site does not sit beside obvious hazards, and the ground and equipment match the children's size and age. In low-resource settings, the safest play space is often a simple, well-bounded patch of ground with a clear line of sight.
:::

## Scope

This guide is for simple, practical play spaces for young children in homes, courtyards, compounds, schools, clinics, shelters, and shared community yards. It focuses on low-cost choices that reduce injury without requiring commercial playground equipment.

Use it when you need answers to questions like:

- Where should the play area go?
- What ground surface is safest?
- How do I keep mixed-age play from turning into injury?
- What should adults check every day?

If a child has already been exposed to fire, poison, water, traffic, or a serious fall, treat it as an emergency and use the related hazard guides.

## The Core Rule

A play area is safe only when all four of these are true:

1. Children are away from fire, tools, water, animals, and traffic.
2. An adult can see or hear the whole space quickly.
3. The ground is soft enough for ordinary falls.
4. The layout is simple enough to inspect in a few seconds.

If one of these fails, change the site or close the area.

## Best Siting Rules

Put the play area where adults already spend time, not in a hidden corner.

### Keep It Away From These Hazards

- Roads, driveways, cart paths, bicycle lanes, and vehicle loading spots
- Wells, tanks, ponds, ditches, irrigation channels, and flood-prone ground
- Cookfires, stoves, fuel storage, ash pits, and heating areas
- Tools, saws, nails, wire, scrap metal, repair benches, and work sheds
- Animal pens, feeding areas, guard dogs, livestock movement paths, and manure piles
- Stairs, ledges, roofs, retaining walls, steep banks, and loose stone piles

### Choose Ground That Can Be Watched

- The area should be visible from the place where adults sit, cook, sew, or work
- The adult should not need to walk around a wall, hedge, or stack of supplies to see the children
- The area should drain after rain and not turn into mud or a standing-water trap
- The area should be big enough for play but small enough that a caregiver can count children at a glance

:::tip
If the best supervision spot is also a hazardous work area, the site is wrong. A play area should fit the way adults actually live, not force adults to choose between watching children and doing unsafe work nearby.
:::

## Layout and Sight Lines

The best layout is open, boring, and easy to understand.

- Make one obvious entry and exit
- Keep the area free of blind corners
- Place the adult chair, stool, or standing spot so the whole area stays in view
- Keep toys, buckets, ropes, and loose materials in one storage spot outside the play zone
- Use a simple boundary: fence, posts, stones, rope, low wall, or shrubs
- If a toddler can step over the boundary, treat it as a marker, not a barrier

Children should not have to cross through the kitchen, tool zone, animal path, or road to reach the play area.

## Surfaces and Falls

Most childhood injuries come from falls, slips, and hard impact. The ground matters.

### Better Surfaces

| Surface | Best Use | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Grass | Free play, walking, sitting | Good if level and not hiding holes, glass, or animal waste |
| Sand | Climbing, digging, light jumping | Rake often; keep it dry enough to drain |
| Wood chips or mulch | Under low climbing or swinging | Keep clean and replace when packed down |
| Clean leaf litter | Temporary low-risk play | Remove sticks, thorns, trash, and manure |

### Surfaces To Avoid Under Fall Zones

- Concrete
- Brick
- Stone
- Packed clay
- Broken pavement
- Loose gravel

Those surfaces are fine for paths, not for the landing area under climbing, swinging, or jumping.

### Surface Rules

- Keep the ground level and free of hidden holes
- Remove roots, sharp rocks, broken glass, wire, and splinters
- Do not let puddles stay in the play area after rain
- If the surface becomes slick or eroded, close the area until it is repaired

### Fall Height Rule

Low play is safer than high play.

- Keep climbing low enough that a fall is a bruise, not a major impact
- If a child can climb higher than the adult can comfortably reach from the ground, the structure is too ambitious for a basic low-resource play area
- Any raised edge, step, or platform should be simple, stable, and easy to step down from

## Equipment and Home-Build Rules

Simple is safer.

- Use sturdy, low, stable equipment
- Avoid sharp edges, pinch points, protruding nails, loose screws, and splinters
- Do not use cracked pallets, rotten wood, rusted metal, broken plastic, or bent wire
- Do not use ropes or cords long enough to wrap around the neck
- Do not hang seats or toys where they can strike a wall, tree, post, or another child
- Check that any bench, step, or frame does not tip when a child leans on it
- If an adult cannot shake or press the structure without seeing movement, repair it before use

### Good Low-Cost Play Items

- Balls
- Buckets for digging or carrying water under supervision
- Sturdy boxes or crates that do not collapse
- Blankets or mats on clean ground
- Low climbing logs or tires only if they are dry, stable, and free of standing water

### Bad Low-Cost Play Items

- Tires that collect water
- Broken furniture
- Loose rope swings tied to weak branches
- Metal scraps
- Anything with rust, splinters, or sharp edges

## Mixed-Age Play

Mixed-age play needs boundaries. A toddler and an older child do not play at the same risk level.

- Keep toddlers separate from rough play, racing, wrestling, and ball games
- Give older children a different space or a different time if they want active play
- Do not let older children use the younger-child area as a launch pad, hiding spot, or running lane
- Do not let a child carry a baby sibling while also climbing or running
- Do not make older children the only supervision plan

If mixed ages must share a space, split the area by activity, not by wishful thinking.

### Practical Split

- One zone for walkers and preschoolers: low movement, low climbing, soft ground
- One zone for older children: running, rough games, and more active movement
- One adult watch point that sees both zones

## Supervision That Works

Good supervision is a system, not a feeling.

- Keep the play area within direct sight or easy hearing distance
- Give the supervising adult one clear job
- Use a named handoff like "I am on play watch" so responsibility is obvious
- Count children when they enter, leave, and after any interruption
- If an adult cannot see every child from one place, the layout is too complex
- If the caregiver has to split attention between fire, cooking, animals, and play, move the play area or assign another watcher

For the youngest children, supervision should be close enough to stop a dangerous move before it becomes an injury.

## Daily Inspection Routine

Check the area before children enter it.

1. Walk the ground and remove glass, wire, sharp rocks, feces, dead animals, and trash
2. Look for puddles, holes, loose boards, or unstable stones
3. Shake climbing pieces, posts, and benches
4. Check that gates, fences, and boundary markers still close or hold
5. Look for animal tracks, nests, wasps, or new hazards from weather
6. Put away ropes, buckets, tools, and loose materials when play ends

After heavy rain, strong wind, or a repair job nearby, inspect again before use.

## Common Mistakes

- Putting the play area beside the kitchen because adults are "already there"
- Using a driveway, path, or gate area as a play zone
- Building the area where adults cannot actually see the children
- Letting water collect in tires, buckets, tubs, or broken containers
- Building high platforms or swings on soft or uneven ground
- Leaving ropes, wires, cords, bags, or scrap metal in reach
- Allowing older children to dominate the space while younger children play at the edges
- Assuming one fence solves every risk
- Keeping broken equipment in service because it still "mostly works"

## When To Close The Area

Close the play area right away if:

- A child was injured there and the cause is not fixed
- Water is pooling or draining through the play surface
- Animals are entering the space
- New construction, tools, or traffic are now nearby
- The adult supervisor cannot see or hear the whole area
- The surface has become hard, slippery, or full of holes

If you cannot supervise the area, it is not a play area.
