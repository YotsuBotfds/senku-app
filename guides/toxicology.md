## Overview

Toxicology encompasses poison identification, mechanism of action, and emergency treatment of toxic exposures. For advanced poisoning protocols including heavy metal chelation, mushroom poisoning management, and improvised antidote preparation, see <a href="../toxicology-poisoning-response.html">Toxicology and Poisoning Response</a>. In survival and off-grid medicine, poisoning represents both accidental exposure (contaminated food/water, unidentified plants, industrial residue) and deliberate harm (warfare agents, insecticides). Prevalence increases in collapse scenarios: compromised supply chains, loss of food safety testing, exposure to agricultural chemicals during resource scavenging. Urgency is **critical**—many toxins cause irreversible harm or death within minutes to hours. Early recognition and immediate treatment are essential for survival.

### When to Treat as Emergency

- Any exposure to unknown substance
- Symptoms appearing after ingestion of unknown plant/animal
- Respiratory distress, altered consciousness, cardiac arrhythmias
- Severe gastrointestinal bleeding or fluid loss
- Exposure timeline within treatment window for specific antidotes

## Recognition & Assessment

### Systematic Poison Exposure Evaluation

The SAMPLE mnemonic guides rapid assessment:

-   **Signs/Symptoms:** Visible physical signs (pinpoint pupils, excessive salivation, burns around mouth), reported symptoms (burning sensation, nausea, weakness)
-   **Allergens/Exposures:** Prior reactions to substances, known sensitivities
-   **Medications/Supplements:** Current medications that may interact with toxin, toxins present in medications
-   **Pertinent History:** Medical conditions (liver disease increases toxin retention, kidney disease prevents elimination), pregnancy status
-   **Last intake:** Time of last meal (affects GI absorption), time of exposure (critical for treatment window)
-   **Events/Environment:** Witnessed exposures, substance location, access to labels or identification

### Detailed Exposure History

-   **Type of substance:** Identify if possible (label, plant identification, substance witnesses saw, smell/appearance)
-   **Route of exposure:** Ingestion (swallowed), inhalation (breathed), dermal (skin contact), injection (bite/sting, needle)
-   **Amount:** Quantity ingested/exposed; estimate using visible container size, estimate of ingested volume from vomitus
-   **Time of exposure:** Critical for symptom progression and treatment window (many antidotes effective only within 30 min–2 hours)
-   **Interval since exposure:** Helps predict symptom onset timing
-   **Pre-exposure factors:** Medications, allergies, medical conditions, pregnancy status (affects treatment options)

### Vital Signs Baseline

Record initial assessment:
- Temperature (toxins may cause hyperthermia or hypothermia)
- Heart rate (expect tachycardia with stimulants, bradycardia with opioids/organophosphates)
- Respirations (note rate and effort; respiratory distress indicates urgent airway risk)
- Blood pressure (hypotension suggests shock or CNS depressants; hypertension with stimulants/sympathomimetics)
- Oxygen saturation (if pulse oximetry available; respiratory toxins cause hypoxemia)

### Toxidrome Recognition (Symptom-Based Approach)

When poison identity unknown: categorize by symptom pattern to guide treatment.

:::info-box
**Cholinergic Toxidrome** (excess acetylcholine): Pinpoint pupils, profuse salivation/drooling, muscle fasciculations (visible twitching), paralysis, respiratory distress, bronchospasm. Caused by organophosphates, carbamates, nerve agents.

**Anticholinergic Toxidrome**: Dilated pupils, dry mouth, agitation, confusion, hallucinations, rapid heart rate, hyperthermia, delirium. Caused by belladonna, atropine, tricyclic antidepressants.

**Opioid Toxidrome**: Miosis (pinpoint pupils), respiratory depression, unconsciousness, bradycardia, low blood pressure. Caused by heroin, morphine, codeine, fentanyl. For opioid dosing and naloxone reversal protocols, see <a href="../medications.html#analgesics">Essential Medications — Analgesics</a>.

**CNS Depressant Toxidrome**: Altered consciousness progressing to coma, slow breathing (<12/min), low blood pressure, bradycardia, absent reflexes. Caused by sedatives, barbiturates, benzodiazepines, alcohol.

**CNS Stimulant Toxidrome**: Agitation, rapid heart rate (>100/min), elevated blood pressure, hyperthermia, dilated pupils, seizures, hallucinations. Caused by cocaine, amphetamines, caffeine overdose, stimulant medications.
:::

<section id="equipment">

## Equipment & Materials

### Essential Toxicology Kit (Survival Setting)

**Core supplies to stock:**
- Activated charcoal powder, 100g bulk (shelf life 5+ years if kept dry)
- Gloves (nitrile or latex, unpowdered to avoid contamination), 100+ pairs
- Plastic bags for contaminated materials and clothing
- Soap and clean water containers (or bleach solution in squeeze bottles)
- Emergency antidotes per region (atropine auto-injectors if organophosphate exposure risk, naloxone if opioid dependency in community)
- Epinephrine auto-injectors (0.3mg or 0.5mg) for anaphylaxis
- Antihistamine tablets (diphenhydramine 25mg or cetirizine 10mg), bottle of 100
- Hydrocortisone cream 1% for localized skin reactions
- pH paper or vinegar/baking soda for unknown substance identification
- Laminated plant identification cards specific to your region

### Improvised Alternatives Without Commercial Supplies

- **Activated charcoal:** Burn hardwood (oak, birch, ash preferred), crush cooled charcoal to fine powder, mix 1:8 with water to slurry. Less effective than commercial but acceptable for emergency use.
- **Adsorbent binders:** Powdered clay (if available), fine soil, crushed chalk (calcium carbonate) can partially absorb some toxins (less effective than activated charcoal but available in survival).
- **Antidote improvisation:** For organophosphate exposure without atropine, anticholinergic plants (belladonna, datura) have historically been used but are dangerous (risk overdose). Better: supportive care only.
- **Decontamination:** Plain soap and water, or wood ash mixed with water as abrasive cleaner. Dilute household bleach (1:10 ratio) if available for disinfection.
- **Epinephrine substitute:** Without auto-injectors, no substitute exists. Prepare for possible anaphylaxis death without it.

### Personal Protective Equipment for Treatment

- Heavy-duty gloves or double-gloving if handling contaminated materials
- Face mask or cloth covering (inhalation exposure prevention)
- Eye protection (face shield if available)
- Full-coverage clothing (long sleeves, pants)
- Designated decontamination area away from living/food space

<section id="history">

## Historical Toxicology Context

### Ancient Poison Use & Detection

Toxicology dates millennia: Egyptian papyri document poisonings; Cleopatra allegedly tested poisons on condemned prisoners; medieval tasters employed by nobility (one tasted all food before master). History demonstrates: (1) poisons deliberate/accidental highly prevalent, (2) detection difficult without modern chemistry, (3) antidotes/treatments developed empirically through trial/observation.

### Industrial Revolution Era (1800s-1900s)

Forensic toxicology emerged: arsenic/strychnine/morphine detection via chemical tests. Antidotes developed: activated charcoal (1830s), specific antidotes for common poisons. Recognition that dose determines toxicity (Paracelsus principle: "all substances are poisons, the dose makes the poison").

### Modern Toxicology (1950s-Present)

Mass spectrometry, gas chromatography enable precise identification. Antidotes developed for most common poisons. But survival medicine lacks these technologies—emphasis returns to: (1) observation and symptom-based diagnosis, (2) supportive care, (3) pre-stocked antidotes, (4) prevention through knowledge.

## Common Chemical Toxins

### Organophosphates and Carbamates (Insecticides)

**Mechanism:** Inhibit acetylcholinesterase, causing acetylcholine accumulation. **Symptoms:** SLUDGE (Salivation, Lacrimation, Urination, Defecation, GI upset, Emesis) + pinpoint pupils, muscle fasciculations, respiratory distress, seizures. **Timeline:** Acute onset (minutes to hours). **Treatment:** Atropine (blocks acetylcholine effects) IV 2-5mg initial, repeat every 5-10 minutes until signs of atropinization (dry mouth, increased heart rate, dilated pupils). Pralidoxime (PAM) regenerates cholinesterase if available. Decontamination: remove clothing, thorough skin washing.

### Corrosive Substances (Acids/Bases)

**Acids (sulfuric, hydrochloric, nitric):** Cause tissue coagulation, may be less destructive than bases due to more superficial burn. **Bases (ammonia, sodium hydroxide):** Liquefactive necrosis, deeper penetration, more severe injury. **Symptoms:** Burning mouth/throat, difficulty swallowing, drooling, vomiting blood. **Treatment:** DO NOT induce vomiting (re-exposure to esophagus). Dilute: small sips water or milk if conscious. Do NOT neutralize (chemical reaction generates heat). Seek professional care urgently (risk esophageal stricture, perforation).

### Carbon Monoxide

**Mechanism:** Binds hemoglobin with 200-250x higher affinity than oxygen, prevents oxygen delivery. **Symptoms:** Mild (headache, dizziness), moderate (confusion, ataxia, syncope), severe (coma, seizures, death). **Diagnosis:** Carboxyhemoglobin level if available (>20% diagnostic). **Treatment:** Move to fresh air immediately. High-flow oxygen (100% O2) accelerates CO elimination (reduces half-life from 4+ hours to 1-2 hours). Hyperbaric oxygen (if available) further reduces half-life to 20-30 minutes; indicated if severe symptoms, coma, pregnancy, or COHb >25-40%.

### Cyanide

**Mechanism:** Binds cytochrome oxidase, blocks cellular respiration even with adequate oxygen. **Symptoms:** Rapid onset: headache, rapid breathing, confusion, loss of consciousness, cardiac arrhythmias, coma. **Sources:** Smoke inhalation (fires), pesticides, industrial accidents. **Treatment:** Remove from source. Oxygen therapy. If available: cyanide antidote kit (hydroxocobalamin, or older sodium nitrite/thiosulfate) IV. Hydroxocobalamin: 5g IV directly binds cyanide, converting to cyanocobalamin (excreted). No more effective than supportive care without antidote, but prevents progression.

### Mushroom Toxins

**Amanita phalloides (death cap):** Alpha-amanitin inhibits RNA polymerase, causes liver/kidney failure. Latency 6-24 hours (deceptive), then GI symptoms, liver failure. Mortality >50% without treatment. **Management:** Aggressive decontamination (activated charcoal, hemodialysis), milk thistle (silymarin) if available, supportive care through liver recovery. **Ibotenic acid (Amanita muscaria, fly agaric):** Produces intoxication (euphoria, hallucinations, confusion), usually self-limited. Management: supportive, safety monitoring. **Psilocybin mushrooms:** Hallucinogenic, mild toxicity; management supportive (reassurance, safe environment).

## Animal Toxins

### Snake Venom

**Hemotoxic venom (rattlesnake, viper):** Tissue destruction, swelling, coagulopathy, shock. **Neurotoxic (cobra, coral snake):** Respiratory paralysis, minimal local swelling. **Management:** Immobilize bitten limb below heart, remove constrictive items (rings, bracelets). Do NOT cut/suck (ineffective, causes more damage). Elevate if possible to reduce swelling. Antivenom if available (species-specific; CroFab or Anavip in US for North American snakes). Supportive care: fluids, pain management, monitor for coagulopathy, respiratory status. Complications: compartment syndrome (may require fasciotomy), chronic swelling.

### Insect Venom (Bee, Wasp)

**Local reaction:** Localized swelling/pain. **Systemic reaction (anaphylaxis):** Urticaria, angioedema, respiratory distress, hypotension, shock. **Management:** Local: remove stinger (scrape off, don't squeeze), ice, NSAIDs, antihistamines. Anaphylaxis: epinephrine IM 0.3-0.5mg (upper thigh), then antihistamines, corticosteroids. Monitor for biphasic reaction (return of symptoms 12+ hours later); may need repeat epinephrine. Prevent re-exposure: avoid nest locations, protective clothing, consider desensitization if history anaphylaxis.

### Marine Toxins

**Stingray/sea urchin sting:** Local pain, inflammation, possible venom. Management: immerse in hot water (45°C/113°F for 45 minutes) to inactivate venom, remove spines, clean wound. **Scombroid poisoning (histamine from fish):** Flushing, itching, headache, diarrhea. Usually self-limited. Management: antihistamines, supportive care. **Ciguatera (from reef fish toxin):** GI upset, neurological symptoms (paresthesias, muscle weakness, cold reversal—sensation of hot/cold opposite). Chronic symptoms possible. Management: supportive, IV calcium for neurological symptoms, avoid affected fish species.

## Step-by-Step Poisoning Management Protocol

### Phase 1: Immediate Stabilization (0–5 minutes)

1. **Scene safety:** Ensure no ongoing exposure risk. Don protective equipment before approaching contaminated area. If chemical spill/hazmat, avoid direct contact; attempt to move patient away from source if safe.
2. **ABCs:** Assess airway patency, breathing effort, circulation (pulse/perfusion).
   - If unconscious: position in recovery position (left lateral decubitus) to allow fluid drainage if vomiting occurs.
   - If respiratory distress: prepare for manual ventilation; check for airway obstruction (swelling, food).
   - If no pulse: initiate CPR if trained.
3. **Decontamination (initial):** Remove from source immediately. If chemical exposure: remove contaminated clothing carefully (avoid self-contamination). Don gloves and containment area.
4. **Oxygen:** Provide high-flow oxygen if available (100% O2 via face mask, or bag-valve-mask ventilation if respiratory compromise).

### Phase 2: Assessment & History (5–15 minutes)

5. **Identify poison:** Examine label, note substance name, container, amount. Ask witnesses about appearance, smell, ingestion time. Obtain any remaining substance sample if safe (label and store separately).
6. **Exposure history:** Document time of exposure, route (ingestion/inhalation/dermal/injection), estimated amount.
7. **Baseline vital signs:** HR, RR, BP, temperature, oxygen saturation. Establish pupil size (pinpoint/dilated/normal), skin color/temperature/moisture.
8. **Targeted symptoms:** Note specific complaints and timeline. Ask: burning sensation, drooling, difficulty breathing, altered consciousness, muscle twitching, seizures, chest pain.
9. **Medical history:** Medications, allergies, medical conditions, pregnancy status. These modify treatment options and toxin susceptibility.

### Phase 3: Decontamination (Ongoing)

10. **Skin/eye decontamination:** Wash thoroughly with soap and water, 5–10 minutes minimum. For eyes: irrigate with clean water or saline 15–20 minutes. Continue if irritation persists.
11. **Clothing disposal:** Place contaminated items in sealed bag; do not return to regular laundry.
12. **Rescuer decontamination:** Change gloves, wash hands after patient care to prevent secondary contamination.

### Phase 4: Poison-Specific Interventions

**Decision tree based on toxin type (if known):**

:::warning
**If substance is corrosive acid/base:** DO NOT induce vomiting. Dilute only with small sips water/milk (100mL total). Seek urgent professional care.

**If opioid poisoning (suspected):** Administer naloxone IM 0.4–2mg if available; repeat every 2–3 minutes until responsiveness improves.

**If organophosphate/carbamate exposure:** Administer atropine IM 2–5mg initial, then repeat every 5–10 minutes until signs of atropinization (dry mouth, dilated pupils, increased heart rate). If pralidoxime available, also administer 15–25mg/kg IV over 5–30 minutes.

**If CNS depressant overdose:** Airway protection essential; support breathing, maintain circulation. Specific antidotes (flumazenil for benzodiazepines) only in hospital setting.
:::

13. **Activated charcoal (if appropriate):** For ingested toxins (not corrosives, not metals). Dose: 50–100g mixed with water to slurry consistency. Give orally if fully conscious and can swallow. Administer within 1–2 hours of ingestion for maximal effectiveness. Do NOT use if altered consciousness (aspiration risk).
14. **Antidote administration (if available and indicated):** Follow specific antidote protocols (see antidote table).
15. **Supportive care:** Maintain airway/breathing, position for drainage, IV fluids if trained and available, symptom management (seizures → benzodiazepines if available; hyperthermia → cool baths/ice packs).

### Phase 5: Ongoing Monitoring (Continuous)

16. **Monitor closely:** Recheck vital signs every 15–30 minutes. Observe for symptom progression, deterioration, or unexpected reactions.
17. **Document:** Keep timeline of interventions, symptom changes, vital signs. Critical for tracking efficacy and identifying complications.
18. **Reassess antidote timing:** Some antidotes require repeated dosing. Reassess after treatment interval; be prepared to redose if symptoms recur.

## Poisoning Management Details

### Activated Charcoal (Adsorption)

Binds toxins in GI tract, preventing absorption. **Dose:** 50–100g (or 1g/kg body weight). Mix with water to slurry (thick but pourable consistency). Give orally if conscious and can protect airway. **Timing:** Most effective within 1–2 hours of ingestion; less effective after 4+ hours. **Ineffective for:** Alcohols (methanol, ethylene glycol), metals (iron, lithium), corrosives. **Do NOT use if:** Altered consciousness (aspiration risk), unable to swallow. **After administration:** Follow with cathartic (sorbitol 1–2g/kg orally, or magnesium citrate if available) to promote bowel evacuation and prevent toxin reabsorption from stool.

:::tip
Activated charcoal has an unpleasant taste and gritty texture. Mix with water to a smoothie consistency, and encourage the patient to drink through a straw to reduce contact with mouth. Multiple smaller doses may be tolerated better than one large dose if the patient is nauseated.
:::

### Induced Vomiting (NOT Recommended)

Previously standard practice but now contraindicated in most cases:
- Risk of aspiration of vomitus into lungs
- Re-exposure of esophagus to corrosive substances (worsens injury)
- Adverse effects from emetic agents themselves (ipecac causes cardiotoxicity)
- **Exception:** Only consider for non-corrosive ingestions in fully alert patients within 30 minutes of ingestion and if no contraindications. Generally avoid.

Gastric lavage (stomach pumping) similarly discouraged; risks aspiration, esophageal damage, and patient discomfort. Not recommended outside clinical setting.

### Supportive Care as Primary Treatment

Most poisonings require primarily supportive care, as specific antidotes often unavailable:
- **Airway/breathing:** Maintain patent airway, provide ventilatory support if needed. If respiratory depression severe, manual ventilation may be necessary; prepare for prolonged ventilation needs.
- **Circulation:** Establish IV access if trained. Administer fluids to maintain perfusion; consider vasopressors if available and patient in shock.
- **Symptom management:** Seizures → benzodiazepines (diazepam 5–10mg IM or rectal if IV unavailable); hyperthermia → cool baths, ice packs to groin/armpits (major blood vessels); agitation → reassurance, quiet environment, restraint only if danger to self.
- **Monitoring:** Check vital signs frequently (every 15–30 min in acute phase). Monitor for deterioration, complications, or unexpected reactions.

<section id="complications">

## Complications & Life-Threatening Signs

:::danger
**Respiratory Failure / Airway Compromise**

Signs: Stridor, severe labored breathing, absent breath sounds, unconsciousness with inability to protect airway.

Action: Position for airway patency, remove any obstructions. Provide manual ventilation if possible. This is immediately life-threatening; proceed with extreme caution. Death may occur within 2–5 minutes without airway management.
:::

:::danger
**Cardiac Arrhythmias / Cardiac Arrest**

Signs: Palpitations, severe tachycardia or bradycardia, syncope, loss of pulse, no perfusion.

Action: Initiate CPR immediately if trained. Certain toxins (organophosphates, digitalis, cardiac glycosides) can trigger arrhythmias hours after exposure; continuous cardiac monitoring essential if available. Attempt defibrillation if AED available and trained.
:::

:::danger
**Status Epilepticus (Uncontrolled Seizures)**

Signs: Repeated seizures without return to consciousness between episodes, lasting >5 minutes total.

Action: Protect airway, position on side. Administer benzodiazepines (diazepam 10mg IM/IV or 20mg rectal if IV unavailable) immediately. Repeat dose every 5 minutes if seizures continue. Monitor respiratory status closely; be prepared for airway compromise during/after seizure.
:::

:::warning
**Pulmonary Edema / Acute Respiratory Distress**

Signs: Progressive shortness of breath, pink/frothy sputum, crackling sounds on chest, cyanosis, hypoxemia.

Action: Sit patient upright, provide high-flow oxygen. This can develop rapidly after certain exposures (chlorine gas, cyanide, some organophosphates) and may lead to death within hours. Monitor breathing rate and effort closely; if deteriorating, prepare for manual ventilation.
:::

:::warning
**Hyperthermia (Toxin-Induced Fever)**

Signs: Core temperature >40°C (104°F), sweating (early) or no sweating (late), confusion, altered behavior, muscle rigidity.

Action: Cool patient actively: cold water baths, ice packs to major blood vessel sites (groin, armpits, neck), cool drinks if conscious. Remove excess clothing. Hyperthermia can cause brain damage and death; rapid cooling is essential.
:::

:::warning
**Severe Hypotension / Shock**

Signs: Systolic BP <90 mmHg, weak/rapid pulse, cool/mottled skin, altered consciousness, delayed capillary refill.

Action: Lay patient flat, elevate legs. Start IV fluids if trained (rapid bolus 500mL normal saline, repeat as needed). Monitor perfusion closely. Shock can progress to multi-organ failure; aggressive fluid resuscitation and vasopressor support (if available) may be necessary.
:::

### When to Stop Resuscitation

In survival medicine without hospital support, continued resuscitation becomes futile if:
- No pulse for >20 minutes despite adequate CPR and airway management
- Obvious signs of death (rigor mortis, dependent lividity, decomposition)
- Resources required for continued care exceed survival group's capacity
- Severe injuries incompatible with survival (massive trauma, burns >80% TBSA)

Document time of death and cause if possible for future reference.

<section id="decontamination">

## Decontamination

### Chemical Exposure Decontamination Protocol

**Skin exposure:**
1. Remove contaminated clothing carefully (avoid self-contamination); place in sealed bag.
2. Wash extensively with soap and water. Thorough decontamination more important than rapid. Minimum 5–10 minutes of contact time.
3. Continue if skin irritation persists; some chemicals require extended washing (phenol requires 15+ minutes).
4. Pay special attention to skin folds, fingernails, hair where contaminant may hide.
5. Rinse thoroughly; avoid spreading contaminant to other areas.

**Eye exposure:**
1. Irrigate immediately with clean water or normal saline.
2. Hold eyelids open; flush from nasal side outward to avoid draining into other eye.
3. Continue irrigation minimum 15–20 minutes.
4. If chemical powder: remove visible particles first with cloth, then irrigate.
5. After irrigation, apply topical antibiotic ointment (if available) and loosely bandage.

**Inhalation exposure:**
1. Move to fresh air immediately. If still in contaminated area, relocate upwind/away from source.
2. Provide high-flow oxygen if available (100% O2 reduces some gas toxicities).
3. Monitor breathing closely; observe for delayed pulmonary edema (can occur hours after exposure to gases like chlorine, phosgene).

**Ingestion:**
1. Rinse mouth with water (do NOT swallow rinse water).
2. Spit rinse water out.
3. Do NOT induce vomiting unless specifically indicated (and instructed above).
4. Consider activated charcoal per protocol if appropriate.

### Biological Contamination (Blood/Body Fluids)

- Use appropriate PPE (gloves, gown, mask).
- Clean with soap and water first (removes most organisms).
- Disinfect with 0.5% bleach solution (1 part household bleach to 9 parts water) or 70% isopropyl alcohol. Allow contact time 1–10 minutes depending on disinfectant.
- Properly dispose of contaminated materials in sealed bags away from living/food areas.
- Biological hazards include bloodborne pathogens (hepatitis B/C, HIV if present in community); standard precautions essential.

### Decontamination of Rescuers

Critical to prevent secondary contamination:
1. Change gloves frequently (after each contact, contamination visible).
2. Wash hands and exposed skin with soap/water after patient care.
3. If heavy contamination suspected: remove outer clothing, shower/bathe, change to clean clothing before resuming other tasks.
4. Prevents spread of contamination to other patients, food, water, supplies.
5. In high-contamination incidents: designate decontamination zone separate from living/treatment areas.

<section id="recovery">

## Recovery & Aftercare

### Immediate Post-Treatment Period (First 24–48 hours)

**Monitoring requirements:**
- Continue vital sign checks every 30–60 minutes initially, then every 2–4 hours as patient stabilizes.
- Observe for delayed symptom onset or biphasic reactions (symptoms reappear after initial improvement). Common with certain toxins (organophosphates, insecticides) 4–12 hours after exposure.
- Maintain hydration: offer clear fluids if conscious and able to swallow. Monitor urine output; dark/scanty urine indicates insufficient hydration. Goal: 0.5–1mL/kg/hr urine output.
- Nutrition: Hold food temporarily if GI upset present. Resume clear liquids after 4–6 hours if tolerated, then soft diet.
- Pain management: Treat specific symptoms (headache, muscle aches) with available analgesics.

### 2–7 Day Recovery Phase

**Gradual return to function:**
- Most patients recover within 3–5 days if treated appropriately, though CNS toxins may cause longer mental fog.
- Allow rest; avoid strenuous activity for minimum 3–4 days.
- Continue monitoring for delayed complications (secondary infections from skin burns, aspiration pneumonia if vomited, organ failure from systemic toxins).
- Nutritional support essential: high-protein diet aids tissue repair. Eggs, legumes, bone broth if available.

### Long-Term Monitoring (Weeks 1–4)

:::tip
**Signs of Recovery vs. Deterioration**

Recovery: Improved alertness, appetite return, stable vital signs, return to baseline function within 1–2 weeks.

Deterioration: Persistent altered consciousness, fever, worsening organ function (jaundice, dark urine indicating liver damage; decreased urination indicating kidney damage), recurring symptoms.
:::

**Monitoring for organ damage:**
- Liver toxicity (hepatotoxins like acetaminophen, mushrooms): Monitor for jaundice, dark urine, pale stools, RUQ abdominal pain. Can develop over 2–3 days post-exposure.
- Kidney damage (nephrotoxins like antifreeze, heavy metals): Monitor urine color and volume; decreased output or dark urine worrying. May develop days to weeks after exposure.
- Neurological sequelae (organophosphates, some organics): Lingering weakness, tremor, altered sensation may persist weeks. Supportive care and gradual activity resumption typical.
- Pulmonary damage (inhalation injuries, pulmonary edema): Monitor for persistent cough, shortness of breath, fever; may develop secondary pneumonia.

### Signs of Deterioration Requiring Intervention

:::warning
**Watch for these warning signs:**
- Fever (>38.5°C/101°F), especially if accompanied by cough or difficulty breathing (possible secondary pneumonia)
- Altered consciousness returning or worsening
- Vomiting or blood in vomitus (ongoing GI damage or hemorrhage)
- Urine dark/diminished (kidney damage)
- Jaundice, RUQ pain, pale stools (liver failure)
- Persistent shortness of breath or chest pain
- Seizure recurrence

If any present: restart intensive monitoring, consider repeat antidote dosing if appropriate, prepare for possible multi-organ support.
:::

### Documentation for Continuity

Record for future reference:
- Date/time of exposure
- Substance identified and estimated amount
- Route of exposure
- Timeline of symptom onset and progression
- Interventions provided and timing
- Patient response and recovery timeline
- Any complications or unexpected reactions

This documentation aids future treatment if similar exposure occurs, and helps develop community knowledge of local hazards.

</section>

<section id="troubleshooting">

## Troubleshooting

### Procedure Not Working – No Improvement After Antidote

**Problem:** Patient given antidote but symptoms persist or worsen.

**Likely causes:**
1. Wrong toxin identified; antidote ineffective for actual poison (e.g., giving naloxone for opioid poisoning when poison is stimulant).
2. Insufficient antidote dose or wrong timing (antidotes have treatment windows; too late to be effective).
3. Antidote has short half-life; symptom recurrence common (organophosphate antidotes, naloxone require redosing).
4. Ongoing exposure not terminated (patient still in contaminated environment, or toxin still in GI tract).

**Solutions:**
- Reassess toxin identification; observe toxidrome and reconsider differential diagnosis.
- Redose antidote if available and within treatment window. Document timing carefully.
- Verify decontamination complete; move patient away from exposure source.
- Increase supportive care intensity: airway/breathing support, fluid resuscitation.

### Unexpected Symptom Appearance

**Problem:** New symptoms appearing, not typical for identified toxin.

**Likely causes:**
1. Misidentification of toxin; new symptoms suggest different poison.
2. Multiple toxins ingested (intentional poisoning or contaminated substance with adulterants).
3. Complication from treatment (e.g., aspiration pneumonia from vomiting, secondary infection from contaminated charcoal).
4. Drug interaction or pre-existing condition complicating presentation.

**Solutions:**
- Reassess exposure history; ask directly about multiple substances consumed.
- Consider combination toxidrome (cholinergic + anticholinergic drugs together possible).
- Monitor for new complications (fever, cough, altered breath sounds = pneumonia; increased swelling = cellulitis/infection).
- Support symptoms as they appear; may require multiple interventions simultaneously.

### Activated Charcoal Not Tolerated (Vomiting, Refusal)

**Problem:** Patient refuses charcoal due to taste/grit, or vomits after administration.

**Solutions:**
- Mix charcoal with juice or sweet substance to improve palatability.
- Administer in small frequent doses rather than large single dose.
- Use straw to minimize mouth contact.
- If vomiting: do NOT repeat dose immediately; wait 30–60 minutes, then try again with half dose.
- If persistent vomiting: supportive care with fluids and anti-nausea measures (ginger, ondansetron if available).
- Charcoal still effective if some reaches GI tract; even partial dose better than none.

### Antidote Unavailable for Identified Toxin

**Problem:** Poison identified but specific antidote not stocked or available.

**Solutions:**
- Proceed with supportive care: airway/breathing maintenance, circulation support, symptom management.
- Use other available medications: sedatives for agitation, antipyretics for fever, anticonvulsants for seizures, etc.
- Supportive care alone can prevent death for many toxins; recovery slower but possible.
- Document for future emergency supply planning; consider stocking antidotes for common regional poisonings.

</section>

<section id="field-adaptations">

## Field Adaptations (Without Ideal Equipment)

### Without Activated Charcoal

**Improvised absorbent:** Burn hardwood until charred; crush to fine powder. Mix with water to slurry. Less effective than commercial but acceptable emergency measure. Dosage: 1–2 tablespoons (roughly 15–30g) mixed with water, orally.

**Alternative binders:** Powdered clay (kaolin) or fine soil may have minor absorptive effect; limited effectiveness but available. Avoid if soil contamination risk.

### Without Antidote (Organophosphate/Carbamate Exposure)

**Without atropine:** Supportive care only. Manage airway, provide ventilation, control secretions.
- Suction or manual removal of saliva/mucus to prevent aspiration.
- Reposition frequently to avoid stasis pneumonia.
- Anticholinergic plants (belladonna, datura) historically used but extremely dangerous (risk of overdose and death); NOT recommended.

### Without IV Access

**IM/Intranasal administration:** Most emergency antidotes can be given IM if IV unavailable.
- Naloxone: available as nasal spray (2mg/0.1mL spray, repeat every 2–3 minutes).
- Atropine, epinephrine: IM injection into vastus lateralis (thigh) or deltoid (shoulder).
- Intranasal medications faster than oral but slower than IV; still effective.

**Oral medications:** Some medications can be ground and mixed with liquid for unconscious patients (via nasogastric tube if available, or mixed with honey/syrup and wiped on gums for slow absorption). Not ideal but better than nothing.

### Without Oxygen

**Mouth-to-mouth/mouth-to-nose ventilation:** Less efficient than bag-valve-mask but maintains oxygenation. Continue until patient breathing spontaneously or until resuscitation deemed futile (20+ minutes).

**Positioning:** Semi-upright position reduces aspiration risk if patient vomits. Recovery position (left lateral decubitus) if unconscious.

### Without Decontamination Supplies

**Soap/water:** Most important. Thorough scrubbing with available soap and clean water effective for most chemical exposures.

**Abrasive cleaning:** Wood ash mixed with water can help remove oily/sticky contaminants.

**Dilution:** Dilute chemical toxins with large volumes of water; remove as much as possible.

**Cloth:** Clean cloth to manually remove residual contamination before washing.

</section>

<section id="mechanisms">

## Toxin Mechanisms & Recognition

### Absorption & Distribution

Toxins reach target organs via blood circulation. Lipid-soluble toxins cross blood-brain barrier (CNS effects). Protein-bound toxins limited to intravascular space. Tissue accumulation (bioaccumulation) occurs with certain toxins (heavy metals, pesticides). Half-life determines elimination: long half-life toxins persist (mercury, lead); short half-life cleared quickly (alcohol metabolized ~15g/hour).

### Dose-Response Relationship

Paracelsus principle: dose determines poison. Small amount aspirin: therapeutic. Large amount: fatal. LD50 (lethal dose for 50% of population) varies: ricin (castor bean) extremely potent (LD50 ~3mg/kg oral); salt relatively benign (LD50 ~6000mg/kg). Context matters: individual differences (age, weight, genetics) affect susceptibility.

</section>

<section id="safety">

## Safety Warnings

:::warning
### Critical Poisoning Response Warnings

-   Unknown poison identity = assume life-threatening. Treat symptomatically, decontaminate, monitor closely for progression.
-   Corrosive ingestion = do NOT induce vomiting. Dilute with small sips water/milk only. Seek professional care urgently (risk esophageal perforation, stricture formation).
-   Cyanide exposure = rapid deterioration possible. Remove from source immediately, give oxygen, prepare for cardiac arrest.
-   Altered consciousness + toxic exposure = airway compromise risk. Position for drainage (left lateral decubitus), be prepared to protect airway, avoid aspiration.
-   Repeat dosing needed = some antidotes require repetition. Don't assume single dose sufficient; reassess after treatment interval.
:::

:::tip
### Best Practices for Poison Prevention

-   Label all chemicals clearly with contents and toxicity warning
-   Store chemicals away from food, medications, and children's access
-   Pre-learn identification of regional poisonous plants and animals
-   Stock activated charcoal, basic antidotes if possible (naloxone for opioid communities, atropine for organophosphate regions)
-   Maintain detailed symptom/exposure logs for medical reference
-   Know local poison control methods (center numbers if civilization exists)
:::

### Common Mistakes & Consequences

-   **Induced vomiting for corrosives:** Causes re-exposure of esophagus to caustic substance, severe tissue damage, esophageal perforation risk. **Consequence:** Death from perforation/mediastinitis, or chronic stricture requiring surgical intervention. **Prevention:** Never induce vomiting; dilute only.

-   **Underestimating symptom progression:** Some poisons have delayed onset (mushroom toxins 6–24 hours, antifreeze 12+ hours before neurological symptoms). Patient may appear well initially, then deteriorate rapidly. **Consequence:** Miss critical treatment window; organ failure or death. **Prevention:** Continue monitoring for 24–48 hours minimum even if initially asymptomatic.

-   **Inadequate decontamination:** Rescuers contaminated themselves; secondary contamination of supplies/other patients. **Consequence:** Multiple people poisoned; loss of care capacity. **Prevention:** Change gloves frequently, wash hands/skin between patient contacts, use PPE appropriately.

-   **Antidote non-availability assumption:** Assuming specific antidote required means patient cannot be saved. Actually, supportive care alone prevents death for many toxins; recovery slower but possible. **Consequence:** Premature cessation of care, preventable death. **Prevention:** Always attempt supportive care; use creative solutions (oxygen for CO toxicity even without hyperbaric chamber; manual ventilation for respiratory depression).

-   **Ignoring medication/supplement interactions:** Patient on warfarin (anticoagulant) ingests plant with anticoagulant properties = severe bleeding risk. Toxin severity varies dramatically based on pre-existing medications. **Consequence:** Unexpected severity, inadequate treatment, complications. **Prevention:** Obtain complete history of ALL substances consumed (medications, supplements, herbs, OTC drugs, recreational substances).

-   **Failing to reassess after antidote:** Antidotes like naloxone and organophosphate antidotes have short half-lives. Patient improves initially, then symptoms recur as antidote metabolized. **Consequence:** Patient deteriorates rapidly without redosing. **Prevention:** Monitor closely; reassess at treatment intervals (e.g., every 2–3 minutes for naloxone); redose if symptoms return.

-   **Contamination of water/food supply:** Inadequate containment of toxic substance; contaminant seeps into community water or food stores. **Consequence:** Mass casualty event, loss of critical resources, community collapse. **Prevention:** Secure toxic substances immediately; store in sealed, labeled containers away from living/food areas; dispose of contaminated materials properly.
