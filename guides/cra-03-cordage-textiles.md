## Fiber Sources & Identification

Cordage quality depends on fiber source. Different plants and animals provide fibers with varying properties: strength, flexibility, weather resistance, and durability.

### Animal Fibers:

-   **Hair (horse, cow, deer):** Coarse but strong; excellent for rough cordage and brushes
-   **Intestine:** Natural tubes useful for containers; can be twisted into cordage
-   **Sinew (tendon):** Extremely strong; primary cordage for bows and fishing line

<section id="fiber-extraction">

## Fiber Extraction Techniques

### Water Retting (Softening Plant Fibers):

1.  Harvest plant stems in morning (before heat reduces sap)
2.  Bundle stems and submerge in pond/stream water
3.  Soak 1-2 weeks (until fibers separate easily from woody core)
4.  Remove from water; spread on ground for drying (2-3 days)
5.  Break dried stems to expose fibers
6.  Beat softly with wooden mallet to separate fibers
7.  Comb with crude comb (remove short fibers, wood fragments)

### Dry Retting (Alternative):

Bundle plants; expose to sun/rain for 2-3 weeks. Weather breaks down pectin naturally. Slower than water retting but requires less water.

### Bark Processing:

1.  Harvest bark from softwood tree (birch, willow, cedar) in spring (sap facilitates peeling)
2.  Score bark vertically; peel away from trunk
3.  Separate inner bark from outer bark (inner bark contains fibers)
4.  Soak inner bark 1-2 days; beat to separate fibers
5.  Dry and prepare for spinning

### Sinew Extraction:

1.  Remove large tendons from animal legs/spine immediately after slaughter
2.  Dry sinew fully (2-4 weeks) before processing
3.  Beat dried sinew to separate into fibers
4.  Soak briefly to restore flexibility
5.  Twist fibers together to create cordage

</section>

<section id="fiber-prep">

## Fiber Preparation & Drying

### Cleaning:

Remove plant matter, dirt, and debris from extracted fibers. Soak in water, then beat gently with wooden mallet. Repeat until water runs clear. Dry thoroughly before spinning (moisture prevents twisting).

### Sorting by Length:

Long fibers (combed out) are "line fibers"—suitable for strong cordage. Short fibers remaining are "tow"—used for coarser cordage or padding. Separate by carding (combing action) with two wire brushes.

### Storage:

Keep dried fibers in cool, dry location protected from insects. Bundle fibers in handfuls; wrap loosely to prevent mold. Use within 1-2 years of drying (fibers deteriorate gradually).

</section>

<section id="spinning">

## Spinning Techniques

### Simple Hand Spinning (No Equipment):

1.  Roll prepared fibers between palms, twisting as you roll
2.  As yarn builds, extend hands apart to create length
3.  Fibers naturally align and bind together during twisting
4.  Build yarn gradually (0.5-1 meter per minute pace)
5.  Weak spots appear as necks in the yarn; add extra twist when necks appear

### Drop Spindle Spinning:

Spindle (wooden shaft with whorl for momentum) hangs by thread while twisting fibers. Preparation: loop fibers into hanging thread. Work: hold fibers at top of spindle; allow spindle weight to twist fibers while gradually extending arm to create yarn. Efficiency: 2-4 meters per minute. Superior to hand spinning for consistent results.

**Drop Spindle Construction & Technique:**

1. **Spindle shaft:** Wooden dowel or stick 20-30cm long, 5-10mm diameter. Should be relatively straight and balanced.
2. **Whorl (weight disk):** Wooden disk (5-8cm diameter, 1-2cm thick) attached to spindle shaft 5cm from bottom. Weight provides momentum; heavier whorl (added lead weight) increases twist rate.
3. **Hook or notch:** Small hook at top of spindle (notch carved or nail inserted) catches thread to prevent slipping.
4. **Preparation:** Tie existing thread or yarn to hook; loop fibers into hanging thread; spindle hangs freely.
5. **Spinning technique:**
   - Hold fiber bundle at shoulder height with dominant hand
   - Pinch fibers with thumb/forefinger at desired thickness (controls yarn diameter)
   - Allow spindle weight to twist fibers naturally (clockwise rotation typical)
   - As spindle turns, extend arm downward (increases yarn length created)
   - When spindle reaches hip level, stop (reset by winding created yarn onto spindle shaft)
   - Repeat process continuously
6. **Consistency improvement:** Maintain even hand position and controlled release rate. Rhythm becomes automatic after 100+ meters of practice.

**Common errors:** Pinching too tightly (fiber doesn't twist evenly), releasing too quickly (yarn becomes weak), inconsistent rhythm (creates lumps). Practice improves dramatically over time.

### Spinning Wheel:

Mechanical device creating continuous twist. Treadle-powered wheel ideal for survival situations (no electricity). Consistency: much faster and more uniform than spindle (10-20 meters per minute possible). Requires wheel construction or salvage, but investment pays dividends in production speed.

**Spinning Wheel Components:**

1. **Large wheel:** Main spinning wheel (30-50cm diameter) powered by treadle. Wheel rotates continuously, driving flyer.
2. **Flyer:** Bobbin-shaped component with twisted fiber guide. Rotates faster than main wheel (gear ratio amplifies speed 3-5x).
3. **Bobbin:** Spool receiving spun yarn. Rotates at flyer speed, winding finished yarn.
4. **Treadle:** Foot-powered pedal driving main wheel through belt or crank. Operator controls speed by pedal rhythm.
5. **Fiber input:** Fibers fed through tension guide (controls twist rate) into flyer (creates spiral twist).

**Operating technique:**
- Begin pedaling smoothly (maintain steady rhythm)
- Feed fiber into flyer opening; allow tension guide to control twist rate
- Guide fiber to bobbin opening (controlled winding)
- Pedal speed determines yarn thickness (faster pedaling = finer yarn)
- Maintain continuous treadle motion for uniform results

**Advantage:** Produces 10-20 meters per minute (5-10x spindle speed). Enables one-person production meeting community needs. Disadvantage: requires initial investment/salvage of wheel; takes 1-2 weeks to master.

### Yarn Properties:

-   **Ply:** Twist angle affects strength. Tight twist = stronger but stiffer; loose twist = more elastic but weaker
-   **Weight:** Thinner yarn requires more twist; thicker yarn requires less twist
-   **Smoothness:** Consistent twist produces uniform appearance; variable twist looks coarse

<section id="twisting">

## Twisting & Cabling

### Two-Ply Twist:

1.  Spin two separate yarns (approximately equal thickness for balanced result)
2.  Hold ends together at one point; secure ends with temporary knot
3.  Twist both yarns together in opposite direction to their individual twist (if yarns twisted clockwise, twist ply counter-clockwise)
4.  Result: balanced yarn with doubled strength, improved elasticity, and uniform appearance

**Two-ply details:** Tension must remain consistent between two yarns (uneven tension creates lumpy ply). Walk backward while twisting to maintain even twist rate (similar to hand spinning technique).

### Three-Ply or Higher:

Twist multiple yarns together for even greater strength. Four-ply typical for rope-weight cordage. Twist direction: alternate direction from previous ply for mechanical binding.

**Ply calculation:** Each additional ply multiplies strength approximately. Three-ply shows 2.5-3x single yarn strength. Four-ply approximately 3-3.5x. Five-ply and higher show diminishing returns (complexity increases faster than strength gain).

**Multi-ply technique:**
1. Spin 3-4 base yarns to thickness equal to final single strand
2. Hold together at top; secure with temporary tie
3. Separate slightly (two in each hand) and begin counter-rotation
4. Gradually bring hands together while maintaining twist tension
5. Continue walking backward as twist forms throughout length
6. Result: multi-ply cord ready for rope making

### Cabling (Creating Large Rope):

1.  Prepare multiple 3-4 ply cords (each cord 8-10mm diameter typical)
2.  Bundle 3 cords together (arrange in triangle formation for balanced cabling)
3.  Twist bundle in opposite direction to cord twist
4.  Creates rope with excellent strength and flexibility
5.  Typical rope: 3 cords x 4-ply each = very strong cordage (supports 500+ kg load)

**Cabling process details:**
1. Secure three cords at one end; spread to triangle formation (each cord separated by 120 degrees)
2. Clamp one person at far end; second person begins counter-twisting at working end
3. As twist progresses, walk backward (increases cabling tension and uniformity)
4. Watch for barrel-shaped thickening (indicates over-twisting; reduce pressure)
5. Twist approximately 50-60 rotations per meter of rope (slightly less than single-strand rope)
6. Secure final end by binding (prevents unraveling)

**Cabling strength factors:** Cord quality (consistency in ply prevents weak spots), twist angle (45-50 degrees optimal), tension uniformity (maintains structural integrity). Professional rope makers achieve 90-95% of theoretical strength; initial attempts often 70-80%.

:::info-box
**Rope strength hierarchy:** Single yarn < 2-ply cord < 3-ply cord < 3-strand rope < cabled rope. Cabled rope of same diameter as 3-strand rope provides 20-30% additional strength at cost of complexity.
:::

</section>

<section id="rope-making">

## Rope Making Methods

### Three-Strand Rope (Most Common):

1.  Prepare 3 equal-length cords (minimum 1.5m for functional rope 50cm long)
2.  Bundle cords together at one end; tie temporary knot
3.  Separate into 3 strands; hold with one hand
4.  Twist strands in opposite direction while walking backward with other hand
5.  Each step increases rope length and tightness
6.  Complete approximately 100 twists per meter of rope
7.  Secure end with binding (prevents unraveling)

### Four-Strand Rope:

Superior strength with same diameter compared to three-strand. Construction identical except 4 strands instead of 3. Slightly slower to braid but more durable result.

### Testing Rope Quality:

Bend rope into circle—should return to straight form. Rope that remains bent lacks adequate twist. Pull rope with force—should not slip or unravel. Properly made rope withstands 80-90% of component fiber strength.

:::tip
**Tip:** Create trial ropes from readily available materials (cattail fluff, dried grass) before using valuable fibers. Technique improves significantly over 10-20 practice ropes.
:::

</section>

<section id="net-making">

## Net Construction

### Simple Hand-Tied Net (Fishing or Trapping):

1.  Prepare cordage: 6-8 meters length, 3-5mm diameter
2.  Create frame: bend cordage into rectangle or triangle
3.  Tie cross-strings at 10-15cm intervals creating grid
4.  Tie diagonal strings creating diamond-shaped mesh (typical fish net opening: 2-3cm)
5.  Secure all intersections with square knots
6.  Complete net: 1-2 square meters size, 2-3 hours labor

**Net construction technique:**
- Frame should be rigid (use flexible but strong cordage; birch or willow branches acceptable for temporary nets)
- Cross-strings should have slight tension (prevents sagging)
- Diagonal strings create strength through triangulation
- Knot placement: all knots at same distance from intersection (creates uniform appearance and equal stress distribution)
- Test loading: apply tension to net before deployment (identify weak spots for reinforcement)

### Knot Mesh Method:

Alternative: create mesh by tying individual knots rather than weaving. Knots ensure mesh openings remain uniform. Typical knot: square knot between adjacent lines. More secure but slower than woven mesh.

**Knot mesh advantages:** All openings identical size (important for size-selective fishing), redundant structure (if one section fails, others remain intact), easier mesh adjustment (tie knots closer for smaller openings, farther for larger).

**Drawback:** Time-intensive (each knot requires multiple steps). Practical for 0.5-1 square meter nets; larger nets impractical using this method.

### Weighted Edges (Fish Nets):

Attach weights (stones wrapped in cordage, or bone/clay weights) to bottom edge. Weights keep net bottom submerged while floats (wood or cork) keep top above water. Fish entering net push it down, creating collapse that traps fish.

**Weight calculation:** Use approximately 50-100g per 10cm perimeter (varies by water depth/current). Test net in water before deployment—should sink bottom edge immediately while float line supports top edge.

**Float selection:** Cork preferred (excellent buoyancy, durable). Wood pieces acceptable (birch, pine). Seal wood with oil/paint to prevent water absorption (waterlogged wood loses buoyancy).

**Installation:** Tie floats to top edge at 30-50cm intervals; tie weights to bottom edge at same intervals (symmetrical spacing improves stability).

</section>

<section id="fishing-line">

## Fishing Line & Specialized Cordage

### Fine Fishing Line from Sinew:

1.  Prepare sinew fibers (very thin, 1-2mm)
2.  Twist 3-4 fibers together lightly
3.  Result: extremely strong line (sufficient to land fish 2-5kg)
4.  Knot strength: use clinch knot or improved clinch knot
5.  Diameter: 1-2mm; invisible underwater

### Hair/Intestine Fishing Line:

Horse hair bundled and twisted creates surprisingly strong fishing line (2-3kg test strength). Intestine (gut) from fish or animal dried and twisted creates very fine, strong line. Both materials less visible underwater than plant fiber cordage.

### Specialized Knots for Fishing:

-   **Palomar knot:** Strong, simple, reliable for attaching lure/hook
-   **Clinch knot:** Excellent strength; requires 5-7 wraps
-   **Surgeon's knot:** Joins two lines with strength

<section id="common-mistakes">

## Common Mistakes & Solutions

### Mistake 1: Inadequate Fiber Preparation

**Error:** Spinning fibers that still contain woody material or dirt. Results: weak cordage that breaks during use, poor twist consistency.

**Solution:** Thoroughly clean and comb fibers before spinning. Remove short fibers and fragments. Allow proper drying (moisture content 10-15% optimal).

### Mistake 2: Inconsistent Twist

**Error:** Varying twist rate while spinning. Some sections over-twisted, others under-twisted. Results: weak cordage that breaks at under-twisted sections.

**Solution:** Establish consistent pace and hand positions. Practice spinning on simple materials first. Rhythm is more important than speed.

### Mistake 3: Insufficient Final Twist in Rope

**Error:** Twisting strands together only 50-70 times per meter. Rope lacks sufficient tightness to bind fibers. Results: fibers slip under load.

**Solution:** Twist at least 80-100 times per meter of rope. Rope should appear very tight and dense. Test-load rope to verify strength before trusting with critical tasks.

### Mistake 4: Poor Knot Selection in Net

**Error:** Using simple overhand knots in fishing nets. Knots slip under load as fish struggles. Results: net unravels during use.

**Solution:** Use square knots, improved clinch knots, or other reliable knots. Test knot strength by pulling hard before completing entire net.

</section>

<section id="production-rates">

## Cordage Production Rates

### Hand Spinning Output:

- Beginner: 0.3-0.5 meters per minute (20-30 m/hour)
- Intermediate: 1-2 meters per minute (60-120 m/hour)
- Advanced: 2-3 meters per minute (120-180 m/hour)

**Note:** Speed varies by fiber type. Flax/nettle produces faster than hemp. Thinner yarn requires higher twist rate, reducing speed.

**Optimization factors:** Pre-prepared fibers (cleaned, dried, sorted by length) increase speed 20-30% compared to working with raw fibers. Consistent hand position and rhythm improve speed more than aggressive hand motion. Experienced spinners develop automatic muscle memory enabling speed without concentration.

### Drop Spindle Production:

- Beginner: 1-2 meters per minute (60-120 m/hour)
- Intermediate: 2-4 meters per minute (120-240 m/hour)
- Advanced: 4-6 meters per minute (240-360 m/hour)

Spindle speed 2-3x hand spinning for most operators. Slight learning curve (20-50 meters needed to develop rhythm) but sustainable once technique established.

### Spinning Wheel Production:

- Beginner (learning): 5-8 meters per minute (300-480 m/hour)
- Intermediate: 10-15 meters per minute (600-900 m/hour)
- Advanced: 15-20 meters per minute (900-1200 m/hour)

Wheel provides 5-10x spindle speed once operator masters treadle coordination. Initial 2-4 weeks of practice required to reach intermediate efficiency.

### Rope Making Output:

Three-strand rope production: 1-3 meters per hour (includes gathering strands, twisting, securing ends). Team production (two people): one person twists, one person walks backward for tension—doubles output (2-6 meters per hour).

**Rope production calculation:** To produce 100 meters rope requires approximately: 300-400 meters 3-strand cord (each strand 30-40cm per 10cm rope), or 6-8 hours single-operator time. Team doubles output to 3-4 hours.

### Annual Production Potential:

One skilled cordage maker, working 6 hours daily, 300 days yearly = 1,800-2,200 meters rope annually. Sufficient for: tool handles (50-100 items), nets (5-10 units), binding/rigging (extensive use), trading stock.

**Team production (2-3 people):** 5,000-8,000 meters annual production. Enables commercial operation: supply local community, regional markets, trade goods for other essentials.

**Bottleneck analysis:** Fiber preparation typically requires 40% of total time; spinning 35%; rope making 25%. Parallel workflow (one person prepares fiber while another spins, third makes rope) maximizes throughput for group operations.

</section>

<section id="fiber-quality">

## Advanced Fiber Selection

### Fiber Microbiology:

Properly retted fiber: microbes break down pectins (plant glue holding fiber to wood). Under-retting: pectins remain, fiber stiff. Over-retting: fiber weakens, breaks during processing. Ideal retting shows: fibers separate easily from woody core without tearing.

### Climate Effects on Retting:

- **Cool climate (10-15°C):** Retting takes 3-4 weeks; slower but produces superior fiber
- **Warm climate (20-25°C):** Retting takes 1-2 weeks; faster but requires careful timing
- **Very warm (>25°C):** Retting can complete in 5-7 days; risk of over-retting increases

### Fiber Testing:

Test retting completion: bend fiber bundle sharply. Properly retted: fibers separate easily, woody core breaks cleanly. Under-retted: fibers resist separation, bend without breaking.

:::warning
**Over-Retting Risk:** Extended retting produces weak fiber. Mushy consistency when wet indicates over-retting—fibers have begun deteriorating. Start fresh if over-retted.
:::

:::info-box
**Fiber Quality Hierarchy:** Premium fiber (long, uniform, clean) can be spun finer and stronger. Lower-quality fiber (short, woody debris) produces thicker, weaker cordage. Sort fibers by length before spinning for consistent results.
:::

## Specialized Cordage Applications

### Water-Resistant Cordage (Tar Coating):

Traditional sailors coated rope with tar to waterproof. Application:
1. Heat tar to liquid state (70-80°C)
2. Dip finished rope briefly (1-2 seconds)
3. Hang to cool
4. Result: water-resistant coating extends rope life in marine use

Disadvantage: tar makes rope stiff; reduces flexibility.

### Emergency Cordage (Bark/Plant Alternatives):

If conventional fibers unavailable:
- **Willow/birch bark strips:** Soak and strip inner bark, twist into cordage
- **Grass bundles:** Dry grass twisted produces weak but usable cordage (temporary use only)
- **Vine/root cordage:** Flexible but weaker than plant fiber cordage

### Double-Twisted Cordage (Maximum Strength):

For critical applications (safety lines, load-bearing):
1. Create initial twisted cordage normally
2. Twist cordage again in opposite direction
3. Result: reinforced cordage 20-30% stronger than single twist

Drawback: time-consuming; rarely justified except for life-safety applications.

:::danger
**Critical Cordage Safety:** Any cordage used for life-safety (climbing, water safety, rescue) requires maximum strength and reliability. Use best-quality fiber, double-twist technique, and extensive testing before deployment. Cordage failure during rescue operation is catastrophic.
:::

## Cordage Storage & Preservation

### Proper Storage:

- **Location:** Cool, dry, dark (heat/moisture/sunlight degrade fiber)
- **Coiling:** Coil rope loosely (tight coiling creates permanent kinks)
- **Support:** Hang on pegs or store on racks (prevents kinking)
- **Moisture control:** Separate pieces with paper (prevents mildew)

### Shelf Life:

Properly stored cordage: 2-3 years with minimal deterioration. Beyond 5 years: strength declines 20-30%. Very old cordage: visible weakness (brittleness, discoloration).

### Mold Prevention:

Signs of mold: dark spots, musty smell, sliminess. Prevention:
- Keep storage dry (ventilation crucial)
- Remove moldy pieces immediately (prevents spread)
- Dry damp cordage in sun before storage

Moldy cordage: attempt drying in sun/heat (sometimes recovers); otherwise discard.

<section id="safety-considerations">

## Safety During Cordage Work

### Repetitive Strain Prevention:

Spinning and rope making involve repetitive motions. Prevent injury:
- **Breaks:** Every hour, take 10-minute break; stretch arms and shoulders
- **Posture:** Maintain upright position; don't hunch over work
- **Tool design:** Use ergonomic spindle grip to reduce hand fatigue
- **Rotation:** Alternate between spinning, fiber preparation, other tasks

### Chemical Safety (Retting):

Water retting produces: organic materials, microbes, hydrogen sulfide gas (bad smell). Safety precautions:
- Work outdoors or in ventilated area
- Avoid prolonged exposure to retting water (causes skin irritation)
- Drain retting water away from living areas
- Avoid ingesting retting water (contains bacteria)

### Sharp Tool Safety (Fiber Combing):

Cards and combs have sharp metal teeth. Prevent cuts:
- Keep hands clear of teeth during use
- Don't hand cards to others while fingers engaged
- Store cards with protective covers
- Use proper technique to avoid hand-to-teeth contact
