Spinning is the craft of transforming raw plant and animal fibers into yarn—a continuous strand strong enough to be woven, knitted, or braided. Before the Industrial Revolution, spinning was ubiquitous; nearly every household produced its own yarn. The skill remains vital for self-sufficiency and is experiencing renewed interest.

The process begins with raw fiber preparation (carding, combing) and progresses through twisting individual fibers together on a spindle, then plying multiple yarns together to increase strength and versatility. Understanding fiber properties, twist direction, and weight standards enables production of yarn suited to specific uses: fine yarn for linen cloth, thick yarn for blankets, elastic yarn for clothing.

This guide covers fiber preparation, spindle and wheel spinning, and the fundamentals of yarn construction.

## Fiber Preparation: Carding and Combing

This guide starts after raw fiber has already been harvested and processed into usable spinning stock. For retting, breaking, scutching, and hackling at the plant-fiber stage, hand off to <a href="../natural-fiber-extraction.html">Natural Fiber Extraction and Processing</a>.

Raw fibers from animals or plants are tangled, dirty, and unusable. Preparation aligns them and removes debris.

### Washing Raw Fiber

Before carding, raw fiber should be cleaned of dirt, plant matter, and oils.

**Method:**

1. Fill a large pot with warm water (not hot—extreme temperature can felt wool).
2. Submerge the raw fiber and let it soak for 15–20 minutes without agitation.
3. Drain the water gently (do not wring).
4. Refill with clean water and soak again. Repeat 3–4 times until the water runs clear.
5. Final rinse in cool water to set the fiber's crimp (natural waves in fiber structure).
6. Squeeze out excess water gently and spread the fiber on a clean cloth to dry completely (1–3 days depending on humidity and fiber type).

:::info-box
Never agitate wet wool. Wool fibers have scales along their surface; agitation causes the scales to lock together, a process called felting. Once felted, the fiber is ruined.
:::

### Carding

Carding aligns fibers and opens them up, making them ready for spinning.

**Cards (tools):**
- Two wooden paddles (about 4 × 8 inches each) with rows of short, stiff wires embedded in the surface.
- Wires are angled so that passing one card over the other aligns fibers and draws them from one card to the other.
- Cards come in different sizes; finer cards (closer wire spacing) are for fine fibers like cotton and fine wool; coarser cards for long or coarse fibers.

**Carding process:**

1. Load one card (the stationary card) with a handful of fiber, spreading it evenly across the wires. Hold this card in your lap or clamped to a table.
2. Take the second card (the working card) and stroke it over the stationary card, moving toward your body, at a low angle.
3. The fibers gradually transfer from the stationary card to the working card.
4. Once most fibers have transferred, gently pull the working card away, using a peeling motion to keep fibers aligned.
5. Repeat, carding the fiber back and forth between cards 4–6 times until it is well-aligned and fluffy (this is called a "rolag" or "carded batt").
6. When the carded fiber is fluffy and all fibers appear aligned, roll it loosely into a cigar shape for storage until spinning.

**Fiber loss:** Some fiber is lost in carding (stuck to the cards, lost as dust). Expect 85–90% yield.

### Combing

Combing aligns fibers more thoroughly than carding and produces a smoother, finer yarn. It's used primarily for long fibers like flax, hemp, and long-staple wool.

**Combs (tools):**
- Multiple long, thin teeth set in a wooden or metal frame.
- Combs come in different teeth spacings; fine combs for fine fibers, coarse combs for long fibers.

**Combing process:**

1. Secure one comb (the stationary comb) in a table vise or clamp.
2. Hold a bundle of fiber loosely in one hand.
3. Using the second comb (the working comb), comb through the fiber with the teeth angled slightly forward.
4. Work from the tip of the fiber bundle toward the root, combing out one section at a time.
5. Repeat with the working comb in the opposite direction (reverse comb) until all fibers are parallel.
6. Pull the straightened fibers from the stationary comb as a "top" or "sliver."

**Yields and fineness:** Combing removes more waste than carding but produces a finer, stronger yarn suited to linen, hemp, or fine wool fabric.

### Hackling (for Bast Fibers)

Hackling is specialized combing for plant fibers (flax, hemp, nettle) that have been retted (see Cordage guide for retting details).

1. The hackle is a large comb with many fine, sharp teeth.
2. The retted fiber is pulled through the hackle repeatedly, aligning the finest fibers and removing woody debris.
3. Multiple passes through progressively finer hackles separate the fiber into grades: coarse (short fibers), medium, and fine.
4. Fine hackled fiber produces the smoothest, strongest yarn.

## Drop Spindle: Construction and Use

The drop spindle is the simplest spinning tool—a weight on a stick that twists fiber into yarn.

### Spindle Design and Construction

**Components:**

1. **Whorl:** A heavy disk (2–4 inches diameter, 1/2–1 inch thick) that provides rotational inertia.
   - Traditionally wood, ceramic, or stone.
   - Heavier whorls spin longer and twist finer fibers; lighter whorls are faster but suited to coarse fibers.

2. **Shaft:** A thin wooden rod (8–12 inches long, 1/4 inch diameter) inserted through the whorl's center.
   - Wood should be straight-grained and smooth.
   - Ash, maple, or birch are traditional.

3. **Hooks:** One or two small hooks (made from bent wire or carved wood) at the top of the shaft to guide the yarn.

**Construction:**

1. Turn or carve a wooden whorl to the desired diameter and thickness.
2. Drill a 1/4-inch hole through the center.
3. Sand the shaft smooth and insert it through the whorl.
4. Carve or bend a hook from a small piece of wire and secure it to the shaft tip.
5. The whorl should spin freely; if friction is present, enlarge the hole slightly or use beeswax to reduce friction.

### Spindle Spinning Technique

**Setup:**

1. Secure carded fiber (a rolag) to the spindle's hook.
2. Draft the fiber by pinching a small portion between your thumb and forefinger and gently pulling away from the rolag, drawing out a thin strand.
3. Let the spindle hang freely, twisting as it drops.

**The rhythm:**

1. **Draft:** Pull a steady stream of fine fiber from the rolag, maintaining tension with your fingers.
2. **Twist:** The spinning spindle twists the drawn fiber, securing it into a strong yarn.
3. **Wind on:** Once a length of yarn has been spun (typically 12–24 inches), stop the spindle and wind the yarn onto the shaft, leaving a short tail free at the hook.
4. Repeat.

**Spindle speed control:**
- Wind the yarn up the shaft closer to the whorl to slow the spindle (increases control for fine yarn).
- Wind lower on the shaft for faster spinning (faster production of thicker yarn).

### Spindle Yield and Fineness

A practiced spinner can produce about 1 ounce of yarn per hour with a drop spindle. Fineness depends on drafting skill and fiber type:
- **Fine yarn:** 2000–3000 yards per pound
- **Medium yarn:** 1000–1500 yards per pound
- **Coarse yarn:** 500–1000 yards per pound

### Hand-Spindle Design Variations

Traditional drop spindles exist in numerous designs, each optimized for different fiber types, production rates, and skill levels.

#### Top-Whorl Spindle

**Design:** Whorl positioned near the top of the shaft, just below the hook.

**Characteristics:**
- Most common historical design (medieval Europe)
- Shorter falling distance before yarn is wound on shaft
- Produces tighter twist faster
- Better for coarse, sturdy fibers (wool, hemp)
- Requires more frequent yarn winding (whorl fills up faster)

**Specifications:**
- Whorl diameter: 1.5–2.5 inches
- Whorl weight: 0.5–1 oz (lighter for fine control, heavier for momentum)
- Shaft length: 8–10 inches
- Shaft diameter: 1/4 inch
- Hook position: 1 inch above whorl

**Best for:** Beginners; wool spinning; coarse yarn production.

#### Bottom-Whorl Spindle

**Design:** Whorl positioned near the bottom of the shaft, 1–2 inches above the pointed end.

**Characteristics:**
- Longer falling distance creates more twist per rotation
- Lower center of mass creates more momentum
- Better for fine fibers (cotton, silk) requiring high twist
- Capacity larger (more yarn winds on shaft before requiring unloading)
- Slower spin rate suitable for delicate fiber handling

**Specifications:**
- Whorl diameter: 2–3 inches (larger than top-whorl for momentum)
- Whorl weight: 1–1.5 oz (heavier to maintain spin longer)
- Shaft length: 9–12 inches
- Shaft diameter: 1/4 inch
- Shaft taper: slight taper toward bottom for balance

**Best for:** Fine yarns; silk, cotton, and flax; experienced spinners.

#### Turkish Spindle (or Turkish Drop Spindle)

**Design:** Whorl positioned mid-shaft with an open, X-shaped or butterfly-shaped structure above and below the whorl (no continuous shaft).

**Characteristics:**
- Yarn winds directly on arms of the spindle, not on a continuous shaft
- No threading required; yarn simply wraps around arms
- Very portable and lightweight
- Fast yarn removal (simply unwind from arms)
- Requires skilled hand tension to prevent tangling
- Excellent for producing multiple-ply (plying from the spindle)

**Specifications:**
- Whorl diameter: 2–2.5 inches
- Whorl weight: 0.75–1.25 oz
- Arm length: 4–6 inches (extending from whorl)
- Arm diameter: 1/8 inch hardwood or bamboo

**Construction:**
- Whorl is central disk (wood or ceramic)
- Four arms (or two long crossed arms) extend from whorl
- No shaft; the arms serve as spinning mechanism and yarn storage

**Best for:** Portable spinning; producing plied yarn; experienced spinners comfortable with more complex mechanics.

#### Navajo Spindle (Supported Spindle)

**Design:** Whorl positioned at bottom, but spindle is supported from above (not freely suspended).

**Characteristics:**
- Spindle rests in a bowl or on a surface; does not hang freely
- Hand rotates whorl against table or in bowl
- No gravity assist; operator controls all momentum
- Excellent for very fine fibers (requires precise control)
- Less fatigue for long spinning sessions (no continuous holding)
- Common in Navajo, Andean, and Middle Eastern traditions

**Specifications:**
- Whorl diameter: 3–4 inches (larger for stable surface contact)
- Whorl weight: 1.5–2.5 oz (heavier to maintain rotation against bowl friction)
- Shaft length: 6–8 inches (shorter; not suspended)
- Shaft diameter: 1/4 inch

**Operation:**
- Place spindle whorl in bowl or on flat surface
- Rotate whorl with hand or fingers to spin shaft
- Fiber enters from hand-held rolag or prepared top
- Yarn winds on shaft as it spins

**Best for:** Fine, delicate fibers; long production sessions; experienced spinners.

### Whorl Weight and Fiber Type

Whorl weight directly affects spinning characteristics and suitable fiber types.

| Whorl Weight | Best Fiber | Spin Rate | Yarn Fineness | Momentum | Notes |
|-----------|-----------|----------|---------------|----------|-------|
| **0.3–0.5 oz** | Fine (silk, fine wool) | Very high | Very fine | Low | Lightweight; requires constant re-spinning; sensitive to technique |
| **0.5–1.0 oz** | Medium (wool, linen) | High | Medium-fine | Medium | Versatile; good for most applications |
| **1.0–1.5 oz** | Coarse (hemp, jute) | Moderate | Medium-coarse | High | Heavy momentum; stable spin; good for beginners |
| **1.5–2.5+ oz** | Very coarse (thick roving) | Low | Coarse/thick | Very high | Sustained spin; heavy drop; for supported spindles |

**Selection method:** Start with 0.75–1.0 oz whorl. If spinning is too fast and hard to control, reduce whorl weight. If spindle dies too quickly, increase weight.

### Shaft Specifications and Balance

The shaft carries yarn weight and must be straight and balanced.

**Shaft characteristics:**

- **Material:** Ash, maple, or birch (straight grain, moderate weight)
- **Diameter:** 1/4 inch optimal (too thin = flex/whip; too thick = heavy, hard to rotate)
- **Length:** 8–12 inches typical (longer = more yarn storage, harder to manage; shorter = less storage, faster wind-on)
- **Straightness:** Critical; a bent shaft causes wobbling and uneven yarn
- **Taper:** Slight taper toward bottom (1/4 inch at top, 3/16 inch at bottom) improves balance

**Balancing the spindle:**

- Before adding whorl, balance the bare shaft by hanging from a string at its center of gravity
- The point where it balances is where the whorl should be positioned
- A well-balanced spindle spins more freely and requires less initial momentum

### Supported Spindle Designs for Very Fine Fibers

For extremely fine yarns (cashmere, silk, angora), a supported spindle provides superior control.

**Supported spindle setup:**

- Spindle shaft rests in a bowl, cup, or Y-shaped support (does not hang freely)
- Whorl extends below shaft and makes contact with bowl bottom
- Operator spins whorl with fingertips or hand rotation against supporting surface
- Very fine fibers are drafted with extreme precision

**Advantages:**
- No gravity assist; operator controls all spinning momentum (maximum precision)
- Suitable for fibers that break easily if twisted too fast
- Less arm fatigue (spindle is supported, not held)
- Excellent for producing multiple plied yarns from the spindle

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires skill and practice (no gravity assist to help maintain spin)
- Slower production rate (control prioritized over speed)
- More complex setup

## Spinning Wheel Principles and Operation

A spinning wheel mechanizes the spindle's rotation, allowing faster and more consistent yarn production. For building a wheel from scratch, see <a href="../spinning-wheel-construction.html">Spinning Wheel Construction</a>.

### Wheel Components and Mechanics

**Major parts:**

1. **Spinning wheel:** A large (12–18 inch diameter) wooden wheel turned by a foot treadle or hand crank.
2. **Flyer assembly:** A shaped wooden or metal component with multiple hooks that:
   - Rotates at high speed (ratio of 5:1 to 12:1 relative to the wheel)
   - Twists incoming fiber
   - Moves the fiber along the spindle, distributing it evenly
3. **Spindle:** A tapered metal or wooden rod where twisted yarn accumulates.
4. **Treadle:** A foot pedal that drives the spinning wheel via a drive band (cord or rope loop) connecting the wheel to the spindle whorl (on a foot wheel) or a crank handle (on a hand wheel).
5. **Orifice:** The opening where fiber enters the flyer; fiber is guided through the orifice and under one of the hooks.

### Operation

**Setup:**

1. Thread the already-spun yarn through the orifice and around a hook on the flyer.
2. Secure the yarn end to the spindle bobbin (a removable spool).
3. Begin treadling with one foot while feeding fiber with both hands into the orifice.

**Spinning:**

1. **Treadle control:** Push the treadle in a smooth, steady rhythm. An uneven rhythm produces uneven yarn.
2. **Fiber feed:** Maintain steady tension as you draft the fiber from your prepared rolag or top.
3. **Twist direction:** The direction the wheel turns determines twist direction (S or Z; see Twist Direction, below).
4. **Flyer hook:** As the spindle fills, move the yarn from hook to hook along the flyer to distribute it evenly. This prevents tangling and maximizes spindle capacity.

**Production rate:**
- A skilled wheel spinner can produce 6–12 ounces of yarn per hour, depending on fineness.

### Wheel Maintenance

- Keep the wheel well-oiled (all pivot points) but not dripping—excess oil attracts dust.
- Inspect the drive band regularly; replace if frayed or cracked.
- Check the ratio (number of wheel turns per flyer turn) by counting: one full wheel rotation should make the flyer spin 5–12 times depending on drive band ratio.
- The whorl (the drive wheel groove on the spindle) and the main wheel should be in good contact; if the drive band is slack, adjust the spindle position forward.

For detailed alignment and tuning procedures, see the <a href="../spinning-wheel-construction.html#alignment-tuning">Alignment & Tuning</a> section of Spinning Wheel Construction.

## Twist Direction (S/Z Twist) and Implications

Yarn twist direction affects fabric appearance, strength, and feel.

### S-Twist vs. Z-Twist

Twist direction is named after the visual pattern of the twisting fibers.

**S-twist (left-handed twist):**
- When you hold the yarn vertically, the fibers spiral upward from lower-left to upper-right.
- The pattern resembles the diagonal stroke of the letter "S."
- Produced when the spindle rotates clockwise (from above).

**Z-twist (right-handed twist):**
- Fibers spiral upward from lower-right to upper-left (like the letter "Z").
- Produced when the spindle rotates counterclockwise (from above).
- More common in modern spinning.

### Twist Balance and Ply

In a single-ply yarn (yarn that hasn't been plied with other yarns), twist direction affects how the yarn behaves:
- **S-twist yarn:** Slightly less common, tends to unwind if not locked by plying.
- **Z-twist yarn:** More stable, less likely to unwind.

When two yarns are plied together:
- **S-plied Z-twist yarn:** Two Z-twist singles (plural: singles) are twisted together in an S direction. This is the most common construction; the opposing twists lock each other in place, creating a stable, balanced yarn.
- **Z-plied S-twist yarn:** Less common but also stable.
- **Same-direction plying (e.g., S-plied S-twist):** Not recommended; the plied yarn can twist too tightly or too loosely and is unstable.

### Twist Characteristics

The amount of twist (the number of twists per inch) affects yarn properties:

| Twist Per Inch | Characteristic |
|---|---|
| <5 | Soft, fluffy, weak (suitable for thick blankets) |
| 5-8 | Balanced, good elasticity, moderate strength |
| 8-12 | Strong, less fluffy, harder feel |
| >12 | Very strong but harsh, prone to tangling |

A well-balanced yarn for general weaving has about 6–8 twists per inch.

## Plying: Combining Multiple Yarns

Plying is the process of twisting two or more single yarns together to create a stronger, more balanced yarn.

### Two-Ply Construction

**Method:**

1. Spin two separate singles with Z-twist.
2. Wind each onto a separate bobbin.
3. Thread both bobbins into an S-twist flyer (on the spinning wheel, reverse the wheel direction).
4. Hold the two yarns together at the orifice and feed them through simultaneously as you treadle.
5. The flyer twists the two yarns together, combining them into a 2-ply yarn.

**Result:** A 2-ply yarn is roughly twice as thick as a single, much stronger, and balanced (S-twist plying Z-twist singles creates an inherently stable yarn).

### Multi-Ply Options

**3-ply:** Three singles twisted together. Often all the same weight, producing a smooth, symmetrical yarn. Excellent for fine work (linen cloth, fine knit garments).

**4-ply:** Two 2-ply yarns plied together. Creates a thicker yarn with excellent elasticity, ideal for heavy clothing and blankets.

**Balanced 2-ply:** Asymmetrical: one thick single and one thin single plied together. Combines the strength of 2-ply with the color variation of the two different-colored singles.

### Plying Calculations

To produce a plied yarn of a desired weight, estimate the singles' thickness:
- **Target:** 2-ply yarn of 1000 yards per pound
- **Singles:** Plan for each single to be about 1500–2000 yards per pound (plying reduces length by about 10–15%)
- **Test:** Spin a small sample, ply it, and measure yards per pound to confirm.

## Fiber Types and Characteristics

Different fibers have different properties suited to different applications.

### Animal Fibers

**Wool (sheep):**
- **Properties:** Elastic, insulating, water-resistant, takes dye readily, can felt if mishandled.
- **Staple length:** 1–6 inches (longer in coarse breeds, shorter in fine merino).
- **Best for:** Blankets, sweaters, socks (elastic and insulating).
- **Yield:** 1–4 pounds clean fiber per sheep depending on breed and age.

**Alpaca:**
- **Properties:** Very fine (finer than wool), silky, less elastic, less water-resistant than wool, excellent insulation.
- **Staple length:** 2–4 inches.
- **Best for:** Fine clothing, wraps, luxury items.
- **Yield:** 1–2 pounds per animal per year.

**Goat hair (mohair, cashmere):**
- **Mohair:** Coarse, lustrous, excellent for dyed textiles.
- **Cashmere:** Very fine, soft, expensive, limited yield.
- **Best for:** Luxury garments, dyed textiles (mohair).

### Plant Fibers

**Flax (linen):**
- **Properties:** Cool, strong, low elasticity, lustrous, poor insulation.
- **Staple length:** 12–36 inches.
- **Best for:** Summer clothing, tablecloths, fine linens.
- **Yield:** 20–50 pounds per acre (depends on retting and combing efficiency).

**Hemp:**
- **Properties:** Very strong, coarse, similar to flax but even stronger.
- **Staple length:** 10–30 inches.
- **Best for:** Durable textiles (work clothes), rope, canvas.
- **Yield:** 30–70 pounds per acre.

**Cotton:**
- **Properties:** Soft, low elasticity, not water-resistant, takes dye readily.
- **Staple length:** 1–2 inches.
- **Best for:** Fine summer clothing, soft blankets.
- **Yield:** Limited in temperate climates; significant in warm regions.

**Nettle:**
- **Properties:** Similar to flax, less well-known, produces very strong yarn.
- **Staple length:** 4–8 inches.
- **Best for:** Durable fine textiles.
- **Yield:** Abundant as a wild or semi-cultivated plant.

## Yarn Weight Standards and Gauge

Yarn weight describes thickness and is standardized so spinners and weavers can exchange knowledge.

### Standard Yarn Weights

| Weight | Ply | Yards per Pound | Recommended Use |
|--------|-----|---|---|
| Lace | 0-1 | 4000+ | Fine shawls, intricate lace |
| Fingering | 1-2 | 2000-3500 | Fine socks, stockings |
| Sport | 2-3 | 1500-2000 | Fine garments, decorative textiles |
| Worsted | 4-5 | 800-1500 | Blankets, heavy garments |
| Chunky | 8-12 | 300-800 | Very heavy blankets, rugs |
| Roving | 16+ | <300 | Felting, heavy rugs (not spun tightly) |

### Measuring Yarn Weight

1. Wind yarn into a 4-inch loop.
2. Weigh the loop on a postal scale or balance scale.
3. Calculate: (weight in ounces × 16 × loop diameter / 4) = yards per pound approximately.
4. More accurately, wind yarn into a 100-yard skein, weigh it, and extrapolate.

:::tip
Keep a yarn sample reference book: wind small samples of each yarn weight onto cards and label with weight, fiber, ply, and intended use. This helps you quickly identify yarn weight when assessing old textiles or planning new projects.
:::
