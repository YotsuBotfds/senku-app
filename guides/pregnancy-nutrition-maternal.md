**Important Medical Note:** This guide is for pregnancy nutrition in austere/resource-limited settings. Pregnant women should seek professional prenatal care when available. Many complications discussed here are manageable with medical supervision but can be life-threatening without it. When professional care is unavailable, this guide provides evidence-based nutrition and self-monitoring strategies.
:::

<section id="overview">

## Overview: Pregnancy Nutrition in Austere Settings

Maternal malnutrition is a leading cause of preventable maternal and infant mortality in resource-limited settings. Pregnancy creates **dramatic nutritional demands**—the fetus and placenta become major nutrient sinks, drawing minerals, vitamins, and energy from maternal reserves. When food is scarce, maternal nutrition suffers first.

This guide focuses on:
1. **Nutritional requirements** by trimester
2. **Critical micronutrients** and their dietary sources
3. **Prevention and management of anemia, preeclampsia, gestational diabetes**
4. **Food safety** (including foraged foods)
5. **Breastfeeding nutrition** and postpartum recovery
6. **Strategies for pregnancy during famine/scarcity**
7. **Warning signs requiring emergency care**

</section>

<section id="caloric-requirements">

## Caloric & Macronutrient Requirements

Pregnancy increases metabolic demands. Energy needs increase **progressively** across the three trimesters, with minimal increase in the first trimester and substantial increases in the second and third.

<div class="calorie-chart">

### Daily Caloric Additions (Beyond Baseline)

| Trimester | Week Range | Additional Calories/Day | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| **First Trimester** | 1–13 | 0 | Minimal fetal growth; early pregnancy can decrease appetite |
| **Second Trimester** | 14–26 | +340 kcal/day | Rapid fetal growth; increased blood volume & placental development |
| **Third Trimester** | 27–40 | +452 kcal/day | Maximum fetal growth; maternal fat stores accumulating |

**In austere settings:** If baseline caloric intake is marginal, pregnant women may need to focus on **nutrient density** (calories per unit volume) rather than absolute volume. Small frequent meals of energy-dense foods (nuts, seeds, oils, legumes) are preferable to large meals of low-calorie bulk foods.

### Protein Requirements

| Group | Daily Requirement | Additional for Pregnancy |
|---|---|---|
| **Non-pregnant adult** | 0.8 g/kg body weight | — |
| **Pregnant woman** | 1.1 g/kg body weight | **+25 g/day** (or 71 g/day total) |
| **Breastfeeding woman** | 1.3 g/kg body weight | +25 g/day above pregnancy |

**Food sources:** Meat, fish, eggs, dairy, legumes (beans, lentils, peas), nuts, seeds. Plant-based proteins should be varied to ensure complete amino acid profile (combine grains + legumes, e.g., rice + beans).

</div>

</section>

<section id="critical-micronutrients">

## Critical Micronutrients in Pregnancy

### Folate (Vitamin B9)

**Why critical:** Folate deficiency in pregnancy → neural tube defects (spina bifida, anencephaly), cleft palate, spontaneous abortion

**Requirements:**
- Pre-pregnancy: 400 mcg/day
- Pregnancy: **600 mcg/day** (+200 mcg)
- Breastfeeding: 500 mcg/day

**High-risk groups:**
- Multiple pregnancies (twins, triplets)
- Anticonvulsant use
- Diabetes (poorly controlled)
- History of neural tube defect (increase to 4000 mcg/day if available)

**Food sources:**
<div class="food-sources">
**Best:** Leafy greens (spinach, kale, lettuce), asparagus, broccoli, Brussels sprouts, legumes (lentils, chickpeas)
**Moderate:** Whole grains, eggs, beets, nuts, seeds
**Caution:** Cooking destroys 50–90% of folate; prefer raw or lightly steamed

If supplements unavailable, emphasize fresh leafy greens daily. In austere settings without supplement access, consuming handful of raw leafy greens daily provides ~200–300 mcg.
</div>

### Iron

**Why critical:** Pregnancy increases blood volume 50%; iron requirements increase due to expanded plasma. Iron deficiency anemia → fatigue, preterm labor, low birth weight, maternal death (hemorrhage)

**Requirements:**
- Pre-pregnancy: 18 mg/day
- Pregnancy: **27 mg/day** (+9 mg)
- Breastfeeding: 9 mg/day (lactational amenorrhea reduces losses)

**Clinical assessment:**
- **Hgb <11 g/dL in first/third trimester** = anemia (diagnose; treat)
- **Hgb <10.5 g/dL in second trimester** = anemia (most hemodilution occurs mid-pregnancy)
- **Iron deficiency suspected if:** Anemia + low ferritin or low serum iron (if lab available)

**Treatment of iron deficiency anemia:**

| Intervention | Dosing | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Iron supplement** | 27–65 mg elemental iron daily (from ferrous salt: sulfate, gluconate, fumarate) | Raises Hgb by 1 g/dL every 3–4 weeks with compliance |
| **Dietary iron (animal source)** | Meat, poultry, fish (heme iron; 15–35% absorption) | Modest increase; poor in austere settings with limited meat |
| **Dietary iron (plant source)** | Legumes, fortified grains, dried fruit (non-heme iron; 2–20% absorption) | Better with vitamin C co-administration |
| **Vitamin C co-administration** | Add citrus, tomato, or fermented vegetables at each meal | Enhances non-heme iron absorption 3–4 fold |

<div class="food-sources">
**High-iron foods:**
- **Animal:** Meat (beef, liver), poultry, fish, shellfish
- **Plant:** Legumes (lentils, beans, peas), whole grains, dark leafy greens, nuts, seeds, dried fruit

**Maximize absorption:** Pair iron-rich meals with vitamin C (orange juice, tomato, fermented cabbage) and avoid coffee/tea (tannins inhibit absorption) at the same meal.
</div>

### Calcium

**Why critical:** Fetus requires 200–300 mg calcium/day for bone development. If maternal intake inadequate, calcium mobilized from maternal skeleton → osteoporosis risk post-pregnancy; also linked to preeclampsia

**Requirements:**
- Adult (all ages): 1000 mg/day
- Pregnancy: **1000 mg/day** (same as non-pregnant; needs do NOT increase despite fetal demand)
- Breastfeeding: 1000 mg/day

**Note:** Pregnancy increases intestinal calcium absorption efficiency (~60% vs. 30% non-pregnant), partially compensating for dietary inadequacy.

<div class="food-sources">
**Best:** Dairy (milk, yogurt, cheese), leafy greens (kale, collards, bok choy), legumes, nuts (almonds, sesame seeds), fortified grains

**Challenge in austere settings:** Dairy access is limited. Emphasize leafy greens, legumes, and foraged greens (see foraging section).

**Calcium intake assessment:** Austere diet with ~2 cups dairy + leafy greens + legumes daily provides ~800–1000 mg calcium.
</div>

### Iodine

**Why critical:** Iodine required for thyroid hormone synthesis. Deficiency → hypothyroidism, cretinism (severe intellectual disability + hearing loss in fetus), spontaneous abortion, stillbirth

**Requirements:**
- Pre-pregnancy: 150 mcg/day
- Pregnancy: **220 mcg/day** (+70 mcg)
- Breastfeeding: 290 mcg/day (+140 mcg; iodine concentrated in breast milk)

<div class="food-sources">
**Best:** Iodized salt (1 teaspoon provides ~150 mcg), seaweed (variable; some species very high risk of excess)
**Good:** Dairy (iodine in cow feed), eggs, seafood
**Poor:** Inland regions with iodine-depleted soil (most vegetables/grains low iodine unless soil supplemented)

**Challenge in austere settings:** Iodized salt access may be limited. If salt unavailable or non-iodized, pregnant women at risk of deficiency. Seaweed is risky (some species accumulate excess iodine → thyroiditis; others very low). Dairy and eggs (if available) are safer sources.
</div>

### Vitamin A

**Why critical:** Vitamin A essential for fetal eye development, immune function, and bone development. **Excess is teratogenic** (>10,000 IU/day increases birth defects); deficiency impairs fetal development

**Requirements:**
- Pre-pregnancy: 700 mcg/day (RDA)
- Pregnancy: **770 mcg/day** (+70 mcg)
- Breastfeeding: 1300 mcg/day (high requirement for milk production)

**Safe upper limit:** 3000 mcg/day (10,000 IU/day); avoid excess supplementation

<div class="food-sources">
**Best (beta-carotene → vitamin A):** Orange/yellow vegetables (carrots, sweet potato, squash, pumpkin), dark leafy greens, mango, papaya
**Animal sources (preformed):** Liver, eggs, dairy

**Caution:** Liver (especially beef/game liver) is very high in preformed vitamin A. Small portions safe, but regular liver consumption risks excess.

**Foraged sources:** Many wild greens (dandelion, amaranth, purslane) are excellent sources.
</div>

</section>

<section id="other-micronutrients">

## Other Important Micronutrients

### Zinc

**Requirements:** Pregnancy +3 mg/day (total 11 mg/day)
**Role:** Immune function, fetal growth, labor progression
**Sources:** Meat, shellfish, legumes, nuts, seeds, whole grains
**Deficiency symptoms:** Growth restriction, preterm birth, weak immune response

### Vitamin D

**Requirements:** 600 IU/day (pre-pregnancy and pregnancy same); breastfeeding same
**Role:** Calcium absorption, immune function, fetal bone development
**Challenge in austere settings:** Limited supplementation; sunlight exposure (15–30 min/day direct sun) provides endogenous synthesis if skin type permits
**Sources:** Fatty fish (salmon, mackerel), egg yolks, fortified dairy, sunlight exposure

### Vitamin B12

**Requirements:** Pregnancy +0.2 mcg/day (total 2.6 mcg/day)
**Role:** DNA synthesis, nerve function, fetal neural development
**Risk group:** Vegans/strict vegetarians (no animal sources)
**Sources:** Meat, poultry, fish, dairy, eggs, fermented foods (tempeh, miso contain small amounts)

### Vitamin C

**Requirements:** Pregnancy +10 mg/day (total 85 mg/day)
**Role:** Collagen synthesis, immune function, iron absorption
**Sources:** Citrus, tomatoes, peppers, leafy greens, berries

</section>

<section id="trimester-specific">

## Trimester-Specific Considerations

<div class="trimester-box">

### First Trimester (Weeks 1–13)

**Nutritional focus:**
- **Folate:** Critical for neural tube closure (closes by week 4 — before many women know pregnant). Ensure adequate intake/supplementation immediately upon pregnancy diagnosis
- **Nausea management:** Morning sickness affects 50–80% of pregnancies. Frequent small meals, ginger (if available), B6 supplementation (if available) may help
- **Continued ovulation:** No caloric increase needed yet
- **Food aversions:** Many women develop aversions to meat, dairy, or certain foods. Ensure varied diet to meet requirements despite aversions
- **Alcohol, tobacco, teratogens:** Strict avoidance; critical for organ development

</div>

<div class="trimester-box">

### Second Trimester (Weeks 14–26)

**Nutritional focus:**
- **Caloric increase:** +340 kcal/day now relevant (rapid fetal growth)
- **Iron:** Anemia screening; supplementation if Hgb <11 g/dL. This is the window where iron supplementation is most effective
- **Calcium:** Assess intake; ensure adequate daily
- **Weight gain:** Expect ~12–14 lbs (5–6 kg) by end of this trimester (total expected 25–35 lbs for normal BMI pregnancy)
- **Appetite improvement:** Morning sickness usually resolves; appetite increases — use to improve nutrition

</div>

<div class="trimester-box">

### Third Trimester (Weeks 27–40)

**Nutritional focus:**
- **Caloric increase:** +452 kcal/day (continued fetal growth + maternal fat storage for lactation)
- **Protein:** Ensure 71 g/day; needed for fetal growth, placental development, uterine expansion
- **Iron:** Continue supplementation if anemia; increasing energy demands increase hemorrhage risk at delivery
- **Calcium:** Critical for fetal bone mineralization (third trimester is major bone deposition)
- **Frequent small meals:** GI space decreases as uterus enlarges; frequent meals aid digestion and caloric intake
- **Fluid intake:** Increased thirst normal; maintain hydration (dehydration increases preterm labor risk)

</div>

</section>

<section id="anemia-prevention">

## Anemia Prevention & Management

Iron deficiency anemia affects 15–25% of pregnant women globally; higher in low-income settings.

### Anemia Screening & Diagnosis

**When to check (if lab available):**
- First prenatal visit: Baseline hemoglobin
- 24–28 weeks: Repeat (to identify new anemia and start treatment)
- 32–36 weeks: If previously anemic or on supplements

**Interpretation:**
- **Hgb >11 g/dL (1st/3rd trimester) or >10.5 g/dL (2nd trimester):** Normal
- **Hgb 7–11 g/dL:** Mild anemia; supplement
- **Hgb 5–7 g/dL:** Moderate anemia; supplement + investigation for other causes
- **Hgb <5 g/dL:** Severe anemia; risk of heart failure + preterm labor; urgent management needed

### Treatment Protocol

**Iron supplementation:**
- **Standard:** 27–65 mg elemental iron daily as ferrous salt (ferrous sulfate 325 mg = 65 mg elemental iron; ferrous gluconate 325 mg = 36 mg elemental iron)
- **Absorption:** Takes on empty stomach (though GI upset common); if intolerant, take with food (50% reduction in absorption but better compliance)
- **Duration:** Until Hgb normalizes + continue through postpartum (at least 3 months post-delivery to replenish stores)
- **Side effects:** GI upset, constipation, dark stools (normal); nausea may occur (take with small snack)

**Dietary optimization:**
- Consume iron-rich foods at every meal
- Pair with vitamin C source (citrus, tomato)
- Avoid coffee/tea with meals (inhibit absorption)

### Red Flags Requiring Evaluation

- Hemoglobin does not increase after 4 weeks of iron supplementation (consider absorption problem, ongoing bleeding, or non-iron cause such as vitamin B12/folate deficiency)
- Severe anemia (Hgb <7 g/dL) at any time — increased preterm labor and hemorrhage risk
- Palpitations, shortness of breath, severe fatigue (signs of cardiac stress from anemia)

</section>

<section id="preeclampsia">

## Preeclampsia: Prevention & Warning Signs

Preeclampsia (high blood pressure + proteinuria in pregnancy) affects 3–7% of pregnancies and is a leading cause of maternal death in low-resource settings.

### Risk Factors

- First pregnancy
- Age >35 or <18 years
- Obesity (BMI >30)
- Chronic hypertension
- Diabetes (pre-pregnancy or gestational)
- Previous preeclampsia
- Multiple pregnancy (twins, triplets)

### Nutritional Prevention: Calcium & Protein

**Calcium role:** Adequate calcium intake (1000 mg/day) reduces preeclampsia risk by ~35%. Mechanism unclear but appears calcium-dependent.

**Protein role:** Adequate protein (71 g/day) reduces preeclampsia risk. Protein deficiency → maternal vascular damage → hypertension.

**Practical approach:** Ensure minimum 1000 mg calcium + 71 g protein daily. Dairy, leafy greens, legumes, and meat/fish are key.

### Warning Signs of Preeclampsia

<div class="warning-signs">

**Seek emergency care immediately if you experience:**
- **Severe headache** (especially frontal or posterior)
- **Visual changes** (blurred vision, flashing lights, scotomata)
- **Upper abdominal pain** (RUQ or epigastric; may indicate hepatic edema/rupture)
- **Persistent nausea/vomiting** (different from morning sickness)
- **Sudden severe swelling** (face, hands, generalized; not typical pregnancy swelling)
- **Seizure** (eclampsia — medical emergency)
- **Shortness of breath, chest pain** (pulmonary edema)

**Blood pressure:** If available to check, systolic ≥160 or diastolic ≥110 at any time = severe hypertension; warrants evaluation

</div>

### Management in Austere Settings

**Severe preeclampsia without access to hospital:**
- Bed rest (reduces demands on cardiovascular system)
- High-protein, adequate-calcium diet
- Salt restriction (traditional advice; current evidence is less clear, but moderate intake reasonable)
- Hydration (dehydration worsens preeclampsia)
- **Close monitoring for seizures; antihypertensive medication if available (hydralazine, nifedipine)**
- **If seizure or signs of eclampsia: magnesium sulfate 4 g IV + 1 g/hr IM if available; arrange emergency evacuation**

</section>

<section id="gestational-diabetes">

## Gestational Diabetes: Dietary Management

Gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM) affects 2–10% of pregnancies. Increases risk of large-for-gestational-age babies, birth injuries, and neonatal hypoglycemia.

### Risk Factors for GDM

- Obesity (BMI >30)
- Age >25 years
- Family history of diabetes
- Previous GDM or delivery of large baby (>9 lbs)
- Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)

### Dietary Management of GDM

**General approach:** Frequent small meals (5–6/day); complex carbohydrates with protein/fat; avoid simple sugars

| Meal Type | Carbohydrate (g) | Protein (g) | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Breakfast** | 30 | 8–12 | Oatmeal (½ cup) + egg + whole wheat toast |
| **Morning snack** | 15 | 6–8 | Yogurt + nuts OR cheese + apple |
| **Lunch** | 30–45 | 15–20 | Whole grain bread + meat + vegetables |
| **Afternoon snack** | 15 | 6–8 | Legume soup OR nut butter + whole grain |
| **Dinner** | 45–60 | 20–25 | Rice + beans + meat + vegetables |
| **Evening snack** | 15 | 6–8 | Milk + whole grain crackers |

**Carbohydrate quality:** Emphasize high-fiber, low-glycemic carbohydrates:
- **Best:** Legumes, whole grains, non-starchy vegetables, fruits with skin
- **Avoid:** White bread, white rice, sugary beverages, pastries, candy

**Fasting glucose:** If able to check (using glucometer), target <95 mg/dL fasting; 2-hour post-meal <120 mg/dL

**If diet alone insufficient:** Insulin therapy may be needed (austere settings: insulin is critical medication; prioritize access)

</section>

<section id="food-safety">

## Food Safety in Pregnancy

Pregnancy impairs immune function, increasing susceptibility to foodborne infection. Some pathogens pose specific fetal risk.

### Pathogens to Avoid

| Pathogen | Foods | Fetal Risk |
|---|---|---|
| **Listeria monocytogenes** | Unpasteurized dairy, soft cheeses (feta, brie), cold deli meats, pâté | Miscarriage, preterm labor, neonatal sepsis (2nd/3rd trimester) |
| **Toxoplasma gondii** | Undercooked meat, cat feces on vegetables/soil | Congenital toxoplasmosis; blindness, intellectual disability if untreated |
| **Salmonella** | Raw/undercooked eggs, poultry, unpasteurized milk | Gastroenteritis (usually not teratogenic unless severe sepsis) |
| **E. coli O157:H7** | Undercooked ground beef | Severe diarrhea; hemolytic uremic syndrome (rare) |

### Food Preparation Safety

- **Cook meat to safe internal temperature:** Beef/pork 160°F (71°C), poultry 165°F (74°C), fish 145°F (63°C)
- **Avoid:** Raw/undercooked eggs (including sunny-side-up), raw/undercooked meat
- **Dairy:** Use pasteurized milk/cheese; avoid unpasteurized/raw dairy
- **Deli meats:** If consumed, heat to steaming hot (71°C) to kill Listeria
- **Fruits/vegetables:** Wash thoroughly; peel vegetables when possible
- **Kitchen hygiene:** Wash hands, cutting boards, utensils with soap/water after contact with raw meat

</section>

<section id="foraging">

## Foraging & Wild Food Safety in Pregnancy

In austere settings, foraged foods provide critical nutrition. However, pregnant women must be cautious about toxins and contaminants.

### Safe Foraged Foods for Pregnancy

<div class="food-sources">

**Excellent (nutritious, safe):**
- **Leafy greens:** Amaranth, purslane, dandelion greens, chickweed, lamb's quarters (all high in iron, calcium, folate)
- **Root vegetables:** Wild onions, cattail tubers, groundnuts (high in carbohydrates, minerals)
- **Legumes:** Wild beans, lentils, peas (high in protein, iron, folate)
- **Nuts/seeds:** Acorns (after leaching), walnuts, pine nuts, sunflower seeds (high fat, protein)

**Good (safe with proper preparation):**
- **Mushrooms:** Only species you can identify with 100% certainty as non-toxic; avoid mushrooms if any doubt
- **Berries:** Only species you identify as safe; avoid unidentified berries
- **Wild grains:** Rye, barley, wheat berries (safe if clean and free of mold)

</div>

### Foraged Foods to AVOID in Pregnancy

- **Plants with alkaloids:** Nightshades (if unripe; cooked ripe tomatoes/peppers safe), hemlock (parsnip-like; highly toxic)
- **Heavily contaminated areas:** Forage away from roads (exhaust pollution), industrial sites, sewage runoff
- **Moldy foods:** Any grain, nut, or legume with mold or musty smell (aflatoxins are teratogenic)
- **Unidentified mushrooms:** Risk of hepatotoxins, neurotoxins far outweighs benefit
- **Plants high in oxalates (raw):** Rhubarb leaves (not stalks), raw spinach in excess (cooking reduces oxalates)

### Preparation for Safety

- **Cook when possible:** Heating destroys many plant toxins and reduces pathogenic bacteria
- **Soak/leach:** Some traditional foods require soaking (e.g., acorns soaked 12–24 hrs to remove tannins)
- **Dry properly:** Wet-stored grains/legumes grow mold; air-dry thoroughly or sun-dry

</section>

### Postpartum Nutritional Recovery

<div class="postpartum-grid">

<div class="postpartum-item">
**Iron Replenishment**
- Continue iron supplementation 3–6 months post-delivery
- Replenish stores lost to pregnancy + delivery blood loss
- Breastfeeding amenorrhea (lack of periods) reduces ongoing iron losses
- Continue dietary iron sources
</div>

<div class="postpartum-item">
**Protein & Healing**
- Protein critical for wound healing (perineal tear/episiotomy)
- Ensure 71 g/day throughout postpartum period
- Protein aids collagen deposition and tissue repair
- Allow 4–6 weeks for complete perineal healing
</div>

<div class="postpartum-item">
**Caloric Needs**
- Breastfeeding increases caloric needs +500 kcal/day
- Weight loss: ~1–2 lbs/month during exclusive breastfeeding (not aggressive dieting; allow gradual loss)
- Severe caloric restriction reduces milk supply
- Ensure adequate caloric intake to maintain milk quality/quantity
</div>

<div class="postpartum-item">
**Fluids & Hydration**
- Lochia (postpartum bleeding) increases fluid losses
- Breastfeeding increases thirst
- Drink at least 3 L water daily
- Herbal teas (red raspberry leaf, nettle) traditional in some cultures for postpartum recovery
</div>

</div>

### Medications & Breastfeeding

Most common medications are safe while breastfeeding, including:
- **Antibiotics:** Penicillins, cephalosporins, macrolides (avoid some fluoroquinolones)
- **Pain relief:** Acetaminophen, ibuprofen (short-term)
- **Blood pressure:** ACE inhibitors, labetalol
- **Anti-seizure:** Phenytoin, carbamazepine

**Avoid:** Radioactive contrast agents, chemotherapy, some psychiatric medications. When in doubt, seek specialist advice if available.

<section id="pregnancy-scarcity">

## Pregnancy Nutrition During Famine & Scarcity

In famine settings, pregnant women face competing demands: fetal nutrition vs. maternal survival.

### Nutritional Triage in Scarcity

**Priority nutrient distribution (if total calories insufficient):**
1. **Micronutrient density first:** Leafy greens, legumes, seeds — calorie-efficient protein + minerals
2. **Folate & iron:** Prevent neural tube defects + anemia (critical; no substitute)
3. **Protein:** Fetal development requires protein; maternal protein loss threatens lactation
4. **Energy-dense foods:** Oils, nuts, seeds (small volume, high calories)

**Rationing strategy:**
- Pregnant women should receive priority access to vegetables, legumes, fats
- Avoid exclusive reliance on grain/starch (low micronutrient density)
- If supplements available (folate, iron, multivitamin): pregnant women are highest priority

### Fetal Adaptation to Maternal Scarcity

Evidence shows the fetus is remarkably resilient to maternal undernutrition in mid-pregnancy; however:
- **First trimester:** Organ development; severe malnutrition → neural tube defects, organ malformations
- **Third trimester:** Fetal growth + maternal fat storage for lactation; severe restriction → small-for-gestational-age, preterm birth

### Postpartum Lactation During Scarcity

Lactation is energetically expensive (500 kcal/day). Severely malnourished mothers produce adequate milk only briefly; supply drops after 6–12 weeks. Strategies:

- **Early, frequent breastfeeding:** Stimulates milk production; continue even if milk supply seems low
- **Maternal nutrition support:** Nutritious meals + caloric supplements for lactating mother reduce child mortality 12–23%
- **Complementary feeding at 6 months:** Begin appropriate foods (mashed vegetables, legumes, grains) even if maternal milk continues
- **Partial breastfeeding:** If maternal nutrition fails, combine breast milk with appropriate complementary foods rather than weaning completely

</section>

<section id="emergency-signs">

## Emergency Warning Signs

Seek immediate medical care for:

- **Severe abdominal pain** with vaginal bleeding (ectopic pregnancy, placental abruption)
- **Uncontrolled hypertension** (>160 systolic, >110 diastolic) with headache/visual changes (preeclampsia)
- **Vaginal hemorrhage soaking >1 pad per hour** (placenta previa, abruption, other complications)
- **Severe persistent vomiting** preventing all food/fluid intake (hyperemesis; risk of electrolyte imbalance, dehydration)
- **Sudden loss of fetal movement** (fetal distress; may precede stillbirth)
- **Fever >38.5°C (101.3°F) with abdominal pain** (chorioamnionitis or other intrauterine infection)
- **Signs of preeclampsia:** Severe headache, visual changes, upper abdominal pain, seizure
- **Syncope or severe dizziness** (may indicate maternal blood loss, hypoglycemia, severe anemia)
