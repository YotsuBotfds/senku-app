## Overview

Post-collapse salvage operations often target abandoned or damaged buildings. Before removing materials or occupying a structure, a thorough structural and contamination assessment is critical. This guide provides step-by-step protocols to determine if a building is salvageable, safe to enter, and free from hazards.

Assessment decisions directly impact team safety and salvage yield. Rushing this process has caused injuries, collapses, and long-term health effects from exposure.

:::info-box
**Step 1 — Is this building safe to salvage? Quick triage check:**

- Visible wall lean >5°, large collapse area, or roof sagging from outside? → **Mark UNSAFE — do not proceed.**
- Gas smell or electrical hazard (wet panels, live wires)? → **Mark UNSAFE — utility disconnect required first.**
- Extensive mold (>30% of surfaces) or confirmed asbestos? → **Mark HIGH RISK — restrict access, consult specialists.**
- Minor cracks and water stains, but no imminent failure signs? → **Proceed with CAUTION — complete full assessment below.**
- No exterior red flags and you have PPE plus a 3+ person team? → **Proceed to exterior assessment below.**

For safe entry protocols (buddy system, exit planning, emergency extraction), see [Structural Safety for Building Entry](../structural-safety-building-entry.html). For what materials to grab first before they decay, see [Time-Decay Priority Framework](../salvage-time-decay-priorities.html).
:::

## Pre-Assessment Safety Protocol

### Team Composition

Minimum recommended team for major assessment:
- **Team lead:** Experienced in construction or salvage (makes decision to continue/abort)
- **Structural observer:** Identifies load-bearing elements and failure signs
- **Hazard specialist:** Trained in asbestos, mold, chemical detection
- **Safety officer:** Monitors team health, enforces protocols, manages evacuation
- **Assistant:** Carries equipment, documents findings, establishes perimeter

### Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

Before entering any structure:
- **Hard hat:** Helmet with chin strap (falling debris)
- **Respirator or N95 mask:** Minimum (upgrade to cartridge type for chemical/mold concerns)
- **Work gloves:** Leather or nitrile (handling sharp/contaminated materials)
- **Safety glasses:** Impact-resistant (dust, splinters)
- **High-visibility vest:** Allows team location tracking
- **Steel-toed boots:** Protect feet from sharp metal, nails
- **Long sleeves and pants:** Minimize skin contact with contamination

:::warning
Respirators require proper fit-testing and training. Do not rely on surgical masks for unknown airborne hazards. If chemical contamination is suspected, cartridge-based respirators with appropriate filters are necessary.
:::

### Equipment Checklist

- Multimeter (electrical hazard detection)
- pH test strips or water testing kit (chemical contamination)
- Moisture meter (mold assessment)
- Flashlight with extra batteries
- Camera or documentation tablet
- Tape measure and level
- Sketch paper and pen
- Gas detector (if natural gas lines present)
- Water testing kit (if water damage suspected)
- Notebook for findings

## Exterior Assessment

### Approach & Perimeter

1. **Establish 5 m safety perimeter** around building
2. **Look for signs of imminent collapse:**
   - Visible wall lean >5 degrees
   - Large cracks visible from distance
   - Roof sagging or partially collapsed sections
3. **Check for environmental hazards nearby:**
   - Chemical storage or manufacturing facilities
   - Electrical transformers (PCB leaks)
   - Buried tanks (fuel, sewage)
4. **If structural failure appears imminent:** Do not approach closer. Mark as unsafe. Return to assess only if structure stabilizes or can be shored.

### Roof Assessment (from Ground)

- **Visual inspection:** Look for collapsed sections, missing roofing material, sagging lines
- **Gutter alignment:** Sagging gutters indicate supporting beam failure
- **Chimney condition:** Leaning chimneys indicate settling or foundation movement
- **Visible cracks:** Horizontal cracks suggest structural failure; vertical cracks are usually less urgent

### Foundation & Base

- **Crack pattern:** Diagonal cracks (45°) indicate structural settling; horizontal cracks indicate lateral pressure
- **Exterior walls:** Look for bowing or bulging (indicates internal pressure or foundation failure)
- **Water damage:** Staining, mineral deposits, or mold indicate moisture infiltration history

## Interior Structural Assessment

### Loading the Building Safely

Enter only if exterior assessment shows no imminent collapse risk:

1. **Establish entry protocol:** Only team members with hard hats and PPE enter
2. **Main door:** Enter through primary entrance when possible (load-bearing frame is well-designed here)
3. **Ground floor first:** Assess main floor before considering upper levels
4. **Stay near walls initially:** Walls are typically reinforced; interior space may be weakened
5. **Mark safe zones:** Place markers or tape on floor showing verified safe walking paths

### Load-Bearing Structure Identification

Understanding which elements carry weight is crucial for salvage planning:

**Load-bearing walls** (primary supports):
- Usually aligned vertically from foundation to roof
- Thicker than non-load-bearing walls (often >20 cm)
- Located at building perimeter or central lines
- Contain concentrated cracks or settling patterns
- Removing these causes catastrophic collapse

**Non-load-bearing walls** (interior partition walls):
- Thinner (often <10 cm)
- Do not span full ceiling height
- Less reinforced
- Can usually be removed safely (after verification by structural assessment)

**Columns and posts:**
- Single vertical supports carrying roof/floor loads
- Usually reinforced (metal or heavy timber)
- Damage to columns is critical (risk of localized collapse)

**Beams:**
- Horizontal members spanning between walls/columns
- Sagging indicates overload or material degradation
- Visible cracking or severe rust on steel beams indicates failure risk

**Floor joists:**
- Smaller horizontal members supporting flooring
- If floor feels springy or slopes unevenly, joists are failing
- Wet or rotted joists signal wood degradation

### Settling & Foundation Failure Signs

:::info-box
Settling is common in older buildings. Minor settling with stable cracks is manageable; active settling is dangerous.
:::

**Indicators of problematic settling:**

- **Horizontal cracks in concrete foundation:** >3 mm width indicates significant pressure
- **Vertical cracks in basement walls:** Bowing indicates lateral pressure (foundation may be failing)
- **Tilting walls:** Use a level on walls; >5 degree tilt is concerning
- **Gaping cracks between structure and adjacent buildings:** Indicates differential settling (unequal support)
- **Doors/windows that won't close:** Indicates frame distortion from settling

**Check for active settling:**
- Install a small pointer across a major crack
- Return after 1 week
- If pointer has moved, settling is ongoing—avoid upper levels

## Electrical & Gas Hazards

### Electrical Assessment

:::danger
Do not assume electrical systems are de-energized. Utility disconnection may not be complete. Treat all electrical systems as live unless verified safe.
:::

**Visual inspection:**
- **Visible electrical panels:** Note their location (in basement, near entrance)
- **Wiring condition:** Cloth-wrapped wiring (pre-1950s) degrades and may short circuit
- **Water damage to electrical:** If electrical panels or wiring are wet, electrical hazard is present
- **Scorched marks or melted insulation:** Indicates past overheating or short circuits

**Safety protocol:**
1. Use a **multimeter** to test electrical outlet safety (if present)
2. If any outlets show voltage, utility may still be connected—do not proceed
3. Assume metal pipes, radiators, and plumbing fixtures may be electrically live if building is connected to grid
4. If utility disconnect is confirmed: Switch off main breaker, verify with multimeter, lock out breaker

### Natural Gas & Propane Systems

**Identify gas connections:**
- **Gas meter location:** Usually external (side of building)
- **Gas appliances:** Stoves, heaters, water heaters (may retain fuel in lines)
- **Gas smell:** If detectable, evacuate immediately—do not use electrical devices or create sparks

**Safety protocol:**
1. Use **gas detection kit** (if available) to test for gas leaks
2. If smell detected but not confirmed as gas, evacuate and return with professional assessment
3. Assume all gas lines are pressurized unless physically cut and drained

:::warning
Do not attempt to disconnect gas lines yourself. Require trained utility technician or certified salvage professional to isolate gas systems.
:::

## Contamination Assessment

### Mold & Moisture

**Visual mold assessment:**
- Color: Black, green, white, or orange fuzzy growth (various mold types)
- Location: Most common in basements, bathrooms, areas with water intrusion
- Extent: Estimate percentage of wall/surface affected

**Moisture meter testing:**
- Wood moisture >20% supports mold growth
- Concrete moisture >3% indicates water infiltration
- If areas consistently read >15%, mold remediation is likely needed

**Health risk factors:**
- Large mold areas (>1 m²) require professional removal
- Immunocompromised team members should avoid moldy areas
- Small isolated mold can be cleaned (100:1 water:bleach solution)

:::info-box
Mold assessment requires caution. If black mold (Stachybotrys) is suspected, professional testing is recommended. Do not assume all mold is harmless.
:::

### Asbestos & Lead Identification

**Asbestos** (pre-1980 buildings primarily):
- Often found in: Pipe insulation, floor tiles, ceiling tiles, roofing, siding, adhesives
- Visual: Fibrous, white/gray material; often crumbly or loose
- **Do not disturb or sample—assume it is asbestos**
- If asbestos is present or suspected, mark area as off-limits and document location

**Lead paint** (pre-1978 buildings):
- Visual: Paint layers on walls, trim, doors (color doesn't indicate lead)
- Risk: Lead dust when sanding or scraping paint
- **Do not sand or scrape suspected lead paint**
- If salvaging painted materials, assume lead and use wet-down procedures to prevent dust

**Identification protocol:**
1. Estimate building age (construction date if visible on foundation or records)
2. If pre-1980: Assume asbestos in insulation, tiles, roofing
3. If pre-1978: Assume lead paint on all interior/exterior surfaces
4. Mark contaminated areas clearly (tape, signs, photographer documentation)
5. If extensive asbestos or lead: Consider area unsalvageable (removal costs too high)

### Chemical Contamination

**Potential chemical sources:**
- Underground storage tanks (fuel, solvents) on property
- Manufacturing facilities (cleaning solvents, heavy metals)
- Labs or workshops (pesticides, acids, cyanides)
- Art/printing studios (toluene, xylene, heavy metal inks)

**Visual clues:**
- Staining on floor or walls (distinctive colors: orange, brown, white mineral deposits)
- Distinctive odors (sweet, pungent, chemical smell)
- Corroded metal containers or drums
- Labeled storage areas (check for hazard symbols)

**Testing:**
- **pH strips:** Test water or soil for extreme acidity/alkalinity (indicates chemical contamination)
- **Visual inspection:** Look for deteriorated containers, labels, spill sites
- **If suspected:** Document location, do not sample, consult professional hazmat team before proceeding

## Roof & Upper Floor Assessment

### Roof Condition from Below

1. **Look for water staining** on ceiling: Brown stains indicate past water infiltration
2. **Check for sagging:** Use a level; ceiling should be straight, not wavy
3. **Estimate missing roof material:** Large areas of exposed rafters indicate roof failure
4. **Inspect attic access:** If safe to check, look at structural members and insulation condition

### Upper Floor Assessment

Only assess upper floors if ground floor is confirmed structurally sound:

1. **Test floor with weight:** Bounce gently—excessive give indicates joist failure
2. **Look for water staining:** Indicates potential rot in wooden joists
3. **Check for settlement:** Use level on floor; slopes >1/4 inch per 3 meters indicate settling
4. **Inspect penetrations:** Where pipes/vents go through floor, check for rot around openings

:::warning
If upper floors show significant spring or sag, assume joist failure is present. Restrict access and do not load upper floors during salvage operations.
:::

## Team Protocols & Documentation

### Daily Safety Briefing

Before entering building each day:
1. Review findings from previous assessment
2. Assign roles (lead, structural observer, hazard specialist, safety officer)
3. Identify hazard zones and no-go areas
4. Establish evacuation point and procedures
5. Verify all team members have appropriate PPE
6. Test all safety equipment (radios, first aid kit)

### Documentation System

Create a **structure assessment form** (on paper or digital):
- Building address and date
- Exterior assessment results
- Interior structural findings (photo locations, measurements)
- Hazard locations (marked on sketch with coordinates)
- Load-bearing vs. non-load-bearing walls identified
- Utility connections (electrical, gas, water)
- Salvage recommendation (SAFE/CAUTION/HIGH RISK/UNSAFE)
- Team lead signature and date

### Signage & Perimeter Control

- Mark unsafe areas with **red tape or signs** ("UNSAFE - Do Not Enter")
- Mark off-limits hazard zones with **yellow tape** ("CAUTION - Hazard Present")
- Maintain **5 m perimeter** around building during active assessment
- Post safety rules at main entrance (PPE required, no entry alone, emergency procedures)

### Medical Response Plan

Establish protocol for injury during assessment:
1. Immediate area evacuation (if structural hazard)
2. First aid station at safe distance from building
3. Identify nearest medical facility (distance, directions)
4. Radio/communication system to call for help
5. Designated first aid responder on team

## Common Hazards & Quick Mitigation

| Hazard | Quick Mitigation | Long-Term Solution |
|--------|------------------|--------------------|
| Weak upper floors | Restrict access to ground floor only | Professional joist inspection/replacement |
| Leaking roof | Avoid collection under leak | Temporary tarp/roofing repair |
| Active gas leak | Evacuate; ventilate with open doors | Utility isolation by professional |
| Mold (isolated) | Don PPE; isolate area | Cleaning with water/bleach solution |
| Electrical hazard | Assume live; do not touch | Utility main breaker shutoff + lock-out |
| Sagging walls | Mark for observation; avoid lateral loads | Structural engineer assessment |
