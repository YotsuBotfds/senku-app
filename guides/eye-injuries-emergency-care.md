:::danger
Eye injuries are time-sensitive and can result in permanent blindness if improperly managed. Chemical burns require immediate irrigation. Penetrating injuries require complete immobilization. Seek professional ophthalmologic care whenever available; this guide is for austere settings only.
:::

## Complaint-First Triage

Use this table to jump straight to the right protocol. If the complaint is **not** an emergency, it may belong in [Vision Correction & Field Optometry](vision-correction-optometry.md) instead.

Bleach, drain cleaner, oven cleaner, battery acid, pool chemicals, solvents, and unknown cleaner splashes belong here first, not in mild pink-eye care. Start irrigation immediately for chemical eye exposure and keep irrigating while arranging urgent evaluation. Blunt trauma, penetrating trauma, high-speed debris, metal/wood chips, anything poking out, and debris that may be embedded also belong here first: do not flush, remove, rub, or press on the eye; shield the eye without pressure, shield/cover both eyes if possible to reduce eye movement, and seek urgent eye evaluation.

Index these as emergency embedded-object patterns, not routine irritation: "metal chip flew into eye while grinding and stuck", "stick scratched eye and something poking out", "flying debris + darker vision", and "high-speed debris/embedded object". For any of those patterns, do not flush, remove, rub, press, or apply direct pressure. Shield or cover the eye without pressure, shield/cover both eyes if possible, keep the head still, and get urgent eye care.

| Complaint | Urgency | Go to |
|-----------|---------|-------|
| **Chemical splash in eye** | EMERGENCY — seconds count | [Chemical Burns: Irrigation Protocol](#chemical-burn) — start flushing **now**, read details while irrigating |
| **Something in my eye** | Urgent (within the hour) | [Foreign Body Removal](#foreign-body); if embedded, high-speed, metal/wood, or poking out, do not flush or remove; shield and use [Penetrating Injury](#penetrating-injury) |
| **Sudden vision loss** (not gradual blur) | EMERGENCY | Rule out [Penetrating Injury](#penetrating-injury), [Blunt Trauma / Hyphema](#blunt-trauma); if no trauma, suspect retinal/optic nerve — evacuate |
| **Eye hit / punched / struck** | Urgent | [Blunt Trauma and Hyphema](#blunt-trauma); vision loss, halos, blood in the eye, abnormal pupil, or severe pain need urgent evaluation; check for [Orbital Fracture](#orbital-fracture) |
| **Red, sticky, crusty eye** | Routine | [Conjunctivitis and Infection](#infection) |
| **Lost or broke my glasses** | Routine — not an emergency | See [Vision Correction & Field Optometry](vision-correction-optometry.md) for frame repair, replacement, and lens grinding |
| **Gradual blurring, need stronger glasses** | Routine | See [Vision Correction & Field Optometry](vision-correction-optometry.md) |

:::warning
If in doubt, treat as an emergency. Permanent vision loss can be irreversible within hours for chemical burns, penetrating injuries, and some retinal emergencies.

## Eye Anatomy Review

Understanding eye structure is essential for injury assessment and treatment.

<div class="eye-anatomy">

### External Structures
- **Eyelids:** Protective flaps with tear-producing glands.
- **Conjunctiva:** Thin mucous membrane covering sclera and inner eyelids.
- **Sclera:** White outer layer of eyeball (opaque).
- **Cornea:** Clear dome covering iris and pupil (anterior-most part of eye).

### Internal Structures
- **Iris:** Colored muscle surrounding pupil; controls light entry.
- **Pupil:** Opening through which light enters (dilates in darkness, constricts in bright light).
- **Lens:** Clear structure behind pupil; focuses light on retina.
- **Aqueous humor:** Clear fluid filling anterior chamber (between cornea and lens).
- **Vitreous humor:** Gel filling posterior chamber (large interior space).
- **Retina:** Light-sensitive tissue lining back of eye; converts light to neural signals.
- **Optic nerve:** Carries visual signals to brain.

### Protective Features
- **Corneal reflex:** Eyelid closes reflexively if object approaches (protective).
- **Tear film:** Cleanses and lubricates surface; contains antibiotics (lysozyme).
- **Orbital bone:** Bony socket protects eyeball from blunt trauma.

</div>

<section id="chemical-burn">

## Chemical Burns: Immediate Irrigation Protocol

Chemical burns are the most time-sensitive eye injury. Bleach, drain cleaner, oven cleaner, battery acid, pool chemicals, solvents, and unknown cleaners can damage the eye even when the surface looks only red at first. Damage progresses for minutes to hours after contact. Immediate action is critical.

For any chemical splash in the eye, the first action is irrigation: flush immediately with running water or saline, hold the lids open, remove contacts only if they come out easily during rinsing, continue for at least 15-20 minutes and preferably longer for strong cleaners, and arrange urgent evaluation/Poison Control guidance. Do not neutralize with vinegar, baking soda, milk, eye drops, or other chemicals.

### Irrigation Procedure

<div class="injury-protocol">

**Immediate action (first 30 seconds):**

1. **Stop exposure** — Cease exposure to chemical source.
2. **Open eyelids** — Patient or self; force eyelids wide.
3. **Flush with water immediately** — Use available water: tap, bottle, shower, bucket. Do NOT delay finding "optimal" fluid.
4. **Pour water continuously** — Pour water from inside (near nose) across to outside (near temple). Gravity assists; water drains toward cheek.
5. **Have patient blink repeatedly** — Helps flush chemical deeper and into corners.

**Irrigation timeline:**
- **Do not stop just because pain or burning worsens at first.** Worsening pain can happen as trapped chemical moves; stopping irrigation leaves chemical in contact with the eye.
- **0–5 minutes:** Maximum urgency. More important than transporting to medical facility.
- **5–30 minutes:** Continue irrigation while transporting.
- **30–60 minutes:** Complete at least 60 minutes of continuous irrigation.
- **>60 minutes:** Continue if available; benefit diminishes but water does no harm.

**Field irrigation (no clean water):**
- Tap water is acceptable (contamination risk is secondary to chemical removal).
- Saline (if available) is optimal but do not delay waiting for it.
- Milk (if in rural setting) can be used if water unavailable.
- Do NOT use acids to neutralize bases, or vice versa (exothermic reaction causes heat injury).

**Eyelid eversion (if chemical in eye socket):**
1. Have patient look down.
2. Grasp upper eyelid lashes gently.
3. Pull eyelid upward and outward; rotate lid inside-out over applicator stick (cotton swab).
4. Continue irrigation inside eyelid to dislodge embedded particles.
5. Return lid to normal position.

</div>

### Chemical Burn Assessment After Irrigation

| Severity | Appearance | Prognosis |
|----------|-----------|-----------|
| **Mild** | Conjunctival redness, minimal corneal involvement | Usually full recovery |
| **Moderate** | Corneal haze (can see through but cloudy), conjunctival ulceration | Risk of some vision loss if deep penetration |
| **Severe** | Cornea opaque/white (can't see through), widespread tissue necrosis, lid scarring | High risk of permanent vision loss or blindness |

**Prognostic sign:** Limbus (junction between cornea and sclera) blanching (loss of blood vessels) indicates severe deep injury; prognosis guarded.

### Post-Irrigation Care

- **Patch eye loosely** after irrigation (prevents reflex blinking, reduces pain).
- **Apply antibiotic ointment** if available (tetracycline or erythromycin) to reduce infection.
- **Give analgesics** (acetaminophen, ibuprofen) to help patient tolerate eye opening for continued care.
- **Re-irrigate** at 1, 4, and 12 hours after initial irrigation in austere settings (chemical may re-dissolve after initial flush).
- **Arrange urgent eye evaluation/evacuation** for chemical eye exposure, especially alkali cleaners such as drain cleaner or any vision change, severe pain, cloudy cornea, or persistent redness.

<section id="corneal-abrasion">

## Corneal Abrasion

Corneal abrasion is loss of corneal surface (top 50 micrometers) due to scratch, foreign body, or thermal burn.

### Diagnosis

**Symptoms:**
- Foreign body sensation ("sand in eye")
- Severe pain with eye movement (much worse than conjunctival injury)
- Tearing, photophobia (light sensitivity)
- Redness, mild discharge

**Examination:**
- Inspect for visible scratch or defect using good lighting (flashlight).
- Fluorescein stain (if available): Dyes damaged area bright green under blue light. Very sensitive.
- Evert eyelids to rule out embedded foreign body.

### Management

1. **Remove foreign body** (see next section) if present.
2. **Apply antibiotic ointment:** Tetracycline, erythromycin, or chloramphenicol ointment 4× daily to reduce infection risk.
3. **Patch eye loosely** with sterile patch to prevent blinking (reduces pain and allows healing).
4. **Give analgesics** (acetaminophen or ibuprofen).
5. **Avoid contact lenses** until healed (typically 24–72 hours for small abrasions).
6. **Re-examine at 24 hours:** Check for secondary infection (purulent discharge, increasing pain).

**Healing timeline:** Small abrasions heal in 24–48 hours; larger ones 3–7 days. Cornea regenerates rapidly.

**Complications:** Recurrent abrasion (scar pulls open), infection (keratitis), anterior uveitis (internal inflammation). If pain persists >72 hours or discharge develops, assume infection and increase antibiotic frequency.

</section>

<section id="foreign-body">

## Foreign Body Removal

Dust, sand, eyelash, or object under eyelid.

### Location Identification

**Under upper eyelid:**
1. Have patient look down.
2. Grasp upper lashes gently, pull lid up and away from eye.
3. Shine light into exposed area; look for particle.

**Under lower eyelid:**
1. Have patient look up.
2. Pull lower lid downward; inspect white sclera under eyelid.

**Embedded in cornea or sclera:**
1. Examine with good lighting.
2. Visible dark spot on clear cornea = embedded particle.

### Removal Technique

**Non-embedded particles (upper or lower lid):**
1. Have patient blink gently several times (tears may wash particle out).
2. **Wet cotton swab:** Lightly touch tip to particle; withdrawal should dislodge it.
3. **Eyelid retraction:** Grasp eyelid, pull gently to expose particle; allow patient to blink it out.
4. **Irrigation:** Flush with water or saline to dislodge and wash away.

**Embedded, high-speed, or penetrating particles (cornea, sclera, or anything poking out):**
- Treat metal chips, grinding debris, glass, thorns, splinters, and any object that looks stuck in or protruding from the eye as a possible penetrating injury.
- This includes "metal chip flew into eye while grinding and stuck", "stick scratched eye and something poking out", "flying debris + darker vision", and "high-speed debris/embedded object".
- Do NOT flush, rub, press on the eye, apply direct pressure, or attempt removal.
- Shield the injured eye with a rigid cup or loose cover with no pressure; shield/cover both eyes if possible to reduce eye movement.
- Seek urgent eye evaluation/evacuation. Rough extraction or pressure can cause internal bleeding, fluid loss from the eye, scarring, and permanent vision loss.

**Irregular particles (thorns, wood splinters):**
- More damaging; increased infection risk.
- Consider antibiotics: tetracycline ointment 4× daily for 5 days.
- Watch for signs of infection (purulent discharge, increasing redness).

### Post-Removal Care

- **Antibiotic ointment:** Apply after removal.
- **Patch loosely:** Reduces discomfort; allows healing.
- **Re-examine at 24 hours:** Ensure no secondary abrasion or infection.

</section>

## Blunt Trauma and Hyphema

Blunt impact to eye (punch, ball, object) can cause bleeding into anterior chamber (hyphema) or retinal/optic nerve damage.

For blunt trauma with pain, halos, vision change or darkened vision, blood in the eye, abnormal pupil, severe light sensitivity, or concern for globe injury, use a rigid eye shield or loose cover with **no pressure** and seek urgent eye evaluation before treating it like mild irritation or pink eye. Do not apply direct pressure to the eyeball.

Flying debris with darker vision or reduced vision is not a simple rinse case. If debris may still be in the eye, do not flush, remove, rub, press, or apply direct pressure; shield without pressure, keep the head still, cover both eyes if possible, and seek urgent eye care.

### Hyphema (Blood in Anterior Chamber)

**Appearance:** Red blood visible in front of iris; may partially obscure iris/pupil.

**Severity grading:**
- **Microhyphema:** Blood cells visible only under microscope.
- **Grade 1:** Blood fills <1/4 of anterior chamber.
- **Grade 2:** Blood fills 1/4 to 1/2 of chamber.
- **Grade 3:** Blood fills 1/2 to entire chamber (total hyphema).

**Management:**
1. **Activity restriction:** Avoid exertion, heavy lifting, strenuous movement (raises intraocular pressure, increases bleeding).
2. **Head elevation:** Sleep with head elevated 30° (gravity assists blood settlement).
3. **Eye patching:** Limit eye movement.
4. **Avoid NSAIDs:** Ibuprofen/naproxen increase bleeding; use acetaminophen instead.
5. **Topical steroids:** Reduce inflammation (if available: dexamethasone or prednisolone drops 4x daily). **Note:** Topical ophthalmic steroids should only be used for specific indications (hyphema, post-surgical inflammation, uveitis) and require monitoring for elevated intraocular pressure. Do not use topical steroids for more than 7 days without reassessment — prolonged use causes steroid-induced glaucoma and cataracts. Contraindicated if herpes simplex keratitis, fungal, or bacterial eye infection is suspected (steroids worsen these infections).
6. **Monitor intraocular pressure:** If available, measure with tonometer. High pressure (>30 mmHg) risks optic nerve damage; may need aqueous humor drainage (professional procedure).

**Timeline:** Blood reabsorbs over 2-4 weeks. Vision improves as blood clears.

**Complications:** Corneal staining (blood pigment deposits), elevated intraocular pressure, post-traumatic glaucoma (delayed), retinal detachment (if severe trauma).

### Commotio Retinae (Retinal Bruising)

Blunt impact can bruise retina (retinal cells swell, impairing vision transiently).

**Symptoms:** Blurred vision, floaters (dark spots in visual field), photopsia (flashing lights).

**Management:** Supportive. Vision improves over weeks as retinal swelling resolves. Avoid strenuous activity.

**Prognosis:** Usually resolves without permanent damage; severe cases risk retinal detachment.

</section>

<section id="penetrating-injury">

## Penetrating Injury: Do NOT Remove

Penetrating object (glass, metal, wood through eye wall) is surgical emergency. Improper handling worsens outcome catastrophically.

### Immediate Management

:::warning
**Critical rule: Do NOT attempt to remove penetrating object.** Removal causes massive internal bleeding and often permanent blindness.
:::

1. **Immobilize object:** Do not touch or attempt removal.
2. **No flushing or pressure:** Do not flush, rub, press, or apply direct pressure to an eye with a protruding or embedded object.
3. **Shield eye:** Cover with protective cup or loose dressing (not pressure patching, which may dislodge object).
4. **Both eyes:** Patch non-injured eye to reduce eye movement (any movement affects injured eye; reduces motion of embedded object).
5. **Head immobilization:** Have patient remain still; neck collar helps prevent movement.
6. **Evacuate immediately:** Penetrating eye injuries require surgical intervention within hours (infection and internal hemorrhage worsen prognosis).
7. **Pain control:** Acetaminophen (avoid NSAIDs).
8. **NPO (nothing by mouth):** Patient may require surgery; withhold food/water.

**Prognosis:** Highly dependent on penetration depth, location, and time to surgery. Many cases result in significant vision loss or blindness despite optimal care.

</section>

<section id="orbital-fracture">

## Orbital Fracture

Blunt trauma to eye socket can fracture orbital bones (maxilla, zygomatic, orbital rim).

### Types of Orbital Fractures

**Orbital rim fracture:** Fracture at edge of eye socket. Usually stable; manageable with pain control and ice.

**Blowout fracture (orbital floor):** Blunt impact raises pressure inside orbit; thin floor bone fractures. Orbital contents (fat, muscles) prolapse downward into maxillary sinus below.

### Blowout Fracture Diagnosis

**Symptoms:**
- Numbness in cheek/upper lip (infraorbital nerve compression).
- Diplopia (double vision), especially looking upward (inferior rectus muscle trapped in fracture).
- Enophthalmos (eye appears sunken; orbital fat descends).
- Periorbital ecchymosis (black eye), swelling.

**Examination:**
- Ask patient to look upward (upgrade if inferior rectus trapped; superior gaze limitation).
- Palpate orbital rim (step-off deformity indicating fracture).
- Test sensation over cheek (numbness = trigeminal nerve injury from fracture fragment).

### Management

**Acute (first week):**
1. **Ice:** 15 min on, 15 min off to reduce swelling.
2. **Head elevation:** Reduces swelling.
3. **Analgesics:** Acetaminophen or ibuprofen.
4. **Avoid strenuous activity:** Increases orbital swelling.

**Surgical repair:** Indicated if:
- Significant enophthalmos (cosmetic concern, vision risk)
- Diplopia persisting >2 weeks (muscle entrapment)
- Large fracture (>50% floor involvement)

Timing: Repair ideally within 1–2 weeks. Swelling delays surgery; ice and elevation reduce swelling.

**Prognosis:** Many blowout fractures stabilize without surgery (swelling resolves, muscles reattach). Surgery necessary if persistent symptoms after 2–3 weeks.

</section>

## Conjunctivitis and Infection

### Bacterial Conjunctivitis

**Cause:** Bacteria (Staphylococcus, Streptococcus, Pseudomonas).

**Symptoms:** Purulent discharge (thick, yellow), conjunctival redness, gritty feeling, minimal pain (distinguishes from keratitis).

**Treatment:**
- Antibiotic ointment: Tetracycline 4× daily for 7 days
- Antibiotic drops: Chloramphenicol or trimethoprim/sulfamethoxazole 4× daily
- Clean eyelids gently with warm water to remove discharge

**Contagion:** Highly contagious; separate from others; wash hands frequently.

**Timeline:** Improves within 2–3 days of antibiotics.

### Viral Conjunctivitis

**Cause:** Adenovirus, herpes simplex, varicella-zoster.

**Symptoms:** Watery discharge (clear), conjunctival redness, minimal pain, preauricular lymph node enlargement (swollen node in front of ear).

**Treatment:**
- Supportive: Cold compresses, artificial tears, analgesics
- Topical antivirals (if available): Acyclovir ointment for HSV
- Avoid antibiotics (do not help viral infection; unnecessary)

**Contagion:** Viral more contagious than bacterial; standard hygiene critical.

**Timeline:** Self-limited; resolves in 1–2 weeks.

### Herpetic Keratitis

Herpes simplex virus infecting cornea.

**Symptoms:** Severe eye pain, photophobia, foreign body sensation, branching (dendritic) ulcer visible on cornea under fluorescein.

**Treatment:**
- Topical acyclovir ointment 5× daily
- Cycloplegic drops (if available: cyclopentolate) to paralyze ciliary muscle and reduce pain
- Analgesics
- Avoid topical steroids (worsen herpetic infection)

**Prognosis:** Most cases resolve within 1–2 weeks with antiviral therapy; recurrence common.

## Eye Patching and Shield Application

Patching protects eye from further trauma and reduces discomfort by preventing blinking.

### Patch Application (Loose)

1. **Cleanse eyelid:** Remove any discharge, dirt.
2. **Position patch:** Place over closed eye, extending from forehead to cheek.
3. **Secure gently:** Use tape to hold patch in place; do NOT apply pressure (risk of pressure necrosis).
4. **Both eyes optional:** For severe injuries or to prevent eye movement, patch both eyes (less common; impairs mobility).

**Duration:** 24–72 hours typically. Remove daily to inspect for infection/healing.

### Eye Shield (Protective Cup)

For penetrating injuries or severe trauma:

1. **Use rigid cup:** Commercial plastic shield or improvised container (small cup, egg carton section).
2. **Secure around eye:** Tape securely to prevent movement.
3. **Do NOT apply pressure:** Shield should be loose enough to fit finger under.
4. **Maintain for 24+ hours:** Until medical evacuation or professional assessment.

</section>

## Vision Testing: Snellen Chart

Assessing visual acuity (sharpness) helps determine severity of eye injury.

### Snellen Chart Setup

Standard eye chart with rows of letters decreasing in size from top to bottom.

- **20/20 line:** Normal vision (can read letters 20 feet away that standard person can read 20 feet away).
- **20/40 line:** Reduced vision (can only read at 20 feet what standard person reads at 40 feet).
- **20/200 line:** Legal blindness threshold in many jurisdictions.

### Testing Procedure

1. **Patient stands 20 feet from chart** (or scale distance if space limited).
2. **Occlude non-test eye:** Hold hand/patch over eye not being tested.
3. **Ask patient to read aloud:** Point to progressively smaller lines.
4. **Stop when unreadable:** Note line where patient begins making errors.
5. **Record acuity:** e.g., "20/40 OD" (right eye, 20/40 vision).
6. **Repeat for other eye:** Occlude first eye; test second.

### Interpretation

- **20/20:** Normal vision
- **20/30–20/60:** Mild reduction (still functional)
- **20/100–20/200:** Moderate-to-severe reduction (driving illegal)
- **<20/200:** Legal blindness (usually)
- **Count fingers:** Patient can count fingers at arm's length (severe vision loss)
- **Light perception:** Patient sees only light/dark distinction (near total blindness)
- **No light perception:** Complete blindness

**Injured eye trend:** Improving acuity over days suggests recovery; worsening acuity suggests progression and need for urgent evaluation.
