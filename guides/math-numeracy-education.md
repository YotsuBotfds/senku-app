## Overview

In a low-technology or resource-constrained environment, the ability to calculate, measure, and reason mathematically becomes essential for survival and community function. This guide covers practical methods for teaching arithmetic, fractions, measurement, and geometry without reliance on calculators or formal schooling infrastructure.

Mathematical literacy supports tasks across all domains: rationing food, measuring land, trading fairly, managing labor, constructing shelters, calculating medicine dosages, and preserving harvests. The methods here emphasize direct manipulation of physical objects (beans, stones, strings, rods), mental techniques, and hands-on construction—all proven to develop deep understanding more effectively than abstract notation alone.

Teaching math in resilient communities requires adapting to mixed-age groups, varied prior knowledge, and minimal printed materials. This guide provides frameworks for all-ages instruction and strategies for creating teaching materials from found objects.

</section>

<section id="number-systems-foundations">

## Number Systems & Foundational Concepts

Before formal arithmetic, learners must understand what numbers represent: quantity, order, and magnitude.

**Concrete-to-Abstract Progression:**

Start with objects: beans, pebbles, seeds, or sticks. Five-year-olds count beans into piles; ten-year-olds count beans into groups of five and ten, recognizing patterns. Adults already have intuitive grasp but may struggle with new bases or systems.

- **One-to-one correspondence:** Each object represents one unit. Learners touch each bean as they count: "one, two, three."
- **Subitizing:** Instantly recognizing small quantities without counting. Most children achieve this for groups up to five by age four.
- **Conservation of quantity:** Understanding that five beans remain five whether scattered or grouped—a milestone around age six.
- **Cardinality:** The final count ("five") represents the total, not just the last object touched.

**The Tally System (Base 1):**

Tally marks (four vertical lines + diagonal slash for five) are the simplest permanent number recording system. Useful for:
- Tracking days, harvests, or labor hours.
- Teaching place value implicitly: five single marks grouped into one "bundle" of five.
- Accessible to pre-literate populations.

Spend 1–2 weeks using tally for real counting (days until market, fish caught) before introducing symbolic numerals.

**Base-10 System (Decimal):**

Most human cultures use base-10 (ten fingers as origin). Teach base-10 using bundles:

1. Count out 10 beans. Place them in a cup or tie in cloth. This is "one ten."
2. Make 10 tens, bundled again. This is "one hundred."
3. Make 10 hundreds. This is "one thousand."

Write the word "ten" and the numeral "10" on bark, clay, or slate. Associate the symbol with the bundle. Repeat for 100 and 1000.

**Alternative Bases (Indigenous/Practical):**

Some cultures used base-20 or base-5. Teach one alternative base to illustrate that decimal is arbitrary:

- **Base-5:** Count on fingers of one hand. "One hand is five. Two hands are ten." Useful for body-based measurement.
- **Base-20:** Count fingers and toes. Mayan and other systems used this; teaches children to think flexibly about quantity grouping.

This demystifies "math" and prevents the false assumption that base-10 is the only way to count.

## Arithmetic Operations Without Calculators

All four operations (addition, subtraction, multiplication, division) can be performed with beans, rods, tally marks, or mental techniques.

**Addition with Objects:**

Three beans + two beans = five beans. Combine the piles, count the total. Repeat with varied objects (stones, seeds) and quantities (up to 20 initially). Introduce symbolic notation:

```
[bean] [bean] [bean] + [bean] [bean] = [bean] [bean] [bean] [bean] [bean]
```

Then: "3 + 2 = 5" on a slate board.

**Mental Addition (Subitizing & Decomposition):**

Once learners master bean counting up to 20:
- Teach "making ten": 8 + 7 = 8 + (2 + 5) = (8 + 2) + 5 = 10 + 5 = 15.
- Practice "doubles": 5 + 5 = 10, 6 + 6 = 12. These are fast to memorize.
- Use a number line drawn on the ground or in sand: mark 1–20. Count forward by hops.

**Subtraction with Objects:**

Start with a pile of 10 beans. Remove 3. Count remaining: 7. Write "10 − 3 = 7."

Introduce the concept of "missing addend": "What do you add to 3 to make 10?" This inverts subtraction and prepares learners for mental math. (Answer: 7.)

**Multiplication as Repeated Groups:**

Five piles of three beans each = 15 beans. Show this many times, then introduce: "5 groups of 3 = 15, or 5 × 3 = 15."

Create a physical multiplication table: make rows of stones on the ground.
```
Row 1:  o
Row 2:  o o
Row 3:  o o o
Row 4:  o o o o
Row 5:  o o o o o
```

Count the stones in each row, writing "1, 2, 3, 4, 5." Now show 5 rows of 4 stones and count the total (20). Record "5 × 4 = 20."

**Division as Sharing & Grouping:**

Divide 12 beans equally among 3 people: 4 beans per person. Write "12 ÷ 3 = 4."

Also teach grouping: "How many groups of 3 can you make from 12?" Answer: 4 groups.

**Long Multiplication (Vedic or Lattice Methods):**

For larger numbers, teach the lattice method, which is visual and less error-prone than standard long multiplication:

For 23 × 17:
```
    2   3
   -------
 1 | 2 | 3 |
   -------
 7 | 4 | 2 | 1 |
   -------
```

Fill each cell with the product of the row and column digits:
- 1 × 2 = 2 (top-left cell)
- 1 × 3 = 3 (top-right)
- 7 × 2 = 14 (bottom-left; write 1 in upper triangle, 4 in lower)
- 7 × 3 = 21 (bottom-right)

Add along diagonals and carry over. This visual method requires less memorization than standard algorithms and works well for mixed-age groups.

## Fractions, Ratios & Proportions

Fractions are among the hardest concepts to teach without formal schooling, yet they're essential for cooking, trading, land division, and sharing.

**Fractions Through Cutting:**

Start with a flat object: bread, bark, cloth, or paper. Cut it in half. Show both pieces are equal; together they make the whole. Write "1 ÷ 2 = 1/2."

Cut a third piece (whole ÷ 3). Emphasize that the pieces must be equal. Repeat with fourths, fifths.

Show that 2 halves make 1 whole; 3 thirds make 1 whole; etc.

**Using a Rope or String:**

Fold a length of rope into equal segments:
- Fold in half: each segment is 1/2.
- Fold into thirds: each segment is 1/3.
- Fold into quarters: each segment is 1/4.

Mark the folds with dye or notches. Learners can now physically compare fractions: "Is 1/3 larger or smaller than 1/2?" (Smaller—visually obvious.)

**Adding & Subtracting Fractions:**

Use a concrete cake or bread divided into equal slices.
- 1/4 of the cake + 1/4 of the cake = 2/4 = 1/2.
- 3/4 − 1/4 = 2/4.

Only after extensive concrete practice, introduce: "You can only add fractions with the same denominator. Find a common denominator."

**Ratios and Scaling:**

Ratios appear in recipes, trade, and land measurement. Teach with real examples:

Recipe: "For every 2 cups of flour, use 1 cup of water." (Ratio 2:1.)

Scale it up: "If you need to feed 8 people instead of 4, double the recipe: 4 cups flour, 2 cups water." (Scaled 2:1 ratio.)

Draw this as boxes:
```
Flour: [__] [__]
Water: [__]
```

For 8 people:
```
Flour: [__] [__] [__] [__]
Water: [__] [__]
```

**Percentages (Late Intermediate/Adult Level):**

Once fractions are solid, percentages are easier: percent means "per hundred."

50% = 50/100 = 1/2
25% = 25/100 = 1/4

For trade or pricing: "An item costs 100 tokens. A 25% discount means you pay 75 tokens." (Remove 25/100 of the price.)

:::tip
**Fraction Teaching Order:** Halves → thirds → fourths → fifths and beyond. Use physical objects (bread, rope, fields) for the first 6 months of fraction teaching. Only then introduce symbolic notation (1/2, 3/4, etc.).
:::

<section id="mental-math-techniques">

## Mental Math Techniques

Rapid mental calculation (without paper or devices) is valuable for everyday life and reduces dependence on written records.

**Doubling & Halving:**

These are fast and generalizable.
- 2 × 47 = 94 (double 47).
- 96 ÷ 2 = 48 (halve 96).
- 8 × 35 = 4 × 70 = 280 (double 35 to 70, halve the multiplier 8 to 4, then multiply 4 × 70 mentally).

**Breaking Numbers Apart (Decomposition):**

37 + 25 = (30 + 7) + (20 + 5) = (30 + 20) + (7 + 5) = 50 + 12 = 62.

Teach learners to break numbers into tens and ones, then recombine.

**Making Round Numbers:**

99 + 13 = 100 + 12 = 112 (round 99 up to 100, adjust the other number down by 1).

This is faster than 99 + 13 = (90 + 9) + (10 + 3) = 100 + 12 = 112, but both work.

**Vedic Subtraction (Complement Method):**

Rather than borrowing, add the complement to reach a round number:

100 − 37 = ? instead, ask: "What do you add to 37 to reach 100?" Answer: 63.

Train this: 100 − 27 = 73; 100 − 45 = 55; 1000 − 234 = 766.

**Multiplication by 11 (Pattern Recognition):**

23 × 11 = 253 (add the two digits: 2 + 3 = 5; write 2, 5, 3).

34 × 11 = 374 (3 + 4 = 7; write 3, 7, 4).

This is a trick that builds memorization and pattern confidence.

## The Abacus & Counting Boards

An abacus (or counting board with stones/beads) serves as a portable, non-destructible arithmetic device.

**Simple Abacus Construction:**

String beads or drilled stones on wooden dowels, mounted in a frame. Two designs:

1. **Roman Abacus (Dust Board):** A wooden frame with sand or dust, marked with lines. Move pebbles along the lines (units, tens, hundreds). Erased by brushing smooth; reusable indefinitely.

2. **Bead Abacus:** Wooden frame, 6–8 dowels, 10 beads per dowel. Bottom position = 0; move beads upward to increase the digit. The upper beads (4–5) are separated by a bar, grouping five-bead units for speed.

**Abacus Arithmetic:**

Addition of 23 + 17:
- Set 23: two beads (tens) + three beads (ones).
- Add 17: move one bead to tens column (now three beads), add seven to ones.
- Ones column: 3 + 7 = 10. Move 10 ones (one full bar) to tens.
- Result: 4 tens + 0 ones = 40.

Subtraction, multiplication, and division are equally visual.

**Teaching Abacus:**

Start with small numbers (single digits). Let learners manipulate the abacus freely while you ask: "What is 3 + 2? Show me on the abacus." Over weeks, learners internalize the bead positions and can solve problems faster on the abacus than with beans—because the beads don't scatter.

:::info-box
**Abacus Advantage:** An abacus is a permanent tool that never "breaks." A child can use the same abacus for 50 years, teaching their own children. It requires no electricity, paper, or erasure. In resource-limited settings, a single abacus per classroom serves all learners.
:::

## Geometry Through Construction

Formal geometry (theorems, proofs) is unnecessary for survival math, but spatial reasoning—angles, areas, volume—is vital for building, land measurement, and navigation.

**Angles & Directions (Compass & Theodolite):**

Teach eight cardinal directions (N, NE, E, SE, S, SW, W, NW) using the sun or a magnetic compass. Mark these on the ground or on a board.

Angles in degrees: A full circle is 360°. A right angle (corner of a square building) is 90°. A straight line is 180°.

**Making Right Angles (Pythagorean Triple):**

The 3-4-5 triangle has a right angle. To verify or construct:
1. Measure a rope into 12 equal segments (knots at each segment).
2. Form a triangle with sides of 3, 4, and 5 segments.
3. If the angle opposite the longest side (hypotenuse) is 90°, the triangle is right-angled.

This works because 3² + 4² = 9 + 16 = 25 = 5². Use this to square the corners of buildings or fields.

**Area & Land Measurement:**

For rectangular land: area = length × width (meters or rods).

For triangular land: area = base × height ÷ 2.

For irregular land, divide into triangles or rectangles, calculate each, and sum.

Teach measurement with pace-lengths, hand-widths, or measuring ropes (knotted at regular intervals). Write down measurements on bark or clay—don't rely on memory.

**Volume (Capacity for Storage & Containers):**

Volume = length × width × height (for a box).

For a cylindrical grain store: volume = π × radius² × height. If learners haven't encountered π, use the approximation π ≈ 3.14, or teach it as a mystery constant: "The circumference of any circle is about 3.14 times its diameter."

Practical: Use a container of known volume (e.g., a clay pot that holds exactly 10 liters) to calibrate measuring by pouring.

<section id="teaching-children-vs-adults">

## Teaching Children vs. Adults

Mathematical readiness differs dramatically by age. Tailor instruction accordingly.

**Ages 4–7 (Concrete Foundations):**
- Use beans, stones, and physical objects exclusively.
- Teach quantity recognition (1–10) before numbers 11–20.
- Single-digit addition only.
- Introduce tally marks and the numeral 0–9.
- Learning is play: games, songs, counting rhymes.
- Very short lessons (10–15 minutes).

**Ages 8–12 (Mixed Concrete & Abstract):**
- Transition from beans to tally marks to symbols.
- Two-digit addition and subtraction.
- Multiplication tables (memorized through repeated use, not rote).
- Early fractions (halves, quarters).
- Simple word problems with concrete contexts ("If you catch 5 fish and I catch 3, how many together?").
- Lessons 20–30 minutes; mix teaching with independent practice.

**Ages 13+ (Abstract Reasoning):**
- Multi-digit multiplication and division.
- Fractions, decimals, percentages.
- Introduction to algebra (using variables: "If x + 3 = 7, what is x?").
- Geometry, area, volume.
- Applied problem-solving (trade calculations, crop yield predictions).
- Lessons 30–45 minutes; discussion and exploration encouraged.

**Adult Learners (Often Returning to Math):**
- Often have gaps from childhood. Diagnose where understanding breaks down.
- Motivated by immediate relevance: land measurement, fair trade, dosage calculation.
- May have shame around math; reassure that all concepts can be learned.
- Faster cognitive pace than children but may require repeated concrete examples to unblock misconceptions.
- Can handle abstractions quickly once concrete examples demonstrate the logic.

## Creating Manipulatives from Found Materials

Formal math curricula often recommend purchasing blocks, counters, and kits. In resource-limited settings, make equivalents from:

- **Beans, seeds, pebbles:** Counters for arithmetic.
- **Sticks or dowels:** Number lines (marked 1–100 with notches); Vedic multipliers; geometry tools.
- **String or rope:** Measure lengths; form shapes; tie knots to mark fractions.
- **Cloth scraps:** Cut into squares, rectangles, triangles for geometry.
- **Clay:** Form shapes; mark numbers; make dice (cubes).
- **Wood scraps:** Build an abacus frame; create blocks for volume exploration.
- **Bark or pressed paper:** Write numerals; record addition facts; tally counts.

These are often superior to plastic toys because learners help make them and develop ownership.

</section>

## Assessment Without Standardized Tests

Formal testing isn't feasible or necessary in resilient communities. Instead, use observation and practical demonstration:

**Observation Checklist:**
- Can the learner count by ones to 20 without errors?
- Do they use fingers or objects, or can they count abstractly?
- Can they solve single-digit addition problems (8 + 3 = ?) without materials?
- Can they recognize numerals 0–20 by sight?
- Do they understand conservation (that 5 beans remain 5 whether scattered or grouped)?

**Practical Demonstration:**
- Ask them to measure a field using paced steps, then calculate the area.
- Have them divide a loaf of bread into equal portions for the group.
- Ask them to calculate a fair trade: "If a hen costs 10 tokens and a goat costs 30, how many hens equal one goat?"
- Request they teach a younger child a concept they've learned.

These tasks reveal understanding far better than written tests.

</section>

## Integration With Survival Skills

Math taught in isolation feels abstract. Embed it in real activities:

- **Rationing:** "We have 30 kg of grain for 10 people for 3 months. How much per person per day?" (Math + food security.)
- **Building:** "The roof needs to span 5 meters. Beams are spaced 1.5 meters apart. How many beams do we need?" (Math + shelter.)
- **Gardening:** "Each turnip plant needs 20 cm² of space. Our garden is 2 m × 3 m. How many plants fit?" (Math + food production.)
- **Medicine:** "The tincture is 1 part herb to 10 parts alcohol. We need 100 mL. How much herb?" (Math + health.)
- **Labor scheduling:** "If 5 people can harvest a field in 8 days, how long will it take 10 people?" (Math + cooperation.)

Every skill domain becomes a context for practicing numeracy.

## Summary

Teaching mathematics without calculators or formal infrastructure requires returning to concrete, manipulative-based methods. Start with objects (beans, stones) to establish quantity and number concepts, progressing gradually to symbolic notation and mental calculation. Fractions, ratios, and geometry emerge naturally from practical activities like cooking, land division, and building. Abacuses and counting boards provide permanent, reusable tools. All-ages teaching flourishes when instruction integrates real survival tasks—rationing, measuring, trading—making math visibly purposeful. Assessment flows from observation and practical demonstration rather than standardized tests. The result is a mathematically competent population capable of calculating, measuring, and reasoning without electricity or external devices—a foundation for self-sufficiency and fair community exchange.
