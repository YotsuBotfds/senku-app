<section id="first-check">

## First Check: Is This Safe For Home Care?

Home care is reasonable only when all of these are true:

- Vision is basically normal
- Pain is mild, not severe
- The eye is not injured by a hit, puncture, or chemical
- The red eye is not accompanied by a badly cloudy cornea or a very abnormal pupil
- You are not wearing contact lenses with significant redness, pain, or light sensitivity

Think of this as a quick filter:

- **Red and itchy or watery** often points to allergy or mild irritation
- **Red and goopy** often points to conjunctivitis
- **Feels like something is in the eye** often points to dust, a lash, or a tiny corneal scratch
- **Tender bump on the lid** often points to a stye
- **Burning after screen time** often points to eye strain or dryness

If the story does not fit a mild problem, stop and use urgent-care guidance.

<section id="safe-flushing">

## Safe Flushing For "Something In My Eye"

If someone says, "something is in my eye," the safest first move is usually gentle flushing.

### What flushing helps

- Dust
- Eyelash
- Small loose debris
- Mild irritants like sweat, smoke, or wind-blown particles

### What to do

1. **Wash your hands first.**
2. **Remove contact lenses if present and easy to remove.**
3. **Blink several times.**
4. **Rinse the eye with clean water or sterile saline.**
5. **Flush from the inner corner toward the outer corner** so material drains away from the other eye.
6. **Keep flushing for several minutes if the foreign-body sensation persists.**

### What is safe to use

- Clean tap water
- Bottled water
- Sterile saline
- Eyewash solution

### What not to do

- Do not rub the eye hard
- Do not use tweezers, pins, or cotton swabs on the eyeball
- Do not try to scrape anything off the cornea
- Do not put chemical drops in the eye unless they are meant for that purpose

### If the object may still be stuck

If flushing does not improve the sensation, or pain is sharp and persistent, the problem may be a corneal scratch or a retained foreign body. That needs evaluation, especially if light hurts or vision is worse.

<section id="pink-eye">

## Pink Eye / Conjunctivitis Home Care

People search for this in a lot of ways:

- "pink eye"
- "red eyes and goopy discharge"
- "my eye is red and crusted shut"
- "conjunctivitis at home"

Conjunctivitis means inflammation of the thin membrane over the white of the eye and inner eyelids. Common causes include viral infection, bacterial infection, allergy, and irritation.

### What it usually feels like

- Redness
- Watery or sticky discharge
- Gritty or sandy feeling
- Mild burning or itching
- Eyelids stuck together in the morning

### Home care that is usually safe

- **Wash hands often.**
- **Use a clean warm compress** to loosen crusting.
- **Gently clean the eyelids** with clean water or saline.
- **Use artificial tears** if the eye feels dry or irritated.
- **Avoid touching or rubbing the eye.**
- **Throw away or replace eye makeup** used during the infection.
- **Do not share towels, washcloths, or pillowcases.**

### If allergy seems more likely

Itchy, watery, both-eyes-at-once redness with sneezing or seasonality often fits allergy more than infection. Helpful measures include:

- Cool compresses
- Oral antihistamine if appropriate
- Allergy eye drops if already available and suitable
- Avoiding the trigger

### If infection seems more likely

Thick discharge and crusting suggest infection more than allergy. Viral conjunctivitis often spreads easily but usually improves on its own. Bacterial conjunctivitis can also improve, but severe redness, pain, or reduced vision should not be treated as "just pink eye."

### Contagion tips

- Stay home from close contact when drainage is heavy
- Wash hands after touching the face
- Clean shared surfaces
- Replace contaminated eye cosmetics and contact supplies

<section id="stye">

## Styes And Eyelid Bumps

A stye is a tender bump at the edge of the eyelid, usually from a blocked or infected oil gland. It is often painful to touch and may look like a pimple on the lid.

### Home care for a stye

1. **Apply a warm compress** for 10 to 15 minutes at a time, several times a day.
2. **Keep the lid clean** with gentle washing.
3. **Do not squeeze or pop it.**
4. **Avoid eye makeup and contact lenses** until it improves.

Warm compresses work because heat helps the blocked gland drain.

### When it is probably not just a stye

Get checked if the swelling spreads across the eyelid, fever appears, eye movement becomes painful, or the eyeball itself becomes very red or painful. Those features raise concern for a deeper infection.

<section id="eye-strain">

## Eye Strain And Dry Eye Comfort Measures

Eye strain often shows up after screen use, reading, driving at night, fine work, or poor lighting. It can cause burning, fatigue, headache, and a dry or heavy feeling.

### Good comfort measures

- Follow the **20-20-20 rule**: every 20 minutes, look 20 feet away for 20 seconds
- Reduce glare and harsh brightness
- Increase font size instead of leaning closer
- Keep screens at a comfortable distance
- Blink more often during close work
- Use lubricating artificial tears if the eye is dry
- Sleep and hydration help more than many people expect

### Helpful habits

- Make sure the work area has enough light
- Take breaks from contacts if the eyes feel dry and irritated
- Clean contact lenses and cases correctly
- Replace worn-out prescription glasses if strain is becoming regular

If redness is mild and comes with burning after long screen time, dryness or strain is more likely than infection.

</section>

<section id="what-not-to-do">

## What Not To Do

- Do not use steroid eye drops unless a clinician specifically told you to
- Do not use old prescription eye drops without knowing what they are
- Do not wear contact lenses through a red-eye episode
- Do not patch a painful, infected, or contact-lens-related eye unless a clinician tells you to
- Do not keep flushing if the problem is actually a chemical exposure that needs emergency irrigation guidance instead

If you are dealing with a chemical splash, use the eye-injury emergency guide and irrigate immediately.

</section>

<section id="urgent-care">

## Danger Signs That Need Urgent Care

Seek urgent or same-day care if any of these are present:

- **Vision changes**
- **Moderate to severe eye pain**
- **Strong light sensitivity**
- **A cloudy, hazy, or white-looking cornea**
- **A misshapen, fixed, or very different pupil**
- **Blood in the eye after injury**
- **Chemical exposure**
- **Penetrating injury or anything embedded in the eye**
- **Contact lens wear with redness, pain, or discharge**
- **Worsening swelling around the eye**
- **Fever with eye pain or eyelid swelling**
- **Pain with eye movement**
- **Double vision**
- **A severe headache with nausea and a very red eye**
- **Symptoms that are getting worse instead of better**

Special caution for contact lens wearers: red eye plus pain or light sensitivity can be a corneal infection, not simple conjunctivitis. That is not a routine home-care problem.

If someone says "my eye is red and goopy" but also has bad pain, blurry vision, or light sensitivity, do not assume it is harmless pink eye.

</section>

<section id="home-care-summary">

## Practical Home-Care Summary

For a mild red eye, the basic home plan is:

1. Wash hands.
2. Remove contacts if present.
3. Rinse gently if debris is suspected.
4. Use warm compresses for styes or crusting.
5. Use cool compresses for allergy-type itch and redness.
6. Use artificial tears for dryness or irritation.
7. Rest the eyes and reduce screen strain.
8. Watch for danger signs.

That simple approach covers most questions like "how do I treat pink eye at home" and "what should I do if something is in my eye" while still keeping the unsafe cases out of home treatment.
