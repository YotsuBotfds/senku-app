## Overview

Ceramics—objects made from clay hardened by heat—have been one of humanity's most important technologies for at least 20,000 years. This guide covers **low-fire, hand-built pottery**: finding and preparing clay, hand-building techniques (coil, pinch, and slab methods), surface treatment and decoration, and firing without a permanent kiln (pit firing, open fire, bonfire kiln). No potter's wheel or constructed kiln is required.

For **kiln-fired ceramics** — wheel throwing, permanent updraft and downdraft kiln construction, glazing, brick making, and tile/pipe production — see <a href="../pottery-ceramics.html">Pottery & Ceramics</a>.

A person with clay knowledge can create a lifetime supply of cooking pots, water storage, oil lamps, and decorative objects. Ceramics represent the transition from nomadic (easy-to-transport) technologies to settled (fixed, heavy, but durable) ones.

## Identifying and Preparing Clay

The first step is finding suitable clay. Not all mud is clay; clay has specific properties that make it suitable for pottery.

### Identifying Clay Deposits

**Where to find clay:**
- Stream beds and riverbanks (clay often settles at the base of bends)
- Hillside seeps (clay deposits often seep water)
- Eroded banks (layered deposits visible in cut banks)
- Marine deposits in coastal areas (particularly good quality)
- Glacial deposits in northern regions

**How to identify clay:**
1. **Feel test:** Wet the material. True clay feels smooth and slippery, not gritty
2. **Stick test:** Clay sticks to your hands and is hard to wash off; mud washes away easily
3. **Bend test:** Roll wet clay into a rope 3 mm diameter. Clay bends around your finger without cracking; mud snaps immediately
4. **Water test:** Squeeze clay in water. It doesn't dissolve; mud will separate into particles
5. **Plastic test:** Wet clay can be molded and holds its shape; mud spreads and flattens

**Clay color varies by location:**
- Red/orange: iron oxide (hematite) rich; common
- Gray/white: kaolin (china clay) or illite; good for fine pottery
- Black: organic matter and sulfides; often very plastic but fires dark
- Brown/tan: iron and organic matter mix; very common

### Clay Preparation (Wedging)

Raw clay from the source contains rocks, roots, and air pockets that must be removed.

**Process:**
1. **Digging and initial cleanup:** Dig out clay with a shovel; remove large stones and debris by hand
2. **Soaking:** Place clay in a pit or bucket and add water; mix until the consistency is like thick peanut butter
3. **Settling:** Allow the mixture to sit for 12–24 hours; heavy particles (sand, silt) settle to the bottom; clay stays suspended
4. **Decanting:** Carefully pour off the top layer (containing clay) into a new bucket; discard the settled material
5. **Repeat settling:** You can repeat this process 2–3 times to remove more impurities
6. **Drying:** Spread the clay mixture on cloth or a stone surface; allow to dry until it reaches a firm, workable consistency (like stiff dough)

**Wedging (final preparation):**
1. Cut the dried clay into fist-sized chunks
2. Slam each chunk onto a hard surface repeatedly (10–20 times per chunk)
3. Reform into a ball and repeat; this removes air pockets and develops uniform consistency
4. The clay is ready when it's free of air bubbles and feels uniform in texture

:::warning
Air pockets in clay cause vessels to explode during firing. Thorough wedging is essential. Spend at least 10 minutes wedging before beginning a project.
:::

### Additive Materials (Temper)

Pure clay shrinks significantly during drying and firing, often cracking. Adding temper (non-plastic particles) reduces shrinkage and improves strength.

**Temper materials:**
- **Sand:** Most common; reduces shrinkage by 10–30%
- **Grit (broken pottery):** Called "grog"; excellent temper, allows drying without cracking
- **Plant fibers:** Chopped straw or plant stalks; burn out during firing but reduce shrinkage
- **Mica:** Improves durability
- **Volcanic ash:** If available; historically used

**Temper ratio:**
- 10–30% by volume is typical
- More temper reduces shrinkage but makes the clay less plastic and harder to work
- Less temper creates stronger, more waterproof vessels but requires more careful drying

**Adding temper:**
1. Mix dry temper material into clay (break temper into small particles, less than 5 mm)
2. Mix thoroughly by hand or with a tool
3. The clay should feel slightly grainy but still be plastic enough to mold

## Hand-Building Techniques

Without a wheel, hand-building remains the primary method for forming vessels. Three techniques dominate: pinch, coil, and slab.

### Pinch Pots (Smallest Vessels)

Pinch pots are made by starting with a clay ball and gradually thinning and enlarging it.

**Process:**
1. Form a ball of clay (orange-sized or smaller for beginners)
2. Poke a thumb into the center (leave 1 cm of clay at the bottom)
3. Slowly rotate the ball while pinching the walls upward and outward
4. Work gradually; exert steady, gentle pressure
5. Continue until the wall is even thickness (3–5 mm) and the desired shape is achieved
6. Refine the rim (top edge) by smoothing or creating a lip

**Advantages:**
- Faster than other methods
- Intuitive; easier for beginners
- Suitable for small vessels (cups, bowls, oil lamps)

**Disadvantages:**
- Difficult to make large vessels (more than 15 cm diameter)
- Walls are often uneven; requires experience for uniform thickness

### Coil Building (Medium and Large Vessels)

Coil building stacks rolled clay "ropes" on top of each other, creating tall, large vessels.

**Coil preparation:**
1. Roll clay on a smooth surface, applying even pressure with both hands
2. Create ropes 1–2 cm diameter and 30–50 cm long
3. Length is limited by your arm reach; make many coils before assembling

**Assembly:**
1. **Base:** Form a flat slab or pinch pot base (5–8 mm thick), 10–20 cm diameter
2. **First coil:** Score (roughen) the top surface of the base with a knife or tool
3. Apply slip (liquid clay) to the scored surface
4. Press the first coil firmly into place, overlapping slightly
5. **Subsequent coils:** Score the top surface of each coil, apply slip, and add the next coil
6. Continue spiraling upward until you reach the desired height
7. **Smoothing:** Option to smooth the joints (by scraping with a tool or wiping with a damp cloth) or leave them visible (decorative)

**Advantages:**
- Allows creation of very large vessels (1+ meter tall)
- Flexible form; can make wide, narrow, or bulbous shapes

**Disadvantages:**
- Time-consuming; large vessels require many coils
- Requires skill to keep walls straight and uniform in thickness

**Variations:**
- **Concentric coil:** Coil positioned inside the previous one (rather than on top); creates very strong, thin-walled vessels
- **Multiple simultaneous coils:** Weave 2–3 coils together before stacking; creates a lattice effect (decorative but weaker)

### Slab Building (Flat and Geometric Forms)

Slabs are sheets of clay, typically 5–10 mm thick, that are combined to create boxes, cylinders, or complex geometric forms.

**Slab creation:**
1. Spread clay on a flat surface
2. Use a wooden paddle or flat stick to beat it thin (5–10 mm)
3. Alternatively, roll it with a cylindrical object (smooth stone or wood), like rolling dough
4. Cut to desired shape (knife or sharpened stick)

**Slab assembly (box shape):**
1. Create five slabs: one base (25 × 25 cm), four walls (25 × 15 cm each), and one lid (27 × 27 cm)
2. Score all edges that will be joined
3. Apply slip to scored surfaces
4. Press walls firmly to base, supporting with props (stones, sticks) while they set
5. Create corner joints (important for strength); press coils of clay into inside corners to reinforce

**Slab assembly (cylinder):**
1. Create a rolled slab the length of desired height; wrap it into a cylinder and overlap 2–3 cm
2. Score overlapped edges, apply slip, and press firmly together
3. Create base and lid separately (pinch pot or slab form)
4. Attach with slip and reinforcing coils

**Advantages:**
- Creates geometric, controlled forms
- Good for boxes, storage vessels, and decorative pieces
- Allows for surface variation (texture, pattern)

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires careful control of slab thickness and shape
- Corners are stress points; reinforcement is critical

## Surface Finishing and Decoration

Before firing, the surface can be smoothed, patterned, or decorated.

### Surface Smoothing

**Tools for smoothing:**
- Smooth stone (river-rounded stone)
- Leather or cloth
- Bone or antler scraper
- Damp hand or sponge

**Process:**
1. Allow the clay to set until it's leather-hard (no longer sticky, but still somewhat soft)
2. Use the smoothing tool to rub the surface gently
3. Damp tools work better; water lubricates the process
4. Don't oversmooch; some texture can be desirable

### Decoration Techniques

**Incising (carving lines):**
1. Use a sharp bone or stone point
2. Create patterns (geometric lines, spirals, or representational designs)
3. Best done at leather-hard stage; harder clay produces crisp lines

**Impressing (creating texture):**
1. Press objects into the clay surface (shells, bone, stick ends, carved stamps)
2. Works well at leather-hard stage
3. Can create all-over texture or specific patterns

**Slip (liquid clay) decoration:**
1. Prepare slip: clay mixed with water to a thick paint-like consistency
2. Apply with a brush or stick
3. Can cover the whole surface or create patterns
4. Different colored clay makes a contrasting slip (red clay on buff clay, for example)

**Appliqué (attached clay shapes):**
1. Create small clay shapes (snakes, balls, petals)
2. Score and slip the surface where they'll attach
3. Press firmly into place
4. Common for ceremonial or decorative vessels

**Burnishing (polishing):**
1. Use a smooth, hard object (polished stone or shell) to rub the surface repeatedly
2. Creates a lustrous, smooth finish
3. Most effective when the clay is leather-hard or nearly dry
4. Produces a subtle shine and improves water resistance

### Finishing the Rim and Foot

**Rim (top edge):**
- Smooth by hand or tool
- Can be rounded (safety and aesthetics)
- Create a lip (inward fold) to help with pouring
- Thicken slightly for strength

**Foot (base):**
- Trim excess clay
- Smooth the bottom surface (prevents scratches to other surfaces when in use)
- Some potters create a slight ring at the base (allows the vessel to sit stably)

## Drying Before Firing

Improper drying causes cracking and explosive failure during firing. Clay must dry slowly and evenly.

**Drying timeline:**
1. **First 24 hours:** Keep in a sheltered location away from direct sun and wind
2. **Days 2–7:** Gradually increase air circulation
3. **Days 7–14:** Allow to dry completely (until it's bone-dry, feels light, and no longer cools by evaporation)

**Common drying problems:**
- **Cracking:** Caused by uneven drying; the outer surface hardens while the interior is still wet, creating stress. Fix: cover loosely with cloth to slow surface drying
- **Warping:** Causes by uneven drying or movement during handling. Fix: place on a stable surface and don't move until dry
- **Large air pockets:** Were missed during wedging; will explode during firing. Fix: drill small holes in the base to allow air to escape during firing

**Optimal conditions:**
- Shade (avoid direct sun; too-fast drying causes cracking)
- Gentle air circulation (slow drying prevents problems)
- Stable temperature (rapid temperature changes cause stress)
- Moisture-saturated environment initially (cover with cloth; uncover gradually)

## Firing Methods

Firing transforms clay into ceramic (a permanent mineral state). Different firing methods produce different results.

### Pit Firing (Simplest Method)

Pit firing requires no constructed kiln; the firing occurs in a shallow pit or above-ground fire.

**Setup:**
1. Dig a shallow pit (30 cm deep, 1 meter diameter) or use a pre-existing fire ring
2. Create a layer of wood and organic material (leaves, straw, dung) 15 cm thick
3. Arrange clay vessels on this bed (don't let them touch each other)
4. Cover with more organic material (20–30 cm layer)
5. Create an air space above vessels: prop sticks to hold the covering materials off the vessels
6. Cover the entire pit with soil (5 cm thick, with a few gaps for air)

**Firing:**
1. Light the fire at multiple points around the pit's edge
2. Allow to burn for 2–8 hours (longer for larger vessels)
3. Don't open the pit while hot; allow to cool completely (12–24 hours)
4. Carefully uncover and remove vessels

**Results:**
- Vessels reach 700–900°C (sufficient to permanently harden clay)
- Color varies based on oxygen presence: red/orange in oxygen-rich areas, black/gray in smoke-rich areas
- Often mottled, creating an aesthetic effect
- Waterproof but less durable than higher-fired ceramics

### Open Firing (Bonfire)

Vessels are placed directly in a hot fire.

**Process:**
1. Build a hot bed of coals
2. Place vessels in the hottest part of the fire
3. Surround with hot coals and wood
4. Maintain high temperature for 1–2 hours
5. Allow to cool in place

**Results:**
- Similar to pit firing; vessels reach 800–1000°C
- More oxygen-rich firing produces redder colors
- Less controlled than pit firing; more risk of uneven firing or breakage

### Bonfire Kiln (Temporary Kiln Structure)

A simple bonfire kiln uses stacked wood and clay to create an insulating chamber.

**Construction:**
1. Create a base: arrange clay vessels on the ground in a circle (diameter 2 meters)
2. Cover with large pieces of broken pottery or stone (creating an insulating layer)
3. Build a dome above using dried clay or mud bricks
4. Leave openings at the bottom for air; one or more openings at the top for draft
5. Surround the entire structure with wood (stacked tightly)

**Firing:**
1. Light the fire outside the kiln
2. Push burning wood into the bottom openings (feed continuously for 4–8 hours)
3. Smoke and heat travel through the kiln, heating vessels
4. Carefully monitor; avoid rapid changes in temperature
5. Allow to cool completely before opening

**Results:**
- More even firing than open bonfire
- Vessels can reach 1000–1100°C
- Much more reliable than pit or open firing

### Permanent Kilns

Permanent kilns (updraft, downdraft) allow repeated use, precise temperature control, and temperatures above 1100°C for stoneware and glazing. Construction and operation of permanent kilns is outside the scope of this low-fire guide. See <a href="../pottery-ceramics.html#kiln-construction">Pottery & Ceramics — Kiln Construction</a> for updraft kiln, downdraft kiln, and temperature control details.

### Firing Safety and Results

**Temperature targets:**
- 700–900°C: Basic hardening; vessels may still absorb water
- 900–1100°C: Full ceramic properties; waterproof
- 1100–1200°C: High-fired stoneware; very durable and vitrified

**Safety:**
- Wear protective gear when handling hot vessels (leather gloves, long sleeves)
- Allow vessels to cool completely; don't immerse hot ceramics in water (causes explosive shattering)
- Ensure good ventilation; firing produces carbon monoxide and smoke
- Never fire indoors without proper chimney and ventilation

**Post-firing inspection:**
- Check for cracks or weakness
- Test water vessels by filling and monitoring for leaks (some minor seepage is normal initially)
- Use only after confirming stability

## Troubleshooting Pottery Problems

**Problem: Vessel cracks while drying**
- Cause: Too-fast drying; uneven thickness
- Fix: Slow drying by covering loosely; ensure even wall thickness during forming

**Problem: Vessel breaks during firing**
- Cause: Air pockets in clay; thermal shock; uneven temperature during firing
- Fix: Thorough wedging before forming; slow heating and cooling; monitor firing carefully

**Problem: Fired vessel is porous and leaks water**
- Cause: Not fired hot enough; inadequate clay preparation (too much sand)
- Fix: Use higher firing temperature if possible; reduce temper amount; add burnishing or slip to make water-resistant

**Problem: Surface is uneven or rough**
- Cause: Poor clay preparation; inadequate smoothing
- Fix: Better clay purification; more thorough surface finishing before firing

**Problem: Vessel warps out of shape**
- Cause: Uneven drying; handling during leather-hard stage
- Fix: More stable drying environment; less movement during the critical phase; stiffer walls if making large vessels

:::tip
Keep detailed records of successful firings: temperature (approximate), duration, fuel type, and results. This helps you predict and improve outcomes over time. Successful potters develop an intuitive sense for the process through careful observation and documentation.
:::

### Everyday Repair and Sealing for Fired Vessels

If a fired pot cracks or leaks, you don't always need to remake it:

- **Single crack, no missing pieces:** Clean the crack, press heated pine pitch into the gap, smooth, and let cool. Pine pitch hardens into a durable, water-tight seal.
- **Slow seepage through porous walls:** Melt beeswax and brush a thin coat on the interior. Let harden. Repeat if needed. This is the standard field fix for earthenware water jugs.
- **Multiple cracks or shattered:** Don't repair for liquid use. Crush into grog (temper for new clay) and make a replacement vessel.

For full repair techniques (adhesive join, riveting) and sealing methods (beeswax, pine pitch, clay slip), see <a href="../pottery-ceramics.html">Pottery & Ceramics</a> — repair-reuse section, and <a href="../storage-containers-vessels.html">Storage Container and Vessel Making</a> — sealing methods.

<section id="conclusion">

## Conclusion

Pottery production opens access to durable, fireproof containers for storage, cooking, and water management—transforming a community's self-sufficiency. From simple pinch pots to coiled storage jars, the techniques in this guide require only clay (available in most locations) and heat (fire). Begin with simple pit-fired vessels, gain confidence and skill, and advance toward more complex forms and kiln structures. Over months and years, ceramic production becomes as essential to settlement as agriculture or animal husbandry, creating the foundation for lasting civilization.
