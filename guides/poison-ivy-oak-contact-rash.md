<section id="overview">

## Overview: What Poison Ivy and Poison Oak Actually Do

Poison ivy, poison oak, and poison sumac cause an **allergic contact dermatitis**. The rash is triggered by **urushiol**, an oil on the plant that sticks to skin, clothing, tools, shoes, pets, and almost any surface it touches.

If you think you were exposed recently, the first step is to **wash the oil off now** and clean anything that may still carry it. If you are still trying to identify the plant or prevent future exposure, use [hazardous-plants-avoidance-identification](./hazardous-plants-avoidance-identification.md) instead.

The most important point is this: **the rash itself does not keep "spreading" from one spot to another once the oil is removed**. New patches often appear over time because:

- Different parts of the skin got different amounts of urushiol
- Oil remained under nails, on clothes, on gear, or on pets
- The reaction shows up later in places that were exposed more lightly

That means the best early treatment is **washing off the oil fast** and cleaning everything that may still carry it.

Common symptoms include:

- Itching
- Redness
- Small bumps or streaks
- Fluid-filled blisters
- Swelling, especially on thin skin like eyelids or genitals

The rash usually starts **12 to 48 hours after exposure**, but it can appear later if the exposure was light or repeated.

</section>

<section id="after-exposure">

## I Touched Poison Ivy. What Do I Do Right Now?

If exposure may have happened within the last few hours, act immediately:

1. Remove contaminated clothing, gloves, belts, shoes, and gear.
2. Wash skin with **lukewarm water and soap** as soon as possible.
3. Wash under fingernails carefully.
4. Rinse well.
5. Repeat washing if there is any chance oil is still present.

If you have not started to rash yet, this still helps. **Earlier is better**, but late washing is still worth doing because oil can stay on skin and objects.

If soap is not available, wash with water anyway. Soap works better because it helps lift the oil off the skin instead of just moving it around.

### Best washing approach

- Use lukewarm or cool water
- Use regular soap, dish soap, or detergent-safe skin cleanser
- Wash gently but thoroughly
- Pay close attention to hands, wrists, forearms, neck, ankles, and under rings or watches
- Clean under nails with a brush or nail stick

Avoid very hot water at first. Hot water can irritate skin and make itching feel worse.

</section>

<section id="spreading">

## How Do I Stop It From Spreading?

The short answer is: **remove the oil source**.

If the rash is appearing in more places, the usual causes are contaminated objects or delayed reaction, not the rash "infecting" new skin.

To stop new areas from showing up:

- Wash skin promptly and again if needed
- Do not touch other body parts until your hands are clean
- Change clothes immediately
- Clean tools, phone cases, watch bands, and shoes
- Wash pets if they may have brushed against the plant
- Clean anything that touched the plant before you touched your face, eyes, or other skin

Once urushiol is off the skin, **blister fluid does not spread poison ivy to other people**. The main risk is still oil residue on skin or objects.

</section>

<section id="home-care">

## Home Care After Exposure

If there are no urgent warning signs, most poison ivy and poison oak rashes are treated at home by calming the skin and preventing more urushiol contact.

Use the next sections for the main home-care steps:

- Itch relief
- Blister care
- Avoiding the common mistakes that make the rash worse

If the rash is on the face or near the eyes, is very widespread, or comes with trouble breathing or major swelling, skip home care and go to the urgent warning signs section.

</section>

<section id="clothes-tools">

## Contaminated Clothes, Tools, and Gear

Anything that touched the plant can keep causing rash until it is cleaned.

### Clothes and laundry

- Put contaminated clothes in a separate pile or bag
- Wear gloves if you have to handle them before washing
- Wash them separately from regular laundry
- Use detergent and the warmest water the fabric can safely handle
- Run a second rinse if you want extra cleanup
- Dry them normally after washing

If the item is delicate or dry-clean only, tell the cleaner that it may contain **urushiol** from poison ivy or poison oak. Do not just toss it into a shared hamper with clean laundry.

After handling dirty clothes, wash your hands and forearms again.

### Tools, shoes, and hard surfaces

For non-porous items such as tools, handles, phones, car keys, and garden equipment:

- Wear gloves while cleaning
- Wash with soap and water or detergent
- Wipe down any surface that may have had plant sap or oil on it
- Let the item dry before reuse

For porous items such as leather gloves, straps, or some shoe materials, cleaning may be harder. If you cannot clean them well, they may need to be set aside or discarded.

### Pets

Pets can carry urushiol in their fur even if they do not get a rash.

- Wear gloves when washing a pet after exposure
- Use pet shampoo or mild soap and water
- Rinse well
- Clean collars, harnesses, and bedding separately

</section>

<section id="itch-relief">

## Itch Relief That Actually Helps

The rash is often very itchy. The goal is to calm the skin without making it worse.

### Good options

- Cool compresses for 15 to 20 minutes at a time
- Colloidal oatmeal baths or soaks
- Calamine lotion
- 1% hydrocortisone cream on intact skin
- Loose, breathable clothing
- Keeping fingernails short to reduce skin damage from scratching

### Oral antihistamines

Oral antihistamines can help some people sleep when itching is bad, even though they do not remove the urushiol.

- Non-drowsy options are better for daytime use
- Sedating options may help at night if itching is keeping you awake

If you use any medicine, follow the package directions and avoid doubling up on products that contain the same ingredient.

### When itch treatment is not enough

If the rash is widespread, severe, or involving sensitive skin, a clinician may recommend prescription treatment such as a steroid medicine. The earlier severe cases are evaluated, the better.

</section>

<section id="blisters">

## Blister Care

Blisters are common and do not mean the rash is infected.

What to do:

- Leave blisters intact if you can
- Do not pop them
- Keep the area clean and dry
- Use cool compresses
- Cover weeping areas with a clean, non-stick dressing if needed
- Change dressings if they become wet or dirty

If a blister opens on its own, wash the area gently with soap and water, pat dry, and cover lightly.

Watch for infection if you see:

- Increasing pain
- Pus
- Spreading redness
- Warmth
- Fever

</section>

<section id="what-not-to-do">

## What Not to Do

Avoid common mistakes that make the rash worse:

- Do not scratch if you can help it
- Do not scrub the skin hard
- Do not use bleach, gasoline, or harsh solvents on skin
- Do not assume the rash is "still spreading" from blister fluid
- Do not burn poison ivy, poison oak, or poison sumac
- Do not reuse contaminated clothing or gloves without washing them
- Do not forget pets, car seats, tools, and shoe laces

Smoke from burning Toxicodendron plants can cause dangerous exposure and severe respiratory irritation. If plant material was burned and you inhaled smoke, that is a different and more urgent problem than a skin rash.

</section>

<section id="red-flags">

## Urgent Warning Signs That Need Evaluation

Get medical evaluation if any of the following apply:

- Trouble breathing, wheezing, throat tightness, or mouth swelling
- Rash involving the eyes, eyelids, lips, face, or genitals
- Severe swelling that limits opening the eyes or moving a hand/foot
- Rash covering a large area of the body
- Signs of infection such as pus, fever, or worsening pain
- Rash that is not improving after about 1 to 2 weeks
- Severe itching or swelling that makes sleep, eating, or daily function difficult
- Exposure in a child, older adult, or someone with a weaker immune system when the rash is extensive

Seek urgent care sooner rather than later if the rash is on the face or near the eyes. Those areas can swell quickly.

If breathing is affected, treat it as an emergency.

</section>

<section id="quick-answers">

## Quick Answers

**I touched poison ivy. What do I do?**
Wash skin now with soap and lukewarm water, change clothes, and clean anything that may still have oil on it.

**How do I stop it from spreading?**
Remove the oil from skin, clothing, tools, pets, and surfaces. The rash itself is not contagious once the oil is gone.

**How should I wash contaminated clothes?**
Wash them separately with detergent, then dry normally. Clean gloves, shoes, and gear too.

**Can I pop the blisters?**
No. Leave them intact unless they open on their own.

**When should I get checked?**
If the rash is on the face or eyes, if swelling is severe, if you have trouble breathing, or if the rash is getting infected or very widespread.
