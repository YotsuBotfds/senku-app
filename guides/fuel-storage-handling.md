:::danger
**Fire and Explosion Hazard**
Improper fuel storage causes fires, explosions, and toxic exposure. Liquid fuels (gasoline, kerosene, diesel) produce flammable vapors that ignite from sparks, static discharge, or heat — even at a distance from the container. Never store fuel indoors or near ignition sources. Use only approved containers with proper venting. Biogas (methane) is explosive at 5–15% air concentration and is odorless in pure form. Wood gasification produces carbon monoxide, which is lethal in enclosed spaces.
:::

<section id="overview">

## Overview

Reliable fuel supply determines whether a settlement has heat, power, and cooking capability. Fuel storage and handling encompasses managing firewood and biomass, producing charcoal and biogas, storing liquid fuels safely, and maintaining fuel quality over months or years. Proper storage prevents spoilage, contamination, and dangerous conditions while maximizing available energy.

A settlement with 12+ months of stored fuel and the ability to produce more can weather supply disruptions and maintain essential services year-round.

</section>

<section id="firewood-management">

## Firewood: Selection, Storage, and Quality

Wood is the most common fuel in low-technology settings.

### Selecting Firewood

**Ideal fuel wood characteristics:**
- Hard, dense wood (oak, ash, hickory, birch)
- Low moisture content (dry wood)
- Splits relatively easily
- Produces good heat (low ash content)

**Wood to avoid:**
- Softwoods (pine, spruce): produce excessive smoke and creosote buildup
- Wet wood: smolders instead of burning; produces smoke and little heat
- Treated or painted wood: produces toxic fumes
- Diseased wood: can harbor pests or disease

### Firewood Seasoning (Drying)

**Drying process:**
1. Cut wood to length (25–45 cm) immediately after felling
2. Split into pieces (allows faster drying; creates more surface area)
3. Stack in a well-ventilated location (not in direct rain)
4. Stack with good air circulation between pieces (stack as a lattice, not tightly)
5. Allow to dry for 6–12 months (less in warm, dry climates; more in humid areas)

**Determining if wood is dry:**
- Moisture meter: ideal; shows exact moisture content (goal: under 20%)
- Visual inspection: dry wood has cracks radiating from the center (checking)
- Weight: freshly cut wood is heavier; dry wood is noticeably lighter
- Sound: strike two pieces together; dry wood makes a sharp clap; wet wood makes a dull thud

**Accelerating drying:**
- Cover the top and sides of the stack (but leave the ends open for air circulation)
- This protects from rain while allowing ventilation
- Stack in a sunny location (sun hastens drying)

### Firewood Storage

**Storage location:**
- Dry, sheltered area (not on the wet ground)
- Good air circulation (prevents mold growth)
- Protected from rain but not completely enclosed (promotes air circulation)
- Near where wood will be used (minimizes transportation)

**Storage configuration:**
- Stack neatly (utilizes space efficiently)
- Stack on wood or stone (off the ground; prevents rot)
- Height: 1.5–2 meters (stable and accessible)
- Width: 1–1.5 meters (too wide becomes unstable)
- Length: variable (can be very long to save space)

**Volume calculation:**
- A "cord" of wood is a standard measure: 128 cubic feet (stacked in a 4'×4'×8' pile)
- One cord heats a typical household for 2–4 weeks (depending on climate and efficiency)
- A settlement of 100 people needs 1000+ cords annually in cold climates; 200–300 cords in warm climates

**Inventory management:**
- Track wood supply: know how many cords you have
- Rotate stock (use older wood first)
- Plan harvesting/processing schedule year-round
- Monitor consumption relative to remaining stock

### Fire Safety with Wood

**Chimney and flue maintenance:**
- Clean chimneys regularly (every 1–2 weeks during burning season)
- Remove creosote buildup (built up from smoke; flammable)
- Prevent chimney fires by burning dry wood only

**Wood storage safety:**
- Keep wood away from heat sources and sparks
- Don't store wood in enclosed areas with living spaces (fire hazard)
- Keep clear areas around wood piles (prevent fire spread)

## Charcoal Production and Use

Charcoal is wood that has been partially burned (carbonized) in low-oxygen conditions. It burns hotter and cleaner than raw wood.

### Charcoal Production Method (Earth Kiln)

**Simple earth kiln design:**
1. Dig a shallow pit (30 cm deep, 1–2 meters diameter)
2. Create a wood dome over the pit:
   - Stack firewood in a dome shape (like an igloo)
   - Leave a small opening at the top and side
3. Cover the dome with earth (5–10 cm thick)
4. Leave the bottom gaps open (for air circulation)

**Burning process:**
1. Light the fire through the side opening
2. Maintain a slow burn (smoldering, not flaming)
3. Wood gradually converts to charcoal (outer layers burn slowly, interior is protected)
4. Burn for 6–24 hours (depending on wood size and desired charcoal quality)
5. Close all openings when charcoal is formed (this stops the burn)
6. Allow to cool completely (12–24 hours)

**Yield:**
- Raw wood: ~30% by weight converts to charcoal (losses are gases and tar)
- Example: 100 kg of wood yields ~30 kg of charcoal

**Advantages:**
- Charcoal produces more heat than raw wood per unit weight
- Cleaner burning (less smoke, easier to control temperature)
- Valuable for cooking, heating, and metalworking

### Charcoal Uses and Storage

**Applications:**
- Cooking (intense heat; good for outdoor grills and ovens)
- Heating (slow-burning, long-lasting)
- Metalworking (reaches higher temperatures than raw wood)
- Water filtration (charcoal filters impurities)
- Gunpowder production (contains carbon)

**Storage:**
- Keep completely dry (moisture causes deterioration)
- Store in sealed containers (protects from weather)
- Keep away from moisture sources
- Long storage life if kept dry (years or decades)

**Volume measurement:**
- Charcoal is lighter than wood; less volume needed for equivalent heat
- 1 kg of charcoal ~= 3 kg of raw wood in heating value
- Estimate: 20–30 kg of charcoal annually per person (depending on use)

## Liquid Fuel Storage and Safety

Oil, diesel, and other liquid fuels require careful storage to prevent loss and contamination.

### Fuel Storage Containers

**Container materials:**
- **Steel drums:** Traditional; rust-prone if sealed improperly
- **Plastic (HDPE):** For non-petroleum fuels (ethanol, biodiesel); petroleum can degrade plastic
- **Ceramic or glass:** Inert; excellent for long-term storage but fragile
- **Underground tanks:** Protect from weather; risk contamination if they leak

**Container specifications:**
- Airtight seals (prevent evaporation and oxidation)
- UV protection (light degrades fuel)
- Temperature control (extreme heat causes expansion/loss; extreme cold can cause separation)
- Drainage (water accumulates at the bottom; drain regularly)

### Fuel Types and Storage Requirements

**Firewood oil/lamp oil:**
- Mineral oil commonly used
- Stores indefinitely if sealed and protected from light
- Keep cool (excessive heat causes oxidation)
- Drain any accumulated water regularly

**Diesel fuel:**
- Biodegrades slowly even in proper storage (2–5 year shelf life)
- Add stabilizer to extend shelf life
- Drain water accumulation (diesel readily absorbs water)
- Filter before use (if any sediment accumulates)

**Gasoline:**
- Short shelf life (6 months to 2 years)
- Ethanol blends degrade faster than pure gasoline
- Add fuel stabilizer for longer storage
- Extremely volatile (fire hazard; handle with care)
- Store in cool, well-ventilated location

**Biodiesel:**
- Shorter shelf life than diesel (can degrade in 6–12 months)
- More prone to microbial growth (bacteria in water)
- Use biocides if storing longer than a few months
- Must be used more quickly than petroleum diesel

### Fuel Safety

**Fire prevention:**
- Store fuel away from ignition sources (flames, sparks, hot surfaces)
- Store in well-ventilated area (fumes can accumulate)
- Use appropriate containers (sealing prevents explosive vapor buildup)
- Keep fire suppression equipment nearby (sand, water, fire extinguisher)

**Health and environmental:**
- Never store fuel near water sources (spill contamination)
- Keep fuel away from living areas (vapor inhalation hazard)
- Properly dispose of old, degraded fuel (don't pour down drains)
- Prevent spills (contain and clean immediately)

### Inventory Tracking

**Recording fuel use:**
- Track how much fuel is consumed monthly
- Record seasonal variations
- Estimate future needs based on historical use
- Plan replenishment schedule before stock runs out

## Biogas Production from Organic Waste

Biogas (primarily methane) is produced when bacteria decompose organic matter without oxygen.

### Anaerobic Digester Design (Simplified)

**Basic system:**
1. Sealed container (tank, drum, or specially built chamber)
2. Feed organic waste (animal manure, food scraps, plant material) mixed with water
3. Sealed environment (no air) encourages anaerobic bacteria
4. Gas outlet (captures methane produced)
5. Digestate outlet (removed processed waste)

**Tank specifications:**
- Volume: 2–10 liters per kg daily feed (depends on feed type and temperature)
- Airtight (essential for anaerobic conditions)
- Thermally insulated (bacteria work better warm; 30–40°C is ideal)
- Heater (if needed to maintain temperature)
- Gas collection system (piping from top of tank)

### Feeding and Management

**Feed materials (feedstock):**
- Animal manure: cow, pig, or chicken manure (excellent source)
- Food waste: vegetable scraps, fruit, cooked food (good source)
- Plant material: grass, leaves, paper (slower to decompose but usable)
- Mixed feed: combination is often better than single source

**Feed rate:**
- Start slowly (allow bacteria to establish)
- 1–2 kg per day per cubic meter of digester volume (typical)
- Too much feed overwhelms the system (stops biogas production)
- Too little feed: underutilizes the digester

**Retention time:**
- The time feedstock stays in the digester before exiting
- 20–40 days typical (longer in cold climates)
- Longer retention: more biogas produced per unit feedstock
- Temperature affects retention time (cold requires longer)

### Gas Production and Use

**Biogas composition:**
- 50–70% methane (the useful fuel)
- 30–50% carbon dioxide
- Small amounts of hydrogen sulfide (causes odor and corrosion)

**Gas production rate:**
- Highly variable depending on feedstock and temperature
- Animal manure: 0.3–0.5 liters per liter of digestate daily
- Food waste: 0.5–1.0 liters per liter of digestate daily
- Rough estimate: 50–200 kg feedstock per day produces enough biogas to cook for a family

**Gas usage:**
- Cooking (high-temperature flame; similar to natural gas)
- Heating water
- Lighting (via biogas lamp)
- Small engines (modified to run on biogas)

**Gas storage:**
- Store in flexible bladder tanks or inverted drums
- Pressure regulator to control delivery rate
- Pipe biogas to appliances
- Safety relief valve (prevents overpressure)

### System Challenges

**Temperature control:**
- Bacteria are most active 30–40°C
- In cold climates, heating the digester becomes energy-intensive
- In warm climates, maintaining stable temperature is easier

**Odor control:**
- Hydrogen sulfide produces rotten-egg smell
- Dosing with iron oxide reduces sulfides
- Proper sealing contains odors

**Monitoring and adjustment:**
- Track gas production (indicates system health)
- Monitor pH (bacteria prefer neutral; acidity indicates problems)
- Adjust feed rate if gas production drops

:::info-box
**Biogas advantages:** Converts waste to useful fuel; reduces methane emissions (which would otherwise escape to atmosphere); produces nutrient-rich digestate suitable for agriculture. **Disadvantages:** Requires consistent feedstock; temperature-dependent; requires some technical management.
:::

## Wood Gasification Systems

Wood gasification converts solid wood into combustible gas (synthesis gas), allowing use in engines and appliances.

### Basic Wood Gasifier Design

**Components:**
1. **Combustion chamber:** Where wood burns in limited oxygen
2. **Reduction zone:** Hot chamber where combustion gases react with hot charcoal
3. **Cooling zone:** Gas cools and tar condenses
4. **Filter:** Removes tar and particulates
5. **Gas outlet:** Clean gas to engine or appliance

**Simplified process:**
1. Feed wood pieces into combustion chamber
2. Limited air (controlled oxygen) causes wood to burn slowly
3. This produces combustible gas rather than just heat
4. Gas is cooled and filtered
5. Resulting gas can power engines or be burned for heat

**Advantages:**
- Converts low-grade wood (twigs, waste, smaller pieces) into useful fuel
- More efficient than burning wood directly
- Gas can be transported via pipes (like natural gas)

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires sophisticated construction
- Complex operation and maintenance
- Tar production requires regular filter cleaning
- Produces carbon monoxide; never operate in enclosed spaces. See [`smoke-inhalation-carbon-monoxide-fire-gas-exposure.md`](smoke-inhalation-carbon-monoxide-fire-gas-exposure.md) for recognition and field treatment of CO exposure.

### Practical Gasifier System

A simple, practical design suitable for heating:

1. Build an insulated steel box (30 cm × 30 cm × 60 cm tall)
2. Create air inlet at the bottom (controllable valve)
3. Create gas outlet at the top (connected to cooler/filter)
4. Feed wood pieces into the top
5. Light wood in combustion chamber
6. Control air intake to maintain slow, efficient burn
7. Collect gas for use in heater or other appliance

**Performance:**
- 1 kg of wood produces ~3–4 cubic meters of combustible gas
- This gas burns in a stove/heater producing considerable heat
- More efficient than direct wood burning (70% efficiency vs. 40%)

## Fuel Inventory and Long-Term Planning

### Calculating Fuel Needs

**Annual consumption estimation:**
1. For heating: climate determines need (cold regions: 50–100 cords annually; warm regions: 10–20 cords)
2. For cooking: 5–10 kg charcoal or 2–4 cords firewood per household annually
3. For power generation: highly variable (depends on generator type and use)
4. For industrial use: specific to application

**Seasonal variation:**
- Winter heating: much higher consumption
- Summer: minimal heating; possibly irrigation pumping needs
- Plan fuel supply around these variations

### Storage Capacity

**Calculate storage needed:**
- 12–18 months supply is prudent (accounts for bad harvesting years)
- Example: family consuming 4 cords annually should maintain 6–8 cords in storage
- Community of 100 people: maintain 1200+ cords (allowing for 12-month supply at 10 cords/person annually in cold climate)

**Storage location:**
- Central depot (protects community fuel supply)
- Multiple smaller stores (redundancy; if one burns, others remain)
- Safe from fire and weather
- Good inventory tracking

### Year-Round Fuel Production

**Firewood:**
- Harvest in fall/winter (trees are easier to fell; less risk of disturbing animals)
- Dry for winter (previous year's cutting is ready for winter use)
- Continuous cutting and processing throughout the year (produces next year's fuel)

**Charcoal:**
- Produce continuously in small batches (steady supply)
- Production increases in fall/winter (when charcoal demand peaks)
- Store carefully to maintain quality

**Biogas:**
- Continuous production (suitable feedstock always available)
- Production declines in cold weather (slower bacterial action)
- Plan storage for peak production season; draw down during winter

**Wood gasification:**
- Produce as needed (systems typically small-scale)
- Feed with scrap wood and small branches (adds value to otherwise-waste material)

### Contingency Planning

**Emergency fuel reserves:**
- Maintain fuel specifically for emergencies (kept sealed and protected)
- Use rotating stock (consume emergency reserve, replace with new fuel regularly)
- Separate from main inventory (prevents accidental use of emergency supply)

**Supply disruption response:**
- If normal fuel sources fail (harvest disaster, supply route disrupted):
  - Draw from emergency reserve
  - Immediately increase production rate
  - Implement fuel conservation (reduce consumption, prioritize essential uses)
  - Activate backup fuel sources (charcoal production from tree scraps, biogas from waste, etc.)

## Troubleshooting Fuel Problems

**Problem: Firewood doesn't burn well; produces excessive smoke**
- Cause: Wood is wet; wrong species
- Fix: Ensure wood is fully dried; use hardwoods instead of softwoods

**Problem: Charcoal production is very low yield**
- Cause: Kiln is open to air (oxygen entering); wood was wet
- Fix: Better seal the kiln; use dry wood; extend burning time

**Problem: Diesel fuel becomes cloudy in cold weather**
- Cause: Paraffin wax solidifying (normal in cold climates)
- Fix: Add cold-flow improver; store in warmer location; use biodiesel blend

**Problem: Biogas production stops or becomes very low**
- Cause: Temperature drop; insufficient feedstock; system is overloaded
- Fix: Heat the digester if possible; increase or change feedstock type; reduce feed rate temporarily to reset

**Problem: Persistent tar smell from biogas (hydrogen sulfide)**
- Cause: Untreated biogas from digester
- Fix: Add iron oxide to digester; improve gas scrubbing/treatment before use

:::tip
Fuel management is critical to long-term settlement viability. Invest time in systematic inventory tracking, diversified fuel sources, and contingency planning. A fuel crisis can cripple a community more quickly than almost any other failure.
:::

## Conclusion

Reliable fuel supply is the backbone of settlement resilience. Whether using traditional firewood, producing charcoal, capturing biogas from waste, or deploying more sophisticated systems like wood gasifiers, understanding fuel characteristics, storage, and management allows year-round energy security. Invest in multiple fuel sources (redundancy), maintain substantial reserves (at least 12 months), and practice consistent inventory management. A settlement with abundant, well-managed fuel is positioned to weather disruptions and maintain essential services regardless of external circumstances.
