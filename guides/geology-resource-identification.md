## Overview

Geology is the study of Earth's materials and structures. For survival and self-sufficiency, understanding geology enables you to locate water, identify useful minerals and ores, assess soil quality, select building materials, and avoid geological hazards. This guide focuses on practical field identification methods—using only simple tools (hammer, magnifying glass, pocket knife) and no electronics. You will learn to identify minerals by their properties, classify rocks by origin and composition, and interpret landscape features to locate resources and assess their practical value.

A geologist becomes a naturalist and prospector, reading the landscape as an open book. Where others see random stone, a trained eye sees history, resources, and opportunity.

## Mineral Identification in the Field

A mineral is a naturally occurring, crystalline solid with a fixed chemical composition. Every mineral has distinctive properties that allow identification without specialized equipment.

### Mohs Hardness Scale

Hardness indicates resistance to scratching. The Mohs scale ranks 1 (softest) to 10 (hardest). To test hardness:

1. Attempt to scratch the mineral with items of known hardness (fingernail, copper coin, steel blade, etc.)
2. Attempt to scratch items with the mineral
3. Note the highest item that scratches the mineral, and the lowest item scratched by the mineral
4. Cross-reference to the Mohs scale

<div class="hardness-scale">
<strong>Mohs Hardness Scale:</strong>
<ul style="margin: 8px 0; padding-left: 20px;">
<li>1 (Talc): Can be scratched by fingernail</li>
<li>2 (Gypsum): Fingernail scratches easily</li>
<li>3 (Calcite): Copper coin scratches easily</li>
<li>4 (Fluorite): Copper coin scratches with moderate pressure</li>
<li>5 (Apatite): Copper coin scratches with difficulty; steel blade scratches easily</li>
<li>6 (Feldspar): Steel blade scratches slowly; will scratch glass</li>
<li>7 (Quartz): Scratches glass; difficult to scratch with steel blade</li>
<li>8 (Topaz): Scratches quartz; scratches glass easily</li>
<li>9 (Corundum): Scratches topaz; very hard</li>
<li>10 (Diamond): Hardest natural mineral; scratches all others</li>
</ul>
</div>

### Streak Test

Streak is the color of a mineral's powder, which differs from the bulk color. Streak is revealed by scratching the mineral on an unglazed ceramic tile (or a rough rock surface).

**Key minerals by streak:**

| Mineral | Bulk Color | Streak | Identification Notes |
|---------|-----------|--------|----------------------|
| Magnetite | Black | Black | Magnetic (attracts compass needle) |
| Hematite | Red, gray, or black | Red | Primary iron ore; heavy; metallic luster |
| Limonite | Yellow-brown | Yellow-brown | Iron oxide; forms in oxidized zones |
| Galena | Metallic gray | Gray | Lead ore; very dense; cubic cleavage |
| Chalcopyrite | Brass-yellow | Greenish-black | Copper ore; softer than pyrite |
| Pyrite | Pale brass-yellow | Greenish-black | "Fool's gold"; harder than chalcopyrite |
| Graphite | Gray-black | Gray-black | Leaves mark on paper; metallic feel |
| Gold | Yellow | Yellow | Soft; very dense; malleable |
| Azurite | Blue | Blue | Copper mineral; soft; forms malachite |
| Malachite | Green | Green | Copper mineral; banded; less hard than azurite |

:::info-box
**Streak reveals composition.** Red streak indicates iron content (hematite, limonite). Blue streak suggests copper (azurite, malachite). Green indicates copper or nickel. This narrows identification dramatically.
:::

### Luster

Luster describes how light reflects off the mineral's surface. Common lusters:

- **Metallic:** Shiny, reflective like metal (galena, hematite, magnetite, copper, gold)
- **Vitreous (glassy):** Clear, shiny like glass (quartz, feldspar, beryl)
- **Adamantine:** Very shiny like diamond (diamond, corundum, rare earth minerals)
- **Resinous:** Shiny like resin or plastic (sulfur, sphalerite)
- **Silky:** Smooth shine like silk fabric (fibrous minerals, talc)
- **Dull/Earthy:** No shine; powdery (clay, limonite, bauxite)
- **Pearly:** Iridescent, like pearl (mica, fluorite on cleavage faces)

**Field use:** Metallic luster typically indicates ore minerals (potentially valuable). Vitreous luster often indicates quartz or feldspar (common, but quartz is hard and useful for tools).

### Cleavage and Fracture

Cleavage is the tendency to break along flat planes aligned with the crystal structure. Fracture is irregular breaking without planes.

**Cleavage patterns:**

| Mineral | Cleavage | Property |
|---------|----------|----------|
| Mica | Perfect parallel sheets | Sheets peel like paper |
| Feldspar | Two perfect perpendicular directions | Rectangular pieces |
| Galena | Cubic—three perpendicular directions | Cubic chunks |
| Calcite | Three directions at angles (rhombohedral) | Rhombic fragments |
| Quartz | No cleavage (conchoidal fracture) | Breaks into curved chips like glass |

**Field identification:** Tap the mineral gently with a hammer. Minerals with cleavage break into regular shapes (sheets, cubes, rhombic pieces). Minerals without cleavage (like quartz) break into irregular shapes with curved surfaces (conchoidal fracture, similar to broken glass).

:::warning
Never hammer a mineral on concrete or paved surfaces—chips may ricochet dangerously. Hammer over a cloth or plastic bag to contain fragments, and wear safety glasses.
:::

### Color and Other Properties

Color is useful but not definitive—many minerals occur in multiple colors due to trace impurities. However, color combined with other properties narrows identification:

**Magnetic properties:** Pass a compass near the mineral. Magnetite attracts a compass needle strongly; hematite weakly. Most other minerals are non-magnetic.

**Density (relative weight):** Hold similar-sized specimens. Ore minerals are typically much heavier than their size suggests. Galena, hematite, and magnetite are notably heavy; quartz and feldspar are lighter.

**Transparency:** Hold the mineral toward light. Transparent minerals (quartz, calcite) allow light through; translucent minerals (mica, jade) let light through but are not clear; opaque minerals (hematite, galena) block light.

**Reaction to acid:** Carbonate minerals (calcite, azurite, malachite) effervesce (bubble) when a dilute acid (vinegar) is applied. This is a quick field test for carbonates.

## Rock Type Classification

Rocks are aggregates of minerals. The three rock types reflect their formation process: igneous (from magma), sedimentary (from compressed sediment), and metamorphic (from existing rocks altered by heat/pressure).

### Igneous Rocks

Formed from cooling magma. If magma cools slowly underground, large crystals form (plutonic rocks). If magma erupts and cools quickly, tiny crystals or glass forms (volcanic rocks).

**Common igneous rocks:**

| Rock | Crystal Size | Appearance | Common Minerals | Practical Use |
|------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|---------------|
| Granite | Large crystals | Speckled, grainy, hard | Quartz, feldspar, mica, magnetite | Building stone; grinding tools |
| Basalt | Tiny crystals | Black, fine-grained, dense | Pyroxene, magnetite, plagioclase | Aggregate; lava rock for tools |
| Obsidian | Glassy, amorphous | Black, translucent, very sharp | Volcanic glass (no crystals) | Cutting tools (sharper than flint) |
| Pumice | Vesicular (porous) | Light, floats, bubbly texture | Volcanic glass, minerals | Grinding stone; polishing |
| Andesite | Fine crystals | Gray, grainy, moderate density | Plagioclase, pyroxene | Aggregate; construction |

**Field identification:** Look for crystal size and color. Granite is light-colored and grainy; basalt is black and fine; obsidian is dark and glassy.

### Sedimentary Rocks

Formed from compressed sediment (sand, mud, shells, pebbles). Sedimentary rocks are softer than igneous and often show layering.

**Common sedimentary rocks:**

| Rock | Composition | Appearance | Practical Use |
|------|------------|-----------|---------------|
| Sandstone | Sand-sized quartz grains cemented | Grainy surface, soft, often tan/red | Building stone; whetstone (if fine-grained) |
| Shale | Clay and silt compressed | Layered, breaks into sheets, soft | Clay source (for ceramics); poor building stone |
| Limestone | Calcite or shell fragments | White/gray, hard, effervesce with acid | Mortar; fertilizer; building stone |
| Conglomerate | Pebbles/gravel cemented together | Visible rounded pebbles in matrix | Coarse aggregate |
| Coal | Compressed plant material | Black, soft, burns, poor strength | Fuel; carbon source |

**Field identification:** Scratch with a knife—sedimentary rocks are softer than granite. Look for visible grains or shells. Test with acid (limestone effervesces).

### Metamorphic Rocks

Formed from existing rocks altered by heat, pressure, and chemical change deep in the earth. Metamorphic rocks are often banded or foliated (layered).

**Common metamorphic rocks:**

| Rock | Parent Rock | Appearance | Practical Use |
|------|-----------|-----------|---------------|
| Slate | Shale | Foliated, breaks into sheets, dark | Roofing tiles; paving stones |
| Marble | Limestone | Crystalline, white or colored, harder than limestone | Sculpture; building stone |
| Schist | Various | Foliated, shiny (mica), wavy layers | Roofing; less durable than slate |
| Gneiss | Granite or igneous rocks | Banded, stripes of light/dark minerals | Building stone; less workable than granite |
| Quartzite | Sandstone | Hard, non-foliated, white/gray | Tool stone (harder than quartz) |

**Field identification:** Look for foliation (stripes/layers) or banding. Metamorphic rocks are often harder than their parent rocks. Slate breaks cleanly into sheets; marble is crystalline.

:::info-box
**Quick rock classification:** Strike the rock with a hammer. Igneous rocks break into jagged chunks; sedimentary rocks crumble or break irregularly; metamorphic rocks often break along foliation planes (sheets or bands).
:::

## Resource Implications of Geology

A trained eye reading the landscape can predict where to find water, useful minerals, clay, and building materials.

### Ore and Mineral Deposits

**Primary indicators of ore deposits:**

1. **Bright colors:** Red (hematite), green (copper minerals), blue (azurite), yellow (sulfur, limonite) indicate concentrated minerals. Barren rock is typically gray or brown.

2. **Oxidized zones (gossans):** Outcrops with bright colors and soft, crumbly texture near ore deposits. Oxidation occurs when sulfide ore (underneath) weathers.

3. **Linear discoloration:** Streaks of unusual color following a crack or fault in rock indicate mineralized vein.

4. **Quartz outcrops:** Barren quartz is common, but quartz veins often accompany ore. Where you see white quartz, look for discolored quartz or adjacent colored minerals.

5. **Heavy rocks:** Specimens notably heavier than normal rock for their size often indicate ore (ore minerals are dense).

**Ore location strategy:**
1. Walk upslope from an outcrop to find the primary deposit
2. Look for color anomalies on hillsides (red, green, blue against gray rock)
3. Examine stream gravels and sands—ore minerals are dense and settle at the bottom of channels
4. Dig or pan stream sediments; heavy particles are potential ore

### Water-Bearing Formations

Geological features reveal where water is accessible:

**Favorable formations:**
- **Sandstone and conglomerate:** Porous and permeable; water moves through easily; wells shallow (5–20 meters)
- **Limestone and karst:** Fractured by dissolution; caves hold water; springs are common; water quality varies
- **Alluvium (river deposits):** Porous, at low elevation; water table high; wells shallow and productive
- **Basalt:** Fractured and vesicular; water can be present in fractures; lava tubes may hold water

**Unfavorable formations:**
- **Granite (unfractured):** Dense, poor permeability; water is scarce; wells deep (50+ meters) or rely on surface sources
- **Shale and clay:** Impermeable; water does not percolate; surface water collection preferred
- **Metamorphic rocks:** Variable; water only in fractured zones

**Topography clues:**
- **Seeps and springs:** Occur where water-bearing layer meets impermeable layer; natural discharge points
- **Green vegetation:** In arid regions, indicates shallow water table
- **Valley floors:** Alluvial deposits; water table typically high
- **Hill summits:** Water deeper; groundwater less reliable

### Clay Deposits and Soil Type

**Clay identification:**
- Hold soil in wet hand; knead like dough
- Clay soil sticks together, feels slippery when wet, is hard when dry
- Sandy soil is gritty, does not cohere
- Mix soil with water in a jar; clay settles slowly (hours); sand settles instantly

**Clay deposit locations:**
- Valley floors with ancient water channels
- Areas with shale or metamorphic bedrock (parent rock for clay)
- Whitish or reddish fine-grained deposits (indicator of kaolin or iron-rich clay)

**Clay evaluation:**
1. Fire a sample in a kiln or open flame—clay hardens; good clay changes color (red, brown, white depending on iron and composition)
2. Pure white firing indicates kaolin (porcelain clay); red-firing suggests iron content (earthenware)
3. Plastic (workable) clay can be shaped without cracking; lean clay has little plasticity

### Building Materials

**Stone selection:**
- **Granite and basalt:** Hard, durable; good for foundation and load-bearing
- **Limestone and sandstone:** Softer; good for walls if stable; easier to work than granite
- **Slate and gneiss:** Sheet-like fracture; good for roofing and paving

**Sand and aggregate:**
- River- and beach-sand is well-sorted (uniform grain size); good for mortar
- Stream gravel is rounded; good for concrete aggregate
- Desert sand is very fine and angular; less ideal for concrete

## Field Prospecting Basics

Prospecting is systematic exploration for ore or other valuable minerals. Field prospecting requires patience, observation, and systematic sampling.

### Prospecting Method: Reconnaissance and Sampling

1. **Desktop research:** Study geological maps (if available), note known mineral occurrences nearby, identify promising formations

2. **Reconnaissance walk:** Traverse the area systematically, examining outcrops and surface materials. Mark promising areas with GPS or landmarks.

3. **Sample collection:**
   - Collect samples from discolored outcrops, mineralized veins, gossans
   - Take multiple samples from the same location (spatial variation is high)
   - Label samples with location, date, and brief description

4. **Sample analysis:**
   - Identify minerals by hand specimen (hardness, streak, luster)
   - Test magnetic minerals with compass
   - Look for ore indicators (metallic luster, red/green/blue color, heavy)

5. **Follow up:** If initial samples show promise, return to that area and dig or excavate to assess deposit size and quality.

### Stream Panning for Heavy Minerals

Heavy minerals (ore, gemstones) settle at the bottom of streams.

**Panning procedure:**
1. Fill a pan with stream sediment (bottom layer, coarse sand, gravel)
2. Fill with water and stir to suspend lighter material
3. Tip the pan to pour off lighter sediment and water
4. Repeat until only heavy material remains (black sand, small pebbles, ore particles)
5. Examine the remaining concentrate under magnification for ore, gemstones, or other valuable minerals

**Value indicators:** Shiny metallic particles (gold, copper, iron ore), gemstone crystals, or colored stones indicate rich deposits uphill.

## Soil Type and Parent Rock Analysis

Soil composition reflects the parent rock it derives from and the weathering processes at work.

### Soil Parent Rock Determination

1. **Dig a soil pit** (30–50 cm deep) and examine the soil profile (layers)
2. **Texture analysis:** Feel the soil; sand feels gritty, silt feels smooth, clay is sticky when wet
3. **Parent rock fragments:** Look for stones at depth; identify rock type (granite, basalt, limestone, shale)
4. **Color:** Red or brown suggests iron oxidation; white/gray suggests leaching in wet climate

**Example interpretations:**
- Sandy soil over granite bedrock = derived from granitic parent; coarse texture; good drainage
- Red clay over limestone = weathered limestone in tropical/humid climate; slow drainage
- Dark, humic soil = rich organic matter; good for agriculture

### Soil Assessment for Agriculture and Construction

**Agricultural suitability:**
- Test texture (loam is ideal—balanced sand, silt, clay)
- Observe color (dark = organic matter = fertility)
- Check drainage (gray color/wet = poor drainage; brown/dry = good drainage)

**Construction suitability:**
- Sandy soil: Good drainage; poor load-bearing; needs compaction
- Clay soil: Poor drainage; good load-bearing when dry; swells when wet
- Rocky soil: Excellent bearing; difficult excavation; good foundation

## Mapping Geological Features

Creating a simple geological map documents resources and hazards for future reference.

### Map Components

1. **Scale:** State distance (1 cm = 100 meters, etc.)
2. **Rock types:** Mark areas of granite, basalt, sandstone, etc. Use symbols or colors
3. **Mineral deposits:** Mark ore outcrops, gossans, or discolored zones with specific symbols
4. **Water sources:** Mark springs, seeps, reliable wells, streams
5. **Soil type:** Note clay deposits, agricultural soil areas
6. **Topography:** Simple contours or elevation notes

### Documentation Technique

1. Sketch a base map showing major landmarks (ridges, valleys, large rocks)
2. Walk boundaries between geological units and mark on map
3. Note color, texture, and mineral composition of each unit
4. Sample and describe outcrops; record location
5. Note water sources and their reliability
6. Mark hazards (steep slopes, unstable areas, deep canyons)

**Value:** A geological map becomes an invaluable reference for future resource decisions—where to dig a well, how to find clay, which stone to quarry for building.

## Geological Hazard Identification

Some geological features indicate hazards to avoid.

**Active fault lines:** Offsetting in rock layers, recent rockfall, spring-line following a straight path (indicates fault fracture). Avoid building near active faults.

**Unstable slopes:** Recent rockfall debris, trees tilted uphill (creeping), vertical rock faces, excessive erosion. Avoid steep slopes and toe of slopes (rockfall hazard).

**Karst terrain (limestone):** Sinkholes, caves, underground streams. Sinkholes can suddenly collapse; caves are dangerous if unexplored; water contamination risk is high in karst areas.

**Swamp and wetland:** Soft ground; poor building sites; disease vector (mosquitoes, parasites in tropics).

**Acid mine drainage:** Orange/red water seeping from rock; water kills aquatic life and corrodes equipment. Avoid drinking or using this water.

<section id="summary">

## Summary

Geology is the foundation of environmental assessment. By identifying minerals using simple tests (hardness, streak, luster, cleavage), you determine what resources are present. Rock classification reveals the formation history and resource potential of a landscape. Careful observation of outcrops, water features, and soil reveals where to find water, clay, ore, and building materials. Systematic prospecting and simple panning can locate valuable minerals. Mapping geological features creates a reference for future resource use. In survival and self-sufficiency, the ability to read the landscape geologically transforms random terrain into a mapped resource base.
