<section id="why-dyeing">

## 1\. Why Dyeing & Color

Color has been central to human civilization far longer than written history. Beyond aesthetics, color served as a tool of **identity** , **commerce** , **communication** , and **power** . A warrior's dyed tunic identified his clan; a merchant's purple-dyed cloth announced his wealth; flags communicated across battlefields and seas; military uniforms distinguished combatants. Color also served practical purposes: **camouflage** in hunting and warfare, **marking** property and rank, and **psychological effect** on morale and perception.

Historically, **color was more valuable than gold** . Consider these examples:

-   **Tyrian Purple** (Byzantine purple): Extracted from murex snail glands, a single gram of dye required approximately 10,000 snails and 2,000 liters of urine as a mordant. A purple-dyed cloth could cost more than its weight in gold. Roman law restricted purple dye to imperial use; wearing it without permission meant death.
-   **Ultramarine** : Ground from lapis lazuli stone (mined in Afghanistan), ultramarine cost more than gold paint in Renaissance Europe. Artists mixed it sparingly with other pigments; it was reserved for sacred imagery—the robes of the Virgin Mary.
-   **Indigo** : Controlled by monopolies in India and Japan; European merchants traded spices and gold for indigo powder.
-   **Cochineal Red** : From a Central American insect, cochineal was so valuable Spain guarded its source for centuries. A single pound of cochineal dye could purchase a house.

Natural dyes represent knowledge accumulated over millennia: which plants yield which colors, how to make those colors permanent, how to make them brighter or shift their hue. This knowledge was jealously guarded and passed through apprenticeships. Understanding natural dyeing connects us to the chemistry of color and the history it shaped.

<section id="mordanting">

## 2\. Mordanting

Most natural dyes do not bind permanently to fiber without a **mordant** —a chemical that acts as a bridge between dye molecule and fiber. Without mordant, color washes out and fades in sunlight. The word "mordant" comes from Latin *mordere* , "to bite," as it chemically bites into both fiber and dye.

### Common Mordants

-   **Alum (Potassium Aluminum Sulfate)** : The most important and safest mordant. Brightens colors, improves dye uptake on all fibers. Non-toxic, though large quantities can irritate skin. Cost-effective and reliable.
-   **Iron (Ferrous Sulfate)** : Darkens and shifts colors toward gray, brown, or black. Called the "saddening" agent because it dulls brightness. Use sparingly—too much iron makes fiber brittle.
-   **Copper (Copper Sulfate)** : Shifts colors toward green and deepens hues. Toxic—handle with care, use gloves, avoid inhalation. Small quantities go far.
-   **Tannin** : Extracted from oak bark, tea leaves, or pomegranate rind. Used as a pre-mordant, especially for cellulose fibers, to improve dye uptake. Not a primary mordant but an additive.
-   **Cream of Tartar** : A mild mordant that brightens colors and softens fiber. Often used with alum to improve results.

### Mordanting Process

:::info-box
**Standard Alum Mordant Bath (for 1 pound of fiber):**

-   1 ounce alum (potassium aluminum sulfate)
-   ½ teaspoon cream of tartar (optional)
-   4-5 gallons water
:::

1.  **Pre-wet fiber** : Soak wool or silk in warm water for 15 minutes to open fiber structure.
2.  **Dissolve mordant** : Dissolve alum in hot water; pour into large dye pot with 4-5 gallons water. Heat to 185°F (85°C).
3.  **Add wet fiber** : Gently place pre-wetted fiber into mordant bath. Never plunge hot fiber into cold water—thermal shock causes matting and felting.
4.  **Simmer 1 hour** : Maintain 185°F for 60 minutes. Fiber should move freely and color evenly.
5.  **Cool overnight in bath** : Turn off heat and allow fiber to cool completely in mordant bath (6-8 hours or overnight). This allows maximum mordant absorption.
6.  **Remove and use** : Fiber is now mordanted and ready for dye bath. Can be dyed immediately or stored damp (use within a few days) or dried for later use.

:::tip
**Tip:** Mordanted fiber stores better if completely dried. Spread on clean cloth in warm, dry place for 2-3 days. Store in paper bags (not plastic), away from light and pests.
:::

:::warning
**Warning:** If using iron or copper, wear gloves and work in ventilated space. Never ingest. Dispose of used mordant baths according to local regulations—copper and iron can contaminate water supplies.
:::

### Lightfastness Grades Explained

**Lightfastness** is measured on a scale of 1-8, where 1 is very fugitive (fades within weeks) and 8 is permanent (remains colorfast for centuries). A Grade 5 or higher is considered acceptably lightfast for most uses. Fugitive dyes (Grades 1-4) are useful for temporary textiles, educational dyeing, or combined with other dyes to shift hue.

<section id="indigo-vat">

## 4\. Indigo Vat

Indigo is unique among natural dyes. Unlike other dyes that dissolve in water and chemically bond to fiber, indigo is **insoluble in water** . To use indigo, you must **reduce** it—remove dissolved oxygen from the dye bath—to create a soluble form that fiber can absorb. When the dyed fiber is exposed to air, indigo immediately oxidizes back to its insoluble blue form, locking color permanently into the fiber.

This chemistry makes indigo the most lightfast natural dye (Grade 8) and explains its historical importance: once indigo-dyed, cloth never fades. A single garment can be dipped multiple times for progressively deeper blue.

### Traditional Indigo Sources

-   **Indigofera tinctoria** (tropical indigo plant): Yields the most indigo per plant; grown commercially in India, Japan, Indonesia.
-   **Isatis tinctoria** (woad): Temperate-climate plant; historically grown in Europe, Japan, China. Contains less indigo but still valuable.
-   **Strobilanthes tinctorius** (Japanese indigo): Fast-growing, yields good color in temperate climates.

### Processing Indigo Leaves to Indigo Pigment

1.  **Soak fresh leaves** : Harvest leaves when plant is in full growth. Soak in water for 1-2 hours at room temperature.
2.  **Beat to froth** : Whisk or stir vigorously with paddle to oxidize indigo and separate it from plant material. Water turns yellow-green, then blue foam appears on surface (15-30 minutes of beating).
3.  **Settle** : Let beaten mixture settle for 1-2 hours. Blue indigo particles sink to bottom; plant material floats. Carefully pour off water, collect the blue sediment.
4.  **Drain and press** : Pour sediment through cloth, press out water. Forms a blue paste.
5.  **Dry** : Spread paste thin on cloth or paper in warm, dry, shaded place. Takes 1-2 weeks to fully dry. Store dried indigo in sealed container away from light.

:::tip
**Tip:** Dried indigo powder can be purchased commercially if leaf processing is not feasible. Natural indigo costs $20-50/ounce, synthetic indigo (chemically identical but factory-made) costs $3-10/ounce.
:::

### Creating a Fermentation Indigo Vat

This is the traditional and most reliable method. The vat requires reduction—oxygen must be removed from the dye bath—and a source of alcohol or sugar for fermentation to consume oxygen.

:::info-box
**Natural Fermentation Vat (per 5 gallons of water):**

-   1 ounce natural indigo powder
-   ½ cup wheat bran or rice bran
-   1 tablespoon washing soda (sodium carbonate)
-   Water (room temperature or warm, not hot)
:::

1.  **Prepare vat container** : Use non-metal pot (glass, ceramic, or plastic). Metal reacts with indigo.
2.  **Soak indigo** : Mix indigo with small amount of warm water to form smooth paste. Let sit 30 minutes.
3.  **Add to vat** : Pour indigo paste and bran into 5 gallons warm water. Add washing soda. Stir very gently—avoid splashing and incorporating air.
4.  **Cover and ferment** : Cover vat with cloth (not airtight lid—vat needs to breathe but stay clean). Let sit in warm place (65-75°F ideal) for 3-7 days. Smell will become increasingly fermented (like beer or vinegar).
5.  **Check reduction** : Vat is reduced when: (a) Top layer appears yellow-green or pale, not blue; (b) Small amount removed and exposed to air turns blue (indicating indigo is soluble, not yet oxidized); (c) Vat smells fermented. This is correct.
6.  **Skim blue foam** : If thick blue froth forms on surface, skim it off (this is oxidized indigo particles—they won't dye). Gently stir vat to keep it reduced.

### Dyeing with Indigo Vat

1.  **Prepare fiber** : Pre-wet fiber in warm water. Wet fiber accepts color more evenly than dry fiber.
2.  **Warm vat gently** : Bring vat to 140-160°F (60-70°C). Higher temperatures work faster but use more indigo. Do not boil (air incorporation will oxidize vat).
3.  **Immerse fiber gently** : Submerge wet fiber in warm vat. Move gently, avoiding air bubbles. Fiber will appear yellow-green (soluble indigo).
4.  **Soak 15-20 minutes** : Allow fiber to soak and absorb indigo. Keep temperature steady and gentle movement.
5.  **Remove and oxidize** : Carefully remove fiber, let excess vat drip back into pot. Spread fiber in air (or gently squeeze in clean water) to expose to oxygen. Color shifts from yellow-green to blue within seconds as indigo oxidizes.
6.  **Rinse gently** : Rinse in cool water until water runs clear. Indigo is now set permanently into fiber.
7.  **Repeat for deeper blue** : Return fiber to vat for additional dips. Each dip adds more indigo. 2-3 dips yields medium blue; 8-10 dips yields deep navy.
8.  **Final rinse and dry** : After desired color is reached, rinse thoroughly in cool water and hang to dry.

### Vat Maintenance

-   **Keep vat reduced** : After each dyeing session, vat should be gently stirred (avoid air incorporation) and covered. It will reduce again over 12-24 hours.
-   **Add fresh indigo** : After 20-30 dippings, indigo becomes depleted. Add ¼ ounce fresh indigo paste + 1 teaspoon bran. Let vat re-reduce for 24 hours before using.
-   **Replenish with food** : If vat stops fermenting (becomes alkaline and smells like ammonia), add more bran and let sit 24-48 hours to restart fermentation.
-   **Store long-term** : A well-maintained indigo vat can last years. Skim off mold if it appears; stir and re-reduce vat before use.

:::warning
**Historical Note:** Before chemical reduction methods, fermentation vats often used **stale urine** as the oxygen-consuming agent and ammonia source. Medieval dyers maintained expensive vats for years, treating them as valuable assets. Modern methods using fructose, thiourea dioxide, or other reduction agents are faster, but fermentation vats produce superior, more permanent color.
:::

<section id="fiber-prep">

## 5\. Fiber Preparation

Not all fibers accept dye equally. Before dyeing, fiber must be **scoured** (cleaned of oils and processing chemicals) and, depending on fiber type, treated with **pre-mordants** to optimize color uptake.

### Fiber Types and Dye Uptake

-   **Protein Fibers** (wool, silk, alpaca, angora): Have amino acid structures that naturally bond with most dyes. Accept dye readily. Require alum mordant for best results but can accept some dyes without it. Most natural dyes were historically developed for wool.
-   **Cellulose Fibers** (cotton, linen, hemp, ramie): Lack amino acid structure; dyes don't bond as readily. Require pre-mordant (tannin treatment) before main mordant. Also require longer dyeing times and higher temperatures. Generally more challenging than protein fibers.

### Scouring Process

**Scouring removes:** Natural lanolin oil (in wool), sizing (starch applied during manufacturing), dirt, and other surface contaminants that prevent even dye uptake.

:::info-box
**Standard Scour (for 1 pound of fiber):**

-   1 tablespoon soda ash (sodium carbonate)
-   1 tablespoon mild soap (castile or dish soap)
-   4-5 gallons water
:::

1.  **Fill pot with water** : Use soft water (rainwater is best; distilled water acceptable). Hard water (high in calcium/magnesium) can prevent soap from lathering.
2.  **Add soda ash and soap** : Heat water to 180°F (82°C) and dissolve soda ash and soap. This creates alkaline bath that opens fiber and releases oils.
3.  **Add wet fiber** : Pre-wet fiber in cool water, then gently place in scour bath. Fiber should move freely. Never crowd—a 1-pound batch needs 4-5 gallons water to scour evenly.
4.  **Simmer 45-60 minutes** : Maintain 180°F for full hour. Fiber will lighten as oils are removed.
5.  **Cool slowly and rinse** : Turn off heat. Allow to cool 15 minutes, then rinse in progressively cooler water until soap scent is gone and rinse water runs clear.
6.  **Use immediately or store** : Scoured fiber is ready for mordanting. Can store damp (use within 2 days) or dry for later.

### Pre-Mordanting Cellulose Fibers

Cotton and linen require a pre-mordant (typically tannin) before the main mordant (alum) to ensure color permanence.

:::info-box
**Tannin Pre-Mordant (for 1 pound of cotton/linen):**

-   2 tablespoons tannin powder, OR
-   1 cup strong black tea (or oak bark decoction), OR
-   2 tablespoons pomegranate rind powder
-   4-5 gallons water
:::

1.  **Prepare tannin bath** : Dissolve tannin in hot water (160-180°F). If using tea or oak bark, steep for 1 hour, strain to remove solids.
2.  **Add scoured fiber** : Place wet, scoured cellulose fiber in tannin bath. Simmer gently for 30-45 minutes.
3.  **Cool and rinse slightly** : Cool in bath, then give a quick rinse in cool water (don't rinse completely—slight tannin residue helps fix alum mordant).
4.  **Apply alum mordant immediately** : While fiber is still damp, proceed to alum mordanting (see Mordanting section). The combination of tannin + alum significantly improves color on cellulose fibers.

### Silk Weighting

**Weighting** is an optional step historically used to add body and sheen to silk cloth. Metal salts are applied to the fiber, making dyed silk feel heavier and look more lustrous. This is primarily an aesthetic step, not necessary for color uptake.

:::info-box
**Basic Silk Weighting (for 1 pound of silk):**

-   ½ ounce tin chloride (or ferrous sulfate for different effect)
-   4-5 gallons water
-   pH controller (acetic acid—vinegar—to lower pH to 4-5)
:::

1.  **Prepare bath** : Dissolve tin chloride in warm water (140°F). Add vinegar to lower pH slightly.
2.  **Add pre-scoured, pre-mordanted silk** : Immerse dyed or mordanted (but not yet dyed) silk. Simmer 20-30 minutes.
3.  **Cool and rinse** : Cool in bath, rinse gently. Silk will feel heavier and softer.

:::warning
**Warning:** Excessive weighting can damage silk. Over-weighted silk becomes brittle and tears easily. Use sparingly. Modern textiles are rarely weighted due to quality concerns.
:::

## 6\. Paint & Pigment Making

### Pigments vs. Dyes

**Pigments** are colored minerals or organic matter **insoluble in water** —they must be suspended in a liquid medium (oil, water, egg) to apply as paint. **Dyes** dissolve in liquid and chemically bond to fiber. For paint, pigments are essential; dyes alone cannot create paint.

### Natural Pigments

-   **Ochre** (iron oxides): Yellow ochre (iron hydroxide), red ochre (iron oxide), brown ochre. Mined as earth. Grind to fine powder. Extremely permanent, non-toxic. Cost: $3-10/pound.
-   **Umber** (brown iron oxide + manganese): Dark brown, rich earth pigment. Found in Cyprus historically (burnt umber is umber heated—darkens it further). Permanent, non-toxic. Cost: $5-15/pound.
-   **Sienna** (hydrated iron oxide): Warm reddish-brown earth pigment. Raw sienna is lighter; burnt sienna is heated and darkened. Permanent. Cost: $5-15/pound.
-   **Kaolin** (china clay): White pigment, very fine particle size. Used to lighten other pigments or as base for pale colors. Non-toxic. Cost: $2-5/pound.
-   **Charcoal/Lampblack** (carbon): Jet black pigment made by burning organic material incompletely and collecting soot. Very permanent. Cost: $5-20/pound.
-   **Bone Black** (charred bone): Warmer, less intense black than lampblack. Made by burning bone and grinding to powder. Permanent. Cost: $10-25/pound.
-   **Madder Lake** (madder root precipitated on alum): Red to crimson pigment. Made by dyeing aluminum hydroxide with madder, then collecting colored particles. Permanent. Cost: $15-50/pound.
-   **Indigo Pigment** (indigotin): Blue pigment. Can use dried indigo powder directly in paint media. Permanent. Cost: $20-50/pound.

### Making Pigments from Raw Materials

#### Grinding Earth Pigments

1.  **Collect and dry** : Gather colored earth or mineral, dry completely in sun or oven (200°F, 1-2 hours).
2.  **Crush roughly** : Break into small pieces using mortar and pestle or stone hammer.
3.  **Grind fine** : Use mortar and pestle with repeated circular motions. Pigment should feel silky, with no grit between teeth. Takes 30 minutes to 2 hours depending on starting material hardness.
4.  **Sift if possible** : For finer particles, pass through fine cloth or sieve. Collect particles that pass through.
5.  **Store in sealed container** : Away from light and moisture.

#### Making Lampblack (Carbon Black Pigment)

1.  **Burn organic material incompletely** : Candles, fat, or oils produce most soot. Light a candle and hold clean ceramic plate or copper plate 2 inches above flame (not in flame—in the smoke).
2.  **Collect soot** : After 5 minutes, soot layer accumulates on plate. Scrape off with flexible tool (bone or plastic).
3.  **Repeat until sufficient quantity** : Collect until you have enough pigment (even a few minutes produces visible soot; ½ cup soot is achievable in an hour of careful work).
4.  **Grind and refine** : Grind collected soot in mortar to break up particles. Add small amount of water to form paste, grind further. This removes impurities and makes pigment finer. Dry the paste in sun or warm place (24-48 hours).
5.  **Use as pigment** : Add to paint binders.

:::warning
**Warning:** Collect soot in well-ventilated area. Do not inhale soot particles. Wear dust mask if collecting large quantities.
:::

### Paint Binders

A binder carries pigment and attaches it to surface. Different binders create different paint types:

#### Linseed Oil (Oil Paint)

**Oil painting medium:** Most traditional and durable paint. Linseed oil penetrates wood and canvas, hardens over time (oxidation, not evaporation), creating permanent, flexible film.

:::info-box
**Simple Oil Paint (by weight):**

-   2 parts linseed oil
-   1 part pigment powder
:::

1.  **Measure pigment** : Use weight for best results (or approximate: 3 tablespoons pigment per ¼ cup oil).
2.  **Mix slowly** : Pour linseed oil into glass or ceramic container (not metal—oil may react). Gradually add pigment while stirring with stick or palette knife.
3.  **Grind/mix thoroughly** : Stir until pigment is completely suspended, with no lumps or dry streaks. Takes 5-10 minutes.
4.  **Store in sealed jar** : Away from light. Oil paint will skin over (form protective layer) if exposed to air. To prevent, cover paint with thin layer of water or keep in sealed container.

#### Egg Tempera Paint

**Egg tempera:** Ancient binder, water-resistant when dry, fast-drying, brilliant colors. Used for medieval manuscript illumination and Renaissance panel painting.

:::info-box
**Egg Tempera (per egg yolk):**

-   1 egg yolk (separator required or use whole egg carefully)
-   1 tablespoon water
-   1 teaspoon linseed oil (or walnut oil)
-   Pinch of salt
-   Pigment powder as needed
:::

1.  **Separate egg yolk** : Carefully separate yolk from white. Yolk alone is the binder.
2.  **Mix binder** : In small container, whisk yolk with water, oil, and salt until emulsified (smooth, consistent).
3.  **Add pigment** : Gradually add pigment powder and stir. Add only as much pigment as egg can hold (about 1-2 tablespoons per egg yolk).
4.  **Use immediately** : Egg tempera dries quickly (30 minutes to 1 hour) as water evaporates. Cannot be stored long-term—egg spoils.
5.  **Apply thin layers** : Tempera is not built up like oil paint. Apply in thin coats; multiple thin layers are better than one thick layer.

#### Hide Glue + Chalk Gesso (Base Primer)

**Gesso:** A primer base painted on canvas or wood before oil or tempera painting. Seals surface, provides smooth tooth for paint adhesion, and lightens ground.

:::info-box
**Simple Gesso:**

-   2 parts hide glue (liquid or granules reconstituted)
-   2 parts chalk powder (or whiting—calcium carbonate)
-   1 part water
:::

1.  **Prepare glue** : If using granules, soak in water and gently warm (not boil) until dissolved. Liquid hide glue can be used directly.
2.  **Mix chalk** : In separate container, mix chalk with water to form thin paste, breaking up all lumps.
3.  **Combine** : Pour chalk paste into warm glue while stirring. Should resemble thick cream.
4.  **Apply to surface** : Paint gesso onto stretched canvas or wood panel in thin, even coats with brush. Apply 2-3 coats, allowing each to dry (1-2 hours) before next.
5.  **Sand lightly** : After gesso dries completely, lightly sand with fine sandpaper. This "tooths" the surface for better paint adhesion.

#### Casein Paint (Milk Paint)

**Casein paint:** Made from milk protein, produces matte, durable interior paint. Fast-drying, water-resistant when dry, non-toxic.

:::info-box
**Milk Paint (for 1 quart):**

-   1 cup milk (any fat content, fresh or powdered)
-   1 tablespoon lime powder (calcium hydroxide) or washing soda
-   Pigment powder as needed (1-3 tablespoons)
:::

1.  **Curdle milk** : Heat milk gently (don't boil). Add lime powder or washing soda, which causes milk to curdle into white paste (casein). This separates milk solids from liquid.
2.  **Strain off liquid** : Pour through cloth, collect the thick casein paste.
3.  **Mix pigment** : Stir pigment into casein paste until well mixed.
4.  **Adjust consistency** : Add water if too thick (should be paint-like consistency). Add lime if too thin.
5.  **Apply to interior surfaces** : Paint onto plaster, wood, or gesso. Dries in 1-2 hours, becomes water-resistant.

:::warning
**Warning:** Casein paint is only for interior use (interior walls, furniture). Not suitable for outdoor or wet surfaces. Once dried, cannot be redissolved in water.
:::

#### Fresco Pigment (Lime Water)

**Fresco:** Painting on wet plaster with pigment suspended in lime water. As plaster dries, calcium carbonate forms, trapping pigment. Extremely permanent.

:::info-box
**Fresco Pigments:**

-   Lime-stable pigments only: ochre, umber, raw sienna, charcoal, bone black
-   NOT: ultramarine (incompatible with alkaline lime)
-   Mixed with distilled water or lime water (calcium hydroxide solution)
:::

See pottery-ceramics.html for detailed fresco and lime plaster preparation.

#### Encaustic Paint (Wax-Based)

**Encaustic:** Pigment suspended in melted beeswax. Ancient technique, extremely permanent, rich colors. Requires heat during application.

:::info-box
**Encaustic Paint:**

-   Beeswax (or paraffin wax, though less traditional)
-   Resin (damar resin, 10% by weight) for hardness
-   Pigment powder
:::

1.  **Melt wax gently** : In double boiler or heat-safe container, warm beeswax to 185-200°F (85-93°C). Do not overheat—wax may smoke or ignite.
2.  **Add resin** : If using damar resin for hardness, dissolve resin in small amount of turpentine first, then stir into warm wax.
3.  **Mix pigment** : Stir pigment powder into warm wax. Use palette knife or stick (metal utensils conduct heat). Pigment should be completely suspended.
4.  **Apply while warm** : Use heated brushes or palette knife to apply to wood or special encaustic surface. Paint dries quickly as wax cools.
5.  **Heat-set optional** : For smooth, fused finish, heat finished painting with heat gun or torch (carefully) to melt wax layers together.

:::warning
**Warning:** Encaustic requires careful heat management. Never allow wax to overheat (smoke indicates decomposition). Use only in well-ventilated space. Do not use on surfaces that will contact skin (wax is hot during application).
:::

## 7\. Ink Making

### Iron Gall Ink (Standard Historical Ink)

**Iron gall ink** was the standard writing ink for over 1,500 years, from Roman times through the 20th century. It produces black color that darkens with age as the iron oxidizes. All medieval manuscripts, Renaissance documents, and historical letters were written in iron gall ink. The ink is created by a chemical reaction between tannin (from oak galls) and iron sulfate.

#### What are Oak Galls?

Oak galls are abnormal growths on oak trees caused by parasitic wasp larvae. The wasp lays eggs in oak leaf or bud; the tree responds by forming a spherical growth around the larva. These galls contain extremely high concentrations of tannin (up to 50% by weight)—far more than oak bark. Galls are collected after falling from tree in autumn, dried, and ground to powder.

:::info-box
**Iron Gall Ink (traditional recipe):**

-   1 ounce oak galls, ground to powder
-   ½ ounce ferrous sulfate (copperas)
-   ¼ ounce gum arabic (binding agent)
-   ½ cup water
:::

1.  **Prepare gall decoction** : Pour water into container, heat to boiling. Add powdered oak galls. Simmer 30 minutes, stirring occasionally. Liquid turns brown as tannin dissolves.
2.  **Strain** : Pour through fine cloth, collecting the dark brown liquid. Discard gall solids.
3.  **Dissolve iron sulfate** : In separate container, dissolve ferrous sulfate in small amount of warm water (becomes greenish).
4.  **Mix iron and gall** : Pour iron sulfate solution into gall liquid slowly, stirring constantly. Immediate reaction: liquid turns dark blue-black (tannin + iron = ferric tannate, the black dye).
5.  **Add gum arabic** : Dissolve gum arabic in small amount of warm water (becomes sticky, translucent). Add to ink mixture, stirring well. Gum arabic acts as binder and prevents ink from separating.
6.  **Let settle 24 hours** : Ink is murky initially but becomes clearer after settling. Some sediment (excess iron or tannin) may sink to bottom—this is normal.
7.  **Use or store** : Ink is ready to use. Will continue to darken over weeks as iron oxidizes. Store in sealed bottle with tight cap to prevent evaporation. Ink can keep for years.

### Color Evolution of Iron Gall Ink

Iron gall ink starts as dark brown or gray, becoming progressively blacker over days and weeks as iron oxidizes from ferrous (Fe²⁺) to ferric (Fe³⁺) form. This darkening property was well-known to medieval scribes and was sometimes used deliberately: important documents were written with iron gall ink knowing the color would gradually darken with age, as if the words were becoming more permanent over time.

### Carbon Ink (Soot-Based)

**Carbon ink** is black ink made from finely ground soot suspended in binding medium. Less permanent than iron gall ink (does not darken with age), but very black when applied and excellent for artistic calligraphy. Used in East Asia for millennia.

:::info-box
**Carbon Ink (soot-based):**

-   2 tablespoons lampblack (soot pigment, see Section 6)
-   1 tablespoon hide glue (liquid or granules)
-   1 tablespoon water
-   Pinch of salt
:::

1.  **Prepare glue** : Warm hide glue gently (not boiling). If using granules, rehydrate first.
2.  **Mix components** : In container, combine lampblack, warm glue, water, and salt. Stir very thoroughly—soot particles are very fine and tend to clump.
3.  **Grind smoothly** : If possible, grind mixture in mortar with pestle for 10-15 minutes. This breaks up soot particles and creates smooth, consistent ink. Without grinding, ink feels gritty when applied.
4.  **Store in sealed container** : Carbon ink keeps indefinitely if sealed from air.

### Berry Ink (Temporary)

**Berry ink** is a simple, temporary ink made from crushed berries. Fugitive (fades quickly), but useful for writing, drawing, and children's projects. Never used for important documents.

:::info-box
**Berry Ink:**

-   ½ cup fresh berries (blackberry, blueberry, or pokeberry—darkest berries work best)
-   1 tablespoon vinegar (or lemon juice)
-   1 teaspoon salt
:::

1.  **Crush berries** : Mash berries in bowl with fork or pestle, breaking down fruit to release juice.
2.  **Strain** : Pour through fine cloth, pressing to extract all juice. Discard pulp.
3.  **Add vinegar and salt** : Stir vinegar and salt into berry juice. Vinegar acts as preservative and darkens color; salt acts as fixative (slightly improves permanence, though ink will still fade).
4.  **Let sit 24 hours** : Color may deepen slightly.
5.  **Use for writing or drawing** : Ink is ready. Will fade noticeably within weeks to months in light, but acceptable for temporary use.

See bookbinding-printing.html for detailed application of ink to quill, brush, and printing surfaces.

## 8\. Staining Wood

Unlike paint (which sits on surface), wood stain penetrates wood grain, darkening or coloring wood while allowing grain pattern to show. Natural stains are water-based extracts of plants or reactions between plant tannins and metals.

### Walnut Hull Extract (Dark Brown Stain)

Walnut hulls contain juglone, a natural dye that stains wood dark brown to black without additional mordant.

:::info-box
**Walnut Hull Stain:**

-   1 quart green or brown walnut hulls (freshly fallen from tree)
-   2 quarts water
:::

1.  **Prepare hulls** : Crush walnut hulls roughly—they should fit in pot. Wear gloves (juglone stains hands brown-black permanently for weeks).
2.  **Boil extraction** : Cover hulls with water in pot, bring to boil. Simmer 1-2 hours, until liquid is dark brown or black. Add more water if liquid evaporates.
3.  **Strain** : Pour through cloth, collecting dark liquid. Discard hulls.
4.  **Apply to wood** : Brush or wipe stain onto wood surface. Penetrates quickly. Can apply multiple coats for darker color—each coat darkens. Stain dries within hours.
5.  **Seal if desired** : Once stain is dry, apply clear finish (linseed oil, beeswax, or lacquer) to protect and bring out grain.

### Iron Vinegar (Black/Ebonizing Stain)

Combining tannin-rich wood or plant material with iron creates a black stain. This technique, called "ebonizing," darkens wood to nearly black. The chemical reaction is dramatic and immediate.

:::info-box
**Iron Vinegar Stain:**

-   1 quart white vinegar (5% acetic acid)
-   Steel wool pad (or iron nails, iron filing—any iron)
-   Wood chips from tannin-rich wood (oak, walnut, chestnut)
:::

1.  **Prepare iron vinegar** : Shred or roughly chop steel wool, place in jar with white vinegar. Let sit 24 hours—vinegar dissolves iron, becoming dark brown solution. This is iron sulfate (or ferrous acetate) in solution.
2.  **Optional: add tannin** : For darker, more permanent result, simmer wood chips in separate pot for 30 minutes to extract tannin, strain, and mix with iron vinegar solution. Tannin + iron creates deeper black.
3.  **Apply to wood** : Brush iron vinegar solution onto wood surface. Reaction is immediate: wood turns gray, then dark gray-green, then progressively darker brown-black as iron oxidizes and polymerizes.
4.  **Multiple coats** : Apply 2-3 coats for very dark, nearly black finish. Allow to dry 1-2 hours between coats.
5.  **Seal** : Once dry, seal with linseed oil or clear finish to protect stain and prevent iron from continuing to oxidize (which can cause surface discoloration).

### Tea or Coffee Stain (Light to Medium Brown)

Strong brewed tea or coffee can stain wood light to medium brown, depending on tea type and number of applications.

1.  **Brew strong tea or coffee** : Use 1 tablespoon tea leaves or ground coffee per cup of water, simmer 1 hour, strain. Should be very dark liquid.
2.  **Apply to wood** : Brush onto wood surface. Stain is absorbed quickly. Dries in 1-2 hours.
3.  **Repeat for darker color** : Each additional coat slightly darkens wood.
4.  **Seal if desired** : Protect with linseed oil or lacquer.

### Beet or Plant Juice Stain (Pink to Red)

Beet juice and other red plant extracts can produce pink to light red stains on wood. Less permanent than walnut or iron stains, but useful for decorative work.

1.  **Extract juice** : Grate beets (or other red plants like pokeberries or bloodroot) and squeeze juice through cloth.
2.  **Apply directly** : Brush juice onto wood. Color is absorbed quickly but is fugitive—will fade within months in sunlight.
3.  **Use for temporary decoration** : Acceptable for interior furniture or objects not exposed to light.

## 9\. Practical Dyeing Recipes

The following are step-by-step recipes for four foundational natural dyes. Each assumes 1 pound of fiber (wool or mordanted cellulose) and produces approximately 4-6 ounces of finished dyed fiber.

### Recipe 1: Onion Skin Gold

#### Onion Skin Gold Dye

**Yield:** Bright golden yellow on wool; warm tan on cotton (pre-mordanted)

**Ingredients:**

-   1 pound mordanted fiber (wool with alum, or cotton with tannin + alum)
-   2 pounds dry onion skins (outer papery layers only—discard any green inner skins)
-   4-5 gallons water

**Equipment:**

-   Large stainless steel or glass pot (20+ quarts)
-   Thermometer
-   Wooden spoon or stick for stirring
-   Cloth for straining (cheesecloth or old sheet)

**Step 1: Prepare dye bath (1-2 hours before dyeing)** — Fill pot with 4-5 gallons of soft water (rainwater preferred). Add onion skins and bring to boil. Simmer 45 minutes—liquid turns yellow-gold. Strain out skins, collecting liquid. This is your dye bath.
**Step 2: Prepare fiber** — Pre-wet mordanted fiber in warm water for 15 minutes. This opens fiber structure and ensures even dye uptake.
**Step 3: Heat dye bath** — Reheat dye bath to 185°F (85°C). Use thermometer to monitor temperature.
**Step 4: Add fiber carefully** — Gently lower pre-wetted fiber into warm dye bath. Stir gently with stick or spoon—fiber should move freely with no bunching.
**Step 5: Maintain temperature 1 hour** — Keep temperature at 180-190°F for 60 minutes, stirring occasionally. Fiber will gradually shift from pale yellow to golden as dye is absorbed.
**Step 6: Cool slowly** — Turn off heat. Allow fiber to cool in dye bath for 30 minutes (can cool to room temperature overnight for deeper color).
**Step 7: Remove and rinse** — Remove fiber from dye bath. Squeeze out excess dye (save for additional lighter dye baths). Rinse in cool water until rinse water runs clear (3-4 rinses minimum).
**Step 8: Dry** — Hang fiber in warm, shaded place. Avoid direct sunlight (UV fades fresh dye). Dries in 24-48 hours.

**Notes:** This is one of the most accessible natural dyes, as onion skins are a kitchen byproduct. The shade ranges from pale lemon (single dip) to deep golden yellow (multiple dips, or extended heating). Shift the color to orange-brown by adding iron at the end (see Section 2: "Iron" for iron dye mordant details).

### Recipe 2: Walnut Hull Brown

#### Walnut Hull Brown Dye

**Yield:** Rich brown on wool; warm tan on cotton

**Ingredients:**

-   1 pound mordanted fiber
-   2-3 pounds fresh green walnut hulls (or dried, ground to powder)
-   4-5 gallons water

**Step 1: Prepare hulls** — Crush walnut hulls roughly. Wear gloves—juglone stains hands permanently. Fresh green hulls are best; dried hulls must be ground to powder. Fresh hulls are easier to process.
**Step 2: Extract dye** — Add crushed hulls to 4-5 gallons water in pot. Bring to boil, simmer 2-3 hours, stirring occasionally. Water turns dark brown. Add more water if evaporation occurs. Strain out hulls, collecting dark brown liquid.
**Step 3: Prepare fiber** — Pre-wet fiber in warm water 15 minutes.
**Step 4: Heat dye bath to 185°F** — Reheat extracted dye bath to 185°F.
**Step 5: Add fiber** — Gently lower pre-wetted fiber into dye bath. Stir gently, ensuring fiber moves freely.
**Step 6: Dye 1-2 hours** — Maintain 180-190°F temperature for 60-120 minutes. Longer heating = deeper brown. Stir occasionally.
**Step 7: Cool and rest** — Turn off heat, allow to cool 30 minutes to overnight.
**Step 8: Rinse and dry** — Remove fiber, rinse in cool water until clear. Hang to dry in warm, shaded place.

**Notes:** Walnut hull dye does not require additional mordant (juglone dyes directly). Optional: add iron sulfate at the end of dyeing for deep brown-black color. For darker shade, repeat dyeing process (re-dip in same bath or prepare fresh bath).

### Recipe 3: Indigo Blue (3-Dip)

#### Indigo Blue — Medium Blue (3 Dips)

**Yield:** Medium blue on wool or cotton

**Ingredients:**

-   1 pound pre-scoured fiber (can be with or without alum mordant—indigo bonds via oxidation, not chemical bonding)
-   Established indigo vat (see Section 4 for vat preparation)

**Step 1: Prepare fiber** — Pre-wet fiber thoroughly in cool water. Wet fiber accepts color more evenly than dry fiber.
**Step 2: Prepare vat** — If indigo vat has been sitting, gently stir (avoid air incorporation) to reduce any oxidized indigo on surface. Vat should smell slightly fermented. Warm vat gently to 140-160°F.
**Step 3: First dip (10-15 minutes)** — Submerge pre-wetted fiber gently in warm vat. Fiber will appear yellow-green (soluble indigo). Move fiber gently, allowing indigo to penetrate thoroughly. Do not squeeze or agitate harshly (can trap air bubbles).
**Step 4: First oxidation** — Carefully remove fiber from vat, allow excess vat to drip back into pot. Squeeze gently (do not wring). Spread fiber in air for 5-10 minutes—color shifts from yellow-green to greenish-blue as indigo oxidizes.
**Step 5: Rinse gently** — Rinse in cool water until water runs mostly clear. This removes excess indigo particles but does not affect indigo now bonded to fiber (oxidized indigo is insoluble).
**Step 6: Second and third dips** — Repeat steps 3-5 two more times. Each dip adds more indigo, progressively deepening blue. After third dip, fiber should be medium blue (not navy, but clear blue).
**Step 7: Final rinse and dry** — After third dip and oxidation, rinse thoroughly in cool water until water runs clear. Hang fiber to dry in warm place. Indigo color is now permanent—will not fade with washing or sunlight (Grade 8 lightfastness).

**Notes:** For darker navy, repeat dipping 5-8 times (process is the same, just more dips). For lighter blue, use fewer dips (1-2 dips = light blue). Indigo vat improves with use—after 20-30 dippings, vat becomes richer and more efficient.

### Recipe 4: Madder Red

#### Madder Root Red

**Yield:** Deep red to crimson on wool; warm red-brown on cotton

**Ingredients:**

-   1 pound alum-mordanted fiber (madder requires alum mordant—essential for good color)
-   2-3 ounces dried madder root powder (or whole root pieces ground fine)
-   4-5 gallons water

**Step 1: Slow extraction is key** — Unlike other dyes that can be boiled, madder requires gentle heating. This is critical: boiling madder destroys the alizarin dye molecule, producing muddy brown instead of bright red. Madder should never reach boiling point.
**Step 2: Prepare bath** — Place madder powder (or finely ground root) in pot with 4-5 gallons of cool water. Stir well. Allow to soak 30 minutes to rehydrate particles.
**Step 3: Add fiber directly to cold bath** — Pre-wet alum-mordanted fiber, add to cold madder + water mixture. This is unusual—fiber goes in before heating. Stir gently.
**Step 4: Heat slowly to 140°F (no hotter)** — Over 60 minutes, gradually raise temperature from cold to 140°F (60°C). Use thermometer constantly. Do NOT exceed 140°F—if temperature goes above 145°F, alizarin is lost. This slow heating allows fiber to absorb madder molecules gently.
**Step 5: Hold at 140°F for 45-60 minutes** — Maintain temperature for 60 minutes. Fiber will gradually turn red as madder bonds with alum-mordanted fibers. Stir occasionally, gently.
**Step 6: Cool slowly in bath** — Turn off heat. Allow fiber to cool completely in madder bath (2-4 hours minimum, overnight ideal). Cooling allows maximum madder absorption.
**Step 7: Remove and rinse** — Gently remove fiber from bath. Rinse in progressively cooler water until rinse water runs clear (4-5 rinses). Be gentle—warm fiber is tender.
**Step 8: Optional: shift color with iron** — For deepening red toward crimson, while fiber is still damp, immerse in weak iron sulfate bath (¼ teaspoon iron sulfate in 1 gallon water, 140°F) for 5-10 minutes. This shifts red toward deeper crimson. Remove, rinse, dry.
**Step 9: Dry** — Hang in warm, shaded place. Red color will deepen slightly as fiber dries. Fully dry in 24-48 hours.

**Notes:** Madder is the most complex natural dye—temperature control is essential. The slow, gentle process may seem excessive, but this is why madder produced the richest reds in historical textiles. If process is rushed or boiled, resulting color is muddy and dull. Madder extractions can be used repeatedly (just add fresh fiber to same bath) until color becomes pale.
