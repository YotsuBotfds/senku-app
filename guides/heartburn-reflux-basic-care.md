:::warning
**Disclaimer:** This guide is for basic home care when professional medical care is unavailable or delayed. Heartburn and reflux are common, but chest pain, black stools, vomiting blood, trouble swallowing, or severe worsening pain can mean something more serious. If you have those signs, seek urgent care.
Coffee-ground vomit, vomiting blood, throwing up blood, dark clots, or black tarry / sticky stool are GI-bleed red flags; route those prompts to acute-abdominal-emergencies, not this guide.
Reflux or sour-stomach wording with weakness, dizziness, pale skin, collapse, black sticky stool, or any vomiting of blood is acute-abdominal-emergencies first. Do not lead with hydration, rest, or routine reflux care for black tarry/sticky stool with dizziness or weakness.
Vomiting blood after alcohol or heavy drinking is an acute GI-bleed emergency until proven otherwise; do not route it to nosebleed care unless an active nosebleed is clearly the source.
If the belly is getting hard or swollen, the person is guarding, will not walk upright because of belly pain, has vomiting with no stool or gas, or has fever with one very tender abdominal spot, route to acute-abdominal-emergencies instead of reflux care.
If the main problem is rectal bleeding, anal pain or itching, or a lump near the anus after straining, use the hemorrhoids guide instead.
:::

<section id="overview">

## Overview: What Heartburn and Reflux Feel Like

Heartburn is a burning pain or sour feeling behind the breastbone or in the upper stomach. Reflux happens when stomach contents come back up into the esophagus. Some people also call this a "sour stomach," "acid stomach," or "indigestion."

This guide is for symptoms felt above the waist. If the main symptom is bright red blood on toilet paper, pain during or after a bowel movement, anal itching, or a protruding anal lump, route to hemorrhoids-basic-care instead.
If the main symptom is coffee-ground vomit, throwing up blood after drinking alcohol, vomiting blood, black tarry / sticky stool with dizziness or weakness, or stomach pain plus vomiting blood, route to acute-abdominal-emergencies instead of routine reflux care.
If the pain is becoming sharply localized, the belly is hard or swollen, or the person is bent over and does not want to walk upright, do not treat it as routine sour stomach or reflux.

Many mild cases come from:
- Large meals
- Eating fast
- Fatty, spicy, or very acidic foods
- Coffee, tea, chocolate, peppermint, alcohol, or smoking
- Lying down soon after eating
- Tight belts or clothing that press on the stomach
- Pregnancy
- Some medicines, especially if they irritate the stomach

Most mild reflux can be helped by changing habits first. Home care is safer than guessing at strong remedies.

</section>

<section id="what-to-do-now">

## What To Do Right Now

### 1. Stay upright after eating

- Do not lie down for at least 2 to 3 hours after a meal
- If you need to rest, sit upright or recline with your upper body raised
- Avoid bending over right after eating

### 2. Eat smaller meals

- Smaller meals put less pressure on the stomach
- Eat slowly and stop before you feel overly full
- If one large meal triggers symptoms, spread food across smaller meals

### 3. Reduce common triggers

Common triggers include:
- Fried or very fatty foods
- Hot peppers and heavy spice
- Tomato sauces and citrus if they bother you
- Coffee, tea, cola, and energy drinks
- Chocolate
- Peppermint
- Alcohol
- Smoking or chewing tobacco

If one meal, one drink, or one habit clearly triggers symptoms, start by cutting that trigger for a short trial instead of changing everything at once.

You do not need to avoid every possible trigger forever. Start by cutting the foods that clearly cause symptoms.

### 4. Protect sleep

- Raise the head of the bed if nighttime reflux is a problem
- A wedge or blocks under the bed legs can help more than extra pillows
- Do not eat a large meal close to bedtime

### 5. Choose gentle foods for a short time

If your stomach feels sour or irritated, try bland foods that are easier to tolerate:
- Oatmeal
- Rice
- Toast
- Bananas
- Crackers
- Plain soup

Avoid pushing strong foods just because they are available. If a food repeatedly makes symptoms worse, pause it for now.

</section>

<section id="medicines">

## Medicine If Available

If over-the-counter medicine is available, follow the label exactly.

- **Antacids** can help mild, short-lived heartburn now, especially after a known trigger meal
- **Acid reducers** may help if symptoms keep coming back, but they are not always the fastest immediate relief for pain happening right this minute
- Do not mix many products at once unless the label says it is safe
- If you have kidney disease, are pregnant, or take several regular medicines, check for label warnings before using anything new

Home remedies should not be treated as guaranteed fixes. A short trial of safer eating habits and posture changes is usually the first step.

</section>

<section id="what-not-to-do">

## What Not To Rely On

- Do not lie flat after eating and hope the discomfort passes
- Do not keep eating large meals if they clearly make the problem worse
- Do not use strong home remedies or harsh substances in the stomach
- Do not assume "sour stomach" is always harmless if the pain is severe or changing
- Do not keep taking pain medicine on an empty stomach if it seems to worsen symptoms
- Do not ignore a pattern where aspirin, ibuprofen, naproxen, or similar pain medicines seem to trigger burning, stomach pain, or nausea

</section>

<section id="when-to-worry">

## When To Worry: Red Flags That Need Medical Care

Seek urgent care if heartburn or reflux comes with any of the following:

- Chest pain, pressure, or pain spreading to the arm, jaw, back, or shoulder
- Trouble breathing, sweating, fainting, or sudden weakness
- Vomiting blood or material that looks like coffee grounds
- Black tarry or black sticky stool, especially with weakness, dizziness, or pale skin, or any bloody stool with those systemic symptoms
- Throwing up blood after alcohol or heavy drinking
- Severe or worsening upper abdominal pain
- Pain that wakes you often or keeps getting worse instead of better
- Trouble swallowing, food getting stuck, or painful swallowing
- Unexplained weight loss
- Repeated vomiting
- Fever with belly pain
- A hard, swollen, or very tender abdomen
- Belly pain localizing to one very tender spot, especially if walking, coughing, or bumps make it worse
- Vomiting with no stool or gas
- Guarding or refusing to walk upright because of belly pain

These signs can point to ulcer, GI bleeding, infection, blockage, gallbladder disease, or a heart problem rather than simple reflux.
When the pattern looks like guarding, obstruction, or one very tender feverish spot, stop reflux care and route to acute-abdominal-emergencies.

:::danger
**Treat chest pain seriously.** Heartburn can feel like chest discomfort, but heart attack pain can also be mistaken for reflux. If the pain is severe, new, crushing, or comes with shortness of breath, sweating, nausea, or pain spreading away from the chest, seek emergency help.
:::

</section>

<section id="prevention">

## Prevention For Everyday Use

- Eat smaller meals instead of one very large meal
- Leave time between dinner and sleep
- Stay upright after eating
- Avoid foods that repeatedly trigger symptoms
- Keep belts and waistbands loose after meals
- If reflux happens often at night, raise the head of the bed
- If symptoms started after a new medicine, read the label and check whether stomach upset is a known side effect

If symptoms happen more than a few times a week, keep a simple log of meals, timing, and symptoms. Repeated reflux is worth attention even if the pain is mild.

Get checked sooner if:

- symptoms start after regular NSAID use such as aspirin, ibuprofen, or naproxen
- symptoms happen more than a few times a week
- posture and meal-timing changes are not helping after a short trial
- the problem keeps returning even with smaller meals and trigger reduction

</section>

<section id="summary">

## Bottom Line

For most mild heartburn and sour stomach:

1. Eat smaller meals
2. Stay upright after eating
3. Avoid lying down for 2 to 3 hours
4. Raise the head of the bed if night symptoms are common
5. Reduce foods and habits that clearly trigger symptoms
6. Watch closely for red flags that could mean something more serious

If symptoms are frequent, severe, or changing, do not keep guessing at home care.

</section>
