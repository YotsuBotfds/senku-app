## Scope Boundary

## Universal Design: Serving Everyone

Universal design is the principle that spaces and products should work for the widest range of people, regardless of ability. It benefits everyone:

- **Ramps** help wheelchair users AND people pushing carts, parents with strollers, elderly with walking aids
- **Large text** helps people with weak vision AND those reading in dim light
- **Wide doorways** help wheelchairs AND allow moving large items
- **Light switches at mid-height** help people in wheelchairs AND reduce awkward reaching for anyone
- **Grab bars** help elderly AND assist anyone carrying load

In rebuilding communities, accessibility design costs little more than inaccessible design but serves far more people.

## Wheelchair Accessibility for Schoolhouses, Shelters & Community Spaces

Wheelchairs require unobstructed, relatively smooth surfaces and adequate maneuvering space.

### Ramps

**Slope (rise-to-run ratio):**
- Maximum slope: 1:12 (1 unit rise per 12 units horizontal distance)
- Example: To rise 1 meter, need 12 meters of ramp length
- Gentler is better for ease of use and safety

**Width:**
- Minimum: 1 meter (single wheelchair, barely fits)
- Better: 1.2Ã¢â‚¬â€œ1.5 meters (allows passing, some flexibility)
- Width measured clear of railings/edges

**Surface:**
- Smooth, non-slip material (concrete, compacted gravel, wood)
- No loose gravel (wheels sink)
- No metal grating (wheel gets caught)
- Avoid ice/standing water (traction lost)

**Railings:**
- Both sides recommended
- Height: 85Ã¢â‚¬â€œ95 cm from ramp surface
- Diameter: 3Ã¢â‚¬â€œ4 cm (hand-grip size)
- Graspable material (wood, metal, not slippery plastic)

**Landings:**
- At top and bottom of ramp: flat area at least 1 meter Ãƒâ€” 1 meter
- Between long ramps: rest landing every 9 meters
- Prevents momentum loss, allows turning

:::tip
Manual wheelchair users expend significant energy on ramps. Minimize ramp length. If multiple ramps possible, choose gentler slope over steeper.
:::

### Doorways and Passages

**Door width:**
- Minimum: 75 cm (single wheelchair, tight fit)
- Better: 80Ã¢â‚¬â€œ85 cm (comfortable, clear passage)
- Measured clear of door swing and frame

**Door handles:**
- Type: Lever handles (not round knobs, not complex mechanisms)
- Height: 80Ã¢â‚¬â€œ110 cm
- Accessible to one hand without gripping hard
- Avoid push-button doors (can't operate while holding mobility aid)

**Thresholds:**
- Eliminate completely if possible
- If necessary: maximum 1 cm height (slightly beveled edge)
- Avoid sharp edges (damage wheelchair tires, create vibration)

**Passage width:**
- Minimum: 80 cm (single wheelchair)
- Better: 1 meter (allows passing other wheelchair or person)
- Allow for turns: wider area at corners

### Flooring

- **Smooth and level:** Wheelchair rolls easily
- **Non-slip surface:** Prevents spinning, accidents
- **No loose edges:** Carpet edges can catch wheels
- **Good lighting:** Shadows create obstacles visually

### Bathroom Accessibility

- **Turning space:** Clear floor area 1.5 m Ãƒâ€” 1.5 m (for turning wheelchair)
- **Toilet height:** 45Ã¢â‚¬â€œ50 cm (standard wheelchair transfer height)
- **Grab bars:** Around toilet, next to tub/shower (installed into solid framing, supports 150+ kg weight)
- **Sink clearance:** Knee space underneath (60 cm minimum) for wheelchair user to position close
- **Shower:** Zero-threshold entry (no lip) or beveled threshold (<1 cm)

## Visual Impairment Accommodations

Blind and low-vision people navigate using non-visual cues: touch, sound, scent, memorization.

### Tactile Indicators and Wayfinding

**Raised tactile dots (bumps):**
- Signal hazards or direction changes
- Placed at edge of stairs, platform edges
- Also used at street intersections, building entrances
- Dome-shaped bumps, spaced 60 cm apart in guiding strips

**Texture changes:**
- Different flooring signals different spaces (carpet vs. concrete)
- Smooth vs. rough indicates hazard zones
- Helps blind users orient without asking directions

**Walls and edges:**
- Consistent wall placement allows navigation by touch
- Aligned handrails provide guidance
- Open spaces feel disorienting; subtle guides help

### Hazard Mitigation

**Head-height obstacles:**
- Overhanging signs, branches, lights must be >2 meters high (above cane strike height)
- Alternatively, protect with railings so cane hits railing, not user's head

**Floor-level obstacles:**
- Minimize protruding objects (chairs, boxes, loose items)
- Secure carpet edges so no tripping

**Stairs:**
- Bold colored stripe on edges (visible to low-vision users)
- Even step heights (varied heights cause falls)
- Handrails on both sides with continuous grip
- Landing platforms with level surface

### Lighting and Contrast

**General lighting:**
- Brighter is better for low-vision users (4Ã¢â‚¬â€œ5x normal office brightness helpful)
- Even lighting (no dark shadows, not too bright to glare)
- Avoid flicker (causes discomfort, seizures)

**Color contrast:**
- Dark on light or light on dark (avoid similar tones)
- Examples: white on black, light yellow on dark blue (avoid: gray on silver)
- Helps low-vision users distinguish spaces and objects

### Information Access

- **Signage:** Text + tactile labels (Braille if readers available)
- **Verbal description:** Audio tours or guides describing spaces
- **Important info:** Posted in multiple formats (visual, audio, tactile)

<section id="hearing-impairment">

## Hearing Impairment Adaptations

Deaf and hard-of-hearing people miss auditory information. Provide visual and tactile alternatives.

### Visual Alerts

**Instead of buzzers/alarms:**
- Flashing lights (red for alarm, green for OK)
- Light placement: visible from anywhere in space
- Brightness: noticeably different from ambient

**Doorbell/knock:**
- Visual indicator in home (flashing light)
- Vibrating alert (for portable use)
- Avoid audio-only notification

### Written and Visual Communication

**Signs:**
- Written information accompanying any audio announcement
- Visual-friendly format (clear font, high contrast, not too small)

**Meetings and events:**
- Designated note-taker or captionist
- Real-time written transcription of discussions
- Visual slides with key points

### Acoustic Environment

**Reduce background noise:**
- Soft furnishings (curtains, carpets) absorb sound
- Separate noisy and quiet zones in buildings
- Allows those with hearing aids to hear better

**Meeting spaces:**
- Consider those who lip-read (good lighting on speaker's face, no backlight)
- Face people when speaking (not to wall)
- Slow, clear speech helps people who read lips

## Elderly-Friendly Design

Aging brings reduced strength, flexibility, balance, and reaction time. Design compensates.

### Mobility Support

| Feature | Benefit | Placement |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| Grab bars | Stability, prevents falls | Bathrooms, stairs, ramps |
| Handrails | Support when walking | Along stairs, long hallways |
| Railings | Balance support | Open edges, stairs |
| Wider pathways | Less claustrophobic, allows cane/walker | Hallways, passages |
| No ramps >1:12 slope | Easier for weak knees | All ramps |

### Strength-Reducing Design

- **Light door handles:** Avoid heavy pull doors; use lever handles
- **Low-force switches:** Light switches that don't require hard pressing
- **Cabinets/drawers:** Minimalist latches; slides easily; open with light pressure
- **Faucets:** Single-lever (not two-knob) design; minimum force required

### Balance and Fall Prevention

- **No loose rugs:** Trip hazard
- **Even flooring:** No sudden level changes
- **Clear sight lines:** No clutter blocking view of path
- **Adequate lighting:** Helps eyes adjust; prevents falls from poor visibility
- **Sit-down spaces:** Benches, chairs at regular intervals for rest

### Kitchen and Eating

- **Counter height:** Standard 85 cm is good (some elderly prefer 90 cm)
- **Stool:** Allows sitting while preparing food (reduces fatigue)
- **Items at reachable height:** No overhead cabinets (reaching difficult); mid-chest height better
- **Dishes/cups:** Lightweight, thermal isolation (so hot water doesn't burn hands immediately)

### Stairs and Steps

**Avoid when possible:** Long-term aging, people cannot climb multiple flights.

**If stairs necessary:**
- Even step height (7Ã¢â‚¬â€œ20 cm, ideally uniform)
- Non-slip surface
- Handrails both sides, continuous grip
- Light-colored edges (visible to low-vision users)
- Landing platforms every 9 meters

<section id="children-safety">

## Children-Safe Environments

Children are small, curious, and lack hazard awareness. Design protects while allowing play.

### Size and Scale

- **Furniture height:** Accessible to children (not all fixtures at adult level)
- **Doorway heights:** Minimum 2 meters (children won't hit head; adult stooping prevents)
- **Windows:** Sills low enough for children to see out (learning, not just containment)

### Hazard Elimination

| Hazard | Solution |
|--------|----------|
| Sharp edges/corners | Round edges, padding, bumpers |
| Choking hazards | Objects >3 cm diameter (not mouth-sized) |
| Strangulation hazards | No cords, strings >20 cm near children |
| Falls from height | Railings on elevated areas (1.1 m high minimum) |
| Hot surfaces | Barriers around heat sources (stove, fire) |
| Toxic substances | Locked storage for poisons, chemicals, medicines |
| Drowning | Fenced water access, adult supervision |
| Entrapment | No spaces where head fits but body doesn't |

### Play Spaces

- **Soft surfaces:** Grass, wood chips, mulch under play equipment
- **Open sight lines:** Adults can see entire play area
- **Equipment spacing:** Objects spaced 2 meters apart minimum (prevents head contact if falling)
- **Age-appropriate:** Younger children (<5) separate from older children (>8)

### Supervision and Layout

- **Clear entrances:** Defined exit/entry points (prevents wandering away)
- **Seating for adults:** Placed strategically so adults can monitor play
- **Fencing:** Encloses play area from hazards (traffic, water)

### Classroom Accessibility Layout

When the accessible space is also a classroom, keep the room easy to supervise and easy to move through:

- **Teacher station:** A small desk or table near the edge of the room for planning, record-keeping, and quick access to materials.
- **Sight lines:** Teacher should be able to see the whole room without blocked corners.
- **Quiet corner:** If possible, provide a screen-off or low-distraction area for individual help or brief assessments.
- **Material access:** Keep commonly used items within easy reach so students and helpers do not need to cross the room constantly.
- **Wide movement paths:** Leave enough space between zones for wheelchairs, walkers, and children carrying materials.

## One-Handed Operation Design

People with one hand due to injury, amputation, or arthritis need accessible controls.

### Door and Cabinet Handles

- **Type:** Lever handles (not round knobs requiring twist/rotation)
- **Force:** Minimal pressure required (<5 N / 0.5 kg weight)
- **Height:** 80Ã¢â‚¬â€œ110 cm (accessible whether standing or seated)
- **Placement:** Clear of obstacles; easy to reach

### Controls and Switches

- **Light switches:** Broad, flat buttons (easy to press with palm or fist)
- **Door openers:** Lever or automatic (not narrow knobs)
- **Faucets:** Single-lever (turn one lever) not two-knob (need both hands)
- **Locks:** Accessible from one hand without twisting wrist unnaturally

### Workspace

- **Counter edges:** Beveled (not sharp right angle) allows arm movement without injury
- **Shelving:** Adjustable height; items at waist level (not requiring reaching)
- **Storage:** Drawers instead of cabinets (easier one-handed access)
- **Workspace:** Open sides so one-handed person can approach and work comfortably

### Kitchen Design

- **Counter surface:** Continuous, smooth transition (no gaps)
- **Placement:** Stove, sink, preparation space in triangle layout (minimizes movement)
- **Tools:** Within arm's reach when positioned at counter
- **Support surfaces:** Wide counters (not narrow) support one hand's stability

## Ground-Level Living Design

Multiple floors limit accessibility. Single-level or ground-floor design serves more people.

### Master Bedroom on Ground Floor

- Elderly and disabled can sleep without stairs
- Reduces falls going up/down at night
- No need to relocate bedrooms for aging-in-place

### Bathrooms on Ground Floor

- Critical: bathroom access without stairs is vital
- Elderly and injured cannot navigate stairs for toileting
- Allows aging in home long-term

### Kitchen/Eating on Ground Floor

- Main living activities accessible without stairs
- Reduces trips carrying heavy items
- Socializing happens at accessible level

### Layout: Avoiding Stairs

**When ground-level not possible:**
- Minimum: Entrance-level main living space (living room, kitchen)
- Bedrooms on level with main entrance (not basement or upper floors)
- Primary bathroom accessible without stairs

**Slope instead of steps:**
- Gentle slope (ramp) easier than stairs for everyone
- Takes more space but universally accessible

## Grab Bars and Support Features

Physical support prevents falls and allows independent living for people with mobility limitations.

### Bathroom Installation

**Toilet area:**
- Bars on both sides of toilet (85Ã¢â‚¬â€œ95 cm high)
- 60 cm from front edge of toilet
- Supports 150+ kg (installed into studs, not drywall alone)

**Shower/tub:**
- Vertical bars at tub entrance (stability entering/exiting)
- Horizontal bar along tub edge (security while bathing)
- Placement: 75Ã¢â‚¬â€œ95 cm high (for reaching while standing)

**Overall bathroom:**
- Continuous handrails from toilet to tub/shower
- Handrails along entire tub edge
- Never leave gaps in protection

### Installation Standards

- **Mounting:** Into wooden studs or solid backing; not into hollow drywall alone
- **Materials:** Stainless steel or coated metal (not slippery; rust-resistant)
- **Diameter:** 2.5Ã¢â‚¬â€œ3 cm (hand-grip size)
- **Weight:** Support minimum 150 kg (supports falling weight, not just standing)
- **Spacing:** 60 cm centers for wall-mounted bars

### Non-Bathroom Applications

- **Stairs:** Railings on both sides (85Ã¢â‚¬â€œ95 cm high)
- **Ramps:** Railings if steep
- **Hallways:** Rails along walls in narrow passages
- **Entrances:** Support at door threshold if slight step present

:::warning
Improperly installed grab bars are dangerous: they give false confidence, then fail under load. Always install into solid framing; test before allowing regular use.
:::

## Inclusive Community Planning

Accessibility is most effective when planned at community scale, not just building-by-building.

### Accessible Routes

**Sidewalks and pathways:**
- Smooth, level surface (1Ã¢â‚¬â€œ1.5 meters wide)
- Curb ramps at street crossings (1:12 slope, beveled edges)
- No obstacles (parked vehicles, overhanging branches, street furniture blocking path)
- Regular maintenance (avoid potholes, uneven settling)

**Public buildings:**
- Accessible entrance (ramp or level entry)
- Accessible restrooms
- Accessible drinking fountain (height suitable for wheelchair users: 100 cm)
- Accessible seating in meeting spaces

Schoolhouses and other shared community buildings should have one obvious accessible entrance and clear interior circulation so people are not forced to use a separate back route unless there is no safer option.

**Public transportation:**
- Vehicles with level boarding (no steps) or lifts
- Seats for elderly and disabled clearly marked
- Audio AND visual announcements of stops
- Drivers trained to assist passengers

### Community Spaces

**Parks and recreation:**
- Accessible playgrounds (accessible route, adaptive equipment)
- Paved paths for wheeled mobility aids
- Benches at regular intervals (for rest)
- Accessible restrooms

**Markets and shopping:**
- Accessible entrance and circulation
- Aisles wide enough for wheelchair navigation (1 meter)
- Shelving at varying heights (items visible and reachable)
- Checkout counter with accessible section (lowered height)

**Community events:**
- Accessible venue (level entry, restrooms, seating)
- Accessible parking (if applicable)
- Sign language interpreter or captioning (for deaf attendees)
- Advance notice of accommodations available

### Inclusionary Mindset

Accessible design requires:

- **Awareness:** Recognizing disabilities as normal human variation
- **Accountability:** Community expects accessibility; businesses provide it
- **Participation:** Include disabled people in planning (those affected know best)
- **Iterative improvement:** Start with basics; expand over time as capacity builds
