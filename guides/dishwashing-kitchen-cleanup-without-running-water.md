## Overview

Dishwashing without running water is mostly a sequencing problem. The job is not just "make it look clean." The job is:

- remove food and grease
- wash away residue with soap and friction
- rinse with clean water
- sanitize food-contact items when needed
- dry everything fully before storage

The same basic workflow works for plates, bowls, cups, spoons, knives, chopping boards, pots, and kitchen tools. You can do it with a basin, bucket, tub, or any other clean container that can hold water.

This guide is for common user questions like:

- How do I wash dishes without a sink?
- How do I clean greasy pots with little water?
- How do I sanitize a cutting board after raw meat?
- What should I do with dishes after vomiting or diarrhea?
- How do I dry and store clean dishes so they stay clean?

For broader food handling and raw-meat safety, cross-check `kitchen-food-prep-safety.md`.

<section id="core-rules">

## Core Rules

- Scrape first, wash second, rinse third, sanitize last if needed.
- Keep clean items separate from dirty items until the whole cycle is done.
- Use the cleanest water you have for the final rinse.
- Air-dry items instead of wiping them with a dirty towel.
- Clean food-contact surfaces before they dry out and stick.
- Do not let greasy wash water become the place where clean items touch.
- If you cannot clean a porous item well, stop using it for food.

The safest low-water habit is simple: do the dirtiest step first, then keep moving toward cleaner steps without going backward.

</section>

<section id="setup">

## Set Up a Simple Wash Station

You do not need a sink. You do need a place where dirty, clean, and drying items do not mix.

### Basic supplies

- one wash container
- one rinse container if possible
- soap or other food-safe cleaner
- scrub brush, pad, or cloth dedicated to kitchen work
- clean towel or drying rack
- scraper, spoon, or spatula for food scraps
- small bucket or jar for clean rinse water
- trash can, compost bucket, or feed bucket for scraps where appropriate

### Helpful layout

- dirty dishes on one side
- washing container in the middle
- clean drying area on the other side
- trash or scrap bucket close to the wash area

If you can, keep the drying area higher than the wash area so drips do not run back onto clean items.

### Water order

If water is scarce, reserve the cleanest water for:

1. the final rinse
2. food-contact items
3. cutting boards and knives
4. ordinary plates and bowls
5. greasy pots and pans last

<section id="workflow">

## Wash, Rinse, Sanitize

The basic sequence is the same even when you only have a little water.

### 1. Scrape

Remove food scraps before washing.

- Scrape plates into trash, compost, or a feed bucket if that is already part of your system.
- Wipe out pots and pans with paper, cloth, or a spatula before adding water.
- Empty cups and bowls completely.
- Save the wash water from getting cloudy too quickly.

### 2. Wash

Use soap and friction.

- Fill the wash container with warm or hot water if available.
- Add a small amount of soap.
- Work from the cleanest items to the dirtiest.
- Scrub seams, handles, rims, lids, and undersides.
- Change the water when it gets greasy, cloudy, or full of food bits.

### 3. Rinse

Rinse away soap and loosened residue.

- Use the cleanest water available.
- Pour rinse water over items if dipping them would contaminate the rinse container.
- Rinse until the item no longer feels slippery.
- If soap taste or smell remains, rinse again.

### 4. Sanitize if needed

Sanitize food-contact items when they may touch ready-to-eat food or when contamination risk is higher.

Good times to sanitize:

- after raw meat, poultry, fish, or egg contact
- after a sick person used the item
- after the item touched vomit, stool, or dirty cleanup water
- after raw juices spilled on it

Options:

- use a food-safe sanitizer according to the label
- use an emergency bleach solution: for unscented household bleach (roughly 5–6 % sodium hypochlorite), mix 1 tablespoon bleach per 1 gallon of clean water (or 1 teaspoon per quart); keep the surface wet for at least 1 full minute; let it air-dry and do not rinse off
- use boiling water: pour water at a full rolling boil over the clean surface and keep it wet for at least 30 seconds; only use on items that tolerate boiling water without warping or cracking
- use a heat-based method that is already known to be safe for that item

Sanitizer only works well after the item has been cleaned first.

</section>

<section id="cutting-boards">

## Cutting Boards and Knives

Cutting boards and knives need special attention because they touch both raw and ready-to-eat food.

### Best practice

- use separate boards if you have them
- if you only have one board, prep ready-to-eat food first and raw meat last
- wash the board and knife immediately after raw meat work
- sanitize them before they touch salad, bread, fruit, or cooked food again
- dry the board upright so moisture does not sit in grooves

### For plastic or other nonporous boards

- scrape off scraps
- wash with soap and clean water
- scrub both faces and edges
- rinse well
- sanitize if the board touched raw meat or illness contamination
- air-dry fully

### For wood boards

Wood can be used safely if it is kept clean, dry, and not deeply damaged.

- scrub with hot soapy water
- rinse thoroughly
- dry upright or on edge
- oil lightly only with a food-safe oil if that is already part of your maintenance routine
- retire the board if it stays sour-smelling, deeply cracked, or moldy

### Knives and utensils

- clean the blade, handle, and any joints or rivets
- do not leave food residue on the handle
- dry the knife before storage so rust does not start
- keep a dedicated brush or cloth for food tools if possible

Never use a dirty dish cloth on a clean knife.

</section>

<section id="greasy-pots">

## Greasy Pots and Pans

Greasy cookware is the hardest part of dishwashing because fat sticks to surfaces and makes dirty water spread faster.

### First move: remove grease before washing

- scrape leftover fat and sauce into waste or a separate grease container
- wipe the pan with paper, cloth, or a spatula before adding water
- let burnt-on grease soften in hot water before scrubbing

### Best cleaning method

1. Put the pot or pan in hot water.
2. Add a small amount of soap.
3. Let stubborn grease soak briefly.
4. Scrub with a brush or abrasive pad that is safe for the cookware.
5. Rinse with the cleanest water you have.
6. Dry immediately to prevent rust or water spots.

### Important grease habits

- clean greasy items after lighter dishes if your water is limited
- do not keep reusing the same greasy wash water for everything
- do not pour cooking grease into the ground, pit latrine, or greywater area if you can avoid it
- let grease cool in a container so it can be collected or disposed of more safely

For cast iron, dry it fully and re-oil it lightly if that is already standard practice.

</section>

<section id="illness-boundaries">

## Illness Contamination Boundaries

When someone is vomiting, has diarrhea, or is otherwise contaminated, the kitchen needs boundaries.

### Use separate items if you can

- give the sick person their own cup, bowl, plate, and utensils
- keep those items together until washing
- wash them after ordinary dishes if you have limited water
- handle them with gloves if they are visibly contaminated

### Treat body fluids differently

If a dish, cloth, or utensil has visible vomit, stool, blood, or sewage on it:

1. keep it out of the normal dish stack
2. remove solids or thick residue first
3. wash with soap and clean water
4. sanitize the item if it is food-contact safe and still worth saving
5. discard porous items that cannot be cleaned well

### Keep the kitchen out of the sick-room cycle

- do not wash sick-room cloths in the same basin as clean dishes
- do not use the same towel for dish drying and illness cleanup
- do not let a contaminated item sit in the food-prep area
- wash your hands before returning to food work

If the whole household is sick, simplify dishes to the smallest number of items you can safely manage.

</section>

<section id="low-water">

## Water-Saving Methods

Low-water dishwashing is about saving the cleanest water for the last steps.

### Batch washing

- wash a few items at a time instead of filling one huge pile
- save cups and utensils for the cleanest water
- do greasy pots last
- refill water only when needed

### One-basin method

If you only have one basin:

1. scrape all scraps first
2. wash the cleanest items first
3. drain the water when it gets dirty
4. refill with cleaner water for the rinse

### Two-basin method

If you have two containers:

- one for washing
- one for rinsing

That simple separation saves water and keeps the final rinse cleaner.

### Wipe-before-wash

Before adding water, wipe off as much residue as possible.

- use a scraper for hardened food
- wipe out excess grease
- pre-rinse only if the item is coated in something that would otherwise ruin the whole wash batch

The less grime you put in the wash water, the longer that water stays useful.

</section>

<section id="drying-storage">

## Drying and Storage

Drying is part of cleaning. If items stay wet, they re-contaminate more easily and start to smell.

### Drying rules

- air-dry when possible
- stand plates on edge or upside down
- keep cups open-side down only if the surface underneath is clean
- do not stack wet items tightly
- do not wipe with a dirty towel

### Storage rules

- store only fully dry items
- keep clean dishes away from trash, pests, and raw food
- cover dishes if flies, dust, or rodents are a problem
- keep cutting boards upright or separated so they can finish drying

### If a towel is used

If you use a towel to handle or dry clean items:

- keep the towel clean
- change it often
- wash it separately from gross cleanup cloths
- do not use a damp towel that has been sitting in the room for days

Drying and storage are what keep a good wash from becoming a dirty dish again.

</section>

<section id="what-not-to-do">

## What Not To Do

- Do not rinse raw meat or raw poultry just to "clean" it.
- Do not use the same cloth for raw meat cleanup and clean dishes.
- Do not put clean dishes on a dirty counter or wet floor.
- Do not stack wet dishes before they are dry.
- Do not keep using wash water after it becomes greasy or cloudy.
- Do not assume a quick rinse makes a contaminated item safe.
- Do not mix bleach with ammonia, vinegar, or random cleaners.
- Do not pour hot grease into a drain, pit, or bucket that will later be used for food cleanup.
- Do not use a sour-smelling sponge or brush forever.
- Do not wipe a freshly sanitized surface with a dirty rag.

If you are unsure whether an item is safe to keep, move it out of circulation until it can be cleaned properly.

</section>

<section id="quick-answers">

## Quick Answers

**How do I wash dishes without a sink?**  
Use a basin, bucket, or tub. Scrape first, wash with soap, rinse with clean water, and air-dry.

**How do I clean greasy pots with little water?**  
Scrape off grease first, soak briefly in hot soapy water, scrub, rinse, and dry right away.

**How do I sanitize a cutting board after raw meat?**  
Wash it well first, rinse, then sanitize. For bleach: 1 tablespoon unscented household bleach per gallon of water, keep wet 1 minute, air-dry. For boiling water: pour at a full rolling boil, keep wet 30 seconds. Only use methods the board can tolerate.

**What should I do after vomiting or diarrhea contamination?**  
Keep the item separate from ordinary dishes, clean it with soap and water, sanitize it if appropriate, or discard it if you cannot clean it safely.

**How do I keep dishes clean after washing?**  
Air-dry fully, store them away from trash and pests, and do not mix them back into dirty cleanup areas.
