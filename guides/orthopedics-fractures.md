:::warning
Skeletal traction, fracture reduction, and splinting carry risks of nerve damage, vascular injury, compartment syndrome, and improper healing. These procedures require medical supervision. Compartment syndrome is a surgical emergency — watch for the 5 P's: Pain, Pallor, Pulselessness, Paresthesia, Paralysis.
:::

<section id="section1">

## 1\. Fracture Types and Assessment

Understanding fracture type guides treatment approach. Fractures range from stable breaks requiring simple immobilization to life-threatening injuries requiring emergency surgery.

### Classification by Bone Integrity

#### Closed Fracture (Simple Fracture)

-   **Definition:** Bone is broken but skin is intact.
-   **Appearance:** Swelling, bruising, deformity, but no open wound.
-   **Infection risk:** Low, but can still develop from improper handling.
-   **Examples:** Most simple arm breaks, leg breaks without penetrating skin.
-   **Treatment:** Splint, reduce if needed, immobilize.

#### Open Fracture (Compound Fracture)

-   **Definition:** Bone is broken AND skin is broken; bone may protrude through skin or internal fragments wound soft tissues.
-   **Appearance:** Open wound, possibly bone visible, severe swelling, high contamination risk.
-   **Infection risk:** Critical. Bone exposed to contamination develops osteomyelitis (bone infection) if not cleaned and treated.
-   **Examples:** High-velocity trauma, gunshot wounds, crushing injuries.
-   **Treatment:** EMERGENCY. Irrigate, debride, give antibiotics, and arrange surgery.
-   **First aid:** Cover wound with sterile dressing (do not try to push bone back in), elevate limb, apply gentle traction if possible, arrange emergency evacuation.

## 2\. Neurovascular Assessment (CMS)

Before and after any intervention, assess Circulation, Motion, and Sensation (CMS) to ensure the limb is viable and no nerves/vessels are damaged.

### Circulation Check

-   **Pulse:** Check pulse distal to fracture (wrist for arm fractures, ankle for leg fractures). Compare to uninjured side.
-   **Color:** Look for pale, mottled, or cyanotic (blue) skin—indicates poor circulation.
-   **Temperature:** Touch skin—cold limb indicates poor blood flow.
-   **Capillary refill:** Press fingernail or skin; color should return within 2 seconds when released. Longer indicates poor perfusion.
-   **Critical sign:** No pulse + pale/cold limb = medical emergency. Limb will die without circulation. Attempt gentle traction-reduction to restore blood flow immediately. If traction fails, evacuate emergently.

### Motion Check

-   **Active motion:** Ask patient to wiggle toes/fingers. Inability may indicate nerve injury or patient reluctance from pain.
-   **Passive motion:** Gently move digits; patient should feel sensation. Paralysis suggests nerve damage.
-   **Critical nerves:** Radial nerve (wrist drop if injured), ulnar nerve (claw hand), median nerve (thumb opposition loss), peroneal nerve (foot drop), tibial nerve (calf/foot sensation loss).

### Sensation Check

-   **Test by area:** Light touch on fingers/toes distal to fracture. Compare sides.
-   **Dermatomes:** Understand nerve distribution—specific areas served by specific nerves.
-   **Paresthesia:** Tingling or "pins and needles" usually indicates swelling around nerve (not permanent damage). Will improve with ice and elevation.
-   **Complete numbness:** Suggests significant nerve injury. Document clearly and aim for evacuation.

**CMS changes after reduction:** If circulation, motion, or sensation worsens after splinting or reduction, IMMEDIATELY loosen splint/cast. Tight immobilization can cause compartment syndrome (swelling builds pressure inside fascial compartment, cutting off circulation, causing tissue death). Symptoms: severe pain out of proportion to injury, pain with passive stretch of muscles, paresthesia, eventual paralysis and tissue death.

</section>

## 3\. Splinting Techniques

### When to Splint

-   Any fracture or suspected fracture
-   Dislocations (after reduction)
-   Severe soft tissue injuries with instability
-   Any injury causing significant swelling and pain

### SAM Splint Technique

SAM splints (structural aluminum malleable) are lightweight, reusable, and moldable. Without SAM splints, improvise with rigid materials.

#### Basic Application

1.  **Measure:** Compare splint to uninjured limb to determine proper length.
2.  **Pad:** Wrap splint with soft material (cloth, foam, clothing) to prevent pressure sores.
3.  **Mold:** Shape splint to limb contours while applying to injured limb.
4.  **Secure:** Wrap with elastic bandage or cloth strips in figure-8 pattern. Not too tight—should slip one finger under wrapping.
5.  **Support:** Elevate limb on pillows or sling.
6.  **Check CMS:** Before and after securing, verify circulation, motion, sensation.

### Improvised Splinting Materials

### Specific Splinting Patterns

#### Clavicle Fracture (Figure-8 Sling)

1.  Position patient upright with shoulders back.
2.  Start with cloth strip around waist height, one end over each shoulder.
3.  Cross ends behind back, bring up under opposite armpit.
4.  Pull snug to draw shoulders back and support arm. Tie in front of chest.
5.  Have patient wear arm in sling against body for 2-3 weeks.
6.  Gradually increase motion as pain decreases.

#### Finger Fractures (Buddy Taping)

1.  Pad between fingers with thin cloth to prevent skin irritation.
2.  Tape injured finger to adjacent uninjured finger with elastic or cloth tape.
3.  Wrap around both fingers together, leaving joints free to move slightly.
4.  Ensure tape is snug but not cutting off circulation.
5.  Injured finger moves with uninjured "buddy" finger, providing support.
6.  Leave in place 3-4 weeks for healing.

#### Arm Fracture (Above and Below Joint Splint)

1.  Create splint from cardboard, wood, or rigid material from elbow to wrist for forearm fracture.
2.  For upper arm, extend from shoulder to wrist.
3.  Pad all contact areas.
4.  Apply to underside of arm.
5.  Secure with multiple wraps across length, including above and below fracture site.
6.  Place arm in sling for elevation and further support.

#### Leg Fracture (Long Leg Splint)

1.  Create splint from medial side of leg (inner thigh to inside of ankle).
2.  Can use rolled magazine, cardboard, or SAM splint.
3.  Pad heavily to prevent pressure on bone prominences.
4.  Secure with wraps every 3-4 inches along entire length.
5.  Do not wrap so tightly that circulation is compromised.
6.  Elevate on pillows to reduce swelling.

## 4\. Traction and Immobilization

For severe fractures (femur, tibia) that are angulated or displaced, traction realigns the limb and relieves pain. Skin traction uses adhesive strips; skeletal traction uses pins through bone (requires surgery).

### Skin Traction for Femur Fracture (Buck's Traction)

#### Components

-   **Traction boot/adhesive strips:** Applied to lower leg below knee, spanning from ankle to knee.
-   **Pulleys:** Suspended at end of bed.
-   **Rope:** Attached to boot/strips, runs through pulley.
-   **Weights:** Attached to rope to provide traction force.

#### Setup

1.  **Patient position:** Supine on bed/stretcher with hip flexed 20-30 degrees, knee flexed 20-30 degrees.
2.  **Adhesive application:** If using adhesive strips, clean and dry leg, apply strips bilaterally (both sides) along entire lower leg from ankle to just below knee.
3.  **Attachment:** Attach rope to traction boot or adhesive strips.
4.  **Pulley system:** Run rope through pulley at end of bed, then vertically down to weights.
5.  **Weight calculation:** General rule is 10 lb per person's body weight divided by 10. For 70 kg person: 7-10 lbs. Start low and increase gradually as tolerated.
6.  **Suspension:** Ensure limb hangs freely; buttocks should be slightly lifted off bed.

#### Pain Management

-   Pain decreases significantly as traction is applied (muscles relax, fracture fragments align).
-   Give pain medication before applying traction.
-   Gradual weight increase more tolerable than sudden application.
-   X-ray or physical exam should show improved alignment after 24-48 hours of traction.

#### Complications

-   **Skin breakdown:** Check frequently for sores, especially at ankle and knee. Reposition strips if skin reddening appears.
-   **Compartment syndrome:** Monitor for severe pain, swelling, paresthesia. Loosen traction immediately if suspected.
-   **DVT (deep vein thrombosis):** Immobility increases clot risk. Teach passive leg exercises, apply calf compression if available.

### Simple Traction for Tibial (Shin) Fracture

-   Create improvised traction using cloth strips and weights (buckets filled with water, sand).
-   Foot piece can be carved from wood.
-   Elevate fracture on pillows with gentle downward pull via weights.
-   Less sophisticated but functional for temporary immobilization pending professional care.

## 5\. Cast Making and Care

Plaster of Paris casts provide rigid immobilization. Without commercial plaster, alternatives exist but are less ideal.

### Plaster of Paris Cast Application

#### Materials

-   Plaster of Paris bandages (rolls of cloth impregnated with calcium sulfate)
-   Padding material (cotton, foam, cloth strips)
-   Water (room temperature)
-   Bucket or basin for soaking
-   Scissors

#### Technique

1.  **Prepare limb:** Place stockinette (thin cloth sleeve) over limb. Apply 1-2 inches of soft padding over all bony prominences (ankle, knee, wrist, elbow—anywhere bone is close to skin).
2.  **Soak plaster:** Unroll plaster bandages. Soak in room temperature water for 3-5 seconds until completely wet. Squeeze out excess water.
3.  **Apply layers:** Wrap plaster around limb, starting at distal (far) end. Each wrap should overlap previous by 50%. Apply 4-6 layers minimum for lower extremity, 2-3 for upper extremity. Mold plaster gently to limb as you apply.
4.  **Finish:** Fold edges of stockinette over cast edge for neat appearance and comfort.
5.  **Drying:** Cast hardens in 30-60 minutes (faster in warm/dry conditions). Do not move limb during drying.
6.  **Trim:** Once hardened, trim cast edges as needed for mobility and access.

### Alternative Cast Materials (Without Commercial Plaster)

### Cast Care

-   **Elevation:** Keep cast elevated on pillows for first 48 hours to reduce swelling.
-   **CMS checks:** Check circulation, motion, sensation regularly. Swelling inside cast can cut off blood flow.
-   **Loosening:** If patient reports tightness, pain, numbness, or coldness, loosen cast wrapping immediately. Do not cut into cast; unwrap outer layer.
-   **Skin care:** Keep skin around cast edges clean and dry. Prevent moisture accumulation which causes infection.
-   **Padding:** If pressure sores develop, pad inside of cast around sore area.
-   **Duration:** Most fractures require 4-8 weeks of casting depending on type and location. Immobilization is lost if cast gets wet or breaks.
-   **Removal:** Once healing is adequate (assess by minimal pain, no swelling, fracture stability on exam), remove cast. Limb will be weak—gradual mobilization required.

## 6\. Joint Injuries and Dislocations

### RICE Protocol (Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation)

For sprains and minor joint injuries:

-   **Rest:** Immobilize joint, avoid use for 24-48 hours.
-   **Ice:** Cold compress 15 minutes every 2-3 hours for first 48 hours. Reduces swelling and pain.
-   **Compression:** Elastic bandage wrap. Not so tight as to cut off circulation.
-   **Elevation:** Raise joint above heart level to reduce swelling.

### Shoulder Dislocation (Anterior—90% of cases)

#### Recognition

-   Severe pain and inability to move arm
-   Arm held in fixed position (often internally rotated)
-   Visible "lump" at front of shoulder (humeral head)
-   Mechanism: Usually from fall onto outstretched arm or direct blow

#### Reduction Technique

**External Rotation Method (Cunningham technique):**

1.  Patient lies supine on bed.
2.  Flex patient's elbow to 90 degrees, keeping upper arm at 90 degrees to body.
3.  Slowly externally rotate forearm (bring hand toward head). Patient relaxes.
4.  Continue rotating slowly until shoulder "pops" back into place (usually at 80-90 degrees external rotation).
5.  Takes 5-15 minutes of gentle pressure; do not force.
6.  Once reduced, immobilize arm in sling for 2-3 weeks.

#### Alternative: Stimson Technique

-   Patient lies prone on high surface (bed, stretcher) with dislocated arm hanging off edge.
-   Attach 10-15 lb weight to wrist.
-   Muscle fatigue and gravity slowly reduce dislocation over 15-30 minutes.
-   Simple, often painless, but slower.

### Finger/Toe Dislocation

#### Reduction

1.  Support injured hand/foot. Grasp dislocated digit above and below joint.
2.  Apply slow longitudinal traction to lengthen digit, then gently push distal phalanx back into alignment.
3.  Ice immediately after reduction.
4.  Buddy tape to adjacent digit for 2-3 weeks (see finger fracture technique above).

### Patella (Kneecap) Dislocation

#### Recognition

-   Severe knee pain, knee won't straighten
-   Patella visibly displaced to outer side of knee
-   Patient holding knee in flexed position

#### Reduction

1.  Patient supine. Straighten leg gently while supporting under knee.
2.  As leg straightens, quadriceps muscle contracts and patella usually "pops" back into place.
3.  If not, apply gentle medial (inward) pressure on patella while extending leg.
4.  Once reduced, splint knee in extended position for 4-6 weeks.

</section>

## 7\. Spinal Precautions

Spinal injuries can cause permanent paralysis if not handled carefully. Always suspect spine injury in high-impact trauma.

### When to Suspect Spinal Injury

-   High-impact trauma (motor vehicle accident, high fall, diving into water)
-   Severe mechanism with any head or torso injury
-   Any loss of consciousness
-   Midline neck or back pain
-   Altered mental status (may not be able to report symptoms)
-   Obvious deformity of spine

### Initial Management

#### Immobilization

-   **Do not move patient.** Preserve spinal alignment in position found.
-   **Cervical spine:** Prevent neck movement. Support head with hands, sandbags, or improvised collar.
-   **Torso:** Pad around body to maintain alignment.
-   **Handling:** If movement necessary, use "log-rolling" technique—entire spine moves as unit, no bending.

#### Log-Rolling Technique

1.  Patient supine on hard surface. Line up 3-4 rescuers along one side.
2.  One person holds head/neck, one at torso, one at pelvis, one at legs.
3.  On command, all rescuers roll patient as single unit. Spine stays straight, no twisting.
4.  Roll slowly and gently.
5.  Use to assess back for injury or move patient to stretcher.

### Improvised Cervical Collar

1.  Measure from chin to chest, encircling neck.
2.  Roll newspaper or cloth to 2-3 inches thick.
3.  Wrap around neck, securing with tape. Should support chin and prevent flexion/extension.
4.  Ensure it does NOT cut off breathing or carotid arteries. Should fit loosely—one finger fits under.
5.  For longer immobilization, use foam or soft cervical collar if available.

### Evacuation

Spinal injury patients require professional imaging and evaluation. Do not attempt definitive treatment in field. Arrange evacuation as soon as safely possible while maintaining strict spinal precautions.

</section>

## Common Mistakes in Fracture Management

:::danger
Applying plaster casts or splints too tightly is one of the leading causes of permanent disability in field medicine. Compartment syndrome can develop within hours of an overly tight application. If a patient reports severe pain, numbness, coldness, or purple/mottled skin distal to a cast or splint, immediately loosen or remove it. Tight immobilization can cause tissue necrosis irreversible within 4-6 hours.
:::

1. **Not checking CMS before and after intervention.** Always verify circulation, motion, and sensation before and immediately after splinting, reduction, or traction. Delayed recognition of compromised limb viability leads to unnecessary amputation.

2. **Forcing reduction too aggressively.** Gentleness is critical. Excessive force can tear blood vessels, nerves, and muscles. If reduction does not occur with gentle, steady pressure within 5-15 minutes, stop and immobilize—surgical intervention is needed.

3. **Ignoring open fractures.** An open fracture is an infection waiting to happen. Even a small skin break over a fracture site requires immediate irrigation, antibiotics, and professional care. Infection (osteomyelitis) develops in 5-30% of open fractures treated without antibiotics.

4. **Immobilizing without padding.** Direct contact between hard splint and skin causes pressure sores within 24-48 hours. Always pad bony prominences (ankle, heel, elbow, wrist) with at least 1 inch of soft material.

5. **Failing to document baseline assessment.** Before any intervention, record color, temperature, pulses, motor function, and sensation. This baseline is essential if complications develop later and you must justify your actions to receiving physicians.

6. **Not elevating fractured limbs.** Swelling is inevitable. Gravity pulls fluid into the limb, worsening swelling and increasing compartment syndrome risk. Elevate above heart level for first 48 hours after any fracture.

7. **Waiting to splint "severe" pain without first medication.** Patients in severe pain tense muscles, making reduction and splinting more difficult and more painful. Administer pain medication 20-30 minutes before attempting reduction.

</section>

## 8\. Recognition and Assessment

Accurate diagnosis of fracture type, location, and severity guides all subsequent treatment. The goal is to identify if the injury requires emergency evacuation or can be managed in the field.

### Primary Assessment Sequence

1. **Mechanism of injury:** How did the trauma occur? High-speed impact, crush injury, or low-energy fall? This predicts fracture severity.

2. **Pain location:** Exactly where does it hurt? Palpate carefully to identify the fracture site. Gentle, localized pressure over the fracture typically elicits sharp tenderness.

3. **Deformity:** Compare injured limb to uninjured side. Look for shortening, angulation, rotation, or swelling.

4. **Swelling and bruising:** Rapid onset swelling suggests significant soft tissue damage and fracture. Bruising over bony prominences confirms trauma to that area.

5. **CMS assessment:** Check circulation, motion, sensation before ANY manipulation (see Section 2 above).

6. **Bone crepitus:** Gently press on fracture site. A grinding sensation ("crepitus") confirms fracture. Do not deliberately cause crepitus—it is painful and unnecessary if diagnosis is clear.

### Fracture Severity Classification

:::info-box
**Stable fracture:** Patient can bear some weight, minimal swelling, no neurovascular compromise. Can typically be managed in field with splinting.

**Unstable fracture:** Patient cannot bear weight, significant swelling, possible deformity, possible neurovascular compromise. May require reduction and urgent evacuation.

**Life-threatening fracture:** Multiple fractures, polytrauma, open fracture with massive bleeding, femur fracture (high risk of internal bleeding), spinal injury with neurological deficit. EMERGENCY—arrange evacuation immediately.
:::

</section>

## 10\. Step-by-Step Procedure: Complete Fracture Management Protocol

### Phase 1: Initial Assessment and Preparation (0-10 minutes)

1. **Scene safety:** Ensure scene is safe. Perform primary survey (airway, breathing, circulation, disability, exposure).

2. **Patient history:** Ask about mechanism of injury, time of injury, last tetanus status, allergies, medications.

3. **Visual inspection:** Note skin color, temperature, gross deformity, open wounds, swelling.

4. **Neurovascular assessment:** Check and document circulation, motion, sensation distal to fracture. Record baseline before any intervention.

5. **Pain assessment:** Ask patient to rate pain 0-10. This is your baseline for treatment efficacy.

6. **Prepare medications:** If reduction will be attempted, calculate pain medication dose and have it ready.

### Phase 2: Pain Management and Reduction (10-30 minutes)

:::tip
Administer analgesics 20-30 minutes before attempting reduction. Waiting allows medication to take effect and muscles to relax, making reduction easier and less painful.
:::

1. **Administer pain medication** (see Dosage section below).

2. **Gentle traction:** Apply continuous longitudinal traction to lengthen limb and reduce fragments. Take 3-5 minutes to gently distract the fracture.

3. **Reduction attempt:** Using technique appropriate to fracture type (see specific sections), attempt to realign bone fragments. Move slowly. Patient may feel "pop" or "clunk" when fragments reduce. If reduction does not occur within 5-15 minutes of gentle pressure, discontinue—further forcing risks tissue damage.

4. **Re-check CMS:** Immediately after reduction, reassess circulation, motion, sensation. If worse, reverse the reduction immediately.

5. **Immobilize:** Once satisfactory reduction achieved (or if no reduction attempted due to stable fracture), apply splint or cast.

### Phase 3: Immobilization (30-60 minutes)

1. **Prepare materials:** Gather all needed padding, splint material, tape. If using plaster, soak rolls in water.

2. **Apply padding:** Cover all bony prominences with at least 1 inch of soft padding.

3. **Apply splint or cast:** Use appropriate technique for fracture location (see specific sections for SAM splint, improvised, or plaster).

4. **Wrap securely:** Ensure immobilization is snug but not tight. Should be able to slip one finger under wrapping.

5. **Elevate:** Position limb on pillows above heart level.

6. **Re-check CMS:** Before finishing, verify circulation, motion, sensation again. Mark skin with pen at the location you tested sensation—mark location of pulses you felt. This allows follow-up assessment.

7. **Document:** Record immobilization type, CMS status post-procedure, pain level post-reduction, any medications given with dose and time.

</section>

## 11\. Dosage and Medication Reference

:::danger
Do not give strong opioids without establishing baseline respiratory status, oxygen saturation, and mental alertness. Risk of respiratory depression is severe in hypoxic or altitude settings. Monitor patient continuously during and after medication administration.
:::

### Pain Medication Dosing for Fracture Reduction

**Ibuprofen (NSAID):**
- Dose: 400-600 mg orally
- Onset: 30-45 minutes
- Duration: 4-6 hours
- Repeat: Every 6 hours as needed (max 2400 mg/day)
- Advantages: Safe, reduces inflammation, available over-the-counter
- Disadvantages: Slower onset, less potent for severe pain
- Contraindications: GI ulcer, kidney disease, allergy

**Acetaminophen (Paracetamol):**
- Dose: 500-1000 mg orally
- Onset: 30-60 minutes
- Duration: 4-6 hours
- Repeat: Every 6 hours (max 3000-4000 mg/day depending on region)
- Advantages: Fewer GI side effects, safe in allergy to NSAIDs
- Disadvantages: Limited anti-inflammatory effect, slower onset
- Note: Never combine with opioids without verifying total daily acetaminophen dose

:::warning
Acetaminophen overdose causes irreversible liver damage within 24-48 hours. Verify total daily intake when combining with other medications (many over-the-counter pain relievers contain acetaminophen). If patient has history of liver disease or heavy alcohol use, avoid acetaminophen entirely. Maximum safe dose is 3000-4000 mg per day depending on region and patient health status.
:::

**Morphine (Strong Opioid):**
- Dose: 5-10 mg IM/IV; 10-20 mg oral (if available)
- Onset: 15-30 minutes IM, 30-60 minutes oral
- Duration: 3-4 hours
- Advantages: Potent pain relief, anxiolytic effect
- Disadvantages: Risk of respiratory depression, nausea, constipation, addiction potential
- Contraindications: Respiratory depression, low oxygen saturation, head injury, hypotension
- Monitoring: Watch respiratory rate (should stay >12/min). Have naloxone available as reversal agent.

**Naloxone (Opioid Reversal):**
- Dose: 0.4-2 mg IV/IM (use if morphine overdose causes respiratory depression)
- Onset: 2-3 minutes
- Repeat: Every 2-3 minutes until respiratory rate normalizes
- Note: Much shorter duration than morphine—repeated doses may be needed

**Midazolam (Benzodiazepine—sedation):**
- Dose: 2-5 mg IV/IM; 5-10 mg oral
- Onset: 5-10 minutes IV, 15-30 minutes oral
- Duration: 30-60 minutes IV, 1-2 hours oral
- Use: For severe anxiety or if stronger sedation needed during reduction
- Contraindications: Respiratory depression, sleep apnea
- Note: Can be combined with low-dose morphine for synergistic effect

### Antibiotic Dosing for Open Fractures (Contamination Prevention)

:::warning
All open fractures require antibiotics to prevent osteomyelitis. First-line is a broad-spectrum antibiotic covering skin and gut flora. If available, start IV antibiotics immediately upon recognition of open fracture.
:::

**Cephalexin (Oral alternative or if IV unavailable):**
- Dose: 500 mg orally every 6 hours (2000 mg/day)
- Duration: 10-14 days for open fracture prophylaxis
- Effective against Staphylococcus, Streptococcus

**Ceftriaxone (IV, preferred for open fracture):**
- Dose: 1 g IV every 8-12 hours (2-4 g/day)
- Excellent for gram-positive and gram-negative organisms
- Note: Add aminoglycoside (e.g., gentamicin 5-7 mg/kg once daily) if heavily contaminated

**Gentamicin (Aminoglycoside):**
- Dose: 5-7 mg/kg IV once daily
- Monitor: Kidney function, hearing (ototoxic)
- Use: For heavily contaminated or very high-risk wounds

**Tetanus Prophylaxis:**
- If wound is clean and patient vaccinated within 10 years: No booster needed
- If wound is clean and patient not vaccinated or >10 years since booster: Give Td (tetanus-diphtheria) 0.5 mL IM
- If wound is dirty/contaminated: Always give tetanus booster regardless of history

## 12\. Complications and When to Stop

### Acute Complications During Immobilization

#### Compartment Syndrome (Emergency)

:::danger
Compartment syndrome is a true surgical emergency. Tissue death begins within 4-6 hours of onset. If suspected, immediately loosen or remove splint/cast. This is the ONLY treatment available in field—the patient will die or lose limb function if circulation is not restored immediately.
:::

**Signs:**
- Severe pain out of proportion to visible injury
- Pain with passive (gentle, external) stretching of muscles in that compartment
- Paresthesia (tingling, "pins and needles")
- Skin pallor, mottling, or purple discoloration
- Eventually: paralysis and coldness (indicates tissue death)

**When to suspect:**
- Very tight cast or splint applied
- Massive swelling after fracture
- High-energy crush injury
- Fracture requiring heavy traction

**Immediate action:**
1. Remove all wrapping, splint, or cast immediately
2. Elevate limb above heart level
3. If already cast, cut through cast without cutting skin—place scissors under cast, slide length of cast, then cut through plaster
4. Re-assess CMS every 15 minutes
5. If pain remains severe or symptoms worsen, consider open fasciotomy (incision through fascia to relieve pressure)—this requires surgical expertise and should be attempted only if evacuation is not possible

**Fasciotomy procedure (emergency only):**
- Make 2-3 small incisions (2-3 inches each) over middle of each muscle compartment in limb
- Incisions should be longitudinal (lengthwise)
- This releases pressure allowing blood flow to be restored
- This is a last-resort measure for limb salvage; professional surgery is needed for proper closure later
- Risk of infection is high—ensure maximum cleanliness, use antibiotics

#### Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)

**Signs:**
- Unilateral (one-sided) calf or thigh swelling
- Warmth, redness over calf
- Calf pain with dorsiflexion of foot ("Homan's sign")
- Low-grade fever

**Prevention:**
- Early mobilization (even passive motion) of non-fractured joints
- Leg elevation
- Calf compression if available
- Encourage patient to move toes/feet regularly
- Adequate hydration

**Treatment (limited in field):**
- Elevation and rest
- Warm compress may reduce pain (unlike acute fracture which needs ice)
- Anticoagulation (heparin or warfarin) if available—prevents clot growth and embolization
- Monitor for signs of pulmonary embolism: sudden shortness of breath, chest pain, tachycardia

#### Cast/Splint Pressure Sores

**Signs:**
- Redness, blistering, or skin breakdown under cast/splint
- Localized pain or tenderness

**Prevention:**
- Pad all contact areas thoroughly
- Monitor frequently for pressure signs
- Loosen wrapping if skin appears red or patient reports discomfort

**Treatment:**
- Loosen or remove cast
- Pad the sore area with extra padding
- Reapply cast once swelling around sore decreases
- If breakdown is severe, allow wound to air dry and heal before reapplying immobilization

### Complications from Reduction Attempts

#### Failed Reduction

**Definition:** Bone fragments do not return to anatomical alignment despite gentle traction attempts.

**Action:**
- Stop attempting reduction
- Immobilize in current position
- Document attempted procedure and result
- Arrange urgent evacuation for surgical reduction
- Do NOT use force—repeated forceful attempts cause neurovascular injury

#### Neurovascular Injury During Reduction

**Signs:**
- Loss of pulse distal to fracture
- Sudden onset paralysis or numbness
- Skin becomes cold and pale/mottled

**Causes:**
- Excessive traction force
- Bone fragment lacerating vessel or nerve
- Swelling compressing structures

**Immediate action:**
1. Gently reverse the reduction—this often restores circulation immediately
2. If circulation does not return, attempt gentle re-reduction with slightly different angle
3. If still unsuccessful, immobilize in current position and arrange emergency evacuation
4. Limb viability decreases markedly after 4-6 hours without adequate circulation

### Chronic Complications

#### Malunion (Improper Healing)

**Definition:** Bone heals in incorrect alignment.

**Prevention:**
- Accurate reduction before immobilization
- Maintaining proper alignment throughout immobilization period
- Regular re-imaging if available
- Gentle mobilization once initial healing begins (around 3-4 weeks for upper extremity, 6+ weeks for lower)

**Recognition:**
- Persistent deformity after cast removal
- Limited motion in adjacent joints
- Limb-length discrepancy in lower extremity

#### Non-union (Bone Fails to Heal)

**Risk factors:**
- Open fractures (especially contaminated)
- Bone ends not in contact
- Smoking
- Poor nutrition
- Infection (osteomyelitis)

**Signs:**
- Persistent pain and motion at fracture site after 8+ weeks
- X-ray shows no bridging bone callus
- Patient unable to bear weight

**Prevention:**
- Ensure anatomic reduction
- Maintain immobilization until adequate bridging callus visible
- Screen for and treat infection immediately

#### Infection/Osteomyelitis

**Most common in open fractures.** Presents 48 hours to weeks after injury.

**Signs:**
- Fever, chills, malaise
- Localized swelling, warmth, tenderness at fracture site
- Drainage from wound (especially if open fracture)
- X-ray shows bone destruction, widened fracture gap

**Prevention:**
- Immediate antibiotics for all open fractures
- Thorough wound cleaning/debridement
- Maintain antiseptic technique during wound care

**Treatment:**
- IV antibiotics (4-6 weeks typical course)
- Surgical debridement if available
- Immobilization continues during antibiotic treatment

### Post-Cast Rehabilitation

After cast removal, the limb will be significantly weakened. Swelling may recur initially. Gradual progression is essential.

:::tip
The first 2 weeks after cast removal are critical. Gentle, frequent motion prevents re-stiffening. Expect significant weakness—muscles atrophy in just 4-6 weeks of immobilization.
:::

:::warning
Returning to full weight-bearing or use too rapidly is a leading cause of re-fracture and delayed union. Many patients are eager to use their limb and push too hard, undoing weeks of healing. Stick to prescribed progressive timeframes: non-weight-bearing periods must be honored, and gradual increases in activity must be pain-guided (no sharp pain, only dull muscle soreness is acceptable).
:::

**Week 1-2 post-cast:**
- Gentle active range-of-motion exercises 5-6 times daily (move joint through comfortable range yourself)
- Ice 15 minutes after activity to prevent swelling recurrence
- Elevation still helpful, especially lower extremity
- No resistance or weight-bearing yet
- Continue padding if skin is fragile from cast

**Week 3-4:**
- Progress to active-assisted motion (you guide joint through motion with assistance)
- Begin gentle resistance exercises (no weights, just motion against gravity or soft resistance)
- Weight-bearing as tolerated (walking with crutches for lower extremity)
- Expect muscle soreness—this is normal

**Week 5+:**
- Progressive weight-bearing or use
- Gradual return to strengthening exercises
- Return to normal activities as tolerated
- Full functional recovery typically 3-6 months

### Swelling Management

:::info-box
Swelling commonly recurs 2-4 weeks after cast removal when patient begins mobilizing. This is NOT a sign of fracture re-breaking. Swelling is the body's normal response to motion and mobilization after prolonged immobilization. Manage with ice, elevation, and compression wraps.
:::

- Elevation: Keep limb above heart level when resting
- Ice: 15 minutes every 2-3 hours as needed
- Compression: Elastic bandage wrap reduces swelling (not as tight as initial post-fracture wrap)
- Activity modification: Avoid prolonged dependent position (dangling arm or leg)
- Gradual progression: Increase activity gradually to prevent rapid swelling recurrence

## 14\. Troubleshooting

### Problem: Patient Reports Severe Pain After Splinting (Splint Appears Properly Applied)

**Possible causes:**
1. Splint is too tight (most common)
2. Inadequate padding over bony prominence
3. Fracture fragments need repositioning
4. Developing compartment syndrome

**Solution:**
- Loosen splint immediately—unwrap outer layer and reassess
- Check for pressure marks on skin
- If pain resolves with loosening: reapply with less tension
- If pain persists: consider compartment syndrome (see Complications section)—may require splint removal and elevation
- If pain is localized to fracture site: consider inadequate reduction—gentle re-traction and repositioning may help

### Problem: Cast/Splint Becomes Wet or Damaged

**Plaster cast (water-soluble):**
- Once wet, structural integrity compromised
- Attempt to dry with heat (hair dryer) if partial wetting
- If severely soaked: remove and reapply new cast
- Skin underneath must be dried to prevent maceration (softening, breakdown)

**SAM or fiberglass splint (water-resistant):**
- Allow to air dry
- Can be washed without major structural loss
- Pad and re-secure if wrapping loosens

**Prevention:**
- Wrap cast/splint in plastic during bathing or rain
- Instruct patient to keep dry

### Problem: Swelling Increasing 24-48 Hours After Immobilization

**Normal vs. concerning:**
- Some increase expected as inflammatory response peaks at 24-48 hours
- Swelling should plateau and begin decreasing by day 3-4
- If swelling continues to increase or becomes severe: concern for compartment syndrome

**Management:**
- Ensure elevation above heart level
- Apply ice 15 minutes every 2-3 hours
- Loosen splint/cast if patient reports tightness or pain
- Re-check CMS
- If severe swelling with pain: consider removing splint, elevating, icing for 24 hours, then reapplying once swelling peaks

### Problem: Patient Reports Numbness or Tingling in Fingers/Toes After Splinting

**Possible causes:**
1. Swelling compressing nerve
2. Splint placement compressing nerve
3. Proper reduction not achieved (nerve damage from fracture)

**Immediate actions:**
1. Loosen splint
2. Elevate limb
3. Ice to reduce swelling
4. Re-check motor function (can patient move digits?)
5. Re-test sensation after 30 minutes

**If numbness resolves with loosening:** Splint was too tight. Reapply more loosely.

**If numbness persists:** Likely nerve injury from fracture or from reduction attempt. Document carefully and arrange professional evaluation.

### Problem: Patient Cannot Tolerate Pain Medication Dose

**Possible reasons:**
1. Allergy or side effect (nausea, dizziness)
2. Dose too high
3. Medication not appropriate for patient (elderly, renal disease, etc.)

**Solutions:**
- Reduce dose by 25-50%
- Switch medication class (NSAID to opioid, or vice versa)
- Add non-medication pain control: elevation, ice, immobilization, distraction, breathing exercises
- Allow longer time between doses if pain is controlled adequately
- Document all attempts and patient tolerances

## 15\. Field Adaptations (When Resources Are Limited)

### Without Commercial Plaster or Fiberglass Casts

**Papier-mâché option:**
1. Tear newspaper into 2-3 inch strips
2. Prepare paste: Mix flour with water (1 part flour, 2 parts water) and heat until thick, OR use wheat glue if available
3. Dip strips in paste
4. Wrap around padded limb, overlapping each layer by 50%
5. Apply 6-8 layers for rigidity
6. Allow to dry for 24-48 hours in warm, dry place
7. Trim edges as needed
8. Less water-resistant than plaster but functional for field immobilization

**Starch-based paste (if no flour):**
1. Mix cornstarch or wheat starch with water until thick paste consistency
2. Soak cloth strips
3. Wrap and layer
4. Drying takes longer (48 hours) and paste may soften if exposed to moisture
5. Reinforce with splint if additional rigidity needed

**Reinforced cloth option:**
1. Layer thick cloth strips with tape and wooden or cardboard splints
2. Create sandwich: padding, cloth, splint, cloth, more padding
3. Wrap tightly with tape and elastic bandages
4. Less ideal than solid cast but provides functional immobilization

### Without Pain Medication

**Non-pharmacological pain control:**
1. **Ice:** Apply for 15 minutes every 2-3 hours. Reduces swelling and numbs area.
2. **Elevation:** Gravity reduces swelling and pain.
3. **Immobilization:** Prevents motion causing pain.
4. **Distraction:** Conversation, music, focusing on non-injury body parts.
5. **Breathing techniques:** Slow, deep breathing reduces pain perception.
6. **Positioning:** Find comfortable position before attempting reduction.
7. **Patience:** Allow 30-60 minutes of gentle traction before attempting reduction—muscles naturally relax with time.

**Herbal/traditional pain relief (limited efficacy but worth attempting):**
1. **Willow bark:** Contains salicin, similar to aspirin. Simmer bark 5-10 minutes, drink as tea.
2. **Ginger:** Anti-inflammatory. Steep fresh ginger in hot water, drink several times daily.
3. **Turmeric:** Contains curcumin (anti-inflammatory). Mix with warm milk or water.
4. **Ice from snow/water:** Numbing effect, reduces swelling.

### Without Proper Slings

**Improvised arm sling:**
1. Use cloth strip or piece of clothing (t-shirt, scarf)
2. Tie around neck
3. Support forearm in sling
4. Adjust height so arm is at heart level
5. Can secure arm to body with additional tape or cloth wrap for additional stability

**Improvised leg support:**
1. Elevate on stacked pillows, blankets, or cushions
2. Position slightly flexed (not straight) for comfort
3. Use rolled blanket behind knee
4. Keep elevated when resting

### Traction Without Equipment

**Improvised Buck's traction for femur fracture:**
1. Patient supine on bed with hips flexed 20-30 degrees
2. Tie cloth strips around ankle and just below knee
3. Attach rope to ankle strips
4. Run rope over pulley improvised from stick or branch at end of bed
5. Attach weights: filled water bottles, sand bags, rocks in sling
6. Start with 5-7 lbs and increase gradually to 10-15 lbs
7. Assess reduction by looking for limb lengthening and decreased angulation
8. Monitor for compartment syndrome hourly

### Antibiotics Without IV Access

**Oral antibiotics for open fracture prophylaxis:**
- Cephalexin: 500 mg every 6 hours
- Amoxicillin-clavulanate (if available): 875 mg every 12 hours (covers more anaerobes)
- Course: 10-14 days minimum
- Monitor for fever, swelling, drainage which suggest inadequate coverage
