## Five Actions for This Weekend

This app is a reference tool — like a fire extinguisher or first aid kit. You wouldn't buy a fire extinguisher and leave it in the closet untouched. The same principle applies here. **Do these five concrete things in the next two days.** None take longer than 45 minutes. All significantly increase your odds of surviving or managing a crisis.

### 1. Get CPR Certified (or Recertified)

**Time required:** 4 hours (initial) or 1 hour (recertification)

**Where:** Search "CPR certification near me" or check your local hospital, fire station, American Red Cross chapter, or YMCA. Many community colleges offer it for $30–$60. Some allow you to do the video module at home and pay less for an in-person skills checkpoint.

**Why this matters:** CPR certification is not the same as watching a YouTube video. An instructor physically confirms you are doing chest compressions at the right depth (at least 2 inches), the right rate (100–120 compressions per minute), and in the right position. They also teach you when NOT to do CPR and how to use an AED. During a cardiac arrest, the difference between correct and incorrect CPR is whether someone lives or dies.

**Specific to Minnesota:** Minnesota requires EMTs and health care providers to recertify every 2 years. If you are not licensed, there is no legal requirement to recertify — but medical guidelines recommend refreshing your skills every 2 years because compression depth and hand position fade from memory. If you got certified five years ago, take the refresher.

**After certification:**
- Keep your card somewhere visible (wallet, on your fridge, in your phone case).
- Tell your family members and roommates that you are certified. Witnesses often freeze and don't call 911 because they think someone else will. If they know you are trained, they'll call you.
- In a real emergency, 911 dispatchers can walk you through CPR over the phone if your certification has lapsed. Do not let a lapsed card stop you from trying.

:::warning
CPR effectiveness drops dramatically if you are alone and hesitate to start. If you find someone unresponsive and not breathing normally, call 911 immediately, then start CPR. The risk of harm from CPR is lower than the certainty of death without it. Modern guidelines explicitly state it is safe to use hard, fast compressions on any adult you find unconscious and non-responsive.
:::

### 2. Learn to Shut Off Your Home's Gas and Water

**Time required:** 30 minutes

**Gas:** Walk to your gas meter (usually on the outside of your house, basement, or crawl space). You will see a dial and a valve handle. The handle points along the pipe when gas is ON; turn it 90 degrees (perpendicular to the pipe) to shut it OFF. Practice this once so you know how much force it takes. Do this now, not during a leak when you are panicking.

**Important gas safety rule:** Once you shut off the gas, a licensed technician must turn it back on. Do NOT try to relight your pilot light yourself after shutting off the gas. Call your gas company or a licensed plumber.

**Water:** Most homes have a main water shutoff valve. Check these locations in order:
1. Inside your home near the water meter (often in a basement, crawl space, or utility closet)
2. Outside your home near the street (in a box, often marked "WATER")
3. Inside your home where the main line enters (often near the foundation)

The valve is either a **lever handle** (turn it parallel to the pipe to shut off) or a **round knob** (turn it clockwise). Practice turning it until you feel resistance — do not force it.

**Why this matters:**
- **Gas:** If you smell gas or hear a hiss after an earthquake or explosion, shut off the gas immediately. A gas leak can cause a fire or explosion hours later. If you wait for the gas company to send someone, you risk losing your home.
- **Water:** Burst pipes flood basements and rot structural wood. A ruptured water line inside your home can dump thousands of gallons in 24 hours. If you turn off the main shutoff, you stop the leak while waiting for a plumber.

**After practicing:**
- Tag your shutoffs with bright tape or paint. Write "GAS" or "WATER" on the wall or pipe so you and emergency responders can find them in smoke or darkness.
- Show your family members where these valves are and how to operate them. Do a practice run with each person.

:::tip
Take a photo of your gas meter dial and water meter reading, and text them to yourself. In an emergency, you can confirm the meter is no longer spinning after you shut it off.
:::

### 3. Download Offline Maps of Your County and Surrounding Area

**Time required:** 20 minutes

**How:** This app (Offline Compendium) does not include maps, but open-source offline mapping apps do. The best options are:

1. **OsmAnd+ (Android)** — $3–4 one-time purchase. Download maps for your county and neighboring counties. Very granular (can select state-level or county-level regions). Works entirely offline.
2. **Maps.me (Android and iOS, free)** — Download country-level maps. Less detailed than OsmAnd+ but simpler and works offline.
3. **Gaia GPS (Android and iOS, free tier available)** — Originally designed for hikers. Can download topographic maps offline.

**Installation checklist:**
- Install the app.
- Download maps for your county AND neighboring counties (in case you need to evacuate).
- Open the app once after downloading to confirm the maps loaded.
- Leave the app installed and keep it updated via your app store.

**Why this matters:**
- GPS works without internet (it is a one-way receiver from satellites). But mapping apps need downloaded maps to display your location and routes.
- In a widespread power outage or internet failure, Google Maps stops working. An offline map app still works.
- A printed map is even better (see section on physical printouts below), but a phone map with offline data is faster to use in real time.

**Specific scenarios where you need this:**
- **Flooding:** You need to know which roads are lowest-lying and which neighborhoods drain toward rivers. Offline maps show elevation and water features.
- **Evacuation order:** Police announce an evacuation zone by address range or neighborhood name. Your offline map lets you see if your address is in the zone and what the safest exit routes are.
- **Utilities failure:** If you get lost or your car breaks down and your phone is at 5% battery, you still have a compass and a map.

:::info-box
GPS satellites broadcast location data continuously. Your phone's GPS receiver never connects to the internet — it just listens. You can pinpoint your location on an offline map as long as your phone's battery lasts and it has a clear view of the sky. No cell service required.
:::

### 4. Locate and Test Your Fire Extinguisher

**Time required:** 10 minutes

**If you don't have one:** Buy a multi-purpose ABC extinguisher (works on most common household fires — wood, grease, and electrical). A 5-pound model costs $20–40 and is the standard size for homes. Mount it in your kitchen on the wall near an escape route (NOT directly over the stove where a fire might trap you).

**Testing/inspection:**
- Pick it up and read the gauge. The needle should be in the green zone.
- Look at the date tag. If it is older than 5 years or the gauge is in the red zone, get it refilled or replaced ($10–20 per refill).
- Check that the safety pin is in place (prevents accidental discharge).

**How to use it (PASS method):**
1. **Pull** the safety pin.
2. **Aim** the nozzle at the base of the fire.
3. **Squeeze** the handle.
4. **Sweep** side to side at the base.

**CRITICAL:** Only use a fire extinguisher on a small, contained fire. If the fire is:
- Spreading rapidly
- Blocking your exit
- Producing thick black smoke
- Involving a large propane tank or chemical

...leave your home immediately and call 911 from outside. Do not play hero. Firefighters are trained for this.

:::warning
Never use water on a grease fire (it will explode and scatter burning grease). Never use water on an electrical fire. Use an ABC extinguisher or baking soda for grease fires. For electrical fires, cut the power if you can safely do so, then use an ABC extinguisher.
:::

### 5. Memorize Your Family's Medical Histories

**Time required:** 30 minutes

**What to write down for each household member:**
- Full name and date of birth
- Blood type
- Allergies (food, medication, environmental — be specific: "anaphylaxis to peanuts", "rash to penicillin")
- Current medications (name, dosage, frequency)
- Chronic conditions (asthma, diabetes, heart disease, etc.)
- Surgical history (if relevant to emergency care — e.g., "has pacemaker")
- Emergency contact names and phone numbers
- Insurance provider and policy number (if you have it)

**Where to store this:**
1. **In your wallet:** A laminated card with blood type, allergies, and emergency contacts. Update it every year.
2. **On your phone:** A contact card labeled "ICE - Mom" (In Case of Emergency) with these details.
3. **Posted at home:** A sheet on your fridge with all household members' info. First responders are trained to look there.
4. **With this app:** On the next page, write it in the notes section of the <a href="../home-inventory.html">Home Inventory</a> guide. Your notes sync locally but survive device resets.

**Why you need to memorize it:** If your phone dies or you lose your wallet, EMTs still need to know your blood type and allergies. You cannot always rely on documentation existing or being legible. Spend 10 minutes committing key facts to memory — your blood type, your critical allergies, your essential medications.

**Specific to Minnesota:**
- Minnesota is a "right to refuse" state. Adults (18+) can refuse blood transfusions or medical treatment even if it will save their life, as long as they are conscious and of sound mind. Your medical history may include a documented refusal (religious objection, personal choice). Make sure paramedics and ER staff know this.
- If you take blood thinners (warfarin, apixaban), your blood type AND current medication must be known immediately in trauma situations.

## Device Preparation

You have the app installed on your primary phone. That is good, but not enough. This section covers how to ensure it survives the situations that make you need it most.

### Principle: Redundancy

Think of it this way: if your phone dies or breaks, you lose the app. If you have a second device with the app AND physical printed guides, you still have reference material. The goal is not paranoia — it is the same logic that makes you keep a spare tire in your car.

### Primary Device: Power and Offline Maps

**Keep a dedicated power bank in your emergency supply kit (next to first aid, flashlight, water).**

**Specs:**
- Capacity: 10,000 mAh or larger (enough to fully charge your phone 2–3 times)
- Include the correct cable for your phone (USB-C, Lightning, or Micro-USB — bring both if you have mixed devices)
- Cost: $15–40

**Seasonal rotation (this is important):**
- **Winter (October–April):** Lithium batteries lose capacity in cold. Bring the power bank with you in your car/bag. At home, store it indoors at room temperature. Check the charge level monthly and top it up if it drops below 50%.
- **Summer (May–September):** Leave it charged in your kit at home. Check monthly.
- **After extended power outages:** Check that it still holds a full charge. Lithium batteries can fail silently if repeatedly deep-discharged.

**Testing:** Once per season, charge your power bank fully, let it sit for a week, then use it to charge your phone from 0% to 100%. It should work smoothly with no overheating.

:::tip
A multi-port power bank (USB-A + USB-C) is better than a single-port model. Keeps you from needing multiple cables. Brands like Anker and RavPower are reliable and reasonably priced.
:::

### Secondary Device: Dedicated Old Phone

**What:** An old smartphone (iPhone 6s or newer, Android 7+) that you rarely use for calls or internet. Restore it to factory settings to wipe old data.

**Install on this device:**
1. This app (Offline Compendium) — download it once and bookmark it on the home screen
2. OsmAnd+ or Maps.me with offline maps of your county and region
3. A notes app (Apple Notes, Google Keep, or Samsung Notes) so you can write observations/temperatures/water intake during extended crises
4. A PDF reader (most phones have one built-in)

**Leave it:** Plugged in at home on a charger, in airplane mode (to save battery), in a closet or drawer away from heat and light. Check it quarterly to confirm it still powers on and holds a charge.

**Why a dedicated device:** If your primary phone dies, breaks, or is in a pocket where you cannot reach it, this device ensures you still have the app and maps. It also means you are not draining your primary phone's battery trying to read guides while your partner is trying to call for help on the same device.

:::warning
Old phones accumulate battery degradation over time. A 3-year-old phone might have a battery that only holds 60% of original capacity. If your secondary device is truly old, consider replacing the battery ($40–80 from a repair shop) before stashing it. A dead battery is useless in an emergency.
:::

### Device Power Consumption Reference

To keep your devices running during extended outages, understand how much power they consume. This table shows typical power draw for common devices in different modes.

| Device | Active Use | Standby/Sleep | Reading Mode | Battery Capacity | Runtime (Battery) |
|--------|-----------|---------------|--------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Smartphone (typical) | 2–4 W | 0.2–0.5 W | 0.5–1 W (screen dim) | 3000–5000 mAh | 8–12 hours (moderate use) |
| Tablet | 5–8 W | 0.5–1 W | 1–2 W (reading) | 5000–10000 mAh | 6–10 hours (mixed use) |
| E-reader (e-ink) | 0.5–1 W (display refresh only) | 0.1–0.2 W | 0.1–0.2 W (continuous) | 1000–3000 mAh | 2–4 weeks (heavy reading) |
| Laptop | 15–30 W | 5–10 W | N/A | 40–100 Wh | 4–10 hours (moderate use) |

**Notes on power consumption:**

- **Screen brightness is the largest power drain** on phones and tablets. Reducing brightness to 20% cuts power consumption by 40–50%.
- **E-readers are vastly more efficient** than backlit devices. A single charge can sustain weeks of reading. Prioritize e-readers if available.
- **Processor load matters**. Watching videos (CPU + GPU active) uses 3–4× more power than reading text.
- **Cellular/Wi-Fi use drains 30–40% faster** than airplane mode. Turn off radios when not needed.

**Power-Saving Configuration Tips:**

1. **Phones and tablets:**
   - Set brightness to 20% or use auto-brightness
   - Turn off Wi-Fi and Bluetooth when not in use
   - Enable "Low Power Mode" (reduces CPU speed, extends battery 20–30%)
   - Disable location services
   - Close unused apps running in background
   - Turn off cellular data; use Wi-Fi only for specific tasks

2. **E-readers:**
   - Keep Wi-Fi off (only turn on for downloads)
   - Reduce page refresh rate if device allows
   - Use airplane mode
   - Disable highlighting and annotation features (reduces CPU)

3. **Laptops:**
   - Reduce screen brightness to minimum readable level
   - Use battery saver mode
   - Close unnecessary programs and browser tabs
   - Disable Bluetooth and Wi-Fi when not needed
   - Use SSD (faster, less power) instead of spinning disk if possible
   - Avoid gaming or video editing on battery

**Seasonal rotation for power banks:**

Power banks lose capacity in cold and extreme heat. Manage them seasonally:

- **Winter:** Keep power banks inside your clothing or bag where body heat keeps them warm. Lithium batteries lose 30–50% capacity below 0°C. Check charge monthly and top up if below 50%.
- **Summer:** Store in cool location (not direct sun). Heat reduces lifespan. Use as-is monthly.
- **After outages:** After extended power outages or cold exposure, test the power bank fully (charge and discharge completely). If capacity is severely reduced, replace it.

### Managing Your App Library on Device

**Install vs. Web App:**
- The Offline Compendium is a Progressive Web App (PWA). On most phones, you can bookmark it and add it to your home screen, so it appears as an icon just like a native app.
- To do this: Open this site in your browser, tap the three-dot menu (⋯) or Share button, look for "Add to Home Screen" or "Install App", and follow the prompts.
- Once added to your home screen, the app caches guides and media locally using a service worker. This means it works offline even if you close your browser.

**Testing offline mode:**
- Add the app to your home screen.
- Turn on airplane mode.
- Open the app from your home screen. Guides should load from cache.
- If a guide fails to load, you may not have visited it yet. Visit guides while online to pre-cache them.

**What doesn't cache offline:**
- The first-time load of the app after installation (the app itself needs to download the index page)
- Any guide you have never viewed before
- External links (links to Wikipedia, medical references, etc.) — these require internet

**What does cache offline:**
- All 290+ guides once you have viewed them
- Search and category filters
- Your progress tracking and notes
- Offline maps (if installed separately)

## Physical Printouts: The 10 Most Critical Guides

Digital tools fail. Phones run out of battery. Servers go down. Paper does not. **Print the 10 guides you are most likely to need in a crisis and keep them in your emergency supply kit.**

### Criteria for Choosing Which Guides to Print

Ask yourself:
1. **Am I trained in this?** If you are not CPR-certified, CPR instructions are useless (you need hands-on training). If you have never stitched a wound, printed instructions will not teach it. Skip those.
2. **Do I have the tools?** If you do not own a hunting rifle, don't print rifle-disassembly guides. If you do own one, print it.
3. **Is this a reference I will actually use?** The goal is reference material you will consult during a crisis, not a collection that makes you feel prepared.

### Recommended Guides to Print (Minnesota-Focused)

| Guide | Why Print It | Format |
|-------|-----------|--------|
| CPR and First Response | You are certified now. Print the compression rhythm and when NOT to do CPR. | 4 pages, color diagrams helpful |
| Severe Bleeding Control | Tourniquets, wound packing, and direct pressure. Muscle memory is best, but written steps help. | 3 pages |
| Hypothermia and Cold Injury | Minnesota winter reality. Recognize stages and rewarming procedures. | 3 pages |
| Dehydration and Water Safety | Boiling, bleach purification, recognizing dehydration symptoms. | 2 pages |
| Power Failure: First 72 Hours | What to do when the grid goes down. Addresses heating, food, water, light. | 5 pages |
| Shelter and Insulation Repair | Sealing gaps, emergency insulation, preventing heat loss in winter. | 3 pages |
| Basic Wound Care | Cleaning, sutures, signs of infection. | 2 pages |
| Fire Starting and Safety | In case you need emergency heat and your furnace is offline. | 4 pages |
| Foraging: Upper Midwest Plants | Ramps, wild leeks, acorns, cattails — identify and use safe edibles. | 4 pages |
| Family Emergency Plan Communication | How to meet up, out-of-state contact, leaving messages. | 2 pages |

**Total:** ~32 pages, ~5 MB uncompressed PDF. Fits in a file folder or 1-inch binder.

### How to Print Guides

1. Open each guide in your web browser.
2. Use Ctrl+P (or Cmd+P on Mac) to open the print dialog.
3. Change the destination to "Save as PDF".
4. Adjust margins if needed (0.5 inch margins fit most content without cutting anything).
5. Disable headers and footers to save space.
6. Save to a folder on your computer.
7. Print the PDFs double-sided (if your printer supports it) to save paper, or single-sided if your printer is unreliable.

**Paper:** Standard 8.5" × 11" white copy paper. Avoid glossy paper — it yellows faster and is harder to read in low light.

**Storage:** Place in a gallon-sized zip-lock bag (waterproof), then in your emergency supply kit. Check annually for water damage or yellowing. Reprint if guides update or if you want to maintain readability.

### Lamination (Optional)

If you want your printed guides to survive water and heavy handling, take them to a UPS Store or office supply shop and laminate them for $0.50–1.00 per page. Benefits: waterproof, durable, easy to write on with dry-erase markers. Downside: slightly harder to flip pages quickly.

:::info-box
A printed guide is not a substitute for hands-on training. Tourniquets, rescue breathing, and water purification are skills that require practice. Print guides as a reference to jog your memory during a stressful moment, not as your only source of learning.
:::

## Preparing for Extended Offline Scenarios

### What Happens When the Grid Goes Down

If a regional power outage lasts more than a few hours, cell towers fail. Internet stops working. Your phone's 4G/5G is useless. Your app is still useful because guides are cached, but you lose two critical tools: GPS navigation (requires a data connection even with offline maps in some apps) and the ability to call or text.

**What this means for the app:**
- All 290+ guides remain readable (if you have viewed them once before going offline, they are cached).
- Offline maps (OsmAnd+, Maps.me) still work and can pinpoint your location via GPS.
- You cannot search the app's search index if your device is in full airplane mode, but you can browse by category.
- Your notes and progress are stored locally and won't be lost.

**Device preparation for extended outages:**
- Keep at least one fully charged power bank per household member who has a phone.
- Keep charging cables in multiple places (car, home kit, bag).
- Bring a car charger if you have a vehicle.

### Faraday Cage (Optional, Advanced)

If you want to go further: a Faraday cage is an enclosure that blocks electromagnetic pulses (like those from a nuclear detonation or solar flare). It protects electronic devices stored inside. A simple DIY version is a galvanized metal trash can with a wooden or plastic stand inside, wrapped in a layer of cardboard to prevent direct metal contact with your devices.

**Reality check:** If you are building a full-scale Faraday cage and bunker, you are no longer in "preparedness" territory — you are in "advanced survivalism." For most people, a charged power bank in your kit and a secondary device with cached guides is far more practical and likely to be useful.

**If you want to attempt it:**
- Buy a metal trash can ($30–50).
- Build a wooden stand inside.
- Wrap your secondary phone and power bank in a few layers of aluminum foil, then place on the stand.
- Close the lid.
- Test yearly by turning off your phone, placing it inside for a day, then turning it on outside to confirm it still powers up normally.

## Maintenance Schedule

Preparedness is not a one-time project. Set calendar reminders to refresh these items:

| Task | Frequency | When |
|------|-----------|------|
| Check power bank charge level | Monthly | 1st of each month |
| Test secondary phone | Quarterly | Start of each season (Mar, Jun, Sep, Dec) |
| Verify fire extinguisher gauge | Annually | January (at New Year) |
| Review and update medical info | Annually | On each household member's birthday |
| Reprint updated guides | As needed or annually | January; or if guide updates are significant |
| Test offline map download | Quarterly | Start of each season |
| Review emergency contact list | Annually | January |

Set phone calendar reminders for these dates so you do not forget.

:::tip
The best emergency kit is the one you check. A dusty bin in the basement does you no good if you are not familiar with its contents. Every January, pull out your kit, verify the power bank still holds charge, check that cables are not frayed, and confirm you remember where the gas and water shutoffs are. This takes 15 minutes and could save your life.
:::

## Why This Matters: The Bigger Picture

You are reading this app because you want to be more capable and less dependent on systems that might fail. The five actions in this guide are the foundation: CPR, home utilities, maps, fire safety, and medical knowledge. These are the skills and information that improve survival odds in 90% of civilian crises.

The guides in this app are references, not substitutes for hands-on training or professional credentials. A CPR course teaches you skills. A guide refreshes your memory. A printed map guides your route. An offline app keeps you reading when the internet fails.

**This weekend:** Do the five things. It will take a few hours, and you will genuinely increase your family's resilience. Then, as you have time, read the other guides in this app. Build your skills and knowledge. Keep your emergency kit updated and checked.

The app is only useful if you are ready to use it. Now you are.
