## 1\. Overview: Insulin Dependency & Survival

This guide addresses a critical medical scenario: supporting Type 1 diabetic patients when insulin supplies are unavailable. Without insulin, Type 1 diabetes is fatal within days to weeks from diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA). This is not a hypothetical—it is a true life-or-death situation requiring serious preparation.

### ⚠️ CRITICAL DISCLAIMER

This guide provides historical medical knowledge for trained personnel in catastrophic collapse scenarios. It is NOT medical advice. It is NOT a substitute for professional diabetes care. Insulin extraction is dangerous—incorrect procedures result in contaminated, ineffective, or toxic products that harm or kill patients. This information is strictly educational.

### The Severity of Type 1 Diabetes Without Insulin

**Hours to days:** Blood glucose rises uncontrolled. Patient experiences thirst, frequent urination, fatigue, headache.

**Days 2-3:** Ketoacidosis develops. Glucose cannot enter cells (no insulin), so cells break down fat for energy, producing ketones. Blood becomes acidic. Patient develops nausea, vomiting, fruity-smelling breath, confusion.

**Days 4-7:** Severe ketoacidosis. Blood pH drops dangerously. Respiratory and cardiovascular systems fail. Patient loses consciousness. Death occurs within hours to days without insulin intervention.

This scenario is not theoretical—during wars, famines, and disasters, people with Type 1 diabetes have died from insulin unavailability.

### Historical Preparedness Context

Before commercial insulin (discovered 1921, mass-produced 1923), Type 1 diabetes was uniformly fatal. Patients survived only through extreme diet restriction. The discovery of insulin-producing organisms in animal pancreases was revolutionary. Early insulin was crude, but it worked. This guide presents the Banting-Best method used in 1921-1922, adapted for survival scenarios.

:::note
**Historical Note:** Frederick Banting and Charles Best extracted insulin from dog and beef pancreases using acid-alcohol methods. Their crude extract saved lives immediately. Within months, pharmaceutical companies optimized and scaled the process. The knowledge of insulin extraction was once common among research labs—it can be rediscovered if necessary.
:::

### When This Knowledge Is Needed

This guide is relevant in scenarios where:

-   Insulin supplies are exhausted and cannot be replaced
-   Patients with Type 1 diabetes are present and survival of collapse scenario is otherwise possible
-   Trained medical personnel are available to oversee extraction and administration
-   Livestock (cattle, pigs, sheep) are available as pancreas sources
-   Basic chemistry equipment and understanding exists

:::warning
**⚠️ LEGAL WARNING:** In normal circumstances, insulin extraction and administration by non-pharmaceutical, non-medical personnel is illegal. This guide is strictly for catastrophic collapse scenarios where normal legal and medical systems have failed. Use of this information in normal times is illegal and dangerous.
:::

## 2\. Understanding Diabetes: Type 1 vs Type 2

Different diabetes types respond differently to intervention. Insulin extraction is only life-saving for Type 1. Type 2 may be managed with diet, exercise, and occasionally older medications.

### Type 1 Diabetes

#### Mechanism

Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells (the cells producing insulin). Patient produces little or no insulin. Blood glucose cannot enter most cells without insulin. Cells starve while blood glucose is dangerously high. This is a metabolic emergency.

#### Onset

Usually childhood or young adulthood. Develops rapidly (days to weeks). Often follows viral infection.

#### Presentation

-   Extreme thirst and frequent urination
-   Rapid weight loss (despite hunger)
-   Extreme fatigue and weakness
-   Fruity-smelling breath (ketones)
-   Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain
-   Confusion or altered consciousness in severe cases

#### Insulin Requirement

**ESSENTIAL FOR SURVIVAL.** Without insulin, Type 1 patients die. This is not negotiable. Any insulin, even crude, is better than no insulin. Even herbal alternatives and dietary restrictions help only marginally.

### Type 2 Diabetes

#### Mechanism

Insulin resistance—patient produces insulin but cells don't respond effectively. Pancreas may eventually fail to produce adequate insulin. This is metabolic dysfunction rather than absolute insulin deficiency.

#### Onset

Usually adulthood (though increasingly younger). Develops slowly (months to years). Strong association with obesity and poor diet.

#### Presentation

-   Increased thirst and urination (less severe than Type 1)
-   Fatigue
-   Blurred vision
-   Often asymptomatic initially—discovered on screening

#### Management Without Insulin

Type 2 may be managed with:

-   **Diet:** Strict carbohydrate restriction (low-glycemic diet). This dramatically improves blood glucose control.
-   **Exercise:** Regular physical activity improves insulin sensitivity
-   **Weight loss:** Reduces insulin resistance
-   **Older medications:** If available (metformin, sulfonylureas, etc.)

In a collapse scenario, Type 2 patients should focus on diet and lifestyle changes rather than insulin extraction. Insulin extraction is not essential for Type 2 survival.

### Distinguishing Type 1 from Type 2

If medical history is unavailable:

## 3\. SAFETY WARNINGS & TRAINING REQUIREMENTS

### ⚠️ THIS PROCESS IS GENUINELY DANGEROUS

Insulin extraction involves:

-   Toxic chemical solvents (ethanol, acetone)
-   Extreme pH solutions (strongly acidic)
-   Biological contamination (animal tissues, bacteria)
-   Fire hazards (organic solvents)
-   Inhalation injuries (solvent vapors)
-   Skin and eye injuries from strong acids
-   Improper insulin (contaminated, ineffective, toxic) that harms or kills patients

### Who Should Attempt This?

Insulin extraction should ONLY be attempted by:

-   Trained chemists or pharmaceutical technicians
-   Trained medical personnel with chemistry background
-   People who have studied this specific process in depth
-   Under medical supervision for a patient in life-threatening condition

This is NOT a process for:

-   Untrained or minimally trained people
-   First-time experimenters
-   People without protective equipment
-   People without understanding of chemistry basics

### Essential Safety Equipment

DO NOT ATTEMPT without:

-   **Eye protection:** Chemical splash goggles (not regular glasses)
-   **Skin protection:** Chemical-resistant gloves (nitrile NOT sufficient for strong solvents—need butyl rubber or neoprene)
-   **Respiratory protection:** If volatile solvents used, need activated charcoal cartridge respirator or equivalent
-   **Body protection:** Lab coat or apron protecting skin
-   **Ventilation:** Work in well-ventilated area or fume hood. Never work in enclosed space with solvent vapors.
-   **Fire safety:** Fire extinguisher rated for organic solvent fires (Class B or ABC) immediately available
-   **Acid safety:** Large volume of water nearby for emergency dilution of spills
-   **Medical supplies:** First aid kit, eye wash station if possible

### Chemical Hazards Detailed

#### Ethanol (Alcohol)

**Flash point:** 13°C (highly flammable)

**Hazards:** Flammable, CNS depressant (inhalation causes dizziness, respiratory depression), causes dehydration, eye irritant

**Exposure limits:** OSHA 1000 ppm (8-hour TWA)

**Safety:** Keep away from open flames. Use in ventilated area. Never heat ethanol without flame-free heating.

#### Acetone (if used)

**Flash point:** -20°C (extremely flammable)

**Hazards:** Highly flammable, solvent for many plastics, CNS depressant, skin irritant, eye irritant

**Exposure limits:** OSHA 1000 ppm

**Safety:** Absolutely no open flames. Use only in well-ventilated area with electric heating if required. Acetone is more dangerous than ethanol.

#### Hydrochloric Acid

**Hazards:** Severe burn hazard (skin, eyes, respiratory tract), corrosive, produces toxic hydrogen chloride gas

**Exposure limits:** Ceiling limit 5 ppm

**Safety:** Always add acid to water (never water to acid). Use chemical-resistant gloves. Ensure excellent ventilation. Large volume water immediately available.

#### Sodium or Potassium Hydroxide (if used)

**Hazards:** Severe burn hazard, corrosive, produces heat when dissolved, caustic

**Safety:** Use small quantities. Always dissolve in water slowly. Causes deep chemical burns. Contact immediately washed with large volume water.

### Biological Contamination Hazards

Animal pancreases are biological material that may contain:

-   **Bacteria:** E. coli, Salmonella, Clostridium species (potentially deadly)
-   **Viruses:** Various zoonotic viruses (avian flu in poultry, others)
-   **Prions:** BSE (mad cow disease) in cattle, scrapie in sheep (always fatal if transmitted)
-   **Parasites:** Various parasitic organisms
-   **Toxins:** Bacterial toxins, endotoxins

**Extraction process reduces but does not eliminate these risks.** Final product may contain contaminants causing infection or illness.

### Failed Extraction Consequences

Improperly extracted insulin that is:

-   **Contaminated:** Patient develops infection, sepsis, death
-   **Low potency:** Patient's blood glucose remains high, diabetic ketoacidosis, death
-   **Degraded:** No effect, patient's condition worsens
-   **Contaminated with toxins:** Patient develops acute illness, potential organ failure, death

Inadequate insulin is worse than no insulin in some ways—it gives false hope while patient continues to deteriorate.

:::warning
**⚠️ ULTIMATE WARNING:** This process will likely fail on first attempt. Expect to refine technique through multiple iterations. Until you have produced insulin that demonstrably lowers blood glucose in a patient without causing adverse effects, you have not succeeded. Testing on animals first is essential.
:::

### Legal & Ethical Considerations

In normal circumstances, this is illegal. In a true collapse scenario, saving a patient's life supersedes legal restrictions. However:

-   Document all efforts, dates, procedures for medical record
-   Obtain explicit informed consent from patient (or guardian if incapacitated)
-   Explain risks clearly—patient must understand what they are receiving
-   Test on animals if possible before human use
-   Monitor patient closely for adverse effects
-   Use only in true emergency (life-threatening without insulin)

## 4\. Historical Context: Banting-Best Method (1921)

Frederick Banting and Charles Best developed insulin extraction in 1921-1922 at the University of Toronto. Their method was crude by modern standards, but it worked. Understanding the history provides context for why this method was chosen and what alternatives exist.

### The Problem (1921)

Type 1 diabetes was uniformly fatal. Patients could survive only through near-starvation diets limiting carbohydrates. Life expectancy after diagnosis was measured in months to a few years. Researchers understood that the pancreas was involved but couldn't isolate the responsible substance.

### The Discovery

Banting and Best reasoned that insulin was produced by the islets of Langerhans (specific pancreatic cells) but was being destroyed by digestive enzymes in the pancreas. Their insight: remove or inactivate the digestive tissue, leaving insulin-producing tissue intact.

### The Original Method

1.  **Ligature the pancreatic duct:** In living dogs, they tied off the pancreatic duct, causing the digestive portion of the pancreas to atrophy while leaving islet cells intact.
2.  **Extract from atrophied pancreas:** After weeks, removed pancreas and extracted with alcohol-acid solution.
3.  **Test on diabetic dogs:** Injected extract into diabetic dogs. Their blood glucose dropped dramatically.

This was only the beginning. They then developed methods to extract insulin from normal (non-ligated) pancreases, working with pharmaceutical companies to scale production.

### Refinements

The method evolved rapidly:

-   **1921:** Initial extraction from ligated pancreases
-   **1921-1922:** Extraction from normal pancreases using acid-alcohol method
-   **1922:** First human patient treated (Leonard Thompson, a 14-year-old boy dying from diabetes)
-   **1923:** Eli Lilly pharmaceutical company begins mass production
-   **1923 onwards:** Continuous refinement of purification, potency standardization, and production

### What This Guide Uses

This guide presents a survival-adapted version of the acid-alcohol method used in 1921-1922. It is:

-   **Simpler than modern pharmaceutical extraction:** Modern methods use ion exchange chromatography, crystallization, and other sophisticated techniques. The basic method is old but functional.
-   **More dangerous than modern production:** Modern facilities have contamination control, precise chemical handling, quality assurance. Survival extraction lacks these protections.
-   **Less pure but effective:** Early insulin extracts contained insulin but also other pancreatic proteins, salts, and potentially contaminants. Despite this, they worked—people were saved from death.

:::note
**Historical Perspective:** The first patient treated with Banting-Best insulin (Leonard Thompson) was expected to die within weeks. His blood glucose was dangerously high despite near-starvation diet. Within hours of the first injection of crude insulin extract, his symptoms improved. He went on to live 13 more years. That crude extract, produced in a university lab with basic equipment, saved his life.
:::

## 5\. Animal Pancreas Sourcing & Handling

Insulin is produced only by the pancreas and is found in all mammals. Any mammalian pancreas contains some insulin, though quantities vary. For extraction, fresh tissue is critical.

### Suitable Animals (in order of preference)

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Animal</th><th scope="row">Pancreas Size</th><th scope="row">Insulin Yield</th><th scope="row">Advantages</th><th scope="row">Disadvantages</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Cattle (cow, steer)</td><td>Large (30-50g)</td><td>High</td><td>Large yield, easy source, good anatomy</td><td>BSE/prion risk, larger tissue to process</td></tr><tr><td>Pigs</td><td>Medium (15-25g)</td><td>Good</td><td>Good anatomy, easier processing, less prion risk</td><td>More difficult to obtain than cattle in many situations</td></tr><tr><td>Sheep</td><td>Small (10-15g)</td><td>Moderate</td><td>Small, easier processing, good insulin</td><td>Scrapie prion risk (though lower than cattle BSE)</td></tr><tr><td>Poultry (chicken, duck)</td><td>Tiny (1-3g)</td><td>Low</td><td>Readily available, easy to process</td><td>Very small yield, multiple animals needed, poultry flu risk</td></tr></tbody></table>

:::warning
**⚠️ PRION DISEASE RISK:** Cattle pancreases carry potential BSE (mad cow disease) risk. Sheep carry potential scrapie risk. These are always-fatal prion diseases transmissible through contaminated material. While insulin extraction inactivates most prions, complete safety cannot be guaranteed. Pigs are lower prion risk than cattle or sheep.
:::

### Sourcing Fresh Pancreas

**Best source:** Immediate post-slaughter. When an animal is slaughtered, extract organs immediately.

#### Extraction Steps

1.  Access abdominal cavity (after removing skin and muscle)
2.  Identify pancreas—elongated gland, pale tan color, adjacent to stomach and small intestine
3.  Carefully remove pancreas, avoiding rupture
4.  Remove adherent fat and connective tissue gently
5.  Place on clean surface—do NOT rinse (water dilutes content)
6.  Weigh pancreas if possible—record weight
7.  Proceed to processing immediately or preserve if delay necessary

### Preservation if Immediate Processing Impossible

If processing must be delayed:

#### Short-term (1-3 hours)

-   Keep at 4°C if refrigeration available (wrap in clean cloth, place in cool location)
-   Do NOT freeze (freezing damages tissue structure, reduces insulin yield)

#### Longer-term (up to 24 hours)

-   Submerge in cold saline solution (0.9% salt in water) at 4°C
-   This prevents decomposition without freezing damage
-   Yields are lower but still functional

### Pancreas Preparation Before Extraction

**Step 1: Inspection**

Examine tissue for obvious contamination (fecal matter, bile, damage). If heavily contaminated, rinse gently with cool water. Do not over-handle—this damages tissue.
**Step 2: Blotting**

Pat dry gently with clean cloth. Excess moisture dilutes your extract.
**Step 3: Weighing**

If possible, weigh tissue. This helps estimate insulin yield. Average pancreas yields approximately 100-500 units of insulin per 100g of tissue (unrefined extract).
**Step 4: Mincing**

Using a sharp knife or meat grinder (if available), mince pancreas into fine pieces (~2-5mm). Smaller pieces = faster extraction, higher yield. Handle as little as possible with bare hands—wear gloves if available.

**Now tissue is ready for extraction process.**

:::tip
**Pro Tip:** The fresher the tissue, the better the yield. Every hour of delay reduces final insulin potency. If possible, extract immediately after slaughter. Early morning start ensures good light and working conditions.
:::

## 6\. The Historical Extraction Process (Acid-Alcohol Method)

This is the core procedure. The principle: dissolve insulin and some other proteins using acidified alcohol, then remove most impurities through precipitation and washing.

### ⚠️ CHEMICAL HAZARD

This process uses strong acid and organic solvent. Review Section 3 safety warnings thoroughly before beginning. Have all safety equipment and emergency response ready.

### Materials Required

**Chemicals:**

-   Ethanol (95% or higher alcohol content)—approximately 500 mL per pancreas
-   Hydrochloric acid (HCl) or vinegar (acetic acid) if HCl unavailable—for acidification
-   Water (distilled if possible, but clean water acceptable)
-   Sodium chloride (table salt) or sodium acetate
-   Optional: activated charcoal (for further purification)

**Equipment:**

-   Large glass container (1-2 liter) with lid
-   Smaller glass containers for transfers
-   Measuring containers (graduated cylinder or estimated volume)
-   Thermometer (if heating required)
-   Cheesecloth or fine mesh strainer
-   Glass stirring rod
-   Graduated measuring spoons
-   Clean cloth for filtering
-   Syringes for storage (if sterile available)

### Detailed Extraction Steps

**Step 1: Prepare Acidified Ethanol Solution**

**Procedure:** Mix 500 mL of ethanol with 6-8 mL of hydrochloric acid (or 20 mL of vinegar if HCl unavailable). Mix thoroughly. This acidified alcohol is the extraction solvent.

**Chemistry:** The acid inactivates digestive enzymes in the pancreas that would otherwise destroy insulin. Ethanol dissolves lipids and proteins, including insulin.

**Safety:** Perform in well-ventilated area. Avoid inhaling fumes. Acid added to alcohol may generate heat—do not add rapidly.
**Step 2: Combine Minced Pancreas with Acidified Ethanol**

**Procedure:** Place minced pancreas tissue in large glass container. Pour acidified ethanol over tissue until completely submerged (tissue-to-solvent ratio approximately 1:4 to 1:5). Stir thoroughly.

**Expected:** Tissue begins to discolor and break apart. Solvent becomes slightly cloudy or tan-colored.
**Step 3: Maceration (Soaking & Extraction)**

**Procedure:** Cover container and allow to stand for 6-12 hours at room temperature, stirring occasionally (every 1-2 hours). OR if heat available: warm to 30-40°C (do NOT boil or heat excessively—heat degrades insulin), maintain temperature for 2-4 hours.

**Chemistry:** During this time, insulin and other soluble proteins gradually dissolve into the acidified ethanol.

**Expected:** Solvent becomes darker (brown or tan). Tissue breaks apart into fine particles.

**Safety Note:** If heating, use water bath (ethanol in container submerged in hot water). NEVER place ethanol directly over flame—fire hazard.
**Step 4: Filtration**

**Procedure:** Carefully pour mixture through cheesecloth or fine mesh strainer into a clean container. Catch the liquid (this is your crude insulin extract). Discard the solid tissue residue.

**Expected:** Liquid is tan to brown colored, relatively clear or slightly cloudy.

**Important:** This liquid contains insulin, other pancreatic proteins, salts, and other compounds. It is not yet pure insulin but is concentrated.
**Step 5: Optional Charcoal Filtration (Purification)**

**Procedure:** Add powdered activated charcoal to the filtered extract (approximately 1 gram per 100 mL of extract). Stir for 30 minutes. Filter again through cheesecloth.

**Chemistry:** Charcoal absorbs some impurities and undesired proteins, making extract cleaner. This step improves final product quality.

**Optional:** This step is helpful if you have charcoal but not essential.
**Step 6: Concentration (Optional, Improves Potency)**

**Procedure:** If time and equipment allow, slowly evaporate solvent by placing extract in shallow container in warm (not hot) location with air circulation. Ethanol is volatile and will evaporate. Goal: reduce volume to approximately 1/4 to 1/3 of original.

**Safety:** Do this in well-ventilated area—ethanol vapor is flammable and CNS depressant. No open flames nearby.

**Expected:** Liquid becomes darker and more concentrated. Volume decreases over hours to days.

**Advantage:** More concentrated extract = less volume needed per dose, easier injection. Also increases potency per mL.

**Note:** This step is optional. Non-concentrated extract is usable but requires larger injection volumes.

### Understanding Your Product

**What you now have:** A crude but functional insulin-containing extract. It contains:

-   Insulin (the desired product)
-   Other pancreatic proteins (glucagon, somatostatin, etc.)
-   Salts and minerals from tissue
-   Possibly contaminants (bacteria, degradation products)

**It is NOT pure insulin.** However, early insulin therapy used similarly crude extracts. The mixture works because the other pancreatic proteins are relatively benign, and the overall effect is still insulin's main action: lowering blood glucose.

:::warning
**⚠️ CRUDE EXTRACT RISK:** Contamination with bacteria or degradation products is possible. Before human use, if possible: test on animal (dog, rabbit, guinea pig) by injecting and monitoring for adverse reaction. If animal develops severe reaction, extract is likely contaminated—discard.
:::

### Storage of Extract

If you cannot immediately use extract:

-   **Room temperature:** 2-4 weeks in sealed container, away from light
-   **Refrigerated (4°C):** Several weeks, sealed container
-   **Frozen (-20°C if possible):** Several months, sealed container
-   **Alcohol preservation:** If extract is already in ethanol, it preserves better. Can store room temperature for months.

**Best practice:** Use immediately. With each day of storage, potency decreases slightly.

## 7\. Purification Steps (Advanced)

The basic extraction produces usable insulin extract, but further purification improves safety and potency. These steps require more chemistry knowledge and equipment.

### When Purification is Worth Doing

Purification increases safety and efficacy but requires time and resources. In a true survival scenario, basic extract may be used immediately. Purification is worthwhile if:

-   Time allows before patient's condition becomes critical
-   Equipment and chemicals for purification are available
-   You have chemistry background sufficient for the process
-   Patient can be monitored and dosing adjusted if product quality varies

### Simple Purification: Precipitation Method

This method removes some impurities by selective precipitation (making unwanted proteins solid so they fall out).

**Step 1: Adjust pH**

Your crude extract is acidic. Add sodium acetate or sodium hydroxide slowly until pH reaches approximately 7 (neutral). You can test with pH paper if available, or estimate by the following: once acidified, the acidic extract will stop becoming more neutral as you add base.

**Chemistry:** Many proteins precipitate at their isoelectric point. Insulin's isoelectric point is around pH 5-6, but adjusting pH helps remove other proteins.
**Step 2: Cold Acetone Precipitation**

**Procedure:** Add cold acetone (chilled in cool location) to the pH-adjusted extract in ratio approximately 4:1 (acetone to extract). Add slowly while stirring. Proteins will precipitate (become solid).

**Safety:** Acetone is MORE flammable than ethanol. Ensure excellent ventilation. No flames. Use in well-ventilated area or outdoors if possible.

**Expected:** Fine white or tan precipitate forms, making solution cloudy.
**Step 3: Collect Precipitate**

Filter through fine cloth to collect precipitate. Rinse precipitate with cold acetone to remove dissolved impurities.
**Step 4: Dissolve in Saline**

Dissolve the collected precipitate in sterile saline solution (0.9% salt in sterile water). This creates your purified insulin solution, now in physiologic saline suitable for injection.

**Result:** Extract is cleaner and safer for injection, though still crude by modern pharmaceutical standards.

### Alternative Purification: Isoelectric Focusing

This requires more specialized equipment (electrophoresis apparatus) and chemistry knowledge. It is included for completeness but is likely not feasible in survival scenarios.

**Principle:** Proteins migrate in electric field based on charge. Insulin can be separated from other proteins by adjusting pH of the medium.

**Feasibility:** Very low for survival scenarios. Skip this method unless you have specific equipment and expertise.

### The Reality of Crude Insulin

Early Banting-Best insulin was crude and still worked. Modern insulin is highly purified and standardized, but crude insulin saved millions of lives. In a survival scenario:

-   **Basic extract (Step 6):** Usable immediately, crude but effective
-   **Charcoal filtration (Step 5):** Modest improvement, reasonable effort
-   **Precipitation purification (Section 7):** Significant improvement if chemistry skills available
-   **Advanced purification:** Likely not feasible in survival situation

Don't let perfect be enemy of good. Basic extract is better than no insulin for a dying patient.

## 8\. Potency Estimation Methods

Before using insulin, you must estimate how much is in your extract. Modern insulin is measured in International Units (IU). One IU is defined as the amount of insulin that lowers blood glucose in a fasting rabbit by a specific amount.

### Historical Context

Before standardized IU, potency was estimated by animal testing. Early Banting-Best insulin used rabbit models to quantify units. This method is reproducible even without modern equipment.

### Estimation by Pancreas Weight

**Rule of thumb:** Fresh pancreas tissue yields approximately 100-500 IU per 100g of tissue, depending on:

-   Animal species (cattle > pigs > sheep > poultry)
-   Animal health and nutrition (well-nourished yields more)
-   Extraction efficiency (crude ~20-40%, purified ~40-60%)
-   Concentration of final extract

**Example:** 30g pancreas from a beef cattle → estimated 100-150 IU in final extract (if basic extraction, ~40% efficient).

**Limitation:** This is very rough. Actual potency can vary 2-fold either direction.

### Animal Testing (Rabbit Method)

If available, test on animal to estimate potency. This is crude but functional.

#### Procedure

1.  **Prepare diabetic rabbit:** Inject rabbit with alloxan (if available) to destroy pancreatic islet cells, creating diabetes. Alternative: use naturally diabetic rabbit if available. Alternatively, use fasting—rabbits fasted 24 hours have high blood glucose.
2.  **Measure baseline blood glucose:** Prick ear (or foot) and test blood glucose with glucose meter if available. Record baseline value.
3.  **Inject known volume of insulin extract:** Inject subcutaneously (under skin) or intravenously.
4.  **Monitor blood glucose:** Retest at 30 minutes, 1 hour, 2 hours. Record values.
5.  **Calculate potency:** Insulin lowers blood glucose. Approximately 1 IU lowers blood glucose by 3-5 mg/dL in a rabbit. Use this to back-calculate units in your extract.

**Example:** Inject 1 mL of extract into fasting rabbit with baseline glucose of 200 mg/dL. One hour later, glucose is 100 mg/dL (drop of 100). At 3-5 mg/dL per IU, this represents approximately 20-33 IU in that 1 mL, or 20-33 IU/mL of extract.

**Advantages:** Provides real data on potency

**Disadvantages:** Requires animal testing, glucose meter, and the ability to interpret results. Also, results may vary by rabbit individual variation.

### Indirect Estimation: Glucose Meter Testing

If a patient's glucose meter is available, initial small doses can be used to estimate potency empirically:

1.  Measure patient's baseline blood glucose with meter (record time and value)
2.  Inject small volume of insulin extract (e.g., 1 mL)
3.  Recheck blood glucose after 30-60 minutes
4.  Note change: (initial - final) / mL injected = rough estimate of IU/mL
5.  Adjust subsequent doses based on this

**Limitation:** This is trial-and-error dosing. Only acceptable if patient is in extreme danger and formal testing impossible. There is risk of hypoglycemia (too low blood glucose, also dangerous).

### No-Test Assumption

If no testing possible, assume moderate potency and start with conservative doses:

-   Assume extract is approximately 10-20 IU/mL
-   Start with 1-2 mL (10-40 IU) and monitor patient response
-   Adjust upward if blood glucose remains high, downward if patient develops hypoglycemic symptoms
-   Keep detailed records of doses and patient response

:::warning
**⚠️ OVERDOSE RISK:** Too much insulin causes hypoglycemia (blood glucose too low), which can cause seizures, coma, or death. Low blood glucose is acutely dangerous while high blood glucose develops over hours-days. Err on side of underdosing initially.
:::

## 9\. Storage Requirements: The Cold Chain Problem

Modern insulin requires consistent cold chain storage (2-8°C refrigerated, -20°C for long-term). This is one of the greatest challenges in a collapse scenario.

### The Problem

Insulin degrades rapidly at room temperature. Potency decreases approximately 10% per month at 25°C. Within months, room-temperature insulin becomes ineffective. Without refrigeration, your extracted insulin has limited usefulness.

### Optimal Storage Conditions

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Temperature</th><th scope="row">Potency Retention</th><th scope="row">Duration Viable</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>-20°C (freezer)</td><td>Excellent (&gt;95% per year)</td><td>Years</td></tr><tr><td>2-8°C (refrigerated)</td><td>Good (&gt;99% per month if sealed)</td><td>Months to years</td></tr><tr><td>15-25°C (room temperature)</td><td>Poor (~90% per month)</td><td>Weeks to months</td></tr><tr><td>&gt;25°C (warm)</td><td>Very poor (&gt;20% loss per week)</td><td>Days to weeks</td></tr></tbody></table>

### Refrigeration Without Electricity

In a collapse scenario, conventional refrigeration may not be available. Options:

#### Ice-Based Cooling

-   **Well water:** If well available, draw cold water regularly. Submerge sealed insulin container in cool well water (temperature stays 10-15°C year-round in temperate climates).
-   **Ice:** If ice-making or access to natural ice possible, place insulin container in cooler with ice. Refresh ice daily.
-   **Evaporative cooling:** Wrap sealed container in damp cloth in shade. Evaporation provides cooling. Maintains temperature 10-20°C depending on humidity and airflow.
-   **Underground storage:** Bury sealed container in cool location (cave, underground chamber, deep basement). Ground maintains cool temperature year-round.

#### Specialized Cooling (if available)

-   **Propane refrigerator:** Some RV or off-grid refrigerators use propane. Requires fuel but doesn't need electricity.
-   **Solar-powered refrigerator:** Requires solar panels and battery, but possible in sunny climates.
-   **Thermoelectric cooler:** Uses 12V power (from battery or generator). Less efficient than compressor coolers but usable.

### Long-Term Storage Strategy

**The cold chain is the limiting factor in a collapse scenario.** Solutions:

1.  **Extract frequently:** Produce small batches regularly (weekly or monthly) rather than large quantities at once. Fresh insulin is more potent.
2.  **Store multiple batches:** Keep some in coldest location (freezer if available), some in refrigerator, some at room temperature for immediate use. Rotation ensures availability even if cold chain fails.
3.  **Stabilization attempts:** Some research suggests specific chemicals (potassium chloride, albumin) stabilize insulin. If these are available, their addition might extend storage life. Experiment carefully—additive toxicity is a risk.
4.  **Accept degradation:** Plan for insulin to degrade. Increase dosing over time to compensate for potency loss. Monitor patient blood glucose closely and adjust.

### Practical Cold Chain Without Electricity

**Best practical approach for survival:**

-   Store insulin in sealed glass vial or syringe
-   Keep in well water if available (10-15°C)
-   If no well: bury in cool basement or underground location
-   Insulate container with ground, straw, or other materials to stabilize temperature
-   Check and use oldest stock first
-   Produce fresh batches regularly if possible

:::tip
**Pro Tip:** In a true collapse scenario with a Type 1 diabetic patient, extracting insulin becomes ongoing necessity, not one-time event. Budget time and resources for regular extraction (monthly production of fresh insulin) rather than trying to store large quantities long-term. Fresh insulin + cold well storage is more reliable than hoping large stored batches remain potent.
:::

## 10\. Dosing Considerations (With Critical Caveats)

### ⚠️ DOSING WARNING

Dosing insulin is medical practice requiring medical training. Incorrect dosing causes hypoglycemia (too low blood glucose—immediate danger, seizures, coma, death) or hyperglycemia (too high—dangerous but develops over hours-days). This section provides general principles only. In actual use, medical personnel must oversee dosing.

### General Insulin Dosing Principles

**Normal insulin requirement for Type 1 diabetics:** 0.5-1 IU per kilogram of body weight per day, divided into multiple doses (basal and bolus). Example: 70 kg person requires ~35-70 IU per day total.

**Crude extracted insulin:** If potency unknown, start lower and increase based on patient response.

### Initial Dosing (Unknown Potency)

If potency not measured:

**Conservative Start Approach**

-   Day 1: 1-2 mL extract (estimated 10-20 IU) once or twice daily
-   Monitor blood glucose before and 2 hours after injection
-   If blood glucose drops by 30-50 mg/dL: This is good—reasonable dosing achieved
-   If blood glucose drops <30 mg/dL: Dose too small, increase next dose
-   If blood glucose drops >100 mg/dL: Dose too large, reduce next dose

### Adjusting Dose

Daily adjustments based on patient response:

-   **High blood glucose (>250 mg/dL):** Increase insulin dose by 10-20%
-   **Moderate high (>180 mg/dL):** Increase by 5-10%
-   **Target range (80-130 mg/dL):** Maintain current dose
-   **Low-moderate (<80 mg/dL):** Decrease by 5-10%
-   **Dangerously low (<60 mg/dL):** Hold dose, give glucose, reduce future doses by 20%

### Signs of Adequate Dosing

**Patient on good insulin dosing should show:**

-   Decreased thirst and urination
-   Improved energy and mental clarity
-   Cessation of fruity-smelling breath (ketone resolution)
-   Blood glucose in 80-180 mg/dL range before meals
-   Weight stabilization (no continued weight loss)

### Signs of Underdosing

-   Persistent thirst and frequent urination
-   Continued fatigue and weakness
-   Blood glucose remains >250 mg/dL
-   Fruity breath odor continues (ketones still present)
-   Progressive weight loss continues

### Signs of Overdosing (Hypoglycemia)

**IMMEDIATE DANGER:**

-   Shakiness, sweating, rapid heartbeat
-   Confusion, difficulty concentrating
-   Irritability or aggressive behavior
-   Blurred vision
-   Seizures (if severe)
-   Loss of consciousness (medical emergency)

**If hypoglycemia suspected:**

1.  Check blood glucose if meter available (value <60 mg/dL confirms)
2.  Give quick carbohydrate: glucose tablets, fruit juice, candy, honey
3.  Wait 15 minutes, recheck blood glucose
4.  If still low, repeat glucose
5.  Once patient recovers, reduce next insulin dose

### Meal-Related Dosing

Modern insulin therapy uses carbohydrate counting and insulin-to-carb ratios. In a survival scenario, simplified approach:

-   **Large meal:** +20-30% additional insulin at meal time
-   **Small meal:** Standard basal dose only
-   **No meal/extended fast:** Reduce insulin, monitor closely for hypoglycemia

This is crude but manageable with close monitoring.

### Frequency of Dosing

Crude extracted insulin characteristics:

-   Onset: 30 minutes to 1 hour
-   Peak: 2-4 hours
-   Duration: 4-6 hours

**Recommended schedule:** Inject 3-4 times daily (with meals and at bedtime), or every 6 hours if patient cannot eat regularly.

:::warning
**⚠️ MEDICAL SUPERVISION REQUIRED:** In any real scenario, dosing must be supervised by someone with medical training (nurse, EMT, physician, or person trained in diabetes management). This section provides framework only. Incorrect dosing kills patients. Do not guess.
:::

## 11\. Administration Basics

Insulin must be injected (cannot be taken orally—digestive enzymes destroy it). Injection technique is crucial for safety and efficacy.

### Injection Routes

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Route</th><th scope="row">Onset</th><th scope="row">Advantages</th><th scope="row">Disadvantages</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Subcutaneous (under skin)</td><td>30-60 min</td><td>Safe, standard method, easy to learn</td><td>Slower onset than IV</td></tr><tr><td>Intravenous (in vein)</td><td>5-15 min</td><td>Rapid onset, useful in emergency</td><td>Requires medical skill, infection risk, difficult to manage</td></tr><tr><td>Intramuscular (in muscle)</td><td>10-20 min</td><td>Faster than subcutaneous</td><td>Tissue damage risk, difficult to standardize</td></tr></tbody></table>

**Recommended for survival scenario:** Subcutaneous injection. It's safest, easiest to learn, and suitable for chronic management.

### Subcutaneous Injection Technique

#### Equipment Needed

-   Syringe (1-3 mL capacity) with fine needle (25-28 gauge)
-   Sterilized needle—if not pre-sterilized, boil in water for 15 minutes
-   Alcohol prep pad or alcohol-soaked cloth (70% ethanol)
-   Insulin extract in sealed container
-   Sterile technique (washed hands, clean supplies)

#### Procedure

1.  **Hand hygiene:** Wash hands thoroughly with soap and water
2.  **Site selection:** Choose injection site—abdomen, thigh, or arm. Rotate sites to prevent lipohypertrophy (lumpy tissue from repeated injections in same spot). Abdomen absorbs fastest.
3.  **Site preparation:** Cleanse injection site with alcohol prep (or alcohol-soaked cloth). Allow to dry.
4.  **Draw insulin:** Open insulin container (maintaining sterility of needle). Draw prescribed amount into syringe, removing air bubbles.
5.  **Pinch and inject:** Pinch the skin at injection site (creates subcutaneous space). Insert needle at 45-90 degree angle through the pinched skin into fat layer (not muscle). Inject slowly.
6.  **Withdraw and apply pressure:** Remove needle, apply gentle pressure with alcohol pad for a few seconds.
7.  **Documentation:** Record time, dose, site, patient's blood glucose if measured, any patient response.

#### Injection Site Anatomy

**Subcutaneous layer:** Between skin (epidermis/dermis) and muscle. In abdomen, approximately 5-10mm deep. In thigh, approximately 10-15mm deep.

**Needle depth:** Standard needle (25-28 gauge) injected at 45-90 degrees reaches subcutaneous layer reliably.

### Intravenous Administration (Emergency Only)

**Use only if:** Subcutaneous access impossible AND patient in immediate danger (severe ketoacidosis)

**Requires:** IV access, medical training, ability to monitor IV site

**Procedure:** Dilute insulin extract in normal saline (0.9% salt water) to achieve lower concentration (to prevent local tissue damage). Inject slowly into IV line while monitoring vital signs. Requires medical oversight.

**Risk:** IV injection is dangerous—risk of hypoglycemia is higher due to rapid absorption. Only use if no alternative.

### Handling Crude Insulin Safely

-   **Sterile technique:** Use sterilized needles and syringes. Boil equipment in water for 15+ minutes if not sterile.
-   **Temperature:** Do not use hot insulin (heat degrades). Bring to room temperature if refrigerated.
-   **Visual inspection:** Check for discoloration, cloudiness, or particles. Discard if appearance abnormal.
-   **Expiration:** Use oldest stock first. Room-temperature insulin loses potency—assume 10% loss per month.
-   **Disposal:** Used needles in sharps container (or puncture-proof container). Do not reuse needles.

:::tip
**Pro Tip:** In a survival scenario, syringe scarcity may limit injections. Consider making improvised delivery systems: sharpened bone or thorn inserted into natural tube (bamboo, plant stem) can serve as needle-substitute for emergencies. This is crude but functional if needed.
:::

## 12\. Monitoring Blood Glucose

The gold standard for monitoring insulin effectiveness and preventing hypoglycemia is blood glucose measurement. Without this, dosing is flying blind.

### Glucose Meter Availability

**Best case:** Patient has glucose meter with strips and lancets. These are portable, require small blood sample, give results in seconds.

**Supplies needed:**

-   Glucose meter (reliable meters cost $20-50 and last years)
-   Test strips (expensive, ~$0.50-1 per strip; each meter requires specific strips)
-   Lancets (small sharp devices for pricking finger; typically $0.10 each)
-   Batteries (if meter requires them)

**Challenge in collapse:** Test strips may become unavailable. Meters are useless without strips. Rationing strips is necessary if supply limited.

### Glucose Meter Testing Schedule

**Ideal:** Test before meals and at bedtime (4 tests daily)

**Practical in resource-limited scenario:** Test once or twice daily (morning and evening, or before largest meal and bedtime). This provides minimum monitoring.

**Emergency overdose check:** Test immediately if patient shows hypoglycemic symptoms (trembling, confusion, rapid heartbeat).

### Target Blood Glucose Ranges

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Situation</th><th scope="row">Target Range</th><th scope="row">Notes</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Fasting (before meal)</td><td>80-130 mg/dL</td><td>Optimal control</td></tr><tr><td>Post-meal (2 hours)</td><td>&lt;180 mg/dL</td><td>Should not spike excessively</td></tr><tr><td>Emergency (acute illness)</td><td>150-250 mg/dL</td><td>Prioritize avoiding hypoglycemia</td></tr><tr><td>Bedtime</td><td>120-160 mg/dL</td><td>Slightly higher to prevent night hypoglycemia</td></tr></tbody></table>

### Testing Without Glucose Meter (Urine Ketone Testing)

If glucose meter unavailable, urine ketone test strips provide indirect assessment. Ketones are present when blood glucose is very high and insulin insufficient. This is not precise but useful.

#### Urine Ketone Test

-   **Negative:** No ketones—insulin adequate
-   **Trace to small:** Minimal ketones—acceptable control
-   **Moderate to large:** Significant ketones—insulin insufficient, increase dose

**Limitations:** Does not tell you actual blood glucose (could be 200 or 400), only indicates general adequacy. Not suitable for precise dosing.

### Clinical Signs of Blood Glucose Status

**Without any testing, monitor these signs:**

#### High Blood Glucose (Hyperglycemia)

-   Excessive thirst
-   Frequent urination
-   Fatigue and weakness
-   Fruity-smelling breath (ketones)
-   Nausea, vomiting (in severe cases)
-   Blurred vision

#### Low Blood Glucose (Hypoglycemia)

-   Trembling, shakiness
-   Sweating, clammy skin
-   Rapid heartbeat
-   Anxiety, nervousness
-   Confusion, difficulty concentrating
-   Headache
-   Irritability
-   Loss of consciousness (if severe)

### Monitoring Frequency Based on Stability

**New insulin therapy (first 1-2 weeks):** Test at least twice daily. Adjusting doses while finding optimal potency requires close monitoring.

**Stable therapy (dose established):** Test once daily or every other day. More frequent if patient becomes ill or intake changes.

**Emergency situation:** Test before each meal and bedtime at minimum. Monitoring prevents overlooking hypoglycemia.

:::warning
**⚠️ HYPOGLYCEMIA RISK:** Low blood glucose is an immediate medical emergency. Patient can lose consciousness or die. Always maintain glucose source (sugar, juice, food) available for rapid treatment. Treat any sign of hypoglycemia immediately.
:::

## 13\. Alternative Diabetes Management (Non-Insulin Approaches)

While Type 1 diabetes requires insulin, minimizing insulin requirement through other measures extends supply and improves outcomes. Additionally, Type 2 diabetics may be managed without insulin extraction.

### Dietary Management

#### Carbohydrate Restriction (Most Important)

Blood glucose is driven by carbohydrate intake. Restricting carbohydrates dramatically reduces insulin requirement.

**Strategy:** Eliminate or severely reduce:

-   Grains (wheat, rice, corn, oats)
-   Sugar and sweetened foods
-   Most fruits (except berries in small amounts)
-   Starchy vegetables (potatoes, carrots, beets)

**Focus on:**

-   Protein (meat, fish, eggs, legumes)
-   Healthy fats (nuts, seeds, oils, fatty fish)
-   Non-starchy vegetables (leafy greens, broccoli, cauliflower)

**Example:** A patient eating primarily meat, fish, eggs, and vegetables may reduce insulin requirement by 50-75%.

#### Meal Frequency

More frequent smaller meals vs. fewer larger meals. In survival scenario, frequent large meals may not be possible. Adjust to available food schedule and monitor response.

#### Specific Foods to Avoid/Emphasize

-   **Avoid:** Bread, pasta, rice, sugar, candy, soda, sweetened beverages, desserts, processed foods
-   **Emphasize:** Meat, fish, eggs, nuts, healthy oils, vegetables, legumes

### Physical Activity

Exercise improves insulin sensitivity, allowing cells to respond better to available insulin.

**Recommendations:**

-   Regular walking or light exercise (30 minutes, most days)
-   Strength training (improves muscle insulin sensitivity)
-   Maintain activity even in collapse scenario—chores, work provide activity

**Caution:** Intense exercise can cause hypoglycemia. Adjust insulin doses on exercise days or monitor closely.

### Stress Management

Stress hormones (cortisol, adrenaline) raise blood glucose. Reducing stress improves control.

-   Sleep optimization (even poor sleep worsens glucose control)
-   Relaxation practices (meditation, breathing exercises, rest)
-   Social connection and emotional support

### Herbal/Traditional Approaches (Limited Evidence)

**These are NOT substitutes for insulin but may provide modest benefit:**

-   **Cinnamon:** Some studies suggest modest glucose reduction. Take as tea or food additive.
-   **Bitter melon:** Traditional use in some cultures. Contains insulin-like compounds. Evidence mixed but low risk.
-   **Fenugreek seeds:** Traditional use, some evidence for glucose reduction. Can be consumed as tea or added to food.
-   **Ginseng:** May improve insulin sensitivity. Traditional use in Asian medicine.

**Reality:** These herbal approaches alone cannot replace insulin in Type 1. However, they may allow modest dose reduction if combined with dietary and lifestyle changes. Use as supplementary measures, not primary treatment.

### Management of Type 2 Diabetes Without Insulin

**Type 2 can often be managed with diet alone in survival scenario:**

-   Strict carbohydrate restriction (as above)
-   Weight loss (if overweight—improves insulin sensitivity significantly)
-   Regular exercise
-   Stress reduction

**Expected outcome:** Blood glucose control improves dramatically with lifestyle changes alone in Type 2. Many Type 2 patients can achieve normal glucose on carbohydrate-restricted diet without any medication.

### Monitoring Progress Without Glucose Meter

**Clinical improvement signs:**

-   Decreased thirst
-   Normal urination frequency
-   Increased energy
-   Weight normalization (after initial loss)
-   Clear mental focus
-   Absence of fruity breath odor (no ketones)

**If these improve, management is working.** If not, adjust approach (more carbohydrate restriction, more exercise, etc.) or increase insulin if Type 1.

## 14\. Common Mistakes & How to Avoid Them

Learning from others' errors accelerates competency. These are critical mistakes in insulin extraction and management:

### Extraction Mistakes

#### Mistake 1: Using Putrid or Contaminated Pancreas

**Problem:** Starting with compromised tissue, hoping extraction will clean it.

**Result:** Final extract is toxic or ineffective.

**Prevention:** Use fresh tissue only. If pancreas smells putrid, discard it. Fresh tissue is non-negotiable.

#### Mistake 2: Boiling Pancreas Tissue

**Problem:** Thinking heat sterilization of raw tissue will improve outcome.

**Result:** Boiling denatures proteins including insulin. Yield drops to near-zero.

**Prevention:** Do NOT heat raw tissue. The acid-alcohol combination is sufficient for processing. Heat comes later only if charcoal filtration or evaporation desired.

#### Mistake 3: Using Water Instead of Acidified Alcohol

**Problem:** Water-based extraction (thinking it's safer than alcohol).

**Result:** Pancreatic enzymes remain active and destroy insulin. Yield is near-zero.

**Prevention:** The acid-alcohol combination is essential. Acid inactivates enzymes, alcohol dissolves proteins. Both are required.

#### Mistake 4: Insufficient Tissue Mincing

**Problem:** Using large tissue chunks instead of fine mince.

**Result:** Poor penetration of solvent, incomplete extraction, low yield.

**Prevention:** Mince tissue into 2-5mm pieces. Smaller = faster and more complete extraction.

#### Mistake 5: Using Contaminated Equipment

**Problem:** Starting with unclean containers, glassware, or tools.

**Result:** Contaminants in final extract cause infections or toxicity in patient.

**Prevention:** Sterilize all equipment. Boil glassware and tools in water for 15+ minutes before use. Use clean, well-rinsed containers.

### Potency & Storage Mistakes

#### Mistake 6: Assuming All Pancreases Yield Same Amount

**Problem:** Not accounting for variation in pancreas size and quality.

**Result:** Vastly miscalculating insulin potency. Overdosing or underdosing patient.

**Prevention:** Always estimate potency through animal testing or careful initial dosing with monitoring. Never assume.

#### Mistake 7: Storing Extract at Room Temperature Without Monitoring Degradation

**Problem:** Believing stored insulin remains potent indefinitely.

**Result:** As months pass, insulin degrades. Patient requires increasingly higher doses without understanding why.

**Prevention:** Plan for degradation. Assume 10% loss per month at room temperature. Refrigerate if possible. Test old stock periodically on animal or patient to verify potency.

#### Mistake 8: Not Rotating Stock (FIFO)

**Problem:** Using newer batches while older batches degrade and become less potent.

**Result:** Old stock becomes useless waste.

**Prevention:** Label all extracts with production date. Use oldest first. Keep detailed inventory.

### Dosing & Administration Mistakes

#### Mistake 9: Starting Too High a Dose

**Problem:** Eager to bring blood glucose down quickly, injecting large dose of unknown-potency extract.

**Result:** Severe hypoglycemia, patient loses consciousness or dies.

**Prevention:** Start conservatively low (1-2 mL of crude extract). Titrate upward based on response. Low and slow is the safe approach.

#### Mistake 10: Not Rotating Injection Sites

**Problem:** Injecting in the same location repeatedly.

**Result:** Lipohypertrophy (fatty lumps) develop, absorption becomes unpredictable, insulin effectiveness varies widely.

**Prevention:** Rotate sites systematically. Abdomen, thigh, arm—vary location with each injection. Mark sites on diagram to track rotation.

#### Mistake 11: Reusing Needles

**Problem:** Trying to extend needle supply by reusing.

**Result:** Needle becomes dull (tissue trauma, increased pain), tip breaks, contamination from previous use.

**Prevention:** Use clean needle for each injection. If needles extremely scarce, sterilize by boiling, but replace regularly (every 3-5 uses maximum).

#### Mistake 12: Injecting Cold Insulin

**Problem:** Taking insulin directly from cold storage and injecting.

**Result:** Cold insulin causes tissue reaction and pain. Absorption may be slowed.

**Prevention:** Bring insulin to room temperature before injection (15-20 minutes). Gently warm syringe in hand or keep at room temperature before use.

#### Mistake 13: Not Monitoring Blood Glucose

**Problem:** Injecting insulin without ability or willingness to measure glucose response.

**Result:** Flying blind. Patient could be hypoglycemic and you wouldn't know. Adjustments are guesswork.

**Prevention:** Prioritize glucose meter and test strips above almost everything else. This is essential for safe dosing. If meter unavailable, at minimum test urine ketones regularly.

### General Management Mistakes

#### Mistake 14: Ignoring Dietary Modification

**Problem:** Extracting insulin but patient continues eating high-carbohydrate diet.

**Result:** Requiring extremely high insulin doses, rapid depletion of supply, poor control anyway.

**Prevention:** Carbohydrate restriction is not optional—it's essential to insulin management. Patient cannot eat normally. Work with dietary restrictions to minimize insulin requirement.

#### Mistake 15: Not Preparing for Scale

**Problem:** Extracting insulin for one patient without planning for others who might need it.

**Result:** Depletion of livestock, inability to serve community needs.

**Prevention:** Plan extraction scale based on available pancreas supply and patient needs. One cattle pancreas (~40g) yields approximately 200-400 IU, enough for ~4-6 days of a Type 1 patient's insulin. Multiple animals needed for long-term supply.

#### Mistake 16: Failing to Document

**Problem:** No records of extraction methods, potency estimates, patient dosing, or response.

**Result:** Can't replicate successful extractions. Can't learn from failures. No medical record if complications arise.

**Prevention:** Keep detailed written records: extraction date, pancreas source (animal, weight), solvent used, procedure variations, estimated potency, patient glucose readings, doses given, reactions observed.
