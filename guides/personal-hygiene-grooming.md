<section id="overview">

## Overview

Personal hygiene is mostly about removing sweat, oils, food residue, and bacteria before they turn into odor, irritation, or infection. You do not need a perfect bathroom setup to stay clean. With a basin, a cloth, a little soap, and a consistent routine, most people can stay presentable and healthy even when there is no running water.

The biggest priorities are:
- Keep hands, face, armpits, groin, feet, and mouth clean.
- Dry skin well after washing.
- Change socks, underwear, and sweaty shirts often.
- Brush teeth or clean the mouth every day.
- Trim nails and hair before they become hard to manage.

If water is scarce, clean the highest-odor, highest-germ areas first instead of trying to wash everything perfectly.

:::tip
If you remember only one rule, remember this: a short, repeatable routine done every day works better than an occasional deep clean.
:::

## Daily Low-Water Routine

A simple low-water routine can be done in 5 to 10 minutes:

1. Wash hands with soap and a small amount of water.
2. Wipe face, neck, armpits, groin, butt, and feet with a damp cloth.
3. Clean mouth and teeth.
4. Put on clean underwear, socks, and the driest shirt you have.
5. Air out or wash any item that smells strongly of sweat.

For a day with almost no water, focus on:
- Hands after toileting, eating, or touching dirty surfaces.
- Armpits and groin, where odor builds fastest.
- Feet, especially if shoes are worn all day.
- Teeth, because oral bacteria do not pause just because water is limited.

If you have a little more water, add a quick wipe-down of the chest, back, and hairline.

### When you have no soap, no cloth, and almost no water

- Rinse your hands in any available water, even cold. Rub them together vigorously. Friction alone removes some dirt and germs.
- Air out sweaty areas by removing layers briefly in a ventilated space.
- Sunlight kills some skin bacteria. Expose armpits and feet to direct sun for a few minutes if privacy allows.
- Change into the driest, least sweaty clothes you have. Rotating between two sets lets each one air out.
- Rub your teeth and gums with a clean finger and water, or chew a clean twig from a non-toxic tree to stimulate saliva and dislodge food.
- Keep nails short by scraping them on a rough surface if you have no clippers. Short nails collect less dirt.

## Bathing Without Plumbing

The easiest no-plumbing bath is a basin bath or sponge bath. You need a bowl, bucket, pot, or other clean container, a cloth, and soap if available.

### Basin Bath

1. Fill one container with a small amount of clean water.
2. Wet the cloth, add a little soap, and scrub one area at a time.
3. Wipe the soap off with a second damp cloth or a second small container of water.
4. Dry the skin as you go.
5. Change the water if it becomes visibly dirty.

### What to Wash First

When water is limited, wash in this order:
- Face and neck.
- Armpits.
- Groin and butt.
- Feet.
- Chest, back, arms, and legs.
- Hair and scalp, if you have enough water left.

### Sponge Bath Tips

- Use a separate cloth for the mouth, face, and groin if possible.
- Do not re-dip a dirty cloth into your clean rinse water.
- Dry under skin folds, breasts, between toes, and around the waistband.
- If you cannot shower, a careful wipe-down is still worthwhile.

### Washing Hair With Little Water

Hair does not need to be shampooed daily unless it gets oily fast or dirty from work.

- Wet the scalp, not the whole head, if water is scarce.
- Work a small amount of soap or shampoo into the scalp with fingertips.
- Rinse well so residue does not cause itch.
- Dry the scalp fully before sleeping if possible.

If hair is very dry, curly, or tightly coiled, washing too often can make it break. In that case, use a damp cloth on the scalp and wash only as needed.

### Drying Matters

Wet skin trapped under clothing can lead to rash, chafing, and odor. After bathing:
- Pat dry instead of rubbing hard.
- Give attention to armpits, groin, feet, and behind the ears.
- Put on clean, dry clothes.

## Oral Hygiene Basics

The best oral hygiene routine is simple:

- Brush teeth twice a day if possible.
- Clean along the gumline.
- Clean between the teeth.
- Clean the tongue.
- Rinse after sticky or sugary food when brushing is not possible.

If you have only one chance to clean your mouth each day, do it before sleep. Nighttime plaque buildup is a major cause of cavities and bad breath.

### How to Brush

1. Use a soft brush if you have one.
2. Put a pea-sized amount of toothpaste on the brush.
3. Angle the bristles toward the gumline.
4. Brush outer, inner, and chewing surfaces.
5. Brush the tongue gently.
6. Spit out the foam and rinse lightly.

Brush gently. Hard scrubbing can irritate gums and wear enamel over time.

### Toothbrush Substitutes

If you do not have a toothbrush, these substitutes are better than nothing:
- A clean washcloth wrapped around a finger.
- Clean gauze or cloth around a finger.
- A miswak or chew stick from a safe, clean plant.
- A very soft cloth used with water or paste.

Avoid anything that is rough, dirty, splintery, or contaminated with chemicals.

### Toothpaste Substitutes

If you need a simple toothpaste substitute, the safest everyday option is a mild baking soda paste:

- 1 teaspoon baking soda.
- A few drops of water.
- Mix to a soft paste.

Use a small amount and brush lightly. Baking soda is mildly abrasive, so do not scrub aggressively.

Other low-resource options:
- Water alone, if that is all you have.
- A tiny pinch of salt in water as a rinse, not as a scrub.
- A commercial fluoride toothpaste, if available, is still the best choice.

Do not use soap, detergent, bleach, peroxide, or gritty ash as a toothpaste substitute. They can irritate or injure the mouth.

### Making Toothpaste at Home

If you mean "how do I make toothpaste," keep it simple:

- Baking soda plus a few drops of water makes a usable brushing paste.
- If you want flavor, a drop of food-grade peppermint flavoring is enough.
- Store it dry or make only a small amount at a time.

For most people, the brush action matters more than the paste itself. If you have no paste, brushing with water is still useful.

### Mouth Rinse Options

- Plain water rinse is fine after meals.
- Salt water rinse can help if gums feel sore.
- Alcohol-heavy mouthwash is optional, not required.

If your mouth is very dry, drink water when you can. Dry mouth raises cavity risk.

## Hair and Nail Care

Clean hair and short nails make hygiene easier.

### Hair Care

Hair care is mostly about keeping it clean enough not to smell, itch, or mat.

- Brush or comb hair daily if it tangles easily.
- Wash the scalp more often than the ends.
- Let hair dry before covering it tightly.
- Tie long hair back for work, cooking, or sleeping if that reduces tangles.

### How to Cut Hair With Scissors

If you need to cut hair with scissors, start with a conservative trim:

1. Use clean, sharp scissors.
2. Comb the hair straight before cutting.
3. Cut only a little at a time.
4. Trim from the ends, not deep into the hair.
5. Check both sides in a mirror before cutting more.

For a simple home trim:
- Slightly damp hair is easier to control, but do not soak it if water is limited.
- Hold small sections between fingers and cut just below your fingers.
- Use vertical snips at the ends if you want a softer edge.
- Do not try to create a complex fade or layered style with dull scissors.

If you are cutting someone else’s hair, work slowly and keep the first pass longer than you think you need. It is easy to cut too much and impossible to put hair back.

### Nail Care

Long nails collect dirt and bacteria.

- Trim nails straight across.
- File sharp corners if they snag.
- Clean under nails with a nail brush, washcloth, or wooden stick.
- Trim nails after bathing when they are softer.

Keep toenails especially short if your feet sweat a lot or if your shoes are tight.

## Deodorant and Odor Control

Body odor comes from sweat, skin bacteria, clothing, and trapped moisture. The best odor control is not a fancy product. It is washing, drying, and changing clothes.

### What Works Best

- Wash armpits, groin, butt, and feet daily.
- Dry skin fully before dressing.
- Change underwear and socks every day.
- Rotate shirts so sweaty fabric can air out.
- Keep shoes dry and let them air between wears.

### Deodorant and Antiperspirant

If you have deodorant or antiperspirant, use it on clean, dry skin. Antiperspirant reduces sweat. Deodorant masks or reduces odor.

If you do not have commercial products:
- A light dusting of baking soda may help some people under the arms.
- Cornstarch or arrowroot can help absorb moisture.
- A clean cloth wipe on the armpits mid-day can reset odor.

Do a small skin test first if you have sensitive skin. Some powders can irritate or cake in skin folds.

### Foot Odor

Foot odor is often the strongest smell in low-water living.

- Wash feet well, especially between the toes.
- Dry between the toes after washing.
- Change socks daily or more often if they get wet.
- Air out shoes whenever possible.
- If shoes smell bad, wipe the inside with a damp cloth and let them dry fully.

If you can only wash one area besides your face, wash your feet.

### Clothing and Odor

Clothes hold smell even when your skin is clean.

- Wash underwear and socks first.
- Air shirts and pants outdoors or near moving air.
- Turn clothing inside out to dry sweaty linings faster.
- Do not store damp clothes in a bag.

For full hand-wash and drying steps, see `laundry-clothes-washing.md`. For musty or moldy fabric that keeps smelling after washing, see `mold-prevention-remediation.md`.

## Shaving and Grooming Basics

Shaving is optional. Grooming is mostly about looking neat, reducing irritation, and keeping hair from trapping sweat or dirt.

### Basic Shaving Rules

- Soften hair with warm water, soap, or shaving cream if you have it.
- Use a clean, sharp blade.
- Shave with short strokes.
- Shave in the direction of hair growth first.
- Do not press hard.
- Rinse the blade often.

### Safer Shaving Habits

- Do not share razors.
- Replace dull blades sooner rather than later.
- Avoid dry shaving if you can, because it increases nicks and razor burn.
- After shaving, rinse, dry, and use a simple moisturizer if your skin gets irritated.

### Beards and Facial Hair

If you keep facial hair:
- Wash the beard like skin, not just the surface.
- Comb it so food and dust do not stay trapped.
- Trim strays with scissors before the beard becomes uneven.

### Body Grooming

For chest hair, underarm hair, or other body hair, the goal is usually to reduce sweat trapping and make washing easier. Trim rather than shave if you are prone to bumps or ingrown hairs.

## Low-Water Cleanliness Kit

If you want a small everyday kit for low-water hygiene, keep these items together:

- Soft toothbrush.
- Small tube of fluoride toothpaste.
- A clean washcloth or microfiber cloth.
- Small soap or body wash.
- Comb and scissors.
- Nail clipper or nail scissors.
- Deodorant or antiperspirant.
- Spare socks and underwear.
- Small towel.
- A container for basin washing.

If space is tight, prioritize toothbrush, soap, washcloth, socks, underwear, and a towel.

<section id="troubleshooting">

## Troubleshooting and Red Flags

Some hygiene problems are more than hygiene problems.

Get medical or dental help if you notice:
- A swollen face or jaw.
- Tooth pain with fever.
- A skin rash that spreads or oozes.
- A wound that smells bad or has pus.
- Severe underarm or groin rash that does not improve.
- Sudden, strong body odor with illness, weakness, or weight loss.

If you are improvising hygiene for several days, the most important habits are still simple: wash the high-odor areas, dry thoroughly, and keep the mouth and feet clean.

:::tip
When in doubt, do the smallest useful version of the routine instead of skipping it entirely. A 2-minute cleanup is better than a perfect routine that never happens.
:::
