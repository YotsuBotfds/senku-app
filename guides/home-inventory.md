## Overview

This guide walks you through building a complete home preparedness system — the supplies, documents, and strategies you need to shelter in place during extended grid outages, supply chain disruptions, or seasonal emergencies. The scenario anchoring this guide is **two weeks without grid power in Minnesota winter**, because if you can survive that, you can handle almost any common outage.

The goal is not paranoia or perfectionism — it's **readiness without obsession**. You'll maintain rotating stock, use practical shelf-stable products you actually eat, and integrate these supplies into your daily life so they age well and money isn't wasted.

:::tip
#### Quick routing for home-inventory questions

- what should I stock first for a two-week outage: start at ``Water Storage & Purification`` and ``Food: Shelf-Stable Staples & Cooking Without Power``
- how much water and food do we need for 4 people: jump to ``Water Storage & Purification`` and ``Food: Shelf-Stable Staples & Cooking Without Power``
- where should we keep documents, cash, and meds: jump to the checklist section near ``Documents & Cash``
- give me a no-nonsense prep list I can act on: use ``Assembly Checklist: Building Your Home Inventory``
:::
:::info-box
**The Core Scenario:** Outdoor temperature is -10°F. Electric heat, gas delivery, water pumps, and grocery stores are offline. Cell networks work but internet is intermittent. You have two weeks to shelter in place in a single room with your family.
:::

This scenario touches every category in this guide: water, food, heat, light, medical care, and critical documents. Plan around it, and you'll be ready for the vast majority of real emergencies.

## Water Storage & Purification

Water is the highest-priority item. You need **1 gallon per person per day** for drinking and cooking. Add 0.5 gallons per person per day if you can't bathe or flush toilets with snowmelt, bringing the total to 1.5 gallons per day per person.

For a family of four for two weeks: **42 gallons minimum** (4 people × 14 days × 1.5 gallons).

### Storage Strategy

**Primary storage: Tap water in food-grade containers**

- Use **5-gallon food-grade buckets** (sold at hardware stores for ~$10–15). Costco sells them, or ask restaurants/bakeries for used frosting buckets (free, thoroughly rinsed).
- Fill with **municipal tap water** (chlorinated city water lasts 6+ months without treatment). Do NOT use well water without testing first.
- Rotate every 6 months in spring and fall — use the old water to fill houseplants or wash the car, fill fresh buckets.
- Store in a basement, garage, or cool closet. Avoid direct sunlight (UV kills chlorine).
- A 5-gallon bucket weighs 40 lbs when full — keep them in pairs or groups so you don't accidentally create a lifting hazard.

**For a family of four:**
- 8–10 five-gallon buckets = 40–50 gallons, covers two weeks comfortably
- Label each with the fill date using waterproof tape and permanent marker
- Cost: ~$100 one-time (buckets), $0 ongoing (tap water, rotation)

**Secondary storage: Pre-packaged water**

- Buy 3–5 cases of bottled water (16.9 fl oz bottles, 35 bottles per case) per person
- Rotate yearly (drink the old bottles in summer, replace in fall)
- Store unopened cases in garage; open cases in the kitchen pantry
- This gives you grab-and-go water for your go-bag and short-notice evacuations

**Tertiary option: Faucet-mounted rain barrel**

- A 55-gallon food-grade drum with a rain-gutter downspout diverter provides seasonal backup
- Lasts 2–3 months during spring/summer rain but freezes solid in Minnesota winter
- Treat collected rainwater before drinking (see purification, below)

### Purification for Stored Water

Tap water in buckets lasts because of residual chlorine. If you use well water, rainwater, or snow:

**Boiling:** The most reliable method.
- 1 minute at a rolling boil (3 minutes above 6,500 ft elevation, not applicable in Minnesota)
- Kills viruses and bacteria; does NOT remove chemicals
- Requires fuel: a camping stove, rocket mass heater, or wood fire
- Store 20–30 gallons of water specifically for boiling (in addition to drinking/cooking water)

**Bleach tablets or solution:**
- **Unscented household bleach (5–6% sodium hypochlorite):** Add 2 drops per quart, 8 drops per gallon. Wait 30 minutes. Slight chlorine smell = success. Store in dark glass bottles.
- **Calcium hypochlorite (pool shock):** More stable long-term (2–3 years). Follow package instructions; typically 1 teaspoon per gallon.
- **Pre-made tablets (Aquamira, Potable Aqua):** Expensive but foolproof. ~$15 for 50 tablets.

**Filters (secondary layer):**
- A **LifeStraw or Sawyer mini** (~$25–40) removes bacteria and protozoa but not viruses or chemicals
- Use filters for water clarity and taste; always boil or chemically treat first for safety
- Replace cartridges every 3–5 years

### Snow Melt (Minnesota Winter Survival)

In deep winter, clean snow becomes your water source.

- **Collect only fresh snow** (top layer of recent snowfall, not crusty old snow)
- **Never eat snow directly** — it causes dehydration by cooling your core and requiring calories to melt
- **Melt over heat:** Use a camping stove, fireplace, or wood heat. Expect 10:1 snow-to-water ratio (10 cups of packed snow = 1 cup water). Takes time.
- **Store 1–2 gallons of snow in a sealed container near your heat source**, allowing gravity to drip meltwater into a bucket below
- **Treat the meltwater** — boil or add bleach — before drinking, as "clean" snow can carry airborne contaminants

:::warning
If you plan to rely on snow melt for water in winter, practice this *before* you need it. Melt a pot during the next snowfall. You'll quickly see how labor-intensive and slow it is.
:::

## Food: Shelf-Stable Staples & Cooking Without Power

You won't starve during a two-week outage — humans can survive weeks without food. But you'll feel better, stay warmer, and maintain morale if you eat well. Focus on **calorie-dense, shelf-stable, rotation-friendly foods** that don't require fancy cooking.

### Calorie Calculation

- **Sedentary adult:** 1,800–2,000 calories/day (you'll move less in a sheltered room)
- **Moderately active (heating, snow work):** 2,500–3,000 calories/day
- **Working hard (snow removal, rescue):** 3,500–4,500 calories/day

For a family of four at moderate activity (2,500 cal/person/day), you need **40,000 calories for 4 people × 14 days**. That's roughly:
- 100 lbs of mixed shelf-stable food (rice, beans, pasta, canned goods, nuts, peanut butter)
- OR 4–5 cases of MREs/camping meals (~1,250 cal/meal)
- OR a mix of both (recommended)

### Core Shelf-Stable Foods (Buy Now, Eat Year-Round)

**Dry staples (10+ year shelf life, oxygen-absorbing packets):**

| Item | Quantity (family of 4) | Calories | Cost | Notes |
|------|------------------------|----------|------|-------|
| **White rice** (mylar bags w/ O₂ absorbers) | 25 lbs | 45,000 | $15–25 | Cooks in 15 min boiling water, high carbs |
| **Dried beans** (pintos, blacks, lentils) | 15 lbs mixed | 21,000 | $12–18 | Soak overnight or quick-boil 10 min, then simmer 1 hr (fuel-intensive) |
| **Pasta** (canned or boxed, oxygen-sealed) | 15 lbs | 27,000 | $10–15 | Cooks in 10 min, comfort food |
| **Peanut butter** (vacuum-sealed jars) | 4 × 16 oz | 12,000 | $8–12 | No cooking, high fat (energy), flavor booster |
| **Olive/coconut oil** (shelf-stable bottles) | 3 liters | 25,000 | $15–25 | Cooking fuel, high calories |
| **Honey** (large jar) | 2 lbs | 6,000 | $8–15 | Never spoils, high calories, sweetener |
| **Nuts & seeds** (bulk: almonds, pumpkin, sunflower) | 5 lbs | 28,000 | $20–30 | High fat, shelf-stable 1–2 years |
| **Dried fruit** (raisins, cranberries) | 3 lbs | 9,000 | $10–15 | Morale food, long shelf life |
| **Oats** (bulk, oxygen-sealed) | 10 lbs | 14,000 | $6–10 | Breakfast, fills stomach, warm comfort food |
| **Flour & baking soda** (for cooking) | 5 lbs flour, 2 lbs baking soda | 8,000 | $6–10 | Baking if you have heat (flatbread, biscuits) |

**Subtotal: ~195,000 calories = ~37,500 cal/person × 4 people × 2 weeks** ✓

**Canned goods (3–5 year shelf life, require water or oil to cook):**

- **Canned vegetables:** 6–8 large cans (corn, green beans, peas) = 600–800 cal. Eat cold or heat.
- **Canned fruit:** 6–8 cans (peaches, pears, pineapple) = 500–700 cal. Boosts morale, provides quick carbs.
- **Canned fish:** 6 tins of sardines, mackerel, or albacore tuna = 2,000+ cal, high omega-3 fat.
- **Canned meat:** 6 cans of chicken, beef, or spam = 2,000–3,000 cal, ready-to-eat protein.
- **Canned soups & chili:** 8 condensed cans (soup) or 4 large cans (chili) = 1,500–2,000 cal. Add water/rehydrate or eat thick.
- **Canned beans:** 6 large cans = 1,500 cal, protein + carbs (faster than dried if you have heat).

**Subtotal: ~8,000–10,000 calories**, more for convenience than calories.

**No-cook foods (morale & quick energy):**

- **Granola bars, energy bars, protein bars:** 2 per person per week = 28 bars × 250 cal = 7,000 cal. Rotate yearly.
- **Trail mix & dried nuts:** 3 lbs mixed = 15,000 cal, high morale factor.
- **Crackers, hardtack, canned biscuits:** 5 boxes = 3,000 cal. Shelf life 1–2 years.
- **Chocolate, hard candy, gum:** 2 lbs mixed = 3,000 cal. Psychological boost.

**Subtotal: ~28,000 calories**, mostly for morale.

### Cooking Without Grid Power

You have **three fuel options**, in order of practicality:

**1. Propane camping stove (best all-around)**

- Use a 2-burner **Coleman/Primus propane stove** (~$40–60)
- Standard **16 oz propane canisters** (~$6 each, available at any camping store)
- **Consumption:** 1 canister runs 2–3 hours of medium-flame cooking
- **For two weeks:** Stock 12–15 canisters (~$70–90) in a cool, dry place
- **Setup:** Use in a well-ventilated room or outdoors. Never in an enclosed bedroom (carbon monoxide risk)
- **Pros:** Fast, precise, no ash, portable
- **Cons:** Propane evaporates if stored >5 years; canisters rust outdoors

:::warning
Propane stoves produce carbon monoxide and use oxygen. Only use in spaces where you can crack a window or door. A kitchen with ventilation is ideal. Never use a camping stove in a sealed bedroom.
:::

**2. Wood-burning camp stove (fuel abundant, slower)**

- A small **barrel stove, rocket mass heater, or metal drum with grate** (DIY or $100–300 commercial)
- Burn scrap wood, fallen branches, pallets
- **Fuel source:** Free if you have wooded land or can salvage wood before the outage
- **Setup:** Use in a fireplace, shed, or well-vented space. Requires chimney or stovepipe to roof/out a window
- **Cooking:** Place cast-iron skillet or kettle on top of stove; takes longer than propane but free fuel
- **Pros:** Free fuel if available, builds heat and cooks simultaneously
- **Cons:** Slow, smoky, requires good ventilation, dangerous if not properly installed

**3. Charcoal/canned heat (emergency backup)**

- **Canned heat (Sterno):** Safe indoors, ~3-hour burn time per can, ~$2 per can. 30 cans = $60 for minimal cooking.
- **Charcoal briquettes:** Free to cheap if you have a BBQ, but produces carbon monoxide — use outdoors only or in open garage.
- **Alcohol stove (DIY):** Burn isopropyl alcohol in a metal cup — efficient, smokeless, safe indoors. ~$10 to build, fuel costs $5/liter.

**Recommended mix:** Stock 12–15 propane canisters (primary), 20–30 cans of Sterno (backup), and learn to use your fireplace or existing wood stove (if available).

### No-Cook Meals (Filling & Practical)

If cooking fuel runs low:

- **Peanut butter sandwiches:** Crackers or hardtack + peanut butter + honey. 500–800 cal, no cooking.
- **Trail mix bowls:** Nuts, dried fruit, chocolate chips, granola. 600 cal, satisfying.
- **Canned goods eaten straight:** Tuna from the tin, beans from the can, peaches from the can. Cold but edible.
- **Overnight oats:** Oats + powdered milk + water (or melted snow), mixed in a jar the night before. Eats cold, 400 cal.
- **Protein bars + peanut butter:** Two bars + 2 tbsp peanut butter = 600–700 cal, no fuel needed.

## Heat & Power: Heating One Room for Two Weeks

In Minnesota winter, heating is *life or death*. Your goal is not comfort — it's survival. You're going to heat **one small room (100–150 sq ft) to 55–60°F** — cold, but survivable in layers.

### The Heat Problem Defined

- **Outside:** -10°F, wind gusts to 30 mph
- **House:** No grid power. Electric furnace, gas boiler, or heat pump all down.
- **Goal:** One room stays above 50°F; family sleeps under blankets/in sleeping bags at 55–60°F
- **Duration:** 14 days

### Heat Source Options (Ranked by Practicality)

**1. Wood stove or fireplace (if you have one) — BEST**

- An operating wood stove can heat 800–1,200 sq ft (focus heat on one room by closing doors)
- **Fuel:** 3–4 cords of seasoned wood for one winter. In an emergency two-week scenario, you need 1/2 cord = 64 cubic feet. Start stockpiling now.
- **Setup:** Must have a clear chimney (have it inspected/cleaned yearly, ~$150). Ensure stove is safely installed with hearth protection and wall clearance per code.
- **Pros:** Heats while you cook, free warmth, doesn't use stored fuel
- **Cons:** Requires preparation (wood stock), regular refueling, chimney maintenance

**If you don't have a wood stove:**
- Can you install a wood-burning insert in your fireplace? (~$1,500–3,000, but invaluable in rural Minnesota)
- Temporary solution: Use a **rocket mass heater** or **DIY barrel stove** vented out a window (requires materials + skill)

**2. Portable propane heater (safe indoor models)**

- **Buddy indoor heater** (Mr. Buddy brand, ~$100–150) or similar **catalytic propane heater**
- Produces **8,000–10,000 BTU/hour** (heats ~200 sq ft)
- **Fuel:** Uses standard 1 lb propane canisters (~$4 each, 4–5 hours burn time) or adapter hose to larger cylinder
- **For two weeks:** 10 lbs propane = 10 × 1 lb canisters (~$40) OR 1 × 20 lb tank (~$25, plus adapter)
- **Setup:** Place in the center of your one room. Ensure good air circulation. No venting required (they produce CO, so crack a window).
- **Pros:** Portable, affordable, works without electricity, heats quickly
- **Cons:** Propane supply limited, uses stored fuel, requires carbon monoxide awareness

:::info-box
**Indoor propane heaters produce carbon monoxide** — a colorless, odorless gas that kills silently. If you use one:
- Keep a battery-powered CO detector in the room
- Crack a window or door to allow oxygen in and CO out
- Don't seal the room completely
- Limit use to 2–3 hours at a time with breaks for fresh air
- If anyone feels dizzy, nauseous, or has a headache, evacuate to fresh air immediately
:::

**3. Electric heater (if you have backup power)**

- A portable 750–1500W electric heater costs ~$30–50 but requires:
  - **Generator:** 5–10 kW portable generator (~$400–800), drinks 2–3 gallons of fuel/day (store 50 gallons for two weeks = $150–200 in fuel)
  - **Solar setup:** 2,000W of solar panels (~$2,000) + 10 kWh battery bank (~$5,000–10,000) — overkill for this scenario
- Electric heaters are **inefficient backup** unless you're already running a generator for other needs

**4. Body heat + insulation (lowest-cost, requires discipline)**

- Gather family + pets in one small room (bedroom, closet, tent-in-a-room)
- Layer everyone in wool clothing and sleeping bags
- Insulate the room: Hang blankets over windows, seal door gaps with towels
- Use hot water bottles (fill 2-liter bottles with boiled water, place in sleeping bags)
- Result: Room stays 45–55°F, but bodies + crowding keep sleeping area 55–65°F
- **Cost:** $0 (you have blankets); **Effectiveness:** 50% (uncomfortable but survivable)

### Recommended Heat Strategy

**Primary:** If you have a wood stove or fireplace, keep it stocked and ready. Start now — buy or cut 1 cord of firewood by September, store it dry under a roof.

**Backup:** Stock 30–50 lbs of propane (Mix: 10 × 1 lb canisters + 1 × 20 lb tank for heater + stove). Cost: ~$80 total.

**Insulation prep:**
- Weatherstrip doors and windows in fall (~$30 in supplies, prevents 15% heat loss)
- Hang thermal curtains or emergency blankets on south/west windows (~$50, captures solar heat during day)
- Tape seams of windows with painter's tape on inside (bubble insulation film costs $20 for whole window, looks ugly but works)

### Backup Power (Optional, but useful)

If you want to run lights, charge devices, or heat without wood:

**Portable generator (5–7 kW):**
- Cost: $400–800
- Fuel consumption: 2–3 gal/day at half load
- For two weeks: 30–40 gallons = $90–120 in fuel
- Run for 3–4 hours/day (mornings to charge devices, evenings for lights/small heater)
- **Drawback:** Loud, requires fuel storage, carbon monoxide (must be outside, not in garage)

**Smaller portable: 2 kW inverter generator (Honda EU2200i, ~$1,200)**
- Quieter, uses 0.5 gal/day, lighter, more efficient
- Can't run a space heater (needs 1500W+), but great for lights and charging
- Better for long-term preparedness (quiet enough for daily use during outage)

**Solar trickle charger (low-priority):**
- A 100W solar panel (~$200) with charge controller + battery bank (200 Wh, ~$200–400) keeps phones and headlamps charged
- Useful for long outages but takes days to fill batteries in winter (Minnesota sunlight is weak Nov–Feb)

## Light: Headlamps Over Candles

Never rely on candles as primary light — they're dim, fire hazards, and consume oxygen in sealed rooms.

### Recommended Light Setup

**Headlamps (primary):**
- Each family member gets a **rechargeable headlamp** (Black Diamond, Petzl, Coast, $25–50 each)
- 4 people × $35 = $140 total, one-time
- Hands-free, focused beam, 30–50 hour run time per charge
- For two weeks of use: **Stock 6–8 rechargeable AA/AAA batteries** (keep one set charging during day if you have generator, one set as backup)
- Alternative: **LED flashlights** (2–3 per person, $10–20 each) with 20 AA/AAA batteries stored long-term in cool/dry location

**Battery strategy:**
- Buy in bulk: **120 AA and 60 AAA alkaline batteries** (~$50–70, lasts years in storage)
- Rotate every 5 years (use old ones on low-drain toys, buy fresh)
- Store in cool, dry place (not the attic in summer heat)

**Lanterns (secondary, for shared spaces):**
- **LED camping lantern** (rechargeable or battery-powered, ~$20–40). Provides ambient light for shared meals/activities.
- **Oil lamp** (kerosene or paraffin, ~$20–30). Provide light but are slow and smell. Use as backup only.

**Candles (emergency backup only):**
- Keep 20–30 emergency candles ($10–15) in case all else fails
- Use in well-ventilated rooms only
- Better than nothing in true emergency, but not primary light

## Medical Supplies: Beyond Band-Aids

This section covers both regular medical emergencies and extended-outage medical management. Two weeks isolated means you can't run to the ER for stitches, and refilling prescriptions becomes critical.

### Prescription Medications: The 90-Day Buffer

**Before any outage, work with your pharmacist:**

1. **Ask about a 90-day supply gap.** Most insurers allow one 90-day fill per year without restarting the refill date. Use this to build a buffer now.
2. **Store in cool/dry place.** Not the bathroom (humidity/heat degrades meds). Use a bedroom closet or basement shelf. Keep in original bottles with labels (for identification and reference).
3. **Maintain rotation:** Once your buffer is 90 days ahead, use it in order — take from the buffer, refill normally. Don't let your buffer sit unused (meds degrade, dosages change).
4. **Track expiration dates.** Bring expired pills to the pharmacy for disposal (do not flush).

**For chronic conditions (diabetes, hypertension, etc.):**
- Keep a **written record** of your medication names, doses, and frequency in a waterproof pouch (see Documents section)
- If you're away from your pharmacy during an outage, knowing your exact dose helps any doctor provide care
- Insulin-dependent? Refrigeration is critical. Backup plan: Cooler with ice, or rely on a solar-powered 12V fridge (pricey but worth it if insulin-dependent)

### First Aid Kit: Complete Build

Store a **comprehensive first aid kit** (not a tiny bandage box). Use a large tackle box, Rubbermaid container, or commercial medical kit.

### Advanced Supplies (For Extended Care Without Hospital Access)

**Wound care beyond band-aids:**

- **SAM splint** (~$20–30): A reusable, moldable aluminum-foam splint for fractures, sprains, immobilization. Beats improvised splints.
- **Sterile wound closure strips** (Steri-Strips, $10): Use instead of stitches for deep cuts if bleeding is controlled.
- **Tourniquet** (CAT or SOF-T, $15–25): For severe bleeding you can't stop — learn how to use it (YouTube tutorials); don't rely on it without training.
- **Trauma dressings** (Israeli bandage, $5–8 each, buy 2): For heavy bleeding. Larger than band-aids, designed for serious wounds.
- **Medical tape** (various widths, $10): Better than regular tape for holding bandages.

**Oral rehydration salts:**

- **WHO oral rehydration solution (ORS):** Diarrhea or vomiting causes dehydration. Buy **packets** (Pedialyte, DripDrop, or generic) — 5–10 packets per person, $15–20 total.
- Alternative: Make it at home — 6 tsp sugar + 0.5 tsp salt in 1 liter clean water. Stir, drink small sips.

**Over-the-counter medications (Quantity for extended outage):**

- **Ibuprofen 200mg:** 200 tablets (~$12) — Two weeks of twice-daily use for the whole family
- **Diphenhydramine (Benadryl) 25mg:** 50 tablets (~$6) — Sleep aid, allergies
- **Omeprazole (Prilosec) 20mg:** 14 tablets (~$8) — Heartburn, GERD (if you have chronic reflux)
- **Loperamide (Imodium) 2mg:** 24 capsules (~$6) — Diarrhea
- **Bismuth subsalicylate (Pepto-Bismol) tablets:** 24 tablets (~$6) — Upset stomach

**Prescription alternatives:**

If someone's prescription runs out and pharmacies are closed, talk to your doctor NOW about:
- Temporary OTC substitutes (e.g., OTC antacids for PPI-dependent people)
- Written prescriptions you can fill at any pharmacy, including those with closed networks
- Whether a 90-day supply is possible (see above)

**Special needs:**

- **Asthma/inhalers:** Stock 1–2 spare rescue inhalers (Albuterol) if prescribed. Keep in cool place.
- **Diabetes:** 90-day insulin supply + syringes + test strips + lancets. Insulin degrades in heat — keep cool.
- **EpiPen (severe allergies):** Carry at least 2 pens; check expiration dates yearly. Know how to use them.
- **Prescription glasses/contacts:** Keep a spare pair at home. If power-dependent (progressive lenses), ensure backup at home.

### Illness Isolation Protocol

If someone gets sick (flu, cold, mystery illness):

- **Isolate in one room** with a separate window or door
- **Provide:** Water, food, OTC meds (ibuprofen, cough syrup, throat lozenges), tissues, hand sanitizer
- **Caregiving:** Assign one person to handle meals/care; others stay back
- **Hygiene:** Hand washing (or sanitizer if water is low), separate towels/utensils
- **Monitoring:** Check temperature twice daily (digital thermometer). If fever exceeds 103°F for >3 days, difficulty breathing, or severe dehydration, consider whether you must call for help despite outage

## Critical Documents: Copies, Not Originals

Store originals in a fireproof safe or safety deposit box. Keep copies in three places: home, vehicle, and with an out-of-state contact.

### Documents to Copy

**Personal ID & legal:**
- Driver's licenses (front + back)
- State ID cards
- Social Security card
- Passport
- Birth certificates
- Marriage certificate / divorce decree
- Custody orders (if applicable)

**Financial & property:**
- Deed / mortgage papers
- Vehicle title and registration
- Insurance policies (home, auto, health) — include policy numbers
- Bank account numbers and contact info
- Investment statements (latest)
- 401k/IRA beneficiary forms

**Medical:**
- Prescriptions (name, dose, frequency, pharmacy contact)
- Allergy list (documented, not handwritten)
- Blood type
- Vaccination records / immunization history
- Medical power of attorney / living will

**Utility & contact:**
- Mortgage lender phone number + account
- Utility companies (electric, gas, water) + account numbers
- Insurance agent phone numbers
- Family emergency contacts (out-of-state, especially)
- Doctor + dentist contact info

**Passwords/access (encrypted):**
- Do NOT store passwords in plain text in a document
- Use a password manager (Dashlane, 1Password) and write down the master password in your sealed envelope
- Or: Create a simplified "access guide" with hints (not actual passwords)

### Storage Method

**Waterproof pouch system (~$20 total):**

1. **Waterproof document pouch** (Aquaseal or Ziploc Freezer bag + duct tape)
2. **Folder inside:** Organize copies by category (ID, financial, medical, contacts)
3. **Label clearly:** "FAMILY EMERGENCY DOCUMENTS — KEEP DRY"
4. **Store three copies:**
   - **Home:** In a fireproof safe or bottom drawer (not under pillow — lose in a fire)
   - **Vehicle:** In a go-bag or locked glove box
   - **Out-of-state contact:** Mail a sealed copy to a trusted relative (grandparent, sibling, aunt in another state)

### Digital Backup

- Photograph documents with your phone; email to yourself or upload to a secure cloud storage (Google Drive, encrypted)
- If the internet is down during the outage, the physical copies matter. But digital copies for long-term reference (replacing IDs, etc.) are invaluable
- Encrypt the cloud folder or use a password-protected ZIP

Ask an out-of-state family member to keep a copy of your documents in their house. Explain the system: "If we lose everything in a disaster, I'll call you for a copy. In the meantime, keep it safe." Most family members are glad to help.

## Cash: Small Bills, Accessible, Strategic

In an extended outage, ATMs and card readers don't work. You need **physical cash in small bills**.

### Amount to Keep

**Minimum: $200–300 in small bills** (for a family of 4)
- $5 bills: 20 (= $100)
- $10 bills: 10 (= $100)
- $1 bills: 20 (= $20)
- Quarters, dimes, nickels: $20–30 in coin

**This covers:**
- Fuel for generator or mower (cash-only stations post-outage)
- Food at temporary markets or vending machines
- Supplies (batteries, water bottles) if you need to leave home
- Bribes/favors (unlikely but possible in prolonged chaos)

**Ideal to keep: $500–1,000** (if you can afford it)
- Larger buffer for family of 5+
- Post-outage, cash is king — ATMs and cards are unreliable
- Keep 50% at home, 50% in your vehicle or with out-of-state contact

### Storage Location

- **Not under the mattress** (first place burglars check, lost in fire)
- **Not in a wallet** (on your person, accessible but risky)
- **Best: Sealed envelope in your waterproof document pouch** alongside ID copies
- **Backup: Roll of bills in a ziplock, taped inside your vehicle's spare tire cover**

:::warning
Inflation erodes cash value over years. If you keep $1,000 in cash, expect it to lose ~3% purchasing power annually (at 3% inflation). This is acceptable given the insurance benefit of having accessible cash during an outage.
:::

## Cold-Climate Specific: Minnesota Winter Preparation

### Vehicle Winter Kit (Non-Negotiable by October)

By October 1st, every adult who drives should have:

**In the car year-round (Oct–April):**
- Ice scraper + snow brush
- Jumper cables or portable jump starter
- Blanket (wool or sleeping bag)
- Flashlight + extra batteries
- Flares or reflective triangles
- Phone charger (car 12V)
- Water + snacks
- Hand warmers (chemical heat packs, $1–2 each, buy a box of 40, ~$15)
- Tire pressure gauge + portable air pump

**In your home by November 1st:**
- **Wool blankets** (2–3 per person, $20–40 each) — keeps working even if wet
- **Space heater fuel** (propane canisters or kerosene) — top up tanks before November
- **Firewood stock** (1 cord minimum if you have a wood stove, ordered and stacked by October)
- **Snow removal** (shovel, snow blower with fuel, sand/kitty litter for icy steps)

### Pipe Freeze Prevention

If you lose water pressure during winter, pipes freeze solid in 12–24 hours at -10°F.

**Prevention:**
- **Insulate exposed pipes** (basement rim joists, exterior walls) with pipe foam sleeves (~$10 for whole house). Do this in fall.
- **Let cold-side faucet drip** when extreme cold hits. A steady drip prevents freezing. This uses minimal water but saves pipes.
- **Open cabinet doors** under sinks (especially on exterior walls) so heat reaches the pipes underneath.
- **Shut off and drain** any exterior hose bibs and pool drains before November 1st.

**If pipes freeze:**
- Never use an open flame to thaw (fire risk, fragile copper)
- Use a **heat tape** (electric element, but requires power) or **hair dryer on low** (portable, works)
- Call a plumber (might be overwhelmed during cold snap — have backup number ready)
- Thaw from the faucet end backward toward the main, opening the faucet so pressure can escape

## Maintenance & Rotation: Keeping Your Inventory Current

An unused inventory is a waste. Your preparedness succeeds by **using what you store**.

### Quarterly Checklist

**Every 3 months (Jan, Apr, Jul, Oct):**

1. **Inspect stored food:** Check for opened packages, pest damage, or spoilage
2. **Rotate stock:** Use the oldest first (FIFO — first in, first out)
3. **Check fuel:** Propane canisters don't leak, but visually inspect for rust
4. **Verify batteries:** Check that headlamps still charge; test alkaline batteries monthly in a low-power device
5. **Review documents:** Update if contact info or address changed

### Annual (Springtime)

1. **Rotate water:** Empty and refill 5-gallon buckets. Use the old water on plants or for cleaning.
2. **Inspect medications:** Check expiration dates. Dispose of expired pills at a pharmacy; replenish if needed.
3. **Check first aid kit:** Replace used items (bandages, ointment). Add any new OTC meds you now take.
4. **Test backup heat:** If you have a wood stove, have the chimney inspected and cleaned ($100–150). Test fireplace if haven't used in a year.
5. **Update documents:** Print new ID/insurance cards if they've expired. Refresh emergency contact list.
6. **Review cash:** If you've spent from your emergency fund, replenish it.

### If an Outage Happens

**During the outage (use your supplies):**
- Eat from your shelf-stable food
- Drink from your water storage
- Use fuel as needed to heat/cook
- Take medications and first aid supplies as needed

**After the outage:**
1. Restore water service, clean buckets, refill
2. Restock fuel/propane
3. Replenish medications and first aid items
4. Replace any expired food
5. Reset timer on quarterly/annual checks

## Summary: The Two-Week Scenario Revisited

If you've completed this checklist, you are **ready for a two-week grid-down winter scenario**:

- **Water:** 42–50 gallons stored + purification capacity (boiling + bleach)
- **Food:** 40,000 calories in shelf-stable staples + canned goods
- **Heat:** Wood stove OR propane heater + fuel for 14 days
- **Light:** Headlamps + batteries + backup lantern + emergency candles
- **Medical:** Prescriptions buffered + comprehensive first aid + OTC meds
- **Documents:** Copied and stored in three places
- **Cash:** $200–500 accessible in small bills
- **Cold prep:** Vehicle kit, wool blankets, pipe insulation by October

This isn't perfect — you might be cold at night, you'll eat simply, and you'll be bored. But you'll survive, your family will be sheltered and fed, and you won't be dependent on emergency services or neighbors in crisis.

The beauty of this system is that it **doesn't require paranoia or huge expense**. Rotating your food means it all gets eaten and replaced. Keeping cash and documents updated means they're always current. Using your supplies during the year ensures nothing goes stale.

Start today. Pick one section (water? food?) and build it out this week. In 2–3 months, you'll be completely ready.
