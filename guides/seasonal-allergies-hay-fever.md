:::warning
This guide is for everyday allergy relief and risk reduction. It does not replace medical care, allergy testing, or an asthma action plan. If breathing is getting worse, if the face or throat is swelling, or if the symptoms do not fit a simple seasonal pattern, treat it as urgent.
:::

## Overview

Seasonal allergies, often called hay fever, are usually caused by pollen from trees, grasses, and weeds. Some people also react to indoor triggers such as dust mites, mold, pet dander, or dust stirred up while cleaning.

Common symptoms include:

- Sneezing
- Runny nose
- Stuffy nose
- Itchy nose, throat, ears, or roof of the mouth
- Watery, itchy, or red eyes
- Mild cough from throat irritation or post-nasal drip

This guide is for questions like:

- How do I reduce pollen exposure?
- What should I do indoors when allergies flare?
- What helps with itchy eyes and a runny nose?
- When is this probably asthma or something more serious?

## Common Triggers

Seasonal allergies are usually worse when pollen is high. Indoor allergies can overlap with seasonal allergies and make the problem feel constant.

### Common triggers

- Tree, grass, and weed pollen
- Dust and dust mites
- Mold
- Pet dander
- Smoke
- Strong odors from fuel, cleaners, paint, or perfume
- Dry, dusty air

### Clues that point to allergies

- Symptoms come and go with seasons, cleaning, mowing, or windy days
- Eyes itch as well as the nose
- Clear runny mucus is more common than thick infected-looking mucus
- Symptoms improve when the person leaves the trigger area

## Reduce Exposure

The best relief often comes from reducing contact with the trigger.

### When pollen is the problem

- Keep windows closed when pollen counts are high.
- Stay indoors during windy days if that usually makes symptoms worse.
- Shower or rinse off after being outdoors for long periods.
- Change clothes after coming inside.
- Do not sit on a bed or couch in the same clothes worn outdoors all day.
- If possible, dry laundry indoors or on low-pollen days.

### When dust is the problem

- Clean with a damp cloth instead of dry sweeping when possible.
- Avoid shaking bedding and rugs indoors.
- Keep storage areas tidy so dust does not build up.
- Use the least dusty route when moving around the house.
- Wear a well-fitting mask for dusty chores if that is available and tolerated.

### When mold is the problem

- Fix leaks and damp areas early.
- Keep bathrooms, basements, and storage spaces as dry as possible.
- Remove wet laundry, towels, and bedding quickly.
- Do not ignore a musty smell that keeps coming back.

### When pets worsen symptoms

- Keep pets out of the sleeping area if possible.
- Wash hands after handling animals before touching the face.
- Brush or clean animals outside if that does not worsen symptoms from dander or dust.

## Indoor Habits

Good indoor habits can make a large difference even when pollen is outside.

### Helpful habits

- Sleep with clean bedding.
- Keep the sleeping area dust-free.
- Use fan or ventilation only if it does not pull in pollen or smoke.
- Close doors and windows during the worst exposure times.
- Keep the head elevated if nasal drainage is worse at night.

### Habits that often make symptoms worse

- Sweeping dust into the air
- Burning smoky fires indoors without need
- Sleeping in dusty clothing
- Leaving wet laundry or damp bedding in a closed room
- Using strong scented cleaners or sprays around the person

:::tip
If the symptoms always get worse after a certain chore, room, or season, write it down. That pattern is often more useful than a one-time guess.
:::

## Everyday Comfort Measures

These steps do not cure allergies, but they can make the day easier.

### Nose and throat comfort

- Rinse the nose with saline if available and if the person can use it safely.
- Drink enough fluids to keep mucus from becoming too thick.
- Use warm drinks if they feel soothing.
- Rest the voice if throat irritation is causing coughing.

### Eye comfort

- Rinse eyes with clean water or sterile saline if something has irritated them.
- Avoid rubbing the eyes.
- Wear sunglasses outdoors if pollen and wind are irritating the eyes.

### General comfort

- Rest after high-exposure activity.
- Keep the sleeping area cleaner than the rest of the house if that is possible.
- Use the simplest relief that does not add new irritation.

:::warning
Avoid putting unknown herbs, oils, or homemade mixtures into the eyes or nose. Some natural products irritate allergies more than they help.
:::

## Medicines And Self-Care

If over-the-counter allergy medicine is available and appropriate for the person, follow the label exactly or a clinician's advice.

Practical points:

- Allergy medicine often works better if started before the worst exposure rather than after symptoms are already severe.
- Non-drowsy products may still make some people sleepy or dizzy. If one medicine makes the person too sleepy, do not use it for driving, tool work, climbing, or other risky tasks.
- Be extra careful with mixed cold-and-allergy products. Some include decongestants that may be a bad fit for people with high blood pressure, heart disease, an overactive thyroid, glaucoma, trouble urinating, or pregnancy unless a clinician or pharmacist says the product is appropriate.
- Do not combine multiple medicines unless the label or a clinician says it is safe.
- Pregnancy, breastfeeding, small children, and people who already take several regular medicines deserve a label check before starting something new.
- If symptoms keep returning every season, allergy testing may help identify the main trigger.

## Overlap With Asthma

Hay fever and asthma often travel together. A person with allergies may also get cough, wheeze, chest tightness, or shortness of breath when pollen is high.

### Think about asthma overlap when there is:

- Wheezing
- Tight chest
- Cough that worsens at night or with exertion
- Trouble speaking full sentences because of breathing
- Symptoms that get worse after pollen, dust, smoke, or exercise

### What to do

- Remove the trigger if you can.
- Follow the person's asthma action plan if they have one.
- Use prescribed rescue medicine exactly as directed.
- Do not assume it is "just allergies" if breathing is involved.

:::danger
If the person is getting short of breath, cannot speak normally, is using extra effort to breathe, or has blue or gray lips, treat it as an emergency.
:::

## When It May Be More Than Allergies

Allergy symptoms are often annoying but not dangerous. Some patterns suggest infection or another problem.

### Possible infection signs

- Fever
- Feeling very ill instead of just itchy or stuffy
- Thick yellow or green discharge with worsening facial pain or pressure
- Symptoms that last a long time, happen year-round without a clear trigger pattern, or do not improve
- One-sided severe sinus pain
- Ongoing one-sided blocked nose or one-sided persistent sinus pressure
- New bad breath, tooth pain, or worsening facial swelling

### Possible more serious allergic reaction

- Lip, tongue, or throat swelling
- Trouble breathing
- Widespread hives
- Vomiting, fainting, or dizziness after a trigger exposure
- Symptoms that start quickly after food, medicine, a sting, or another allergen

:::danger
Swelling of the throat, trouble breathing, fainting, or a fast worsening reaction is not seasonal allergies. That is an emergency until proven otherwise.
:::

## Simple Daily Plan

If you want the shortest practical routine:

1. Check whether pollen, dust, mold, or smoke is the main trigger.
2. Keep windows closed when symptoms are flaring.
3. Shower or rinse off after outdoor exposure.
4. Change clothes after coming inside.
5. Keep bedding and the sleeping area as dust-free as possible.
6. Use saline, fluids, and other simple comfort measures.
7. Watch for asthma symptoms, fever, facial pain, or swelling.

## When To Get Help

Get medical help sooner if:

- Symptoms are severe or keep returning often
- Over-the-counter measures are not enough
- The person has asthma and breathing is getting worse
- The symptoms look like infection rather than allergy
- There is swelling, hives, fainting, or trouble breathing
- A child is having frequent seasonal symptoms and not sleeping well
