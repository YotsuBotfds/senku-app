## Overview

A horse or ox without proper harness is merely an animal. With harness, it becomes a tool for work—pulling plows, carts, and logs. The harness is a carefully engineered system that transfers the animal's pulling power safely and comfortably to the load. A poorly fitted harness can injure the animal, reduce efficiency, and fail under load.

The saddler and harness maker work closely with tanners, blacksmiths, and weavers. They shape leather, attach metal fittings, and integrate webbing and cordage into functional systems. The most critical aspect is understanding animal anatomy and ergonomics—a saddle or collar that doesn't fit creates pressure points, sores, and pain that render the animal unusable.

This guide covers the main types of draft harness, saddle construction, fitting methods, and maintenance.


<section id="collar-types">

## Collar Types and Construction

The collar distributes pulling force across the animal's shoulder and chest, supporting a significant portion of the load.

### Horse Collar (Breast-and-Wither Design)

This is the most common collar for plow horses and light draft work.

**Construction:**

1. **Leather shell:** Heavy leather (1/4 inch thickness) is shaped into a U shape that sits on the horse's shoulders.
   - Outer surface is heavily padded with straw, hair, or wool batting to distribute pressure over a large area.
   - Inner surface (contacting the horse's shoulder) is smooth leather.

2. **Sewing:** The two halves of the leather shell are sewn together along the bottom using saddle stitching (thick thread, multiple stitches, very strong).
   - Thread: Waxed linen or flax cord.
   - Stitch pattern: The stitches should be spaced about 1/8 inch apart and pulled taut but not excessively tight (excessive tension can tear leather as it flexes).

3. **Padding construction:**
   - Stuff the collar cavity with clean straw, horse hair, or wool batting.
   - Sew the padding in place with looser stitches through the leather (4–6 stitches per inch).
   - Proper padding is essential—a hard or unevenly padded collar causes pressure sores.

4. **Leather finishing:**
   - The outer surface may be burnished smooth or left textured for grip.
   - Oil the finished collar with neatsfoot oil (rendered cattle foot oil) to keep it supple and water-resistant.

**Fit assessment:**
- A horse collar should fit snugly but not tight. You should fit one finger between the leather and the horse's shoulder when the collar is in place.
- The collar sits forward on the shoulders, not on the neck (which would restrict breathing).
- Pressure should be distributed across the whole shoulder, not concentrated on any one spot.

### Breast Collar (Lighter Duty)

A simplified collar for light plow work or pack animals. It's a broad strap (6–8 inches wide) that runs across the chest.

**Advantages:**
- Simpler to make than a padded collar.
- Lighter weight.
- Easier to put on and take off.

**Disadvantages:**
- Lower load capacity than a full horse collar.
- Less effective for steep uphill pulling.
- Requires a girth (belly band) to prevent the collar from shifting backward.

**Construction:**
1. Cut heavy leather into a rectangular strap, 6–8 inches wide and 24–30 inches long, shaped to curve slightly over the chest.
2. Pad the inner surface with 1/2-inch wool batting or felt, sewn in place.
3. Attach D-rings (metal loops) on the outside for trace attachment (see Traces below).

### Ox Yoke

Not a collar but a different mechanism entirely. The ox yoke is a wooden beam (8–12 inches diameter) fitted with a pair of wooden bows that rest on the oxen's shoulders and necks.

**Construction:**
1. Two oxen stand side by side under the yoke.
2. Each bow (made from bent ash or oak) wraps partially around the ox's neck.
3. The yoke beam is bolted to the bows at a height that distributes load across the shoulders and neck.
4. Padding (straw, leather) is wrapped around the neck bows to prevent chafing.

**Advantages:** Very strong, distributes load over the back rather than the chest.

## Traces, Measurement, and Attachment Points

Traces are the straps or chains that connect the harness to the load (cart, plow, or heavy object).

### Trace Materials and Dimensions

**Leather traces:**
- Heavy leather strip (1.5–2 inches wide, 1/4 inch thick)
- Length: Depends on the vehicle and hitch point. Typically 4–6 feet for a plow, 6–8 feet for a cart.
- Durability is high but they can rot in wet conditions without maintenance.

**Chain traces:**
- Iron or steel links, about 1/2 inch diameter, riveted together.
- Virtually indestructible but heavier and can chafe without padding.
- Often used in pairs (one per animal) with a leather strap connecting them for flexibility.

**Rope traces:**
- Made from twisted plant fiber (hemp, flax) or twisted leather strips.
- Lighter than chain, more water-resistant than leather.
- Lower load capacity; best for light work.

### Measuring for Trace Length

1. Hitch the animal to a standard plow or cart.
2. Have a helper hold the animal in position while standing at the load attachment point.
3. Measure from the harness's trace attachment ring (on the collar or breast collar) to the load's hitch point.
4. Add 1 foot for attachment hardware and connection flexibility.
5. Traces that are too short restrict the animal's natural stride; traces that are too long create slack, allowing the load to bang against the animal.

### Attachment Methods

**To the collar (leather traces):**
1. Sew the trace end to a heavy leather pad that is sewn to the collar, or
2. Attach the trace with a metal ring (D-ring or terret ring) that is sewn to the collar, allowing the trace to slide through the ring (this allows some adjustment of effective length).

**To the load (plow or cart):**
1. Traces are attached to a wooden beam or iron bar that sits below the animal's pulling height.
2. Single traces: Each animal has one trace attached to the center of the load hitch point.
3. Pair of traces: Each animal has two traces (left and right) that spread the load across a wider area and provide directional control.

:::info-box
A well-designed hitch point sits at about the height of the animal's withers (the highest point of the shoulder). If the hitch point is too low, the animal must bend its back; if too high, it must strain its neck.
:::

## Bridle Construction and Bit Selection

A bridle controls the horse's direction and speed through the bit (metal mouthpiece) and reins.

### Bridle Components

**Headstall:** The leather framework that holds the bit and bridle in place.
- Crown piece: Leather strap that runs over the horse's head between the ears.
- Cheekpieces: Leather straps on each side of the face that hold the bit.
- Cavesson (noseband): A leather strap around the muzzle that can be tightened to influence the horse's head carriage.
- Throatlatch: A strap under the jaw that prevents the bridle from slipping back.

**Bit:** The metal mouthpiece.
- Snaffle bit: Single joint in the center, allows a more natural mouth position, good control for trained horses.
- Curb bit: Shanks on each side with leverage, provides stronger control, requires experienced riding.
- Mullen mouth (straight bar): Gentle, even pressure, less precise control.

**Reins:** Long leather straps attached to the bit rings.
- Length: 8–10 feet for driving reins (controlling a plow team), 5–6 feet for riding reins.
- Width: 1–1.5 inches, split down the middle for riding, sewn together for driving.

### Bridle Assembly

1. **Cut leather pieces** for crown piece, cheekpieces, and noseband, sized to fit the specific horse's measurements (head circumference, face length, muzzle width).

2. **Attach the bit:**
   - Sew or rivet the cheekpieces to the bit rings. Leather should be reinforced at the attachment point with additional stitching or a metal burr washer.
   - The bit should sit at the correct height in the horse's mouth (just touching the corners, not pulling the mouth up or down).

3. **Assemble the headstall:**
   - Sew the crown piece to the cheekpieces using saddle stitching (see Harness Assembly, below).
   - Attach the noseband and throatlatch with wraps of leather that are stitched, creating a sliding joint that allows adjustment.

4. **Attach reins:**
   - Sew reins to the bit rings with multiple rows of stitching for strength.
   - Each rein is attached to one ring; the two reins are typically tied together at the end (or split into four shorter reins for more precise control).

### Bit Selection

**Bit size:** The width of the bar (the part in the horse's mouth) should be 1/4 inch less than the width between the horse's lips.
- Too wide: The bit slides side to side in the horse's mouth, confusing signals.
- Too narrow: The bit pinches and causes pain.

**Bit shape:**
- **Thin bar:** Concentrates pressure, more precise control, but can be harsh.
- **Thick bar:** Distributes pressure over a larger area, gentler, but less precise.

**Jointed vs. straight bar:**
- **Jointed:** Allows the horse to chew slightly, more comfortable for long workdays.
- **Straight:** Even pressure across both sides of the mouth, good for training.

<section id="saddle-tree">

## Saddle Tree Construction

The saddle tree is the wooden frame that distributes the rider's weight across the horse's back and provides attachment points for cargo.

### Tree Materials and Design

**Wood:** Ash or hickory for strength and flexibility.

**Components:**

1. **Arch (or fork):** The front of the saddle tree, a rigid curved frame that sits above the horse's withers.
   - Two curved wooden rails (one on each side) joined together.
   - Reinforced with crosspieces for strength.

2. **Seat:** The middle section where the rider sits.
   - A flat or slightly curved wooden platform (1/4 inch thick hardwood) that spans the width of the horse's back.
   - Shaped to fit the horse's back contour.

3. **Cantle:** The back of the saddle tree, raised upward.
   - Provides a backrest for the rider.
   - Usually 4–6 inches higher than the seat.

4. **Side bars (panels):** Wooden or leather-covered wooden extensions that distribute pressure along the horse's sides.
   - Extend 8–12 inches along the horse's flanks on each side.
   - Must be wide enough to distribute pressure (minimum 3–4 inches) but not so wide that they restrict the animal's breathing.

### Tree Construction Steps

1. **Bend and shape the arch:**
   - Steam ash wood until pliable.
   - Bend around a form to create the proper curvature.
   - Clamp and dry slowly to set the curve permanently.

2. **Assemble the arch components:**
   - Lap-joint or mortise the arch rails together at the top center.
   - Reinforce with wooden dowels or strips glued across.

3. **Attach the seat board:**
   - Glue and screw the seat board to the arch and cantle frames.
   - Sand the seat smooth for comfort.

4. **Fit the side bars:**
   - Shape wooden bars to follow the horse's rib contour.
   - Attach to the main frame with leather straps or wooden pegs, allowing slight flex (this distributes movement and prevents pinching).

5. **Test the fit:**
   - Place the tree on a horse's back.
   - Check that the arch sits clear of the withers (at least 1 inch gap).
   - The side bars should contact the ribs uniformly.
   - No pressure points should be visible.

## Seat Leather Work and Covering

Once the tree is complete, leather and padding are added to create a comfortable, waterproof saddle.

### Padding and Underside Construction

1. **Quilted padding:**
   - Cover the seat board and side bars with 1/2-inch wool felt or cotton batting.
   - Stitch the padding in a grid pattern (4-inch squares) to prevent bunching and ensure uniform density.
   - This layer absorbs sweat and impacts.

2. **Leather skirts:**
   - Wide leather panels (12–18 inches long) that hang down from the side bars to protect the horse's flanks and the rider's legs.
   - Soft leather (suede or well-oiled leather) is preferred for comfort.
   - Skirts are sewn to the side bars and attached with leather thongs so they can flex independently.

### Seat Covering

1. **Leather selection:** Medium-weight leather (3/32 inch thick) for the seat and back; heavier leather (1/8 inch) for the sides and skirts.

2. **Cutting and shaping:**
   - Cut leather panels to fit the seat, cantle, and arch, allowing 1 inch overlap on all edges.
   - Dampen the leather with water until pliable.
   - Stretch the leather over the tree and nail or staple it to the underside of the side bars and the rear of the cantle.
   - Allow to dry under tension so the leather shrinks and tightens.

3. **Stitching:**
   - Once dry, remove nails and replace with hand stitching (saddle stitch) around all edges.
   - Stitch through the leather and into the wooden frame for maximum strength.
   - Stitches should be 1/8 inch apart and pulled taut.

4. **Finishing:**
   - Oil all leather with neatsfoot oil to keep it supple and water-resistant.
   - Buff the surface smooth.
   - Allow 2 weeks for the oil to fully absorb before using the saddle.

## Rigging and Attachment Hardware

Rigging refers to the system of metal rings, loops, and attachment points that connect saddle to cinch (the belt that holds the saddle on).

### Ring and Loop Placement

**D-rings:** Heavy metal rings (1.5–2 inches diameter) sewn onto the side of the saddle for:
- Cinch attachment
- Cargo rope attachment
- Hobble ring (for tying horses)

**Terret rings:** Smaller rings (1 inch diameter) that guide the reins and hold up other straps.

**Placement:**
- Front cinch attachment: Two D-rings, one on each side, positioned 6–8 inches behind the arch.
- Rear cinch attachment: Two D-rings positioned about 18–24 inches behind the front cinch.
- Horn ring (for pack saddles): A single heavy ring at the front center for attaching lead ropes and cargo.

### Cinch and Girth Attachment

The cinch (also called girth in English saddles) is a broad band that wraps around the horse's barrel and holds the saddle in place.

**Cinch materials:**
- Heavy cotton canvas or wool felt, 4–6 inches wide.
- Often faced with leather on both sides for durability.
- Padding underneath (1/2 inch wool felt) for comfort.

**Attachment to the saddle:**
1. Sew the cinch ends to the front D-rings with strong leather attachment straps.
2. The cinch passes under the horse's belly.
3. An auxiliary strap (rear cinch) may be attached to the rear D-rings to prevent the saddle from shifting backward.

**Cinch tightness:**
- Should be tight enough that the saddle won't shift under load (a loose saddle rotates on the horse's back).
- Should allow one finger under the cinch when tightened.
- Re-tighten after 5–10 minutes of riding as the horse's back expands with breathing.

## Harness Fitting and Adjustment

Proper fit is essential for animal comfort and performance. An ill-fitting harness causes sores, inefficiency, and behavioral problems.

### Fitting Procedure

1. **Dry run on the horse:**
   - Place all harness components on the horse without hitching to anything.
   - Lead the horse at walk and trot to observe fit and movement.
   - Identify any pressure points or areas where the harness rubs.

2. **Pressure point inspection:**
   - Run your hand under the collar, along the withers, and along the ribcage.
   - A well-fitting harness should be snug but not tight; you should fit one finger between leather and skin.
   - Any areas where the horse flinches or the skin shows redness need adjustment.

3. **Adjustments:**
   - If the collar is too narrow or high, have a saddler re-fit it or add additional padding.
   - If the saddle pinches, the tree may need widening (a skilled job) or re-padding.
   - If the girth is in the wrong position, move the D-ring attachment points.

### Seasonal and Maintenance Adjustments

- **Spring:** Check for winter damage. Re-oil all leather. Re-stuff collars if padding has compressed.
- **Summer:** After heavy use, inspect stitching and hardware. Replace any worn leather.
- **Fall:** Deep clean and condition all leather. Tighten loose stitching.
- **Winter:** Store saddles and harnesses indoors in a dry place. Oil all leather before storage to prevent cracking.

### Common Problems and Solutions

| Problem | Cause | Solution |
|---------|-------|----------|
| Girth sores (rub marks) | Girth too loose or in wrong position | Retighten girth; move attachment point forward |
| Wither pressure sores | Collar too narrow or tree arch too narrow | Have saddler widen arch or refit collar padding |
| Saddle shifting backward | Girth not tight enough or cinch placement wrong | Tighten girth; add rear cinch |
| Bit pulling corner of mouth | Bit too narrow | Size up to next size bit |
| Rein too short (horse can't turn head) | Reins measured incorrectly | Adjust attachment point or use longer reins |

:::tip
A leather working journal helps track a specific animal's measurements and preferences. Record collar tightness, bit size, and any adjustments made. This information is invaluable when ordering replacements or working with a new animal of similar size.
:::
