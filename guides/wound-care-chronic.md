## Overview

:::info-box
**Start here if:** the wound has stayed open for weeks, keeps coming back, is a pressure sore or bedsore, is a diabetic foot ulcer, or needs an ongoing dressing/offloading plan rather than one-time cleaning.

**Use the sibling guide instead** for everyday symptom-first questions like "how do I clean this cut," "is this wound infected," "why does it smell bad," or "what should I use to wash and cover it today." See <a href="../wound-hygiene-infection-prevention.html">Wound Hygiene, Infection Prevention & Field Sanitation</a>.
:::

Chronic wounds are those that fail to heal in the expected timeframe (typically >3 weeks) or remain open for >1 month. They differ from acute wounds in pathophysiology and management. Common causes include pressure ulcers, diabetic neuropathic ulcers, venous insufficiency, and arterial insufficiency. In austere or underserved healthcare environments, chronic wound management relies on locally available supplies, systematic assessment, infection control, and patient education.

**Critical disclaimer:** This guide is educational. Chronic wounds often require professional wound care, vascular assessment, or surgical intervention. In settings where these are unavailable, focus on preventing infection, promoting healing, and reducing suffering. Seek professional evaluation when possible.

This guide emphasizes the TIME framework (Tissue, Inflammation, Moisture, Epithelialization) and practical approaches using improvised or low-cost materials.

## Pressure Ulcer Staging and Prevention

Pressure ulcers (decubitus ulcers, bedsores) develop from prolonged pressure, shear, and friction over bony prominences. They are common in immobilized patients, the elderly, and those with spinal cord injury.

### Anatomic Sites

- **Sacrum (tailbone) and coccyx:** Most common (45% of pressure ulcers)
- **Heels (calcaneus):** Second most common
- **Hip (greater trochanter):** Third
- **Elbows, back of head, other bony areas:** Less common but possible

### Pressure Ulcer Stages

**Stage 1:** Non-blanchable erythema (redness that does not blanch/lighten when pressed). Skin intact. Prevention or light topical care may reverse.

**Stage 2:** Partial-thickness skin loss involving epidermis and dermis. Appears as an abrasion, blister (opened or intact), or shallow crater. Some exudate (fluid discharge) may be present.

**Stage 3:** Full-thickness skin loss extending into the subcutaneous tissue. Appears as a deep crater. Edges may be undermined (tissue loss extending beyond visible wound). Adipose (fat) tissue may be visible.

**Stage 4:** Full-thickness skin loss with involvement of muscle, bone, or deep structures (tendons, cartilage). Often has tunneling and undermining. High risk of serious infection (cellulitis, osteomyelitis).

**Unstageable:** Full-thickness wound covered with eschar (black, dead tissue) or slough (yellow/white devitalized tissue) that obscures the wound base.

**Deep tissue injury:** Area of discoloration (purple or maroon), usually on a bony prominence, indicating underlying tissue damage beneath intact skin.

### Prevention

**Pressure relief:**
- **Turn patient every 2 hours** if bedridden (or more frequently if skin shows early signs of ulceration)
- **Use pressure-redistributing surfaces:** Foam, gel, or air mattresses (if available); improvise with stacked blankets or cloth
- **Position changes:** Alternate supine, left side, right side, prone (if tolerated)
- **Elevate heels:** Use pillow under calves to lift heels off surface

**Moisture management:**
- Keep skin dry; manage incontinence promptly
- Avoid prolonged wetness from sweat or urine
- Use absorbent pads changed frequently

**Nutrition:**
- Protein-rich diet supports tissue repair
- Hydration (adequate fluid intake)
- Vitamins C and A support collagen synthesis
- Zinc aids healing

**Skin care:**
- Gentle cleansing with mild soap
- Moisturize intact skin
- Avoid harsh chemicals or friction

### Management of Established Pressure Ulcers

See the Wound Bed Preparation (TIME) section below.

## Diabetic Foot Ulcer Assessment and Management

Diabetes causes two main foot complications: peripheral neuropathy (loss of sensation) and peripheral arterial disease (reduced blood flow). These combine to create slow-healing, infection-prone ulcers.

### Assessment: Wagner Classification

The Wagner classification helps predict healing potential and infection risk.

| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0 | No ulceration; foot at risk (callus, deformity) |
| 1 | Superficial ulcer not extending below dermis; no infection |
| 2 | Ulcer penetrates through dermis; may involve subcutaneous tissue; no abscess or bone involvement |
| 3 | Ulcer penetrates deeper; abscess or osteomyelitis (bone infection) likely |
| 4 | Gangrenous (dead) tissue in forefoot |
| 5 | Gangrene involving the entire foot; amputation likely |

**Treatment approach changes with grade.** Grade 1–2 ulcers may heal with conservative care; Grade 3–5 often require advanced intervention or amputation.

### Assessment: Vascular Status

Diabetic ulcers heal poorly without adequate blood supply. Assess perfusion:

- **Palpate dorsalis pedis and posterior tibial pulses** on both feet
- **Check capillary refill:** Press toenail; color should return within 2 seconds
- **Skin temperature:** Cold feet suggest reduced perfusion
- **Color and hair loss:** Hairless, pale, or mottled skin suggests poor circulation
- **Claudication history:** Does the patient have leg pain with walking?

If vascular disease is suspected, urgent vascular imaging (ultrasound, angiography) and intervention are needed before wound healing will occur.

### Neuropathy Assessment

Loss of sensation increases ulcer risk. Test:

- **Monofilament testing:** Apply a 10-gram monofilament to the sole of the foot; patient should feel it
- **Temperature sensation:** Use hot and cold objects
- **Vibration:** Use a 128-Hz tuning fork on the ankle bone
- **Reflexes:** Tap the Achilles tendon; normal reflex vs diminished/absent

If sensation is lost (neuropathy present), the patient must inspect feet daily and avoid pressure/trauma.

### Management

**Glycemic control:** Blood glucose management is essential. High glucose impairs immune function and delays healing.

**Offloading (pressure relief):**
- **No weight-bearing:** Best option—crutches, wheelchair, or bed rest
- **Total contact cast:** Immobilizes the foot and redistributes pressure (if available and properly fitted)
- **Diabetic shoes with inserts:** Custom-molded to distribute pressure
- **Improvised offloading:** Soft dressing, padding, or foam to protect the ulcer

**Wound care:** Follow TIME framework (see below).

**Infection control:** Diabetic foot ulcers are high-risk for serious infection (ascending cellulitis, osteomyelitis, sepsis). Aggressive antibiotic therapy is often needed.

**Monitoring:** Daily inspection for signs of worsening (increased redness, swelling, drainage, foul odor, systemic fever).

:::warning
Diabetic foot ulcers can rapidly progress to limb-threatening infection. If signs of cellulitis (spreading redness, swelling, warmth, systemic fever), osteomyelitis (bone pain, sinus tract with drainage), or gangrene (black tissue, foul odor) develop, escalate immediately to IV antibiotics and surgical evaluation.
:::

## Daily Dressing Routine and Bandage Reuse

Chronic wounds usually need a simple, repeatable routine more than they need fancy supplies. The goal is to keep the wound clean, prevent the dressing from trapping too much fluid, and know when a cloth dressing has become too contaminated to reuse.

If the main question is basic wound cleaning, early infection warning signs, smell, pus, or field sanitation for a newer wound, use <a href="../wound-hygiene-infection-prevention.html">Wound Hygiene, Infection Prevention & Field Sanitation</a>. This section is for chronic wounds that already need a long-term dressing routine.

### How Often To Change a Dressing

- **Dry, stable wounds:** change daily or every 1-2 days if the dressing stays clean and the wound is not draining much
- **Wounds with drainage or sweat:** change every day, and sooner if the dressing becomes damp
- **Heavily draining wounds:** change 2-3 times daily so the skin around the wound does not macerate
- **Any dressing that is wet, dirty, smelly, loose, or painful to remove:** replace it right away

### What To Use If Gauze Is Not Available

Fold clean cotton cloth into several layers and secure it so it stays in place without pinching. A clean T-shirt, bandanna, pillowcase strip, or similar cotton fabric is usually better than fuzzy towel material because it sheds less lint into the wound.

- Good backup: boiled cloth, washed cotton shirt, clean bandanna
- Better for drainage: layered cloth plus an outer wrap to hold it in place
- Avoid: paper products, loose-fiber towels, or cloth that breaks apart and leaves debris in the wound

### Reuse Rules for Cloth Dressings

- Reuse only if the cloth can be washed with soap and clean water, then boiled, heat-dried, or fully sun-dried before the next use
- Do not reuse cloth that cannot be cleaned free of pus, stool, vomit, grease, or mold
- Keep used dressings separate from clean laundry until washed
- If the cloth still smells bad after washing, throw it out rather than trusting it again

### Dressing Removal

- If a dressing is stuck, wet it with clean water or saline and wait a minute before removing it
- Do not yank off crusted material if it is firmly attached to the wound bed
- Check the wound edges for increasing redness, warmth, swelling, odor, or thicker drainage each time you change the dressing

## Wound Bed Preparation: The TIME Framework

The TIME framework (Tissue, Inflammation, Moisture, Epithelialization) is a systematic approach to chronic wound care.

### T: Tissue Debridement

Remove devitalized tissue (necrotic, slough, eschar) to promote healing and reduce infection risk.

**Methods:**

**Sharp debridement:** Scalpel or scissors to remove dead tissue. Requires skill but is most effective. If available, have trained personnel perform.

**Autolytic debridement:** The body's own enzymes gradually dissolve dead tissue. Achieved by keeping the wound moist under a dressing. Slow but safe; takes days to weeks.

**Enzymatic debridement:** Topical enzymes (collagenase, papain) chemically dissolve necrotic tissue. If available, apply per package directions. Limited effectiveness in austere settings.

**Maggot debridement therapy (MDT):** Fly larvae (Lucilia sericata) consume dead tissue without harming living tissue. See section below.

**Mechanical debridement:** Wet-to-dry dressings (wet gauze dried and removed) remove necrotic tissue but can also damage healthy granulation tissue; avoid if possible.

### Inflammation Management

Excess inflammation delays healing. Address:

- **Infection:** See "Distinguishing Infection from Colonization" below
- **Antimicrobial dressings:** Silver, iodine, or honey-based dressings reduce bacterial load
- **Systemic antibiotics:** For cellulitis or signs of systemic infection
- **Anti-inflammatory topicals:** Topical corticosteroids in non-infected wounds may reduce inflammation (use cautiously)

### M: Moisture Balance

Too much exudate (fluid) maceration (waterlogging) surrounding tissue; too little desiccates (dries out) the wound. Maintain a moist wound bed without saturation.

**Moisture-wicking dressings:**
- **Foam dressings:** Absorb moderate fluid; maintain moisture
- **Alginates (seaweed derivative):** Absorb heavy exudate; form gel in wound
- **Hydrofibers:** Highly absorbent; good for draining wounds
- **Impregnated gauze:** With saline, antiseptic, or honey

**Improvised alternatives:**
- **Saline-soaked gauze:** Soak sterile or clean gauze in normal saline; apply to wound; cover with dry dressing
- **Honey-based dressings:** Medical-grade honey (Manuka) is antimicrobial and promotes healing. If unavailable, raw honey (if available locally) has some antimicrobial properties
- **Cloth dressings:** Clean cloth changed 1–2 times daily

**Managing excessive exudate:**
- Absorb with foam or alginate dressings
- Protect surrounding skin with barrier ointment (zinc oxide, petroleum jelly)
- Prevent maceration by changing dressings frequently

### E: Epithelialization

Once a clean wound bed with good granulation tissue is established, epithelialization (growth of new skin) should occur. Support by:

- **Moist wound environment:** Promotes epithelial cell migration
- **Avoid drying:** Do not use dry gauze; keeps wound moist
- **Pressure relief:** Reduces shear and friction
- **Growth factors:** Topical growth factor products (if available) stimulate epithelialization, but costly
- **Nutrition:** Protein, vitamins, hydration

## Maggot Debridement Therapy (MDT)

Maggot therapy is an ancient, evidence-based treatment gaining renewed interest in austere medicine. Sterilized larvae of the green bottle fly (Lucilia sericata) selectively consume dead tissue while leaving healthy tissue intact.

### Advantages

- Selective debridement (removes only necrotic tissue)
- Antimicrobial secretions reduce bacterial load
- Promotes granulation tissue formation
- Biofilm disruption
- Well-tolerated by most patients
- Low cost (where available commercially; may be free if using wild-collected larvae)

### Disadvantages

- Requires patient psychological acceptance
- Risk of allergic reaction (rare)
- May not be available in all regions
- Requires containment to prevent spread
- Takes 3–7 days; patient compliance needed

### Application

**Sourcing larvae:**
- **Commercially sterilized larvae:** Medical suppliers provide sterile larvae in containers (ideal but may be unavailable)
- **Wild-collection method:** Expose wound to flies (usually Lucilia, Calliphora, Sarcophaga species) in a sheltered area; allow natural infestation; cover after ~24 hours to prevent further infestation. Requires careful monitoring.

**Application protocol (if using commercial sterile larvae):**

1. **Prepare wound:** Clean gently; remove loose dead tissue
2. **Apply barrier:** Place impermeable barrier (plastic sheet, waterproof tape) around wound to contain larvae
3. **Apply larvae:** Place 100–500 larvae directly on wound base, depending on wound size
4. **Cover:** Drape with netting or gauze to allow oxygen and odor escape but prevent escape of larvae
5. **Containment:** Use a secure dressing or wrap
6. **Monitor:** Check daily for signs of distress; normal findings are pain and drainage
7. **Duration:** Keep larvae for 3–7 days; remove before they pupate
8. **Removal:** Once debridement is complete, rinse thoroughly with saline or allow to escape naturally; remove any remaining larvae
9. **Post-treatment:** Manage resulting wound per TIME framework

### Risks

**Allergy:** Rare but possible; watch for signs of hypersensitivity
**Escape:** Larvae may escape if dressing is loose
**Excessive pain:** Manage with analgesia
**Tissue damage:** If larvae are over-applied or left too long, granulation tissue may be damaged

</section>

## Negative Pressure Wound Therapy Alternatives

Negative pressure wound therapy (NPWT, "vacuum therapy") uses suction to draw fluid from the wound, reducing edema and promoting healing. Commercial NPWT machines are expensive and require electricity. Improvised alternatives exist for austere settings.

### Mechanisms

- Increases blood flow to wound margins
- Reduces bacterial load
- Promotes granulation tissue
- Reduces fluid accumulation

### Improvised NPWT

**Materials:**
- Clean porous foam or gauze
- Plastic sheet or adhesive drape
- Tubing (medical-grade or improvised from clean tube)
- Suction source (hand-operated manual aspirator, if available)

**Basic setup:**
1. Place porous foam or gauze over the wound
2. Cover with plastic sheet or adhesive drape, sealing edges
3. Cut a small hole in the plastic; attach tubing
4. Apply gentle negative pressure via syringe or manual aspirator every 4–6 hours or continuously if possible
5. Change foam every 24–48 hours

**Limitations:**
- Requires manual effort or improvised suction source
- Less effective than powered NPWT
- Risk of contamination
- Labor-intensive

### Alternative Moisture-Management Dressings

Instead of NPWT, consider:
- **Alginates:** Absorb heavy exudate
- **Foam dressings:** Combine absorbency with moisture balance
- **Superabsorbent polymers:** Highly absorbent; keep surrounding skin dry
- **Honey-based dressings:** Reduce fluid while promoting healing

## Distinguishing Infection from Colonization

If you landed here because the wound suddenly smells bad, has new pus, or you need immediate cleaning steps for today's dressing change, also check <a href="../wound-hygiene-infection-prevention.html">Wound Hygiene, Infection Prevention & Field Sanitation</a> for the symptom-first hygiene and infection pathway.

Chronic wounds are colonized (contain bacteria) but not always infected (bacteria causing tissue damage and systemic response).

### Colonization vs Infection

**Colonized wound:**
- Contains bacteria but no clinical signs of infection
- Healing may still progress
- Minimal drainage, no odor
- Surrounding skin normal
- No systemic symptoms (fever, leukocytosis)

**Infected wound:**
- Bacteria cause tissue damage
- Clinical signs present (see below)
- Increased drainage, purulent (pus-like), foul odor
- Surrounding skin erythematous (red), warm, edematous (swollen)
- May have systemic symptoms (fever, chills, malaise)

### Clinical Signs of Infection

**Local signs:**
- Increasing pain, especially with movement
- Increased drainage (quantity or color change from baseline)
- Purulent (yellow/green, thick) drainage
- Foul or "rotten" odor
- Spreading erythema (redness spreading beyond wound edge)
- Edema (swelling) extending beyond visible wound
- Warmth compared to surrounding skin
- Fluctuance (squishy area suggesting abscess)
- Sinus tract or probing (hole extending into deeper tissue)
- Undermining (tissue loss extending beyond visible wound margin)

**Systemic signs:**
- Fever (>38.5°C / 101.3°F)
- Tachycardia (heart rate >100 bpm)
- Leukocytosis (elevated white blood cells, if measurable)
- Altered mental status
- Hypotension (in severe infection)

### Management of Infected Wounds

**Local interventions:**
- Increase dressing changes (to 2–3 times daily if exudate heavy)
- Use antimicrobial dressings (silver, iodine, honey)
- Sharp debridement to remove purulent tissue
- Drain any abscess if fluctuant
- Systemic antibiotics (see below)

**Systemic antibiotics:**
- **Empiric broad-spectrum** until culture/sensitivities available
- **First-line (beta-lactam + beta-lactamase inhibitor):** Amoxicillin-clavulanate 500/125 mg three times daily or cefoxitin 2 g IV/IM every 6–8 hours
- **Penicillin allergy:** Fluoroquinolone (ciprofloxacin 500 mg twice daily) or clindamycin 300–450 mg three times daily
- **Coverage for Staphylococcus aureus + Streptococcus:** Standard for wound infection
- **Duration:** 10–14 days; adjust based on response
- **Recheck:** Wound should show improvement (decreased drainage, erythema resolving) within 48–72 hours. If worsening, consider resistant organism or need for surgical intervention.

**Diabetic foot ulcers:** Infection risk is high. Use aggressive antibiotic therapy even for mild signs. Osteomyelitis (bone infection) requires 4–6 weeks of antibiotics and may require amputation if not controlled.

:::danger
Signs of spreading infection (ascending cellulitis toward the heart, systemic toxicity, or signs of sepsis—fever, tachycardia, hypotension, altered mental status) require emergency evacuation and IV antibiotics.
:::

## Special Considerations and Dressing Selection

### Venous Ulcers

Ulcers from venous insufficiency (swollen legs, varicose veins) occur on the lower leg, often on the medial (inner) ankle. Management includes compression therapy.

- **Compression bandaging:** Wrap the lower leg snugly (not tourniquets-tight) to promote venous return
- **Elevation:** Legs above heart when resting
- **Ambulation:** Walking promotes healing
- **Antimicrobial dressing:** If infection risk is high
- **Skin care:** Prevent dermatitis from excessive moisture

### Arterial Ulcers

Ulcers from arterial insufficiency (poor blood supply) occur on the feet, tips of toes, or areas of trauma. Management focuses on improving perfusion.

- **Do not compress:** Compression worsens arterial disease
- **Optimize perfusion:** Vascular intervention (if available)
- **Pain management:** Often painful; may require opioids
- **Avoid infection:** Careful wound care
- **Smoking cessation:** Critical to prevent progression

### Dressing Selection Summary

| Wound Characteristics | Recommended Dressing |
|---|---|
| Heavy exudate, necrotic tissue | Alginate, foam with frequent changes |
| Moderate exudate, good granulation | Foam, hydrofiber, impregnated gauze |
| Minimal exudate, at-risk for drying | Hydrogel, saline gauze, honey |
| Signs of infection | Antimicrobial dressing (silver, iodine, honey) + systemic antibiotics |
| Malodorous | Antimicrobial dressing, activated charcoal if available |

## Quick Wound Assessment Guide

For those without medical training, systematic wound assessment guides appropriate treatment decisions and monitoring. Use this flowchart-like process to evaluate any wound and determine next steps.

:::card
**Step 1: Stop Bleeding**
- Apply direct pressure with clean cloth for 5-10 minutes
- If bleeding continues after 10 minutes, may indicate artery damage—elevate and seek help
- Once bleeding stops, proceed to assessment
:::

:::card
**Step 2: Wound Type Identification**
Ask these questions:
- **Is the skin broken?** If no (bruise, swelling only) → not a wound; treat with rest/ice
- **Depth?** Superficial (top skin layer) vs. deep (you can see below dermis/yellow tissue)
- **Size?** Small (<1 cm) vs. moderate (1-5 cm) vs. large (>5 cm)
- **Contamination?** Clean/surgical vs. dirty/soil/rust vs. animal bite vs. puncture
- **Location?** Face/scalp (tend to bleed more, scar more visible) vs. trunk/limbs
- **How long ago?** <6 hours (can suture) vs. >12 hours (infection risk higher, sutures contraindicated)
:::

:::card
**Step 3: Severity Assessment**
Choose ONE that applies:

**Minor wound:**
- Superficial cut/scrape
- Small (<1 cm)
- Clean source
- Bleeding stopped
- No gaping edges
→ *Clean, bandage, monitor for infection*

**Moderate wound:**
- Deeper cut (you can see into dermis/fat)
- Medium size (1-5 cm)
- Possibly contaminated
- Edges may gape slightly
- From 6-12 hours ago
→ *Needs cleaning, possibly closing; watch for infection*

**Severe/urgent wound:**
- Very deep (muscle, bone visible)
- Large (>5 cm) or heavily gaping
- Heavy bleeding that won't stop
- Contaminated/animal bite/puncture
- From >24 hours ago
- Shows signs of infection (red/swollen/pus)
→ *Requires professional help if possible; aggressive infection control needed*
:::

:::card
**Step 4: Infection Risk Assessment**
Increase monitoring frequency if:
- Contaminated source (dirty, soil, animal, ocean)
- Puncture wounds (higher infection risk)
- Located on foot or hand (less blood flow)
- In person who is immunocompromised, diabetic, or elderly
- Edges are ragged or crushed (not clean cut)
→ *Check daily for redness spreading beyond edges, warmth, pus, swelling*
:::

:::card
**Step 5: Treatment Pathway & Monitoring Schedule**

**For minor wounds:**
- Clean with mild soap and water
- Apply antibiotic ointment
- Bandage if needed for dirt protection
- Check daily for infection signs
- If no infection signs in 3 days → healing normally

**For moderate wounds:**
- Clean thoroughly (remove debris)
- Close if <12 hours old and clean (sutures or strips)
- Apply antibiotic ointment and dressing
- Check every day for: spreading redness, warmth, pus, increasing pain
- Change dressing every 1-2 days
- If infection signs appear → increase dressing changes to 2x daily, consider antibiotics

**For severe wounds:**
- Focus on infection prevention above all else
- Clean thoroughly, remove debris, soak in saline if available
- Do NOT close (sutures trap bacteria)
- Apply antimicrobial dressing (honey, silver if available)
- Check for infection TWICE daily
- If infection appears (fever, spreading redness, pus) → start systemic antibiotics immediately
- Plan for professional evaluation as soon as possible
:::

:::info-box
**Key Principle:** The biggest risk with wounds is infection, not the wound itself. Any wound can become infected if bacteria multiply. Frequent monitoring (daily or twice-daily checks for infection signs) is more important than perfect closure.

**When to worry:** Redness spreading beyond the original wound edge, warmth beyond the wound, swelling worsening after 24 hours, pus discharge, fever, red streaks extending up the arm/leg toward the heart, increasing pain after initial improvement—all indicate infection developing. Address immediately with increased cleaning, antibiotics if available, and professional evaluation if possible.
:::

<section id="documentation-and-monitoring">

## Documentation and Progress Monitoring

Track wound healing to assess efficacy of interventions.

**At each dressing change, document:**
- **Wound dimensions:** Length × width × depth (in cm)
- **Wound bed characteristics:** % of granulation tissue, % of necrotic tissue, % of slough
- **Exudate:** Amount (scant, moderate, copious), color, odor
- **Surrounding skin:** Color, warmth, edema, integrity
- **Patient pain level:** On a 0–10 scale
- **Signs of infection:** Present or absent (with specifics)
- **Dressing type and any topical treatments applied**
- **Date and time of dressing change**

**Expected healing trajectory:**
- **Week 1–2:** Removal of necrotic tissue; establishment of clean wound bed
- **Week 3–4:** Granulation tissue formation (red, bumpy tissue); wound bed filling in
- **Week 4–8:** Epithelialization; wound margins constricting inward
- **Beyond 8 weeks:** If minimal progress despite appropriate care, reassess diagnosis and intervention

**If no progress or worsening:**
- Reassess vascular status (especially diabetic ulcers)
- Obtain wound culture if infection is suspected
- Ensure adequate nutrition and hydration
- Increase frequency of dressing changes
- Consider advanced interventions (NPWT, growth factors, skin grafts if available)

<section id="summary">

## Summary

Chronic wounds require systematic assessment using staging systems (pressure ulcers, Wagner classification for diabetic ulcers) and the TIME framework for management. Prevention is paramount for pressure ulcers. Diabetic ulcers demand close monitoring for infection and vascular compromise. Maggot therapy is an effective, low-cost alternative debridement method. Distinguish infection from colonization to guide antibiotic use. Documentation of progress guides adjustments in therapy. In austere settings, focus on pressure relief, infection prevention, and systematic dressing management with available materials.

For day-one or symptom-first wound questions such as cleaning, odor, contamination, or "does this look infected," use <a href="../wound-hygiene-infection-prevention.html">Wound Hygiene, Infection Prevention & Field Sanitation</a> first, then return here for chronic-wound follow-up planning if the wound is not healing over time.
