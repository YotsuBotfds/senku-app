:::warning
**Important:** This guide is about recognizing possible STI patterns and reducing transmission risk. Symptoms do not confirm a diagnosis. Many STIs have no symptoms at all, and the same symptom can come from several different infections or from non-infectious irritation. There is no reliable herbal cure or home substitute for testing and prescribed treatment when that is needed.
:::

<section id="overview">

## Overview: What This Guide Can and Cannot Do

Sexually transmitted infections can affect the genitals, mouth, throat, anus, skin, blood, liver, and pregnancy outcomes. Some are common and treatable. Some can cause infertility, chronic pain, miscarriage, newborn infection, organ damage, or long-term illness if missed.

This guide focuses on:

- Recognizing **common symptom patterns** without pretending symptoms are a diagnosis
- Understanding **asymptomatic spread**
- Reducing transmission through **barriers, abstinence during symptoms, hygiene, and testing**
- Communicating with partners without blame or secrecy
- Knowing why **pregnancy and newborns need special caution**
- Knowing when self-care is not enough and urgent care is needed

If you have symptoms after a sexual exposure, or you were told a partner has an STI, the safest assumption is that you need testing even if you feel fine.

If the exposure was **recent**, do not wait for symptoms before seeking help. Some post-exposure prevention steps are time-sensitive and work best when started quickly after sexual assault, blood exposure, condom failure with a high-risk partner, or a known exposure to HIV or hepatitis B.

:::tip
The most dangerous STI mistake is assuming "no symptoms" means "no infection." Many infections spread silently for weeks, months, or longer.
:::

</section>

<section id="recent-exposure">

## Recent Exposure: What To Do Today

If the exposure just happened and you do not have symptoms yet, the next steps still matter.

Use this checklist:

1. Stop sex until the situation is clearer.
2. Write down when the exposure happened and what kind of exposure it was.
3. Arrange testing and medical evaluation rather than waiting for symptoms.
4. Ask promptly whether time-sensitive post-exposure care is appropriate, especially after sexual assault, blood exposure, known HIV exposure, or hepatitis B exposure.
5. Do not assume "I feel fine" means no risk.

Examples of urgent post-exposure questions include:

- Whether HIV post-exposure medicine may still be useful
- Whether hepatitis B vaccination or other hepatitis B post-exposure care is needed
- Whether emergency contraception, pregnancy evaluation, or assault-related care is needed

:::warning
Recent exposure is different from a long-standing symptom question. Some prevention steps can lose value if you wait too long, so same-day or next-day evaluation is safer when the exposure was recent.
:::

</section>

## Recognition: Symptom Patterns That Can Fit an STI

Symptoms are clues, not proof. The same symptom can come from infection, irritation, allergy, friction, or another medical problem.

### Common Symptom Patterns

| Pattern | Possible causes | What to do |
|---|---|---|
| Burning when urinating, discharge, or genital discomfort | Chlamydia, gonorrhea, trichomoniasis, urinary infection, irritation | Do not guess from symptoms alone. Get tested. |
| Genital sores, blisters, ulcers, or crusting | Herpes, syphilis, chancroid, trauma, other skin conditions | Avoid sex. Seek testing and evaluation. |
| New genital rash, bumps, warts, or skin changes | HPV, molluscum, herpes, syphilis, dermatitis, other causes | Avoid self-diagnosis. Get examined. |
| Pelvic pain, pain during sex, fever, or abnormal bleeding | Pelvic inflammatory disease, cervical infection, pregnancy-related problem, other infection | Needs prompt medical assessment. |
| Testicular pain, swelling, or tenderness | Epididymitis, torsion, hernia, trauma, infection | Urgent evaluation, especially if sudden or severe. |
| Sore throat after oral sex | Gonorrhea, chlamydia, other throat infection, viral illness | Testing may be needed even if throat symptoms are mild. |
| Rectal pain, discharge, bleeding, or tenesmus | Rectal STI, hemorrhoids, fissure, inflammatory bowel disease | Needs evaluation. |
| Whole-body rash, especially palms/soles | Secondary syphilis or other illness | Do not ignore. Seek testing and care. |
| Jaundice, dark urine, pale stool, fatigue | Hepatitis B or other liver disease | Needs medical evaluation. |

### Symptoms That Are Especially Important

- **Unusual discharge**
- **Pain with urination**
- **Sores, blisters, ulcers, or warts**
- **Genital itching or burning that does not improve**
- **Lower abdominal or pelvic pain**
- **Bleeding after sex or bleeding between periods**
- **Testicular pain or swelling**
- **Rectal symptoms after anal sex**
- **Throat symptoms after oral sex**

### Why Symptoms Can Mislead You

Many STIs are asymptomatic, especially early on. When symptoms do happen, they can overlap:

- Chlamydia and gonorrhea can look similar
- Vaginal irritation can be yeast, BV, STI, or skin irritation
- Genital sores can be herpes, syphilis, or a non-STI skin problem
- Pelvic pain can be STI-related or unrelated to sex entirely

That means symptom checking can help you decide **how urgent** the problem is, but it cannot safely replace testing.

<section id="asymptomatic">

## Asymptomatic Spread: Why "Feeling Fine" Is Not Reassuring

Some infections spread easily even when the infected person has no symptoms.

Common examples of silent or minimally symptomatic spread include:

- **Chlamydia**
- **Gonorrhea**
- **Hepatitis B**
- **HPV**
- **HIV in early stages**
- **Syphilis in early or latent stages**

This matters because:

- A person can pass an infection to a partner without knowing it
- A person can become more ill later even if the first signs were mild
- Pregnancy and newborn risk can exist before anyone notices symptoms

:::warning
Do not use "I feel normal" as proof that no STI is present. If there was exposure, testing is still the safer choice.
:::

</section>

<section id="transmission">

## How STIs Spread

STIs do not all spread the same way, but risk commonly comes from:

- Vaginal sex without a barrier
- Anal sex without a barrier
- Oral sex, especially when there are sores, cuts, or contact with infected fluids
- Contact with genital sores or infected skin
- Sharing sex toys without cleaning or barrier changes
- Exposure to blood or semen
- Pregnancy, birth, or breastfeeding for certain infections

They are **not** spread by casual contact like sitting near someone, sharing a meal, or using the same toilet seat. That said, some infections can spread through close skin-to-skin contact or blood, so do not use casual-contact rules to dismiss risk.

### Transmission-Reduction Basics

1. **Avoid sex when symptoms are present**
2. **Use condoms or other barriers every time**
3. **Use a new barrier for each act and each partner**
4. **Use lubricant that is compatible with the barrier material**
5. **Do not share sex toys unless cleaned and covered**
6. **Get tested after new partners or possible exposure**
7. **Tell partners about confirmed infection so they can be tested and treated**

:::tip
If a condom breaks, slips off, or was used incorrectly, treat that as a possible exposure and arrange testing.
:::

</section>

<section id="barriers">

## Safer Sex and Barrier Guidance

Barrier methods reduce risk, but they do not make sex risk-free.

### Condoms

Use condoms for vaginal, anal, and many oral exposures when appropriate.

Good practice:

- Check the package for damage and expiration
- Put the condom on before any genital contact
- Leave room at the tip if the design requires it
- Use lubricant to reduce tearing
- Replace the condom if it slips, breaks, or is removed
- Dispose of it after one use

Important cautions:

- Oil-based products can weaken latex condoms
- Two condoms at once can increase friction and breakage
- A condom only protects the area it covers

### Dental Dams and Other Barriers

For oral sex, a dental dam or another barrier can reduce exposure to bodily fluids and skin lesions.

### Gloves

Disposable gloves can help when contact with sores, blood, or genital fluids is expected, especially for caregivers or in partner-assisted care.

### Sex Toys

Do not share unprotected sex toys between people. If a toy is used by more than one person:

- Use a new barrier each time, or
- Clean it thoroughly between uses according to the material and design

### What Barriers Cannot Do

Barriers do not fully prevent infections spread by:

- Skin-to-skin contact outside the covered area
- Contact with sores not covered by the barrier
- Blood exposure if there is direct contact elsewhere
- Infections already present before the barrier was used

<section id="self-diagnosis">

## When Not to Rely on Self-Diagnosis

Do not try to "name the infection" from symptoms alone when any of these apply:

- You have **new sores, ulcers, or blisters**
- You have **pelvic pain, fever, or pain during sex**
- You are **pregnant or could be pregnant**
- A partner has a **confirmed STI**
- You have **multiple partners** or a recent new partner
- You have **oral or anal exposure** and new throat or rectal symptoms
- Symptoms keep returning after home care
- You have symptoms plus **bleeding**, **testicular pain**, or **swollen lymph nodes**

Home care can reduce irritation, but it cannot tell you whether you need antibiotics, antivirals, or no STI treatment at all.

### Common Mistakes

- Assuming vaginal itching is always yeast
- Assuming discharge means the same thing in every person
- Waiting for symptoms after a known recent exposure
- Waiting for symptoms before getting tested
- Treating one partner but not the other exposed partner(s)
- Stopping care too early because symptoms improved

</section>

<section id="partner-communication">

## Partner Communication and Follow-Up

If an STI is possible or confirmed, communication matters because untreated partners can reinfect each other.

### What To Tell a Partner

Keep the message simple and factual:

- You may have been exposed
- Testing is recommended
- Avoid sex until the situation is clear
- If a diagnosis is confirmed, partners may need treatment too

### Practical Record-Keeping

Write down:

- Date symptoms started
- Date of last sexual contact
- Type of exposure if known: vaginal, anal, oral
- Whether a barrier was used
- Any sores, discharge, pain, fever, or bleeding
- Test dates and results
- Treatment dates if treatment was given
- When sex resumed

This record helps with follow-up, retesting, and partner notification.

### Avoid Reinfection

If treatment is prescribed:

- Take it exactly as directed
- Do not share medication
- Avoid sex until the clinician says it is safe or until the treatment course is completed
- Make sure partners are evaluated too

</section>

<section id="pregnancy">

## Pregnancy and Newborn Risk Notes

Pregnancy changes the stakes. Some infections can pass from parent to baby during pregnancy, labor, or birth. Others can make pregnancy outcomes worse.

### Why Pregnancy Needs Special Attention

STIs during pregnancy can be associated with:

- Miscarriage
- Premature birth
- Low birth weight
- Eye or lung infection in the newborn
- Newborn blood infection
- Stillbirth in severe untreated cases
- Increased risk of postpartum complications

### Infections of Special Concern

Examples that can affect pregnancy or newborns include:

- **Syphilis**
- **Gonorrhea**
- **Chlamydia**
- **HIV**
- **Hepatitis B**
- **Herpes**

### Practical Pregnancy Guidance

- Tell prenatal providers about any exposure or symptoms
- Get screened as advised during pregnancy
- Do not assume mild symptoms are harmless
- If a partner is diagnosed, do not wait for symptoms before getting evaluated
- Avoid trying to self-treat with herbs or unverified remedies

### Newborn Warning Signs

If a newborn has any of the following, seek urgent medical help:

- Fever or low temperature
- Poor feeding
- Lethargy
- Eye discharge or redness
- Breathing trouble
- Rash
- Unusual irritability

:::danger
Pregnancy and newborn exposure are not situations for guesswork. If pregnancy is possible, or a newborn may have been exposed, testing and professional evaluation matter even when symptoms are mild.
:::

</section>

<section id="red-flags">

## Urgent Red Flags

Seek urgent medical evaluation if any of the following occur:

- **Severe pelvic or lower abdominal pain**
- **Fever with genital, pelvic, or urinary symptoms**
- **Testicular pain or swelling**
- **Sudden severe pain with swelling**
- **Genital sores with spreading rash or systemic illness**
- **Heavy bleeding not explained by a normal period**
- **Pregnancy with pain, bleeding, or fever**
- **Newborn with fever, poor feeding, or eye symptoms**
- **Confusion, fainting, or signs of sepsis**
- **Painful swallowing or severe throat symptoms after oral exposure**

If there is severe pain, swelling, or fever, do not wait for a symptom pattern to become clearer.

</section>

<section id="practical-takeaways">

## Practical Takeaways

If you only remember a few things, remember these:

1. **Symptoms do not confirm an STI. Testing does.**
2. **No symptoms does not mean no infection.**
3. **Barriers reduce risk but do not eliminate it.**
4. **Tell partners and avoid sex until exposure is clarified.**
5. **Pregnancy and newborn exposure need extra caution.**
6. **There is no reliable herbal substitute for proper diagnosis and treatment.**

When in doubt, choose the path that reduces spread first: pause sex, use barriers correctly, arrange testing, and document what happened.

</section>
