# Property Rights & Land Law Frameworks

Property law is the foundation of any functioning economy and community. Without clear frameworks for ownership, resource allocation, and dispute resolution, communities fragment into conflict. This guide provides practical templates for establishing property rights systems in resource-constrained, low-connectivity environments.

## Categories of Property

<section id="property-categories">

Understanding property types helps you design frameworks that address different goods appropriately.

**Real Property**
Land and permanent structures (buildings, wells, fences, roads). These are immobile, long-lived, and central to settlement patterns and agriculture. Real property claims are inherently visible and difficult to hide.

**Personal Property**
Moveable goods: tools, livestock, food stores, clothing, vehicles. Ownership changes hands frequently; theft risk is high. Personal property frameworks must balance possession-based and title-based claims.

**Intellectual Property**
Recipes, techniques, designs, stories, and seed varieties. Difficult to protect without enforcement power, but critical for innovation and knowledge transmission. In small communities, reputation and reciprocal sharing often substitute for formal IP law.

**Communal Property**
Shared resources managed for collective benefit: forests, pastures, fisheries, roads, water systems. Communal property requires governance structures to prevent tragedy-of-the-commons degradation.

:::info-box
**Property Rights in Collapse Contexts**

The first weeks/months after collapse will have massive, visible property redistribution as people appropriate abandoned goods. Waiting to formalize property law until "things settle" risks cementing squatter possession as the de facto norm. Early agreement on property principles—even loose ones—prevents later conflict escalation and theft-based cycles.
:::

</section>

## Historical Property Systems for Reference

<section id="historical-systems">

These historical and contemporary systems provide templates that have worked at different scales.

**English Common Law Tradition**
Based on hereditary land ownership, freehold and leasehold estates, and clear documentation (deeds). Features: fee simple ownership (permanent, heritable), feudal residue (lord-tenant relationships, quitrents), common-law remedies (trespass, conversion, replevin), and witness-based proof. Advantages: clear hierarchies, long historical track record. Disadvantages: requires expensive documentation and strong central authority to enforce.

**Roman Law Tradition**
Classified property into fundus (landed estates), mancipium (objects requiring formal transfer), and res communes (things not owned—water, air, light). Distinguished possession (physical control) from ownership (legal right). Introduced written contracts and manumission frameworks. Advantages: sophisticated handling of intangible rights. Disadvantages: requires literate bureaucracy and imperial enforcement.

**Indigenous Land Systems**
Many indigenous societies in North America, Australia, and Africa used seasonal territory rotation, usufruct rights (right to use if improving), and spiritual/ancestral attachment to land rather than exclusive ownership. Features: collective tenure, use-based rather than title-based claims, long-term sustainability. Advantages: prevent over-concentration of land, encourage sustainable management, reduce inheritance disputes through non-heritable claims. Disadvantages: require strong oral tradition and enforcement through community reputation.

**Islamic Waqf System**
Religious endowments managed for public benefit (schools, hospitals, water systems). Property is formally held in trust by a manager (trustee) on behalf of beneficiaries (the community or specific lineages). Advantages: removed from inheritance fragmentation, generated stable funding for public goods. Disadvantages: requires religious authority and could accumulate land excessively over time.

**Swiss Alpine Commons (Alpgenossenschaften)**
Cooperative management of high-altitude pastures by village associations. Each member-village had shares; usage rights rotated seasonally. Detailed agreements on herd sizes, boundary maintenance, and dispute resolution. Worked for 500+ years. Advantages: stable, transparent, community-based. Disadvantages: requires detailed record-keeping and strong social cohesion.

</section>

## Establishing Initial Property Claims

<section id="initial-claims">

In post-collapse contexts, property claims initially follow occupation and improvement rather than formal title.

**First-Occupancy / Squatter's Rights**
In the first months, whoever occupies a building or land can reasonably claim it. Formalize this by having them register with a community property authority (even if informal): name, date of occupancy, structure/parcel description, any improvements made. Provide a certificate (physical copy, witnessed, dated). This transforms squatter possession into recognized claim.

**Improvement-Based Claims**
If someone clears overgrown land, repairs a building, digs a well, or plants crops, their labor investment justifies enhanced claims. Document improvements with witness records: date work began, what was improved, community members who observed it. Assign a "improvement value" score (e.g., 1–10 scale) to determine dispute priority later. Someone who cleared and fenced a field has stronger claim than someone who simply walked on it once.

**Inheritance & Succession**
Document who held property before collapse. If records exist, use them. If not, use community memory: ask neighbors who they knew owned each property. Document claims in a ledger with at least two witnesses per entry. Distinguish between "clear heirs" (spouse, children on record), "likely heirs" (extended family per custom), and "disputed heirs."

:::warning
**Preventing Claim Flooding**

Once you announce a claim registry, expect a deluge of retrospective claims to abandoned or unclear properties. Set a **registration deadline** (e.g., 60–90 days). Claims registered after the deadline are invalid except for active occupancy (squatter's rights from the deadline forward). This prevents chronic uncertainty and chain-title problems.
:::

**Neutral Initial Allocation**
If land was genuinely ownerless (never formally claimed, no survivors, no previous records), use lottery, contribution-based allocation (who did the most community labor), or tie allocation to family size (calories needed). Document the method publicly before allocation so it's perceived as fair.

</section>

## Commons Management Using Ostrom's Principles

<section id="commons-management">

Elinor Ostrom's research on long-enduring commons identified eight design principles that prevent tragedy-of-the-commons without central control or privatization.

**Principle 1: Clearly Defined Boundaries**
The commons resource must have clear spatial and membership boundaries. A pasture commons needs fencing or marked boundary stones; a fishery needs metes (specific depths/distances). A community that cannot define its commons boundary cannot enforce rules on it.

*Implementation*: Mark boundaries physically (fence, cairn, blazed trees) and maintain a map with written description (measured in paces, surveyed distances, reference landmarks). Update annually.

**Principle 2: Congruence Between Rules and Local Conditions**
Rules must match the resource's ecology and the community's capacity. Permitting six sheep per person works if pasture carrying capacity is six per person; it fails if capacity is three. Rules that ignore seasonal cycles (drought, flooding) generate crisis.

*Implementation*: Measure resource carrying capacity before setting rules. For pasture: count usable grazing area, estimate forage per season, divide by sheep consumption. For fishery: monitor catches in first year; if declining, reduce allowed catch. Revisit rules annually.

**Principle 3: Participatory Decision-Making**
Those affected by rules must have voice in creating them. Rules imposed from above generate resentment and violation.

*Implementation*: Hold assembly meetings (monthly or seasonal). Use consensus or supermajority voting. Document decisions in writing. Record dissent and concerns—they inform future modifications.

**Principle 4: Monitoring by Monitors Accountable to Users**
Assign named individuals to monitor commons use (check sheep counts, inspect fishing nets, patrol boundaries). These monitors must report to the community and be rotated or re-elected regularly, so they remain accountable.

*Implementation*: Monthly monitoring reports (written or oral, witnessed). Quarterly reviews where monitors report to full assembly. Rotate monitors every 2 years to prevent capture by rule-breakers.

**Principle 5: Graduated Sanctions**
Violations should trigger proportionate penalties, escalating with repetition. First offense: warning. Second: fine or labor duty. Third: loss of commons access. Never jump to harshest penalty immediately.

*Implementation*: Write violation schedule on a posted board:
- First violation: public warning, documented in ledger
- Second violation: fine (1–2 day's labor equivalent) or required commons labor (fence repair)
- Third violation: 1-month suspension of access
- Fourth violation: permanent expulsion

**Principle 6: Conflict Resolution Mechanisms**
Disputes over who used too much, who damaged the resource, or who violated rules must be resolvable without escalating to violence. Establish a panel of respected elders or neutral arbiters.

*Implementation*: Create a 3–5 person disputes panel. Members rotate annually. Any commoner can lodge a complaint in writing; the panel meets monthly and hears cases. Decisions are documented and posted publicly. Include appeal to full assembly if panel decision is challenged.

**Principle 7: Recognition of Rights by External Authorities**
The larger governance structure (town, region) must recognize the commons' right to self-regulate. If external authorities can override local rules or impose conflicting rules, commons management collapses.

*Implementation*: Document the commons agreement and obtain signatures of town council or regional leader, confirming recognition of local autonomy. Include clauses on appeals and conflicts with larger law.

**Principle 8: Nested Enterprises**
For large resources (major fishery, large forest), organize governance at multiple levels (village subunit, regional body, watershed level) with clear escalation channels.

*Implementation*: Small commons (one village's pasture) need only village-level governance. Large commons (major river fishery used by multiple villages) need both village-level monitoring and an inter-village council to resolve cross-village disputes and coordinate on total catch limits.

:::tip
**Pasture Commons Example**

A 5-village coalition manages a 200-acre upland pasture used seasonally (May–September). They implement Ostrom's principles as follows:

1. **Boundaries**: Fence the pasture perimeter; map all entrances and gates.
2. **Congruence**: Year 1 test: allow 3 sheep per household (40 households = 120 sheep). Monitor vegetation decline. Year 2: if good regrowth, allow 4 per household. If degradation, reduce to 2.
3. **Participation**: Spring meeting: all villages send herders and elders to agree on season dates, animal limit, and rules. Decisions by show of hands, documented.
4. **Monitoring**: Assign 10 monitors (2 per village) to rotate weekly presence in pasture. Check animal counts at gates using tally sticks. Report to monthly assembly.
5. **Sanctions**: Rules posted at each gate in pictures (for non-literate herders): "Max 4 sheep per mark" (picture shows 4 stick figures). Violation penalties: first time, 1 day of fence repair; second time, pay 1 sheep to common fund; third time, expulsion for season.
6. **Dispute Resolution**: Any herder can lodge complaint at month-end assembly. A panel of 3 elders (one from each village not party to dispute) investigates and rules within 2 weeks.
7. **Recognition**: Village councils sign charter recognizing pasture assembly's authority over animal limits and sanctions.
8. **Nesting**: If inter-village disputes arise (one village sent too many animals), escalate to regional council of all 5 village headmen.

Result: Such systems historically sustained commons for 300+ years.
:::

</section>

## Water Rights Frameworks

<section id="water-rights">

Water is critical and scarce; two major frameworks exist for allocating it.

**Riparian Rights System**
In riparian systems, property owners adjacent to water have the primary right to use it. Riparian owner can use water for natural needs (household, livestock) and reasonable use (mill, modest irrigation). Cannot divert flow so downstream riparians are harmed. Works well in water-abundant regions (high rainfall, multiple streams).

*Implementation*: Define "natural use" as water sufficient for household (humans + livestock), garden (small-scale), and natural seepage. Define "reasonable use" as water for a productive facility (mill, small irrigation) that does not materially reduce downstream flow. Document each riparian user's allocation in a ledger. In case of dispute or drought, natural uses take priority; reasonable uses may be curtailed.

**Prior Appropriation System**
First person to divert water for beneficial use acquires right to that amount, regardless of property location. Right is junior to (senior) prior appropriators and senior to (junior) later appropriators. Works well in arid regions where water is scarce and allocation must be explicit.

*Implementation*:
1. Establish a priority date for each water use: date person filed claim or began use.
2. Measure water diverted (volume per day or season) at the point of withdrawal using a gauge or weir.
3. Document in a ledger: user name, source stream, amount (e.g., 500 gallons/day), use (household, irrigation, mill), priority date.
4. In shortage, curtail junior rights first; senior users' full allocation remains secure.
5. Establish a water master (elected official) to measure uses, enforce priorities, and settle disputes.

**Hybrid Approach (Recommended for Small Communities)**
Use riparian logic for small household/livestock uses (everyone gets enough to survive). Layer prior appropriation for larger productive uses (mills, irrigation). Priority dates for productive uses start from date of community agreement, so early settlers who establish mills get senior rights.

:::info-box
**Measuring Water Flow**

Without precision instruments, use simple methods:
- **Bucket & stopwatch**: Fill a bucket from the source for 10 seconds; measure volume. Gallons per 10 sec × 6 = gallons per minute.
- **Weir**: Notch a board or stone in a stream at a fixed depth; divert all flow through the notch. Depth of water in notch × width of notch × flow speed (estimated by floating a stick) ≈ flow rate.
- **Visual inspection**: Fast flow (rapids) = more water; slow flow (sluggish) = less. Compare to a reference stream known to be (e.g.) 100 gallons/day; this stream looks twice as fast = 200 gallons/day.

Consistency matters more than precision. Measure the same way each month so you can track trends (increasing/decreasing water availability).
:::

**Seasonal Allocation**
In areas with winter dry-downs or monsoon patterns, allocate water differently by season. Document seasonal rules (summer maximum 500 gal/day per household, winter maximum 200 gal/day). This prevents over-extraction during drought.

**Groundwater**
For wells and springs, use prior appropriation (whoever drills first has primary right). Distinguish shallow wells (hand-dug, community replacement possible) from deep wells (requires expertise, cannot be easily replicated). Deep-well rights may be longer-term or more senior. Document well locations and yields.

</section>

## Salvage Territory Rules

<section id="salvage-territory">

Post-collapse environments have abundant abandoned buildings, vehicles, and materials. Chaotic salvage generates theft, injury, and conflict. Establish clear territory rules.

**Territory Assignment**
Divide nearby towns/regions into salvage territories. Assign each territory to a work crew (5–10 people) via drawing lots or rotation. A crew has exclusive salvage rights in their territory for a set period (e.g., 3 months), then territories rotate.

*Implementation*: Map nearby settlements and subdivide into zones (e.g., "East Town – North District," "West Town"). Draw for initial assignment. Crews begin salvaging; document what they remove. Every 3 months, rotate: each crew moves to the next zone.

**Salvage Records**
Each crew maintains a ledger of items removed: date, building address, item (copper wire, bricks, tools, furniture), estimated value (relative to labor), recipient (who receives the salvaged goods—crew member, community fund, trade pool). This prevents double-salvaging and tracks value contribution.

*Implementation*: Provide each crew a bound ledger. Entries: [Date] [Location] [Item] [Amount] [Recipient]. Weekly crews report totals to community authority. Totals feed into labor credit or barter systems.

**High-Risk Salvage**
Structures with hazards (unstable roof, asbestos, deep basement, flooded basement) require special approval and safety rules. A small safety committee (3–5 people, including one with building experience) reviews high-risk sites. Only trained crews salvage them; safety equipment is mandatory (rope, helmet, observer on ground).

:::warning
**Structural Collapse & Asbestos**

Pre-1980s buildings often contain asbestos (insulation, floor tiles, ceiling tiles). Disturbance releases fibers—acute exposure causes coughing/choking; chronic exposure causes mesothelioma 10–40 years later.

**Protocol**: Never cut or scrape building materials you cannot identify. If visible white/gray insulation, assume asbestos. Do not salvage it; mark the building as "ASBESTOS HAZARD" and move on. Document the location so future generations know to avoid.

Flooding in basements creates drowning and disease hazard. Never enter flooded basement alone. Use rope, buddy system, and check depth with a pole before entering.
:::

**Sharing High-Value Salvage**
Items worth significant labor (copper wire, intact solar panels, medical equipment) are community property, not crew property. Crew gets labor credit (e.g., 10 hours of salvage work = 10 day-labor credits toward future goods). Community stores items for allocation by council or lottery.

</section>

## Inheritance Systems

<section id="inheritance">

Without clear inheritance rules, property disputes trigger cycles of theft, claims, and violence. Establish rules early.

**Types of Inheritance**

*Primogeniture (Eldest Child Inherits All)*
Advantages: keeps property consolidated, simple, avoids fragmentation. Disadvantages: unfair to younger children, can concentrate wealth, may incentivize violence between siblings. Historically used by European nobility; generated lasting inequality.

*Partible Inheritance (Equal Division)*
Property divides equally among all children. Advantages: more egalitarian, reduces inequality. Disadvantages: fragmenting land reduces viability of farms, creates complexity in administration, can lead to land disputes if division is contested. Historically used in Scandinavian and some Islamic traditions.

*Entail with Dowry*
Eldest son inherits land; younger children receive moveable goods (cattle, tools, goods) or dowry for marriage. Keeps land intact while providing junior children resources. Works if enough moveable wealth exists; fails in very poor communities.

*Usufruct (Life Tenancy)*
Person who uses land holds it for life; upon death, it reverts to community and is reallocated to whoever needs it most. No inheritance; no accumulation. Advantages: prevents extreme inequality, keeps property productive. Disadvantages: destroys incentive to improve land, risky for families (never know if they'll keep their farm). Used in some indigenous and socialist systems; requires strong community authority.

**Recommended Hybrid Approach**
- **Primary residence**: Cannot be divided; passes to spouse (if any) or eldest adult child. If no heirs, reverts to community.
- **Land (farm/pasture)**: Cannot be divided in inheritance (would make unviable); passes as primary residence rule. Junior heirs get no land, but receive other property.
- **Moveable goods** (tools, livestock, goods): Divide equally among all children. Spouse receives 1 share; each child receives 1 share. If only 1 child, spouse + child split equally.
- **Craft/business**: Passes to apprentice or eldest child trained in the trade. Other children receive moveable share.

**Intestate Rules** (When No Will Exists)
Document the default inheritance sequence:
1. Spouse (if married)
2. Adult children (prioritize those with dependents)
3. Parents (if deceased, skip)
4. Siblings
5. If no heirs, property reverts to community fund

Prioritize ability to productively manage the property. If eldest child is incompetent (too young, ill, disinterested), junior child who is capable may inherit land with obligation to support elder child/parent.

**Will Documentation**
Allow written wills (witnessed by 2+ community members, recorded in ledger). Encourage oral wills (before 3+ witnesses, recorded immediately in ledger) if writing is rare. Allow modification of default rules if stated clearly in a will. Honor wills above intestate default.

:::tip
**Dispute Prevention**

To minimize inheritance disputes:
1. Encourage elderly to state wishes before death (oral testimony before witnesses).
2. Document multiple times (elder's statement witnessed, written in ledger by clerk).
3. If eldest child is incompetent, allow elder to formally appoint a different heir; record this in ledger.
4. Provide window for challenge (30 days after death) before property transfers; resolve challenges by community court.
:::

</section>

## Recording Property: Deeds & Registries

<section id="recording-property">

Without a recording system, property ownership is disputed after first generation. Establish a registry.

**Deed Components**
Each property transfer (sale, gift, inheritance) is recorded in a deed. Deed includes:
- **Date** (day, month, year)
- **Grantor** (person transferring property, signature or mark)
- **Grantee** (person receiving property, signature or mark)
- **Property description** (address, boundary description, size if known)
- **Consideration** (price paid, or "gift," or "inheritance")
- **Witnesses** (2+ community members who saw the transfer, names, signatures/marks)
- **Clerk signature** (person recording the deed)

Example:
```
15 September 2027
I, Sarah Miller, convey to Thomas Chen my house located on Mill Road,
east side, with garden (approximately 1 acre). Consideration: 50 labor credits.

Witnesses: John Wood, Elder's Council; Martha Hill, Militia Chief
Recorded by: Samuel Records, Clerk
```

**Registry Ledger**
Maintain a bound ledger (or set of ledgers by area) with all deeds recorded in order. Each entry is a deed. Index the ledger by property address or owner name at the back (add index entries as new deeds are recorded).

*Backup*: Create a second copy of the ledger (monthly or quarterly) to reduce loss risk if original is destroyed. Store in separate location (another building or house).

**Property Description Standards**
Without surveying tools, describe property by:
- **Metes and bounds**: Walk the boundary, count paces, note landmarks. "Starting at large oak tree on north road, 50 paces east, 100 paces south to stream, 50 paces west to fence, 100 paces north to starting oak tree."
- **Reference landmarks**: "The red-roofed house south of the mill, lot between Miller's house and blacksmith shop."
- **Area estimate**: "Approximately 1 acre" based on visual comparison to known parcel.
- **Building details**: "Two-story timber house, stone chimney, root cellar."

:::info-box
**Surveying Basics Without Instruments**

To measure a property accurately:

1. **Pace count**: Walk the perimeter at steady pace. Count paces. Measure your pace length by walking 100 known paces (e.g., using rope marked in 1-pace increments) and checking how close you are. Average pace = ~2.5 feet for adult on level ground.

2. **Rope & stakes**: Tie rope to starting corner (peg with witness present). Stretch rope to next corner, pull tight, mark with stake. Count rope lengths. Record distance.

3. **Trigonometry alternative**: If property is triangular, estimate base and height by pacing. Area ≈ base × height ÷ 2 ÷ number of square paces in reference area you know (e.g., your home lot is 50 × 50 paces = 2500 square paces = 1 acre rough estimate).

4. **Witness verification**: Have 2+ neighbors walk the boundary with you; they sign off that description is accurate.

Precision to 5% is acceptable for property recording. Exact surveying is not necessary.
:::

**Search & Verification**
To verify current ownership:
1. Go to registry ledger.
2. Search by property address in index.
3. Find most recent deed for that property.
4. Check grantor name on most recent deed = current owner.
5. Trace back 3–5 deeds to verify unbroken chain of ownership.

If chain is broken (missing transfer, disputed grantee), property is disputed and must go to community court.

</section>

## Surveying & Boundary Marking

<section id="surveying">

Clear boundaries prevent disputes. Mark them physically and document in writing.

**Boundary Markers**
- **Cairns**: Stack of stones at corner points (visible, permanent, difficult to move without notice).
- **Blazed trees**: Chop or mark tree bark at corner points with community mark (circle, cross, arrow). Mark multiple trees along boundary line.
- **Fence or hedge**: Living boundary (hedge) or constructed (stone, wood fence) marks the line visually.
- **Ditch or raised berm**: Dig a ditch or raise soil to mark the line.

**Documentation**
Walk the boundary with 2+ witnesses. Record:
- Starting point (known landmark)
- Direction and distance to each corner (paces, rope lengths, or visual landmarks)
- Marker type at each corner (cairn, tree, fence, etc.)
- End point (return to start)
- Date and witness names

Example: "North boundary of Miller property: Starts at large oak tree on north road. Walk east 100 paces to stone cairn with cross mark. Turn south 80 paces to fence line. Fence continues east to corner marked by white-painted rock. Witnessed by Sarah Wood and Thomas Chen, 10 March 2027."

**Maintenance**
Boundaries degrade (cairns topple, blazes fade, fences rot). Inspect boundaries annually. Rebuild markers that have failed. Document maintenance in ledger.

</section>

## Dispute Resolution Mechanisms

<section id="dispute-resolution">

Property disputes must be resolvable without violence. Establish a clear process.

**Types of Property Disputes**
- **Boundary disputes**: Disagreement over where property line lies.
- **Ownership disputes**: Two people claim the same property.
- **Possession disputes**: One party holds property; other claims rightful owner.
- **Improvement disputes**: One party claims property; another claims right to improvements made.
- **Inheritance disputes**: Multiple heirs claim same property.

**Process**

**Step 1: Informal Mediation**
Disputants meet with a respected neutral party (elder, clergy, community leader) who hears both sides and proposes compromise. Most disputes resolve here. If resolved, document the agreement in writing and sign.

**Step 2: Community Court**
If informal mediation fails, case goes to community court (3–5 judges, selected from rotating volunteer list or appointed by council). Court hears testimony from both parties and witnesses. Judges vote on outcome. Decision is binding.

*Record keeping*: Court records all testimony in writing. Document judges' decision and reasoning. Store records in central ledger indexed by case date and property address.

**Step 3: Appeal**
Losing party can appeal to regional council (if one exists) or full community assembly. Appeal requires new evidence or procedural error; general disagreement with verdict is not sufficient reason to appeal. Regional council hears appeal and issues final decision.

**Evidence Standards**
- **Deed on file**: Most persuasive. Current deed says person is owner.
- **Possession + Improvement**: Long-term occupation + visible improvements (fenced, planted, buildings) is strong.
- **Witnesses to transfer**: Multiple people who saw property transfer support ownership claim.
- **Customary law**: "In our tradition, eldest son inherits" supports inherited claim if customary law is documented.
- **Oral testimony**: Weakest form; vulnerable to lies and faulty memory.

:::tip
**Judging Property Cases**

Key principle: **Prefer clarity and finality over perfect justice.** A property dispute that drags on for years damages community more than an imperfect judgment executed quickly.

When judging a boundary dispute with contradictory evidence, use this hierarchy:
1. Survey record in ledger (most recent)
2. Deed description + witness verification of current markers
3. Long-term possession of property + visible improvements (crops, structures)
4. Testimony of elders who remember original boundaries
5. Mediation-based compromise (split the disputed area)

Once judgment is rendered, it is final (unless clear fraud is later revealed). New disputes on the same property go to next court, not re-litigation of prior judgment.
:::

</section>

## Enforcement Without Police

<section id="enforcement">

Property law means nothing without ability to enforce decisions. Police are expensive and sometimes absent. Use community-based enforcement.

**Social Sanctions**
The most powerful enforcement tool is reputation and social exclusion.
- **Public shaming**: Publicly declare that person X violated property law (stole, violated boundary, ignored court decision). Post declaration in public space (town square). Community ostracizes person: no one trades with them, no one hires them, no one invites them to communal meals.
- **Shunning**: In extreme cases (repeated property crimes), community may refuse to interact with person at all. They can live in community but are cut off from social participation, trade, and mutual aid. This is painful and effective.

**Bond System**
Require property violators to post a bond (cash, livestock, or labor) as security against future violations. If person violates again, bond is forfeited. Example: "John stole property; court orders him to post 100 day-labor credits bond. If he steals again within 2 years, he loses bond and is expelled."

**Bonded Guarantor**
Require a respected community member (elder, religious figure) to vouch for a person's property compliance. Guarantor is liable if person violates. This incentivizes guarantor to monitor person and deters the person from violating (because they'd shame their guarantor).

**Physical Sanctions**
If person persists in property violation despite social sanction and bond:
- **Restitution**: Thief repays stolen item's value in labor (e.g., 30 days of work for community).
- **Restraint**: Confine person to home under guard during day; release at night. Lasts until restitution is complete.
- **Expulsion**: Severe cases (repeated theft, property destruction, violence). Person is expelled from community; must leave and not return. Document in public ledger so other communities know to watch for expelled person.

:::warning
**Proportionality in Enforcement**

Avoid excessive punishment. Death, mutilation, or indefinite imprisonment for property crime is disproportionate and destabilizing. Enforce proportionally: minor theft → fine or public warning; repeated theft → bond + restitution; severe/violent theft → expulsion. This maintains legitimacy and reduces community members' fear of the enforcement system itself.
:::

</section>

## Special Cases

<section id="special-cases">

**Intellectual Property in Small Communities**

Recipes, techniques, and seed varieties are valuable intellectual property. Protect them by:
- **Attribution**: Credit originator when sharing knowledge. "This recipe comes from Sarah's grandmother" maintains reputation and incentive.
- **Apprenticeship**: Share knowledge only with apprentices who commit to confidentiality and attribution. Formalize with agreement.
- **Selective disclosure**: Share key techniques only with trusted community members; hold back one crucial step so knowledge is incomplete if shared further.
- **Community registry of innovations**: Document new techniques with inventor's name and date. Anyone using the technique gives attribution; inventor gets credit (status, trade priority, first claim to improvements).

Example: "The Miller brothers invented a new grain-sorting screen (date: March 2027). Anyone building one pays 2 day-labor credits to the brothers and publicly names it 'Miller Sorter.' The brothers retain right to teach others for a fee."

**Shared Tooling**

Tools are expensive to make; communities often share them. Establish tool-lending pools:
- **Inventory**: List all community tools (location, condition, borrower).
- **Loan rules**: Max 3-day borrow; return in good condition; replacement fee if damaged.
- **Tool master**: Appoint person to manage tool inventory, approve loans, track condition, coordinate repair/replacement.
- **Cost sharing**: When tool needs repair or replacement, cost is shared by all recent borrowers.

**Grazing Rights**

Livestock grazing on communal or borderlands generates disputes. Assign rights by:
- **Stocking rate**: Each household entitled to X sheep/cattle per capita (e.g., 1 cow per adult + 1 cow per 3 children in household).
- **Pasture rotation**: Divide pasture into 4 sections; rotate herds seasonally to prevent overgrazing.
- **Damage liability**: If livestock damage neighbor's crops, owner pays restitution (% of crop loss).
- **Strays**: Unclaimed animals become community property. Owner can recover by paying feed cost + finder's fee.

</section>

## Updating & Amending Property Law

<section id="updating-law">

As communities grow, property law must evolve. Establish a process for amendment.

**Annual Review Assembly**
Hold annual meeting (or every 2 years) where all property law is reviewed. Community members raise issues:
- "Inheritance rule is causing disputes; should we change it?"
- "Salvage territories should rotate every 4 months, not 3."
- "Water allocation is too generous; stream is declining."

**Proposal Process**
Any community member can propose an amendment. They state the issue and proposed change, documented in writing (or oral if recorded). Amendment is debated publicly, then voted on (majority or supermajority, depending on community rule).

**Grandfathering Existing Property**
When law changes, apply new rules only to transfers going forward. Existing property claims remain under old rules. Example: "New inheritance law: eldest child gets primary residence, others get moveable property. This applies to estates settling after January 2028. Estates already settled under old rules (primogeniture) are honored as-is."

**Recording Changes**
Document all amendments in a separate "Amendments Ledger" with: date, issue, old rule, new rule, vote tally, effective date. This creates history so future generations understand why rules exist.

</section>

## Integration with Governance Structures

<section id="governance-integration">

Property law must align with larger governance. Establish how property authority relates to other powers.

**Property Authority vs. Legislative Body**
Decide: Does property court have power to create property law, or only apply existing law created by council/assembly? Usually: council creates general framework; court interprets and applies it. Significant changes (inheritance law, commons rules) go to assembly for vote.

**Appeals to Higher Authority**
If community has regional governance (multiple towns, provinces), establish appeals process. A party dissatisfied with town property court decision can appeal to regional court. Regional court hears cases on procedural grounds or new evidence; it does not retry cases.

**Conflict with Other Laws**
What if property law conflicts with security/defense needs? Example: "Town needs to conscript 50% of available labor for fortification; is that a taking of property rights in labor?" Establish hierarchy: in emergency (military attack, plague), security needs override property temporarily. After emergency ends, restore property rights. Compensate people for emergency labor via tax reduction or community service credit.

**Documentation & Publication**
Post all property laws in public (town square, meeting house, religious building). Read them aloud regularly (monthly at assemblies) so even non-literate community members know the rules. This prevents "law hidden in government books" scenarios and builds legitimacy.

</section>

## Summary & Next Steps

Property law is foundational to post-collapse community stability. Begin with:

1. **Establish a registry** (bound ledger + backup copy).
2. **Clarify deed format** (date, grantor, grantee, property, witnesses).
3. **Set inheritance defaults** (or adopt hybrid system recommended above).
4. **Define boundary markers** (cairns, blazes, fences).
5. **Create dispute court** (3–5 judges, defined process).
6. **Post rules publicly** (read aloud monthly).
7. **Review & amend annually** (assembly proposes, votes on changes).

This system can sustain 50–500 people indefinitely. At 1000+ people, you'll need regional integration and possibly a formal surveying office, but basics remain the same.
