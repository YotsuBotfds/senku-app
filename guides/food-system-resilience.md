## Overview

Food resilience is not about eating well—it's about having *enough to eat* when supply chains fail. This guide walks you from subsistence staples through backup crops, supplemental foraging, and preservation into a year-round nutritional system that survives winter, drought, and disruption.

The bridge connects **production** (what you grow) → **diversity** (what you can pivot to) → **wild supplements** (what the land offers) → **storage** (how you get from harvest to next harvest) → **verification** (are you actually eating enough?).

## What You Need Before Starting

- Access to at least 500 m² of tillable land (or containers + foraging access if land-limited)
- Seed supply for staple crops (wheat, maize, beans, potatoes OR regional equivalents)
- Basic composting setup to close the nutrient loop
- Storage space for grains, preserved vegetables, and salt/oil
- Reference guides: seed-saving, crop-rotation, food-preservation, foraging basics

## Foundation: Staple Crops and Caloric Math

**The Core Three (minimum):**

1. **Starch base** — wheat, maize, rice, or potatoes (lowest caloric cost per m²)
2. **Legumes** — beans, lentils, peas (nitrogen fixation + protein)
3. **Oil/fat source** — flax, sunflower, nuts, animal products (essential fatty acids + caloric density)

**Caloric requirements (adult, moderate activity):**
- Sedentary: ~2,000 kcal/day
- Moderate labor: ~2,500–3,000 kcal/day
- Heavy labor (farming, building): ~3,500–4,000 kcal/day

**Yield ballpark (per 100 m²):**

| Crop | Yield (kg/yr) | Kcal/kg | Per 100m² (kcal) | Servings/person/yr |
|------|---------------|---------|------------------|-------------------|
| Wheat | 300–500 | 3,300 | 990k–1.65M | 1–1.5 people |
| Potatoes | 2,000–3,000 | 770 | 1.54M–2.31M | 2–3 people |
| Maize | 400–600 | 3,650 | 1.46M–2.19M | 1.5–2 people |
| Beans (dry) | 150–250 | 3,300 | 495k–825k | 0.75–1.25 people |
| Lentils | 100–150 | 3,150 | 315k–472k | 0.5–0.75 people |
| Sunflower (seeds) | 80–150 | 5,900 | 472k–885k | 0.75–1.5 people |

**Minimum viable plan for 1 adult, 1 year:**
- 150–200 m² wheat + maize (starch: 1.5M–2M kcal)
- 50–75 m² beans (protein + fat: 400k–600k kcal)
- 30–50 m² potatoes (backup starch + micronutrients)
- 20–30 m² oil source (sunflower, flax, or nut trees)
- 50 m² vegetables for fresh eating and preservation

**Total: ~300–400 m² for true staple independence per person.**

## Tier 2: Backup Crops and Pivot Strategies

Staple crops can fail. Your resilience depends on crop diversity and pre-planned fallbacks.

**Backup starch crops (if primary fails):**
- Sorghum (drought-tolerant; 300–400 kg/100m²)
- Buckwheat (poor soil tolerant; 150–250 kg/100m²)
- Barley (marginal land; 250–400 kg/100m²)
- Millet (heat/drought resistant; 200–350 kg/100m²)
- Taro or cassava (tropical; starch-rich, long-term storage)

**Backup legumes (nitrogen fixation + protein):**
- Chickpeas (drought-tolerant)
- Peas (cool-season, fast-growing)
- Alfalfa (perennial hay + nitrogen fixation)
- Clover (cover crop + forage)

**Nutrient density champions (small space, big return):**
- Kale, collards, chard (cold-hardy, multi-harvest)
- Carrots, beets, parsnips (root cellar storage, 6+ months)
- Squash, pumpkin (long storage, nutrient-dense)
- Cabbage (fermentation, multi-month storage)

:::warning
**Rotation mandate:** Never plant the same crop family in the same field twice in 3 years. Staple crops (grasses, legumes) require crop rotation to avoid soil depletion and disease accumulation.
:::

## Tier 3: Foraged Supplements

No food system is truly resilient without tapping wild edibles. Foraging is not survival—it's supplementation: wild greens, nuts, berries, roots, mushrooms.

**Why foraging matters:**
- Micronutrients (wild greens are nutrient-dense)
- Diversity (plants you don't plant yourself)
- Spring/summer supplementation (before garden production peaks)
- Backup in production failures

**High-ROI foraging categories (region-dependent):**

1. **Spring greens** — dandelion, ramps, chickweed, lamb's quarters (20–40 kcal/100g fresh, high vitamin K/A)
2. **Nuts** — acorns (with leaching), walnuts, hickories, chestnuts (5,000–6,000 kcal/kg)
3. **Berries** — wild strawberry, elderberry, blueberry (50–100 kcal/100g fresh; 1,000+ kcal/kg dried)
4. **Roots** — cattail root, burdock, wild onion (100–300 kcal/100g)
5. **Mushrooms** — chanterelles, morels, oyster mushrooms (200–350 kcal/kg fresh; high umami nutrition)

:::info-box
**Critical:** Learn ID from multiple sources. Taste-test cautiously (tiny amount, wait hours). Keep a backup reference guide. Many edible plants have toxic look-alikes.
:::

**Foraging production planning:**
- Identify 3–5 reliable foraging sites in your area
- Map seasonal timing (what's ready when)
- Plan 2–3 hours/week of foraging, spring-to-fall
- Expected supplementation: 300–800 kcal/day during season

## Tier 4: Food Preservation—Closing the Harvest-to-Winter Gap

Production is worthless if food spoils before you eat it. Preservation methods convert harvest gluts into shelf-stable nutrition.

**Preservation methods (by shelf life):**

| Method | Best For | Shelf Life | Space/Effort | Notes |
|--------|----------|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Root cellar (cool, dark, moist) | Potatoes, root veg, squash | 3–8 months | Low effort, moderate space | 50–60°F, 80–90% humidity |
| Fermentation (salt brine) | Cabbage, beans, vegetables | 6–12+ months | Minimal space, low effort | Builds probiotics |
| Drying (sun, smoke, kiln) | Meat, fish, herbs, fruits | 6–24 months | Moderate space, high labor | Moisture <12% |
| Canning (high-acid, pressure) | Fruits, pickles, tomato sauce | 1–2 years | Moderate space, learning curve | Requires altitude adjustment |
| Oil/fat preservation | Meats, vegetables | 3–6 months | Moderate space, minimal effort | Confit, rillettes |
| Salt curing | Fish, meat, vegetables | 6–12+ months | Low effort, moderate space | Requires salt supply |
| Freezing (root cellar equiv) | Everything | 8–12 months | Very high space | Requires below-freezing temps |

**Target preservation split for year-round eating:**
- 40% root cellar (staples: potatoes, carrots, onions, squash)
- 25% fermented (cabbage, beans, vegetables, condiments)
- 20% canned (fruit preserves, sauces, soups)
- 10% dried (herbs, mushrooms, fruit leather)
- 5% salt-cured or oil-preserved (meat, special items)

:::tip
**Pro move:** Preserve *multiple* methods for critical items. If root cellar fails (flood, freeze-thaw), fermented backups and canned goods survive.
:::

## Tier 5: Nutritional Verification—Are You Actually Eating Enough?

Having food and having *complete nutrition* are different. A diet of only wheat is starvation in slow motion.

**Non-negotiable micronutrients (in staple-based diet):**

| Nutrient | Why | Staple Source | Backup Source |
|----------|-----|---------------|----|
| **Iron** | Oxygen transport, energy | Beans, lentils | Foraged greens, preserved meat |
| **Calcium** | Bones, muscles | Leafy greens, milk | Eggshells (crushed), wild plants |
| **Iodine** | Thyroid, metabolism | Salt, seaweed, fish | Iodized salt (if available) |
| **Vitamin C** | Immune, collagen | Fresh veg, fermented items | Dried rose hips, preserved citrus |
| **Vitamin A** | Vision, immune | Orange root veg, leafy greens | Foraged wild greens, egg yolks |
| **Vitamin D** | Bones, immune | Sun exposure, egg yolks | Mushrooms exposed to sun, fish |
| **Omega-3** | Heart, brain | Flax, walnuts, fish | Foraged nuts, wild fish |

**Monthly nutrition audit (simple checklist):**
- [ ] Eating leafy greens 4+ times/week (vitamin K, folate, iron)
- [ ] Eating legumes 3+ times/week (iron, folate, protein)
- [ ] Eating orange/red vegetables weekly (vitamin A)
- [ ] Getting sun or supplementing vitamin D (winter is critical)
- [ ] Eating fermented foods 2–3 times/week (gut health, vitamin K)
- [ ] Having salt in diet (iodine, electrolytes)

:::warning
**Watch for deficiency signs:** Fatigue, pale skin, joint pain, poor wound healing, mood issues. If you see these, increase micronutrient sources immediately.
:::

## Integration: From Season to Season

**Spring (March–May):**
- Plant staple and early crops
- Forage early greens (dandelion, ramps, chickweed)
- Begin weaning off root cellar storage
- Eat preserved foods, rotating oldest first

**Summer (June–August):**
- Tend crops through peak growth
- Forage berries, early nuts
- Preserve surplus (ferment, dry, can)
- Eat fresh vegetables and fruits

**Fall (September–November):**
- Harvest staples (wheat, maize, beans)
- Dig roots (potatoes, carrots, parsnips)
- Forage nuts, late berries, mushrooms
- Intensive preservation work

**Winter (December–February):**
- No field work (except repairs, planning)
- Live off stored grain, root cellar, fermented, canned goods
- Eat foraged mushrooms, nuts
- Plan next year's crop rotation and foraging locations

## Quick Reference: Foods That Store 12+ Months

- **Grains (intact seeds):** wheat, maize, rice, barley
- **Legumes:** beans, lentils, peas (in dry storage)
- **Roots (cool/dark):** potatoes, carrots, parsnips, beets, onions, garlic
- **Squash:** winter squash, pumpkin (2–4 months; less than others but reliable)
- **Fermented:** sauerkraut, pickled vegetables, fermented sauces (6–12+ months)
- **Salt-cured:** fish, meat (6–12+ months)
- **Oil-preserved:** vegetables, meat confit (3–6 months)
- **Dried:** mushrooms, herbs, fruit, jerky (12+ months if protected from moisture/pests)
