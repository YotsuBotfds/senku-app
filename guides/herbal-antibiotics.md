<section id="pharmaceuticals-runout">

## 1\. When Pharmaceuticals Run Out

Antibiotic medications are among the most crucial pharmaceuticals in any preparedness plan. For a comprehensive reference on pharmaceutical antibiotics, storage, and dosing, see <a href="../medications.html">Essential Medications</a>. Understanding their shelf life and limitations is essential for planning long-term medicine storage.

### Antibiotic Shelf Life & Storage

Most antibiotics, when stored properly in cool, dry conditions (below 77°F / 25°C, low humidity), retain approximately 80-90% of their potency for 5-10 years beyond their official expiration date. Some antibiotics, particularly those in powder or liquid form, degrade faster than solid tablets. The Tetracycline family is an exception—degraded tetracycline can cause kidney damage, so these should not be used past expiration.

:::info-box
**Storage Strategy:** Keep antibiotics in their original containers with desiccant packs. Store in a cool basement, root cellar, or climate-controlled safe. Mark expiration dates clearly and rotate stock annually, using older supplies first.
:::

### What Happens When They're Gone

If antibiotic medications are depleted or unavailable, infection becomes a serious concern. Historically, before antibiotics were discovered (pre-1940s), bacterial infections were a leading cause of death. Simple cuts could become life-threatening, and childbirth, surgery, and routine infections killed millions.

In a prolonged emergency without access to pharmaceutical antibiotics, several strategies become necessary:

-   **Prevention first:** Maintain rigorous hygiene, clean wounds immediately, avoid injury
-   **Early intervention:** Use herbal antimicrobials at the first sign of infection
-   **Aggressive treatment:** Higher doses, more frequent application than typical medicine
-   **Expectation management:** Some infections will not respond to herbal treatment and may be fatal

:::warning
**Critical Warning:** Herbal antimicrobials are NOT a reliable replacement for pharmaceutical antibiotics. They work slowly, unevenly, and may fail completely against serious infections. Use them as a fallback when nothing else is available, not as a first choice. For dangerous plant lookalikes that can be confused with medicinal herbs, see <a href="../sur-03-herbal-medicine.html#toxic-lookalikes">Herbal Medicine Guide — Dangerous Plant Lookalikes</a>.
:::

### Rotation Strategy for Long-Term Supply

If maintaining a pharmaceutical antibiotic stockpile, rotate annually:

1.  Organize by expiration date (oldest first)
2.  Use the oldest supply in minor infections when you would normally use herbal remedies
3.  Replace with fresh pharmacy stock
4.  Keep a small reserve of critical antibiotics (broad-spectrum like amoxicillin, fluoroquinolones) that are always the newest
5.  Document usage to track consumption rates

<section id="evidence-levels">

## 2\. Evidence Levels: How We Know These Plants Work

The difference between "kills bacteria in a petri dish" and "reliably treats infection in a human" is vast. This section explains the hierarchy of evidence for herbal antimicrobials.

### In Vitro vs. In Vivo

**In vitro** means "in glass"—laboratory studies where an herb extract is placed on bacteria in a petri dish. Many plants kill bacteria spectacularly in these tests. However, in vivo studies are conducted on living organisms (animals or humans). Inside the body, herbal compounds must survive digestion, pass through intestinal walls, reach the infection site in sufficient concentration, and maintain activity in complex biological environments. Most herbs that kill bacteria in vitro show minimal effect in living systems.

### The Tier System

Herbal antimicrobials are categorized by the quality of evidence supporting their use:

:::info-box
**Tier 1: Strong Evidence**  
Backed by multiple peer-reviewed clinical trials, long historical use (centuries+), measurable bioactive compounds, and documented effectiveness in human infections. These plants work well in herbal form.
:::

:::info-box
**Tier 2: Moderate Evidence**  
Supported by animal studies, some human trials, or traditional use with modern validation. Effective but less reliable than Tier 1. Results vary based on preparation quality, dosage, and individual response.
:::

:::info-box
**Tier 3: Traditional Use with Limited Evidence**  
Used for centuries in traditional medicine systems (Ayurveda, TCM, Native American) but with limited modern clinical validation. May work, may not. Higher risk of ineffectiveness.
:::

### What Makes Strong Evidence?

-   Identification of active compounds and their mechanisms
-   Demonstrated effectiveness in human clinical trials (not just animals or lab)
-   Consistent results across multiple studies and research groups
-   Long historical use with consistent documentation of success
-   Bioavailability proven (compound reaches site of infection in therapeutic concentrations)
-   Minimal adverse effects, good safety profile

## 5\. Tier 3 & Traditional Use: Limited Modern Evidence

These plants have long histories in traditional medicine systems and show antimicrobial activity in laboratory studies, but have limited rigorous clinical trial data in humans. Use them when nothing else is available or as secondary support to proven remedies.

### Neem (Azadirachta indica)

**Used in:** Ayurvedic medicine for 4,000+ years. **Active compounds:** Azadirachtin, nimbin, fatty acids. **Application:** Topical: neem oil on wounds/fungal infections; Internal: neem leaf powder for bacterial infections. **Dosage:** Topical neem oil 2-3 times daily; Internal powder 1-2 grams 2-3x daily. **Caution:** Can be hepatotoxic at high doses; discontinue if nausea develops.

### Andrographis (Andrographis paniculata)

**Used in:** Traditional Chinese Medicine and Ayurveda. **Active compound:** Andrographolide. **Application:** Internal: tincture or powder for respiratory and GI infections; immune stimulant. **Dosage:** 300-400mg extract 2-3x daily or 2-3 ml tincture 3x daily. **Evidence:** Some studies show benefit for upper respiratory infections, but evidence is mixed.

### Pau d'Arco (Tabebuia species)

**Used in:** South American traditional medicine, particularly against fungal infections. **Active compounds:** Lapachol, beta-lapachone. **Application:** Internal: tea or tincture for fungal and bacterial infections. **Dosage:** 1-2 teaspoons dried bark simmered 10 minutes, 2-3 cups daily. **Efficacy:** Limited human trials; primarily used for candida and histoplasmosis.

### Olive Leaf (Olea europaea)

**Active compound:** Oleuropein, secoiridoid compounds. **Application:** Tea, tincture, or extract for broad-spectrum antimicrobial effect. **Dosage:** 1-2 ml tincture 3x daily or 250-500mg standardized extract daily. **Modern studies:** Shows promise against various pathogens but human clinical trials are limited.

### Tea Tree Oil (Melaleuca alternifolia) - TOPICAL ONLY

**Critical:** Tea tree oil is TOXIC if ingested internally. Use topically only. **Active compounds:** Terpineol, cinneole. **Application:** Topical for ringworm, athlete's foot, minor wounds, fungal infections. **Usage:** Dilute 1-2% in carrier oil (5-10 drops in 1 ounce oil), apply 2-3x daily. Never use undiluted on skin. **Effectiveness:** Well-established for topical fungal/bacterial infections.

<section id="preparation-methods">

## 6\. Preparation Methods: Extracting Medicinal Compounds

The way you prepare an herb determines which compounds are extracted and how bioavailable they are. Different preparation methods suit different plant types and different types of infection.

:::card
### Tincture (Alcohol Extraction)

**Best for:** Most herbs, especially those with resinous or volatile compounds (berberine plants, oregano, propolis, echinacea)

**Why it works:** Alcohol extracts both water-soluble and fat-soluble compounds; excellent extraction efficiency; long shelf life (years); rapid absorption when taken orally

**Standard Ratio:** 1 part dried herb (by weight) to 5 parts alcohol (by volume). Use 80-100 proof alcohol (40-50% ABV) or higher—vodka, brandy, or grain alcohol work.

**Process:**

1.  Fill a glass jar with dried herb (loosely packed, about 1/3 to 1/2 full)
2.  Cover completely with alcohol, seal lid
3.  Store in cool, dark place
4.  Shake daily for first week, then weekly for remaining weeks
5.  Steep 4-6 weeks total
6.  Strain through cheesecloth or fine strainer, press herb to extract remaining liquid
7.  Store in dark bottle away from light

**Dosage:** Typically 1-2 ml (approximately 25-30 drops) per dose, 2-3 times daily. For acute infection, dose every 2-3 hours initially.

**Shelf life:** 5+ years in dark glass bottles
:::

:::card
#### Decoction (Root & Bark Simmering)

**Best for:** Hard plant materials—roots, bark, seeds (berberine plants, thyme root, ginger root)

**Why it works:** Heat breaks down cell walls of woody plant material, releasing compounds water cannot penetrate alone; particularly good for extracting minerals and alkaloids

**Standard Ratio:** 1 teaspoon dried root/bark per cup of water (approximately 1:15 ratio)

**Process:**

1.  Place herb in pot with water (cold water, not pre-boiled)
2.  Bring to boil, then reduce to simmer
3.  Simmer uncovered 20-30 minutes (longer for very dense roots)
4.  Strain while hot, pressing herb to extract liquid
5.  Drink warm

**Dosage:** 1 cup (8 oz), 2-3 times daily. Can be refrigerated for 24 hours and reheated.

**Note:** Do not boil—excessive heat destroys volatile compounds and reduces some effectiveness
:::

:::card
#### Infusion (Leaf & Flower Steeping)

**Best for:** Delicate plant parts—leaves, flowers, aerial parts (thyme, echinacea aerial parts, chamomile)

**Why it works:** Hot water extracts water-soluble compounds without damaging volatile oils as much as boiling would

**Standard Ratio:** 1-2 teaspoons dried herb per cup of water (1:10-15 ratio)

**Process:**

1.  Heat water to just before boiling (steam should be rising, not rolling boil)
2.  Pour over dried herb in cup or teapot
3.  Cover with lid or cloth (prevents volatile oils from escaping)
4.  Steep 15-30 minutes
5.  Strain and drink warm

**Dosage:** 1 cup, 2-4 times daily

**Note:** Make fresh each time; herbal infusions degrade quickly (24-48 hours refrigerated maximum)
:::

:::card
#### Poultice (Direct Application)

**Best for:** Topical infections—wounds, abscesses, skin infections; rapid local delivery of active compounds

**Why it works:** Crushed fresh plant material maintains volatile and heat-sensitive compounds; osmotic action of moist herb draws out infection; direct contact with microorganisms

**Process:**

1.  Harvest and crush fresh herb (or rehydrate dried herb with small amount of water)
2.  Crush to paste-like consistency, releasing plant juices
3.  Apply directly to clean wound (1/4 to 1/2 inch thick)
4.  Cover with clean cloth or gauze
5.  Leave 4-6 hours or overnight, replace daily

**Best plants for poultice:** Fresh garlic (crush 2-3 cloves per wound), plantain (draws out infection), comfrey (promotes wound healing), calendula (antibacterial + healing)
:::

:::card
#### Oil Infusion (Herbal Oil)

**Best for:** Creating salves and topical preparations; extracting fat-soluble compounds; long shelf life

**Why it works:** Oil extracts and preserves fat-soluble compounds (essential oils, vitamins A, E, D); herbs suspended in oil remain potent for months

**Two Methods:**

**Cold Infusion (slower, preserves more volatile compounds):**

1.  Pack clean, dry herb loosely in clean glass jar
2.  Cover completely with carrier oil (olive oil, coconut oil, almond oil)
3.  Cover jar with cloth (not metal lid initially—prevents water buildup)
4.  Set in warm, sunny location 2-6 weeks
5.  Strain through cheesecloth, pressing herb
6.  Store in dark bottle

**Heat Infusion (faster, hotter oil destroys some volatile compounds):**

1.  Heat oil gently (200-250°F) using double boiler, never direct heat
2.  Add dried herb, keep warm (don't boil)
3.  Infuse 1-4 hours, stirring occasionally
4.  Cool and strain through cheesecloth

**Ratio:** Herb loosely packed (approximately 1/3 jar volume) to carrier oil to fill jar

**Shelf life:** 6-12 months in cool conditions; watch for mold or rancidity
:::

:::card
#### Salve (Oil + Beeswax)

**Best for:** Topical application to wounds, abscess, fungal infections; thicker consistency stays in place better than liquid oil

**Process:**

1.  Prepare herbal oil infusion (see above)
2.  Gently heat 1 cup oil infusion with 1-2 oz beeswax until beeswax fully melted (use double boiler)
3.  Test consistency: drop small amount on cool surface; should be firm but spreadable when cool
4.  If too soft, add more beeswax; if too hard, add more oil
5.  Pour into clean glass jars or tins while still warm
6.  Allow to cool completely before sealing

**Ratio:** Approximately 1 oz beeswax per 4-5 oz oil (adjust for desired firmness)

**Application:** Spread thin layer directly on wound or skin infection, 2-3x daily. Cover with cloth if needed.

**Shelf life:** 6-12 months; does not require refrigeration if properly made
:::

## 7\. Wound Care Protocol Without Antibiotics

When pharmaceutical antibiotics are unavailable, wound care becomes critical to prevent infection. This protocol balances aggressive antimicrobial treatment with promoting natural healing.

**Step 1: Clean the Wound**  
Start with the cleanest water available. Boil water and cool it slightly if possible, or use saline solution (1 teaspoon salt per 1 cup boiled water). Gently pour clean water over wound to remove dirt, debris, and dead tissue. Use clean cloth (boiled if possible) to pat—do not scrub aggressively as this damages tissue. Continue until water runs clear. For puncture wounds, encourage gentle bleeding to flush debris.
**Step 2: Apply Primary Antimicrobial**  
**Option A—Honey (preferred for most wounds):** Apply 1/4 to 1/2 inch thick layer of raw honey directly to clean wound. Cover with clean cloth or gauze. Change twice daily. Honey will be absorbed and replaced by wound fluid, which is normal. Continue for 7-10 days or until infection signs resolve.  
**Option B—Fresh Garlic (for dirty wounds, heavy infection risk):** Crush 2-3 fresh cloves to paste (wait 10 minutes for allicin formation). Apply directly to wound. Cover with cloth. This is more aggressive than honey, causes some stinging, but has stronger antimicrobial action. Change 2x daily. Use for 3-5 days, then switch to honey for healing phase.
**Step 3: Cover and Monitor**  
Cover with clean, dry cloth. If no cloth available, leave open to air if in clean environment. Change dressing 2x daily: morning and evening. Each change, repeat steps 1-2 (clean, apply antimicrobial).  
**Monitor for these signs that treatment is working:**  
\- Wound pain decreases by day 3-4  
\- Swelling decreases  
\- Discharge is clear/yellow, not foul-smelling  
\- Edges are not spreading  
\- No fever or spreading redness
**Step 4: Escalate if Failing**  
**Warning signs treatment is failing:**  
\- Redness spreads beyond original wound edge (indicates spreading infection)  
\- Swelling increases instead of decreasing  
\- Discharge becomes foul-smelling (indicates anaerobic/deep infection)  
\- Fever develops or increases (systemic infection)  
\- Red streaks extending from wound (lymphatic infection—medical emergency)  
\- Pus accumulation without drainage  
**Escalation steps:**  
1\. Switch to 3-4x daily dressing changes (from 2x)  
2\. Add garlic paste underneath honey for stronger antimicrobial action  
3\. Begin systemic herbal antimicrobials (berberine tincture, oregano oil internally)  
4\. Consider herbal preparations for fever/immune support (echinacea, thyme)  
5\. Prepare for amputation or isolation if cellulitis/sepsis develops (signs: spreading red area, joint involvement, system symptoms)
**Step 5: Promotion of Healing (After Acute Infection Resolves)**  
Once infection signs are gone (no spreading redness, decreased swelling, clean discharge), switch focus to healing:  
\- Apply turmeric-coconut oil paste daily (anti-inflammatory, promotes healing)  
\- Use comfrey poultice (promotes cell regeneration)  
\- Continue honey applications but with less frequent changes  
\- Ensure adequate nutrition (protein, vitamin C for collagen synthesis)  
\- Allow wound to dry gradually; complete healing takes weeks to months

:::warning
**Critical Threshold:** A spreading red line or fever indicates sepsis (blood infection). This is life-threatening and requires immediate escalation to stronger measures, isolation, and consideration of amputation if the infection cannot be contained. For sepsis recognition criteria and emergency response protocols, see <a href="../infection-control.html#complications">Infection Control — Complications</a>. Without modern antibiotics or amputation capability, sepsis is often fatal.
:::

## 8\. Respiratory Infections: Cough, Bronchitis, Pneumonia Risk

Respiratory infections are among the most common infections. Most are viral (colds, flu) and do not respond to antibiotics. Some develop secondary bacterial infections (pneumonia) which require treatment.

### Initial Response (First 3-5 Days)

Most respiratory infections are viral. Antimicrobials help prevent bacterial superinfection but do not cure the virus. Focus on symptom relief and immune support:

**Steam Inhalation:** Heat water to steam (not boiling; 160-180°F). Add dried herbs: thyme, oregano, eucalyptus (if available). Drape towel over head and breathe steam for 10-15 minutes. Repeat 3-4x daily. Opens airways, delivers antimicrobial compounds directly to respiratory tract.
**Garlic Broth:** Simmer 6-8 crushed fresh cloves of garlic in 2 cups water for 10-15 minutes. Strain, add salt and honey. Drink 1 cup, 3-4x daily. Supports immune system, prevents bacterial superinfection.
**Hot Honey-Lemon Drink:** Mix 1 tablespoon raw honey, juice of 1/2 lemon, in hot water. Drink as often as desired. Soothes throat, provides antimicrobial action, aids cough reflex. Do NOT boil honey.
**Thyme Tea:** Steep 2 teaspoons dried thyme 10-15 minutes in hot water. Drink 3-4 cups daily. Thymol is antimicrobial to respiratory pathogens and helps loosen mucus.

### Secondary Bacterial Infection (Pneumonia)

If after 5-7 days symptoms worsen instead of improve, or if fever returns after improving, suspect bacterial pneumonia. Signs include: productive cough with yellow/green phlegm, sustained fever, difficulty breathing, chest pain.

**Chest Poultice:** Apply warm herb poultice to chest/back to delivery antimicrobials directly and reduce inflammation. Mix 3-4 tablespoons dried thyme or oregano with small amount of boiled water to make thick paste. Heat gently (warm but not hot), spread 1/4-inch thick on cloth, apply to chest/back, cover with dry cloth. Leave 2-4 hours. Repeat 2x daily. Alternatively, use honey-based salve.
**Systemic Antimicrobials:** Begin aggressive herbal treatment:  
\- Oregano oil: 2-3 drops in carrier oil, 3x daily  
\- Berberine (Oregon grape root tincture): 1-2 ml, 3-4x daily  
\- Garlic: 4-6 raw cloves, 3x daily  
\- Thyme tea: 4-5 cups daily  
**Duration:** Continue for 2-3 weeks after symptoms resolve.

:::warning
**Danger Signs—Pneumonia Failure to Respond:** Increasing difficulty breathing, cough becomes worse, fever does not decrease, person becomes delirious or loses consciousness. These indicate severe pneumonia. Without antibiotics and advanced care (oxygen, IV fluids, mechanical ventilation), mortality is high, particularly in elderly or those with other illness.
:::

## 9\. GI Infections: Food Poisoning, Dysentery, Bacterial Diarrhea

Gastrointestinal infections from contaminated food or water are common in emergencies. Most resolve on their own in 2-5 days, but severity depends on pathogen and immune status.

### Acute Phase (First 24-48 Hours)

**Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS):** This is THE most important intervention for any GI infection. Diarrhea causes fluid/electrolyte loss; dehydration kills more people than the infection itself.  
**Make ORS:** 1 liter clean water + 6 teaspoons sugar + 1/2 teaspoon salt. Mix thoroughly. Drink sips frequently (5-10 ml every few minutes) rather than large amounts. Continue for duration of diarrhea plus 24 hours after cessation. If vomiting, drink even smaller sips more frequently.
**Activated Charcoal (for acute poisoning only):** If poisoning is suspected (sudden onset after specific food), activated charcoal binds toxins. Take 1-2 grams in water within 1-2 hours of poisoning. Do NOT use for more than 24 hours as it interferes with nutrient absorption. Not effective once toxin is absorbed (after 2-3 hours).
**Ginger for Nausea:** Ginger reduces nausea without suppressing the body's need to evacuate pathogens. 1-2 teaspoons fresh grated ginger steeped in hot water, sip as tolerated. Helps with fluid intake.

### Treatment Phase (Days 2-5)

**Berberine for Bacterial Diarrhea:** Once acute phase passes, berberine targets bacterial pathogens. Dosage: 500mg (or 1-2 ml of Oregon grape root tincture) 3x daily. Particularly effective for E. coli, Shigella, Cholera-like infections. Continue until diarrhea resolves plus 3 days.
**Blackberry Root for Dysentery (Traditional):** Dysentery (diarrhea with blood/mucus) responds to tannin-rich plants. Blackberry root: 1 teaspoon dried root simmered 20 minutes, 3-4 cups daily. Tannins bind to gut wall, reduce inflammation, stop bleeding. Less evidence than berberine but traditional use is consistent.
**Continue ORS Throughout:** Even after starting antimicrobial treatment, continue frequent ORS sipping. Rehydration is the primary intervention; antimicrobials are secondary.

### Recovery Phase (Days 5+)

Once diarrhea resolves, gut is damaged and dysbiotic (normal bacteria killed). Recovery is slow:

-   Return to bland food gradually (bone broth, rice, plain bread)
-   Continue light meals for 1-2 weeks
-   Avoid dairy and high-fiber foods temporarily (irritates recovering gut)
-   Allow 2-4 weeks for full recovery of gut function

:::warning
**Danger Signs:** Uncontrolled diarrhea (more than 10x daily), blood in stool (indicates invasive infection), signs of severe dehydration (confusion, extreme thirst, no urination for 6+ hours, rapid heart rate), abdominal pain with rigid abdomen (perforated gut). These require emergency care.
:::

## 10\. Antifungals: Yeast, Ringworm, Candida Infections

Fungal infections are difficult to treat with herbs. Most herbal antifungals work topically better than systemically. Fungal infections spread slowly but are difficult to eradicate.

### Topical Fungal Infections (Ringworm, Athlete's Foot)

#### Tea Tree Oil (Melaleuca alternifolia)

**Strength:** Most well-established herbal antifungal for topical use. **Active compounds:** Terpineol, cinneole. **Application:** Dilute 1-2% in carrier oil (5-10 drops per ounce of oil). Apply directly to affected area 2-3x daily. Undiluted tea tree oil can damage skin. **Duration:** Continue 2-4 weeks after visible infection clears (fungi can hide in skin). **Effectiveness:** Comparable to OTC antifungal creams in studies.

#### Garlic (Topical)

**Application:** Fresh crushed garlic cloves applied directly to ringworm or athlete's foot. This causes stinging/irritation. Cover with cloth. Change 2x daily. Less potent than tea tree oil but effective. **Duration:** 3-4 weeks.

#### Apple Cider Vinegar (Topical)

**Application:** Soak affected area in apple cider vinegar 2-3x daily, 15-20 minutes per soak. Acidic environment inhibits fungal growth. Particularly helpful for nail fungus (which is very difficult to treat). **Duration:** 4-12 weeks depending on severity.

### Systemic Candida Infections (Oral Thrush, Candida Overgrowth)

These are more difficult to treat. Candida lives inside the body and herbal compounds must reach therapeutic concentrations systemically.

#### Oregano Oil (Systemic)

**Dosage:** 2-3 drops in carrier oil, 3x daily for 4-6 weeks. **Mechanism:** Carvacrol and thymol inhibit candida cell wall synthesis. **Effectiveness:** Moderate; some people see significant improvement, others minimal.

#### Garlic (Systemic for Candida)

**Dosage:** 3-4 raw cloves, 3-4x daily. **Mechanism:** Allicin is fungicidal. **Duration:** 4-8 weeks. **Caution:** Can cause die-off symptoms (temporary worsening as candida die and release toxins) in first 1-2 weeks.

#### Dietary Support

Candida feeds on sugar and refined carbohydrates. Elimination is critical:  
\- Avoid all sugar, honey, sweet fruits for 4-6 weeks  
\- Avoid refined grains, bread, pasta  
\- Avoid alcohol (fermented, high sugar)  
\- Eat protein, healthy fats, non-starchy vegetables  
\- This alone may allow immune system to regain control

### Other Antifungal Herbs

#### Black Walnut Hull (Juglans nigra)

**Traditional use:** Strong antifungal and antiparasitic. **Dosage:** 1-2 ml tincture 2-3x daily. **Caution:** Can stain skin permanently dark if concentrated tincture contacts skin directly. Strong taste. Limited modern research but consistent traditional use.

#### Pau d'Arco (Tabebuia species)

**Traditional use:** South American remedy for fungal infections including histoplasmosis. **Dosage:** 1-2 teaspoons dried bark simmered 10 minutes, 2-3 cups daily. **Effectiveness:** Limited human trials; some positive results but not as established as tea tree oil.

## 11\. Growing Your Medicine Cabinet: Must-Have Medicinal Plants

Building a medical garden ensures you have fresh herbal antimicrobials available when needed. Start with the highest-priority plants and expand from there.

:::card
### Garlic (Allium sativum)

**Priority:** CRITICAL. No garden should lack garlic. Uses: raw antimicrobial, wound treatment, systemic infections.

:::info-box
**Growing Zone:** 3-9  
**Sunlight:** Full sun (6+ hours)  
**Planting:** Fall (October-November), plant cloves 2 inches deep, 6 inches apart  
**Harvest:** Late spring/early summer (June-July) when tops brown  
**Storage:** Dry in cool dark place, keeps 6-12 months  
**Space needed:** 25-30 plants per 4x8 bed
:::
:::

:::card
#### Oregano (Origanum vulgare)

**Priority:** CRITICAL. Powerful antimicrobial, easy to grow. Uses: GI infections, respiratory infections, oil extraction.

:::info-box
**Growing Zone:** 5-10  
**Sunlight:** Full sun (6+ hours)  
**Planting:** Spring, start from transplants (seeds slow), 12 inches apart  
**Harvest:** Just before flowering (July-August), cut top 1/3  
**Drying:** Hang dry, strip leaves from stem when dry  
**Space needed:** 3-4 plants sufficient for family  
**Note:** Perennial, comes back every year
:::
:::

:::card
#### Thyme (Thymus vulgaris)

**Priority:** CRITICAL. Mild, safe, effective for respiratory and GI infections. Uses: tea, steam inhalation, tincture.

:::info-box
**Growing Zone:** 4-9  
**Sunlight:** Full sun (6+ hours)  
**Planting:** Spring, start from transplants, 12 inches apart  
**Harvest:** Just before flowering, cut top 1/2  
**Drying:** Hang dry or use fresh  
**Space needed:** 3-4 plants sufficient  
**Note:** Perennial, tolerates poor soil

**Priority:** HIGH. Topical wound healer, antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory. Uses: salve, oil, poultice.

**Sunlight:** Full sun to part shade  
**Planting:** Spring or fall, direct seed or transplants, 12-18 inches apart

**Drying:** Hang dry or dehydrator, store in sealed container

**Priority:** HIGH. Common edible weed, excellent topical wound treatment. Uses: poultice, salve, tea.

**Growing Zone:** 3-9 (everywhere)  
**Sunlight:** Tolerates any condition  
**Growing:** Harvesting wild is often easier than cultivating  
**Harvest:** Leaves any time, best before flowering  
**Use:** Fresh leaves often most effective

**Priority:** MEDIUM. Wound healing, styptic (stops bleeding), mild antimicrobial. Uses: poultice, salve, tea.

:::info-box
**Growing Zone:** 2-10

#### Elderberry (Sambucus nigra)

**Priority:** MEDIUM. Immune support for viral infections. Uses: syrup, tea, berries fresh.

**Growing Zone:** 3-9  
**Sunlight:** Full sun to part shade  
**Planting:** Spring, transplants, 6-8 feet apart (shrub, grows large)  
**Harvest:** Berries in August-September when fully dark purple

#### Goldenseal (Hydrastis canadensis)

**Priority:** MEDIUM. Source of berberine, powerful antimicrobial. Endangered in wild; growing at home is ethical. Uses: root tincture, decoction.

:::info-box
**Growing Zone:** 3-8  
**Sunlight:** Shade (replicates forest understory)  
**Planting:** Spring, seeds or small plants, 12 inches apart  
**Harvest:** Root (year 5-6+) in fall, replant some rhizomes  
**Growing:** Slow growing; patience required

#### Comfrey (Symphytum officinale)

**Priority:** MEDIUM. Wound healing (promotes cell regeneration), topical use only. Uses: salve, poultice, oil infusion.

**Sunlight:** Full sun to part shade  
**Planting:** Spring, transplants, 24 inches apart

#### Honey Bee Hive

**Priority:** HIGH if feasible. Source of honey (powerful antimicrobial) and propolis. Not a plant but critical medicine source.

**Setup:** Langstroth or top-bar hive  
**Location:** South-facing, near flowers, protected from wind  
**Harvest:** Honey late summer, 30-60 lbs per hive annually  
**Learning curve:** Moderate; many beekeeping courses available  
**Time commitment:** 1-2 hours per week during season  
**Cost:** $300-500 initial setup per hive  
**Returns:** Honey, propolis, beeswax, pollination service

### Medicine Cabinet Garden Layout (4x16 foot bed)

For additional guidance on growing medicinal herbs across different climate zones, see <a href="../spices-seasonings.html">Spices & Seasonings</a>.

This bed can sustain a family's herbal antimicrobial needs:

-   **Back row (tallest):** 1 Elderberry plant or tall variety herbs
-   **Second row:** 4-5 Echinacea plants, 2 Yarrow
-   **Third row:** 3-4 Oregano, 3-4 Thyme, 3-4 Calendula
-   **Front row:** Plantain (wild or cultivated), 6 Garlic (fall plant)
-   **Comfrey:** Separate location (spreads aggressively)
-   **Goldenseal/Oregon Grape:** Shaded bed if available

### Preservation & Storage

To maintain potency of dried herbs:

-   **Drying:** Hang dry in cool, dark, well-ventilated space. Avoid direct sun (degrades active compounds). Takes 1-3 weeks depending on humidity.
-   **Storage:** Glass jars with lids, stored in cool dark place. Properly dried herbs keep 1-2 years potent, then gradually degrade.
-   **Tinctures:** Superior preservation method—alcohol-based tinctures maintain potency 5+ years.
-   **Freezing:** Best for delicate herbs (basil, mint). Chop, place in ice cube trays with water, freeze, then transfer to freezer bags.
-   **Rotation:** Use oldest first, refill from fresh harvest annually.
