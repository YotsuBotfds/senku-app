## Overview: Investigation in a Post-Infrastructure World

When serious crimes occur in a post-collapse community — theft, assault, murder, arson, sabotage — the community must have some capacity to investigate what happened. Without investigation, justice becomes guesswork, the innocent are punished, the guilty act with impunity, and community trust disintegrates.

**The challenge:** Modern forensic science relies on DNA analysis, fingerprint databases, toxicology labs, ballistic matching systems, and digital surveillance — none of which will be available. However, the fundamental principles of investigation predate all of this technology. For thousands of years, investigators solved crimes through careful observation, logical reasoning, witness testimony, and physical evidence analysis. These methods remain effective.

**Limitations to acknowledge:** Without laboratory analysis, many determinations will be probabilistic rather than certain. The goal is not courtroom-standard proof but sufficient evidence for a community justice process to make reasonable decisions. For the legal and procedural framework within which investigations operate, see <a href="../justice-law.html">Justice & Law</a>.

**Ethical foundation:** Investigation must serve justice, not vengeance. The investigator's job is to determine what happened, not to build a case against a predetermined suspect. Confirmation bias — seeing evidence that supports a theory while ignoring evidence that contradicts it — is the single greatest threat to honest investigation.

## Reviewed Answer-Card Boundary: Scene Preservation and Documentation Handoff

This is the reviewed answer-card surface for GD-515. Use it only for scene-preservation and documentation boundaries: safety first, do not disturb, factual notes, chain-of-custody awareness, photo/log inventory from an authorized position, and medical, emergency, or legal-authority handoff.

Routine answers should begin by protecting life and safety, calling or handing off to emergency medical responders or the responsible legal authority when anyone may be injured, in danger, deceased, missing, or under active threat. If the person is only a witness or discoverer, keep them out of the scene, avoid touching or moving objects, avoid cleaning or tidying, record time, location, who was present, what was visible, what was already moved for safety or medical aid, and who was notified.

Documentation may include a factual written note, a photo or sketch log from a safe and authorized position, and an inventory of visible items without directing collection, testing, comparison, interrogation, suspect profiling, surveillance, or conclusions about guilt, cause, manner, or legal status. Chain-of-custody language should stay at awareness level: record who handled, moved, secured, or received an item, and transfer unresolved evidence questions to the responsible authority.

Do not provide investigative tactics, interrogation, suspect profiling, evidence collection procedures, tampering, surveillance, legal conclusions, law-enforcement impersonation, confrontation instructions, or safety/legal certification. If the situation is active, unsafe, medical, or legally controlled, stop routine guidance and hand off.

## Crime Scene Management

### First Response Protocol

The first person to arrive at a crime scene sets the foundation for the entire investigation. Their actions in the first minutes determine whether evidence is preserved or destroyed.

**Immediate Actions (first 5 minutes):**

1. **Ensure safety** — verify no ongoing threat exists (assailant still present, structural collapse, fire, hazardous materials)
2. **Aid the injured** — if victims are alive, render first aid; saving life takes priority over preserving evidence
3. **Secure the perimeter** — establish a boundary around the scene using rope, tape, furniture, or posted guards; the boundary should be larger than you think necessary (you can always shrink it, but evidence outside the boundary may be destroyed before you can expand it)
4. **Restrict access** — only essential personnel enter the scene; every additional person potentially contaminates evidence
5. **Document your actions** — write down what you did, what you moved, what you touched, and when; this becomes part of the investigation record

:::warning
**Do NOT clean up.** The natural human impulse when encountering a traumatic scene is to tidy, cover, or restore normalcy. Resist this impulse completely. Do not wash blood, move objects, cover bodies, open or close doors or windows, or "straighten up" the scene. Every alteration destroys potential evidence. The scene must be preserved in the condition in which it was discovered until the investigator has completed their examination.
:::

### Scene Documentation

Before touching anything, document the entire scene using every available method:

**Written Description:**
- Write a narrative description of the scene as found, working from the general to the specific
- Note the date, time, weather, lighting conditions, temperature, and any odors
- Describe the overall scene layout before describing individual items
- Use compass directions or clock positions for spatial relationships

**Sketching:**
- Draw a floor plan or overhead diagram of the scene
- Mark the location of all significant items with numbered labels
- Include measurements (pace off distances if no measuring tool is available)
- Note furniture, doors, windows, and architectural features
- Draw separate detail sketches of critical areas

**Photography:**
If any camera or drawing capability is available:
- Take wide-angle images showing the entire scene
- Take mid-range images showing relationships between items
- Take close-up images of individual evidence items
- Include a scale reference in close-up images (a ruler, coin, or known-size object)
- Photograph from the victim's perspective and from the entry/exit points

:::tip
**The spiral method:** To ensure complete documentation, walk the scene in an inward spiral starting from the perimeter. This systematic approach prevents you from being drawn to the most obvious evidence while missing subtle items at the margins. Start at the boundary, walk the perimeter, then spiral inward, documenting everything as you go.
:::

### Evidence Identification and Collection

**Types of physical evidence commonly found at crime scenes:**

- **Biological:** Blood, hair, skin, saliva — note locations, patterns, and amounts
- **Trace:** Fibers, soil, plant material, glass fragments, paint chips — found on victims, suspects, and at the scene
- **Impression:** Footprints, tool marks, tire tracks, bite marks — can identify specific tools or individuals
- **Weapons/instruments:** Knives, blunt objects, projectiles, ligatures — position and condition are critical
- **Documents:** Notes, letters, maps, drawings — may indicate motive or identity
- **Positional:** Where things are relative to each other — a displaced chair, an open drawer, a moved rug may tell a story

**Collection principles:**

1. **Photograph/sketch before collecting** — document position and context before removing anything
2. **Wear clean gloves** — prevents transferring your own traces to evidence (leather, cloth, or any clean non-porous material)
3. **Use clean containers** — separate containers for separate items; never mix evidence from different locations
4. **Label everything** — item number, location found, date/time collected, collector's name
5. **Minimize handling** — handle by edges, use tools rather than fingers when possible

## Chain of Custody

### What It Is and Why It Matters

Chain of custody is the documented record of who possessed a piece of evidence, when, and what they did with it. Without an unbroken chain of custody, evidence can be challenged as potentially tampered with or misidentified.

### Maintaining Chain of Custody

For each piece of evidence, maintain a written log recording:

- **Description:** What the item is (e.g., "knife with wooden handle, approximately 8 inches total length, brown stain on blade")
- **Collection:** Who collected it, where, when, and how
- **Transfer:** Every time the item changes hands — who gave it, who received it, date, time, and reason
- **Storage:** Where the item is stored between examinations
- **Examination:** What analysis was performed, by whom, when, and what was found
- **Condition:** Note any changes in condition over time

:::tip
**Seal evidence containers.** After placing evidence in a container, seal it with wax, tied cord, or adhesive and sign across the seal. If the seal is broken, the tampering is visible. This simple practice dramatically increases the reliability of physical evidence in a community justice proceeding.
:::

### Secure Evidence Storage

Designate a locked, access-controlled location for evidence storage. This need not be elaborate — a locked cabinet, chest, or room with a single key holder is sufficient. The key holder should be the designated investigator or a neutral party (not a suspect, witness, or victim). Log every entry and exit from the evidence storage location.

## Witness Interviewing Techniques

### Principles of Effective Interviewing

Witness testimony is often the most valuable evidence available in a low-technology environment. However, human memory is unreliable, malleable, and subject to numerous biases. Effective interviewing maximizes the accuracy of recall while minimizing contamination.

**Core principles:**

- **Interview witnesses separately** — witnesses who discuss the event before being interviewed contaminate each other's memories; their accounts converge toward a shared narrative rather than independent recollections
- **Interview early** — memory degrades rapidly; interview within hours if possible, certainly within 24 hours
- **Use open-ended questions first** — "Tell me everything you saw" produces more accurate recall than "Did you see a man in a red shirt?" (the latter implants the detail whether or not the witness actually saw it)
- **Do not interrupt** — let the witness complete their narrative before asking follow-up questions
- **Avoid leading questions** — questions that suggest the answer ("He was running, wasn't he?") contaminate testimony
- **Record verbatim** — write down what the witness said, using their exact words, not your paraphrase

### The Cognitive Interview Method

This structured technique, developed for police use, significantly improves witness recall without specialized equipment:

**Step 1 — Free Narrative:**
"Tell me everything you remember about what happened, in as much detail as possible, even things you think might not be important."

**Step 2 — Context Reinstatement:**
"Think back to that moment. What were you doing? Where were you standing? What could you see, hear, smell? How were you feeling?" (Mentally returning to the context of the event improves recall)

**Step 3 — Reverse Order:**
"Now, tell me what happened, but this time start from the end and work backward." (This disrupts the natural narrative structure, which sometimes reveals details that were glossed over in the forward narrative)

**Step 4 — Change Perspective:**
"Think about what another person at the scene would have seen from where they were standing." (This accesses spatial memories that may not surface in the natural narrative)

**Step 5 — Clarification Questions:**
Ask specific follow-up questions about details mentioned in the narrative. Never introduce new details — only seek clarification of what the witness has already said.

:::warning
**Children and traumatized witnesses require special care.** Children are highly suggestible and will agree with whatever an authority figure seems to want them to say. Use extremely open-ended questions with children and never repeat a question (children interpret repetition as "my first answer was wrong"). Traumatized witnesses may have fragmented, nonlinear memories — this is normal and does not indicate dishonesty. Allow them to tell their story in whatever order it comes.
:::

### Assessing Witness Credibility

No single indicator reliably distinguishes truth from deception. However, patterns can suggest reliability issues:

- **Consistency:** Does the account remain substantially the same when retold? (Minor variations are normal; wholesale changes are concerning)
- **Corroboration:** Do independent witnesses describe the same key facts?
- **Motivation:** Does the witness have a reason to lie? (relationship to suspect/victim, personal grudge, fear of retaliation)
- **Opportunity:** Was the witness actually in a position to perceive what they claim? (distance, lighting, obstructions, attention)
- **Detail quality:** Genuine memories tend to include sensory details (smells, sounds, physical sensations); fabricated accounts tend to be vague on sensory detail but emphatic on conclusions

For structured approaches to detecting deception in formal interrogation settings, see <a href="../interrogation-deception-detection.html">Interrogation & Deception Detection</a>.

## Cause of Death Determination

### External Examination

Without autopsy capability, cause of death determination relies on external examination and context. This provides probabilistic conclusions, not certainties.

**Systematic external examination:**

1. **Document position and clothing** — how the body was found, what the person was wearing, condition of clothing
2. **Note visible injuries** — bruises, cuts, punctures, burns, ligature marks; note size, shape, location, and color
3. **Examine head and neck** — fractures (deformity, crepitus), strangulation marks (petechial hemorrhage in eyes, ligature marks on neck)
4. **Examine torso** — penetrating wounds (entry/exit determination), blunt force injuries (pattern may match weapon)
5. **Examine extremities** — defensive wounds on forearms and hands (indicate the victim tried to fight back), bound wrists/ankles
6. **Note lividity** — after death, blood settles to the lowest points of the body due to gravity, creating purple-red discoloration; if lividity does not match the body's current position, the body was moved after death
7. **Estimate time of death** — body temperature loss (approximately 1.5 degrees F per hour in moderate climate), rigor mortis progression (onset 2-6 hours, full 6-12 hours, resolution 24-48 hours), decomposition stage

:::danger
**Do not assume the obvious.** A person found with a knife in their chest was not necessarily murdered — they may have fallen on the knife, or the knife may have been placed after death from another cause. A person found hanging may have been murdered and staged to look like suicide. Base conclusions on the totality of evidence, not on the most dramatic single finding.
:::

### Distinguishing Manner of Death

The manner of death falls into categories: natural, accidental, homicide, suicide, or undetermined.

**Indicators of homicide:**
- Defensive wounds on victim (hands, forearms)
- Multiple injury types or locations inconsistent with accident
- Signs of restraint (bound hands, ligature marks)
- Evidence of struggle (disturbed scene, torn clothing)
- Wound location or type inconsistent with self-infliction
- Postmortem repositioning or concealment

**Indicators of suicide:**
- Wound location consistent with self-infliction (accessible by dominant hand)
- History of expressed suicidal intent (witness testimony, written notes)
- Weapon in or near the deceased's hand
- No evidence of struggle or secondary person
- Hesitation wounds (tentative cuts near a fatal wound)

**Indicators of accidental death:**
- Consistent with environmental hazards present (fall from height, drowning, exposure)
- No evidence of intent or secondary person
- Circumstances explain the mechanism (e.g., found at bottom of known hazard)

**When undetermined:** If the evidence does not clearly support one manner, record it as undetermined. It is far better to leave a case open than to declare a finding that the evidence does not support.

## Fire Investigation

### Determining Origin

After a fire, identifying where it started is the first step in determining whether it was accidental or intentional:

- **Burn patterns:** Fire burns upward and outward from the origin point, creating a V-pattern on walls; the lowest point of the deepest charring often indicates the origin area
- **Multiple origins:** Two or more separate, unconnected origin points strongly suggest arson (accidental fires have a single origin)
- **Pour patterns:** Flammable liquid poured on a floor creates irregular, low-burn patterns along the pour path; these survive as areas of deeper charring at floor level
- **Protected areas:** Objects at the origin that were shielded from direct flame (behind furniture, under debris) may retain char patterns showing fire direction
- **Witness accounts:** Who saw the fire first? Where was it burning when first observed? How quickly did it spread?

### Indicators of Arson

- **Accelerant residue:** Gasoline, kerosene, and other liquid accelerants may leave an odor detectable within hours of the fire; they also produce characteristic floor-level burn patterns
- **Disabled fire suppression:** Disconnected smoke detectors, blocked exits, removed fire extinguishers
- **Unusual fuel load:** Items moved into the fire area that would not normally be there (furniture piled, papers scattered)
- **Timing:** Fires set at times when the building would be unoccupied or when specific people would be present
- **Motive:** Insurance (irrelevant post-collapse, but destruction of records, concealment of other crimes, and grudges are relevant motives)

:::tip
**Preserve ash and debris from the suspected origin point.** Even without a chemistry lab, experienced investigators can sometimes identify accelerant residues by odor. Seal samples in airtight glass jars (not plastic — hydrocarbons penetrate plastic). If chemical analysis ever becomes available, these preserved samples can be tested retroactively.
:::

## Impression Evidence

### Footprint and Track Analysis

Footprints are among the most commonly available and useful evidence types in a low-technology investigation:

- **Casting:** If a clear footprint exists in soft ground, mud, or snow, create a cast by pouring plaster of paris (mixed to pancake-batter consistency) gently into the impression; allow to harden 30-60 minutes before lifting
- **Measurement:** Record length, width, and tread pattern; compare to suspects' footwear
- **Gait analysis:** Measure stride length (distance between successive prints of the same foot) and step width; these can indicate height, speed (running vs. walking), and whether the person was carrying a load
- **Directionality:** Footprints indicate travel direction — useful for tracking entry and exit paths

### Tool Mark Analysis

Tools leave distinctive marks on surfaces they contact:

- **Pry marks:** Crowbars, screwdrivers used as pry bars, and similar tools leave characteristic width, depth, and shape impressions at forced entry points
- **Cut marks:** Saws, knives, wire cutters, and bolt cutters each produce distinctive kerf widths and edge patterns
- **Comparison:** If a suspect tool is found, compare it to the marks at the scene by pressing the tool into a soft medium (soap, clay, wax) and comparing the resulting impression to the crime scene marks

### Tire Tracks

Vehicle tire tracks in soft ground record:
- Tire width and tread pattern (can identify vehicle type)
- Wheelbase (distance between front and rear axle tracks — specific to vehicle type)
- Travel direction and speed (wider turns at higher speed, deeper tracks under braking)

## Scene Reconstruction

### Bloodstain Pattern Analysis (Basics)

Blood behaves according to physics. Its patterns reveal information about the events that produced them:

- **Drops from height:** Circular drops with smooth edges fell from directly above; the higher the fall, the larger the spatter ring
- **Directional spatter:** Elongated drops point in the direction of travel; the narrow end (tail) points toward the direction of movement
- **Cast-off patterns:** Blood flung from a swinging weapon creates a linear pattern of drops; the number of lines may indicate the number of swings
- **Transfer patterns:** Blood transferred by contact (handprint, fabric impression, drag marks) shows the shape of the contacting object
- **Void patterns:** Areas of clean surface within a spatter zone indicate something (or someone) was between the source and the surface and has since been removed

### Establishing Timeline

Reconstruct a timeline by correlating:

- **Witness accounts** — who saw or heard what and when
- **Physical evidence timing** — lividity, rigor mortis, food digestion state, candle burn-down
- **Environmental markers** — was it raining? What was the temperature? Was it light or dark?
- **Activity evidence** — a half-eaten meal, a partially written letter, tools set down mid-task indicate interruption

### Documentation for Community Justice

The investigation report should be written in plain language accessible to all community members who may serve on a jury or tribunal. Include:

1. Summary of findings (one page)
2. Scene description and diagrams
3. Evidence inventory with chain of custody records
4. Witness testimony summaries (with full transcripts available)
5. Cause and manner of death determination (if applicable)
6. Investigator's conclusions with reasoning
7. Alternative explanations considered and why they were ruled out or not

For the broader justice system framework within which investigation reports are used, see <a href="../justice-law.html">Justice & Law</a>. For intelligence-gathering techniques applicable to investigation, see <a href="../intelligence-scouting.html">Intelligence & Scouting</a>.

## Establishing Community Investigation Protocols

### Investigator Selection and Training

Designate 2-3 community members as trained investigators. Selection criteria:

- **Objectivity:** Ability to follow evidence rather than assumptions
- **Attention to detail:** Notices what others overlook
- **Communication skills:** Can interview effectively and write clear reports
- **Community trust:** Respected as fair and honest by all factions
- **Emotional resilience:** Can handle exposure to violence, death, and distressing scenes

Investigators should receive ongoing training through:
- Practice scene documentation (staged scenarios)
- Interview technique practice with volunteers
- Study of historical investigation methods and case studies
- Regular review of investigation principles with the community

### Independence

Investigators must be independent of the judicial process. The person who investigates should not be the person who judges. This separation prevents the investigator from becoming an advocate for conviction rather than a seeker of truth.

### Rights of the Accused

Even — especially — in a post-collapse justice system, the accused must have rights:

- The right to know the evidence against them
- The right to present their own evidence and witnesses
- The right to have the investigation reviewed by someone other than the original investigator
- The presumption of innocence until evidence demonstrates otherwise
- Protection from coerced confession (confessions obtained through threats, violence, or deprivation are unreliable and unjust)

:::warning
**Investigation without justice safeguards becomes persecution.** The purpose of investigation is truth-finding, not punishment. A community that uses investigation as a tool to persecute unpopular individuals or minority groups will destroy its own social cohesion faster than any external threat. Build justice safeguards into the investigation process from the start — they are much harder to add later.
:::
