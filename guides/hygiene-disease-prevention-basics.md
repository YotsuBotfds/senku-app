## Overview: Germ Theory & Why Hygiene Matters

The single most effective thing you can do to prevent disease at home is wash your hands with soap and water before eating, before preparing food, and after using the latrine. The rest of this guide explains why that works and how to apply the same principle to water, food, waste, laundry, and bedding.

Hygiene practices prevent disease transmission. The invisible world of microorganisms—bacteria, viruses, fungi, parasites—causes most infectious diseases. You cannot see them, but you can prevent their transmission.

**Germ Theory (simplified):**
- Microorganisms cause disease
- Microorganisms are transmitted through specific routes (food, water, contact, respiratory droplets, vectors)
- Breaking the transmission route prevents disease

**The stakes are high:** In survival settings without antibiotics, antibacterial soap, or advanced sanitation:
- A simple wound can become infected and cause blood poisoning (sepsis) and death
- Contaminated water causes dysentery (severe diarrhea) leading to fatal dehydration
- Poor hygiene in groups leads to epidemics (cholera, typhoid, plague)
- Lice and parasites cause misery and infection

Hygiene is not optional—it is survival.

## Hand Hygiene: The Foundation

Hands are the most common vehicle for transmitting germs. You touch contaminated surfaces, then touch your face, food, or another person. Effective hand washing breaks this chain.

### When to Wash Hands

<div class="hygiene-step">

**Critical times:**
- After using the toilet or latrine
- After handling human waste, blood, or body fluids
- Before preparing or eating food
- Before treating wounds or assisting sick people
- After coughing, sneezing, or wiping nose
- After touching animals or animal products
- After handling contaminated materials

**In survival settings:** Aim for hand washing at these critical moments. If water is scarce, prioritize the moments with highest disease transmission risk: before eating and after latrine use.

</div>

### Proper Hand Washing Technique

**With soap and water (most effective):**

1. **Wet hands** with available water (warm water is better but cold works)
2. **Apply soap** (soap made from rendered animal fat and lye ash works; see Making Soap section)
3. **Scrub thoroughly:**
   - Palms (rub together)
   - Backs of hands (rub hand over back of other hand)
   - Between fingers (interlock fingers and scrub)
   - Under nails (use fingernail of one hand to scrub under nails of other)
   - Wrists (rub up to wrists)
4. **Duration:** At least 20 seconds of scrubbing (sing "Happy Birthday" twice as a timer)
5. **Rinse thoroughly** with clean running water
6. **Dry** with clean cloth (or air dry if no cloth available)

:::danger
**Do NOT reuse water for hand washing if it's contaminated.** If water source is questionable (pond, stream), prioritize hand washing with it after latrine use (when hand contamination risk is highest). Save cleaner water for cooking and drinking.
:::

### Waterless Cleaning (When Water Is Scarce)

If water is limited:
- **Ash rub:** Rubbing hands with fine ash (from cooking fire) can remove gross contamination and some microorganisms. Not as effective as soap and water but better than nothing.
- **Sand scrub:** Coarse sand can physically remove dirt and some germs. Rub hands with damp sand, rinse with minimal water.
- **Fire exposure:** Holding hands briefly near (not in) flames can kill some surface microorganisms. Careful not to burn yourself.

**Best practice:** Conserve water for critical hand washing (before eating, after latrine). Use ash or sand for less critical cleaning.

### If You Have No Soap and No Water

- Avoid touching your face, mouth, nose, and eyes. Most hand-to-face transfers happen without thinking.
- Use a clean cloth, leaf, or piece of paper as a barrier when handling food or touching shared surfaces.
- Rub hands with wood ash from a cold fire. Ash is alkaline and removes some germs through friction and chemical action. Shake off the ash before handling food.
- Rub hands with clean dry sand or dirt, then brush them off. Friction removes visible soil and some organisms.
- Keep nails short. Dirt and germs concentrate under nails regardless of other hygiene.
- Designate one person to handle food if possible. Fewer hands means fewer transmission paths.

## Making Soap from Ash & Animal Fat

In survival settings, you can make effective soap from lye (extracted from wood ash) and rendered animal fat. This takes time but produces usable cleaning product.

### Simple Lye Water Method

**Equipment needed:**
- Wood ash (from hardwood fire, not softwood)
- Water
- Container (wooden barrel, hollowed log, or ceramic vessel)
- Cloth for filtering

**Steps:**

1. **Collect ash:** Save ash from cooking fire. Hardwood (oak, maple, ash tree) produces better lye than softwood (pine).

2. **Layer ash and water:** In container, alternate layers of ash and water. Top layer should be water.

3. **Wait:** Let sit for 24–48 hours. The water extracts alkali from ash, becoming lye water.

4. **Test lye strength:** Dip an egg (or potato) in the lye water. If it floats, lye is strong enough. If it sinks, you need stronger lye (more ash).

5. **Filter:** Carefully pour off clear liquid (lye water) through cloth into another container.

### Making the Soap

**Equipment needed:**
- Rendered animal fat (saved from cooking; straining removes meat bits)
- Lye water
- Stick for stirring
- Temperature control (ideally warm location)
- Container for soap

**Steps:**

1. **Prepare fat:** Render animal fat by heating it slowly until liquid, then strain through cloth.

2. **Mix lye and fat:** Slowly add lye water to melted fat while stirring constantly. The mixture will heat from the chemical reaction (exothermic).

:::danger
**Safety:** Lye is caustic and burns skin and eyes. Avoid splashing. If lye touches skin, immediately rinse with copious water. Wear protection (cloth wrapping hands, keep eyes away from steam).
:::

3. **Stir:** Continue stirring for 30–60 minutes. The mixture will thicken and change color (typically brown or tan).

4. **Watch for "trace":** When the mixture falls from the spoon in a pattern that holds briefly on the surface, soap has formed ("trace point").

5. **Pour into molds:** Pour into container lined with cloth and let cool/set for 24–48 hours.

6. **Cut into bars:** Once set, cut into usable bars. Let age for 2–4 weeks before use (this allows soap to firm up and excess lye to neutralize).

<section id="personal-hygiene">

## Personal Hygiene Without Running Water

When water is scarce, a damp-cloth sponge bath focusing on hands, face, armpits, and genitals each day keeps the worst infection risks under control. Full-body bathing can wait; hand washing cannot.

In austere settings, hygiene practices must be modified for water scarcity.

### Bathing & Whole-Body Washing

- **Sponge bath:** Use minimal water. Wet cloth, wring out, wash one body section at a time, rinse cloth, repeat. Prioritize hands, face, genitals, armpits.
- **Stream bathing:** If near water source, use stream. Wash downstream from water collection point to avoid contaminating drinking water.
- **Frequency:** When water is scarce, prioritize hand hygiene and genital area daily; full body bathing less frequently.

### Clothing Hygiene

Dirty clothing harbors germs and parasites. When possible:
- Change clothes regularly
- Wash clothing in clean water with soap
- Dry thoroughly in sun (UV light kills many pathogens)
- Store clothing where insects cannot access

### Genital & Menstrual Hygiene

- **Daily washing:** Women and men should wash genitals daily with water and minimal soap
- **Menstruation:** Change menstrual pads/cloths frequently; bury or burn used items; wash reusable cloths thoroughly with soap and dry in sun
- **Urinary tract infections (UTI):** Common in women; prevent by wiping front-to-back after urination, keeping area clean, staying hydrated

:::tip
**In survival settings:** UTIs become serious without antibiotics. Prevention is critical. Prioritize genital hygiene and hydration.
:::

### Hair Hygiene

- **Regular washing:** Weekly or biweekly with soap and water
- **Lice prevention:** Lice spread through contact and fomites (hair contact, shared bedding). If lice suspected, wash hair with soap and hot water, inspect body for lice/nits (eggs), treat contacts, wash all bedding
- **Lice approved treatments:** Permethrin (1-5% cream rinse, apply for 10 minutes then rinse) or pyrethrin-based products (natural extract, safe for children; apply, leave 10 minutes, rinse). Both kill adult lice and nymphs. Manual nit combing with fine-toothed comb after treatment removes remaining nits—critical for preventing reinfestation. Retreat after 7-10 days if live lice persist.

:::danger
**Do not use kerosene for lice treatment.** Kerosene is flammable, toxic if absorbed through skin, and can cause chemical burns. It provides no advantage over safe, effective pediculicides.
:::

### Dental Hygiene

- **Tooth brushing:** Daily with twig, cloth, or improvised brush and water/salt paste
- **Tooth infection prevention:** Infected teeth cause serious abscess and sepsis. Extract severely decayed teeth if trained; otherwise prioritize pain management and wait for professional care
- **Salt water rinse:** After meals, rinse mouth with salt water to reduce bacteria

## Disease Transmission Routes: Breaking the Chain of Infection

Infectious diseases spread through specific routes. Understanding these routes helps you prevent transmission.

### Fecal-Oral Route (Most Common in Austere Settings)

<div class="disease-route">

**Route:** Fecal matter → Hands → Food → Mouth → Infection

**Diseases:** Cholera, typhoid, dysentery, hepatitis A, polio, rotavirus, E. coli

**Prevention:**
- Proper latrine placement (far from water sources, downstream if near water)
- Hand washing after defecation and before eating
- Safe water (boiling, chemical treatment, or use water from protected sources)
- Safe food preparation (clean surfaces, cook thoroughly)
- Prevent contamination of communal areas

</div>

### Respiratory Route

<div class="disease-route">

**Route:** Cough/sneeze droplets → Inhalation by another person

**Diseases:** Influenza, measles, tuberculosis (TB), COVID-19, common cold

**Prevention:**
- Respiratory hygiene: Cover cough/sneeze with elbow (not hand), not with bare mouth
- Isolation of sick people (maintain distance, separate sleeping areas if possible)
- Avoid crowded conditions
- Ventilation (outdoor activities when possible, avoid enclosed spaces with sick people)
- Hand hygiene (people touch their nose, mouth, eyes; dirty hands transfer virus)

</div>

### Contact Route

<div class="disease-route">

**Route:** Touching infected person or contaminated surface → Touching own face/food

**Diseases:** Impetigo, ringworm, scabies, herpes, COVID-19, many others

**Prevention:**
- Avoid direct contact with infected skin lesions
- Wash hands after any contact
- Don't share personal items (towels, bedding, eating utensils)
- Keep wounds covered
- Practice good wound hygiene

</div>

### Vector-Borne Transmission

<div class="disease-route">

**Route:** Mosquito, tick, flea, or louse bite → Pathogen enters bloodstream

**Diseases:** Malaria, dengue, typhus, plague, Lyme disease, West Nile virus

**Prevention:**
- Mosquito nets (especially at night when malaria-carrying mosquitoes are active)
- Insect repellent (if available; improvised herbal options have limited effectiveness)
- Protective clothing (long sleeves, long pants, especially at dawn/dusk)
- Control standing water (where mosquitoes breed)
- Tick removal (grasp close to skin, pull straight out, dispose of safely)
- Flea control (keep living areas clean, control rodent populations)

</div>

### Bloodborne Transmission

<div class="disease-route">

**Route:** Infected blood → Open wound or needle stick

**Diseases:** Hepatitis B & C, HIV, others

**Prevention:**
- Treat all blood as potentially infectious
- Wear gloves when handling blood or body fluids
- Use clean needles; never reuse
- Clean blood spills with bleach solution (if available) or boiling water
- Dispose of sharp objects safely (in puncture-resistant container)
- Avoid touching blood with bare hands; wash hands thoroughly after

</div>

## Water Safety: Ensuring Safe Drinking Water

If you are unsure whether water is safe, bring it to a rolling boil for at least one minute (three minutes above 6,500 feet) before drinking — this single step kills the bacteria, viruses, and parasites that cause most waterborne illness.

Contaminated water is the leading cause of infectious disease in austere settings. Water that looks clear may contain deadly microorganisms.

### Water Contamination Sources

- Human and animal waste near water sources
- Decomposing animals
- Industrial contamination
- Surface runoff
- Pathogens: bacteria (cholera, typhoid, E. coli), viruses (hepatitis A), parasites (giardia, cryptosporidium)

### Water Treatment Methods (In Order of Effectiveness)

**1. Boiling (Most effective):**
- Boil water for at least 1 minute (3 minutes at high altitude >6,500 feet)
- Kills bacteria, viruses, parasites
- Doesn't remove chemical contaminants
- Requires fuel and fuel source

**2. Chlorine Treatment:**
- Household bleach (5% sodium hypochlorite): Add 2 drops per quart, wait 30 minutes before drinking
- Chlorine tablets (if available)
- Doesn't kill cryptosporidium effectively
- Doesn't remove sediment

**3. Filtration + Boiling (Combined approach):**
- Filter through cloth/sand to remove sediment (makes boiling faster, removes some parasites)
- Then boil to kill remaining pathogens

**4. Solar Disinfection (SODIS):**
- Fill clear bottle with filtered water
- Place in direct sunlight for 6+ hours (or 2 days if cloudy)
- UV light kills microorganisms
- Requires clear bottles and sunny weather

### Safe Water Practices in Austere Settings

- Designate clean water collection points (far from latrines)
- Use clean containers for collecting, storing, and transporting water
- Don't touch treated water with dirty hands
- Cover water storage containers to prevent contamination and mosquito breeding
- In endemic areas (malaria, dengue), water for bathing/washing doesn't need to be boiled; drinking water must be

## Waste Disposal & Sanitation

Proper disposal of human and animal waste prevents fecal-oral disease transmission.

### Human Waste (Fecal Disposal)

**Latrine Requirements:**
- Location: At least 30 meters downhill from water sources; lower in water table
- Design: Pit at least 1–1.5 meters deep; cover to prevent smell and fly access
- Use: Designate use times if possible; separate if gender-separated facilities available
- Maintenance: Add soil/ash to cover feces daily; when three-quarters full, abandon and dig new pit
- Closure: Fill pit completely when used area exhausted; bury all waste

**No-latrine situations (emergency):**
- If trenching impossible, use sealed bags (if available) and transport for burial/burning
- Bury human waste at least 30 meters from water; at least 30 cm deep
- In dense populations, organize communal latrines to prevent ground contamination

### Animal Waste

- Don't keep animals near living areas; downwind and downstream from water sources
- Regularly remove manure; compost if possible (high temperature kills pathogens)
- Use manure as fertilizer only on non-edible crops or after proper composting

### Garbage & Waste Disposal

- Separate food waste (compost or bury, away from water)
- Burn paper and cardboard if fire safety allows
- Bury non-combustibles (glass, metal) away from water sources
- Don't leave garbage exposed (attracts animals, insects, allows disease vectors to breed)

### Greywater (Washing Water)

- Use in gardens if not containing soap (or use biodegradable soap)
- Don't combine with drinking water source
- Change greywater regularly to prevent mosquito breeding

## Food Handling & Safety

Foodborne illness causes severe diarrhea, vomiting, and dehydration. Prevention focuses on keeping food clean and cooked properly.

### Food Contamination Risks

- Hands of food handler (most common)
- Contaminated water used in cooking/washing
- Contaminated cutting surfaces, utensils
- Improper storage (spoilage, insect/rodent contamination)
- Undercooked meat (bacteria inside)
- Cross-contamination (raw meat → ready-to-eat food)

### Safe Food Handling

- **Wash hands** before preparing food
- **Use clean utensils and surfaces** for food preparation
- **Cook meat thoroughly** (no pink inside; juices run clear)
- **Keep raw and cooked foods separate** (use different cutting boards if possible)
- **Store food properly** (covered, elevated off ground, protected from animals)
- **Discard spoiled food** (bulging cans, foul smell, mold, slime on surface)
- **Don't reuse water** from washing contaminated raw meat for other foods

### Contaminated Food Signs

- Foul smell
- Mold
- Unusual color
- Insects or rodent signs
- Bulging cans
- Slimy texture on surface

:::danger
**Do not taste questionable food to test it.** Foodborne pathogens don't always cause obvious taste/smell changes. Consuming contaminated food causes severe illness or death. When in doubt, discard it.
:::

## Breaking the Chain of Infection in Groups

In survival groups, infectious disease spreads rapidly. Coordinated hygiene breaks transmission.

### Isolation of Sick People

- Separate ill person from healthy people if possible
- If not possible, maintain distance (at least 2 meters)
- Ill person uses separate bedding, eating utensils
- Designate one caregiver if possible (reduces exposure)

### Communal Hygiene Practices

- Establish designated defecation areas (latrines) away from living spaces
- Regular cleaning of common areas
- Hand washing stations (water containers, soap)
- Separate drinking and washing water
- Regular food safety training for food handlers

### Surveillance (Watching for Disease Spread)

- Keep note of who becomes sick (helps identify transmission patterns)
- Track symptoms (fever, diarrhea, cough) to identify disease type
- Rapidly isolate new cases
- Report epidemic signs to authorities if possible (unusual number of cases of same illness)

:::warning
**Epidemic warning signs:** Multiple people becoming sick with same illness in short time period indicates possible outbreak. This requires immediate isolation measures, water system checks, and if available, evacuation of high-risk individuals.
:::

## Laundry & Bedding Hygiene

Dirty bedding and clothing harbor pathogens and parasites.

### Laundry Best Practices

- Wash with soap and water (hot water if available is better)
- Rinse thoroughly to remove all soap
- Dry in sun (UV light kills many pathogens)
- Store in clean, dry location away from insects
- Wash sick person's laundry separately from healthy people's laundry
- Change clothes regularly (especially if soiled with sweat, blood, or body fluids)

### Bedding Management

- Change bedding regularly (weekly if possible)
- Wash blankets/sheets in soap and water, dry in sun
- Inspect for parasites (lice, bedbugs, mites)
- Never share bedding between healthy and ill people
- Use netting to prevent insect access (important for malaria/dengue prevention)

<section id="when-to-seek-help">

## When to Seek Professional Medical Care

Certain signs indicate infection or disease requiring professional treatment:

- **Fever lasting more than 48 hours** (may indicate serious infection)
- **Severe diarrhea/vomiting lasting more than 24 hours** (dehydration risk)
- **Cough lasting more than 1 week, especially with blood or difficulty breathing** (possible TB or pneumonia)
- **Skin infection spreading despite hygiene efforts** (may require antibiotics)
- **Wound infection signs:** Increasing redness, warmth, pus, red streaks extending from wound (lymphangitis)
- **High fever (>39°C/102°F) with stiff neck and severe headache** (possible meningitis—emergency)

- **When watchful waiting stops being reasonable:** Any of the above, or a complaint that is clearly getting worse instead of settling down, should move out of home-care territory

Professional care is superior to field management when available.
