**Quick triage - preserve sound food, route spoilage elsewhere:**
- **Already moldy, slimy, leaking, or foul-smelling?** Stop here and use `food-spoilage-assessment` first. Preservation is for sound food, not a fix for bad food.
- **Salvage or preserve?** If the food is still sound but you need it to last longer, choose the method that fits the food: drying for lean foods and most produce, salting/brining for fatty meat, fish, and cabbage, smoking for meat and fish, or canning for sealed long-term storage.
- **No fridge or cellar?** Start with drying, salting/brining, smoking, or fermentation.
- **High-acid foods** (fruits, jams, pickles, tomatoes with added acid): water-bath canning
- **Low-acid foods** (vegetables, meats, poultry, seafood, soups): pressure canning only — water bath cannot kill botulinum spores
- **Meat and fish**: smoking and curing, or pressure canning
- **Salted fish in heat or humidity:** This is a food-preservation and packaging question, not a salt-storage question. Salt slows spoilage only after enough cure plus drying/smoking; heat and humidity can re-wet the fish and restart spoilage.
- **Herbs, fruits, vegetables, mushrooms**: drying and dehydrating → detailed methods and drying-time tables in drying-dehydration-techniques
- **Cabbage, vegetables**: salting, brining, and fermentation
- **Salt + jars + hot humid room?** Treat this as a preservation-plus-packaging boundary, not salt production or tool salvage: choose salting/brining/fermentation here, then use `food-storage-packaging` for sealed, dry, cool storage.
- **Salted fish in a hot humid room?** Keep the answer on food preservation and food-storage packaging: humidity is degrading the cure, so move it cooler/drier, protect it from reabsorbing moisture, and assess any off smell with `food-spoilage-assessment`.
- **Jar, crock, bucket, or bag question while preservation is unfinished?** Container choice does not finish drying, curing, acidification, smoking, or canning. Finish the preservation step first; use containers only to protect food that is already shelf-stable or still safely fermenting under brine.

:::tip
**Emergency method selection — act before spoilage wins:**
- **Vegetables starting to wilt or soften, but still sound:** ferment or brine tonight (fastest, no heat needed) → see fermentation-pickling. If too far gone, assess safety first → see food-spoilage-assessment.
- **Meat with no refrigeration available, but still fresh:** salt-cure or smoke within hours. Thin strips → dry into jerky (160°F until brittle). Thick cuts → heavy dry-salt layer (cover completely) and keep below 60°F. Pressure canning works but takes hours of sustained fuel.

- **Smell changed after drying or after moving containers?** Use food-spoilage-assessment first. Packaging can explain moisture exposure, but an off smell is a safety assessment before it is a storage optimization.

(2) Is it still sound and just needs longer storage? → salt-cure, smoke, dry, ferment, or pressure can it. (3) Do you have fuel and a pressure canner? → pressure can (longest shelf life). (4) Do you have sun or dry heat? → dry/dehydrate (lightweight, no containers needed).

## Water Bath Canning

Water bath canning is the safest method for preserving high-acid foods (pH below 4.6). The boiling water temperature of 212°F (100°C) at sea level is sufficient to kill mold, yeast, and bacteria, but not spores of Clostridium botulinum. This method is ideal for fruits, pickles, tomatoes with added acid, and jams.

![Water bath canning process with jar positioning and heat application](../assets/svgs/food-preservation-1.svg)

### Equipment Needed

#### Essential Equipment:

-   **Large stainless steel pot** \- minimum 21 quarts (20 liters), with rack that keeps jars off bottom
-   **Glass canning jars** \- quart, pint, or half-pint sizes (use only approved canning jars, never recycled food jars)
-   **Metal lids** \- flat metal discs with rubber compound that seal when heated
-   **Screw bands** \- metal rings that hold lids in place during processing
-   **Jar lifter** \- specialized tool for safely removing hot jars from boiling water
-   **Bubble remover/headspace tool** \- plastic stick to remove air bubbles and measure headspace
-   **Wide-mouth funnel** \- for easy filling without dripping
-   **Clean towels** \- for handling hot jars
-   **Reliable thermometer** \- to monitor water temperature (optional but recommended)

### Jar Preparation

:::highlight-box
**Wash jars, lids, and bands** in hot soapy water. For jams and jellies, sterilize jars by boiling for 10 minutes (add 1 minute per 1,000 feet of altitude above sea level). For foods processed more than 10 minutes, hot jar washing is sufficient. Do not sterilize if processing time exceeds 10 minutes.

**Keep jars hot** \- Place filled jars directly into hot water bath to prevent thermal shock and breakage.
:::

### Headspace Requirements

:::tip
**Critical Importance:** Proper headspace (distance between food and jar rim) prevents overflow during processing and ensures proper sealing.

-   **Jams, jellies, pickles, sauces:** 1/4 inch headspace
-   **Most fruits and vegetables:** 1/2 inch headspace
-   **Whole fruits or vegetables:** 1/2 inch headspace

Measure with the bubble remover/headspace tool. If headspace is too small, food may overflow into sealing surface. If too large, vacuum seal may not form properly.
:::

### Processing Steps

1.  Fill pot with water and bring to rolling boil. Water level must be 1-2 inches above jar lids.
2.  Remove jars from hot water one at a time. Fill with prepared food to proper headspace.
3.  Remove air bubbles by running a non-metallic spatula around inside of jar.
4.  Wipe jar rim clean with damp cloth to remove food particles.
5.  Place hot lid on jar and screw band down until finger-tight (not fully tight).
6.  Using jar lifter, place filled jars into boiling water bath, ensuring they don't touch.
7.  Ensure water covers jars by 1-2 inches. Add boiling water if needed.
8.  Cover pot and return to rolling boil. Start processing time when water returns to boil.
9.  After processing time ends, remove jars with jar lifter and place on towel or rack to cool.
10.  Do not tighten bands or move jars for 12-24 hours. Sealing occurs as contents cool.

At higher altitudes, water boils at lower temperatures, so processing times must be increased:

### Testing Jar Seals

:::highlight-box
**After 12-24 hours of cooling:** Test the seal by pressing down on the center of each lid. A properly sealed lid will be concave (curved downward) and will not flex or pop. If the lid flexes up and down, the jar did not seal properly.

**For non-sealed jars:** Refrigerate immediately and use within 3-4 weeks, OR reprocess within 24 hours using a new lid (reheat food to proper temperature first), OR save for immediate consumption.
:::

### Storage and Shelf Life

Store sealed jars in a cool (50-70°F), dark location away from direct sunlight. Properly sealed and stored water bath canned foods maintain quality for 1-2 years, though some nutrient loss and flavor change occurs after 1 year. Label all jars with contents and date.

## Pressure Canning

Pressure canning is the ONLY safe method for low-acid foods (pH 4.6 and above), including vegetables, meats, poultry, seafood, and soups. This critical distinction exists because pressure canning is the only preservation method capable of killing the deadly spores of Clostridium botulinum, a bacterium that thrives in low-oxygen environments and produces one of the most potent toxins known. Water bath canning, by contrast, reaches only 212°F (100°C), which is insufficient to destroy botulinum spores. Understanding why pressure is required and how to use it correctly is essential for safe home food preservation.

### Why Pressure Canning is Critical: Understanding Botulism Risk

Botulism is a potentially fatal food-borne illness caused by botulinum toxin produced by Clostridium botulinum bacteria. This organism is uniquely dangerous because:

- **Spore resistance:** Botulinum spores are heat-resistant and can survive temperatures up to 212°F (100°C). They only die at temperatures of 240°F (116°C) or higher—temperatures that can only be achieved in a pressure canner.
- **Anaerobic survival:** Botulinum bacteria thrive in oxygen-free (anaerobic) environments, making sealed cans ideal breeding grounds. Unlike mold or yeast that require oxygen, this pathogen prefers the absence of it.
- **Low-acid preference:** Botulinum spores germinate preferentially in low-acid foods (pH above 4.6). This includes nearly all vegetables, all meats and fish, and most non-pickled foods.
- **Toxin potency:** Botulinum toxin is one of the deadliest substances known—a tiny amount (0.000001 mg per kilogram of body weight) can be fatal. Toxin production is often invisible: no odor, gas production, or obvious spoilage signs accompany toxin formation.

Failure to use proper pressure canning for low-acid foods directly creates botulism risk. Recipes using water bath, oven, or aspiration methods for low-acid foods have caused paralysis and death.

:::danger
**CRITICAL SAFETY RULE:** Never adapt or "improve" canning recipes. Never use untested methods such as oven canning, sun canning, aspiration-type canning, or processing low-acid foods in a water bath. These methods cannot achieve the temperatures required to kill botulinum spores. A single improperly processed jar can result in botulism poisoning.
:::

### Equipment: Pressure Canners vs Pressure Cookers

This is the most common and dangerous mistake in home canning. A pressure cooker is NOT a pressure canner, and they cannot be used interchangeably.

#### Pressure Canner Requirements:

A pressure canner must have all of the following features:

- **Vent pipe:** Allows air and steam to escape during the venting phase. This is critical—failure to vent for 10 minutes before pressurizing means air remains in the canner, which reduces the actual temperature achieved at a given pressure.
- **Adjustable or weighted pressure gauge:** Allows you to monitor and control the pressure inside the canner.
- **Safety lock (lid lock):** Prevents the lid from opening until pressure has returned to zero.
- **Safety fuse (in some models):** A small metal disc that melts if pressure exceeds safe limits, allowing steam to escape and preventing explosions.
- **Capacity of at least 10 quarts:** Smaller vessels don't allow adequate space between jars for steam circulation.
- **Rack or trivet:** A metal framework that keeps jars off the bottom of the canner, allowing water to circulate beneath them.
- **Gasket or sealing ring:** Ensures an airtight seal. Gaskets should be checked for cracks and replaced every few years of use.

#### Pressure Cooker (NOT suitable for canning):

A pressure cooker is a specialized cooking vessel designed to cook food quickly under pressure. It differs from a canner in critical ways:

- Smaller capacity (typically 4-8 quarts maximum)
- Less sophisticated pressure regulation
- Designed for cooking food rapidly at high pressure, not for sustained low-pressure canning operations
- Lid designs that don't always have proper venting mechanisms
- Limited number of jars that can fit simultaneously

Using a pressure cooker for canning can result in inconsistent pressure, inadequate sterilization, and botulism risk.

#### Gauge Types and Maintenance

Pressure canners use one of two gauge types:

**Dial Gauge (0-15 PSI):**
- Provides continuous, variable pressure readings
- Allows precise altitude adjustment
- More accurate for high-altitude locations (above 2,000 feet)
- Requires annual testing at your county Extension office to verify accuracy
- Dial gauges drift over time and can lose calibration; annual testing is non-negotiable
- Higher initial cost but lower operational cost

**Weighted Gauge (10 or 15 PSI):**
- Simple, reliable design with no moving parts
- Operates at fixed pressures only (typically 10 or 15 PSI)
- Cannot be calibrated or tested; either it works or it doesn't
- Requires no maintenance
- Poor choice at high altitudes (above 4,000 feet) because you cannot adjust pressure upward incrementally
- Lower cost, good for sea level locations

:::warning
If using a dial gauge, **you must have it tested annually** at your county Cooperative Extension office or an approved testing center. Many dial gauges are inaccurate; testing ensures your canner is operating at the correct pressure. Document all tests and keep receipts.
:::

### Essential Equipment Checklist

Beyond the canner itself, you will need:

- **Canning jars** (pint and quart sizes) approved for canning—never use recycled food jars or drinking glasses
- **Metal flat lids** designed for canning—rubber compound seals when heated
- **Screw bands (rings)** to hold lids in place during processing
- **Jar lifter** (specialized long tongs)—essential for safely removing hot jars
- **Bubble remover/headspace tool**—plastic rod with measurement marks
- **Large wide-mouth funnel**—prevents spilling and makes filling easier
- **Clean towels** (cloth or paper)—for handling hot jars and cooling them
- **Reliable thermometer** (optional but recommended)—to verify water temperature before venting
- **Heat source** capable of maintaining consistent temperature (stove burner, portable butane burner, or camping stove)

### The Science of Pressure and Temperature

Pressure increases the boiling point of water. At sea level, water boils at 212°F (100°C). As you increase pressure in a sealed environment, the temperature at which water boils rises significantly:

- **0 PSI (sea level):** 212°F (100°C)
- **5 PSI:** 228°F (109°C)
- **10 PSI:** 240°F (116°C) ← standard for low-acid vegetables
- **15 PSI:** 250°F (121°C) ← required for meats and poultry

This relationship is consistent and reliable. Pressure directly translates to temperature, and it is the temperature that kills botulinum spores. Botulinum spores are destroyed reliably only at 240°F or higher—which requires at least 10 PSI at sea level.

### Altitude Adjustments: Critical Safety Information

Atmospheric pressure decreases with altitude. At higher elevations, water boils at lower temperatures, which means you must increase the pressure in your canner to compensate. Pressure canning and water bath canning require different altitude adjustments:

**WATER BATH CANNING (High-Acid Foods Only):**
For water bath canning, add processing time (not pressure adjustment):

| Altitude (feet) | Time Increase |
|---|---|
| Sea level to 1,000 | No increase |
| 1,001 to 2,000 | Add 5 minutes |
| 2,001 to 3,000 | Add 10 minutes |
| 3,001 to 4,000 | Add 15 minutes |
| 4,001 to 5,000 | Add 20 minutes |
| 5,001 to 6,000 | Add 25 minutes |
| 6,001 to 7,000 | Add 30 minutes |
| Above 7,000 | Add 35+ minutes |

**PRESSURE CANNING (Low-Acid Foods):**
For pressure canning, adjust PRESSURE (not time). Increase the PSI by 1 for every 2,000 feet of elevation above sea level:

**Weighted Gauge Canners** have a major limitation at high altitude: they cannot increase pressure above 15 PSI, and many models lock at 10 PSI. At altitudes above 4,000 feet, a weighted gauge cannot provide sufficient pressure for low-acid vegetables. This is a significant reason to prefer a dial gauge at high elevations, provided it is tested annually.

:::danger
**INCORRECT ALTITUDE ADJUSTMENTS FOR PRESSURE CANNING CAUSE BOTULISM:** Using the wrong altitude formula is a common and deadly mistake. For pressure canning, you must adjust PRESSURE (PSI), not processing time. Using water bath time-based adjustments for pressure canning does NOT safely kill botulinum spores. At high altitude without proper pressure adjustment, you may achieve only 220°F instead of the required 240°F minimum. Even a single improperly processed jar can result in botulism poisoning and death. Always verify your altitude and use the correct pressure adjustment table.
:::

Know your altitude before canning. Contact your local county Extension office if you are unsure of your elevation or need clarification on altitude adjustments.
:::

### Step-by-Step Pressure Canning Process

**Preparation Phase:**

1. **Inspect equipment:** Check the canner's sealing ring (gasket) for cracks or hardening. If damaged, replace it. Verify the vent pipe is clear and unobstructed. Test the pressure gauge (dial gauges only) before use or confirm it was tested within the past 12 months.

2. **Prepare workspace:** Set up your work area with jars, lids, bands, food, and equipment within reach. Have a reliable timer available.

3. **Wash and prepare jars:** Wash jars, lids, and bands in hot soapy water. You do not need to sterilize jars since the pressure canning process itself sterilizes them. Keep them warm—place them in a pot of hot water.

4. **Prepare food:** Follow your tested recipe exactly. Prepare the food to be canned (vegetables cleaned and cut, meat trimmed and cubed, broth heated, etc.). Have it ready to pack when jars are filled.

**Canning Phase:**

5. **Fill the canner:** Add 2-3 inches of hot water to the bottom of the canner. This water heats and circulates steam during processing. Bring water to a boil while you prepare filled jars.

6. **Fill jars with food:** Working quickly, fill each hot jar with the prepared food using the wide-mouth funnel. Leave the proper headspace (usually 1.25 inches) as specified in your recipe. Headspace is the space between the top of the food and the rim of the jar—it allows hot air and steam to bubble out and ensures proper sealing.

7. **Remove air bubbles:** Slide a bubble remover (or clean plastic knife) between the jar and food to release trapped air bubbles. Tap the jar on the counter to settle food. Make sure to maintain proper headspace after removing bubbles.

8. **Wipe jar rims:** Use a clean, damp cloth to thoroughly wipe the rim (the sealing surface) of each jar. Any food residue, oil, or seeds will prevent a proper seal.

9. **Apply lids and bands:** Place a hot metal lid on each jar. Screw the metal band down until it is fingertip-tight—do not use force. The band should be snug enough that it doesn't fall off, but you should still be able to turn it slightly with your fingertip. Overly tight bands can prevent steam from escaping and lead to seal failure.

10. **Place jars in canner:** Using the jar lifter, carefully place each filled, lidded jar upright on the rack in the canner. Jars should not touch each other or the sides of the canner—steam must circulate around each jar. Arrange them in a circle or rows as needed, but do not stack them.

11. **Fasten the canner lid:** Secure the canner lid according to the manufacturer's instructions. Leave the vent pipe open (do not close it or attach the weighted gauge yet).

**Venting Phase (CRITICAL):**

12. **Vent for 10 minutes:** This is one of the most important steps. Let steam pour out of the vent pipe for a full 10 minutes. This phase removes air from the canner and ensures the temperature will reach the desired level. If you skip or shorten venting, air pockets remain in the canner, the actual temperature will be lower than the gauge indicates, and you will achieve inadequate sterilization. Use a timer and do not guess at the duration. Watch to ensure steam is continuously escaping; if you see liquid coming out instead, adjust the heat.

**Pressurization Phase:**

13. **Close the vent and apply gauge:** After the 10-minute venting is complete, close the vent pipe or place the weighted gauge (if using) over the opening, following the manufacturer's instructions. Pressure will begin to build.

14. **Monitor pressure rise:** Watch as pressure increases. This may take 3-10 minutes depending on your heat source and canner size. Adjust heat as needed to bring pressure to the target PSI smoothly. Avoid rapid pressure spikes.

15. **Reach target pressure:** Allow pressure to stabilize at your target PSI (10 PSI for low-acid vegetables, 15 PSI for meats; adjust for altitude as needed). A few seconds of fluctuation is normal; aim for consistency.

**Processing Phase:**

16. **Start processing timer:** Once pressure is stable at the target level, start your processing timer. This is the official start of the processing time.

17. **Maintain pressure:** Monitor the pressure gauge (or weighted gauge) throughout processing. Ideally, pressure should remain constant. Small fluctuations (±2 PSI for dial gauges) are acceptable, but the gauge should not drop significantly or spike above target. Adjust heat as needed to maintain steady pressure.

:::danger
If pressure drops more than 2 PSI below the target at any point during processing, you must:
1. Increase pressure back to target
2. When pressure reaches target again, START THE TIMER OVER from zero

For example, if you are processing for 90 minutes at 15 PSI and pressure drops to 12 PSI at minute 45, increase the heat, bring pressure back to 15 PSI, and reset the timer to 0. You will then process for another full 90 minutes from that point.

This procedure ensures adequate sterilization. Underprocessing for even part of the time creates botulism risk.
:::

18. **Complete the processing time:** Continue maintaining the target pressure for the full duration specified in your recipe. Do not shorten the time.

**Cooling Phase:**

19. **Turn off heat:** When the processing timer ends, turn off the heat source. Do not open the vent or remove the canner from heat yet.

20. **Allow natural pressure return:** Let the canner sit undisturbed until the pressure inside returns to zero naturally. This may take 10-30 minutes depending on the canner size and the amount of food inside. The pressure gauge will gradually drop to zero. **Do not rush this phase.** Forcing pressure down (by running cold water over the canner, for example) can cause siphoning, where boiling liquid is drawn up and out of jars, disrupting seals and leaving exposed food.

21. **Cool for a few minutes:** Once pressure has returned to zero (gauge reads 0 PSI), wait 5-10 additional minutes before opening the canner.

22. **Open the canner:** Carefully open the vent pipe or remove the weighted gauge if not automatic. Tilt the canner lid away from you to direct steam away from your face. The escaping steam is extremely hot and can cause burns.

23. **Remove jars:** Using the jar lifter, remove jars one at a time and place them upright on a clean towel or rack to cool. Space jars 1-2 inches apart to allow air circulation. Do not tilt or move jars.

**Post-Processing:**

24. **Cool completely:** Allow jars to cool undisturbed for 12-24 hours at room temperature. This is when sealing happens. During cooling, you may hear the characteristic "ping" sound as lids seal—a satisfying sign that the process worked.

25. **Test seals:** After 24 hours, test each jar by pressing the center of the lid with your finger. A properly sealed lid will be slightly concave (curved inward) and will not move or pop up and down. If a lid is flat or movable, the seal has failed. Remove the band and try to lift the lid with your finger. If it lifts easily, the jar is not sealed. Unsealed jars must be refrigerated and consumed within a few days, or reprocessed immediately.

26. **Remove bands and label:** Once you confirm seals are good, remove the screw bands. Wash and dry the outside of the jars. Label each jar with the contents and the date of canning. Proper labeling prevents confusion later.

27. **Store properly:** Store sealed jars in a cool (50-70°F), dark location such as a basement, pantry, or closet. Do not store in direct sunlight or in warm locations. Avoid freezing. Properly canned low-acid foods will remain safe for 1-5 years, depending on the product. Meats typically last 3-5 years; vegetables 2-3 years.

### Signs of Spoilage and When to Discard Jars

Pressure-canned foods can spoil. Learn to recognize danger signs:

:::danger
**Discard immediately (without tasting) if:**

- Jar lid is unsealed (center of lid is flat or can be lifted)
- Content bubbles or foams when opened
- Content smells sour, fermented, or abnormal
- Liquid level has dropped significantly (siphoning indicates seal failure)
- There is visible mold or cloudiness in the liquid
- Jar was processed in a water bath instead of a pressure canner (botulism risk)
- Jar was processed using an untested recipe or non-standard method
- Jar was stored in a warm location for extended time
- Jar shows visible cracks or chips

Even if only one of these signs is present, discard the entire jar's contents. Do not taste it to check. Botulism toxin may be present without visible, olfactory, or gustatory evidence.
:::

### Common Mistakes That Lead to Botulism and Other Failures

Understanding common errors helps you avoid them:

1. **Skipping or shortening the venting phase:** This is the leading cause of underprocessing. If venting time is less than 10 minutes, air remains in the canner, actual temperature is lower than the gauge indicates, and botulinum spores may survive. Always vent for the full 10 minutes.

2. **Not adjusting for altitude:** If you live above 2,000 feet and fail to increase pressure, the actual temperature achieved is insufficient. Botulinum spores require 240°F minimum. At high altitude without pressure adjustment, you may reach only 220°F, which is dangerously low.

3. **Using untested or online recipes:** Recipes from blogs, Pinterest, or social media are often not tested by food safety experts. They may omit critical steps (like venting), use incorrect times, or instruct you to can low-acid foods using a water bath. Always use recipes from USDA or the National Center for Home Food Preservation (NCHFP).

4. **Using a pressure cooker instead of a pressure canner:** A pressure cooker is too small, has inferior pressure regulation, and is not designed for canning. Jars may fit, but sealing and sterilization will be inadequate.

5. **Using recycled food jars:** Old commercial jars (from spaghetti sauce, baby food, etc.) are thinner glass and may not seal properly. Their threads are often damaged. Always use canning jars approved for home canning.

6. **Overfilling jars (excessive headspace):** If you leave too little headspace, steam cannot escape properly and sealing fails. Too much headspace leaves excess air in the jar, and the lid may not seal. Follow your recipe precisely. Standard headspace for most low-acid foods is 1.25 inches.

7. **Not maintaining pressure during processing:** If pressure fluctuates wildly or drops significantly, actual processing time may be insufficient. A single pressure drop requires resetting the timer. Small fluctuations (±2 PSI) are acceptable if they happen briefly, but sustained underprocessing is dangerous.

8. **Forcing pressure down after processing:** Running cold water over a hot canner, or opening the vent before pressure returns to zero, can cause siphoning and seal failure. Allow natural pressure return. This takes patience but is essential.

9. **Using over-tight or over-loose bands:** Bands that are too tight prevent steam from escaping during processing and can interfere with sealing. Bands that are too loose allow jars to shift or tip. Use fingertip-tight only.

10. **Processing foods longer than recommended:** More time does NOT increase safety. Excessive processing degrades food quality, may result in mushy texture, and can increase the rate of seal failure on old jars.

11. **Storing in warm locations:** Canned goods stored in warm environments (above 70°F) or direct sunlight deteriorate faster. Botulinum toxin is heat-stable, but long-term storage in heat reduces shelf life and may compromise seal integrity.

12. **Not replacing gaskets or testing dial gauges:** A cracked gasket prevents proper sealing. A dial gauge that has drifted out of calibration may give false readings. Maintenance is not optional.

### Storage, Shelf Life, and Quality

Properly pressure-canned low-acid foods maintain safety for 1-5 years when stored correctly:

- **Meats and fish:** 3-5 years (vacuum-packed muscle meat lasts longer than ground meat)
- **Vegetables:** 2-3 years (some retain quality longer, but texture degrades over time)
- **Soups and combination foods:** 1-2 years (quality degrades as different components separate)

Storage conditions matter:

- **Temperature:** 50-70°F is ideal. Temperatures above 75°F shorten shelf life. Do not freeze canned goods (freezing can damage lids and compromise seals).
- **Light:** Store in darkness to preserve color and nutrients. Exposure to light causes discoloration and nutrient loss.
- **Humidity:** Moderate humidity is acceptable. Very dry conditions can cause metal lids to rust. Very humid conditions can rust bands.
- **Location:** A basement, cool pantry, or underground root cellar is ideal.

After opening a jar, refrigerate the contents and consume within 3-4 days. When served, boil low-acid canned meats and vegetables for 10 minutes to destroy any heat-stable botulinum toxin that might have formed (an additional safety measure, though proper processing makes this unnecessary).

### Testing Your Pressure Gauge (Dial Gauge Canners Only)

If you own a dial gauge canner, have it tested annually or at minimum every 2 years. To find a testing location:

1. Contact your local county Cooperative Extension office
2. Ask for the home food preservation advisor or food safety specialist
3. Bring your canner and ask for the gauge to be tested
4. Document the test result (whether it passed or needs adjustment)
5. If the gauge is off by more than 5 PSI, consider replacement

Dial gauge canners are typically less expensive than new canners, so replacement is often feasible if your gauge fails testing.

## Smoking & Curing Meat

Smoking and curing are ancient methods of meat preservation that combine salt preservation, heat treatment, and antimicrobial properties of smoke to create shelf-stable protein sources. The combination is more effective than any single method alone.

### Smokehouse Construction

:::highlight-box
**Basic Smokehouse Design:** A simple smokehouse can be built from a wooden shed, barrel, or covered box. Key requirements:

-   Separate fire chamber below main chamber to generate smoke without excessive heat (for cold smoking)
-   Insulation to maintain stable temperature
-   Racks or hooks for hanging meat
-   Ventilation holes to control smoke flow and prevent condensation buildup
-   Door that can be sealed but allows smoke circulation
-   Thermometer at meat level to monitor temperature
-   Smoke source: hardwood fire in separate chamber connected by pipe

For cold smoking, construct a smoke pipe 10-15 feet long underground or heavily insulated to cool smoke before it reaches meat.

### Wood Selection

:::tip
**Ideal Woods for Smoking:**

-   **Hardwoods (best):** Oak, hickory, apple, cherry, alder, mesquite
-   **Use dry wood** \- green or wet wood produces harsh smoke and creosote buildup
-   **Avoid softwoods:** Pine, fir, spruce produce resinous smoke that taints meat
-   **Flavor profiles:** Hickory = strong, oak = medium, cherry/apple = mild and sweet
:::

### Brining Formulas

:::card
#### Wet Brine (for 1 gallon water):

**Basic formula:** 1 cup salt, 1 cup sugar, 2 tablespoons sodium nitrite (or Prague Powder #1), 2 tablespoons pickling spice, 1 bay leaf, 6-8 peppercorns

Submerge meat in brine, refrigerate 5-7 days for ham, 7-10 days for bacon, 3-5 days for smaller cuts like ribs.
:::

:::card
#### Dry Cure (per pound of meat):

**Basic formula:** 1 tablespoon salt, 1 teaspoon sugar, 1/4 teaspoon sodium nitrite (Prague Powder #1), spices as desired

Rub thoroughly on all surfaces. Layer in container with additional dry cure, cover, refrigerate 5-7 days per inch of thickness, turning occasionally.
:::

### Common Recipes

#### Jerky (Simple Dry Cure)

:::highlight-box
1.  Slice lean meat (beef, venison, elk) 1/4 inch thick, against the grain
2.  Mix: 1/4 cup salt, 2 tablespoons sugar, 1 tablespoon black pepper, 1 tablespoon garlic powder per 5 lbs meat
3.  Rub seasoning thoroughly into all meat surfaces
4.  Layer in glass container, cover, refrigerate 2-3 days
5.  Hang on jerky rack in smoker or dehydrator at 160-180°F for 4-12 hours until meat bends but doesn't break
6.  Cool completely before storage in airtight container
:::

#### Bacon (Wet Cure)

:::highlight-box
1.  For 5 lb pork belly: combine 1 gallon water, 1 cup salt, 1/2 cup sugar, 2 tablespoons Prague Powder #1
2.  Submerge belly in brine, cover, refrigerate 7-10 days
3.  Remove, rinse thoroughly, pat dry
4.  Hang in smoker at 200-225°F for 1-2 hours with hickory or apple wood smoke
5.  Cool, slice to desired thickness, store in refrigerator wrapped in paper
6.  Use within 2-3 weeks or freeze for longer storage
:::

#### Ham (Dry Cure)

:::highlight-box
1.  For 10-15 lb ham: mix 2 cups salt, 1 cup brown sugar, 3 tablespoons Prague Powder #1, 2 tablespoons black pepper
2.  Rub cure thoroughly on all surfaces and into any crevices
3.  Place in container, cover, refrigerate 12-14 days (approximately 1 day per pound)
4.  Turn ham every 3 days to ensure even curing
5.  After cure period, rinse thoroughly and hang to dry 3-5 days in cool place (below 60°F)
6.  Smoke at 225-250°F for 12-24 hours until internal temperature reaches 160°F
7.  Cool completely, wrap in cloth, store in cool location. Lasts 2-3 weeks at room temperature if well-smoked and dried
:::

#### Smoked Fish

:::highlight-box
1.  Use firm-fleshed fish (salmon, mackerel, trout). Scale and clean, leaving skin on
2.  For 5 lbs fish: make brine with 1 quart water, 1/2 cup salt, 1/4 cup brown sugar, 1 tablespoon pickling spice
3.  Submerge fish 6-8 hours in refrigerated brine
4.  Remove, rinse, pat dry. Air-dry 1 hour until pellicle (glossy surface layer) forms
5.  Hot smoke at 160-225°F for 4-8 hours until flesh flakes easily, reaching 145°F internal temperature
6.  For extended storage, cold smoke at 68-85°F for 2-4 weeks after initial dry cure
7.  Store hot-smoked fish in refrigerator 2-3 weeks or freeze. Cold-smoked lasts longer at cooler temperatures
:::

## Drying & Dehydrating

Drying preserves food by removing moisture that bacteria, mold, and yeast need to grow. Traditional sun drying and modern dehydration both concentrate flavors and nutrients while creating lightweight, shelf-stable foods ideal for off-grid storage.

### Sun Drying Method

:::highlight-box
**Best for:** Herbs, tomatoes, peppers, apricots, plums, grapes (raisins)

-   **Optimal conditions:** Temperatures 85-100°F, low humidity below 30%, good air circulation, sunshine 6+ hours daily
-   **Timeline:** 2-4 weeks depending on food thickness, humidity, and temperature
-   **Advantages:** Free energy, no equipment needed, minimum labor
-   **Disadvantages:** Weather dependent, vulnerable to insects and rain, slower drying
:::

### Solar Dehydrator Construction

A simple solar dehydrator can be built from wood, clear plastic, and screening material at minimal cost. The box design uses passive solar heating with natural convection.

#### Basic Solar Dehydrator Design:

-   **Box frame:** Wooden frame 3-4 feet wide, 6 feet long, 1-2 feet tall (southern slope)
-   **Top cover:** Clear polycarbonate or glass panel angled at 45 degrees to catch sun
-   **Drying racks:** 4-6 removable racks with window screening or food-grade mesh, spaced 4-6 inches apart
-   **Heat absorption:** Black painted interior surfaces or containers of water to absorb and release heat
-   **Ventilation:** Inlet vents near bottom, outlet vents near top for natural convection airflow
-   **Insulation:** Straw or foam insulation on sides and back to maintain warmth
-   **Door:** Hinged or removable cover on south-facing side

### Oven Drying Method

:::tip
**For small batches or supplemental drying:**

-   Set oven to lowest temperature (150-200°F / 65-93°C)
-   Crack door open 4-6 inches to allow moisture escape
-   Place food on oven racks covered with parchment paper or mesh
-   Dry for 4-12 hours depending on food and thickness
-   Check every 1-2 hours to prevent over-drying
-   Use oven thermometer to ensure consistent temperature
:::

### Blanching Before Drying

:::highlight-box
**Blanching stops enzyme action** that causes deterioration. For vegetables to be dried:

-   Boil water, add vegetables 2-3 minutes until partially tender but still crisp
-   Immediately plunge into ice water to stop cooking
-   Drain thoroughly before dehydrating
-   Blanching is optional for herbs, mushrooms, peppers, and tomatoes but recommended for beans, peas, and leafy greens
:::

### Testing Dryness and Conditioning

:::tip
**Food is properly dried when:**

-   No moisture beads appear when squeezed
-   Brittle texture for vegetables and fruits
-   Pliable but no moisture for some fruits (dates, apricots)
-   Herbs crumble easily between fingers

**Conditioning (equilibration):** After drying, place food in sealed glass containers for 7-10 days at room temperature. Any remaining moisture distributes evenly. Shake containers daily. If condensation appears, food needs more drying.

## Salting & Brining

Salt preservation is one of humanity's oldest food preservation methods. Salt kills bacteria by drawing out moisture and creating an inhospitable environment. Salting works best for protein-rich foods like fish, meat, and vegetables.

### Salt Preservation Principles

:::highlight-box
**How salt preserves:**

-   **Osmosis:** Salt draws water from cells, dehydrating food and bacteria
-   **Plasmolysis:** High salt concentration ruptures bacterial cell membranes
-   **Enzyme inhibition:** Salt slows enzymatic decay processes
-   **pH alteration:** Creates environment where harmful bacteria cannot survive

**Effective salt concentration:** Foods preserved with salt require at least 20% salt by weight (very salty) for extended shelf life, or 10-15% for medium-term storage (weeks to months).
:::

### Dry Salting Technique

#### Dry Salting Process:

1.  Layer food (fish, meat, or vegetables) in glass or ceramic vessel
2.  Sprinkle salt layer (1/4 to 1/2 inch thick) on bottom
3.  Place single layer of food on salt bed
4.  Cover with generous salt layer (must completely cover food)
5.  Repeat layers ending with thick salt layer on top
6.  Weigh down with plate and heavy object to compress food
7.  Liquid (brine) will accumulate as salt draws water from food
8.  Keep submerged in brine. If brine level drops below food, make additional brine (20% salt solution) to top up
9.  Store in cool location. Properly salted foods last months to years

### Brine Ratios for Different Foods

### Salt Fish Preparation

:::highlight-box
1.  Use fresh fish (caught that day is ideal). Gut and remove scales if desired (skin provides barrier)
2.  Layer fish in crock or glass container with coarse salt (1/2 cup salt per lb fish)
3.  Cover with plate and weight to compress. Fish will release brine.
4.  After 7-14 days (depending on fish size), fish is cured. Move to storage container with additional salt layer
5.  Store in cool location (50-60°F). Can last 1-2 years if kept dry and salted.
6.  To use: Soak salt fish in fresh water 12-24 hours, changing water 2-3 times to remove excess salt
7.  Cook by simmering 30-45 minutes with aromatics (onion, bay leaf)
:::

### Salt Pork (Salt Beef)

:::tip
**Simple salt pork recipe:**

-   Use fatty cuts: pork shoulder, belly, or beef brisket
-   Cut into 2-3 inch thick blocks
-   Mix cure: 1/4 cup salt + 1 tablespoon sugar + 1 tablespoon black pepper per 2 lbs meat
-   Rub cure thoroughly into all surfaces and pack any crevices
-   Layer in crock with additional cure, cover, refrigerate 7-10 days
-   Turn meat every 2 days. After cure, drain brine and pack in salt in glass jars for storage
-   Use within 4-6 months. To cook: rinse salt off, soak 2-4 hours, then simmer until tender (1-2 hours)
:::

### Sauerkraut (Fermented Cabbage)

:::highlight-box
1.  Remove outer leaves and core from cabbage. Reserve clean outer leaves.
2.  Slice cabbage thinly (1/4 inch). Measure weight.
3.  Layer in crock, sprinkling 2.5-3% salt by weight as you layer (approximately 2.5 tablespoons salt per 2 lb cabbage)
4.  Pound cabbage firmly to break cell walls and release brine
5.  Place reserved cabbage leaf on top, weigh down with plate and jar filled with water
6.  Keep in cool location (60-75°F). Fermentation begins in 3-7 days
7.  Skim off white film (kahm yeast) if it appears on surface. This is harmless but affects flavor.
8.  Fully fermented in 4-6 weeks. Taste regularly until desired sourness achieved.
9.  Transfer to cold storage. Sauerkraut lasts 4-6 months in cold conditions.
:::

### Kimchi (Fermented Spicy Vegetables)

:::highlight-box
1.  For 2 lb Napa cabbage: Remove outer leaves, quarter cabbage lengthwise, rinse.
2.  Make salt brine: 1 quart water + 2 tablespoons salt. Soak cabbage 4-8 hours until flexible.
3.  Drain, reserving brine. Layer cabbage in crock with filling: 2 tablespoons gochugaru (red pepper flakes), 4 cloves minced garlic, 1 tablespoon ginger, 2 green onions chopped, 1 tablespoon fish sauce or salt
4.  Pack tightly in crock, pressing down to submerge in brine. Weigh down.
5.  Keep at room temperature (60-75°F). Fermentation 3-5 weeks.
6.  Taste regularly. Move to cold storage when desired flavor achieved.
7.  Lasts 3-6 months refrigerated. Flavor continues to develop slowly.
:::

### Equilibrium Curing (Precision Salt Method)

Most advanced salt curing method. Calculate exact salt needed based on meat weight for precise final salt content and even saturation.

:::card
**Calculation:** Salt needed (grams) = Meat weight (grams) × 0.025 (for 2.5% salt content)

For example: 1000g meat × 0.025 = 25g salt needed

:::

**Process:**
1. Weigh meat precisely in grams
2. Calculate salt amount using formula above (2.5% is standard; can adjust 2-3% for different saltiness levels)
3. Mix salt thoroughly into meat, ensuring even distribution
4. Vacuum seal or wrap tightly in plastic
5. Refrigerate 1-3 weeks depending on thickness (approximately 1 week per 2 inches of thickness)
6. No rinsing required—salt has saturated evenly throughout

**Advantages over other methods:**
- Produces most consistent results due to mathematical precision
- Uses less total salt than traditional wet brining
- Ideal for learning proper curing ratios before scaling to larger batches
- No risk of over-salting or under-salting
- Finished product has even salt distribution

:::tip
Higher altitudes or dry climates may require slightly increased salt percentages (up to 3%) as environmental moisture affects curing dynamics.
:::

### Olive Curing

:::tip
**Lye-cured olives (most common method):**

-   Use firm, fresh olives. Make 1% lye solution: 1 tablespoon lye per gallon water (CAUTION: caustic)
-   Submerge olives in lye solution. Check progress every 2-4 hours by cutting test olive - lye should have penetrated 2/3 through pit
-   When properly lyed (12-48 hours depending on olive size and temperature), drain lye and rinse 3-4 times with fresh water over 8-12 hours, changing water frequently
-   Place in brine: 5-6% salt solution (1/2 cup salt per gallon water). Store in cool location.
-   Olives cure in brine 2-4 weeks. Last 6 months to 1 year in brine.
:::

<section id="fermentation">

## Fermentation for Preservation

Fermentation preserves food through beneficial bacteria (primarily Lactobacillus) that produce lactic acid, creating an acidic environment hostile to pathogens. Lacto-fermentation enhances nutrition, creates probiotics, and stores vegetables for months without heat or pressure.

### Lacto-Fermentation Basics

:::highlight-box
**Requirements for safe fermentation:**

-   **Salt:** Creates osmotic pressure that draws water from vegetables and salt-tolerant beneficial bacteria thrive
-   **Anaerobic environment:** Vegetables submerged in brine; no oxygen needed for Lactobacillus
-   **Temperature:** 60-75°F optimal; fermentation occurs slower below 50°F, faster above 75°F
-   **Time:** 3-8 weeks depending on temperature and desired sourness
-   **No pathogenic bacteria:** Salt and acid created by fermentation prevent botulism and pathogens
:::

### Salt Ratios for Fermentation

:::card
#### General fermentation salt formula:

**2-5% salt by weight of vegetables**

-   2% salt = mild flavor, faster fermentation, good for delicate vegetables
-   3% salt = balanced flavor and fermentation speed (most common)
-   5% salt = slower fermentation, more sour, better for longer storage and warmer climates

**Calculation example:** For 10 pounds of vegetables at 3% salt, use 4.8 oz (approximately 1/2 cup) of salt.
:::

### Common Fermented Vegetables

### Fermented Hot Sauce

:::highlight-box
1.  Destem and chop hot peppers (habanero, scotch bonnet, or jalapeño). Measure weight.
2.  For 2 lb peppers: calculate 3% salt = 1 oz salt (approximately 2 tablespoons)
3.  Add optional additions: 4 cloves garlic minced, 1 tablespoon ginger grated, 1/4 cup onion minced
4.  Mix peppers with salt and additions thoroughly in glass bowl
5.  Pack firmly into glass jar, pressing down to release juices. Peppers should be submerged in brine.
6.  Cover with cloth or coffee filter held on with rubber band to prevent dust while allowing air exchange
7.  Keep at room temperature (65-75°F). Ferment 4-12 weeks depending on desired flavor depth
8.  After fermentation, blend with vinegar (optional: 1/4 cup vinegar per cup fermented peppers) for smoother sauce
9.  Store in glass jars in cool location. Lasts 6-12 months or longer
:::

### Fruit Wines

:::tip
**Basic fruit wine fermentation:**

-   Crush fruit (berries, apples, grapes) to release juice. Mix 1 part fruit juice with 1 part water
-   Add sugar: 1-2 pounds sugar per gallon liquid (higher sugar = higher alcohol)
-   Add wild ferment starter or wine yeast (if available). Natural fermentation occurs slowly with wild yeasts.
-   Keep in covered (but vented) carboy or jar at 60-75°F. Fermentation takes 3-6 months.
-   Rack off sediment (siphon into new container) after first month, then monthly until clear
-   Finished wine stores indefinitely in sealed bottles at cool temperature (50-60°F)
:::

### Vinegar Making from Fermented Vegetables

:::highlight-box
1.  Take finished fermented vegetable brine (sauerkraut, pickles, or other fermented vegetables)
2.  Transfer brine to wide-mouth jar or crock (wider mouth accelerates vinegar formation)
3.  Cover with cloth held on with rubber band. Keep at room temperature (65-75°F)
4.  Acetic acid bacteria from air will colonize the surface and convert alcohol to acetic acid
5.  Formation time: 4-12 weeks depending on temperature and initial alcohol content
6.  Vinegar is ready when it tastes sour and smells strongly of vinegar. Taste test after 4 weeks.
7.  Strain through cheesecloth, bottle, and seal. Vinegar stores indefinitely.
:::

## Root Cellaring & Cold Storage

Root cellaring stores fresh produce in cool, humid conditions near freezing (32-50°F), slowing respiration and decay. Properly designed and maintained root cellars can store vegetables for months without processing, maintaining nutritional value and quality better than any other method.

### Root Cellar Construction

#### Ideal Root Cellar Features:

-   **Location:** Underground or semi-underground (basement, below-grade), or well-insulated above-ground box
-   **Temperature:** 32-40°F (0-4°C) for most produce. Separate zones optimal: 40-45°F for apples, onions; 35-40°F for most vegetables
-   **Humidity:** 85-95% for most produce. Use wet sand, burlap, or earth floor to maintain moisture.
-   **Ventilation:** Fresh air intake near floor, exhaust vent near ceiling to allow CO2 escape and O2 entry. Can be passive (open vents in cool months) or active (small fan)
-   **Darkness:** Exclude light to prevent sprouting and greening
-   **Shelving:** Wooden slat shelves for air circulation, not solid shelves
-   **Partitioning:** Separate ethylene-producing crops (apples, pears) from ethylene-sensitive crops (potatoes, carrots, leafy greens) to prevent premature ripening
-   **Insulation:** 4-6 inches for above-ground cellars. Underground cellars use earth as insulation.

### Sand Storage Method

:::highlight-box
**Sand storage prevents produce drying and maintains humidity:**

1.  Use clean sand (not salt-treated) or sawdust/peat moss
2.  Line wooden bins with plastic to prevent direct contact with walls (aids drying)
3.  Place 2-3 inches of sand on bottom
4.  Arrange root vegetables (carrots, beets, parsnips) in single layer without touching
5.  Cover with 2-3 inches sand and repeat layers, ending with sand layer
6.  Keep sand slightly moist but not waterlogged (test: handful of sand holds together barely)
7.  Vegetables remain dormant and fresh for 6-9 months
8.  Check monthly for deterioration; remove any spoiling vegetables immediately
:::

### Hay Bale Cold Storage (Alternative)

:::tip
**Hay bale cold box for minimal insulation:**

-   Build wooden frame (4x4 feet minimum, 18-24 inches deep) in shaded, well-drained location
-   Surround frame with hay bales 18-24 inches thick on all sides
-   Top with 12-18 inches hay and removable boards
-   Bury frame bottom 6-12 inches in ground for earth insulation
-   Line interior with burlap or heavy plastic
-   Store vegetables in boxes inside. Maintains 40-50°F in cool months.
-   Works best in climates with cool fall/winter. Not suitable for warm regions.
:::

## Rendering & Fat Preservation

Rendered fats (lard from pork, tallow from beef/mutton) are shelf-stable cooking mediums and valuable trade goods. Clarified butter (ghee) stores without refrigeration. Preserved fats provide essential calories and enable cooking when modern fuels are unavailable.

### Rendering Lard (from Pork Fat)

:::highlight-box
1.  Use pork fatback, leaf lard (around organs), or trim fat from any pork cuts
2.  Cut fat into 1-inch cubes. Remove any meat or blood clots.
3.  Place fat in heavy pot over very low heat. Stir occasionally as fat renders.
4.  Temperature should not exceed 200°F (93°C). Slow rendering produces best quality lard.
5.  Render until solids (cracklings) are light brown and crispy, approximately 2-3 hours for 10 lbs fat
6.  Strain hot lard through fine sieve lined with cheesecloth into clean jars
7.  Allow to cool to room temperature. Lard will solidify and clarify as it cools.
8.  Sealed, rendered lard stores at room temperature 6+ months, refrigerated 1+ years, or indefinitely in cool root cellar
:::

### Rendering Tallow (from Beef Fat)

:::tip
**Tallow rendering is identical to lard but with these differences:**

-   Use beef suet (hard fat around kidneys) or trim fat from beef butchering
-   Cut into smaller 1/2 inch cubes and render slower to avoid scorching
-   Render at even lower temperature (180°F/82°C) to prevent flavor degradation
-   Takes longer to fully render - 4-5 hours for 15 lbs fat
-   Tallow has stronger flavor than lard but stores even longer (1-2 years at room temperature)
-   Best for cooking savory foods or soap making
:::

### Clarified Butter (Ghee)

:::highlight-box
1.  Use unsalted butter (salted butter has longer shelf life fresh). Melt slowly over low heat.
2.  Milk solids will separate and float to surface (foam layer) and sink to bottom
3.  Pour off clear golden liquid (ghee) into clean jar, leaving milk solids behind
4.  Ghee without milk solids stores without refrigeration for 6-12 months
5.  Alternatively: Strain through cheesecloth for absolute clarity and longer shelf life
6.  Ghee has higher smoke point than butter (450°F vs 350°F), better for cooking
:::

### Storing Fats

#### Fat Storage Best Practices:

-   **Containers:** Glass jars with tight lids (best), food-grade plastic, or crocks
-   **Temperature:** Cool (50-70°F) is ideal. Room temperature works for 6+ months. Refrigeration extends to 1-2 years.
-   **Light:** Store in dark location away from sunlight. Light causes rancidity.
-   **Air:** Minimize air exposure. Vacuum seal or use oxygen absorbers for longest storage.
-   **Signs of spoilage:** Off-smell, mold growth, or rancid odor. Discard if any spoilage signs appear.

### Pemmican (Preserved Meat/Fat Mixture)

Pemmican is a concentrated food combining dried meat, rendered fat, and dried berries. Properly made pemmican remains edible for years and provides complete nutrition for survival or extended travel.

:::highlight-box
1.  Prepare dried meat: Slice lean meat (venison, beef, elk) thin and dry to completely brittle (see Drying section)
2.  Pound dried meat into fine powder using mortar and pestle or rock
3.  Mix: 2 parts meat powder + 1 part rendered fat (melted but still warm) + 1 part dried berries (optional)
4.  Press firmly into jars or containers while fat is still warm and pliable
5.  Allow to solidify at room temperature. Once hard, seal jars.
6.  Pemmican keeps years in cool, dry storage without refrigeration
7.  Traditional ratio: High-fat pemmican (1:1 meat:fat) was more portable but rich. Use 2:1 ratio for better flavor balance.
:::

### Using Fat for Soap Making

:::tip
**Basic rendered fat soap (requires safety precautions with lye):**

-   Use rendered tallow or lard for highest quality, most natural soaps
-   Soap making requires lye (sodium hydroxide) and water mixed carefully at precise ratios
-   This is complex chemistry - follow established recipes with exact measurements for safety
-   Benefits: Creates soap from available rendered fats, extremely valuable trade item in off-grid scenarios

Note: Soap making instructions are beyond scope of this document. Consult specialized soap-making references for precise procedures and safety protocols.
:::

## Sugar Preservation

Sugar preserves through osmosis: high sugar concentration draws water from bacteria and mold, preventing growth. Sugar preservation includes jams, jellies, fruit preserves, fruit leathers, candied fruits, and honey storage.

### Jam and Jelly Basics

:::highlight-box
**Key principles for successful jam/jelly:**

-   **Pectin:** Natural pectin in fruits (especially apple skins, lemon peels) gels preserves. Low-pectin fruits require added pectin (commercial or liquid) or extended cooking
-   **Sugar ratio:** Minimum 50% sugar by weight for shelf stability without processing (traditional preserves). For water bath canned jams, 55% sugar required for proper gel and preservation
-   **Acid:** Lemon juice or vinegar lowers pH and improves gel formation
-   **Cooking temperature:** Jam reaches setting point at 220°F (104°C) at sea level. Use candy/jelly thermometer.
:::

### Fruit Preserves Sugar Ratios

<table><thead><tr><th scope="col">Preserve Type</th><th scope="col">Fruit to Sugar Ratio</th><th scope="col">Pectin</th><th scope="col">Acid (lemon juice)</th><th scope="col">Yield per 5 lbs fruit</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Jam (low sugar)</td><td>1:1 (1 lb fruit to 1 lb sugar)</td><td>Optional (added)</td><td>2-3 tablespoons</td><td>3-4 pints</td></tr><tr><td>Jam (traditional)</td><td>1:1.2 (1 lb fruit to 1.2 lb sugar)</td><td>None if natural pectin</td><td>2-3 tablespoons</td><td>3-4 pints</td></tr><tr><td>Jelly (clear, strained)</td><td>1:0.75 (juice to sugar)</td><td>Optional</td><td>Variable</td><td>2-3 pints</td></tr><tr><td>Marmalade</td><td>1:1 (fruit to sugar)</td><td>None if high pectin</td><td>Built-in (citrus)</td><td>3-4 pints</td></tr><tr><td>Preserves (chunky)</td><td>1:1.25 (fruit to sugar)</td><td>Optional</td><td>1-2 tablespoons</td><td>3-4 pints</td></tr></tbody></table>

### Jam Making Process

:::highlight-box
1.  **Prepare fruit:** Wash, remove stems/blemishes. Cut or crush depending on desired texture.
2.  **Measure:** Weigh prepared fruit. Calculate sugar amount based on desired ratio.
3.  **Combine:** Place fruit in heavy pot (copper or stainless steel). Add sugar and lemon juice.
4.  **Cook:** Bring to rolling boil over medium-high heat, stirring frequently to prevent sticking.
5.  **Skim foam:** Remove foam from surface with slotted spoon as mixture cooks.
6.  **Test setting point:** At 220°F (104°C), drop small spoonful on cold plate. If it wrinkles when pushed, setting point is reached (approximately 45 minutes to 2 hours depending on fruit).
7.  **Fill jars:** Ladle hot jam into sterilized jars leaving 1/4 inch headspace. Apply lids.
8.  **Process:** Water bath canning for 10-15 minutes at rolling boil (add 5 minutes per 2,000 feet altitude).
9.  **Cool:** Remove jars and let cool 12-24 hours before checking seals.
:::

### Fruit Leathers

Fruit leather is fruit puree dried to thin, pliable leather. Very shelf-stable and excellent for travel food.

:::tip
1.  Cook fruit (2-3 lbs) with minimal water until very soft, approximately 15-30 minutes
2.  Puree through sieve to remove seeds/skins
3.  Add optional sweetener: 1/4 to 1/2 cup honey or sugar per 2 lbs fruit puree
4.  Spread puree 1/8 to 1/4 inch thick on parchment paper on dehydrator trays or oven racks
5.  Dry at 140-160°F (60-71°C) for 8-24 hours until pliable but not sticky
6.  Roll fruit leather while still warm on parchment paper, or cut into strips after cooling
7.  Store in airtight containers. Lasts 6-12 months without refrigeration.
:::

### Candied Fruit

:::highlight-box
1.  **Prepare fruit:** Use firm fruit (citrus peel, plums, apricots). Cut uniformly into 1/4 to 1/2 inch pieces.
2.  **Blanch:** Boil fruit 5-10 minutes to soften slightly. Drain well.
3.  **Make syrup:** For each pound blanched fruit, combine 1.5 cups water and 2.5 cups sugar. Bring to boil, dissolving sugar.
4.  **Candy process:** Add blanched fruit to hot syrup. Simmer 30 minutes. Cool overnight. Repeat heating and cooling 3-4 times, extending cooking time slightly each time. This gradual sugar penetration creates candied fruit.
5.  **Final step:** After final cooking, drain fruit and spread on racks to dry 2-3 days until no longer sticky.
6.  **Optional coating:** Roll in granulated sugar while still slightly tacky.
7.  **Storage:** Store in airtight jars with parchment between layers. Lasts 1-2 years at room temperature.
:::

### Honey Preservation and Use

:::tip
**Honey is nature's perfect preservation:**

-   Pure honey never spoils due to low moisture and antimicrobial properties
-   May crystallize over time (normal; warm gently to return to liquid state)
-   Store in sealed glass jars away from moisture
-   Can be used as preservation medium: preserve fruits in honey (use 1:1 ratio fruit:honey)
-   Honey preserves meats and fish when mixed with salt
-   Use honey for mead making (fermented honey beverages)
:::

### Maple Syrup Processing

Maple syrup processing requires access to maple trees and significant fuel for boiling. The process concentrates maple sap from ~2% sugar to 66% sugar (syrup) by boiling off water.

:::highlight-box
1.  **Sap collection:** Tap maple trees (40+ year old trees) in early spring when sap begins flowing. Collect 5-10 gallons sap per day per tap.
2.  **Boiling:** Boil sap in shallow pan over intense heat. Sap reduces dramatically (40-50 gallons sap makes 1 gallon syrup).
3.  **Temperature control:** Syrup reaches proper consistency at 220°F (104°C), about 7°F above water's boiling point.
4.  **Filtering:** Hot syrup must be filtered through felt or cheesecloth to remove sediment.
5.  **Bottling:** Bottled hot in hot jars. Syrup will seal as it cools.
6.  **Storage:** Properly processed maple syrup lasts indefinitely sealed at room temperature, or refrigerated after opening (lasts 1+ years open).
:::

## Long-Term Food Storage

Long-term food storage for years requires managing oxygen, moisture, temperature, light, and pests. Modern methods use Mylar bags, oxygen absorbers, nitrogen gas, and food-grade containers to create shelf-stable storage lasting 10+ years.

### Mylar Bags and Oxygen Absorbers

#### Mylar Bag Storage System:

-   **Mylar bags:** Aluminum-lined plastic bags that block light and oxygen. Thickness 4-6 mils recommended for long-term storage.
-   **Oxygen absorbers:** Packets of ferrous oxide that remove oxygen from sealed containers. Available in 100cc, 300cc, 500cc, 1000cc, and 2000cc sizes.
-   **Desiccants (optional):** Silica gel packets to absorb moisture. Use if storing in humid climate.
-   **Sealing method:** Heat seal bags using iron, hair straightener, or commercial heat sealer. Leave small opening, insert oxygen absorber, then seal completely.
-   **Placement in buckets:** Seal Mylar bag, place sealed bag in food-grade bucket with lid for rodent/moisture protection.

### Nitrogen Packing

:::tip
**Professional nitrogen packing:**

-   Displace oxygen with nitrogen gas (inert, won't support combustion or spoilage organisms)
-   Fill container with food, seal nitrogen line into lid, flush container with nitrogen for 30-60 seconds
-   Remove nitrogen line and seal completely
-   More effective than oxygen absorbers for very long-term storage (10-20+ years)
-   Requires nitrogen source (compressed cylinder or generator) - not practical for home storage unless commercial nitrogen available locally
:::

### Food-Grade Buckets

:::highlight-box
**Five-gallon food-grade buckets with lids are ideal for long-term storage:**

-   Capacity: ~35 lbs of flour/sugar, ~40 lbs of rice, ~30 lbs of beans
-   Use only food-grade buckets (marked #2 or #5 recycling symbol, or labeled "food grade")
-   Line bucket with Mylar bag, fill with dry food, insert oxygen absorber, seal Mylar, close bucket lid
-   Store in cool, dry location. Protect from direct sunlight and temperature fluctuations.
-   Label buckets with contents and date clearly
-   Buckets stack efficiently for space savings
:::

Properly stored whole grains and legumes remain viable for 10-30 years. The key is controlling moisture, temperature, pests, and oxygen.

## Pemmican: High-Calorie Survival Food

Pemmican is a concentrated food combining dried meat and fat with dried berries. A single pound provides 3,500+ calories and lasts years when properly made. Traditional food of hunters and travelers for extreme portability.

:::card
**Classic Pemmican Ratio:**

1 pound dried meat powder + 1 pound rendered fat + optional: 1/2 cup dried berries/nuts
:::

### Step-by-Step Production

1.  **Dry meat:** Slice lean meat (all fat removed) into thin strips (1/4 inch). Sun-dry or smoke-dry until completely brittle (5-7 days). Must snap when bent.
2.  **Pound meat:** Using rocks or mallet, pound dried meat into fine powder. Should be uniform powder, not chunks.
3.  **Render fat:** Slowly heat fat trimmings over low flame until liquefied. Strain through cloth to remove solids.
4.  **Mix:** Combine meat powder with warm (not hot) liquid fat in 1:1 weight ratio. Mix thoroughly.
5.  **Optional additions:** Dried berries or nuts add flavor and nutrition (1/2 cup per batch).
6.  **Press into forms:** Pack tightly into bark containers, logs, or molds. Press to remove air pockets.
7.  **Storage:** Cool, dark place (below 60°F). Wrapped in cloth, lasts 10+ years without spoiling.

</section>

## Fruit Leather: Energy-Dense Dried Fruit

Fruit leather is dried fruit puree providing quick carbohydrate energy in minimal weight. Any fruit works. Lasts 1-2 years.

:::card
**Basic Formula:**

5 pounds fresh fruit + 1/2 to 1 pound honey (optional) + water if needed
:::

### Production Steps

1.  **Prepare:** Wash fruit, remove pits/seeds, chop roughly
2.  **Cook:** Simmer with minimal water 15-20 minutes until soft
3.  **Sweeten:** Add honey if desired (optional)
4.  **Spread:** Pour 1/4 inch thick onto dehydrator trays lined with parchment
5.  **Dry:** 135-145°F for 8-12 hours until no longer sticky but still flexible
6.  **Roll & wrap:** Cut into 2-3 inch strips, roll tight, wrap in parchment
7.  **Storage:** Cool, dark place. Lasts 1-2 years.

## Making Vinegar & Vinegar Mother (SCOBY)

Homemade vinegar is an antimicrobial preservative and medicinal. Vinegar mother (SCOBY) is the living culture that converts alcohol to acetic acid, allowing continuous production.

### Creating Vinegar Mother (4-8 weeks)

1.  **Gather:** Fruit juice (apple cider, grape) or wine
2.  **Container:** Wide-mouth jar with cloth cover (allows air, prevents insects)
3.  **Process:** Pour juice into jar, cover with cloth, place in warm location (65-75°F)
4.  **Timeline:**
    -   Weeks 1-2: Fermentation (sugar converts to alcohol)
    -   Weeks 2-4: Acetic acid bacteria colonize (alcohol converts to vinegar)
    -   Weeks 4-8: Mother develops (slime-like bacterial layer)
    -   Week 8: Mother established, vinegar ready
5.  **Taste after week 4.** When tart and no longer sweet, vinegar is ready.

### Maintaining Vinegar Mother

-   **Continuous production:** Every 2-3 weeks, feed with new juice (half the volume in jar). New vinegar ready in 2-4 weeks.
-   **Storage:** Cool place (50-70°F). Can dormant for months. Reactivate with fresh juice.
-   **Appearance:** Floating slime or sediment is normal and indicates healthy culture
-   **Using:** For pickling use 1 part vinegar to 2-3 parts brine. Medicinal: 1 tablespoon in water 2-3 times daily

<section id="grain-storage-co2">

## Long-Term Grain Storage with CO2 (Dry Ice Method)

Properly sealed grains with CO2 can remain viable for 10-30 years. Carbon dioxide displaces oxygen, preventing insects, oxidation, and sprouting.

### CO2 Storage Using Dry Ice

1.  **Container:** Food-grade bucket (tight-fitting lid) or Mylar bag (heat-sealable)
2.  **Fill grain:** Pour wheat, corn, oats into container, leaving 2-3 inches headspace
3.  **Add dry ice:** 1 pound per 5-gallon bucket (use tongs—never touch bare). As it sublimes, CO2 displaces oxygen.
4.  **Wait:** 15-20 minutes until all dry ice converts to gas (watch for fog dissipating)
5.  **Seal immediately:** Close bucket tight OR heat-seal Mylar while CO2 still releasing. This traps CO2 inside.
6.  **Storage:** Cool (50-70°F), dark place. Grains viable 10-30 years.

### Oxygen Absorber Alternative

-   If dry ice unavailable: Use oxygen absorber packets instead (2000cc per 5-gallon bucket)
-   Place on top of grain, seal immediately
-   Absorbers consume oxygen over 24-48 hours, creating anaerobic environment
-   Same storage conditions and shelf life

### Moisture Control

-   **Grain moisture must be below 12%** before sealing. High moisture causes mold/sprouting.
-   **Desiccant packs:** Add food-grade silica gel or homemade (dried rice in cloth) to absorb moisture
-   **Monitor:** If condensation visible inside during storage, seal has failed—use contents sooner

### Best Grains for Long-Term Storage

-   **Wheat berries:** Highest protein, longest storage (30 years). White wheat stores longer than red.
-   **White rice:** Very stable (15-30 years). Brown rice 10-15 years due to oil content.
-   **Dried corn:** 10-15 years. Shelled corn lasts longer than ears.
-   **Oats:** 8-10 years (higher fat content reduces lifespan)
-   **Dried beans:** 15-30 years if stored properly

### Testing Grain Viability

-   **Sprouting test:** Soak 10 grains overnight, plant in soil. If 8+ sprout, viability is good.
-   **Smell:** Musty/off odor indicates mold—discard
-   **Visual:** Check for discoloration, insects, mold

<section id="nixtamalization">

## Nixtamalization: Alkaline Corn Treatment

:::danger
**LIFE-THREATENING DEFICIENCY: Corn-dependent diets WITHOUT nixtamalization cause pellagra** (niacin/B3 deficiency), resulting in dermatitis, diarrhea, dementia, and death. Nixtamalization RELEASES niacin trapped in corn's matrix, making it bioavailable. This is not optional for populations relying on corn as primary staple—it is literally life-or-death essential.
:::

### Why Nixtamalization Matters

Corn contains niacin (vitamin B3) in a bound form that humans cannot absorb. Nixtamalization—treating corn with an alkaline substance—releases this niacin, making it bioavailable. This ancient Mesoamerican technique is survival-critical for populations relying on corn as a primary calorie source.

### The Chemical Process

-   **Alkaline agent:** Calcium hydroxide (slaked lime, "cal"), lye from wood ash, or pure sodium hydroxide
-   **Reaction:** Alkaline solution (pH 12-14) hydrolyzes the corn kernel hull and alters the protein structure
-   **Result:** Niacin becomes extractable; proteins become more complete amino acid profiles; calcium fortification occurs
-   **Culinary bonus:** Masa (nixtamalized corn dough) has superior texture and flavor

### Step-by-Step Nixtamalization Process

:::card
**For 1 pound (450g) of dried corn:**

-   1 gallon water
-   1-2 tablespoons calcium hydroxide (cal/cal viva) OR 3-4 tablespoons wood ash solution OR equivalent lye
:::

1.  **Prepare alkali:**
    -   *If using calcium hydroxide:* Mix powder with water; let settle 30 minutes, use clear liquid
    -   *If using wood ash:* Soak hardwood ash in water (1 cup ash per 1 gallon water), let settle overnight, strain carefully—this makes lye water
2.  **Boil corn:** Bring water + alkali solution to boil in a non-reactive pot (stainless steel or ceramic, never aluminum)
3.  **Add corn:** Pour dried corn into boiling alkaline water; maintain boil for 10-15 minutes. You should see the corn kernels' outer skins beginning to loosen
4.  **Rest:** Remove from heat, cover, and let sit for 8-12 hours (overnight works perfectly)
5.  **Rinse:** Drain through a colander; rinse corn kernels vigorously under running water while rubbing them between your hands to remove the loosened hulls. Rinse at least 3-5 times until water runs mostly clear
6.  **Dry or grind:**
    -   For storage: spread nixtamal on cloth to dry (2-3 days in sun/air)
    -   For immediate use: grind into masa with mortar and pestle, metate, or stone mill

### Masa Production from Nixtamal

**Fresh Masa (for tortillas, tamales):**

:::card
**Basic ratio:** 2 cups nixtamal : 1/2 cup water (adjust for dough texture)

-   Grind nixtamal with water until fine, smooth paste forms
-   Dough should hold together but not be sticky
-   Use immediately for best results
-   Keeps 2-3 days refrigerated
:::

**Dried Masa Flour:**

-   Dry nixtamal completely in sun (3-5 days) or low-heat oven (200°F for 2-3 hours)
-   Grind to fine flour; store in sealed containers for 6+ months
-   Rehydrate with warm water (2 parts flour : 1 part water) to make fresh masa

</section>

## Botulism Prevention by Food Type

:::danger
**CRITICAL:** Clostridium botulinum is deadly. Incorrect processing = fatal toxin. Follow time/temperature/acidity precisely.
:::

### Three Control Methods

### By Food Type

:::highlight-box
**Low-Acid Foods (pH > 4.6):** Vegetables, meats, fish, soups

-   **Method required:** Pressure canning ONLY
-   **Temperature:** 240°F (dial gauge: 11 PSI at sea level, 10 PSI at <1000 ft)
-   **Minimum time:** 75 minutes for quarts of low-acid vegetables; 90-100 min for meat/fish
-   **No exceptions:** Water bath canning WILL NOT work—spores survive boiling
:::

:::highlight-box
**High-Acid Foods (pH < 4.6):** Fruits, pickles, jams, sauerkraut

-   **Method:** Water bath canning (boiling) sufficient
-   **Temperature:** 212°F (boiling)
-   **Minimum times:**
    -   Jams/jellies: 10 minutes
    -   Pickles: 15-20 minutes (pints), 20 minutes (quarts)
    -   Fruit: 15-20 minutes (pints), 20-25 minutes (quarts)
:::

:::highlight-box
**Borderline Foods (pH 4.6-4.8):** Tomatoes (canned plain), some pickled vegetables

-   **Treatment:** Add acid (citric acid, vinegar) to reach pH < 4.6 OR use pressure canning
-   **For tomatoes:** Add 2 tablespoons white vinegar (5% acidity) per quart of tomatoes
-   **Alternative:** Add 1/4 teaspoon citric acid per pint
:::

### Critical Processing Rules

1.  **Never guess pH:** Use pH strips or test kit for homemade fermented/lowered-acid foods
2.  **Never reduce processing time:** Even by 5 minutes—this is where botulism risk lives
3.  **Never open/reprocess failed seals:** Spores may have germinated during cooling. Discard the jar.
4.  **Never use water bath for low-acid foods:** This is the single most common botulism mistake
5.  **High altitude adjustment:** At 1000+ ft elevation, increase pressure canning pressure (see chart in previous section)

<section id="grain-storage">

## Grain Storage: Moisture Content & Mycotoxin Prevention

### Critical Moisture Threshold

:::highlight-box
**Safe storage moisture:** <13% for long-term (6+ months) stability

-   **\>13%:** Mold and mycotoxin growth risk increases dramatically
-   **12-13%:** Marginal; acceptable with good ventilation and cool temps
-   **<12%:** Safe for 1+ year if stored in airtight containers
:::

### Moisture Testing

-   **Float test (rough):** Fresh grain floats in water due to air pockets; properly dried grain sinks slowly
-   **Tooth test:** Properly dried wheat/corn should break with clean snap, not crush. If it bends, moisture is too high
-   **Digital moisture meter:** Most reliable; available at agricultural suppliers. Aim for 10-12% reading.

### Drying Grain

-   **Sun drying:** Spread on clean cloth, turn daily for 5-7 days (weather dependent)
-   **Low-heat oven:** 140°F with door slightly open, 2-4 hours per batch (energy intensive)
-   **Natural ventilation:** Suspended in thin layer with airflow (slowest, most efficient)

### Mycotoxin Prevention

:::danger
**LIFE-THREATENING DANGER: Grain moisture MUST remain below 13% or mycotoxins develop.** Aspergillus mold produces aflatoxin (causes liver cancer and immune system damage). Improper grain storage is a SILENT KILLER—toxins are invisible but extremely dangerous. This is not optional.
:::

**Aflatoxins** (from Aspergillus mold) are carcinogenic and deadly. To prevent:

-   **Maximum moisture: 13% by weight** (test with moisture meter for accuracy—visual inspection is unreliable)
-   Store in cool (below 60°F if possible), dry location
-   Use food-grade storage containers with oxygen absorbers
-   Prevent condensation: Use desiccant packets (silica gel, dried lime) in sealed containers
-   Inspect regularly; discard any grain with visible mold, insect infestation, or musty smell—toxins may be invisible
-   Never store damp grain—mold growth can happen in 48 hours
-   Discard immediately: Any grain showing visible mold, insect damage, or musty/off-odors—toxins may be present even if not visible

</section>

<section id="pemmican-ratios">

## Pemmican: Fat:Meat Ratios for Rancidity Prevention

### What Pemmican Is

Pemmican is rendered fat mixed with dried, powdered meat—a shelf-stable, nutrient-dense food that lasts 10+ years. Fat acts as a preservative by sealing meat from oxygen (rancidity requires oxygen).

### The Critical Fat:Meat Ratio

:::card
**Optimal ratio: 1:1 to 2:1 fat to dried meat (by weight)**

-   **1:1 ratio:** Leaner final product; more prone to rancidity if fat quality is poor; storage life ~5-7 years
-   **1.5:1 ratio:** Best balance of palatability and shelf life; easier to chew; storage life 7-10 years
-   **2:1 ratio:** Very fatty/rich; excellent insulation from oxygen; storage life 10-15+ years; higher calorie density (4700 kcal/lb)
:::

### Fat Selection (Critical for Rancidity Prevention)

-   **Beef tallow:** Highest oxidative stability; best for long-term pemmican (10+ years)
-   **Bison fat:** Excellent stability; traditional choice
-   **Venison fat:** Slightly less stable; use 1.5-2:1 ratio minimum
-   **Pork lard:** Moderate stability; use higher ratio (2:1); storage life ~5 years
-   **Avoid:** Poultry fat (very prone to rancidity), vegetable oils (oxidize quickly)

### Step-by-Step Pemmican Making

1.  **Render fat:** Cut fat into small pieces, heat low and slow (never above 185°F) until fat is liquid and solids settle. Strain through cheesecloth.
2.  **Dry meat:** Lean meat strips jerky-dried (see Jerky section), then pounded into fine powder with mortar/stone
3.  **Mix at temperature:** Warm fat to 160-170°F (pour-able, not hot), mix with meat powder (ratio above)
4.  **Cool in molds:** Pour into patties/bags/containers; cool at room temperature until solidified (2-3 hours)
5.  **Wrap & seal:** Wrap in cloth or paper; store in airtight containers in cool, dark place

### Storage & Rancidity Detection

-   **Shelf life:** 5-15 years depending on fat choice and storage conditions
-   **Best conditions:** Below 50°F, low humidity, dark location
-   **Rancidity signs:** Bitter/soapy taste, off-smell, white powder on surface (fat oxidation). Discard.
-   **Prevention:** Vacuum seal if possible; limit air contact; consider adding salt (1-2 tsp per pound) for additional preservation

</section>

<section id="jerky-drying">

## Jerky: Safe Drying Temperatures & Times

### Why Temperature Matters

Too low (<130°F) = slow drying, risk of pathogenic bacteria survival. Too high (>180°F) = case hardening (outside dries before inside, trapping moisture). Optimal: 160-175°F.

### Safe Temperature & Time Guidelines

:::card
**Beef Jerky (lean meat, 1/4 inch thick strips):**

-   **Temperature:** 160-170°F
-   **Time:** 4-8 hours (depending on humidity and exact thickness)
-   **Doneness:** Bends slightly but doesn't break; no moisture beads on surface when squeezed
:::

:::card
**Venison/Game (lean):**

-   **Temperature:** 160-165°F (game fat can turn rancid)
-   **Time:** 5-10 hours
-   **Note:** Remove visible fat; fat = rancidity risk
:::

:::card
**Poultry Jerky (chicken/turkey breast, very lean):**

-   **Temperature:** 165-170°F (kill Salmonella)
-   **Time:** 3-6 hours
-   **Doneness:** Should break cleanly, not bend
:::

### Pre-Drying Heat Treatment (Food Safety)

If drying below 165°F or using thin slices, pre-treat meat:

-   **Oven method:** Place marinated meat strips on racks; heat at 160°F for 10 minutes before transitioning to dehydrator
-   **Marinade method:** Use acidic marinade (vinegar + salt) for 6-12 hours; acid reduces pathogen load
-   **Curing method:** Dry-cure with salt (1 tablespoon per pound) for 24 hours before drying

### Water Activity & Storage

-   **Target water activity (Aw):** 0.60 or lower for 6+ month shelf life
-   **How to test:** Properly dried jerky should be completely pliable but NOT moist. No moisture appears when squeezed hard.
-   **If still soft/moist at 8 hours:** Temperature may be too low or humidity too high. Continue drying, checking every 30 minutes.
-   **Storage:** Airtight containers in cool location (below 70°F). With vacuum sealing: 6-12 months unrefrigerated. Without: 2-4 weeks (then refrigerate or freeze).

</section>

## Water Activity Levels for Mold Prevention

### Understanding Water Activity (Aw)

Water activity is the percentage of "free water" available for microbial growth (0 = completely dry, 1.0 = pure water). Mold requires Aw > 0.65 to grow. Foods with Aw < 0.60 are shelf-stable indefinitely if protected from rehydration.

### Target Aw by Food Type

### Simple Aw Testing Without Equipment

-   **Squeezing test:** Properly dried food releases NO liquid when squeezed hard. If moisture beads appear, Aw is too high.
-   **Mold test:** If mold appears during storage, Aw was too high (or moisture got back in).
-   **Texture test:** Jerky should be pliable but not moist. Dried fruit should be chewy. Dried vegetables should be brittle.

### Rehydration Risk (Critical)

Once foods reach target Aw, they're only stable if protected from humidity:

-   **Store in airtight containers:** Glass jars with lids, vacuum-sealed bags, food-grade buckets with gamma seal lids
-   **Use desiccants:** Food-grade silica gel packets, dried lime, salt in center of container (prevents rehydration from air)
-   **Keep cool:** Below 70°F slows any residual microbial activity
-   **Exclude light:** Opaque containers prevent nutrient degradation

<section id="hardtack-mold-resistant">

## Hardtack: Recipe & Mold-Resistant Baking

### What Hardtack Is

Hardtack is a dense, low-moisture, shelf-stable biscuit that lasts 10+ years if kept dry. It's pure carbohydrate (flour + water + salt), extremely low Aw, and resistant to mold and pest infestation. Military and survival standard.

### Hardtack Recipe

:::card
**Basic ratio (yields ~20 biscuits):**

-   2 cups all-purpose flour (or whole wheat for nutrition)
-   3/4 cup water (adjust for consistency)
-   1 tablespoon salt (critical—acts as preservative)
-   Optional: 1 tablespoon sugar (if available)
:::

### Step-by-Step Baking Process

1.  **Mix dough:** Combine flour, salt, and optional sugar. Add water slowly while mixing until stiff dough forms. Do NOT knead excessively—you want a dense texture.
2.  **Roll & cut:** Roll dough 1/2 inch thick on floured surface. Cut into 2-3 inch squares. Prick each biscuit 10-15 times with fork (prevents large air pockets).
3.  **Bake low & slow:**
    -   Temperature: 375°F
    -   Time: 30 minutes per side (flip halfway) OR 45-50 minutes per side for extra hardness
    -   Goal: Biscuits should be rock-hard, not chewy. They'll harden more as they cool.
4.  **Cool completely:** Let rest on racks for 24 hours. They will harden significantly.
5.  **Dry further (optional but recommended):**
    -   Place on racks in 200°F oven with door cracked for 2-3 hours to reduce moisture further
    -   Or air-dry in warm, dry location for 5-7 days

### Storage for Maximum Shelf Life

-   **Target moisture:** <8% (hardtack should be impossible to bite, requires soaking/softening)
-   **Container:** Airtight, food-grade (metal tin, vacuum-sealed bags, sealed bucket)
-   **Desiccants:** Include silica gel packets or lime in container
-   **Conditions:** Cool (below 70°F), dry location, no light exposure
-   **Shelf life:** 10-20+ years if protected from moisture and pests

### Using Hardtack

-   **Soaking method:** Soak in hot water, broth, or coffee for 15-30 minutes until softened
-   **Boiling:** Simmer in water/broth to make a porridge
-   **Cooking:** Fry in fat/oil with salt for hardtack cakes (traditional)
-   **Warning:** Hardtack can break teeth—always soften first. Risk of dental damage is real.

</section>

<section id="smoking">

## Smoking (Cold and Hot)

Smoking preserves through dehydration, antimicrobial smoke compounds, and flavor development. Cold smoking (below 90°F) preserves without cooking. Hot smoking (160-225°F) both cooks and preserves.

### Cold Smoking (Preservation Method)

**Purpose:** Preserve without cooking; fish, cheese, meat can remain raw inside but surface-treated with smoke

**Smokehouse construction:**
- Two chambers: firebox (separate, below ground) and smoking chamber (above ground, insulated)
- Tunnel smoke through 10-15 feet underground to cool it naturally
- Target temperature: 50-90°F (smoke entering chamber, not heat)
- Ventilation: slow smoke circulation (not drafty)
- Duration: 8-24 hours for light smoking, weeks for heavy smoking/complete preservation

**Cold smoking process:**
1. Fire hardwood (oak, hickory, applewood) in separate firebox to produce smoke, not heat
2. Route smoke through tunnel system to cool it
3. Hang food in smoking chamber on racks/hooks (don't let pieces touch)
4. Smoke continuously for target duration (initial 8 hours gives flavor, 24+ hours gives preservation)
5. Product ready when surface is dark/bronzed and feels dry to touch
6. Continue smoking weekly for 4-6 weeks for deep preservation (shelf-stable months without refrigeration)

**Cold-smoked products:**
- Fish (salmon, whitefish): mild smoking 8-16 hours, keeps 2-3 weeks unrefrigerated
- Cheese: 4-8 hours smoking, keeps 2-4 weeks unrefrigerated
- Salt-cured meat (already salted): 8-24 hours smoking, keeps months unrefrigerated

:::warning
**Cold smoking is NOT suitable for beginners—botulism risk with insufficient salt cure.** Cold smoking does not kill harmful bacteria. Only experienced curers with proper salt-curing knowledge should attempt cold smoking without professional guidance. Botulism can develop with inadequate salt concentration. Always combine cold smoking with heavy salt curing (20-25% by weight for 1-2 weeks) before smoking begins.
:::

:::tip
Cold smoking requires consistent cool temperatures (difficult in summer). Best done fall/winter or in cool climates. Requires careful humidity control (too dry = brittle, too damp = mold risk).
:::

### Hot Smoking (Cooking + Preservation)

**Purpose:** Cook and add smoke flavor; moderate preservation (needs refrigeration or further curing)

**Smoker construction:**
- Single chamber or firebox below/adjacent to smoking chamber
- Heat source: smoldering wood fire (hardwoods: oak, hickory, apple, mesquite)
- Temperature control: 160-225°F (use thermometer, adjust fire/vents to maintain)
- Ventilation: moderate airflow keeps smoke moving through

**Hot smoking process:**
1. Prepare meat/fish: brine with salt solution (5-6 hours) or dry-cure (12-24 hours)
2. Rinse and air-dry on racks 1-2 hours (allows smoke ring formation)
3. Set smoker to target temperature (160°F for fish, 225°F for thick meat pieces)
4. Add wood chips to create smoke (soak chips 30 minutes to slow burn)
5. Place food in smoker on racks, don't overcrowd
6. Smoke for 4-12 hours depending on thickness (fish 4-6 hours, whole chickens 6-8 hours, thick meat pieces 8-12 hours)
7. Product done when internal temperature reaches 160°F (meat) or 145°F (fish)
8. Cool in open air, then refrigerate

**Wood selection:**
- **Mild flavors:** Apple, cherry, alder (delicate fish, poultry)
- **Medium flavors:** Hickory, oak, maple (red meat, pork)
- **Strong flavors:** Mesquite, walnut (thick cuts only)
- **Avoid:** Softwoods like pine (resinous, bitter), treated wood (toxic)

:::danger
**Botulism risk with smoking:** Smoking alone does NOT eliminate botulism risk in low-acid foods (fish, meat). Smoking + vacuum sealing without refrigeration is dangerous. Either: (1) refrigerate hot-smoked foods, (2) combine smoking with salt curing for additional preservation, or (3) use pressure canning for maximum safety.
:::

</section>

<section id="fermentation">

## Fermentation (Lacto-fermentation)

Fermentation preserves through production of lactic acid by beneficial bacteria. Creates probiotics, increases digestibility, and stores for months/years. Naturally safe method with minimal botulism risk (acidic environment prevents botulism).

### Fermentation Basics

**Principle:** Lactobacillus bacteria naturally present on vegetables consume sugars and produce lactic acid (pH drops to 4.0), creating preservation environment

**Essential ingredients:**
- Vegetables (cabbage, cucumbers, carrots, etc.)
- Salt (5-7% by weight, kills pathogenic bacteria while allowing lactobacillus to thrive)
- Water (to create brine)
- Time (3-7 days to 3+ weeks for fermentation)

**Fermentation vessel:**
- Glass jars (quart or larger)
- Ceramic crocks (traditional, excellent)
- Avoid: metal containers (salt corrodes), plastic (scratches harbor bacteria)
- Cloth cover (breathable, prevents dust/insects but allows gas exchange)

### Sauerkraut (Fermented Cabbage)

**Ingredients for 1 quart:**
- 1.5 lbs cabbage (green or red)
- 1-1.5 tablespoons salt (start with 1 tablespoon, adjust for taste)

**Process:**
1. Remove outer leaves from cabbage, rinse, chop finely
2. Layer cabbage in jar, sprinkling salt between layers
3. Press cabbage with tamper or fist (release juice, submerge vegetables)
4. Liquid should cover cabbage completely (if not enough, add light salt solution: 1 tablespoon salt per cup water)
5. Weigh vegetables down with cabbage leaf, small jar, or glass weight (keep submerged)
6. Cover with cloth, secure with rubber band (allows gas escape, prevents contamination)
7. Ferment at room temperature (65-75°F ideal, but works 50-85°F)
8. Taste daily starting day 3: ready when flavor is pleasant and tangy (3-10 days typically)
9. Transfer to cold storage (cellar, spring house, refrigerator) when fermented to taste

**Safety indicators:**
- Healthy: White cloudy yeast layer on surface (normal, skim if desired), fizzy when stirred, sour smell
- Spoiled: Pink/orange mold (discard entire batch), rotting smell (discard), slimy texture (discard)

**Shelf life:** Sealed jars in cool location (50-70°F) last 4-12 months. Warmer temperatures shorten life.

### Fermented Pickles (Dill Pickles)

**Ingredients for 1 quart:**
- 1.5 lbs small firm cucumbers (picked <12 hours old)
- 3 tablespoons salt (for 5% brine)
- 2-3 sprigs fresh dill
- 3-4 garlic cloves, crushed
- Spices (peppercorns, mustard seed, optional)
- 2 cups water

**Process:**
1. Pack cucumbers in jar with dill, garlic, spices
2. Prepare brine: dissolve 3 tablespoons salt in 2 cups water
3. Pour brine over cucumbers, submerge completely
4. Weight down vegetables (keep under brine, prevent mold)
5. Cover with cloth, ferment at room temperature
6. Taste after 3 days: ready when flavor is tangy and crisp (3-14 days)
7. Transfer to cool storage when desired flavor achieved

**Doneness test:** Cucumber should be green (not yellow) with firm texture, not mushy

:::warning
**Fermentation safety:** The only real risk is mold on exposed surface. Keep vegetables submerged at all times. Discard only if mold appears on surface. Fermented foods are naturally protective against botulism due to acidity and salt.
:::

</section>

<section id="salt-curing">

## Salt Curing (Dry and Brine Methods)

Salt curing preserves through osmosis (salt draws water out of meat and bacteria, creating inhospitable environment). Combined with smoking and/or drying, creates long-term preservation (months to years).

**Boundary for salted foods:** Questions about salted fish, salted meat, brined vegetables, or whether a salted food can sit in heat/humidity belong here first, then `food-storage-packaging` for final storage. Use `salt-production` only for making or purifying salt itself.

### Dry Curing (Salt Rubbed on Surface)

**Ratio:** 1 tablespoon salt per pound of meat

**Process:**
1. Prepare meat: trim excess fat (fat goes rancid), remove blood
2. Mix salt with optional spices (black pepper, garlic, herbs)
3. Rub salt mixture thoroughly on all surfaces of meat
4. Place in non-metal container, cover with cloth
5. Ferment in cool location (50-65°F): meat releases liquid, sits in its own brine
6. Duration: 1-3 weeks depending on thickness (thin cuts 1-2 weeks, thick cuts 3+ weeks, calculate ~7 days per 2 inches)
7. After curing, rinse briefly, air-dry on racks, then smoke and/or store in cool location

**Products:**
- **Bacon (belly):** 1-2 weeks curing, then smoke 4-6 hours, store cool/refrigerated
- **Hams (large cuts):** 3-4 weeks curing, then smoke and/or hang to cure further
- **Prosciutto-style ham:** 3-4 weeks dry cure, then hang in cool dry location for 3-12 months (no smoking)

### Brine Curing (Wet Cure)

**Basic brine ratio:** 5% salt solution (5 tablespoons salt per cup water)

**Process:**
1. Prepare meat, cut into manageable pieces
2. Make brine: boil water, dissolve salt, cool completely before using
3. Submerge meat completely in brine
4. Weight down meat (keep submerged, prevent mold)
5. Ferment in cool location (50-65°F) for 1-3 weeks
6. Salt penetrates center, meat develops cured taste
7. Remove from brine, air-dry, then smoke/dry or store in cool location

**Products:**
- **Corned beef:** 1-2 weeks brine cure, then boil before use
- **Salt pork:** 1-3 weeks brine, used as fat/seasoning in cooking
- **Preserved fish:** 1-2 weeks brine, then air-dry (shelf-stable weeks to months)

**Hot/humid caution for preserved fish:** Shelf-stable salted fish requires a complete cure plus drying and usually smoke or very dry storage. In a hot humid room, even salted fish can absorb moisture; keep it wrapped against pests but able to stay dry, move it to the coolest/driest place available, and discard or assess if it becomes damp, soft, moldy, sour, or putrid.

:::danger
**Nitrate/Nitrite Safety:** Traditional curing used saltpeter (potassium nitrate, KNO₃). Modern curing may use sodium nitrite (NaNO₂). Both are toxic at high doses but effective in tiny amounts (100-200 ppm). Use only if:
1. You understand precise measurement (weigh carefully, never estimate)
2. You follow tested recipes with specific nitrite amounts
3. You do NOT exceed recommended levels

Never use sodium/potassium nitrite without specific tested recipes. Overdose causes poisoning/death. Many traditional preserved meats were safe due to additional smoking and drying, not just nitrite. If uncertain, skip nitrite and rely on salt + smoking + drying + cool storage.
:::

## Root Cellaring (Cool Storage)

Root cellaring preserves through cool temperature (slows respiration and microbial growth) and humidity control. Creates long-term storage (months) for root vegetables, apples, potatoes, and hardy crops.

### Root Cellar Construction

**Requirements:**
- Temperature: 50-60°F (ideally 50-55°F for root vegetables, 55-60°F for fruit)
- Humidity: 85-95% (high humidity prevents drying but prevents mold)
- Darkness: no light (prevents sprouting of potatoes, greening of tubers)
- Ventilation: passive air exchange (prevents stale air and gas buildup)
- Location: below ground or well-insulated

**Simple root cellar options:**
1. Underground pit: Dig 4-6 feet deep, cover with insulated lid, natural temperature regulation
2. Basement corner: Insulate exterior walls, use passive vents to keep cool
3. Spring house: Small structure built over cool spring or groundwater source (naturally cool)
4. Buried container: Food-grade barrel or box buried in earth, topped with insulated lid
5. Shallow cellar: 2-3 feet deep in cool location, covered with straw, earth, and wooden roof

**Ventilation strategy:**
- Two vents: intake (low, outside) and exhaust (high, inside) create natural convection
- Vents should have dampers to close in winter (prevent freezing)
- Summer dampers open to exchange warm air for cool night air

### Storage Life by Crop

| Crop | Temperature | Humidity | Preparation | Storage Life |
|------|-------------|----------|-------------|--------------|
| **Potatoes** | 50-55°F | 90-95% | Cure 2 weeks at 65°F first (hardens skin) | 4-6 months |
| **Carrots** | 50-55°F | 95% | Remove tops, brush dirt, layer in sand | 4-6 months |
| **Beets** | 50-55°F | 95% | Remove tops, brush dirt | 3-4 months |
| **Turnips/Rutabagas** | 50-55°F | 90-95% | Remove tops, brush dirt | 4-6 months |
| **Cabbage** | 50-55°F | 90-95% | Leave roots on, store on slats (air circulation) | 3-4 months |
| **Apples** | 55-60°F | 85-90% | Keep cool, separate ethylene producers | 2-6 months |
| **Onions/Garlic** | 50-55°F (onions), 60-65°F (garlic) | 65-75% (drier) | Cure 2-3 weeks, braid or store in mesh | 4-8 months |
| **Winter squash/pumpkin** | 55-60°F | 50-70% | Cure 2 weeks, store on slats | 3-6 months |

### Storage Tips

- **Ethylene producers separate:** Apples and pears produce ethylene gas, ripen and rot nearby vegetables. Store separately or different shelves.
- **Layer in sand/peat:** For root vegetables, alternating sand layers prevent touching (reduces disease transmission).
- **Inspect regularly:** Remove any rotting specimens immediately (prevents spread).
- **Monitor temperature/humidity:** Check weekly, adjust vents as needed.

## Evaporative Cooling (Passive Cooling, No Ice)

In hot, dry climates without electricity or ice access, evaporative cooling passively reduces temperature through water evaporation.

### Clay Pot Cooler (Zeer Pot)

**Construction (very simple):**
1. Take two clay pots of different sizes (large outer pot, small inner pot)
2. Place small pot inside large pot, seal bottom gap with sand or clay
3. Fill gap between pots with wet sand, wet cloth, or wet straw
4. Cover gap area with wet cloth (keep damp by adding water regularly)
5. Place vegetables/food in inner pot, cover with cloth
6. Evaporating water from sand/cloth cools interior pot to 20-30°F cooler than ambient air

**Effectiveness:** Can keep produce 20-30°F cooler than outside temperature (50-60°F in 80°F weather). Works best in dry, windy climates.

**Maintenance:** Keep sand/cloth wet by adding water every 1-2 days.

### Underground Cooling

**Simple ground pit:**
1. Dig pit in shaded location, 2-3 feet deep
2. Line with straw, blankets, or insulation
3. Partially bury clay pot coolers or sealed containers
4. Natural ground temperature maintains cool even in summer (50-60°F below surface in many climates)
