# Anti-Counterfeit Currency & Minting

Currency only functions as a store of value and medium of exchange if people trust it is genuine and stable. Counterfeiting—creating fake currency—destroys trust and causes inflation and economic collapse. This guide provides practical techniques for producing physically unforgeable currency and detecting fakes, suitable for communities establishing formal monetary systems.

## Why Physical Anti-Counterfeiting Matters

<section id="importance">

In collapse contexts, physical currencies are more practical than digital ones (no computers, no internet). Physical currencies are vulnerable to counterfeiting in ways pre-collapse digital currencies were not. A counterfeiter can:

- **Inflate money supply**: Make fake coins/notes and spend them, increasing total currency without increasing goods. Result: prices rise, purchasing power falls, inflation harms savers and those on fixed income.
- **Undermine trust**: If people suspect currency might be fake, they demand discounts or refuse to accept it. Transactions slow. Economy fragments to barter.
- **Enable fraud**: Counterfeiter steals purchasing power from legitimate coin holders.
- **Reward criminals**: If counterfeiting is profitable, more people attempt it, expanding the problem.

Effective anti-counterfeiting creates cost barrier: making fake coins is harder and more expensive than legitimate earnings. This deters most counterfeiters.

:::info-box
**Historical Currency Enforcement**

Pre-modern societies that survived economically (Roman Empire, medieval kingdoms, Ming Dynasty) had severe penalties for counterfeiting: mutilation, execution, public shaming. These were not cruel for cruelty's sake; they were necessary to preserve currency integrity.

Modern counterfeiting carries severe penalties (10–20 years prison) for the same reason: counterfeiting is a form of theft that destabilizes the entire economy. A community enforcing this penalty is protecting everyone.
:::

</section>

## Metal Coin Production

<section id="metal-coins">

Metal coins (bronze, brass, copper, or silver) are the hardest currency to counterfeit. Striking coins creates unique toolmarks that are difficult to replicate without original dies.

**Alloy Selection**

**Bronze (90% copper, 10% tin)**: Strong, corrosion-resistant, historically standard for coins. Requires access to tin (less common than copper). Melting point: 1085°C.

**Brass (70% copper, 30% zinc)**: Slightly softer, more corrosion-prone, but adequate. Easier to obtain (copper + zinc are both fairly common). Melting point: 900°C. More common in post-collapse contexts.

**Copper**: Pure copper is soft and wears easily, but functional for low-value coins. Melting point: 1085°C.

**Silver**: Precious, high intrinsic value, very corrosion-resistant. Use only for high-denomination coins (10+ day-labor equivalent value). Melting point: 962°C.

**Recommended**: Start with copper or brass coins for small denominations (1-labor-credit value) and mixed alloys for larger denominations.

**Die Carving**

Coins are struck with dies (metal stamps with reverse image carved into them). Each strike of the die onto coin blank creates a unique pattern of tool marks and pressure. Counterfeiter must either:
1. Obtain original dies (theft, difficult to defend against)
2. Carve new dies (requires high skill, time, and tools; dies must precisely match originals or coins look obviously fake)
3. Cast coins using molds (results in visible casting seams, uneven surface; easier to detect)

**Die design principles**:

- **Complex design**: Include denomination (numeral), community symbol (seal, animal, tree), date (year), and master's mark (initials or unique symbol). More detail = harder to replicate.
- **Relief depth**: Carve dies so design stands 2–3 mm above coin surface. Shallow designs are easier to counterfeit; deep designs require precise pressure and proper dies.
- **Unique toolmarks**: Use chisels and burins (pointed engraving tools) to carve dies. Each die has unique toolmarks (tiny scratches, slight variations) from carving. Coins struck with different dies show different patterns. This is difficult for counterfeiter to replicate exactly.
- **Edge lettering (reeding)**: Carve a pattern on coin edge (grooves or letters). Counterfeiter must replicate this on blanks, adding cost and difficulty.

**Die production process**:

1. **Material selection**: Use hardened steel or iron (harder than coin metal). A softer die wears out after 50,000–100,000 strikes. A hardened die lasts 500,000+ strikes.

2. **Blank creation**: Forge a blank (small cylindrical steel bar, ~2 cm diameter, 1 cm height) from scrap or mined metal. Heat-treat to harden.

3. **Design layout**: Transfer design to die surface (scribe into surface, or use chalk to draw). High-precision layout is essential; misalignment makes coins look wrong.

4. **Die carving**: Use burins, chisels, and engraving tools to carefully carve design into die surface. This requires skilled metalsmith; carving takes 20–40 hours per die. Mirror image (dies strike from above and below, so the carved image on die must be reversed).

5. **Hardening**: Heat die to cherry-red and quench in oil. This hardens steel and allows dies to resist repeated striking.

6. **Quality check**: Test die on a coin blank. Check for alignment, depth, and clarity. Touch up any shallow areas.

**Die production cost**: 100–200 day-labor-equivalent per die, plus specialized tools. A currency system needs multiple dies per denomination (to rotate dies and reduce wear wear, and to prevent single-die counterfeiting). Budget 500+ day-labor-equivalent for a complete die set (10–20 dies for multiple denominations).

**Coin Striking Process**

**Setup**:
1. Heat coin blank to ~200°C (warm, not hot; makes metal slightly plastic and easier to strike without cracking).
2. Place blank on anvil or striking surface, centered under top die.
3. Align top die directly above blank.
4. Have ready: hammer (4–8 lb), tongs to hold blank, die holder if available.

**Striking**:
1. Hold blank in place with tongs or clamp.
2. Position top die carefully (use guide or die holder for alignment).
3. Strike die firmly with hammer (single, hard blow is better than multiple lighter ones).
4. Lift die; remove coin; inspect.
5. Repeat for each blank.

**Result**: Each coin has a unique impression of both dies (top and bottom). Variation in pressure, alignment, and blank shape means no two coins are exactly identical—this is feature, not bug. Counterfeiter striking with replicated dies produces coins that look slightly wrong (misaligned, shallow impression, visible die differences) compared to genuine coins.

**Typical production rate**: Skilled striker can produce 100–200 coins/day (accounting for setup, die rotation for wear management, and quality inspection).

:::warning
**Die Wear & Replacement**

Dies wear down with striking. After ~100,000 strikes, impression becomes shallow and unclear. Worn coins are easy to spot (faint details).

**Protocol**:
- Inspect dies weekly. Look for flattening of fine details.
- Mark each die with strike count (tally marks on die shaft).
- When approaching 100,000 strikes, reduce use of die; rotate to backup die.
- Retire worn die and carve replacement.

Worn dies that are still functional can be sold at discount or melted down. Never spend worn coins as if they were new; this damages currency credibility.
:::

**Edge Reeding & Lettering**

To prevent coin shaving (scraping metal from edge to reduce weight while passing coin at full value), add visible pattern to edge.

**Reeding method**: After striking, place coin in a vice. Use a graver (pointed engraving tool) or chisel to cut parallel grooves around edge. Takes 1–2 minutes per coin but deters shaving effectively.

**Lettering method**: Strike edge with a separate die (edge die) that has lettering or pattern carved into it. This is more professional but requires additional tool.

**Simpler alternative**: Make coin thinner and smaller (reduces metal content, so shaving gains nothing). Modern small coins are partly theft-resistant by this design.

</section>

## Paper Currency Production

<section id="paper-currency">

Paper currency is faster to produce than coins but more vulnerable to counterfeiting (easier to print fakes). Use paper currency primarily for smaller transactions; back largest denominations with metal coins or commodity backing.

**Rag Paper Production**

High-quality currency paper is made from cotton or linen rags (not wood pulp, which is weaker and degrades faster).

**Process**:

1. **Rag collection**: Gather worn cloth (old shirts, sheets, linen scraps). Must be clean.

2. **Cutting & shredding**: Cut rags into small pieces (1 cm × 1 cm). Soak in water for 24 hours to soften.

3. **Beating**: Pound rags with wooden mallets or stones on a flat surface for 2–4 hours until fibers separate into pulp. This is labor-intensive; mechanical beater (water-powered mill) is much faster.

4. **Pulping**: Mix pulp with water to form slurry (consistency of thick soup).

5. **Sheet formation**: Dip a wire screen (mould) into pulp slurry. Lift screen; water drains through wires. Fibers remain on screen forming sheet. Transfer sheet to felt blanket.

6. **Pressing**: Stack felt blankets with sheets between them. Press with weights or screw press for 1–2 hours to remove water.

7. **Drying**: Hang sheets on line or lay on board to air-dry (3–7 days depending on humidity).

8. **Sizing**: Optional but improves quality. Soak dried sheets in weak gelatin solution to fill gaps between fibers. This makes paper stronger and prevents ink from soaking through.

**Result**: Handmade paper that is strong, durable, and slightly irregular (irregularity is feature; makes it harder to replicate exactly).

**Production rate**: 50–100 sheets/day with one person working. Sheets are ~8" × 10" (standard currency size).

**Embedded Fiber Patterns**

Before sheet formation, add colored fibers (dyed cotton threads, bits of colored paper, silk threads) to pulp. These create visible patterns throughout the paper.

**Technique**:
1. Cut thin threads (colored cotton, silk) into short lengths (~1 cm).
2. Mix threads into pulp mixture before sheet formation.
3. When sheet forms, threads are embedded throughout paper.
4. Threads are visible when holding paper to light—difficult to replicate without same colored threads and embedding process.

**Alternatives to threads**: Add small mica flakes (glitter-like reflective particles) or colored inks mixed into pulp. These create patterns that shift depending on viewing angle.

**Watermark Creation**

Watermarks are images visible when paper is held to light. Created by wire forms that displace fibers during sheet formation.

**DIY watermark process**:

1. **Wire form creation**: Bend wire (copper or steel) into desired shape (denomination numeral, community symbol, seal). Solder or tie wires to form 3D shape.

2. **Mould modification**: Attach wire form to underside of the wire screen (mould). Form sits ~5 mm below screen surface.

3. **Sheet formation**: When pulp is dipped into mould, fibers pile up around and over the wire form. Fibers are thinner directly above the form (water drains more easily where wire displaces water).

4. **Result**: Finished sheet has thin area (watermark) where fewer fibers accumulated. Watermark is visible by backlighting.

**Challenge**: Creating watermark requires precise wire work and integration into mould. Skill and time-intensive. A counterfeiter attempting to replicate watermark must custom-build modified moulds, which is possible but adds cost and time.

**Professional watermark** (if available): Salvage pre-collapse currency (paper bills) and use as reference. Examine watermark under light and document its characteristics.

**Multi-Layer Printing**

Print currency using multiple color inks, each layer added separately.

**Process**:

1. **Base print**: First layer prints denomination, community seal, and security features in black ink. Use handmade blocks or simple woodcut.

2. **Color overlay**: Additional layers in different colors (red, blue, green) print portraits, dates, or decorative patterns. Each color requires separate carving/plate.

3. **Final print**: Optional third color or additional detail.

**Anti-counterfeiting effect**: Different layers of ink are difficult to replicate with simple copying. Counterfeiter must:
- Obtain same paper (rare/traceable).
- Create multiple carved plates (time-consuming).
- Register (align) multiple prints precisely on single sheet (requires skill).
- Replicate inks exactly (color matching is difficult without lab).

**Production method**:

*Woodcut blocks*: Carve design into wooden block; ink with pigmented oil; press onto paper. Requires one block per color.

*Printing press* (if salvageable): Cast metal type and plates for text and images. Use lever press to apply pressure. Much faster (200+ sheets/day) than hand-printing, but requires equipment and expertise.

*Stamps*: Carve design into rubber or metal; stamp onto paper. Crude but functional for simple designs.

**Serial Numbering**

Each bill gets unique serial number printed (e.g., "AC-001234"). This allows:
- Tracking which bills are in circulation
- Identifying counterfeits (numbering format reveals fakes quickly)
- Removing withdrawn currency (numbered bills can be recalled)

**Serial number assignment**:

1. Assign ranges: Batch 1: AC-000001 to AC-010000; Batch 2: AC-010001 to AC-020000.

2. Print: Include serial number on every bill during printing (add as final step). Alternate numbering between bills helps prevent sequential counterfeiting.

3. Register: Maintain ledger of printed bills: Batch number, range, date printed, signature of printer. Store ledger in secure location.

4. Detection: Any bill with serial number outside the registered ranges, or with duplicate number, is counterfeit.

**High-Security Features** (if paper currency is high-value)

- **Holographic or color-shifting inks**: Reflect different colors at different angles. Difficult to replicate without specialized equipment. Not feasible for post-collapse communities, but mention for reference.

- **Tactile features**: Raised printing (intaglio) creates a raised image felt with fingers. Requires engraving tools and printing press; difficult to counterfeit.

- **Microprinting**: Text so small it's invisible without magnifying glass. Detected by authorities examining bills. Requires precision printing equipment.

- **Inert particles**: Magnetic particles or fluorescent fibers visible under UV light. Difficult to replicate; easily verified by currency inspectors.

For small communities, focus on: embedded threads, watermarks, multi-color printing, and serial numbering. These provide adequate security against casual counterfeiting.

:::info-box
**Paper Currency Lifespan**

Handmade rag paper currency lasts 5–10 years in circulation before wearing out (creasing, ink fading, paper weakening). After ~20 years, bill should be removed and destroyed (to prevent counterfeiter collecting old bills, reinking them, and passing off as new).

**Replacement protocol**:
- Print small quantity of new currency each year (3–5% of total money supply).
- After ~20 years, announce withdrawal of old bills (30-day grace period to exchange at bank/authority).
- Collected old bills are destroyed (burned or pulped).
- This prevents accumulation of degraded/faded bills.
:::

</section>

## Alternative Currencies: Tokens & Tallies

<section id="tokens">

Where paper/metal currency is not feasible, communities use tokens or tally systems.

**Ceramic Tokens**

Simple clay tokens (pressed with denomination mark) can serve as currency.

**Production**:
1. Make clay discs (2–3 cm diameter, 3–5 mm thick).
2. Impress denomination into surface (e.g., "5" for 5 day-labor-equivalent value).
3. Add community seal or mark (pressed with unique stamp).
4. Dry completely (3–7 days).
5. Fire in kiln (or oven) to harden.

**Advantages**: Easy to make, durable, difficult to counterfeit (signature style of potter marks the tokens).

**Disadvantages**: Heavy (difficult to transport large sums), fragile (chips, breaks, disqualify token).

**Use case**: Small communities or low-value transactions; not suitable for large trade networks.

**Wooden Tallies**

Wooden sticks notched to represent value. Historical standard in medieval markets.

**Design**:
1. Cut hazel or oak stick to ~20 cm length, pencil-thick.
2. Make notches on one side: each notch = 1 day-labor value (or custom denomination).
3. Carve community seal or mark on stick (to prevent forgery).
4. Split stick lengthwise (tally stick splits into two halves exactly matched).
5. Each party (creditor and debtor) holds one half; they match when reconciling.

**Advantages**: Quick to produce, portable, split design prevents forgery (counterfeiter must create perfectly matched halves).

**Disadvantages**: Notching takes time; cannot store large sums easily (too many sticks); no standardized denominations (each tally is unique).

**Use case**: Short-term credit (debt acknowledgment). Two parties agree: creditor holds one tally half, debtor holds other. At settlement, they match halves; if they fit exactly, credit is valid.

**Historical note**: Tally sticks were official currency and credit instruments in medieval England and Europe, used for 1000+ years.

</section>

## Ledger-Based Currency (Commodity-Backed)

<section id="ledger-currency">

An alternative to physical currency: ledger entries that represent claims on commodity (grain, labor, ore, etc.).

**System overview**:

A central authority (bank, council, priest) maintains a ledger. Each person has an account. Account balance represents claim on community's commodity stores.

*Example*: Smith works 10 days in communal fields. Ledger records: "Smith, +10 day-labor credits." Smith then trades 3 credits for 50 kg grain. Ledger records: "Smith, +7 day-labor credits" and "Grain store, -50 kg." Later, Smith accumulates credits and exchanges them for land lease, apprenticeship fee, or emergency support.

**Advantages**:
- No physical counterfeiting (ledger is centralized; difficult to falsify).
- Flexible (easy to update, track partial transactions).
- Scalable (works for large communities).

**Disadvantages**:
- Requires literate, trustworthy record-keeper.
- Vulnerability to ledger fraud (corrupt record-keeper falsifies entries).
- Less portable (must trust authority to verify balance; cannot carry currency).

**Anti-fraud measures**:

1. **Dual signatures**: Every entry requires signatures of two people (record-keeper + witness). Changes require both signatures.

2. **Regular audits**: Quarterly, a neutral auditor (elder, community member, priest) reviews ledger. Checks arithmetic, verifies entries against receipts, signs off that ledger is accurate.

3. **Public postings**: Monthly summary of ledger (total accounts, total credits outstanding) is posted publicly. Community can verify totals match their experience.

4. **Certified copies**: Critical entries (large transfers, account openings) are witnessed by 2+ people and copies are distributed.

5. **Ledger backups**: Maintain two copies of ledger, kept in separate locations. If one copy is destroyed or falsified, the other serves as reference.

**Certified Ledger Entries**

For high-value transactions, create a certificate (written document) that records the transaction and serves as proof.

**Format**:
```
[Date: 15 March 2027]
CERTIFICATE OF CREDIT

I, [Authority name], certify that [Person A] has deposited
50 day-labor credits with the community. This certificate
represents claim on the community's stores.

Value: 50 day-labor credits
Holder: John Smith
Serial number: CC-2027-00147
Expiration: 15 March 2037 (10-year redemption period)

Witnessed by: Mary Council, Elder
              Thomas Priest, Witness

Signature: _______________ [Authority]
Date: 15 March 2027
```

**Certificate features**:
- Serial number (prevents duplicates)
- Expiration date (forces redemption, prevents perpetual claims)
- Witness signatures (difficult to forge without obtaining witnesses)
- Clear denomination (no ambiguity)
- Authority signature (authority stands behind certificate)

**Anti-counterfeiting**:
- Use special paper (watermarked if possible, or distinctive color).
- Include unique visual element (seal, wax stamp, fingerprint of authority).
- Serial numbers are sequential; gaps are obvious fakes.
- Certificates are recorded in master ledger; presented certificates must match recorded entries.

**Redemption protocol**:
When holder wants to redeem certificate, they present it to authority. Authority:
1. Checks serial number against master ledger (certificate must be registered).
2. Verifies signatures (compares against known signatures of witnesses).
3. Checks expiration (certificate must not be expired).
4. Records redemption in ledger (marks certificate as redeemed, date noted).
5. Destroys certificate (burns or files permanently).

This prevents double-spending (certificate cannot be presented twice) and forgery (records prevent unknown serial numbers from being redeemed).

</section>

## Anti-Counterfeiting Features Ranked by Difficulty

<section id="anti-counterfeiting-features">

When designing currency, layer multiple anti-counterfeiting features. This table ranks features by difficulty for counterfeiter to replicate:

| Difficulty | Feature | How It Works | Cost to Replicate |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Easy** | Design complexity | Complex design is harder to remember and replicate by hand. | Low (skilled craftsperson can attempt carving dies). |
| **Easy** | Community seal | Unique symbol (seal, animal) that is distinctive and familiar to community. | Low (if seal is publicly known). |
| **Moderate** | Edge reeding | Grooves or lettering on coin edge prevent shaving. Requires separate tool/die. | Moderate (reeding die, edge striking setup). |
| **Moderate** | Multi-color printing | Overlapping colors require multiple plates/blocks and precise registration. | Moderate (carving multiple plates, alignment challenges). |
| **Moderate** | Serial numbering | Each bill has unique number. Missing/duplicate numbers are obvious fakes. | Moderate (requires printing capability but detectable easily). |
| **Moderate** | Unique toolmarks on dies | Each die has subtle toolmark variations from hand-carving. Counterfeiter dies look slightly different. | Moderate (skilled die carver could mimic, but time-intensive). |
| **Hard** | Watermarked paper | Wire-form watermarks embedded in sheet. Visible only under backlighting. | Hard (requires custom wire work and papermaking equipment; easily detected by community). |
| **Hard** | Embedded fibers | Colored threads or flakes mixed into paper. Visible in cross-section; difficult to replicate without same materials. | Hard (requires same materials, paper-making knowledge). |
| **Hard** | Wax seals | Each certificate stamped with unique wax seal (authority's sigil). Wax is difficult to replicate without original seal matrix. | Hard (requires original seal or near-perfect copy; easy to detect variations). |
| **Very Hard** | Ledger-based system | Centralized record of all transactions. Counterfeiter must forge entire ledger and witness signatures to create false accounts. | Very hard (requires access to records, forging multiple signatures, covering tracks). |

**Recommended anti-counterfeiting package for small community currency**:

*For metal coins*:
- Denomination + community seal + date cast into die.
- Edge reeding or lettering.
- Multiple dies (rotate use) creating subtle die variations.

*For paper notes*:
- Embedded fiber threads (colored cotton, silk).
- Watermark (if papermaking capability exists).
- Multi-color printing (2–3 colors).
- Serial numbering with ledger verification.

*For certificates*:
- Watermarked or distinctive paper.
- Unique seal (wax + matrix).
- Serial numbering.
- Witness signatures.
- Recorded in master ledger.

This combination makes counterfeiting difficult without being prohibitively expensive to implement.

</section>

## Detecting Counterfeits

<section id="detecting-counterfeits">

Communities need methods to quickly identify fake currency in circulation.

**Coin Inspection**

**Visual inspection**:
- Compare suspect coin to known-genuine coin side-by-side.
- Check denomination is clearly visible.
- Check for alignment of dies (genuine coins struck cleanly; fake cast coins may be misaligned or off-center).
- Look for casting seams (if coin was cast rather than struck, seam is visible around the edge).
- Check reeding on edge (genuine coins have consistent grooves; fakes may be rough or inconsistent).

**Weight testing**:
- Weigh suspect coin on balance scale (compare to genuine coin).
- Counterfeiter sometimes uses cheaper alloy (lighter) or saves metal by casting thinner.
- A coin that weighs noticeably less (5%+ difference) is likely counterfeit.

**Specific gravity test** (more sophisticated):
- Weigh coin on balance (in air).
- Submerge coin in water; note volume displaced.
- Compute specific gravity: weight ÷ volume.
- Bronze has specific gravity ~8.7; brass ~8.4; copper ~9.0.
- If specific gravity is far outside range (e.g., 5.0 = mostly tin; 6.0 = partially hollow), coin is counterfeit.

**Acid test** (for precious metals):
- Obtain sample of known-genuine coin alloy (from authority).
- Test suspect coin with weak acid (vinegar, lemon juice, or commercial acid test kit if available).
- Acid reacts differently with pure metal vs. alloys; result can be visually compared to reference.
- This requires some chemistry knowledge but is reliable.

**Paper Currency Inspection**

**Visual inspection**:
- Hold bill to light; look for watermark. Genuine bill has clear watermark; counterfeit (printed or cast) does not.
- Feel paper texture; rag paper has distinctive crispness and texture. Wood-pulp counterfeits feel softer, fluffier.
- Examine embedded fibers; look for colored threads. If absent, bill is likely fake.
- Check serial number against ledger (or community list of valid ranges). Unknown serial numbers are counterfeit.

**Color inspection**:
- Genuine multi-color bills have colors in specific positions. Examine if colors are aligned properly.
- Counterfeit printed with misaligned plates show color fringing or overlap errors.
- Check if ink is uniform density (genuine bills have even ink); fake bills may show lighter/darker patches (sign of amateur printing).

**Tactile inspection**:
- Feel bill's edges; genuine bills have clean edges. Counterfeit (especially hand-made or cut) may have rough or uneven edges.
- Feel printed surface; intaglio (raised printing) feels slightly bumpy. Flat counterfeit printing feels smooth.

**Verification by authority**:
When large sum is exchanged, authority can verify:
- Check serial number in master ledger.
- Inspect under magnifying glass for watermark detail.
- Test suspected counterfeits by soaking in water; rag paper holds together; wood-pulp pulps quickly.

**Certificate Inspection**

**Serial number check**:
- Look up serial number in master ledger. If not registered, certificate is counterfeit.

**Signature verification**:
- Compare signatures of authority and witnesses against known samples (kept in secure location or public posting).
- Forged signatures are often slightly different (pressure, angle, letter formation).

**Seal inspection**:
- Examine wax seal closely. Genuine seal has distinctive imprint of seal matrix (unique pattern).
- Counterfeited seal appears smooth or has slightly wrong pattern (counterfeiter's die is imperfect copy).
- If seal is damaged or appears re-sealed (wax is layered, different ages), certificate is suspect.

**Paper inspection**:
- Feel distinctive texture of certificate paper (if special paper is used).
- Hold to light; check for watermark.

:::tip
**Training Currency Inspectors**

Communities should designate and train 2–3 people as "currency inspectors" with authority to:
- Test questionable currency.
- Seize counterfeits (compensation paid to holder if innocent).
- Report counterfeiting attempts to community council/courts.
- Maintain reference samples of genuine currency for comparison.
- Issue "clear" certificates for large sums (authority stamps bills as verified genuine).

Inspectors need training in: visual inspection, weight/scale operation, acid test procedures, ledger lookup, documentation.
:::

</section>

## Establishing a Mint

<section id="establishing-mint">

A formal mint (currency production facility) provides infrastructure for managing currency supply and preventing counterfeiting.

**Mint location & security**

- **Physical location**: Secure building with limited access (walls, lockable doors, guards if needed).
- **Inside**: Furnaces/equipment for metal work and/or printing; die storage; finished currency storage; ledgers and records.
- **Security measures**: Only authorized personnel enter. All materials tracked. Finished currency stored in locked chest. At least 2 people present during production (witness against theft/fraud).

**Mint personnel**

- **Master of the Mint**: Oversees all operations, reports to council or authority. Responsible for quality, security, and accountability.
- **Die carvers**: Skilled craftspeople who carve dies for currency. Typically 2–3 people.
- **Strikers**: Craftspeople who strike coins. Typically 2–4 people (one produces, one witnesses, one records, rotation allows for rest).
- **Paper-makers** (if paper currency): Produce rag paper, embed watermarks, print.
- **Printers** (if printing capability): Print multi-color designs using woodcuts, engraving, or presses.
- **Record-keepers**: Maintain ledger of production (amount produced, dies used, quality checks, destroyed currency, distribution records).
- **Guards**: Secure the mint, prevent theft, supervise access.

**Governance & Transparency**

- **Authority oversight**: Council or regional government has right to inspect mint, verify production, audit records.
- **Regular audits**: Independent auditor (trusted community member) inspects mint monthly. Checks: actual currency against recorded production, dies against inventory, destroyed currency against records.
- **Public transparency**: Quarterly public report on currency production (total coins/notes produced, destroyed, circulating estimated supply).
- **Seal integrity**: Master of Mint adds their seal/mark to all official currency. Seal is unique and difficult to replicate, giving currency authority's guarantee of authenticity.

**Quality Control**

- **Inspection of production**: Every 50 coins, inspect for defects. Misaligned strikes, shallow impressions, or surface defects are segregated and melted down (not released).
- **Weight sampling**: Weekly, select random 10 coins from production. Weigh them. If average is within tolerance (2%), production is sound. If drift is observed, adjust dies or material.
- **Ledger reconciliation**: Daily, record-keeper counts produced currency and verifies against counter/weight. This prevents theft and detects production errors.

**Distribution & Supply Management**

- **Controlled release**: Mint does not spend currency itself; it releases currency to authority (council, bank, or central treasury) who distributes it into circulation.
- **Supply targets**: Council sets target for total currency in circulation (e.g., "5000 day-labor-equivalents total"). Mint produces to meet targets, replacing worn currency and expanding supply as economy grows.
- **Withdrawal of old currency**: As currency wears or reaches end-of-life, it is presented to authority, exchanged for new currency, and destroyed (melted or burned).

:::info-box
**Mint Production Costs vs. Seigniorage**

Pre-collapse governments earned "seigniorage"—the difference between the cost to produce currency and its face value.

Example: A coin costs 2 cents in metal to produce and stamp, but has face value of $1. Government keeps the $0.98 difference.

Post-collapse mints can do the same:
- Cost to produce 1 day-labor-equivalent coin in bronze: ~0.1 day-labor-equivalent (materials + labor).
- Revenue: 1 day-labor-equivalent (face value).
- Seigniorage: ~0.9 day-labor-equivalent profit per coin produced.

This profit funds the mint operations and provides revenue to community treasury. Important: do not abuse this by producing excessive currency (causes inflation). Produce only to meet reasonable supply targets.
:::

</section>

## Denomination Systems & Exchange Rates

<section id="denomination-systems">

Design denomination system that matches community economy.

**Recommended denominations** (for day-labor-equivalent currency):

- **1 unit** (1 day-labor): Copper coin, smallest denomination. Use for daily small transactions (bread, tool repair).
- **5 unit**: Bronze or copper coin. Use for mid-size transactions (tool purchase, small craft).
- **25 unit**: Silver coin or high-value bronze. Use for major purchases (livestock, land lease, apprenticeship).
- **100 unit**: Silver or certified ledger entry. Use for very large transactions (business, property). Few in circulation.

**Rationale**: Denominations are in ratio 1:5:25:100. This allows most transactions with 2–3 coins/notes. Larger denominations exist but see little use (people prefer smaller denominations). System is scalable (if economy inflates, add higher denominations; if deflates, add smaller ones).

**Exchange rate framework**

Currency value is grounded in an external commodity: labor, grain, metal, or other stable good.

**Labor-based** (most common in post-collapse): 1 day-labor-equivalent = 1 unit currency. Labor value is what a typical adult can accomplish in a day (food production, construction, crafting, etc.).

**Grain-based**: 1 unit = 1 kg grain (or regional equivalent). Grain is stable, essential, and produced regularly. Easy for farmers to verify value.

**Metal-based**: 1 unit = 1 gram silver (or copper equivalent). Metals are valuable, portable, and durable. Requires regular revaluation if metal prices fluctuate.

**Multi-backed currency** (most robust): Currency is officially backed by a basket (e.g., 50% labor, 30% grain, 20% metal). This reduces dependence on any single commodity and stabilizes value.

**Maintaining stable exchange rate**

- **No currency debasement**: Never reduce metal content or add cheaper filler to coins (except when explicitly changing denominations). Debasement destroys trust.
- **Control supply**: Limit currency production to balance inflation. If supply grows faster than goods, inflation results.
- **Regular revaluation**: Quarterly or annually, assess if currency value is stable. If prices are rising (inflation) or falling (deflation), community council adjusts production targets for next year.
- **Commodity backing verification**: Annually, verify that sufficient commodity exists to back currency. If 1000 units of currency are in circulation and currency is labor-backed, verify that community has capacity to deliver 1000 days of labor if all currency were returned.

</section>

## Managing Currency Supply

<section id="managing-supply">

Inflation (too much currency relative to goods) is the primary economic threat in post-collapse communities. Manage supply carefully.

**Measuring inflation**

- **Price tracking**: Monthly, record prices of staple goods (grain, salt, fish, tools, labor).
- **Inflation calculation**: If grain price rises 20% over 12 months, inflation is 20%.
- **Cause identification**: Is supply shortage (bad harvest, disaster) or currency expansion (too much production)?

**Inflation control mechanisms**

**If inflation is from currency over-supply**:
- Reduce mint production (produce fewer coins/notes).
- Increase withdrawal of old currency (accelerate replacement cycle).
- Raise "tax rate" (ask people to return some currency to community, reducing supply).

**If inflation is from goods shortage**:
- Increase production of goods (encourage farming, trade).
- Reduce currency supply (people have more currency than goods; reducing currency balances the equation).
- Or accept mild inflation temporarily while goods production catches up.

**Deflation prevention**

Deflation (falling prices, decreasing money supply) is less common but serious. It discourages spending and investment.

- If prices fall more than 5% per year, expand currency supply moderately.
- Encourage spending by offering incentives (discount for early payment, reward for large transactions).
- Invest in production (fund farmers, craftspeople, builders to increase goods supply).

**Long-term monetary policy**

Healthy post-collapse economy targets:
- **Inflation**: 0–3% per year (mild, manageable inflation; better than deflation).
- **Supply growth**: Currency supply grows ~2% per year (matching goods production growth).
- **Purchasing power**: Over 10 years, 1 unit of currency should retain ~80% of purchasing power (acceptable gradual loss vs. pre-collapse).

</section>

## Summary & Implementation

Anti-counterfeiting and currency production are essential for stable economies. Begin with:

1. **Choose currency form**: Metal coins (hardest to counterfeit) or paper (faster to produce, moderate security).
2. **Design anti-counterfeiting features**: At minimum—denomination mark, community seal, unique dies or serial numbers.
3. **Establish mint** with oversight: Secure location, trained personnel, ledger records, periodic audits.
4. **Set denomination system**: Recommended 1:5:25:100 ratios.
5. **Define commodity backing**: Currency = claim on labor, grain, metal, or combination.
6. **Control supply**: Target mild 0–3% inflation; monitor prices monthly; adjust production accordingly.
7. **Train currency inspectors**: 2–3 community members learn inspection techniques.
8. **Set enforcement policy**: Counterfeiting carries severe penalty (expulsion, hard labor, or capital punishment in extreme cases) to deter.

With these systems in place, communities can maintain currency trust and enable complex economic exchange beyond simple barter.
