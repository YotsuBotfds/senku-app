## Overview

Cover crops (also called green manures) are plants grown not for harvest, but to improve soil. When tilled into the soil or left to decompose on the surface, cover crops add organic matter, suppress weeds, fix nitrogen (if legumes), prevent erosion, and break pest cycles. A well-planned cover crop rotation can eliminate the need for external fertilizers and improve soil structure over several seasons.

Cover crops serve different functions depending on the species, season, and incorporation method. Legumes fix nitrogen from the atmosphere (50–200 lbs per acre). Grasses and broadleaves accumulate biomass and organic matter. Living mulches suppress weeds while crops are growing. This guide covers species selection by season and climate, nitrogen fixation values, incorporation timing, and multi-year rotation strategies.

<section id="cover-crop-classes">

## Cover Crop Classes and Functions

**Nitrogen-fixing legumes (Fabaceae):**
These symbiotic plants host Rhizobium bacteria in root nodules, converting atmospheric N₂ into plant-available ammonia.

| **Species** | **Season** | **N Fixed (lbs/acre)** | **Climate** | **Notes** |
|-----------|---------|---------------------|-----------|----------|
| Alfalfa | Spring/summer; winter-hardy | 80–200 | Temperate, semi-arid | Perennial; deep-rooted; takes 2 years for high fixation |
| Clover (red) | Fall/spring; winter-hardy | 80–150 | Temperate | Biennial; cold-hardy; integrates into pasture rotations |
| Clover (white) | Continuous if moisture | 50–100 | Temperate, moist | Perennial; short-lived flowers; good with grasses |
| Vetch (hairy) | Fall/winter; survives freezing | 120–180 | Temperate | Winter-hardy; deep-rooted; kills at first frost (spring termination) |
| Vetch (common) | Spring/early summer | 80–120 | Temperate | Less winter-hardy than hairy vetch; faster growth |
| Pea (field) | Spring/early summer | 80–150 | Temperate, cool | Winter pea in mild regions; spring pea in cold regions |
| Cowpea | Summer; warm-season | 60–120 | Warm temperate, tropical | Drought-tolerant; requires warmth; killed by frost |
| Pigeon pea | Tropical; warm-season | 100–200 | Tropical, subtropical | Perennial shrub; nitrogen-fixing; edible peas |
| Lupine | Spring | 120–200 | Temperate, acidic soil | Deep-rooted; improves highly acidic soils; alpine varieties for mountains |

**Biomass-building grasses and broadleaves:**
These accumulate dry matter and organic carbon but do not fix nitrogen. Use them to add structure, suppress weeds, and feed subsequent crops via decomposition.

| **Species** | **Season** | **Biomass (tons/acre)** | **Carbon (C:N ratio)** | **Notes** |
|-----------|---------|---------------------|---------------------|----------|
| Rye | Fall/winter/spring (winter-hardy) | 4–6 | 30–80:1 | Allelopathic (suppressives winter weeds); slow spring decomposition |
| Oat | Spring; killed by frost | 2–3 | 40–60:1 | Non-winter-hardy; fast-growing; quick decomposition |
| Barley | Fall/spring (winter varieties exist) | 2–4 | 35–50:1 | Winter-hardy varieties in some zones; allelopathic |
| Buckwheat | Summer; frost-killed | 1.5–3 | 20–30:1 | Fast-growing (2 months); breaks compacted soil; smothers weeds |
| Millet | Summer; warm-season | 2–4 | 30–50:1 | Drought-tolerant; requires warmth; quick maturity (60–80 days) |
| Sorghum-sudangrass | Summer; deep-rooted | 4–6 | 30–50:1 | Allelopathic; breaks hardpan; killed by frost |
| Radish (oil radish) | Fall/spring | 1–2 | 20–30:1 | Biofumigant (breaks root diseases); taproots break compaction; winter-hardy |
| Rapeseed (canola) | Fall/winter; hardy | 2–3 | 30–40:1 | Biofumigant; oil seed potential; winter-hardy; slow decomposition |

<section id="nitrogen-fixation-rates">

## Nitrogen Fixation by Species and Management

Legume nitrogen fixation varies with legume species, soil nitrogen status, moisture, and temperature. Well-managed legume cover crops can supply all nitrogen needed by a following heavy-feeder crop.

**Fixation rate factors:**

1. **Species**: Alfalfa and vetch fix more nitrogen than clover or pea under ideal conditions.
2. **Establishment**: Legumes must be established quickly (good seed-to-soil contact, moisture) to form nodules. Poor establishment = poor fixation.
3. **Duration**: Legumes must grow for 3–4 months minimum to fix significant nitrogen. A spring pea planted in May and incorporated in July fixes less than one planted in April and incorporated in August.
4. **Soil nitrogen**: High residual soil nitrogen inhibits nodulation. Avoid applying nitrogen fertilizer to legume cover crops.
5. **Moisture**: Drought stresses nodulation. Legumes fix nitrogen best with 60–70% soil moisture (not waterlogged).
6. **Temperature**: Most temperate legumes fix nitrogen at 50–75°F. Warm-season legumes (cowpea, pigeon pea) need 65°F+.
7. **Inoculation**: Rhizobium bacteria must be present in soil. If sowing legumes for the first time, inoculate seed with appropriate Rhizobium strain (available from seed suppliers) to ensure nodulation in year 1.

**Typical nitrogen credits (lbs N per acre, achievable under good management):**

```
Alfalfa (1-year stand):        80–200 lbs N/acre
Vetch (hairy, 4+ months):      120–180 lbs N/acre
Clover (red, 2-year stand):    80–150 lbs N/acre
Pea (field, spring 3–4 months): 80–120 lbs N/acre
Cowpea (summer 3–4 months):    60–120 lbs N/acre
Clover (white, continuous):     50–100 lbs N/acre
Lupine (1 season):              120–200 lbs N/acre
```

**Nitrogen credit calculation:**
A field receiving 150 lbs N from a hairy vetch cover crop requires 150 lbs less applied nitrogen (or 150 lbs less manure compost) on the following crop. If tomatoes (heavy feeders) need 200 lbs N per acre, a prior vetch cover crop means you apply only 50 lbs external nitrogen (200 – 150 = 50).

:::info-box
Nitrogen fixation is not instantaneous. The nitrogen fixed by a legume cover crop becomes available to the *next* crop, not the current one. Plan rotations with this lag in mind: grow legumes in a season, incorporate in fall/spring, then plant heavy feeders in the following season.
:::

<section id="seasonal-selection">

## Seasonal Selection by Climate

Cover crop species are chosen based on planting season, climate zone, and soil conditions.

**Temperate Zone (Cold Winters, USDA 4–6)**

**Fall planting (September–October), spring incorporation (April–May):**
- **Primary choice**: Hairy vetch (winter-hardy, high nitrogen fixation)
- **Secondary**: Winter rye (allelopathic, fast spring growth), winter pea (if no nitrogen fixation is needed)
- **Alternative**: Clover blend (red + white; slower nitrogen buildup; perennial potential)

**Winter survival**: All these species survive freezing; they slow growth in winter and resume in spring. Hairy vetch emerges in fall (4–6 weeks of growth before winter), then resumes growth in early spring (March), ready to incorporate by May.

**Spring planting (April–May), summer mowing:**
- **Primary choice**: Oat + vetch mix (oat provides structure, vetch fixes nitrogen). Plant in early April; mow at late June (oat flowers, vetch extends); mulch field or till.
- **Alternative**: Spring pea alone if nitrogen fixation is critical; incorporate by July.
- **Quick summer option**: Buckwheat in June–July (matures in 8 weeks; plant in late June, incorporate in late August). Does not winter over.

**Temperate Zone Example Rotation:**

```
Year 1, Fall: Plant hairy vetch in tomato bed
Year 1, Winter: Vetch dormant, roots establish
Year 2, Spring: Vetch resumes growth, flowers, nitrogen fixed
Year 2, April: Incorporate vetch 2–3 weeks before tomato planting
Year 2, May: Plant tomatoes (high nitrogen available from vetch)
Year 2, Fall: After tomato harvest, plant winter rye (no nitrogen fixation; builds biomass)
Year 3, Spring: Incorporate rye
Year 3, May: Plant lettuce (light feeder) in same bed
Year 3, Fall: Plant legume again if rotation requires
```

**Subtropical Zone (Mild Winters, USDA 8–9)**

**Fall planting (October–November), winter growth, spring incorporation:**
- **Primary choice**: Hairy vetch or clover (similar to temperate; winter growth is slower but continuous)
- **Alternative**: Winter pea, field pea

**Spring planting (February–March), early summer incorporation:**
- **Primary choice**: Cowpea (planted after last frost; grows quickly in warmth; incorporate by July)
- **Alternative**: Field pea (if moisture is adequate)

**Summer planting (June–July), fall incorporation:**
- **Avoid**: Most cover crops struggle in intense summer heat. If planting, use heat-tolerant species like millet or sudangrass.

**Subtropical Example:**

```
Fall (Nov): Plant hairy vetch after summer crops
Spring (April): Incorporate vetch; plant tomatoes
Summer: Maintain crop
Fall (Sept): After tomato harvest, plant vetch again OR plant summer-dormant cover (oat + vetch will not survive; skip to rye if cool enough)
Winter: Vetch grows continuously (mild temperatures)
```

**Tropical Zone (No Frost, USDA 10+)**

**Year-round options**: Tropical legumes (pigeon pea, cowpea) and grasses can grow continuously. However, intense rainy seasons may cause waterlogging (problematic for most cover crops) and dry seasons may require irrigation.

- **Dry season (Nov–April)**: Plant pigeon pea (slow initial growth but long-term nitrogen fixer); plant field pea if moisture allows.
- **Transition to rainy season (May)**: Plant cowpea (thrives in warmth and moisture).
- **Rainy season (June–October)**: Cowpea productive; monitor for waterlogging.

**Tropical Example:**

```
Dry season (Nov): Plant pigeon pea
Rainy season (June): Supplementary cowpea in same field
Anytime: Incorporate sections as needed; replacement-plant within 2 weeks
Year-round: Continuous cropping with rotation; no true "off-season"
```

## Incorporation Timing and Termination Methods

Cover crops must be terminated and incorporated before the next cash crop is planted. Timing depends on the cover crop species, incorporation method, and desired nitrogen release timeline.

**Incorporation timeline:**

1. **Peak biomass**: Most nitrogen and carbon accumulate at peak biomass (flowering for legumes, early boot stage for grains). Terminate here for maximum benefit.
2. **Termination method**:
   - **Till (moldboard plow, rototiller)**: Fastest incorporation; 2–3 weeks for decomposition to begin.
   - **Mow + leave on surface**: Slower incorporation; 4–8 weeks for full decomposition; risk of pest harboring if not tilled after 3 weeks.
   - **Herbicide kill** (glyphosate for non-organic): Immediate termination; minimal soil disturbance; 7–10 days to plant (residue remains, decomposing slowly).
3. **Waiting period**: After incorporation, wait 2–3 weeks before planting (allows decomposition to stabilize, prevents phytotoxic compounds from slowing seedling growth). Warm soils decompose faster; cool soils require longer waits.

**Timing by species:**

| **Species** | **Peak Biomass** | **Termination** | **Days to Plant After Incorporation** |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|---------------------------------------|
| Hairy vetch | Flowering (June, temperate) | Till or mow | 14–21 (till releases nitrogen faster) |
| Winter rye | Early boot (late May, temperate) | Till (mow alone too slow) | 14–21 (allelopathic compounds take time to break down) |
| Field pea | Flowering (June–July) | Till or mow | 7–14 |
| Oat | Heading (June–July) | Till or mow | 7–14 |
| Buckwheat | Flowering (August–September) | Till or mow | 7–10 |
| Cowpea | Flowering, before frost/dry | Till or mow | 7–14 |
| Pigeon pea | Perennial; prune and strip leaves as needed | Till or mow | 7–14 |

**Winter-kill vs. overwintering species:**

- **Winter-kill** (killed by frost at first hard freeze): Oat, buckwheat, millet, cowpea, common vetch (zone-dependent). These simplify termination because frost kills the crop; no additional termination needed. However, residue remains on surface and decomposes slowly over winter.
- **Overwintering** (survive freezing, resume growth in spring): Hairy vetch, winter rye, winter pea, clover. These require active termination (till or herbicide) in spring before they regrow and consume soil nitrogen.

:::warning
If you plant a winter-kill cover crop expecting frost to kill it, but winter is mild or short, the crop will regrow in spring and consume soil nitrogen before cash crop planting. Scout field in early spring; if cover is still actively growing, terminate immediately.
:::

## Living Mulch Systems (Growing Cover With Cash Crop)

Rather than alternating cover crops with cash crops, living mulches grow alongside them, suppressing weeds and fixing nitrogen while the cash crop is harvested. This intensifies production and reduces weed management labor.

**Legume living mulches (underseeded into cash crop):**

1. **Legume companion**: White clover, crimson clover (annual), or vetch (annual).
2. **Seeding timing**: Underseed into the cash crop 4–6 weeks before cash crop harvest. For example, underseed clover into corn when corn is 12–18 inches tall and beginning to shade (reduces clover competition with corn).
3. **Growth**: The legume grows slowly under the cash crop's shade, establishing roots and early shoots without competing for resources.
4. **Harvest**: At cash crop harvest, the legume is small but established. It grows vigorously after the cash crop is removed.
5. **Incorporation**: The living mulch is terminated and incorporated before the next cash crop (or allowed to persist if it's a perennial like white clover).

**Temperate corn + legume example:**

```
Mid-June: Plant corn
Mid-August: When corn is 18 inches, overseed white clover (or hairy vetch for fast nitrogen)
September–October: Corn harvested; clover begins rapid growth
October–April: Clover grows, fixing nitrogen, suppressing weeds
May: Incorporate clover (or leave as perennial living mulch)
June: Plant next crop (benefits from clover nitrogen fixation)
```

**Benefits:**
- Reduced weeding during main crop season
- Nitrogen fixation occurs simultaneously with cash crop growth (though clover's nitrogen is available to the *next* crop)
- No wasted land (cover crop occupies space that would be weeded anyway)

**Challenges:**
- Legume competition with cash crop if seeded too early or in too high density
- Moisture stress in dry seasons (two root systems competing for water)
- Difficulty terminating uniform annuals (legume may persist, contaminate next crop, or regrow)

## Multi-Year Rotations and Soil Building

A long-term rotation (3–5 years) that integrates legume and grain cover crops builds soil systematically.

**3-year rotation (temperate zone, average climate):**

```
Year 1:
  Season 1 (Fall): Plant hairy vetch; winter dormant
  Season 2 (Spring): Vetch flowers, nitrogen fixed; incorporate late April
  Season 3 (May–Aug): Plant/grow tomatoes (nitrogen-hungry) in same bed
  Season 4 (Fall): Plant winter rye (biomass buildup, weeds suppressed)

Year 2:
  Season 1 (Spring): Incorporate rye; wait 3 weeks
  Season 2 (May–Sept): Plant legume (pea) for lighter nitrogen crop OR plant lettuce (light feeder)
  Season 3 (Fall): Plant vetch again

Year 3:
  Season 1 (Spring): Incorporate vetch; wait 3 weeks
  Season 2 (May–Sept): Squash or other heavy feeder (benefits from accumulated nitrogen)
  Season 3 (Fall): Plant grain (rye or oat) for biomass; no nitrogen from soil (grain consumes stored nitrogen)

Repeat from Year 1.
```

**Result**: Over 3 years, legume cover crops fix 300–400 lbs N/acre (cumulative), which supports heavy feeders (tomato, squash) without external nitrogen. Grain/grass cover crops build organic matter (4–6 tons/acre annually), improving soil structure and tilth year after year.

**5-year rotation (for organic farming or low-input systems):**

```
Year 1: Hairy vetch (Spring incorporation) → heavy feeder (tomato)
Year 2: Winter rye → light feeder (lettuce) + underseeded legume
Year 3: Incorporated legume → medium feeder (brassica)
Year 4: Buckwheat (summer mow + reseed) → grain cover crop (rye)
Year 5: Incorporated rye → heavy feeder (corn) + underseeded legume

Repeat. Each full cycle builds organic matter (20–30 tons/acre), fixes nitrogen (300–500 lbs N/acre), and suppresses pest cycles.
```

:::info-box
Long-term rotations (3–5 years) that include both legume and grain cover crops eliminate the need for external fertilizers in many systems, especially if manure or compost is added once every 2–3 years. Soil organic matter (and associated nitrogen, water-holding capacity, and biodiversity) increases by 0.1–0.2% per year under good rotation management.
:::

<section id="troubleshooting">

## Troubleshooting Cover Crop Failures

| **Problem** | **Cause** | **Solution** |
|------------|---------|------------|
| Legume fails to nodulate (no nitrogen fixation) | Rhizobium bacteria absent in soil; legume seeded too shallow; soil too acidic | Inoculate seed with Rhizobium (commercial inoculant). Plant at depth 1–1.5 inches. Test soil pH; lime if below 6.0 for most legumes. |
| Legume grows slowly despite adequate moisture | High residual soil nitrogen (inhibits nodulation); poor inoculant; pH too high | If nitrogen was recently added, wait a season before planting legume. Ensure Rhizobium strain matches legume species. Aim for pH 6.5–7.0. |
| Cover crop smothered by weeds | Seeding too shallow; poor seed-to-soil contact; weeds established before seeding | Prepare seedbed thoroughly. Plant at proper depth (check species label). Sow when weeds are dormant (fall for winter crops; spring for summer crops). Consider mowing weeds 1 week before seeding. |
| Cover crop fails to germinate (poor establishment) | Seed too old or not viable; soil too wet or dry; planting at wrong time | Buy fresh seed from reputable supplier. Test soil moisture before planting (not waterlogged, not dust-dry). Plant during season when conditions allow moisture after planting. |
| Grain cover crop is too tall; lodges (falls over) before termination | Over-seeding (too much seed density); excess nitrogen; wind damage | Reduce seeding rate by 20–30%. If legume is mixed in, expect taller stature. Harvest at earlier stage if wind is forecast. |
| Regrowth of winter-hardy cover after termination | Incomplete termination; residual roots; early termination date | Till deeply (6–8 inches) to ensure root destruction. For herbicide termination, increase rate or allow longer interval before secondary growth. Extend waiting period before cash crop planting. |

## Getting Started: Simple First Season

For a beginner, start with one cover crop in one bed:

1. **Choose a bed** (200–400 sq ft). This is your test ground.
2. **Select species based on season**:
   - Fall (Sept–Oct, temperate): Plant hairy vetch
   - Spring (April, temperate): Plant oat + vetch mix
   - Summer (June, warm zones): Plant buckwheat or cowpea
3. **Prepare seedbed**: Remove competing vegetation; loosen soil; rake smooth.
4. **Seed at proper depth**: 1–1.5 inches for most. Press seed firmly into soil.
5. **Water**: If rainfall is not expected within 3 days, water lightly to settle seed.
6. **Monitor**: Scout for weeds every 2–3 weeks; remove competing plants.
7. **Terminate**: At peak biomass (flowering for legumes, early boot for grains), till or mow and incorporate.
8. **Wait 3 weeks**: Allow decomposition; then plant your cash crop.
9. **Observe**: Record nitrogen responsiveness (does the following crop grow more vigorously?), organic matter changes (is soil darker, more crumbly?), and weed suppression.

From this test bed, you can expand to a 3–5 year rotation across multiple beds, confident in the process.
