<section id="overview">

## Overview

This guide is a boundary and handoff surface for sterilization-related
questions. It does not teach sterilization, disinfection, laboratory testing,
medical instrument processing, or water treatment. Its job is to slow down a
mixed request, name the intended use, separate clean and dirty pathways, capture
uncertainty, and route the decision to the qualified owner.

Use it when a user asks about a confusing overlap: water that may be used for
cleaning, cleaning that may be near food or wounds, equipment that may move
between dirty and clean areas, or a medical-adjacent setting where the user is
not sure whether ordinary cleaning, disinfection, sterile processing, water
treatment, public-health review, or licensed clinical oversight is required.

</section>

<section id="intake-before-method">

## Intake Before Any Method

Start by classifying the request before naming a method. Record:

- Source or item: water source, container, surface, tool, equipment, linen,
  food-contact area, waste-adjacent area, wound-adjacent area, clinic area, or
  lab area.
- Intended use: drinking, cooking, washing, routine cleaning, shared-facility
  cleaning, food-contact cleaning, wound-adjacent cleaning, medical care,
  laboratory work, or unknown use.
- Who may be exposed: children, pregnant people, immunocompromised people,
  patients, food recipients, lab workers, cleanup workers, or the public.
- Known contamination: body fluid, sewage, floodwater, chemical, fuel,
  pesticide, unknown residue, animal waste, clinical waste, lab material, or
  ordinary soil and dust.
- Current state: dirty, visibly clean, separated, stored, reused, mixed with
  dirty items, touched after cleaning, splashed, unlabeled, or undocumented.

If intended use or contamination is unknown, keep the answer in intake mode.
Do not convert uncertainty into a treatment plan.

</section>

<section id="uncertainty-log">

## Uncertainty Log

The uncertainty log is the safe output when the user does not know the source,
contamination, prior use, or intended exposure. Keep it short and concrete:

- What is known.
- What is unknown.
- What could change the risk category.
- What is already separated from clean items.
- What is not being used until a qualified owner decides.
- Which owner is needed next.

Uncertainty is a stop-and-sort condition. It is not proof that something is
safe, and it is not a reason to improvise a sterilization or disinfection
procedure.

</section>

<section id="clean-dirty-separation">

## Clean and Dirty Separation

Before any owner handoff, protect separation:

- Keep dirty, unknown, wet, splashed, or exposed items away from clean storage,
  food surfaces, drinking-water containers, wound-care supplies, clinical
  areas, and lab benches.
- Use labels such as dirty, clean, unknown, do not use, awaiting owner review,
  or discard decision pending.
- Move from cleaner areas toward dirtier areas; do not carry dirty items back
  through clean paths.
- Do not reuse a container, cloth, tool, or work surface across dirty and clean
  tasks unless the correct owner has approved the process for that use case.
- If clean and dirty paths have crossed, log the crossover and treat the clean
  status as uncertain.

</section>

<section id="escalation-flags">

## Escalation Flags

Escalate instead of giving routine cleaning advice when any of these are
present:

- Medical care, dental care, wound contact, injections, invasive procedures,
  patient-contact equipment, or reusable instruments.
- Drinking water, cooking water, food-contact surfaces, shared meals, shelters,
  clinics, schools, elder care, or public distribution.
- Laboratory samples, cultures, unknown biological material, sharps, chemicals,
  solvents, fuels, pesticides, or unlabeled residues.
- Sewage, floodwater, body fluids, animal waste, clinical waste, rotten odor,
  unknown storage history, or mixed clean/dirty batches.
- A request to certify safety, prove sterility, declare potability, clear a
  surgical or dental setup, or guarantee pathogen kill.

For these flags, answer with source/use-case intake, separation, and the next
owner. Do not supply procedural parameters.

</section>

<section id="handoff-owners">

## Qualified Owner Handoff

Choose the owner by intended use:

- Drinking or cooking water: water-quality or public-health owner.
- Food-contact cleaning: food-safety owner or facility manager.
- Medical, dental, wound, patient-contact, or reusable instrument use:
  licensed clinical infection-control or sterile-processing owner.
- Laboratory work, cultures, unknown biological material, sharps, or chemical
  residues: laboratory safety, biosafety, chemical-safety, or waste owner.
- Shared housing, shelters, schools, kitchens, wash stations, or public
  facilities: responsible site owner plus public-health, sanitation, plumbing,
  or environmental-health owner as appropriate.

The handoff should preserve the uncertainty log, clean/dirty separation status,
exposure group, intended use, contamination clues, and any stop-use decisions
already made.

</section>

<section id="not-covered">

## Not Covered

This guide intentionally excludes treatment guarantees, sterilization
parameters, chemical recipes or concentrations, medical instrument processing,
autoclave operation, water-potability certification, laboratory testing
protocols, surgical or dental readiness, pathogen kill claims, legal or code
claims, and safety certification.

When a prompt asks for those outputs, provide only the boundary intake,
separation steps, escalation flags, and qualified owner handoff.

</section>

<section id="answer-template">

## Boundary Answer Template

Use this structure for GD-646 answers:

1. Name the intended use and contamination uncertainty.
2. State whether routine advice is inappropriate because an escalation flag is
   present.
3. Preserve clean/dirty separation and label unknown items.
4. List the exact facts to log before handoff.
5. Hand off to the qualified owner for the use case.
6. State that this guide does not certify safety or provide procedural
   sterilization, disinfection, water-treatment, lab, medical, dental, or food
   readiness instructions.

</section>
