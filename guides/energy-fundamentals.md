Energy is the foundation of all power generation. Before you build a water wheel or solar concentrator, you need to understand what energy is, how it transforms, and what that means for practical work. This guide covers the fundamentals that explain all power systems.

<section id="what-is-energy">

## What Is Energy?

**Energy** is the capacity to do work. Work means moving something against resistance—lifting a weight, pushing something, creating heat, or making electricity.

Energy is not a visible thing; it's a property that allows things to happen. You can't see energy directly, but you see its effects.

### Forms of Energy

Energy exists in multiple forms:

**Kinetic Energy (Energy of Motion)**
- Moving objects possess kinetic energy
- Faster movement = more energy
- Heavier objects at same speed = more energy
- Equation: KE = ½ mv² (mass × velocity squared)
- Example: A falling rock has kinetic energy; a river flowing downhill has kinetic energy

**Potential Energy (Stored Energy)**
- Objects in positions where they could move possess potential energy
- Higher position = more gravitational potential energy
- Stretched or compressed objects (springs, etc.) have elastic potential energy
- Equation: PE = mgh (mass × gravity × height)
- Example: Water behind a dam has potential energy; a coiled spring has potential energy

**Thermal Energy (Heat)**
- Movement of molecules and atoms
- Hotter objects have more thermal energy
- Temperature measures average molecular motion
- Example: Fire, hot water, steam

**Chemical Energy (Stored in Bonds)**
- Stored in chemical bonds between atoms
- Released when bonds break and reform
- Example: Gasoline, coal, wood, food

**Electrical Energy**
- Motion of electrons
- Powers lights, motors, electronics
- Can be generated by converting other forms of energy

**Radiant Energy (Light and Radiation)**
- Travels as waves
- Includes visible light, infrared, ultraviolet
- Example: Sunlight

### Energy Is Not Created or Destroyed

This is the First Law of Thermodynamics: **Energy is conserved.**

Energy changes form, but the total amount remains constant (in a closed system). When you burn wood:
- Chemical energy (in wood) → Thermal energy (heat) + Radiant energy (light)
- Total energy out equals total energy in

When a boulder falls off a cliff:
- Potential energy (from height) → Kinetic energy (motion) → Thermal energy (when it hits ground and stops)

Understanding this helps you see where energy comes from and where it goes in any system.

<section id="energy-conversion">

## Converting Energy from One Form to Another

**Power generation is about converting one form of energy into electrical energy that you can use.**

### Common Conversions

**Falling water → Mechanical motion:**
- Water wheel converts kinetic energy of flowing water into spinning wheel
- Potential energy of water at height becomes kinetic energy as it falls

**Mechanical motion → Electrical energy:**
- Generator converts spinning motion into electricity
- Coils of wire spinning in magnetic field generate electric current

**Heat → Mechanical motion:**
- Steam engine converts thermal energy (heat) into motion of pistons
- Heat boils water to steam; expanding steam pushes pistons

**Sunlight → Heat:**
- Dark surface absorbs radiant energy from sun
- Absorbed energy becomes thermal energy (heat)

**Sunlight → Electrical energy:**
- Photovoltaic cells convert photons directly to electrons
- Complex quantum process produces electric current

**Chemical energy → Thermal energy:**
- Fire converts chemical energy in fuel into heat
- Heat then converts to mechanical energy or electricity

### Efficiency

**No energy conversion is 100% efficient.** Some energy always "escapes" as heat, sound, or friction.

**Examples:**
- Windmill: Maybe 35-45% of wind energy converts to mechanical motion; rest escapes as heat/turbulence
- Waterwheel: Maybe 50-80% efficient depending on design
- Internal combustion engine: About 25-40% efficient; most energy leaves as heat
- Steam engine: About 10-30% efficient
- Electrical generator: About 80-95% efficient (very good)

**Practical implication:** Expect losses. A system that seems to produce X power on paper might produce 50% of that in practice.

<section id="power-and-work">

## Power vs. Work

**Work** = energy transferred. Moving a 100-pound weight 10 feet is the same amount of work whether you do it in 1 second or 1 hour.

**Power** = how fast work is done. Doing the same work faster requires more power.

Power = Work / Time

### Units

**Work:** Joules (or foot-pounds in imperial)

**Power:** Watts (Joules per second) or kilowatts (1000 watts)

**Other power units:**
- Horsepower (1 hp = 746 watts; historical unit from steam engines)
- BTU per hour (thermal power)

### Practical Examples

**100-pound weight lifted 10 feet:**
- Work = ~1,355 Joules (roughly)
- If done in 1 second: Power = 1,355 watts (1.8 hp)
- If done in 10 seconds: Power = 135 watts (0.18 hp)

**A waterwheel:**
- Water flowing over wheel continuously = continuous power
- More water flow = more power
- Higher fall height = more power
- Power = (mass of water per second) × (gravity) × (height)

### Why Power Matters

**For electrical use:** You need sufficient power to run devices. A 100-watt generator powers a lightbulb but not a refrigerator.

**For design:** A power system that produces 100 watts consistently is more useful than one producing 1000 watts sporadically.

<section id="storing-energy">

## Storing and Using Stored Energy

Energy storage allows you to use power when it's not being generated.

### Mechanical Storage

**Flywheels:**
- Heavy spinning wheel stores kinetic energy
- When you need power, friction/load slows it down, extracting energy
- Example: Pottery wheel spins freely; glasswork harnesses energy as it slows

**Raised water:**
- Pump water uphill into a tank
- Water at height stores potential energy
- Let water flow downhill through waterwheel when needed
- Example: Hydropower plants use dams

**Compressed springs or weights:**
- Compress spring or raise weight to store potential energy
- Release to do work
- Limited capacity but reliable
- Example: Mechanical clock weights

### Thermal Storage

**Hot water or thermal mass:**
- Heat water (absorbing thermal energy)
- Insulated tank stores heat
- Use heat later for warmth or steam generation
- Example: Solar thermal systems

**Insulated stone or brick:**
- Absorbs heat during sunny day
- Releases heat at night
- Limited capacity but continuous

### Chemical Storage

**Batteries:**
- Convert chemical energy to electrical energy
- Store for later use
- Finite capacity; must be recharged or replaced

**Fuel (wood, coal, oil):**
- Long-term chemical energy storage
- Burn when needed to release energy
- Most abundant for most people historically

### Electrical Storage

**Batteries:** Store electrical energy chemically

**Capacitors:** Store electrical energy in electric field (small quantities)

**Electrical grid:** Large systems store in pumped hydro, compressed air, or thermal systems

<section id="energy-sources">

## Natural Energy Sources

### Renewable (Replenished by Nature)

**Solar Energy**
- Sun provides continuous energy (during day)
- Can be converted to heat (passive) or electricity (photovoltaic)
- Available everywhere (more/less depending on latitude and weather)

**Wind Energy**
- Uneven heating creates atmospheric movement
- Wind turns windmills or turbines
- Available intermittently, varies by location and season

**Hydropower (Falling Water)**
- Evaporation and precipitation create water cycle
- Gravitational potential energy in elevated water
- Continuous if river/stream runs year-round

**Geothermal Energy**
- Heat from Earth's interior
- Available in specific locations (hot springs, volcanic areas)
- Can provide heating or, in some places, electricity

**Tidal Energy**
- Moon's gravity creates tides
- Predictable and continuous
- Useful only near ocean

**Biomass (Burning Plant/Animal Material)**
- Plants convert solar energy via photosynthesis
- Can be burned for heat or converted to biogas
- Renewable if regrown

### Non-Renewable (Finite Supplies)

**Coal**
- Ancient plant material (fossilized)
- High energy content
- Burning produces carbon dioxide and pollution

**Oil and Natural Gas**
- Ancient marine organisms (fossilized)
- Very high energy content
- Burning produces carbon dioxide

**Nuclear**
- Energy from splitting atoms (fission) or fusing atoms (fusion)
- Very high energy density
- Produces radioactive waste

**Peat**
- Partially decomposed plant material
- Intermediate between biomass and coal
- Renewable on geological timescales only

</section>

<section id="estimating-power-available">

## Estimating Power Available

Before building a power system, estimate what's available:

### Sunlight

**Solar power available:**
- On Earth's surface: ~1000 watts per square meter on clear day at solar noon
- Varies by latitude, time of day, weather, season
- Average over 24 hours and seasonal variation: ~150-200 watts per square meter

**Example:** 10 square meter solar panel on average gets 1500-2000 watts of power throughout the day

### Wind

**Wind power available:**
- Power depends on cube of wind speed
- Double wind speed = 8 times more power
- Turbulent/calm locations have huge differences

**Rough estimate:**
- 5 mph wind: Very little useful power
- 10 mph wind: Useful power for small turbines
- 15 mph wind: Good wind site
- 20+ mph wind: Excellent for wind power

### Falling Water

**Power from water:**

Power = (mass of water per second) × (gravity) × (height)

Example calculation:
- Stream flowing at 1000 liters per second (264 gallons/sec)
- Falls 10 meters (33 feet)
- Power available = 1000 kg/s × 10 m/s² × 10 m = 100,000 watts = 100 kilowatts

**This is theoretical maximum. Actual power extracted depends on waterwheel efficiency (maybe 50-70%).**

### Manual Labor

**Human power output:**
- Sustained: ~100 watts (like climbing stairs steadily)
- Short burst: ~300-500 watts
- Rare maximum: ~1500+ watts

**Practical:** Human power is valuable for small tasks but limited for continuous power needs.

<section id="efficiency-losses">

## Understanding Losses and Limits

All real systems lose energy. Knowing where losses occur helps you design better systems.

### Types of Losses

**Friction:** Mechanical moving parts lose energy to friction
- Bearings, gears, sliding surfaces all dissipate energy as heat
- Better lubrication and design reduce (but can't eliminate) friction

**Turbulence:** Fluid flow (water, air, steam) experiences turbulence
- Energy escapes as chaotic motion
- Smooth surfaces and careful design minimize turbulence

**Heat:** Imperfect energy conversions always produce waste heat
- Electrical resistance produces heat in wires
- Friction produces heat
- Entropy means some energy always becomes unavailable as heat

**Conversion inefficiencies:** Each conversion has limits
- No generator is 100% efficient at converting motion to electricity
- No turbine is 100% efficient at extracting energy from flowing fluid

### Accounting for Losses

When designing a system, assume realistic efficiency, not theoretical maximum:

- Waterwheel system: Assume 50% overall efficiency (wheel efficiency × generator efficiency × friction)
- Wind turbine: Assume 25-35% of theoretical maximum
- Solar thermal: Assume 40-70% depending on design
- Mechanical transmission: Each gear stage loses ~10-15% to friction

**Example:**
- Stream has 100 kW available power
- Waterwheel is 60% efficient: Delivers 60 kW to shaft
- Generator is 85% efficient: Produces 51 kW of electricity
- Losses to friction/transmission: ~10%: Final output ~45 kW

Better than half the theoretical maximum, but that's realistic.

</section>

<section id="next-steps">

## Next Steps

Once you understand these fundamentals:

1. **Build a simple system:** Waterwheel or windmill to understand practical power
2. **Measure outputs:** Know how much power you actually generate
3. **Study conversions:** Learn generators, turbines, or other conversion devices
4. **Combine sources:** Use multiple sources (sun + water + wind) for reliability
5. **Store power:** Add batteries or thermal storage to use power when available

Energy fundamentals guide everything in power generation. Master these concepts, and the practical systems become much clearer.
