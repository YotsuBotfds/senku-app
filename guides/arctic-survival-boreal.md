## Frostbite Prevention and Treatment

Frostbite occurs when skin and tissue freeze. Prevention is far easier than treatment.

### Frostbite Stages

| Stage | Temperature | Tissue Damage | Appearance | Recovery |
|-------|-------------|---|---|---|
| **Frostnip** | -1 to 0°C | None | Pale, numb skin | Immediate rewarming |
| **First-degree** | -2 to -5°C | Surface only | Blistering, red/purple | Full (usually) |
| **Second-degree** | -5 to -10°C | Partial thickness | Deep blisters, blackening | Partial; scarring |
| **Third-degree** | <-10°C | Full thickness, muscle | Black, mummified | Amputation likely |

### Prevention Strategies

**Clothing layers:**
- Keep extremities dry (moisture accelerates freezing)
- Insulate continuously (thermal layers, wind breaks)
- Cover all exposed skin in extreme cold (<-20°C)

**Circulation:**
- Keep moving; exercise generates heat
- Don't sit still in extreme cold
- Keep heart rate elevated moderately (not exhausted)

**Awareness:**
- Monitor fingers, toes, nose, ears for numbness
- Buddy system: partners watch each other for white/waxy skin patches
- Never ignore warning signs

**Extremity protection:**
- Mittens better than gloves (fingers together share heat)
- Socks: multiple pairs, wool or synthetic (NOT cotton; never wear tight socks that restrict circulation)
- Insulated boots rated for temperature range
- Hand/foot warmers (reusable chemical packs) in pockets

### Treatment

**Early frostbite (frostnip, first-degree):**
1. Move to shelter immediately
2. Remove wet clothing; dry off
3. Gradual rewarming: warm hands under armpits, feet between knees (NOT rapid hot water; causes pain and tissue damage)
4. No rubbing or massage (damages tissue)
5. Gradually increase activity to restore circulation
6. Monitor for blistering, infection

**Advanced frostbite (second/third-degree):**
1. Do NOT rewarm rapidly (thaw-refrost cycle causes gangrene)
2. Keep frozen if rescue not immediate (counterintuitively, frozen tissue better than thawed-refrozen)
3. Wrap loosely in dry cloth
4. Seek professional medical care for thawing
5. Amputation may be necessary

:::danger
Deep frostbite is a surgical emergency. Do not attempt thawing in field unless certain you can keep tissue thawed (risk of refreezing = gangrene and amputation).
:::

## Snowblindness (UV Exposure)

Snow reflects 80% of ultraviolet light, causing corneal damage and temporary blindness.

### Prevention

**Eye protection:**
- Sunglasses with 100% UV protection (dark enough to require adjustment in darkness)
- Snow goggles (best; seal around eyes to prevent UV seepage)
- Improvised: dark cloth with narrow slit to limit light entry (Inuit traditional method)

**Alternatives if goggles unavailable:**
- Stay in shade when possible
- Travel early morning/evening (lower sun angle)
- Limit snow exposure (dusk/dawn activities)

### Symptoms

- Gritty feeling in eyes
- Excessive tearing, light sensitivity
- Temporary vision loss (usually reversible)
- Usually develops 6–12 hours after exposure

### Treatment

1. **Immediate:** Move to shelter; shield eyes from light
2. **Cool/cold compresses:** Reduces inflammation
3. **Eyedrops:** If available, lubricating drops help
4. **Darkness:** Keep eyes closed; rest in dark shelter
5. **Recovery:** Usually 24–48 hours for full recovery

**Prevention is far better:** Snow blindness can incapacitate the entire group if everyone is affected simultaneously.

## Snow Shelter Construction

In Arctic environments without trees or rock, snow is the primary shelter material.

### Quinzee (Block Snow Shelter)

**Timing:** Best in wet snow (sticky, structural); difficult in powder

**Construction:**

1. **Gather snow:** Pile snow in mound 2–3 meters diameter, 1.5 m tall
2. **Settle:** Allow snow to compress under weight for 2–4 hours (allows wind to bond crystals)
3. **Excavate entrance:** Tunnel into base (lower than interior; heat rises)
4. **Hollow interior:** Remove snow from interior, creating sleeping platform higher than entrance
5. **Ventilation:** Poke hole in roof (snow blocks CO₂ accumulation)
6. **Door:** Plug entrance with snow block or insulating material to reduce drafts

**Interior dimensions:**
- Sleeping platform: 1.5–2 m × 1 m, elevated 40–60 cm above floor
- Entrance: keep low (warmth retention)
- Interior volume: minimize (easier to heat with body warmth)

**Insulation:** Snow acts as insulator; interior warming raises surface temperature but interior stays below -5°C (prevents melting)

**Advantages:** Quick to build; insulating; uses available material
**Disadvantages:** Requires wet snow; labor-intensive; interior humidity high

### Igloo (Snow Block Structure)

**Timing:** Requires dense, compacted snow (formed naturally or forced); takes 4–8 hours

**Construction:**

1. **Find suitable snow:** Must be uniform density, compacted
2. **Cut blocks:** Use snow saw or long knife; blocks ~60 cm × 40 cm × 15 cm
3. **Lay in circle:** Spiral pattern, each block leaning inward
4. **Wedge and fit:** Pack snow into gaps
5. **Entrance tunnel:** Build lower passage (blocks warmth escape)
6. **Top blocks:** Carefully fill crown of dome

**Insulation:** Similar to quinzee; packed snow resists heat transfer

**Advantages:** Structurally sound; long-lasting; beautiful construction
**Disadvantages:** Requires suitable snow type; more time-consuming; skill required

### Snow Trench (Emergency Shelter)

Simplest snow shelter, usable in powder snow:

1. **Dig trench:** 2 m long, 1 m wide, 1 m deep (or as deep as possible in time available)
2. **Cover:** Place branches or insulating material over top as roof
3. **Entrance:** Leave small opening, block with pack
4. **Lie in:** Sleeping platform is trench bottom
5. **Layer:** Line with insulating materials (pine boughs, foam pad) to prevent ground heat loss

**Advantages:** Fast; minimal tools; works in any snow
**Disadvantages:** Less insulated; requires cover material; limited room

### Interior Comfort

**Sleeping platform:**
- Elevated above floor (prevents cold air pooling)
- Insulated from snow (foam pad, pine branches, or just compacted snow)
- Level surface (important for sleep)

**Ventilation:**
- Hole in roof for air circulation
- Prevents CO₂ buildup and condensation
- Small hole (not large drafts)

**Heat management:**
- Cooking inside generates water vapor (can re-freeze on walls, drip)
- Often better to eat cold food, cook outside
- Body heat alone warms interior to -5 to 0°C

:::warning
Enclosed shelters have asphyxiation risk if venting inadequate. Install ventilation hole immediately; test air quality before sleep.
:::

<section id="arctic-clothing">

## Arctic Clothing Layers & Vapor Barrier Concept

Proper layering is the key to surviving extreme cold. Traditional Arctic peoples developed sophisticated layering 1000+ years ago.

### Layer System

| Layer | Material | Purpose |
|-------|----------|---------|
| **Base (inner)** | Wool or synthetic (NOT cotton) | Wicks moisture away from skin |
| **Mid layer** | Wool, fleece, down | Insulation; traps air |
| **Vapor barrier** | Tight plastic, rubber | Prevents moisture from condensing in layers |
| **Outer layer** | Windproof, water-resistant | Wind/precipitation protection |

### Vapor Barrier Concept

**Problem:** In extreme cold, any moisture freezes in clothing layers, destroying insulation. Sweat is lethal.

**Solution:** Vapor barrier (thin plastic or rubber layer) between base and insulation layers prevents body moisture from moving outward and freezing in outer layers.

**Traditional method:** Inuit used animal intestine as vapor barrier; modern: plastic sheet or rubber pants

**Placement:**
- Over base layer or directly on skin
- Under primary insulation
- Tight fit (prevents air circulation through barrier)

**Consequence:** Sweat stays near body; heat loss increases initially but prevents freeze-out of insulation

### Extremity Protection

**Hands:**
- Liner gloves (thin wool, allows dexterity)
- Outer mittens (insulation, wind-proof)
- Chemical warmers in mittens
- Ability to remove one without removing other (work one-handed while keeping other warm)

**Feet:**
- Vapor barrier socks (plastic, rubber)
- Insulation socks (wool, synthetic, 2–3 pairs)
- Insulated boots (rated for temperature range)
- Boot liners (removable for drying)
- No tight socks (restricts circulation)

**Head/Face:**
- Balaclava (full-face coverage)
- Goggles for eyes
- Insulating hat with ear coverage
- Scarf for face

**Whole body:**
- Heavy coat (parka) with hood
- Insulated pants (heavy)
- Wind shell pants

### Temperature-Dependent Adjustment

| Temperature | Clothing Style |
|---|---|
| -10 to 0°C | Standard winter clothes; frequent breaks |
| -20 to -10°C | Full Arctic dress; limit exposure time |
| -30 to -20°C | Constant attention; check for frostbite; don't fall asleep |
| <-40°C | Extreme risk; minimal outdoor time; never alone |

## Ice Fishing

In severe cold when other food sources unavailable, ice fishing provides vital protein.

### Ice Thickness Assessment

**Safety standards:**
- <10 cm: Dangerous; can break through
- 10–15 cm: Marginal; careful, one person at a time
- 15–20 cm: Safe for small group
- 20+ cm: Safe for large groups, vehicles

**Determining thickness:**
- Visual inspection: new clear ice looks darker; old opaque ice looks white
- Auger test: Drill hole; measure ice depth
- Axe test: Chop at edge; check freshness and thickness
- Listen: Ice makes groaning sounds when strained; indicates thickness and stress

### Hole Creation

**Methods:**
- Axe: Chop through ice (slow, labor-intensive)
- Ice auger: Drill through (faster, requires tool)
- Spud bar: Chisel through (moderate speed)
- Chemical: Acid/salt (controversial; can weaken ice)

**Hole diameter:** 20–30 cm (allows fish and line in/out, not so large ice breaks easily)

**Location:**
- Away from current (ice thinner)
- Near vegetation (fish congregate)
- Open water nearby if possible (shows fish feeding patterns)

### Fishing Method

**Simple line:**
- Knotted leader attached to main line
- Lure or bait (fish trap, flies, jigged bait)
- Keep line in hole; watch for tension/movement

**Fish trap:**
- Weighted basket set under hole
- Bait inside
- Check periodically
- Works while you rest/work on other tasks

### Baiting and Lure

**Available baits:**
- Insects (mosquito larvae, water boatmen)
- Small fish
- Meat scraps
- Worms (less available in winter)

**Lure types:**
- Jigging: Move lure up/down to attract fish
- Stationary: Set line, wait
- Multiple lines: Allow checking many holes simultaneously (if legal)

### Winter Fish Biology

Arctic fish are slow-moving and require little food. Fishing success depends on patience and persistence, not speed.

:::tip
Ice fishing is as much about waiting and observation as actual fishing. Successful Arctic fisher observes: Where do fish congregate? When do they feed? What times of day are productive?
:::

## Polar Navigation (Sun Compass, Magnetic Deviation)

In Arctic, sun never rises or sets in winter (constant darkness) and never sets in summer (constant light). Navigation techniques differ from temperate zones.

### Magnetic Navigation Limitations

**Magnetic declination:** Compass points magnetic north, not true north. In Arctic, declination is extreme:
- Northern Canada: declination 20°+ from true north
- Russia: similar distortion
- Must constantly correct magnetic bearing to true direction

**Magnetic variation:** Varies year-to-year in some regions (magnetic pole moves)

**Equipment:** Local declination charts essential; update as you move

### Sun Compass (No Equipment)

In winter (extreme darkness), sun compass useless. But in other seasons:

**Principle:** Sun rises due east, sets due west (at all latitudes)

**Method:**
1. Point toward sunrise (east)
2. Point toward sunset (west)
3. North is perpendicular to east-west line
4. Adjust for latitude: At extreme latitudes, sun stays low; use shadows for more accurate direction

**Limitations:**
- Only works when sun visible
- Accuracy decreases near poles (sun low on horizon)
- Requires clear sky

### Winter Navigation Methods

**Landmarks:**
- Snow formation patterns (sastrugi — wind-formed ridges)
- Topography (valleys, hills)
- Vegetation patterns (if available)
- Star navigation (Polaris marks true north in Northern Hemisphere)

**Celestial navigation:**
- North Star (Polaris) at height above horizon = latitude you're at
- Requires clear sky, navigation knowledge

**Dead reckoning:**
- Estimate distance traveled and direction
- Keep log of daily progress
- Correct for wind drift, difficult terrain
- Error accumulates; verify position regularly

:::warning
Whiteout conditions (snow, clouds, fog) erase all visual navigation references. Risk of walking in circles. In whiteout, stop and shelter; don't navigate blind.
:::

## Blizzard Survival and Whiteout Conditions

A blizzard combines three dangers: extreme cold, wind, and visibility loss.

### Blizzard Prediction

**Warning signs:**
- Pressure drop (if barometer available)
- Wind shift and increase
- Temperature drop
- Clouds lowering
- Animal behavior change (birds sheltering)

**Time window:** 2–12 hours from signs to storm arrival (variable)

### Pre-Blizzard Preparation

1. **Seek shelter immediately:** Don't wait for storm to hit
2. **Gather fuel/food:** Cut wood, food for 24+ hours
3. **Secure shelter:** Patch holes, improve insulation
4. **Shelter animals:** Livestock in enclosed areas
5. **Stockpile water:** Collect now; freezing later prevents access
6. **Secure supplies:** Anchor tents, store items where retrievable

### During Blizzard

**Stay sheltered:**
- Don't travel unless emergency
- Visibility reduced to meters; easy to get lost
- Wind chill lethal within minutes of exposure

**If outdoors (emergency):**
- Build emergency shelter: dig snow cave, pile snow blocks
- Stay put until visibility improves
- Don't travel in blizzard; risk of walking off cliff, in wrong direction

**Navigation in whiteout:**
- Mark path with snow blocks or stakes every 10 meters
- Never leave shelter without safety line to shelter
- Travel between fixed points only (never open navigation)
- One person at a time outside

### Blizzard-Specific Dangers

**Wind chill:** Wind increases heat loss dramatically

| Temperature | Wind 10 kph | Wind 40 kph | Wind 80 kph |
|---|---|---|---|
| -10°C | -13°C | -25°C | -38°C |
| -20°C | -25°C | -40°C | -55°C |
| -40°C | -45°C | -65°C | -80°C |

Wind doubling wind chill effect; 80 kph wind in -20°C creates -55°C equivalent.

**Duration:** Blizzards last 6–48 hours typically; can last longer

**Resource management:**
- Food consumption increases in extreme cold (body burns more calories)
- Dehydration risk (cold air is dry; don't realize sweating/water loss)
- Fuel consumption for heat may exceed supplies

## Arctic Wildlife Hazards

Large predators (polar bears) and dangerous animals require specific protocols.

### Polar Bears

**Behavior:**
- Solitary hunters
- Attracted to food/garbage (excellent smell)
- Aggressive if hungry or defending cubs
- Can run 40 km/h; hard to escape

**Prevention:**
- Store food in bear-proof containers, away from camp
- Never leave food unattended
- Noise: make sounds while traveling (bell, voices) to warn bears away
- Group size: travel in groups of 3+ (bears less likely to approach)
- Weapons: rifle or shotgun if available (deterrent, last resort)

**If encounter (POLAR BEAR SPECIFIC — different rules apply for grizzly/black bears, see wildlife-encounter-protocols guide):**
- Don't run (triggers pursuit instinct)
- Make yourself large; back away slowly
- Group together (appear larger)
- Use noise, flares, or weapons if polar bear charges
- Playing dead NOT effective against polar bears (they are predatory hunters and will continue attacking)
- For grizzly/brown bear encounters, see the wildlife-encounter-protocols guide — the correct response differs significantly

### Musk Oxen

**Behavior:**
- Herd defense: circle with horns out
- Charge if approached
- Dangerous in rutting season (summer)

**Avoidance:**
- Keep distance (100+ meters)
- Don't approach calves (mothers attack)
- Avoid in summer (aggressive)

### Wolves and Feral Dogs

**Behavior:**
- Pack hunters; avoid humans usually
- Aggressive if hungry or protecting kill
- Can run far; hard to outrun

**Avoidance:**
- Noise deters (most packs avoid)
- Fire and light discourage
- Don't approach kills or dens
- Livestock vulnerable; keep near camp

### Wolverines

**Behavior:**
- Small (~15 kg) but extremely aggressive
- Tear into tents, equipment
- Powerful jaws; can damage meat stores

**Prevention:**
- Secure caches high (hang from tree/cliff)
- Steel containers for food
- Trap or scare if in camp area

## Caloric Requirements in Extreme Cold

In Arctic cold, body consumes calories at 2–3x normal rate to generate heat.

### Baseline Caloric Needs

| Activity | Normal Temp | Extreme Cold (-30°C+) |
|---|---|---|
| Sleeping | 1,200–1,500 | 2,000–2,500 |
| Light activity (reading, camp work) | 1,500–2,000 | 3,000–3,500 |
| Moderate activity (hiking, hunting) | 2,000–3,000 | 4,500–6,000 |
| Heavy labor (cutting wood, construction) | 3,000–4,000 | 6,000–8,000 |

**Extreme cold:** Add 500–2,000 calories/day for thermoregulation

### Macronutrient Priorities

**Fat:** 50%+ of calories (fat provides 9 cal/gram vs. 4 for carbs)
- Seal blubber, fish oil, animal fat
- Highest calorie density; critical in cold

**Protein:** 20–30% of calories
- Meat, fish, insects
- Sustains muscle; prevents starvation syndrome

**Carbohydrates:** 20–30% of calories
- Quick energy
- Less critical than fat in cold

### Food Types for Cold

| Food | Calorie/gram | Cold Advantage |
|---|---|---|
| Animal fat | 9 | Highest calories; won't freeze solid |
| Pemmican (dried meat + fat) | 8 | Shelf-stable; lightweight; high calories |
| Fish | 1.8 | Available if fishing; moderate calories |
| Nuts | 6 | Calorie-dense; portable |
| Hard bread | 3.5 | Won't freeze; heavy |
| Chocolate | 5 | Quick energy; morale boost |

### Eating Schedule

**Frequent small meals:** Better than 3 large meals
- Metabolism stays elevated
- Prevents energy crashes
- Easier to chew/digest frozen food in small portions

**Warm drinks:** Essential morale, heat input, hydration
- Warm tea provides 50–100 calories
- Thaws internal temperature
- Psychological benefit significant

:::warning
Extreme cold can suppress appetite while caloric needs surge. People eat less despite needing more, leading to rapid malnutrition. Force feeding necessary; track calories consciously.
:::
