Prosthetics replace lost limbs; orthotics support weak or injured limbs. In resource-limited settings, functional prosthetics and orthotics significantly improve mobility and quality of life.

![Prosthetic limb components and socket fitting diagram](../assets/svgs/prosthetics-fabrication.svg)

:::danger
**Medical Disclaimer**

Prosthetic and orthotic fitting requires assessment and measurement beyond this guide. This content describes basic principles and construction methods; fabrication and fitting should ideally be overseen by trained medical professionals or experienced practitioners. Poorly fitted prosthetics cause pressure sores, gait problems, and secondary injuries. If possible, consult medical personnel before fitting.
:::

<section id="assessment-measurement">

## Assessment and Measurement

### Initial Assessment

Before fabricating, understand:

**Amputation level:**
- Transtibial (below-knee): Knee joint preserved; simpler prosthetic
- Transfemoral (above-knee): Knee replacement required; more complex
- Transradial (below-elbow): Hand/wrist function goal
- Transhumeral (above-elbow): Shoulder control possible

**Residual limb (stump) condition:**
- Length: Determines lever arm and prosthetic design
- Shape: Conical, cylindrical, or irregular (affects socket fitting)
- Skin condition: Scarring, sensitivity, fragility
- Bone: Prominent bone end (risk for pressure sores); skin coverage
- Swelling: Fresh amputations swell; may require temporary prosthetic

**Overall health:**
- Cardiovascular fitness (determines gait capability)
- Other injuries or weakness (affects prosthetic tolerance)
- Mental state: Depression/trauma requires psychological support

### Measurements

**Transtibial (below-knee):**
1. **Residual limb length:** From knee joint to end of stump (in cm)
2. **Circumference:** At mid-point and at end
3. **Knee width:** Medial to lateral knee joint (for socket width)
4. **Other leg length:** Below-knee from knee to floor (determines prosthetic length)

**Transfemoral (above-knee):**
1. **Residual limb length:** Hip to end of stump
2. **Circumference:** At multiple points (hip, mid-thigh, distal end)
3. **Hip width:** For alignment reference
4. **Other leg length:** For prosthetic length determination

**Upper limb:**
1. **Shoulder to residual limb end:** Determines socket location
2. **Residual limb circumference**
3. **Hand size** (if functional hand needed)

<section id="below-knee-prosthetics">

## Below-Knee (Transtibial) Prosthetics

### Simple Peg-Leg

Minimal design; adequate for persons with high pain tolerance.

**Materials:**
- Wood (hardwood; oak or walnut preferred)
- Leather (for socket)
- Metal straps or nails

**Construction:**

1. **Socket creation:**
   - Carve/shape wood block into socket fitting residual limb
   - Internal surface shaped to match stump contour (slightly loose: 0.5–1cm clearance)
   - Depth: From knee level down stump length
   - Secure leather or cloth padding inside for cushioning

2. **Peg shaft:**
   - Wood post extending downward from socket
   - Diameter: 2–3 inches (adequate to support body weight)
   - Length: Measured to match other leg length when standing normally
   - Consider slight bend (2–5 degrees) matching natural stance

3. **Foot:**
   - Flat or slightly curved wood base (prevents tipping)
   - Size: ~3 inches wide, 8 inches long
   - Underside contoured to match ground contact
   - May add rubber or leather covering for grip

4. **Straps:**
   - Leather straps from socket up to thigh/waist (holds prosthetic in place)
   - Wraparound design; laced or buckled
   - Prevents dropping during walking

**Limitations:**
- Primitive gait (stiff leg; no knee bend)
- High energy expenditure
- May cause pain in hip and lower back (compensatory posture)

### Improved Below-Knee Prosthetic

Allows knee bending; significantly better function.

**Components:**

1. **Socket (same as above):** Leather-lined wood socket fitted to residual limb

2. **Knee joint:**
   - Hinge: Metal or wooden joint allowing bend
   - Typically 0–120 degrees flexion range
   - Options:
     - Metal bolt as hinge (simple; allows free swing)
     - Spring-loaded hinge (resists bending; more natural)
     - Polycentric hinge (more durable; complex to fabricate)

3. **Shank (lower leg):**
   - Wood or segmented wood/metal post from knee to ankle
   - Flexible (allows bending and alignment adjustment)
   - Strength: Must support full body weight

4. **Foot:**
   - More complex: Provides ankle function
   - Solid wood with curved bottom (rocker shape)
   - Or: Wooden foot with metal hinge allowing dorsi/plantarflexion (up/down bend)

5. **Control devices (optional):**
   - Knee lock: Lever or mechanism to lock knee when standing (prevents knee buckling during single-leg stance)
   - Swing-phase control: Resistance during swing (prevents leg swinging too fast)

**Assembly:**
1. Socket mounted to knee joint (metal or wood joint)
2. Shank attached to knee joint
3. Foot attached to ankle/shank interface
4. Straps from socket to waist (suspension)

**Gait with improved BK prosthetic:**
- Smoother than peg-leg
- Can walk at near-normal speed
- Reduced back strain
- May still require crutch or cane for balance

<section id="above-knee-prosthetics">

## Above-Knee (Transfemoral) Prosthetics

More complex; requires both hip and knee replacement.

### Socket and Alignment

**Socket design:**
- Fits from below waist to end of residual limb
- Must support body weight; secure socket is critical
- Padding inside (leather, cloth, foam) for comfort
- Straps: Hip belt and thigh straps for suspension

**Alignment:**
- Hip joint: Offset slightly forward (prevents backward hip motion)
- Knee joint: Positioned slightly behind hip (creates stable base; resists knee bending during stance)
- Ankle: Positioned to allow natural foot-floor contact

### Hip and Knee Joints

**Hip joint:**
- Usually ball-and-socket style (allows full range of motion)
- Metal joint (copper or iron) or wooden approximation
- Permits hip flexion (swing leg forward), abduction (out to side), rotation

**Knee joint:**
- More complex than BK; must resist bending during stance (gravity tries to collapse knee)
- Options:
  - Locked knee: Held rigid during stance (requires conscious effort to unlock for swing)
  - Spring-loaded: Resists bending but allows controlled swing
  - Polycentric: Multipoint joint (more natural motion; very difficult to fabricate)
- Typical knee lock: Lever or pin mechanism user operates

### Shank and Foot

Same as improved BK prosthetic (wood shank, jointed or solid foot).

### Control and Function

**Walking:**
1. Stance phase (prosthetic leg supports weight):
   - Knee locked to prevent collapse
   - Hip extended (leg back)

2. Swing phase (non-prosthetic leg stance):
   - User unlocks prosthetic knee
   - Contracts hip flexors to swing prosthetic forward
   - Knee swings like pendulum
   - Relock knee at foot contact

**Challenges:**
- Unlocking/relocking knee manually requires coordination
- Hip fatigue (TF amputees use more hip muscle)
- Energy: TF prosthetic gait requires 20–40% more energy than intact walking
- Balance: Loss of leg below hip significantly affects balance

<section id="upper-limb-prosthetics">

## Upper Limb Prosthetics

### Below-Elbow (Transradial)

**Socket:**
- Fits over residual forearm
- Extends from elbow toward hand
- Secured with arm/shoulder straps

**Hand component:**

**Passive hand (simplest):**
- Wooden hand or leather-padded mitten shape
- Fixed in gentle grasp position
- Allows holding objects but not manipulation
- Cosmetically acceptable; functionally limited

**Active hook prosthetic:**
- Metal hook (opens/closes)
- Operated by shoulder motion (user pulls shoulder back to open hook)
- Cable or strap connects shoulder to hook mechanism
- Very functional; less cosmetic
- Allows powerful grip and tool use

**Design of hook:**
1. Metal hook frame (metal or steel)
2. Hinged hook (rotates open/closed)
3. Spring return (hook closes automatically)
4. Cable attachment point (runs along arm)
5. Shoulder harness (distributes force; prevents arm strain)

### Above-Elbow (Transhumeral)

**Socket:** Extends from shoulder to residual arm end

**Elbow component:**
- Hinged elbow joint (operated by shoulder/back muscles)
- Locking mechanism (locks elbow at different angles for tasks)
- May be manual lock or spring-loaded

**Forearm:** Extends from elbow component

**Hand:** Hook or passive hand (same as BE)

**Control:**
- Complex; requires learning scapular (shoulder blade) control
- Shoulder motion elevates arm; pulls shoulder back to unlock elbow and open hand
- High learning curve; significant residual limb mobility required

</section>

<section id="orthotic-splints">

## Orthotic Splints and Braces

Orthotics support weak, injured, or deformed limbs without replacing them.

### Ankle-Foot Orthosis (AFO)

Supports ankle and foot; prevents inversion/eversion (turning); assists dorsiflexion (lifting foot).

**Use cases:**
- Stroke (weak ankle/foot)
- Cerebral palsy
- Peroneal nerve injury (foot drop)
- Arthritis

**Simple AFO design:**
1. **Foot plate:**
   - Wood or metal plate under foot
   - Extends from toes to heel
   - Prevents downward-only motion (plantarflexion)

2. **Vertical support:**
   - Post extending upward from foot plate
   - Wraps around lower leg (lateral side usually)
   - Prevents ankle turning

3. **Straps:**
   - Ankle strap across foot
   - Calf strap across lower leg
   - Hold foot/ankle in correct alignment

**Fitting:**
- Foot at 90 degrees to lower leg (neutral angle)
- Straps snug but not cutting off circulation
- No pressure on bone prominences

### Knee-Ankle-Foot Orthosis (KAFO)

Supports knee, ankle, and foot; prevents knee buckling.

**Components:**
1. Foot plate (same as AFO)
2. Ankle support (same as AFO)
3. Knee joint (hinge or lock)
4. Thigh cuff (wraps around thigh; secured with straps)
5. Knee lock: Pin or lever preventing knee bending during stance

**Use cases:**
- Quadriceps weakness (cannot straighten knee)
- Knee ligament damage
- Paralysis below knee

### Hand Splint (Resting Position)

Maintains hand in functional position, preventing contractures (permanent shortening of muscles).

**Design:**
1. **Forearm support:**
   - Rigid board extending along forearm
   - Secured with straps
   - Prevents wrist drop

2. **Hand support:**
   - Curved board supporting palm
   - Thumb abducted (away from fingers)
   - Fingers slightly flexed (natural resting position)
   - Prevents full flexion contracture

3. **Padding:**
   - Leather or cloth lining all contact areas
   - Prevents pressure sores

**Wearing schedule:**
- Typically worn during sleep and inactive periods
- Removed for therapy exercises
- Prevents progressive deformity if used consistently

<section id="materials-fabrication">

## Materials and Fabrication Techniques

### Wood Selection

**Hardwoods preferred:**
- **Oak:** Strong, straight grain; good for weight-bearing
- **Walnut:** Dense; resists denting
- **Ash:** Flexible yet strong; good for shanks
- **Beech:** Hard and fine-grained; suitable for joints

**Softwoods acceptable (less durable):**
- **Pine:** Light; adequate for non-load-bearing components
- **Poplar:** Easy to shape; suitable for sockets

### Metal Components

**Hinges/joints:**
- **Iron or steel:** Forged or blacksmithed
- **Copper or brass:** Softer; suitable for ball-and-socket joints
- **Bolts:** Standard bolts or custom-forged hinge pins

**Straps and fasteners:**
- **Metal rivets:** Permanent joint (most durable)
- **Leather laces:** Removable but less strong
- **Metal buckles:** Adjustable (good for fitting adjustments)

### Leather and Padding

**Leather for sockets:**
- Vegetable-tanned leather (not chrome-tanned; chrome causes skin irritation)
- Thick (3–5mm)
- Molded to socket shape
- Lined with softer cloth or chamois

**Padding:**
- Cloth (cotton or linen)
- Felt
- Leather suede (soft side)
- Foam (if available; though foam degrades over years)

### Assembly

**Joining methods:**
1. **Mortise and tenon:** Traditional wood joinery; strong
2. **Bolted:** Holes through wood components; tightened bolt
3. **Riveted:** Permanent joint; very strong but unrepairable
4. **Leather laced:** Flexible; allows adjustment
5. **Glued:** Using hide glue or casein (see adhesives guide)

<section id="socket-fitting">

## Socket Fitting and Comfort

### Pressure Areas to Avoid

**High-risk pressure points (create sores):**

**Transtibial:**
- Tibial crest (sharp bone at front of shin)
- Fibular head (bony prominence outside knee)
- End of tibia (bone end; very sensitive)

**Transfemoral:**
- Ischial tuberosity (sit bones)
- Greater trochanter (hip bone point)
- Adductor longus (muscle attachment; inner thigh)

**Design solution:** Socket material should distribute pressure away from bony points toward muscular areas.

### Pressure Distribution

1. **Relief areas:**
   - Carve/shape socket to have recesses over high-risk bone points
   - Allows pressure to concentrate on muscular areas (sides, back of thigh, etc.)
   - Reduces sore formation significantly

2. **Padding:**
   - Place additional leather/cloth padding in high-stress areas
   - Distribute pressure over wider surface
   - Test fit: Residual limb skin should not blanch (turn white) under finger pressure (indicates excessive pressure)

3. **Straps adjustment:**
   - Straps should be snug but not cutting off blood flow
   - If fingers turn blue/purple, reduce strap tightness
   - Sensation loss indicates poor circulation; loosen immediately

### Break-In Period

**New prosthetic:**
- Wear 1–2 hours first day
- Inspect skin for redness after removal
- Gradually increase wearing time
- Redness disappearing within 30 minutes of removal = acceptable
- Persistent red marks or blistering = socket adjustment needed

**Adjustments during break-in:**
- Trim/pad areas causing pressure points
- Careful grinding/sanding to reshape socket
- Test-fit repeatedly
- Full break-in may require 2–4 weeks

<section id="gait-training">

## Gait Training and Mobility

### Below-Knee Gait

**With improved (bending-knee) prosthetic:**

1. **Stance phase (prosthetic leg):**
   - Weight on prosthetic
   - Knee locked (prevents buckling)
   - Natural heel-to-toe rolling

2. **Swing phase (natural leg):**
   - Weight on natural leg
   - Prosthetic knee unlocks
   - Swing leg forward like pendulum
   - Relock knee at contact (automated or manual)

**Gait pattern:**
- Symmetric stepping (similar cadence each leg)
- Natural-appearing walking once balanced
- Energy cost: 10–20% higher than intact walking

### Above-Knee Gait

More complex; requires conscious control.

**Stance phase:**
- Weight on prosthetic leg
- Knee locked; hip extended (leg behind)
- More pronounced trunk lean (shifts weight over prosthetic leg)

**Swing phase:**
- Weight on natural leg
- Conscious shoulder motion to swing prosthetic forward
- Hip flexion drives prosthetic forward
- Knee swing (like pendulum if loaded correctly)

**Challenges:**
- Hip fatigue (muscles work harder without knee assist)
- Balance: Loss of lower leg affects proprioception
- Mental effort: Conscious control of artificial joint

### Assistive Devices (Crutches, Cane)

**Below-knee:**
- May walk without assistive device (if balance good)
- Cane preferred (reduces energy, improves balance)
- Crutches used early in rehabilitation

**Above-knee:**
- Crutches or walker nearly always used (improves balance, reduces energy)
- Single-point cane unsuitable (provides inadequate support)
- Bilateral crutches or four-point walker preferred

### Training Protocol

1. **Balance training (seated):**
   - Learn socket fit; sitting/standing balance
   - No movement initially

2. **Standing balance:**
   - Stand near support (railing)
   - Practice weight shifting
   - Familiarize with prosthetic mechanics

3. **Walking with assistance:**
   - Walk with therapist/helper support
   - Practice gait mechanics
   - Build endurance

4. **Independent walking:**
   - Gradual increase in distance/speed
   - Obstacle negotiation (stairs, uneven ground)
   - Complex environments (crowded spaces, outdoors)

**Typical timeline:**
- BK: 4–8 weeks to independent community walking
- AK: 8–12 weeks to independent household walking; community walking may require longer

<section id="psychological-support">

## Psychological Support for Amputees

Amputation is significant loss; psychological adjustment is as important as prosthetic function.

### Grief and Adjustment

**Expected reactions:**
- Denial
- Anger
- Grief (sense of loss)
- Depression
- Acceptance (gradual; not universal)

**Timing:**
- Acute phase (immediate): Medical focus
- Early rehabilitation (weeks 1–4): Physical/emotional adjustment
- Extended rehabilitation (months): Adjustment to new baseline

### Support Strategies

1. **Normalize experience:**
   - Introduce to other amputees (peer support; powerful)
   - Discuss realistic expectations
   - Celebrate small wins (standing balance, first steps)

2. **Address phantom limb pain:**
   - Common; person feels pain in missing limb
   - Validate experience (real neurologically)
   - Distraction techniques, visualization, gradual acceptance
   - Usually decreases with time

3. **Encourage activity:**
   - Return to previous activities when possible
   - Identify new activities (sports, work)
   - Build confidence and sense of purpose

4. **Professional support:**
   - Counseling or therapy (if available)
   - Discussion groups (community/religious organizations)
   - Family involvement (partners/children need education too)

<section id="maintenance-adjustment">

## Maintenance and Adjustment

### Residual Limb Changes

**Early changes (first 3–6 months):**
- Swelling decreases (volume loss of 15–30%)
- Socket becomes loose
- Requires new socket or padding adjustments

**Long-term changes:**
- Volume stabilizes (5+ years)
- Bone resorption (slow loss of bone from residual limb)
- Skin changes (thinning, reduced elasticity)
- Requires socket re-fitting periodically

### Maintenance Schedule

**Daily:**
- Inspect prosthetic for damage (cracks, loose joints)
- Check socket cleanliness (wash inside)
- Inspect residual limb for redness, sores, skin integrity

**Weekly:**
- Light repair (tighten bolts, adjust straps)
- Deep clean socket (remove sweat/bacteria)

**Monthly:**
- Check alignment (stand in front of mirror; symmetry check)
- Test joint function (smooth movement, no catching)
- Assess gait changes (indicates need for adjustment)

**Annually (or as needed):**
- Pad replacement (worn padding loses cushioning)
- Strap replacement (leather weakens)
- Socket adjustment/re-fabrication if volume changes significant

### Common Repairs

| Problem | Likely Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Knee lock broken | Excessive force; wear | Rebuild lock mechanism; consider stronger joint |
| Socket cracks | Impact; over-tightening | Small cracks: Glue and wrap leather; large: New socket |
| Straps torn | Normal wear; stress points | Patch or replace leather strap |
| Foot delaminating | Water damage; age | Reglue components; may need foot replacement |
| Loose joints | Bolts loosening | Tighten bolts; consider threadlocker compound |

<section id="conclusion">

## Conclusion

Prosthetic and orthotic fabrication in resource-limited settings requires understanding anatomy, careful measurement, and skilled craftsmanship with wood, leather, and metal. Below-knee prosthetics offer significantly better function than peg-legs; above-knee prosthetics are more complex but improve mobility considerably. Proper socket fitting, pressure relief, and gradual gait training are essential for successful prosthetic use. Psychological support and realistic expectation-setting are equally important as physical fabrication. Regular maintenance and adjustment throughout the life of the prosthetic ensure continued function and comfort.

</section>
