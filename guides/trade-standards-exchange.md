:::warning
**Trade Disputes Cause Conflicts.** Unfair or perceived-unfair trade practices have started more wars than territorial disputes throughout human history. When communities depend on trade for survival essentials — food, medicine, fuel, tools — a breakdown in trade relationships can be as devastating as a natural disaster. Every standard, every exchange rate, and every trade agreement in this guide exists to prevent the disputes that arise from ambiguity, deception, and power imbalance. Take trade governance seriously — your community's external relationships depend on it.
:::

<section id="introduction">

## Introduction to Post-Infrastructure Trade

Trade is the engine of specialization. Without trade, every community must produce everything it needs independently — an impossibility for all but the most basic subsistence. With trade, communities can specialize in what they produce most efficiently and exchange surpluses for what they lack. A farming community trades grain for metalwork. A coastal community trades salt and fish for timber. A community with a skilled medical team trades health services for food and materials.

But trade requires trust, and trust requires standards. When two communities agree to trade "a sack of grain for a bundle of nails," they need shared understanding of: How big is a sack? What quality of grain? How many nails? What size? What if the grain is moldy or the nails are bent? These questions seem trivial but they are the foundation of functioning trade. Without agreed standards, every transaction is a potential dispute.

:::info-box
**Why Barter Fails at Scale:** Pure barter (direct exchange of goods) requires a "double coincidence of wants" — you must have what I want AND I must have what you want, at the same time, in the right quantities. This works for occasional trades between neighbors but collapses when trade involves dozens of goods across multiple communities. Medium-of-exchange systems (commodity money, labor credits, representative tokens) solve this problem by decoupling buying from selling.
:::

## Standardized Weights and Measures

### Why Standardization Comes First

Before you can set exchange rates, negotiate trade agreements, or create currency, you need measurement standards that all parties trust. If your community's "kilogram" is different from your trading partner's "kilogram," no trade agreement is enforceable.

### Establishing Reference Standards

**Weight standards:** Acquire or create a set of reference weights. Salvaged precision weights from laboratories, pharmacies, and industrial facilities are ideal. Failing that, create reference weights from uniform materials:

- Pure water at room temperature: 1 liter = 1 kilogram (close enough for trade purposes)
- Use a balance scale (two-pan) rather than a spring scale — balance scales compare directly against reference weights and do not require calibration
- Create reference weight sets: 10g, 50g, 100g, 500g, 1kg, 5kg, 10kg
- Store reference weights securely — these are community property, not personal tools
- Verify reference weights against each other regularly (four 250g weights should equal one 1kg weight)

**Volume standards:** Create reference vessels for liquid and dry measure:

- 1 liter container (marked and verified against water weight: 1 liter of water at room temperature weighs 1 kg)
- Standard containers for common trade goods: grain bushel, fuel container, etc.
- Mark containers permanently — stamped metal, carved wood, or etched glass

**Length standards:** Create reference length measures:

- 1-meter bar (salvaged precision instrument or carefully measured from known references)
- Duplicate bars for distribution to trade partners
- Verify periodically against the community reference standard

:::tip
**The Market Master's Kit:** Designate a trusted community member as the Market Master responsible for maintaining reference standards and verifying measurements during trade. Equip them with: a verified balance scale, a complete reference weight set, reference volume containers, and a reference length bar. Every significant trade should be measured using the Market Master's verified instruments, not the seller's or buyer's. This eliminates measurement disputes.
:::

For detailed measurement system design, see <a href="../measurement-standards.html">Measurement Standards</a>.

### Quality Standards

Weight and volume are not enough. Quality standards prevent disputes over trade goods:

**Grain grading:**
- Grade A: Clean, dry (under 12% moisture), less than 2% foreign material, no mold or insect damage
- Grade B: Clean, dry, less than 5% foreign material, minor cosmetic defects
- Grade C: May contain up to 10% foreign material, may have minor moisture issues, usable but not premium
- Reject: Moldy, pest-infested, or contaminated — not tradeable for human consumption

**Fuel grading:**
- Gasoline/diesel: Clear, no visible water separation, no visible particulates, burns cleanly
- Biodiesel: Meets minimum viscosity and combustion standards (see testing protocol)
- Firewood: Species identified, seasoned vs. green, approximate moisture content, cut to standard lengths

**Metalwork grading:**
- Specify material (mild steel, hardened steel, copper, etc.)
- Specify dimensions with tolerance (e.g., "nails, 75mm +/- 3mm, mild steel")
- Specify finish (galvanized, oiled, bare)

:::warning
**Fraud Destroys Trade Networks.** A single instance of a community passing off Grade C grain as Grade A, watering fuel, or misrepresenting the weight of goods will destroy that community's trade reputation — potentially permanently. Trade networks are built on trust, and trust is far harder to rebuild than to maintain. Establish and enforce quality standards rigorously, including penalties for community members who commit fraud in external trade.
:::

## Exchange Rate Determination

### The Labor Theory Baseline

The simplest approach to exchange rates: price everything in terms of the labor required to produce it.

**Example calculation:**
- 10 kg of wheat requires approximately 2 hours of skilled agricultural labor (planting through harvest, amortized per kg)
- 1 kg of hand-forged nails requires approximately 3 hours of skilled blacksmith labor
- Therefore: 10 kg wheat = roughly 0.67 kg nails (both represent about 2-3 hours of skilled labor)

This approach is intuitive and perceived as fair. However, it has limitations:
- Not all labor is equally skilled or dangerous
- Scarcity affects value independently of labor (water in the desert is worth more than water by a river, regardless of collection effort)
- Perishability matters (fresh food must trade quickly; preserved food and tools can wait for better terms)

### The Market-Discovery Approach

Allow exchange rates to emerge from actual trade interactions. Establish a regular market day (weekly or bi-weekly) where community members and visiting traders exchange goods. Over time, stable exchange rates emerge from repeated transactions.

**Key principles for market-based pricing:**
- Publish observed exchange rates weekly so all participants have information
- Track trends — rising prices for a good signal increasing scarcity or increasing demand
- Intervene only when manipulation is detected (hoarding to create artificial scarcity, price fixing between colluding traders)

Establish a community reference table of approximate exchange rates. Update quarterly based on observed market rates and seasonal factors.

## Currency Alternatives

### Commodity-Backed Currency

Select a durable, divisible, portable commodity as the basis for currency:

**Salt:** Historically proven currency commodity (the word "salary" derives from salt). Easily measured, universally useful for food preservation, does not spoil. See <a href="../salt-production.html">Salt Production</a>. Drawback: heavy relative to value.

**Grain tokens:** Issue tokens redeemable for a specific weight of grain held in community storage. The token is the currency; the grain is the backing. Requires trusted storage management and regular audits.

**Metal weights:** Small standardized pieces of copper, silver, or gold (if available). Intrinsically valuable, durable, portable. Requires verification capability (scales, possibly acid testing for precious metals).

:::tip
**Whatever You Choose, Keep It Simple:** The best currency system is one that everyone understands. If your currency requires a finance degree to use, it will fail. One community found success with simple clay tokens stamped with their community seal — each token redeemable for 1 hour of skilled labor or 1 kg of wheat at the community store. Everyone understood the system because it was linked to tangible, familiar quantities.
:::

### Labor Credit Systems

Labor credits are a promissory system: when you perform work for the community or another member, you receive credits. When you want goods or services, you spend credits. No physical commodity changes hands.

**Advantages:**
- No need for commodity reserves
- Cannot be stolen or counterfeited easily (credits exist in ledgers, not as physical objects)
- Directly ties value to contribution
- Eliminates the "I have grain but need nails, and the blacksmith wants fish" problem

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires trusted record-keeping (see <a href="../double-entry-bookkeeping.html">Double-Entry Bookkeeping</a>)
- Difficult to use for inter-community trade (other communities may not honor your credits)
- Inflationary if credits are issued without corresponding productive capacity
- Requires agreement on what different types of labor are worth (is 1 hour of farming = 1 hour of surgery?)

### Hybrid Systems

Most successful post-infrastructure economies use hybrid systems:

- **Internal economy:** Labor credits or community tokens for transactions within the community
- **External trade:** Commodity-based exchange (grain, salt, fuel, manufactured goods) for trade with other communities
- **Conversion mechanism:** A community exchange that converts internal credits to external trade goods and vice versa

## Inter-Community Trade Agreements

### Why Formal Agreements Matter

Informal trade between communities works when both sides are honest and conditions are stable. When scarcity hits, disputes arise, or new leadership takes over, informal understandings evaporate. Written agreements survive personnel changes and selective memory.

### Elements of a Trade Agreement

Every inter-community trade agreement should address:

**1. Parties and representatives:** Who is party to the agreement? Who has authority to negotiate on behalf of each community? How are disputes escalated?

**2. Goods covered:** Which goods does each community agree to trade? In what quantities? At what quality standards?

**3. Exchange rates:** Agreed rates for covered goods, or a mechanism for determining rates (e.g., "rates shall be set quarterly by mutual agreement based on observed market values").

**4. Measurement standards:** Which community's weights and measures are used? Ideally, both communities verify using agreed reference standards.

**5. Delivery terms:** Who transports goods? Who bears the risk of loss during transport? Where is the exchange point?

**6. Payment terms:** Immediate exchange? Credit? Advance payment? What happens if one party cannot deliver as promised?

**7. Quality dispute resolution:** How are quality disputes resolved? Who inspects goods? What remedy is available for substandard delivery?

**8. Duration and renewal:** How long does the agreement last? How is it renewed or terminated?

**9. Force majeure:** What happens when one party cannot meet obligations due to circumstances beyond control (drought, attack, epidemic)?

**10. Dispute resolution:** Neutral arbiter? Joint committee? Mediation process? This clause is the most important one — it is what you use when everything else fails.

:::warning
**Never Trade Desperate.** When your community urgently needs something — medicine during an epidemic, food during a famine — you are in the weakest possible negotiating position. Predatory trading partners will exploit this. Build strategic reserves of essential goods specifically to avoid desperate trading situations. Diversify trading partners so no single community can hold you hostage. Negotiate agreements for emergency supply before emergencies happen, when you can negotiate from a position of relative strength.
:::

### Trade Caravans and Logistics

Regular trade requires reliable logistics:

- **Scheduled trade runs:** Establish regular schedules (monthly, quarterly) for trade caravans between partner communities. Regularity builds trust and allows both sides to plan.
- **Security:** Trade goods in transit are targets. Escort caravans appropriately. Vary routes if security conditions warrant. See <a href="../supply-chain-logistics.html">Supply Chain & Logistics</a>.
- **Accounting:** Every trade run must be fully documented: goods sent, goods received, exchange rates used, condition of goods, any disputes. Both parties should maintain records and reconcile periodically.
- **Communication:** Establish a system for communicating trade needs and offers between partner communities between trade runs. Runner systems, radio, signal systems — see <a href="../visual-audio-signal-systems.html">Signals & Communication</a>.

:::tip
**The Trade Fair Model:** Consider hosting periodic trade fairs (monthly or quarterly) where multiple communities gather at a neutral location to trade. This creates competition among sellers (better prices for buyers), allows communities to find new trading partners, and builds broader inter-community relationships. Designate the trade fair as a neutral zone with agreed rules of conduct — no weapons, no coercion, disputes resolved by a fair master. Historical trade fairs were the foundation of economic development for centuries.
:::

## Preventing Exploitation and Market Manipulation

### Price Gouging and Profiteering

In scarcity conditions, individuals or communities may hoard essential goods and demand exorbitant prices. Mechanisms to prevent this:

- **Price ceilings on essentials:** The community governance body sets maximum exchange rates for food, water, medicine, and fuel during declared shortages. Selling above the ceiling is punishable.
- **Anti-hoarding rules:** Maximum personal stock limits for essential goods during shortages. Excess must be sold at fair market rates or contributed to community reserves. For rationing systems, see <a href="../rationing-distribution.html">Rationing & Distribution</a>.
- **Community reserves:** Maintain strategic reserves of essential goods that can be released to stabilize prices during shortages.

### Counterfeiting and Fraud

If your community uses physical currency (tokens, coins, stamped weights):

- Use materials and markings that are difficult to replicate without community tools
- Train trade officials to verify currency authenticity
- Maintain a ledger of all currency issued (for token-based systems)
- Treat counterfeiting as a serious offense — it undermines the entire economic system

### Monitoring and Enforcement

Designate a Trade Standards Officer or committee responsible for:

- Verifying weights and measures used in trade
- Inspecting quality of traded goods
- Investigating complaints of fraud or price manipulation
- Maintaining the reference exchange rate table
- Recommending policy changes to the governance body

This role requires both technical knowledge (measurement, quality assessment) and social trust (perceived fairness and incorruptibility). Compensate this role adequately — a Trade Standards Officer who is underpaid is a Trade Standards Officer who is bribable.

:::info-box
**Trust Is Your Most Valuable Trade Good.** A community known for fair dealing, accurate measures, honest quality representation, and reliable delivery will attract trade partners. A community known for short-weighting, grade inflation, or reneging on agreements will find itself isolated. In the long run, a reputation for fairness is worth more than any short-term gain from deception. See <a href="../reputation-systems-trust.html">Reputation Systems & Trust</a> for building and maintaining institutional trust.
:::
