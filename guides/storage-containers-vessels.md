## Overview: Vessel and Container Types

Storage vessels are essential infrastructure for food preservation, water storage, and material organization in off-grid systems. Different container types serve different purposes: wooden barrels for liquid storage, ceramic vessels for grain/dry goods, baskets for ventilated storage, gourds for small portions.

![Storage containers and vessel making reference diagram](../assets/svgs/storage-containers-vessels-1.svg)

**Key design considerations:**

1. **Material integrity:** Seal must prevent air/moisture infiltration (for dry storage) or prevent leakage (for liquid storage)
2. **Durability:** Container must resist degradation from stored contents (salt corrodes steel, acids weaken clay, oils soften some seals)
3. **Practicality:** Must be constructible with local materials and tools
4. **Cleanability:** Interior must be accessible for cleaning/inspection
5. **Stackability:** Multiple containers should not deform under weight

**This guide covers:**
- Wooden barrel construction (coopering)
- Ceramic vessel throwing and firing
- Woven basket construction
- Gourd preparation and treatment
- Birch bark container assembly
- Sealing and food-safety methods
- Sizing guidelines for common commodities

## Wooden Barrel Construction (Coopering)

### Barrel Anatomy and Sizing

A barrel consists of three main parts:

1. **Staves:** Curved wooden boards forming the barrel sides; typically 20–40 per barrel
2. **Hoops:** Metal or wooden bands holding staves together; typically 5–8 per barrel
3. **Heads:** Flat or slightly curved wooden discs sealing the top and bottom

**Standard barrel sizing (US gallons/liters):**

| Type | Gallons | Liters | Typical Use |
|------|---------|--------|------------|
| **Tight (water) barrel** | 55 | 208 | Water, wine, oil |
| **Flour barrel** | 48 | 182 | Grain, flour |
| **Butter churn** | 5–10 | 19–38 | Butter, small goods |
| **Wooden keg** | 10–30 | 38–114 | Powder, nails, small quantities |

**Constructing a 55-gallon barrel (basic specifications):**

- Stave height: ~850mm
- Barrel diameter at widest: ~570mm
- Stave width: 70–100mm
- Stave thickness: 20–25mm
- Head diameter: ~560mm
- Head thickness: 15–20mm

### Stave Selection and Shaping

**Wood selection:** Oak (traditional, rot-resistant, tight grain) is ideal; ash, chestnut, or other hardwoods acceptable. Softwoods (pine) swell excessively when wet, unsuitable for tight barrels.

**Stave shaping:**

1. **Prepare blanks:** Mill lumber to rough stave dimensions (width + 50mm allowance, thickness ~25mm)
2. **Taper sides:** Taper each stave so top edge is narrower than bottom edge (creates barrel belly when assembled; typically 5–10mm taper over 800mm height)
3. **Curve staves:** Bend staves to barrel profile. Two methods:

   **Steam bending:** Apply steam or boil staves until pliable (~30 min), then clamp to curved form overnight to dry

   **Dry bending:** Stack staves and gradually increase curve over weeks using weights/wedges (slow but doesn't require heat)

4. **Edge joints:** Create tongue-and-groove or simple butt joints where stave edges meet (helps seal, distributes pressure evenly)

**Testing fit:** Loosely assemble staves without hoops; gaps between staves should be <1mm. Adjust as needed.

### Hooping and Pressure

Hoops draw staves together, creating pressure that seals gaps.

**Metal hoops (steel or iron strapping):**

1. Fabricate from flat bar stock (25mm × 3mm typical)
2. Form circular; butt ends and rivet or bolt together
3. Drive hoops over staves, starting with middle hoop, working toward ends
4. Pound with wooden mallet (never metal) to avoid denting; even force around circumference

**Wooden hoops (twisted saplings):**

1. Select flexible wood (ash, hazel, or birch saplings, ~15mm diameter)
2. Bend into circle; overlap ends ~100mm
3. Lash with thin rope or twine (4–6 passes, pulled tight)
4. Slide over staves, tighten lashing

**Hoop tension:** Correct tension produces snug seal without crushing staves. Test: Tap barrel with mallet; should ring (hollow, tight) not thud (loose).

### Heads (Top and Bottom Discs)

Heads can be **flat** (easier to construct, thicker wood needed to prevent sagging) or **slightly dished** (curved upward, thinner wood but more complex).

**Flat head construction:**

1. **Select boards:** Join 3–5 boards edge-to-edge with tongue-and-groove or mortise joints
2. **Cut circle:** Mark center, scriibe circular outline using barrel diameter minus ~10mm (slight gap allows head to drop into barrel and be driven tight)
3. **Sand edges:** Smooth all edges, slightly bevel outer edge (radius ~5mm) to fit snugly
4. **Assembly:** Drop head into barrel, press down firmly; hoops hold it in place

**Groove for head:** Cut shallow groove (3–5mm deep) around inner barrel at head locations; head rests in groove, preventing collapse.

:::info-box
**Dished head advantage:** Curved upward (like a dome), dished heads resist sagging under internal pressure (useful for wine/liquid storage). Require more skill to construct (curved jig, careful steam bending).
:::

### Testing and Sealing

**Initial test:** Fill barrel with water, let sit 24 hours. Small weeping is normal (fibers swell); persistent dripping indicates poor joints.

**Sealing:**

1. **Internal sealant (for liquids):** Apply food-grade sealant inside before filling:
   - **Beeswax:** Melt, brush thin coat on inner surfaces (especially between staves)
   - **Pine pitch:** Heat to pliability, apply with brush; hardens when cool
   - **Lime mortar:** Thin layer of lime + sand mortar on inner bottom

2. **Swelling:** Fill barrel with water, let sit 48 hours (wood swells, seals tighten); drain and refill with storage contents

**Maintenance:** Inspect regularly; replace hoops if rusted/damaged; scrub interior annually.

## Ceramic Storage Vessels

### Hand-Throwing Technique (Wheel or Coil-Building)

**Wheel throwing (requires potter's wheel):**

1. **Center clay:** Place clay ball on spinning wheel; apply gentle pressure to center it
2. **Open:** Widen top opening with thumbs/fingers as wheel spins
3. **Pull walls:** Gradually pull walls upward, thinning clay (typically 5–10mm final thickness)
4. **Shape rim:** Flatten or bevel rim using wooden tool
5. **Cut base:** Remove excess clay from base using wire loop

**Coil-building (no wheel required):**

1. **Roll coils:** Hand-roll clay into long "snakes" (~20mm diameter, 30–50cm length)
2. **Stack coils:** Coil rope in spiral, stacking each coil on previous, smoothing joints
3. **Shape vessel:** Adjust diameter by compressing/expanding coils
4. **Rim finish:** Smooth rim, ensure even thickness

### Vessel Sizing and Proportions

| Use | Capacity | Diameter | Height | Wall Thickness |
|-----|----------|----------|--------|-----------------|
| **Oil/liquid** | 10–20L | 200–250mm | 250–300mm | 8–10mm |
| **Grain/flour** | 20–50L | 300–400mm | 350–450mm | 10–12mm |
| **Fermentation** | 5–10L | 150–200mm | 200–250mm | 8–10mm |

**Wall thickness guidelines:** Thicker walls (10–12mm) resist cracking during drying/firing. Thin walls (<8mm) may crack; very thick walls (>15mm) shrink unevenly and crack.

### Firing and Glazing

**Firing temperatures:**

- **Earthenware:** 1000–1150°C (porous, water-absorbing; adequate for dry goods, requires sealing for liquids)
- **Stoneware:** 1200–1300°C (vitrified, water-resistant; good for liquids)
- **Porcelain:** 1200–1450°C (non-porous, hard; excellent but expensive/difficult to produce)

**Glazing for storage vessels:**

1. **Interior glaze:** Apply to inner surfaces (reduces porosity, aids cleaning)
2. **Exterior glaze:** Optional (aesthetic, also reduces moisture absorption)
3. **Seal gaps:** Glaze seals tiny cracks/pores that would otherwise leak

**Low-fire glaze recipe (earthenware, 1080°C):**
```
Lead oxide (PbO) — 45%
Feldspar — 25%
Silica — 20%
Whiting (CaCO₃) — 10%
```

**Food safety concern:** Lead glazes are toxic. Avoid unless absolutely certain glaze is lead-free. Safer: Stoneware fired to 1200°C+ (vitrification seals surface without glaze).

### Seal and Storage

**Surface sealing (if not fully glazed):**

1. **Beeswax:** Melt, apply thin coat to exterior; hardens to water-resistant finish
2. **Oil:** Food-grade oil (linseed, walnut) rubbed into bisque (unglazed) surface
3. **Resin:** Thin coat of shellac or linseed oil varnish

**Interior sealing:** For dry storage, minimal sealing needed; for liquids, ensure glaze or interior seal is complete.

## Woven Baskets for Ventilated Storage

Baskets are ideal for produce requiring air circulation (potatoes, onions, root crops) without sealed storage.

### Base Construction

**Simple round base (coil method):**

1. **Start coil:** Wrap long fiber material (reed, willow, or prepared plant fiber) around short starter bundle
2. **Spiral:** Spiral coil outward, binding successive turns with thread/twine through weaving
3. **Diameter:** Increase to desired base size (typically 250–400mm for storage baskets)
4. **Square section:** Transition from spiral to square by binding corners tighter

### Sides and Weaving

**Warp construction:**

1. **Insert uprights:** Drive 8–12 vertical stakes into base around perimeter (spaced ~30–50mm)
2. **Weave weft:** Interweave horizontal weavers (reed or plant fiber) over/under stakes in simple or complex patterns

**Weaving patterns:**

- **Simple over-under:** Alternates over one stake, under next (easiest)
- **Randing:** Two weavers interleave, creating diagonal pattern (more complex, stronger)
- **Pairing:** Two weavers work in tandem, twisting between stakes (tight, very strong)

### Rim and Handles

**Rim creation:**

1. **Bend uprights:** Fold each vertical stake over the rim at basket top (horizontal)
2. **Lash rim:** Bind folded stakes together with cordage/thread, creating continuous rim

**Handle attachment:**

1. **Arc shape:** Two strong uprights extended above rim, bent into handle arc
2. **Reinforcement:** Wrap handle with binding to reinforce (2–3 wraps)
3. **Lash attachment:** Lash to rim at both ends with strong twine

### Sealing and Treatment

Baskets are porous (by design, for ventilation), but can be treated for longevity:

1. **Oil finish:** Rub linseed oil into woven material (improves water resistance, darkens color)
2. **Drying:** Dry 48 hours before use
3. **Inspection:** Check for splinters, protruding edges (sand smooth)

**Liners:** Line baskets with burlap or cloth if storing fine items (flour, spices) requiring protection from dust/insects.

## Gourds and Birch Bark Containers

### Gourd Preparation

**Growing and harvesting:**

1. **Harvest timing:** Gourds mature 85–120 days after planting; harvest when stem dries and skin hardens (thumb cannot pierce)
2. **Dry:** Hang in warm, dry location (60–70°C if possible) for 2–3 months until interior is completely dry (shake to hear seeds rattle)
3. **Clean:** Once fully dry, scrape out dried seeds/pulp with spoon

**Shaping and cutting:**

1. **Design:** Sketch desired container shape (hole for opening)
2. **Cut:** Use saw or sharp knife to open gourd; scrape interior smooth
3. **Sand:** Smooth all edges, remove any remaining pulp

**Sealing:**

1. **Interior coat:** Rub interior with beeswax or food-grade oil
2. **Exterior finish:** Sand smooth, apply beeswax for water-resistance
3. **Curing:** Allow 1–2 weeks before storing contents (oil hardens)

**Uses:** Small gourds (1–5L capacity) ideal for spices, seeds, or small dried goods. Larger gourds (5–20L) suitable for grain/flour with good ventilation.

:::tip
Gourds are biodegradable but long-lived (5–10 years typical); ideal for temporary storage. Size variety available (small decorative to large utilitarian).
:::

### Birch Bark Containers

Birch bark naturally resists rot and is flexible when fresh, ideal for quick container construction.

**Materials:**

1. **Birch tree bark:** Harvest in spring when sap flows (bark separates easily); cut 300–400mm wide sheets
2. **Wooden frame:** Create rectangular or cylindrical frame from thin wood strips
3. **Fasteners:** Use wooden pegs, cordage, or simple bolts

**Assembly (rectangular box):**

1. **Cut frame:** Four wood strips (50mm × 25mm) for corners, additional strips for base + top
2. **Assemble frame:** Mortise or bolt corners together
3. **Line with bark:** Wrap bark around frame, overlapping edges by 50mm
4. **Secure bark:** Peg or lash bark to frame at corners and midpoints
5. **Trim:** Cut bark flush with frame edges
6. **Seal seams:** Caulk interior seams with beeswax or pine pitch

**Durability:** Birch bark containers last 3–5 years with maintenance (inspect for splits, replace if interior becomes compromised).

**Advantages:** Quick to construct (hours vs. days for other methods), waterproof when sealed, lightweight, strong.

## Sealing Methods and Food Safety

### Repair or Replace? Everyday Decision Frame

| Condition | Action | Best Seal Method |
|-----------|--------|-----------------|
| **Barrel stave seam weeping** | Repair | Pine pitch pushed into seam; swell with water 48h |
| **Ceramic slow seep through wall** | Repair | Interior beeswax coat; or re-fire with glaze if kiln available |
| **Ceramic single crack, holds shape** | Repair | Pine pitch in crack; beeswax interior coat |
| **Ceramic multiple intersecting cracks** | Replace | Recycle as grog; make new vessel |
| **Barrel hoop loose / stave cracked** | Repair | Replace hoop; drive tight; pitch seal if needed |
| **Gourd split** | Replace | Gourds are fast to replace; repair unreliable for liquids |

:::tip
**Quick field seal for a leaking jug or pot:** Heat pine pitch until pliable (60–70°C), press into the crack or smear along the leaking seam, smooth with a heated stick. Cures in minutes. For earthenware water jugs, an interior beeswax coat is the standard everyday fix.
:::

### Beeswax Sealing

**Best for:** Dry storage, water storage, oil storage.

**Process:**

1. **Prepare container:** Heat to ~40°C to open pores slightly
2. **Melt beeswax:** Heat beeswax to ~80–90°C (in separate pot, never direct heat)
3. **Apply:** Brush thin coat on interior surfaces or dip container interior in melted wax
4. **Cool:** Allow to harden (~30 min at room temperature)
5. **Cure:** Let sit 24–48 hours before filling

**Effectiveness:** Creates water-resistant barrier; oxygen permeability low (good for dry goods).

**Lifespan:** 2–3 years (wax eventually becomes brittle or develops small cracks).

### Pine Pitch Sealing

**Best for:** Very durable seal; suitable for liquids (wine, water).

**Process:**

1. **Prepare pitch:** Heat pine pitch to pliability (~60–70°C)
2. **Apply:** Brush or trowel pitch into cracks/seams (especially staves in barrels)
3. **Smooth:** Smooth with heated tool or wooden scraper
4. **Cool:** Hardens as it cools

**Effectiveness:** Very durable (10+ years), water-tight, accepts pressure from expanding liquids.

**Disadvantage:** Difficult to remove if container must be cleaned/repurposed.

### Clay Slip Sealing

**Best for:** Ceramic vessels, dry storage.

**Process:**

1. **Prepare slip:** Mix clay with water to yogurt-like consistency
2. **Paint:** Brush slip over interior cracks/seams
3. **Dry:** Allow to dry completely (24–48 hours)
4. **Fire:** Kiln-fire to harden (if not glazed during firing)

**Effectiveness:** Decent seal for dry goods; not reliable for liquids.

### Food-Safe Sealant Verification

**Avoid:**

- Lead-containing glazes (toxic)
- Varnishes with toxic fumes (must be food-grade)
- Bituminous sealers (toxic)
- Unknown coatings (assume unsafe unless verified)

**Safe options:**

- Pure beeswax (food-safe)
- Natural linseed oil (fully cured, food-safe)
- Shellac (food-safe if pure, not cut with alcohol)
- Lime/sand mortar (inert)
- Stoneware vitrification (glaze-free, fired >1200°C)

## Container Sizing for Common Commodities

| Commodity | Recommended Vessel Type | Capacity | Considerations |
|-----------|-------------------------|----------|-----------------|
| **Grain/flour** | Ceramic vessel or barrel | 20–50L | Sealed, cool, dry; mouse-proof |
| **Dried beans/legumes** | Ceramic or barrel | 10–20L | Sealed; inspect for weevils |
| **Oil/liquid** | Barrel or ceramic | 50–100L | Must be water-tight; dark storage |
| **Water** | Barrel or large ceramic | 50–200L | Clean interior; covered to prevent algae |
| **Salt** | Sealed ceramic or wooden cask | 5–20L | Highly corrosive; ceramic/glazed preferred |
| **Honey** | Ceramic or clay-sealed wood | 5–20L | Any sealed container; never metal (reacts) |
| **Dried herbs/spices** | Small sealed ceramic or gourds | 0.5–2L | Dry, dark, cool storage |
| **Butter/preserved fat** | Sealed ceramic or barrel | 5–10L | Cool storage (60–80°F); sealed against air |
| **Sauerkraut/ferments** | Ceramic crock | 5–20L | Partially sealed (allows gas escape) |
| **Dried fruit/produce** | Basket (ventilated) or sealed ceramic | 5–20L | Air circulation preferred; sealed if needed |

</section>

**Food-specific boundary:** For dried beans and dried herbs, the vessel is secondary to dryness: use sealed, pest-proof storage only after the food is fully dry. For fermented vegetables, choose a glazed crock, clean glass jar, or intact food-grade plastic bucket only after brine coverage, acidity, and cleanliness are correct; the container does not fix a failed ferment.

## Vessel Construction Checklist

### Wooden Barrel

| Step | Details |
|------|---------|
| **Design** | Determine size, select wood type (oak preferred) |
| **Stave prep** | Mill, taper, curve; test fit without hoops |
| **Hoop setup** | Fabricate 5–8 hoops (metal or saplings) |
| **Assembly** | Arrange staves, install hoops evenly, pound to tightness |
| **Heads** | Cut circular heads, groove barrel for heads |
| **Install heads** | Drop heads into grooves, press/drive to tight fit |
| **Test seal** | Fill with water 24h; observe for leaks |
| **Treatment** | Apply interior sealant if needed |

### Ceramic Vessel

| Step | Details |
|------|---------|
| **Design** | Sketch vessel shape, determine capacity |
| **Form** | Throw on wheel or coil-build |
| **Dry** | Dry slowly (3–7 days) to prevent cracking |
| **Fire** | Fire to appropriate temperature (earthenware vs. stoneware) |
| **Glaze/seal** | Apply glaze or interior sealant |
| **Final fire** | Fire again if glazed |
| **Cool** | Cool slowly to prevent cracking |
| **Test** | Fill with water 24h; observe for leaks |

### Basket

| Step | Details |
|------|---------|
| **Base** | Coil-build base to desired diameter |
| **Uprights** | Insert 8–12 vertical stakes |
| **Sides** | Weave weft material in chosen pattern |
| **Rim** | Fold uprights over, lash together |
| **Handle** | Form handle arc, reinforce with binding |
| **Finish** | Sand smooth, remove splinters |
| **Treatment** | Optional: oil finish for water-resistance |

</section>
