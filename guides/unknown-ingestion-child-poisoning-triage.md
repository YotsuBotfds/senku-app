:::warning
**Top routing for swallowed cleaners and under-sink exposures:**
- If the main problem is mixed-cleaner fumes, coughing, chest tightness, cleaner in the eye, or burning skin after touching an unknown or unlabeled chemical, treat it as a toxic exposure first: move to fresh air if needed, irrigate skin/eyes with running water, call poison control, and use `toxicology-poisoning-response.md`. Do not treat burning skin after an unknown cleaner as routine rash or poison ivy care.
- If a child or adult swallowed cleaner, licked a detergent pod, had unknown liquid in the mouth, or may have drunk something from under the sink, stay in this guide and call poison control immediately.
- If there is still an active spill or mixed-cleaner odor, isolate the room, stop all cleanup or mixing, keep other children and pets out, and do not add more products to "cancel it out."
:::

<section id="overview">

## Overview

Unknown ingestion means you have a patient, often a child, who may have swallowed an unknown substance, pills, cleaner, detergent pod, plant, fuel, alcohol, or household chemical. For any unlabeled or unknown ingestion, call Poison Control / EMS immediately first. The first problem is not naming the poison. The first problem is deciding whether the airway is in danger and whether vomiting or oral treatment will make the situation worse.

If the unknown item might be a household cleaner, drain cleaner, detergent pod, rodenticide, or other under-sink chemical, call poison control immediately and do not try home remedies before you know what it is.

If this started with mixed-cleaner fumes, a corrosive splash, or a strong chemical odor in the room, treat the patient first and the spill second. Scene isolation, fresh air, and water irrigation come before product identification, and complaint-first inhalation or skin/eye exposure should route to `toxicology-poisoning-response.md`.

Cleaner-licking, rat poison, mouse bait, and unlabeled under-sink bottle complaints all route here first.

<section id="first-five-minutes">

## The First Five Minutes

### Step 1: Make the scene safe

- Stop the cleaning task or product mixing. **Do not mix cleaners** or add vinegar, baking soda, ammonia, bleach, or any other product to "fix" the exposure.
- Remove the container, spilled chemical, or fuel from the patient if you can do so safely.
- Isolate the area and keep other children, pets, and bystanders away.
- Do not taste or smell the substance to identify it.
- Wear gloves if the patient has chemical residue on skin or clothing.
- If fumes are present, move everyone to fresh air and ventilate only if you can do so without staying in the fumes.
- If liquid cleaner is on the face, skin, or clothing, remove contaminated clothing and start rinsing exposed skin with water right away.

### Step 2: Check for immediate red flags

Treat as a life-threatening poisoning and prepare evacuation now if any of the following are present:

- Trouble breathing, noisy breathing, wheezing, or blue lips
- Strong cough, chest tightness, or worsening breathing after mixed-cleaner fumes or a burst detergent pod
- Drooling, inability to swallow, or severe mouth pain (drooling after drain cleaner or lye is especially urgent — assume esophageal injury until proven otherwise)
- Repeated vomiting or vomiting blood
- Sleepiness, confusion, seizure, or collapse
- Burns around lips or inside mouth
- Stridor, hoarseness, or facial swelling
- The patient is getting worse instead of better

If any red flag is present, treat this as a same-minute call for poison control, EMS, or the fastest available clinician while you prepare transport.

### Step 3: Do the simple, high-value actions

- If the patient is unresponsive and not breathing normally, start CPR.
- If the patient is breathing but altered, place them on their side.
- If chemical is on skin, remove contaminated clothing and irrigate with water.
- If chemical is in the eye, flush with clean water for at least 15 minutes.
- Do not force milk, water, vinegar, baking soda, lemon juice, charcoal, or any other home remedy into the mouth unless poison control specifically instructs it.
- Call poison control or emergency medical help as soon as someone can do it without delaying airway care. Read the label if you have it, and report the patient's age, the estimated amount, and the time of exposure.
- Save the container, label, pill fragments, or vomit sample for identification.

### Step 4: Ask four questions

1. What might it be?
2. About how much is missing?
3. When did it happen?
4. Is the patient able to speak and swallow normally?

<section id="never-induce-vomiting">

## When NOT to Induce Vomiting

Do **not** induce vomiting for unknown ingestion. In modern field care, induced vomiting causes more harm than benefit in the situations that kill people fastest.

Vomiting is especially dangerous when the swallowed substance may be:

- **Caustic**: drain cleaner, oven cleaner, lye, strong acids, concentrated bleach
- **Hydrocarbon**: gasoline, kerosene, lamp oil, lighter fluid, mineral spirits
- **Foaming or choking hazard**: detergent pods, soaps, solvents
- **Associated with altered mental status**: sleeping pills, alcohol, opioids, unknown mixed overdose

Why this rule exists:

- Caustics burn the throat a second time on the way back up.
- Hydrocarbons are easily aspirated into the lungs and cause chemical pneumonia.
- Drowsy or seizing patients aspirate vomit.

**Do not put fingers in the mouth. Do not use syrup of ipecac. Do not give salt water to trigger vomiting.**

</section>

<section id="substance-groups">

## Rapid Sorting by Substance Group

### Caustics: acids, alkalis, cleaners, lye, drain cleaner, batteries

Aliases: "toddler licked drain cleaner," "child swallowed Drano," "kid drank toilet bowl cleaner," "toddler tasted oven cleaner," "child got into lye," "drain cleaner plus drooling," "lye ingestion child," "toddler licked something under the sink," "child tasted cleaner from under the sink," "toddler licked bathroom cleaner," "child put cleaner in mouth," "child swallowed drain cleaner under the sink and is drooling," "toddler drank drain cleaner drooling," "child swallowed drain cleaner drooling," "drain cleaner under sink drooling child."

Common clues:

- Pain in mouth, throat, chest, or belly
- Drooling, especially after drain cleaner, oven cleaner, or lye — **drooling after caustic ingestion is an urgent escalation sign; arrange emergency transport immediately and keep the patient upright if possible**
- Refusal to swallow
- Burns on lips or tongue
- Vomiting after cleaner exposure
- Child found near open drain cleaner, toilet bowl cleaner, oven cleaner, or rust remover

Field actions:

- If Poison Control explicitly directs it, rinse the mouth gently and give only a small sip or two of water; otherwise do not give anything by mouth.
- Do **not** force large volumes of fluid.
- Do **not** give activated charcoal.
- Do **not** try to neutralize acid with alkali or alkali with acid.
- Do **not** induce vomiting.

Special danger:

- **Button battery or multiple magnets** are surgical emergencies, even if the patient looks well. Arrange evacuation immediately.

### Hydrocarbons: gasoline, kerosene, lamp oil, diesel, lighter fluid

Common clues:

- Fuel smell on breath or clothing
- Coughing right after swallowing
- Vomiting after trying to drink from a bottle or siphon line
- Sleepiness if exposure was mixed inhalation plus ingestion

Field actions:

- Fresh air if fumes were inhaled.
- Keep the patient upright if possible.
- Do **not** induce vomiting.
- Do **not** give activated charcoal.
- Watch for cough, wheeze, fast breathing, or delayed lung symptoms over the next several hours.

### Pills, capsules, gummies, powders, unknown medications

Common clues:

- Missing medication bottle
- Different pill fragments in the mouth or on the floor
- Sudden sleepiness, agitation, pinpoint pupils, fast heart rate, or seizures

Field actions:

- Assume danger if the patient is a small child and the drug is unknown.
- Activated charcoal is only reasonable if **all** of the following are true:
  - likely pill ingestion within about 1 hour
  - patient is fully awake and can swallow
  - no vomiting, no seizure, no airway concern
  - not a caustic, hydrocarbon, iron, lithium, or alcohol
- If any of those are uncertain, **do not give charcoal in the field**.

### Alcohols, toxic alcohols, and mixed unknown liquids

Examples:

- Mouthwash, isopropyl alcohol, methanol, antifreeze, homemade alcohol

Field actions:

- Do not induce vomiting.
- Do not assume "sleeping it off" is safe.
- Watch for confusion, rapid breathing, vision complaints, or worsening drowsiness.
- Arrange urgent evaluation if exposure may involve antifreeze, methanol, windshield fluid, or industrial solvents.

<section id="activated-charcoal">

## Activated Charcoal Boundaries

Activated charcoal can help in some poisonings, but it is dangerous when used indiscriminately.

### Activated charcoal may help when

- The swallowed poison is a pill or plant toxin that binds charcoal
- Exposure was recent, usually within 1 hour
- The patient is awake, cooperative, and swallowing normally
- You are not dealing with caustics, fuels, alcohols, iron, or lithium

### Activated charcoal is a bad idea when

- The patient is sleepy, confused, seizing, or vomiting
- The patient is drooling or cannot swallow normally
- The poison is a **caustic**
- The poison is a **hydrocarbon**
- The poison is an **alcohol**, **iron**, or **lithium**

The failure mode is aspiration. Charcoal in the lungs creates a second emergency.

</section>

<section id="airway-risk">

## Airway Risk and Escalation Triggers

Airway danger is what separates a poisoning scare from a fatal poisoning.

Escalate urgently if you see:

- Hoarseness after smoke or chemical exposure
- Stridor or noisy inhalation
- Drooling or refusal to swallow
- Repeated vomiting
- Worsening drowsiness
- Seizure
- Slow breathing or pauses in breathing

If naloxone is available and the patient has slow breathing with pinpoint pupils after possible opioid exposure, give naloxone and support breathing while preparing evacuation.

</section>

<section id="what-to-bring">

## What to Bring to the Handoff

When transferring care, bring:

- The original container
- Medication bottles with strengths and counts if available
- Approximate time of ingestion
- Patient age and weight if known
- What the patient has done since exposure: vomiting, coughing, seizure, drowsiness
- Any first aid already given

A perfect poison ID is less important than a clean timeline and the exact container.

</section>

<section id="field-priorities">

## Field Priorities Summary

1. Protect airway and breathing first.
2. Do not induce vomiting for unknown ingestion.
3. Keep caustics and hydrocarbons out of the "charcoal" bucket.
4. Save the container and document the timeline.
5. Assume small children are higher risk even after apparently small amounts.
