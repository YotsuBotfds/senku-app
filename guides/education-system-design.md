# Education System Design

Education is the mechanism by which communities preserve knowledge, transmit skills, and prepare the next generation to maintain and improve civilization. In post-collapse environments with reduced connectivity and literacy, education requires careful structural design to function without institutional infrastructure. This guide provides frameworks for building formal education systems that sustain knowledge transmission.

## Scope Boundary

This guide covers **education system structure, curriculum, teacher training, assessment, scheduling, and funding**. It does not cover physical building accessibility (ramps, doorway widths, grab bars, flooring) or room-layout dimensions — those belong in `accessible-shelter-design`. It does not cover teaching methods, phonics instruction, or classroom discipline — those belong in `education-teaching` and `education-teaching-methods`.

- Symptom: *need to build or modify a physical space so someone can enter or move through it* -> route to `accessible-shelter-design` first

## Shared Community Building Schoolhouse Setup

If the schoolhouse must fit inside a shared community building, treat the education zone as a one-room classroom with a clear daily operating footprint rather than as a fully separate institution.

- Pick one learning zone with predictable boundaries so desks, mats, or benches are not moved every hour.
- Put the teacher station on an edge with full sight lines across the room and a direct path to the entrance.
- Reserve at least one quiet corner or screen-off area for reading help, short assessments, or conflict cooling-off.
- Keep the main circulation path open from door to teacher station to student seating so arrivals and helpers do not cut through every lesson group.
- Store common materials at the edge of the room so the class can convert the shared building back to other community uses without losing school organization.
- If the real blocker is stairs, narrow doorways, or wheelchair turning space, route that part to `accessible-shelter-design`, then return here for classroom function.

</section>

## One-Room Schoolhouse Layout

For one-room schoolhouse layout questions, start with supervision flow and stable classroom zones before you optimize anything else.

- Put the teacher station near the edge of the room where the teacher can see every student group without turning constantly.
- Keep the center or one side as the main circulation lane so students, helpers, and materials do not cut through active lessons.
- Group seating by activity or age band rather than in one undifferentiated block so whole-class, small-group, and peer-teaching work can happen in the same room.
- Hold one quiet corner or screen-off spot for reading help, short assessments, or conflict cooling-off.
- Keep the most-used teaching materials at the room edge or next to the teacher station so transitions stay short.
- If a student cannot enter the room or move between zones safely, route the access fix to `accessible-shelter-design` first and then return to this layout.

</section>

## Why Formal Education Matters

<section id="education-importance">

Communities face a knowledge transmission crisis after collapse: pre-collapse literacy and training are unevenly distributed; many critical skills (medicine, agriculture, engineering, governance) are concentrated in aging experts; informal knowledge transmission (parent-child, apprenticeship) alone cannot scale to educate an entire generation; and without deliberate pedagogy, knowledge degradation accelerates (each generation loses detail from the previous).

Formal education addresses this by:

**Standardization**: All children learn core skills (literacy, numeracy, basic health, agriculture) regardless of family background, preventing widening inequality and knowledge loss.

**Efficiency**: Trained teachers deliver knowledge faster than children can acquire it through unstructured trial-and-error.

**Preservation**: Documented curriculum can be replicated across communities and time, reducing dependence on single experts.

**Critical thinking**: Beyond rote knowledge transmission, formal education teaches problem-solving, questioning, and adaptation—essential for communities facing new challenges.

**Specialization pathways**: Formal schooling enables identification and training of people suited for advanced roles (medicine, craft, governance) through structured assessment.

:::info-box
**Economic Argument for Education**

Communities sometimes view education as luxury (expenditure when resources could go to food/defense). Evidence from history contradicts this:

- Literate communities are more efficient (written records reduce disputes, enable specialization).
- Educated farmers adopt innovations (crop rotation, tool optimization) 20–30% faster than untrained ones.
- Trained craftspeople produce higher-value goods.
- Education is compounding: an educated generation produces more goods and resources, enabling more education for the next.

Early education investment (first generation) pays returns in efficiency gains (second generation) that exceed input cost.
:::

</section>

## Educational Philosophy for Resource-Constrained Settings

<section id="philosophy">

Post-collapse education must balance three often-competing goals: **practical survival skills, literacy and numeracy, and critical thinking**.

**Practical Skills Priority**
Students must graduate able to grow food, preserve it, treat basic illness, repair tools, and perform their expected role in community division of labor. Practical skills are non-negotiable; they directly sustain life.

**Literacy and Numeracy as Infrastructure**
Literacy and basic arithmetic are force-multipliers. Literate communities have vastly superior ability to:
- Document knowledge (recipes, techniques, decisions)
- Coordinate complex tasks (labor schedules, resource distribution)
- Resolve disputes (written law, property records)
- Preserve and transmit innovation

Invest heavily in these. Target: 85%+ literacy (read simple text, write name, basic math) by age 10; 60%+ numeracy (arithmetic to 1000, fractions, basic geometry) by age 12.

**Critical Thinking**
The hardest goal, but essential. Students must learn to:
- Ask "why?" and "how do you know?"
- Evaluate evidence and claims skeptically
- Recognize when they don't understand something and ask for clarification
- Experiment with solutions and learn from failure
- Adapt knowledge to new situations

Avoid rote memorization culture. Even in resource-constrained contexts, emphasize understanding over regurgitation.

**Blended Approach**
Integrate practical + academic + critical thinking in each lesson. Example arithmetic lesson: "We harvested 450 kg grain (weighing and recording). We need 0.5 kg/person/day for 80 people. Calculate how many days our supply lasts. If we eat less (0.4 kg/day), how much longer? What if we plant twice as much next year—how much surplus could we store?" This teaches math, nutrition, planning, and decision-making simultaneously.

:::tip
**Pedagogy for Low-Literate Communities**

If teaching population with 30% pre-existing literacy:
- Use oral teaching heavily (stories, dialogue, repetition).
- Use visuals (diagrams, charts, demonstrations).
- Use hands-on practice (students do the task, make mistakes, correct).
- Use peer teaching (students who understand explain to peers).
- Reduce text-heavy materials; rely on pictures, models, and direct experience.

Example: Teaching "how to preserve food" via oral story (narrative of someone preserving food through year), demonstration (make jam), and practice (each student preserves a jar). Avoid abstract text. This works for low literacy and engages different learning styles.
:::

</section>

## Age-Appropriate Curriculum Design

<section id="curriculum-design">

Effective education sequences skill-building from foundational to advanced. Use these age-based tiers.

**Ages 5–8: Foundational Literacy, Numeracy, and Health**

**Literacy Goals**: Recognize letters; sound out simple words; read 50–100 high-frequency words by sight; write own name; begin copying short sentences.

*Content*: Letter recognition; phonetic decoding; sight words (the, and, I, to, is); simple sentences using phonetically regular words; copying practice.

*Materials*: Slate boards + chalk (reusable, no paper); sand trays (draw letters in sand); copywork from simple texts.

**Numeracy Goals**: Count to 20; recognize numerals; understand quantity (more/less); begin addition (1+1, 2+2) using objects; understand daily time (morning/afternoon/night).

*Content*: Counting drills; number recognition; quantity comparison; addition with objects (pebbles, seeds); time sequence (before/after, today/tomorrow).

*Materials*: Pebbles, seeds, or objects for counting; sand tray for writing numbers; water clock or sundial for time.

**Health Goals**: Understand hygiene (handwashing, toileting), basic nutrition (safe food, safe water), and body awareness (naming body parts, identifying pain).

*Content*: Handwashing ritual (demonstrate, practice); food safety rules (wash before eating, cook meat fully); water safety (boil if uncertain); pain-location naming; introduction to common remedies (herbs, rest).

*Materials*: Water, soap; sample food items; medicinal herbs; picture charts of body parts.

**Physical Education**: Games, dance, and strength-building. Minimum 1 hour daily.

*Content*: Running games; climbing; jumping; rhythm/dance; carrying objects; swimming (where applicable).

**Ages 8–12: Expanded Literacy, Applied Numeracy, and Skill Specialization**

**Literacy Goals**: Read with fluency and comprehension; write simple paragraphs; spell correctly; understand word categories (verbs, nouns, adjectives).

*Content*: Fluent reading of age-appropriate texts (stories, agricultural texts, health guides); writing short narratives; spelling rules and practice; basic grammar; reading for information.

*Materials*: Hand-copied texts (students copy from books to practice writing); community-written guides (how to make soap, plant crops); simple published books brought from before collapse.

**Numeracy Goals**: Addition/subtraction to 100; multiplication and division concepts; fractions (1/2, 1/4); measurement (length, weight, volume); money/barter equivalents.

*Content*: Multiplication drills (times tables to 10×10); division as inverse; fraction identification (cutting pies, dividing goods); measuring for practical tasks (cooking, building); keeping trade records (barter ledger).

*Materials*: Objects for practicing arithmetic; scales for weighing; measuring cups and rulers; ledgers for recording transactions.

**Natural Science**: Basic biology, earth science, and simple physics. Emphasize observation and hypothesis.

*Content*: Plant identification (edible, medicinal, dangerous); animal behavior (seasonal patterns, habitat); weather patterns (seasons, rain/sun cycle); simple machines (lever, pulley, incline); water (flow, freezing, evaporation).

*Materials*: Collections of plants and seeds; insect specimens; simple machines (lever made from wood/stone); observational logs.

**Agriculture & Food Production**: Hands-on gardening and food preservation.

*Content*: Seed-saving identification; soil preparation; planting, watering, weeding, harvesting; preservation (drying, pickling, fermentation); storage; pest identification and management (without chemicals when possible).

*Materials*: Garden plot; seeds; tools; drying racks; jars; salt/vinegar for preservation.

**Craft Skills**: Age-appropriate introduction to trades and crafts.

*Content*: Woodworking basics (cutting, joining, finishing); textile work (spinning, weaving, sewing); pottery (if clay available); rope-making; tool maintenance.

*Materials*: Wood scraps; hand tools; fiber (wool, flax); clay; rope; tools to maintain.

**Age 8–12 typically begins apprenticeship in a trade of interest. By age 10–12, some students transition to formal apprenticeship while continuing schooling 2–3 days/week.**

**Ages 12–16: Specialization, Advanced Skills, and Civic Education**

**Literacy Goals**: Advanced reading and writing; critical analysis; document interpretation; record-keeping.

*Content*: Reading longer texts with comprehension (history, science, philosophy); writing essays and detailed records; interpreting maps, tables, and diagrams; legal documents and contracts; poetry and advanced narrative.

*Materials*: Copies of significant texts (survival guides, historical works, technical manuals); student writings and essays.

**Numeracy Goals**: Algebra concepts; geometry; ratio and proportion; advanced measurement; data analysis.

*Content*: Variables and equations (solving for unknown); geometric shape properties and area/volume calculation; ratios (mixture, scale); statistics basics (counting, averages, trends); navigation and mapping.

*Materials*: Geometry tools (straightedge, compass); surveying instruments; maps; examples of data (crop yields, population, weather records).

**Advanced Specialization Track**:

*Medicine/Health*: Anatomy basics; herbal preparation; disease identification; hygiene protocols; wound care and bone-setting.

*Engineering/Craft*: Advanced tool-making; building construction; water systems; millwork; metalworking.

*Agriculture*: Soil science basics; seed improvement; crop rotation planning; animal husbandry.

*Governance/Record-keeping*: Ledger management; law and dispute resolution; census and vital records; map-making and documentation.

*Scholarship/Teaching*: Advanced literacy; mathematics; history; rhetoric and debate.

**Civic Education**: Governance structures, law, rights and responsibilities, decision-making.

*Content*: How governance works (assembly, council, courts); property and inheritance law; trade and contract; justice and dispute resolution; historical examples (how other communities governed); each student's role in community decision-making.

*Materials*: Community laws and records; case studies from community disputes; legal documents.

**Physical Education & Military Skills (optional but common)**: Continuing fitness; if community has militia, basic weapons and defense training begins (ages 13+).

</section>

## One-Room Schoolhouse & Mixed-Age Classroom Management

<section id="mixed-age-teaching">

In small communities, you cannot afford separate classrooms for each age. One teacher may have ages 5–12 in the same space. Manage this using mixed-age pedagogy.

**Multigrade Classroom Structure**

Divide the school day into blocks by type of activity:

**Whole-class block (30–45 min)**: Teacher leads all ages. Content: stories, demonstrations, shared learning. Example: "Today we're learning about how plants grow," teacher plants seeds with all students watching, using age-appropriate language.

**Small-group rotation (90 min)**: Class divides into 3 age groups; each rotates through activities. While one group works with teacher, others work independently or with an older student mentor.

Example rotation:

| Time | Group 1 (5–7) | Group 2 (8–10) | Group 3 (11–14) |
|------|---|---|---|
| 9:00–9:30 | Teacher: letter recognition | Independent: copying practice | Older student: math drills |
| 9:30–10:00 | Independent: drawing/handwriting | Older student: arithmetic | Teacher: reading comprehension |
| 10:00–10:30 | Older student: games/movement | Teacher: science observation | Independent: written assignment |

**Practical activity block (60 min)**: All ages work on practical task (gardening, food preservation, craft) with teacher and older students coaching younger. Natural differentiation: younger do simpler tasks (pulling weeds), older do complex tasks (soil preparation, tool selection).

**Peer Teaching & Mentorship**

Older students (ages 11–14) who have mastered content become "student teachers" for younger groups. This reinforces their learning and provides supervision while teacher works with another group.

*Mentor roles*:
- Supervise younger group during seatwork (check they understand, redirect if off-task).
- Drill younger students (arithmetic facts, spelling, phonetics).
- Help younger students troubleshoot craft/practical tasks.
- Model correct behavior and academic effort.

*Training mentors*: Spend 15 min/week coaching mentors on how to explain, how to correct errors kindly, how to keep group on task. Rotate mentor role (every 4 weeks) so multiple older students develop teaching skill.

**Teacher workspace and room zones**

- Keep a small teacher desk or table near the edge of the room for lesson planning, record-keeping, and organizing materials.
- Place that workspace so the teacher can see the whole room without constantly turning away from students.
- Keep commonly used teaching materials within arm's reach so transitions between whole-group, small-group, and one-on-one work are fast.
- If possible, create a quiet corner or screen-off area for individual help, assessment, or short conferences with students.
- Treat these as room-layout decisions, not curriculum decisions: the purpose is supervision, flow, and attention management.

:::tip
**Managing a 40-person Mixed-Age School (1 teacher, 2 assistants)**

Assume: ages 5–14, 40 students total, 1 full-time teacher, 2 part-time assistants (mornings only).

**Schedule**:
- 8:00–8:45: Whole-group gathering (stories, songs, announcements; teacher).
- 8:45–10:15: Rotations (3 groups, 30 min each):
  - Group A (ages 5–7, ~12 students): Assistant 1 leads literacy/games.
  - Group B (ages 8–10, ~15 students): Teacher leads science/math.
  - Group C (ages 11–14, ~13 students): Assistant 2 leads reading comprehension + assignment review; mentors from this group assist younger groups.
- 10:15–10:45: Break (water, food, outdoor play).
- 10:45–12:00: Practical activity (all mixed ages; gardening, preservation, craft; teacher + assistants circulate coaching).
- 12:00–13:00: Lunch + outdoor time.
- 13:00–14:30: Continuation of morning work OR specialized tracks (ages 11–14 do additional math/scholarship track; younger continue foundational skills).
- 14:30–15:00: Community time (assembly, announcements, storytelling, song).
- 15:00: Dismissal.

**Cost**: 1 full-time teacher (paid in labor credits, goods, or status), 2 part-time assistants (community service or older youth fulfilling education requirement). Estimated 400–500 day-labor equivalent/year for pay, plus materials (slate boards, seeds, tools).

This is lower than pre-collapse schooling cost (~$10k/student/year in money) and sustainable for small communities.
:::

**Managing Behavior in Mixed-Age Settings**

- **Clear expectations**: Post pictures and rules (even if non-literate community). "Raise hand before speaking," "Work quietly on your task," "Help others kindly."
- **Mixed-age authority**: Older students help enforce rules (positive peer pressure; "we're supposed to be quiet").
- **Restorative correction**: If a student disrupts, speak to them privately rather than publicly shaming. Ask "What happened? How can you fix it?" This teaches reflection.
- **Positive reinforcement**: Recognize effort ("Sarah, you tried hard on that math"), not just success. Celebrate progress.
- **Consequences**: For repeated disruption, reduce privileges (not sitting with friends, staying inside at break) rather than punishment. Avoid physical punishment; it models violence.

</section>

## Creating Teaching Materials Without Printing

<section id="teaching-materials">

Print technology may not be available. Create durable, reusable teaching materials.

**Slate Boards & Chalk**
Individual slate boards (small rectangular stones with smooth surface, or commercially made before collapse, salvaged) + chalk made from ground limestone or bone.

*Use*: Students practice writing letters, numerals, arithmetic. Content is temporary (erased with cloth); boards are reusable indefinitely. Cost: nearly zero if using salvaged slates and locally-made chalk.

*Limitation*: Only one student per board; cannot post examples; cannot create handouts.

**Sand Trays**
Shallow wooden boxes filled with sand. Students use sticks or fingers to write or draw.

*Use*: Practice writing, drawing, number formation. Reusable (shake/smooth sand). Free if using wood scraps and local sand.

*Limitation*: Erased immediately (cannot preserve work); hard to maintain in wet climate.

**Copywork**
Students copy text from a source text (written on slate, paper, or carved wood) to practice handwriting, spelling, and reading.

*Use*: Teacher writes a passage (proverb, recipe, law, story) on a slate or board. Students copy it onto their own slate or paper. This serves triple duty: teaches handwriting, reinforces reading, and allows practice while creating a reference copy.

*Limitation*: Slow; limited to available text sources.

**Carved Wood Tablets**
Use stylus or knife to carve information into wood blocks (like ancient clay tablets). Information is permanent until deliberately erased.

*Use*: Store curriculum outlines, important facts, rules, recipes. One master copy used by teacher.

*Limitation*: Time-consuming to create; cannot easily update.

**Oral Tradition & Narrative**
Stories used to teach content. Teacher tells story; students hear, remember, and retell.

*Example*: "The Story of How to Plant Beans" (narrative with characters, action, outcome) teaches bean-planting steps in memorable form. Students hear story repeatedly, eventually memorize it, and can reproduce steps accurately.

*Use*: Teach procedures (preserving food, making tools), historical events, moral lessons, arithmetic.

*Limitation*: Requires skilled storyteller; harder to preserve precise detail (stories evolve); non-readers cannot access independently.

**Models & Specimens**
Physical objects used to teach. A sheep skeleton teaches anatomy; a cross-section of a tree teaches growth rings; a scale model of a loom teaches weaving.

*Use*: Any topic where seeing/touching physical object clarifies concept.

*Limitation*: Must obtain or create models; cannot be easily transported; some degrade (specimens, organic materials).

**Chalkboards & Walls**
Paint one wall of schoolhouse with blackboard paint (made from powdered charcoal + oil). Use as reusable writing surface.

*Use*: Teacher writes lessons, demonstrations, problems for whole class.

*Limitation*: Fixed location; one writing surface per board; must erase to add new content.

:::info-box
**Creating Durable Teaching Texts**

Without printing, prioritize creating copies of the most essential texts:

1. **Alphabet & phonetics** (single page, carved or written large on board; every student memorizes).
2. **Arithmetic facts** (tables, posted on wall; students drill until memorized).
3. **Core curriculum** (recipes, agricultural techniques, medical basics, laws—copied by hand from originals; create multiple copies, store in library).
4. **Student-created works** (collections of student writing, diagrams, solutions—create as reference for future students).

Invest the effort in handcopying the top 10 texts essential to your community (agricultural guide, health guide, laws, history, moral teachings). This forms the core curriculum library. All other content is taught orally or via demonstration.
:::

</section>

## Teacher Recruitment and Training

<section id="teacher-training">

Quality teachers are the bottleneck in education. Recruit and train deliberately.

**Identifying Prospective Teachers**

Look for people with:
- **Literacy and numeracy**: Can read, write, and do math at least to the level they'll teach.
- **Aptitude for explanation**: Can describe processes clearly (observed when giving instructions to others).
- **Patience with repetition**: Can explain the same thing multiple times without frustration.
- **Respect in community**: Community trusts them; people listen to them.
- **Interest in learning**: Ask "Would you like to learn to teach?" Most effective teachers are self-directed learners.

Prospective teachers need not be formally educated or have pre-collapse teaching credentials. Aptitude matters more than background.

**Teach-the-Teacher Cascade Model**

Train a small number of master teachers, who then train other teachers. This scales training without requiring large trainer pool.

**Level 0 (Master Teacher)**: One person with deepest expertise in pedagogy/curriculum. Recruited from outside community (if possible) or trained extensively if internal. Responsible for training all Level 1 teachers.

**Level 1 (School Teachers)**: 3–5 people trained by master teacher. Each teaches a school (or co-teaches if school is large). Level 1 teachers begin training Level 2 teachers.

**Level 2 (Assistant Teachers)**: 10–15 people trained by Level 1 teachers. Assist in classrooms, supervise groups, begin taking lead on some lessons.

**Cascade Timeline**:
- Year 1: Master teacher arrives/is trained. Trains 3 Level 1 teachers intensively (9 months).
- Year 2: Level 1 teachers each train 3–4 Level 2 teachers (part-time, 6 months).
- Year 3: Schools are staffed and functioning; Level 1 teachers continue training replacements as needed.

**Master Teacher Responsibilities**

- **Curriculum design**: Create core curriculum document (what to teach at each age, in what sequence).
- **Demonstration teaching**: Model teaching techniques (students observe, then discuss pedagogy afterward).
- **Feedback & coaching**: Observe each school teacher monthly; provide specific feedback on clarity, pacing, student engagement.
- **Ongoing learning**: Read/study new pedagogical techniques, bring innovations back to teachers.

**Compensation**
Teachers are expensive. Compensate via:
- **Labor credits**: Teacher earns credits toward goods/services (food, repair work, construction labor).
- **Status/respect**: Community recognizes teachers as important; give them priority in community events, input in governance.
- **Release from other labor**: Teacher spends 100% time teaching, not also farming or building.
- **Apprentice stipend**: If prospective teacher is training while young, reduce their other labor obligations.

**Ongoing Professional Development**

After initial training, teachers need regular support:
- **Monthly study groups**: Teachers meet with master teacher to discuss difficult students, pedagogical challenges, new techniques.
- **Peer observation**: Teachers observe each other's classes and provide feedback.
- **Curriculum reviews**: Quarterly review of curriculum and progress; adjust based on observed student outcomes.
- **Continuing education**: Master teacher brings new ideas (from other communities, from books, from experimentation); teachers implement and refine.

</section>

## Assessment and Progress Tracking

<section id="assessment">

Without standardized tests, track progress using simple, frequent assessment.

**Formative Assessment (Ongoing)**

**Observation during practice**: Teacher watches students during seatwork or practical activity. Notes:
- Can they do the task independently?
- Do they understand when to ask for help?
- Are they making progress session to session?

**Questioning**: During lessons, ask frequent questions to check understanding. "Who can tell me what happens when you plant a seed in dry soil?" Answers reveal who understands.

**Checking work**: Review student's slate board, copied work, or practical product. Does it meet expectation? Where are errors?

**Record in simple ledger**: For each student, keep a 1-page profile:
- Name, age, date started school
- Reading level (letter recognition / sounding out / fluent reading / reading for comprehension)
- Math level (counting / addition / subtraction / multiplication)
- Special notes (learns slowly, learns quickly, behavior issues, family circumstances)

Update profile monthly based on observation. This is not a grade; it's a record of progress and learning profile.

**Summative Assessment (End of Year)**

At end of school year, students demonstrate competency:

**Literacy performance**: Student reads aloud from text; teacher notes fluency and comprehension. Can student identify key ideas in a passage?

**Numeracy performance**: Student solves arithmetic problems (addition, subtraction, multiplication as appropriate to age). Can they explain their process?

**Practical skills performance**: Student performs a practical task (makes rope, preserves a food item, repairs a tool, plants a seed correctly, etc.). Is the result functional? Did student understand the steps?

**Character performance**: Teacher notes: does student listen, try hard, help others, persist when confused?

**Outcome recording**: After assessment, teacher writes one-line summary in ledger: "Sarah, age 8, reads with fluency; understands simple stories; does addition to 20; strong in practical skills. Recommended for advancement to age 9–10 group." Or: "James, age 10, reads slowly and needs support; strong in arithmetic; needs continued literacy focus; recommend repeating reading drills with peer mentor."

**Advancement decision**: At end of year, students advance to next age group if they show readiness. Some students may stay in same group for another year (not "held back" in shame; framed as "getting extra time to solidify foundations"). Advancement is based on demonstrated competency, not completion of time.

:::tip
**Tracking Without Numbers**

Some communities may not use numerical grades. Track instead using:
- **Colors**: Green (meeting expectations), Yellow (developing), Red (needs support).
- **Descriptors**: "Reads aloud with good fluency," "Still learning to add," "Very strong in practical skills."
- **Progression markers**: "Completed Level 1 Literacy (recognizes letters), beginning Level 2 (sounds out words)."

This provides clear feedback without the demotivation sometimes caused by number grades.
:::

</section>

## Scheduling Around Agricultural & Work Cycles

<section id="scheduling">

Schools cannot demand constant attendance if community depends on seasonal labor. Build school calendar around work needs.

**Seasonal Schedule**

**Planting season (spring, 8–10 weeks)**: School reduces to 3 days/week (Mon, Wed, Fri). Focus on practical work: preparing soil, planting, field maintenance. Older students (12+) do heavy planting labor; younger students attend school or light tasks.

**Growing season (early summer, 6–8 weeks)**: School is 5 days/week. Work requirements are low (minimal weeding, watering); students attend full-time. This is intensive academic season.

**Harvest season (late summer/fall, 8–12 weeks)**: School reduces to 2–3 days/week. Students do harvest labor (picking, processing, preservation). Harvest work is community priority; school accommodates.

**Winter (13–17 weeks)**: School is 5 days/week, 6 hours/day. This is primary academic season. Work requirements are low (maintenance, indoor tasks).

**Total school days/year**: ~160 days (vs. ~180 in pre-collapse; slightly less, but concentrated in seasons when intensive learning is feasible).

**Mandatory vs. Optional Attendance**

**Ages 5–12**: Mandatory school attendance. Parents who do not send children to school face community pressure (poor reputation, reduced access to shared resources).

**Ages 13–16**: Flexible attendance. Students are transitioning to adult work and apprenticeship. Attend school 2–3 days/week while learning trade. Optional advanced tracks (scholarship, governance) are part-time (afternoon sessions).

**Rationale**: By age 13, basic literacy and numeracy should be established. Continued learning is increasingly through apprenticeship and practical experience. Part-time school allows specialization while maintaining literacy/numeracy.

</section>

## Special Education Adaptations

<section id="special-education">

Communities may have students with disabilities or learning differences. Adapt teaching without excluding them.

**Physical Disabilities**

**Blind or low-vision**: Teach tactile alternatives (students learn to read Braille if community has supplies; otherwise, focus on memory and oral learning). Pair with sighted peer for some tasks. Reduce dependence on writing; allow oral responses.

**Deaf or hard-of-hearing**: Use visual demonstrations more heavily. Write key content on board. Pair with sign-language user (if community has one) or develop gesture-based communication. Focus on written literacy; this is accessible even without hearing.

**Mobility impairment (crutches, wheelchair, partial paralysis)**: Modify practical activities (student supervises and directs rather than physically does all work). Ensure the schoolhouse or shared community learning building has an accessible entrance and usable circulation (smooth floor, wide doors if possible). Adapt tasks (writing instead of heavy work, research instead of field work).

**Learning Differences**

**Slow learners**: Provide more repetition, slower pace, simpler examples. Break tasks into smaller steps. Pair with peer mentor for extra practice. This is not disability; many students need more time. Do not shame; do frame as "taking extra time to solidify foundations."

**Gifted/advanced learners**: Provide enrichment (advanced texts, complex problems, mentorship by teachers/scholars). Do not accelerate past age-appropriate development; instead, deepen knowledge in areas of interest.

**Dyslexia (if recognized)**: Some students struggle with written text despite normal intelligence. Provide more oral teaching, visual demonstrations, and hands-on practice. Do not force memorization of spelling; focus on understanding. Pair with students who read fluently.

**Attention challenges**: Frequent breaks, movement, task variety. Reduce distractions (quiet space, smaller group). Provide kinesthetic activities (movement, hands-on tasks). Some children develop focus as they mature; do not label as "broken."

**Principle**: All students belong in school. Modify teaching, not inclusion. A student with mobility impairment or learning difference is still part of the community learning, contributing in their way, and developing capabilities.

</section>

## Adult Education and Literacy Programs

<section id="adult-education">

Not everyone has access to school. Offer adult literacy to older community members and incoming refugees.

**Adult Literacy Program**

**Schedule**: Evenings (after work) or weekends. 1–2 hours, 2–3 days/week. Community gathers at schoolhouse.

**Content**: Same foundational literacy/numeracy as children (letter recognition, sounding out, sight words, arithmetic basics) but accelerated and contextualized. Use practical, adult-relevant texts (laws, recipes, property records) instead of children's stories.

**Teaching method**: Peer teaching. Advanced readers (literate adults or teachers) lead small groups (3–5 people) of learners. Same mixed-age and hands-on approaches as children's school.

**Motivation**: Emphasize utility. "Learning to read helps you understand laws, keep records, and participate in community decisions."

**No shame**: Frame adult literacy as normal and necessary. People of all ages can learn. Many adults will be non-literate initially; this is expected.

**Duration**: Intensive program: most learners reach basic literacy in 6–12 months of consistent attendance. Some take longer; this is okay.

:::info-box
**Adult Literacy Success Rates**

Communities that emphasize adult literacy see dramatic payoffs:
- Better record-keeping (business transactions, property, governance).
- Faster knowledge transmission (people can read how-to guides rather than needing to observe).
- Better communication (written messages, public announcements).
- Increased civic participation (people understand written law and can contribute to governance).

Target: 80%+ of community literate within 5 years of priority adult education program.
:::

</section>

## Apprenticeship Integration with Formal Schooling

<section id="apprenticeship">

Formal school (ages 5–12) provides foundation. Ages 13–16 integrate apprenticeship (learning a trade) with part-time schooling.

**Apprenticeship Structure**

A young person (age 12–16) learns a craft or skill from a master. Apprenticeship typically lasts 5–7 years; by age 18–23, the apprentice is a journeyperson (competent practitioner) or master (expert).

**Time commitment**: Days: Mornings with master (practical work); afternoons in school (literacy, math, civic education). Weeks: 3 weeks with master, 1 week in school (or other rotation). Years: 2–3 years intense apprenticeship; continuing part-time school.

**Apprenticeship subjects**: Farming, animal husbandry, carpentry, blacksmithing, weaving, masonry, medicine, governance/administration, scholarship/teaching.

**Master responsibility**: Teach practical skill through hands-on guidance. Correct errors kindly. Model good workmanship and ethics. Report apprentice progress to school.

**School responsibility**: Provide literacy, math, and civic education relevant to apprentice's trade. Example: apprentice blacksmith studies math (fractions, geometry for design), reads technical documents (alloys, techniques).

**Integration example**: Carpenter's apprentice spends mornings in shop learning to cut, join, finish wood. Afternoons in school:
- Monday: Arithmetic (geometry for design, measuring, estimating materials).
- Tuesday: Literacy (reading carpentry guides, writing project records).
- Wednesday: Practical (applying learning: design a small project, estimate materials, execute).
- Thursday: Civic (apprentice participates in community assembly as observer; learns governance).
- Friday: Free (rest or extra practice).

By age 18, apprentice is competent carpenter, literate, numerate, and engaged in community governance.

</section>

## Knowledge Preservation & Documentation

<section id="knowledge-preservation">

Education only succeeds if community knowledge is preserved across generations. Document expertise intentionally.

**Expert Documentation Project**

Identify community experts (older farmers, healers, craftspeople, leaders with decades of experience). Record their knowledge:

**Oral interviews**: Sit with expert; record (in writing if possible, or memory) their techniques, recipes, stories, rules of thumb. Example: "What are signs a cow is sick? How do you treat it?" Document responses verbatim.

**Demonstration observation**: Watch expert perform a skill (preserving food, making rope, treating illness). Document steps, materials, timing, and reasoning.

**Written guides**: Compile documentation into guide format (see <a href="../content-authoring.html">Content Authoring guide</a>) and store in community library. Include expert's name (giving them credit and validation).

**Younger documentation team**: Train 2–3 younger people to be "knowledge documentarians." Their role is to interview experts, observe practices, and write guides. By age 30, they know much of community expertise in written form; they can then train next generation of documentarians.

**Timeline**: Document at least one critical skill/knowledge area per year (medicine, agriculture, crafts, governance). In 10 years, most critical community knowledge is preserved.

:::tip
**Documenting "Dangerous" Knowledge Safely**

Some knowledge (how to make weapons, how to make alcohol, how to poison someone) is valuable for legitimate use but dangerous if misused. Document it, but restrict access:
- Store in locked chest or secured location.
- Restrict to adult readers, and those with legitimate need.
- Include safeguards (this knowledge can cause harm; use it responsibly).
- Teach ethics alongside technique: when to use, when to refuse.

Example: Document herbalist's knowledge of strong herbs that can be poisons. Store with herbalist, restricted to people training as healers. Include: "These herbs, in proper dose, heal. In excess, they poison. Use only with full consent and understanding of risk."
:::

</section>

## Library and Reference Material Management

<section id="library-management">

A library serves as knowledge repository and reference for students and teachers. Design it for accessibility and durability.

**Physical location**: Central, accessible location (schoolhouse, shared community building, religious center). Protected from weather and fire (if possible).

**Organization**: Categorize by subject (agriculture, medicine, crafts, history, governance, moral teachings). Use a simple numbering system (e.g., "A-1" for first agriculture book, "M-3" for third medicine book). Create a written catalog so anyone can find books.

**Acquisition**: Accept donations (books from pre-collapse); copy important texts by hand; commission new texts from authors (documentarians, teachers, experts).

**Preservation**: Store books in dry location. Inspect monthly for damage (mold, insect, water). Repair torn pages, rebind loose books. Use acid-free materials if possible; common paper degrades in decades.

**Access rules**: Anyone can read; borrowing requires community member to vouch (takes responsibility for safe return). Borrowed books must be returned within set period (e.g., 2 weeks). Damaged books must be repaired or replaced by borrower.

**Librarian role**: One person manages library (shelving, cataloging, preservation, access). This is a part-time community role (shared with other duties if needed).

See <a href="../library-organization-preservation.html">Library Classification</a> and <a href="../library-archival-preservation.html">Library Archival & Preservation</a> for detailed guidance.

</section>

## Governance of Educational Institutions

<section id="education-governance">

Schools need oversight to ensure they serve community needs and maintain quality.

**School Board**

A 5–7 person board elected or appointed by community council. Board responsibilities:
- **Oversight**: Ensure school operates effectively, teachers are trained, curriculum aligns with community needs.
- **Hiring & evaluation**: Recruit teachers, evaluate their performance annually, provide feedback.
- **Budget**: Allocate community resources (labor credits, goods, land) to school.
- **Parent liaison**: Address concerns from families about their children's education.
- **Policy**: Set school calendar, attendance rules, advancement criteria (in consultation with teachers).

**Teacher voice**: Teachers are not just employees; they contribute to curriculum and policy decisions. Board includes teacher representative or holds separate teacher council monthly to discuss pedagogy and needs.

**Community input**: School board holds annual public meeting to discuss education priorities. Community members can raise concerns ("We need more focus on agriculture," "Health education should start earlier"). Board takes feedback seriously and adjusts if feasible.

**Transparency**: School's curriculum, policies, and progress are public. Anyone can visit school, observe classes (with minimal disruption). Quarterly public reports on student progress and school news.

</section>

## Funding Education

<section id="funding-education">

Education is costly. Fund it explicitly.

**Labor-credit model**: School is budgeted 1000–1500 day-labor-equivalent/year for mixed-age school of 40–60 students. This pays teacher (400–500 credits), assistants (300–400 credits), materials and maintenance (200–300 credits), library (100 credits).

*Funding source*: Community labor tax. Each household contributes 5–10 day-labor-equivalent per year to community fund, which pays for education, defense, infrastructure, and mutual aid. (See <a href="../governance-leadership.html">Governance & Leadership Structures</a> for tax design.)

**In-kind contributions**: Community members donate materials (seeds for school garden, wood for repairs, food for school meals) instead of labor. These are credited against labor tax.

**Fee-based model** (if barter/currency exists): Charge small fee per student (e.g., 5 day-labor-equivalent/year per family, or equivalent in goods). Poor families are exempt or subsidized. This creates accountability (families invested in school) and distributes cost.

**Mixed model** (most robust): Base funding from community labor tax; supplemented by in-kind contributions and small family fees.

**Budget allocation**:
- 40% Teacher salary
- 20% Assistant salaries
- 15% Materials (paper, chalk, seeds, tools, repairs)
- 15% Library (books, preservation, cataloging)
- 10% Contingency / special needs

**Cost benchmarking**: Pre-collapse, formal education cost $5,000–$12,000 per student per year. Post-collapse mixed-age school with volunteer assistants costs $400–$800 per student per year (in labor credits). This is 5–15% of pre-collapse cost, reflecting lower overhead and volunteer contribution.

</section>

## Summary & Next Steps

Education is infrastructure for long-term community viability. Begin implementation:

1. **Identify prospective teachers** (2–3 people with literacy, patience, community respect).
2. **Recruit master teacher** (from outside community if possible, or intensive training of local person).
3. **Design core curriculum** (what to teach at each age; write as simple document).
4. **Establish school location** (existing building or construct simple structure).
5. **Create school calendar** (accommodate seasonal labor; target 160 school days/year).
6. **Train teachers** (6–9 month intensive with master teacher).
7. **Open school** (start with ages 5–12; phase in older students and apprenticeship).
8. **Establish library** (collect books, set up cataloging, designate librarian).
9. **Launch adult literacy program** (concurrent with children's school; same teachers, different schedule).
10. **Annual review** (gather teacher feedback, parent feedback; adjust curriculum and practices).

By year 3, communities operating formal education systems report:
- 70%+ child literacy
- Improved record-keeping and governance
- Faster transmission of agricultural and craft knowledge
- Stronger civic engagement (educated adults participate more in community decisions)
- Better social cohesion (shared knowledge and shared identity)
