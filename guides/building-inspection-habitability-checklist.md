:::danger
**Read this first.** A building can look "mostly fine" and still be unsafe because of hidden collapse, water damage, fire damage, mold, gas, electricity, or blocked exits. If you see active collapse, major sagging, strong gas smell, heavy fire damage, sewage backup, or a floor that feels soft or bouncy, do not keep walking around inside trying to "see if it is okay." Leave and reassess from outside.

If the complaint is visible damage and the real question is "do we enter at all, or do we inspect from outside first?", this guide is the right first stop. If the real question is whether a building can be used, stored in, or occupied after the first inspection, keep the routing here instead of sending it to generic construction or repair guidance.

This guide helps you decide whether a building is:
- **Do not enter**
- **Enter only for short salvage**
- **Use after repair**
- **Reasonable for occupancy after checks**

This is a reviewed triage-only lane for safety closure. It intentionally stops at decision
classification and routing, and does not provide structural reinforcement, extraction/rescue
steps, engineering repair instructions, demolition, utility repair, or roof repair playbooks.

If the answer is unclear, choose the safer category.

If someone is still deciding whether entry is allowed at all, hand that off to [Structural Safety for Building Entry](../structural-safety-building-entry.html) before moving into repair or construction advice.
:::

This guide is for the moment when someone asks, "Can we go in there?", "Can we use this building for storage?", "Can we sleep here after a quick repair?", or "How bad is the damage really?"

The goal is not to make a perfect engineering judgment. The goal is to make a practical yes/no decision fast enough to avoid getting hurt and specific enough to know what kind of work comes next.

If the prompt is really asking whether cracks, sagging, leaning, soft flooring, or other visible damage should stop entry, this guide should answer it first before anything else. If the answer is "no entry," hand off to [Structural Safety for Building Entry](../structural-safety-building-entry.html) for the pre-entry mechanics; if the answer is "yes, but only after checks," stay here rather than jumping to generic construction or repair guidance.

## 1. The Four Decision Levels

Use the four levels below before you do anything else.

| Level | What it means | Typical examples |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Do not enter | The building may collapse, poison, trap, or burn you. | Active fire damage, major sagging, gas smell, heavy water saturation, severe structural movement, live electrical hazard, sewage backup, visible collapse. |
| Enter only for short salvage | The shell may be stable enough for a quick in-and-out visit, but it is not safe for normal use. | Limited roof damage, minor-to-moderate water damage, isolated mold, broken windows, damaged finishes, stripped utilities, unstable room or floor section you can avoid. |
| Use after repair | The building is probably structurally usable, but specific failures still need repair before regular use. | Leaking roof, missing door or window, damaged stairs, broken plumbing, drafty walls, patchable floor damage, localized rot. |
| Reasonable for occupancy after checks | The building is dry enough, stable enough, and safe enough for normal living or long-term storage. | No active leaks, no major sagging, exits work, utilities are either safe or intentionally shut off, no major mold smell, no obvious structural movement. |

Think of the building in this order:
1. Can I enter it?
2. Can I be in it briefly?
3. Can I repair it and then use it?
4. Can I live in it or store goods here?

If you answer "no" at any earlier step, stop there.

## 2. What To Check From Outside First

Never start by wandering room to room. Start at the perimeter and look for the easiest signs of danger.

### Walk the outside in a circle

Look at the building from all sides if possible.

- Is the building leaning?
- Is the roof sagging or visibly uneven?
- Are the walls bowed, pulled apart, or cracked in a new way?
- Has the foundation shifted, sunk, or split?
- Are windows or doors out of square in a way that suggests movement?
- Is there smoke damage, fire staining, or a burned section?
- Is there standing water around the building or water pouring out?
- Is there a gas meter, propane tank, or fuel system with obvious damage?
- Are power lines down, sparking, or touching the structure?

If the outside already looks unstable, do not assume the inside is better.

### Outside red flags that usually mean do not enter

- Roof ridge is sagging or the roof line has dropped.
- A wall is bulging outward or has a large diagonal crack.
- The building has shifted on its foundation.
- You can see daylight through major gaps at corners or under the roof.
- There is burn-through, charred framing, or melted materials in key supports.
- There is floodwater in the structure or the lower walls are visibly soaked.
- The building smells strongly of gas, sewage, or chemical fumes.

### Outside signs that may still allow short salvage

- One section is damaged but the main shell looks stable.
- A roof leak is local and the framing does not sag.
- Windows are broken but the walls and roof remain square.
- One room is wet, but the rest of the structure stays dry.
- Fire damage is limited to finishes or a single compartment and the structure stayed intact.

Short salvage does not mean normal use. It means "get in, get out, do not linger."

## 3. The First Interior Questions

If the outside does not already rule the building out, ask these questions before you go deeper inside:

1. Can I see at least two ways out?
2. Does the floor feel solid at the doorway?
3. Do I smell gas, sewage, smoke, or strong chemical odor?
4. Is the ceiling visibly sagging or stained with fresh water?
5. Are there signs of active movement, such as cracking, popping, dust falling, or new deformation?

If any answer is bad, the building is either do not enter or short salvage only.

### Minimum entry habits

- Enter with another person if possible.
- Carry light, not just confidence.
- Leave the main exit open.
- Keep the route out clear.
- Stay near your exit on the first pass.
- Do not step onto soft, wet, or suspect flooring just to "check it."

If you cannot describe your exit plan in one sentence, you are not ready to keep going inside.

## 4. Do Not Enter

Treat the building as off-limits if any of the following are true.

### Structural danger

- Roof is sagging enough that you can see it from outside.
- Floors are collapsing, soft across a broad area, or bouncing in an unsafe way.
- Walls are separating from each other or from the roof.
- Foundation has failed or one side has dropped.
- There are major diagonal or horizontal cracks that are still growing.
- The building moved after a storm, quake, fire, or flood.

### Fire danger

- The structure had a major fire and load-bearing members may have been weakened.
- Charred beams, melted connectors, or roof collapse are present.
- The ceiling or attic shows obvious heat damage.
- There is hidden smoldering, hot spots, or smoke still coming from materials.

### Water and rot danger

- Floodwater stood in the building long enough to soak framing.
- The lower wall structure is soft, swollen, or crumbling.
- The floor is springy, spongy, or visibly warped in a large area.
- Mold or rot is widespread enough that the structure itself is suspect.

### Utility danger

- You smell gas.
- Wires are down, wet, sparking, or exposed.
- A fuel tank, propane line, or electrical panel is visibly damaged.
- You suspect a hidden ignition source near wet or burned material.
- You are relying on a generator or gasoline-powered pump inside the building or close to doors, windows, or vents.

### Access danger

- There is only one exit and it may be blocked.
- You cannot tell how to get back out from where you plan to go.
- The stairs are broken and no safe alternate exit exists.

If the building falls into this category, the right decision is not "how do I make it work?" The right decision is "do not use this building until a qualified repair or demolition path exists."

## 5. Enter Only For Short Salvage

This category is for buildings that are not safe for normal occupancy but may still be worth a quick, controlled visit for supplies or records.

### Short salvage is acceptable when

- The main structure is standing.
- You can identify a safe route in and out.
- You can stay away from damaged rooms.
- You do not need to cross unstable floors or ceilings.
- The work can be done quickly.

### What short salvage can include

- Removing dry stored goods.
- Taking tools, documents, hardware, or durable materials.
- Photographing or noting what is present before later work.
- Shutting off utilities if that can be done safely from outside or at the entrance.

### What short salvage should not include

- Moving into the building with belongings.
- Cooking, sleeping, or storing food there.
- Working under a damaged ceiling for a long time.
- Removing large structural pieces that could shift load.
- Tearing out moldy or burned areas without a full plan.

### Common short-salvage signs

- One room is destroyed, but another is dry and intact.
- A roof leak is local, but the rest of the building is stable.
- Broken windows or doors expose the interior, but the shell is sound.
- A utility area is damaged, but the living area is still solid enough to inspect.

If the building needs more than a quick visit to be usable, it is not short salvage anymore. It is a repair project.

## 6. Use After Repair

This is the category for buildings that are structurally sound enough to keep, but not ready for normal use yet.

### Typical repair issues

- Leaking roof
- Broken window
- Loose stair or railing
- Drafty wall or missing insulation
- Damaged door or latch
- Small area of rot that can be cut out and replaced
- Minor floor repair
- Plumbing failure that has not damaged the structure
- Localized mold or moisture source that can be fixed

### The key question

Ask: "If I fix the known problems, does the building become solid and dry enough to live in?"

If yes, this is a use-after-repair building.

### Repair before use checklist

- Stop active water entry first.
- Remove soaked materials that will keep feeding mold.
- Replace or brace any damaged framing.
- Fix doors and windows so the building can be secured.
- Make sure stairs and exits are safe.
- Confirm that electricity, gas, and heat sources are either safe or shut down.
- Keep flooded ducted heating/cooling systems off until they are cleaned and dry enough not to spread contamination or mold.
- Dry the building before calling it usable.

### Storage in a repair building

A building can sometimes be good for storage before it is good for sleeping.

Use it for storage only if:
- The space is dry.
- The floor is solid.
- The roof is not leaking on the stored area.
- Pests cannot easily get in.
- The stored goods are raised off the floor.
- The storage area is not contaminated by smoke, sewage, fuel, or heavy mold.

Do not store food or textiles in a place that smells musty, wet, oily, or burned.

## 7. Reasonable For Occupancy After Checks

This is the highest practical category. It means the building is probably safe enough for daily life, not just a quick visit.

### Occupancy requires all of these

- The structure is stable.
- The roof is not actively leaking.
- The floors are solid under normal walking.
- The walls are not moving, bowing, or separating.
- The building has at least one reliable way out, and ideally two.
- Stairs, railings, and doors work well enough to use every day.
- Smoke, gas, sewage, and chemical hazards are absent or controlled.
- Moisture and mold are not active problems.
- The building can be ventilated.
- Heat, water, and sanitation needs can be handled without creating new hazards.

### Occupancy does not mean perfect

An occupiable building may still need:
- Paint
- Minor caulking
- Window repair
- Weatherstripping
- Patch work
- Cleaning
- Pest control
- Better insulation

But it should not have:
- Active structural movement
- Active leaks that wet the interior
- Major hidden rot
- Dangerous smoke damage
- Sewage contamination
- Uncontrolled mold growth

If the building would be safe only "as long as nothing changes," it is not really occupiable.

## 8. What To Inspect Room By Room

If the building passes the first outside and entry checks, inspect it in a fixed order.

### 1. Foundation and lower walls

Look for:
- New cracks
- Spalling concrete
- Rotten sill plates
- Soft lower wall sections
- Water staining near the base
- Termite or insect damage
- Settling on one side

Foundation problems matter because they change the whole building.

### 2. Roof and ceiling

Look for:
- Sagging rafters or trusses
- Stains from old or active leaks
- Soft ceiling material
- Missing sheathing
- Burned or charred structure
- Daylight coming through the roof

If the roof is bad, do not assume the room below is safe.

### 3. Floors

Look for:
- Soft spots
- Bounce or spring
- Warping
- Buckling
- Spongy subfloor
- Rotten joists underneath if accessible

Test flooring gently with body weight, then with a tool if needed.

### 4. Walls and corners

Look for:
- Bowing
- Bulging
- Cracks at corners and openings
- Gaps between wall and ceiling
- Loose plaster or drywall
- Evidence of water running inside the wall

### 5. Doors, windows, and exits

Check whether:
- Doors open and close normally
- Windows can open enough for ventilation and escape if needed
- Locks do not trap people inside
- The main exit is not blocked
- The route to the exit is clear in the dark

### 6. Moisture and mold

Look and smell for:
- Musty odor
- Visible mold
- Wet insulation
- Peeling paint from water
- Efflorescence on masonry
- Condensation on cold surfaces
- Rotting wood

If the building smells wet, assume more moisture exists than you can see.

### 7. Fire damage

Inspect for:
- Soot in hidden areas
- Charred joists or studs
- Heat-damaged metal connectors
- Melted plastic or wiring
- Cracked masonry from heat
- Roof or ceiling areas that sag after fire

Fire damage often weakens hidden parts more than visible parts.

### 8. Utilities and services

Check whether:
- Water supply is safe and shut down if damaged
- Sewer or septic systems are backing up
- Gas lines or tanks are intact
- Electrical service is dry and undamaged
- Heating equipment is safe to use or disabled
- Flooded HVAC, ducts, or forced-air equipment have stayed off until cleaned and dried
- Chimney or flue is intact if the building has one

If you do not know the condition of a utility, do not assume it is safe.

## 9. A Simple Habitability Test

When someone asks, "Can we stay here?", use this short test.

### Ask these eight questions

1. Is the structure standing straight and stable?
2. Is the roof keeping water out?
3. Are the floors solid enough to walk on?
4. Are the exits usable?
5. Is there no gas, smoke, sewage, or electrical danger?
6. Is the building dry enough to avoid major mold?
7. Can the space be cleaned and ventilated?
8. Can heat, water, and sanitation be handled without creating new hazards?

If the answer to most of these is yes, the building may be occupiable after minor work.

If several are no, the building is still in repair territory.

If any are severe no's, the building is not ready for use.

## 10. A Simple Storage Test

When someone asks, "Can we at least store things here?", use this test instead.

### Storage is acceptable only if

- The roof is stable enough not to drop debris.
- The floor can keep items off the ground.
- Water will not reach the goods.
- Pests cannot easily get in.
- Smoke, fuel, sewage, and mold contamination are absent or controlled.

### Good storage signs

- Dry smell
- No active leaks
- No visible rodents or insect nests in the stored area
- No soft floor or falling ceiling material
- No direct sunlight, flooding, or standing water

### Bad storage signs

- Musty or sour odor
- Water marks that are still fresh
- Rodent droppings or nesting
- Mold on walls, boxes, or textiles
- Fire soot or oily residue
- Floor flexing under weight

If the storage space is questionable, use pallets, shelves, or bins to keep goods off the floor and check them often.

## 11. Practical Red Flags That Overrule Everything

Some signs matter more than the rest.

Stop and leave if you find:
- A floor that feels unsafe under normal walking.
- A roof that is visibly sagging.
- Fresh cracking sounds or new movement.
- Strong gas odor.
- Active fire damage or hidden smoldering.
- Sewage contamination.
- Major water saturation in framing.
- A single exit that may fail.
- Severe mold with structural decay.

These are not "maybe later" problems. They are "not this building right now" problems.

## 12. Practical Green Flags

The building is more promising if you find:
- Straight walls and even floors.
- Dry framing.
- Working doors and windows.
- No active smell of gas, sewage, or smoke.
- No obvious sagging in roof or ceiling.
- No fresh water staining.
- Only small, localized repair needs.
- Good ventilation and clear exits.

These green flags do not guarantee safety, but they make the next step more reasonable.

## 13. How To Write Down Your Decision

When you inspect a building, record the result in plain language.

Example notes:
- "Do not enter. Roof sag at center span, gas smell at rear, water in basement."
- "Short salvage only. Shell stable, one room wet, windows broken, exits usable."
- "Use after repair. Roof leak over kitchen, floor solid, no mold smell, needs patch and drying."
- "Reasonable for occupancy after checks. Dry, stable, exits clear, utilities off, minor window repair only."

Those notes are useful because they tell the next person what kind of work remains.

## 14. Final Habitability Order

If you want one simple way to think about the whole guide, use this order:

1. **Do not enter** if the building is unstable, poisoned, burned, flooded, or trapping people.
2. **Enter only for short salvage** if the shell is standing but the building is still risky.
3. **Use after repair** if the structure is solid but specific failures still need fixing.
4. **Reasonable for occupancy after checks** only when the structure, exits, moisture, and utilities all pass the basic test.

That is the practical off-grid habitability ladder.

If you remember nothing else, remember this: a building is not "good enough" just because it is still standing. It is only usable when it is standing, dry enough, stable enough, and easy enough to escape from if something goes wrong.
