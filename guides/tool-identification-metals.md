Before you work with metal, you need to know what you have. This guide covers simple, practical ways to identify metals using basic tools and your senses. No special equipment required—just a magnet, file, and observation.

<section id="why-identification-matters">

## Why Identification Matters

Different metals behave completely differently:
- Steel forges at different temperatures than copper
- Brass produces toxic fumes if overheated; copper does not
- Cast iron is brittle and shatters; mild steel bends and deforms
- Aluminum is light and soft; steel is heavy and hard
- Some metals rust; others don't

Working with the wrong metal can result in:
- Failed projects that crack or break
- Toxic fumes and health hazards
- Wasted time and materials
- Unsafe structures or tools

Taking five minutes to identify what you have prevents hours of frustration.

</section>

<section id="magnet-test">

## The Magnet Test (Ferrous vs. Non-Ferrous)

**Easiest first test:** Does it stick to a magnet?

### Ferrous Metals (Magnetic)

If a magnet sticks strongly, the metal contains iron.

**Ferrous metals:**
- Iron (pure)
- Steel (iron + carbon)
- Cast iron
- Some stainless steels (not all; some are non-magnetic)

**What this tells you:** The metal can be forged at high temperatures. It will rust if exposed to moisture without protection. It's probably salvageable and workable.

### Non-Ferrous Metals (Non-Magnetic)

If the magnet does NOT stick, the metal does not contain iron.

**Non-ferrous metals:**
- Copper
- Brass (copper + zinc)
- Bronze (copper + tin)
- Aluminum
- Lead
- Zinc
- Nickel (sometimes slightly magnetic, but weak)
- Stainless steel (some types)

**What this tells you:** These metals have different working properties. Most are non-rusting. Some are soft (copper, aluminum); some are hard (bronze). Most are valuable to salvage.

### The Strong Magnet Trick

Carry a strong neodymium magnet. Test everything:
- Magnetic? Probably steel or iron; workable in a forge
- Non-magnetic but shiny/copper-colored? Copper or brass
- Non-magnetic, light, silvery? Possibly aluminum
- Non-magnetic, very heavy, soft? Possibly lead (handle carefully)

:::tip
Keep a small strong magnet in your pocket. It takes seconds to test any metal you encounter. This single test eliminates most confusion.
:::

</section>

<section id="spark-test">

## The Spark Test (Steel vs. Cast Iron vs. Others)

Strike the metal sharply with a file or steel bar and observe the sparks. Different metals produce distinctly different spark patterns.

### How to Perform the Spark Test

1. Hold the metal against a grinding wheel or strike it sharply with a file
2. Observe the sparks against a dark background (works better in dimmer light)
3. Note the color, shape, and behavior of the sparks

### Spark Patterns

**High-Carbon Steel (Tool Steel):**
- Bright white or yellow sparks
- Long, forked spark trails
- Many sparks
- Sparks burst into smaller branches
- **Appearance:** Looks like a fireworks display
- **What it means:** Good for tools; will harden when quenched

**Mild Steel (Low-Carbon):**
- Yellow sparks
- Fewer than high-carbon steel
- Less forking and branching
- **Appearance:** Less dramatic than tool steel
- **What it means:** Easier to forge; won't harden as much with quenching

**Cast Iron:**
- Dull red or orange sparks
- Very short, dumpy spark trails
- Few sparks overall
- **Appearance:** Almost looks like it's barely sparking; sparks are stubby
- **What it means:** Brittle; needs careful handling; forges at lower temperature

**Stainless Steel:**
- Fewer, shorter sparks than mild steel
- Yellow but less bright
- Less forking
- **Appearance:** Noticeably less dramatic than steel
- **What it means:** Different working properties; harder to identify

**Copper (Pure):**
- Almost no sparks
- Red sparks if any
- **Appearance:** Barely anything when struck
- **What it means:** Not ferrous; won't spark like steel

**Aluminum:**
- Almost no sparks
- Grayish particles (not true sparks)
- **Appearance:** Looks more like fine dust than sparks
- **What it means:** Non-ferrous; won't spark like steel

### Practice the Spark Test

Different metals produce such distinct patterns that this becomes reliable with practice. Keep a sample of known steel, cast iron, and copper nearby. Compare unknowns to these known materials.

:::warning
**Spark Safety:** When sparking metals, protect your eyes. Wear safety glasses. Sparks are hot and travel fast. Never spark metals near flammable materials.
:::

<section id="weight-and-density">

## Weight and Density

Metals have very different densities. This is a quick way to narrow down possibilities.

### Relative Weight (Holding It)

**Very light (lighter than aluminum):**
- Almost nothing is lighter than aluminum in metal form
- If something metal feels very light, it's likely aluminum

**Light (aluminum-like):**
- Aluminum (2.7 g/cm³)
- Magnesium (very rare, even lighter; don't confuse easily)

**Medium (average):**
- Copper, brass, bronze (8–9 g/cm³)
- A piece of copper feels notably heavier than steel of the same size
- "Heavy for its size" suggests copper, brass, or bronze

**Heavy (feels dense):**
- Steel, iron (7.8 g/cm³)
- Lead (11.3 g/cm³; extremely heavy for its size)
- Lead is so dense that a small piece feels surprisingly heavy

### Quick Density Estimate

**Compare equal volumes:**
1. Take a piece of unknown metal, roughly 1 inch cube
2. Take a known piece of steel, similar size
3. Hold both; compare weight
4. Heavier than steel? Could be copper, brass, or lead
5. Lighter than steel? Could be aluminum (or a thin-walled steel object)

This is rough but useful for elimination.

### Specific Examples

| Metal | Density | Feel |
|-------|---------|------|
| Aluminum | 2.7 | Light, floats in impression |
| Steel | 7.8 | Standard heavy |
| Copper | 8.96 | Heavy; noticeably more than steel |
| Brass | 8.5 | Heavy; like copper |
| Lead | 11.3 | Extremely heavy; startlingly dense |

<section id="visual-inspection">

## Visual Clues

### Color

**Reddish or bright copper color:** Copper (non-magnetic)

**Goldish or yellowish (duller than gold):** Brass (non-magnetic)

**Silver with surface tarnish (greenish or bluish):** Copper or brass with patina

**Shiny silver:** Could be steel, aluminum, stainless, or nickel

**Dull gray with red/orange rust:** Steel or iron

**Silvery with no rust:** Aluminum, stainless, or non-ferrous

### Surface Characteristics

**Rough, pitted, or flaking:** Old steel or iron that has rusted

**Smooth, bright:** Newer steel, aluminum, or protected metal

**Oxidized green or blue coating:** Copper or brass

**Shiny after cleaning:** Steel, aluminum, or stainless steel

### Markings and Stamps

Many metals are stamped or labeled:

**Brass or bronze items:** Often marked "Brass," "Bronze," or "Cu-Sn"

**Stainless steel:** Often marked "Stainless," "SS," or "304/316" (stainless grades)

**Aluminum:** Often marked "Aluminum" or "Alu"

**Check before working:** Always look for stamps, engravings, or labels

<section id="workability-tests">

## Practical Workability Tests

### The File Test

A file can tell you about hardness and content.

**Procedure:**
1. Draw the file across the metal surface with moderate pressure
2. Observe how easily the file cuts
3. Feel for chips or dust

**Hard metals (file barely bites):**
- Tool steel, hardened steel, cast iron
- Requires a good file and serious effort

**Soft metals (file cuts easily):**
- Copper, aluminum, brass (annealed)
- File cuts visibly; produces small shavings

**Medium hardness:**
- Mild steel, unhardened steel
- File cuts with moderate effort

**What this tells you:** Soft metals are easy to work; hard metals require more force or heat.

### The Bending Test (Small Pieces Only)

**Procedure:**
1. Hold a small piece in a vise or pliers
2. Try to bend it sharply at room temperature
3. Observe what happens

**Bends without breaking:** Ductile (copper, brass, mild steel, aluminum)

**Breaks or cracks:** Brittle (cast iron, hardened steel)

**Bends slightly then breaks:** Work-hardened metal or certain alloys

**What this tells you:** Ductile metals are forgiving; brittle metals require careful handling and won't survive sharp bends.

:::warning
Only do this test on small pieces. Never bend large structural pieces without understanding the consequences.
:::

### The Sound Test

Strike the metal with a hammer and listen:

**Clear, ringing tone:** Steel, brass, or bronze

**Dull thud:** Lead, very soft aluminum, or heavily work-hardened metal

**Sharp crack:** Brittle metal (cast iron) or hardened steel

**Repeated ringing:** Good quality, resonant metal (used historically to judge bell metal and tool quality)

This is subtle and requires practice, but experienced metalworkers judge quality partly by sound.

</section>

<section id="salvage-assessment">

## Assessing Salvage

When you find a pile of scrap metal, here's a systematic approach:

### Step 1: Initial Magnet Sort

1. Use your magnet to separate ferrous (magnetic) from non-ferrous (non-magnetic)
2. Set aside ferrous pile (steel/iron)
3. Examine non-ferrous pile (copper, brass, aluminum, etc.)

### Step 2: Identify the Non-Ferrous

Separate the non-ferrous pile by type:

**By visual inspection:**
- Bright copper (reddish): Pure copper
- Yellowish/dull gold: Brass
- Light, silvery: Aluminum
- Very heavy, soft, dull gray: Possible lead (handle carefully)

**By weight:**
- Light: Aluminum
- Medium: Copper, brass
- Heavy: Lead (if soft)

### Step 3: Evaluate Quality

**Is it clean?** (Free of rust, corrosion, oil, paint)

**Is it solid?** (Not hollow, cracked, or corroded through)

**Is it useful for your project?** (Right size, shape, type)

**Estimate value:**
- Pure copper: High value; worth keeping
- Brass: Good value; highly sought
- Aluminum: Lower value; worth keeping only if quantity is large
- Mixed scrap: Lower value; useful for forge work if clean

### Step 4: Clean and Store

1. Remove surface rust with a wire brush
2. If heavily oiled or painted, wash or burn off paint (in outdoor fire only)
3. Store organized by type (ferrous separate from non-ferrous)
4. Keep dry to prevent rust

<section id="special-cases">

## Special Cases: Identifying Difficult Metals

### Stainless Steel

**Identification:**
- Non-magnetic (usually; some types are slightly magnetic)
- Spark test produces fewer sparks than carbon steel
- Doesn't rust
- Often stamped "Stainless" or "304/316"

**Working note:** Harder to forge than mild steel; requires higher temperature.

### Cast Iron

**Identification:**
- Magnetic
- Spark test shows dull, short sparks
- Very brittle (breaks if bent sharply)
- Often used in cookware, engine blocks, old tools
- Sometimes engraved with "Cast Iron"

**Working note:** Forges at lower temperature than steel; shatters if bent cold.

### Lead

**Identification:**
- Non-magnetic
- VERY heavy for size
- Soft enough to dent with a thumbnail
- Often found in old plumbing, batteries, old paint

**Working note:** TOXIC. Handle carefully. Wear gloves. Never inhale dust. Never use in food-contact applications.

:::warning
**Lead Hazard:** Lead is a neurotoxin. Prolonged exposure, especially through breathing dust or fumes, causes serious health damage. Never sand, burn, or heavily abrade lead without proper respiratory protection. Dispose of lead safely according to local regulations.
:::

### Galvanized Steel

**Identification:**
- Magnetic (it's steel)
- Gray, bumpy surface (zinc coating)
- Often stamped "Galv" or "Galvanized"
- Used in construction (roofing, fasteners, piping)

**Working note:** Produces TOXIC fumes if heated. Never forge or melt galvanized steel. Remove coating first if heating is necessary.

:::warning
**Galvanized Hazard:** Heating galvanized steel releases zinc oxide fumes, which cause metal fume fever (painful but not usually fatal). Always avoid heating galvanized steel. If you must, remove coating first chemically or mechanically.
:::

</section>

<section id="identification-summary">

## Quick Identification Summary

| First Step | What You're Testing |
|---------|------------|
| Magnet | Ferrous (contains iron) vs. non-ferrous |
| Spark test | High-carbon steel, mild steel, or cast iron |
| Weight | Narrows down possibilities (light = aluminum; very heavy = lead or copper) |
| Color | Visual elimination (red = copper; gold = brass; silver = steel/aluminum) |
| File test | Hardness and workability |
| Markings | Direct identification if labeled |

**Best practice:** Do multiple tests. One test is suggestive; three tests confirm.

<section id="practice">

## Practice Exercises

### Exercise 1: Assemble a Reference Collection

Gather small samples of:
- Steel (nail, bolt, scrap)
- Copper (wire, sheet, scrap)
- Brass (fitting, shell casing, scrap)
- Aluminum (foil, can, scrap)
- Cast iron (small tool, skillet piece)

Keep these labeled. Use them to practice spark tests and comparisons.

### Exercise 2: Magnet-Sort a Scrap Pile

Find or create a pile of mixed scrap metal. Use only a magnet to separate it into two piles. Compare your result to others; see if anyone missed anything.

### Exercise 3: Blind Identification Challenge

Have someone give you unknown metal pieces (unlabeled). Identify each using all the tests you've learned: magnet, spark, weight, color, markings. Compare your results to what they actually are.

### Exercise 4: Salvage Site Assessment

Visit a scrapyard or salvage site. Assess piles of scrap using your new skills. Estimate what's valuable, what's not, and why.

</section>

<section id="next-steps">

## Next Steps

Once you can reliably identify metals:

1. **Learn working temperatures:** Each metal forges at different temperatures
2. **Understand alloys:** How composition changes working properties
3. **Study color and hardness:** How heat treatment affects steel properties
4. **Develop expertise:** Experienced metalworkers can often identify metals by sound alone

These skills will make you a confident, efficient metalworker who knows exactly what they're working with and how it will behave.
