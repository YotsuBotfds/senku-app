:::danger
**Household chemical first-minute actions come before cleanup advice:**
1. Stop mixing or using the product. **Do not mix cleaners** to "cancel out" fumes or residue, and do not try acid/base neutralization on skin, eyes, or swallowed cleaners.
2. Isolate the area. Move children, pets, and bystanders away. If fumes are present, get to fresh air and ventilate only if you can do so without staying in the plume.
3. Decontaminate with water. Remove contaminated clothing. Flush skin or eyes with running water for 15-20 minutes.
4. If swallowed, do **not** induce vomiting, do **not** force large drinks, and do **not** give charcoal for caustic cleaners. Call poison control immediately. Use `unknown-ingestion-child-poisoning-triage.md` for caregiver-facing swallow triage.
5. Trouble breathing, drooling, chest pain, hoarseness, facial swelling, vomiting blood, collapse, or worsening symptoms means EMS now.

## Overview

Poisoning is ingestion, inhalation, or dermal exposure to a substance that causes harm. For foundational toxicology concepts (toxidromes, common chemical/plant/animal toxins, and decontamination protocols), see <a href="../toxicology.html">Toxicology & Poison Management</a>. In austere environments, poisonings range from accidental (plant ingestion, chemical exposure) to intentional (self-harm, homicide) to occupational. Rapid assessment, decontamination, and supportive care save lives. This guide covers recognition, decontamination, and management of common poisonings, with special emphasis on situations where specific antidotes are unavailable.

**Critical disclaimer:** This guide is educational. In any poisoning emergency, call poison control (if available) or professional emergency services immediately. Treatment details are intended for austere settings only. Many poisonings require ICU-level monitoring and specific antidotes only available at hospitals.

The global poison control center hotline (when available): Call your local emergency number or poison control center for guidance specific to the substance and victim.

For front-loaded caregiver questions such as "my child swallowed something unknown" or "someone inhaled smoke in an enclosed fire," use the dedicated quick guides <a href="../unknown-ingestion-child-poisoning-triage.html">Unknown Ingestion & Child Accidental Poisoning Triage</a> and <a href="../smoke-inhalation-carbon-monoxide-fire-gas-exposure.html">Smoke Inhalation, Carbon Monoxide & Fire-Gas Exposure</a>. Complaint-first household cleaner exposures, drain cleaner, detergent pods, bleach/ammonia mixtures, and under-sink chemicals should route here or to the unknown-ingestion guide first, with poison control called immediately. Use <a href="../chemical-safety.html">Chemical Safety</a> for product-pair lookup and prevention once the scene is safe.

Complaint-first adult medication overdose and medication-error prompts belong here too: too many pain pills, repeated cough/cold medicine doses, pills plus alcohol, slow breathing or pinpoint pupils after pills, new confusion after a double dose, or repeat sleepiness after naloxone. New or excessive serotonergic medication with shaking, sweating, fever, diarrhea, clonus, rigidity, confusion, overheating, twitching, or restless "cannot stop moving" symptoms also belongs here as possible serotonin syndrome. Those are poisoning or toxidrome questions first, not routine medication-selection, home-cold-care, panic, flu, menopause, GI-illness, or dementia-care questions.

<section id="initial-assessment">

## Initial Assessment and Stabilization

### Primary Survey (ABCs)

1. **Airway:** Is it open? Can the patient breathe?
2. **Breathing:** Rate, depth, effort. Signs of respiratory distress?
3. **Circulation:** Pulse, blood pressure, skin perfusion (warm, cool, clammy?).

### Secondary Assessment

**History (SLUDGE mnemonic for cholinergic toxidrome):**
- **Substance:** What was ingested/inhaled/exposed? Amount? When?
- **Route:** Oral (ingestion), inhalation, dermal, injection?
- **Time:** Exactly when did exposure occur?
- **Intentional vs accidental:** Important for prognosis and management
- **Co-ingestions:** Did the patient take other drugs or medications?
- **Pre-existing conditions:** Medical history, allergies?

**Physical exam:**
- **Vital signs:** Temperature, heart rate, respiratory rate, blood pressure
- **Skin:** Color (cyanosis, pallor, flushing), moisture, rash, needle marks
- **Eyes:** Pupil size (miosis = small, mydriasis = large), reactivity
- **Mouth:** Burns (caustic ingestion), breath odor (alcohol, garlic from organophosphates), color of lips/gums
- **Lungs:** Breath sounds, crackles (pulmonary edema), stridor
- **Abdomen:** Distension, tenderness, bowel sounds
- **Neurologic:** Level of consciousness, seizure activity, tremor, ataxia
- **Extremities:** Tremor, muscle rigidity, fasciculations (visible muscle twitching)

### Toxidrome Recognition

A **toxidrome** is a predictable cluster of symptoms from a specific class of poison. Recognizing the toxidrome guides empiric treatment.

## Common Toxidromes and Management

### Cholinergic (Organophosphates, Nerve Agents, Carbamates)

**Mechanism:** Inhibit acetylcholinesterase; excess acetylcholine overstimulates nicotinic and muscarinic receptors.

**Mnemonic - SLUDGE:**
- **S:** Salivation (excess drooling)
- **L:** Lacrimation (tearing)
- **U:** Urination (incontinence)
- **D:** Defecation (diarrhea)
- **G:** GI upset (nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramping)
- **E:** Emesis (vomiting)

**Additional signs:** Miosis (pinpoint pupils), bradycardia, hypotension, muscle fasciculations, respiratory distress, seizures.

**Management:**
- **Atropine:** 2–5 mg IV bolus; repeat every 5–10 minutes until signs of atropine effect (dry mouth, dilated pupils, increased heart rate). Titrate to dry patient.
- **Pralidoxime (2-PAM):** 1–2 g IV bolus over 5–30 minutes (reactivates cholinesterase; most effective early). Repeat every 1 hour for up to 24 hours if available.
- **Supportive care:** Aggressive airway management (may need intubation); oxygen; seizure precautions.

### Anticholinergic (Atropine, Scopolamine, Antihistamines, Antipsychotics)

**Mechanism:** Block acetylcholine at muscarinic receptors.

**Mnemonic - "hot as a hare, dry as a bone, red as a beet, mad as a hatter":**
- Hyperthermia (elevated temperature)
- Dry skin and mucous membranes
- Flushed, red skin
- Altered mental status (confusion, hallucinations, delirium)

**Additional signs:** Tachycardia, dilated pupils (mydriasis), urinary retention, constipation, seizures, arrhythmias.

**Management:**
- **Physostigmine (if available):** 1–2 mg IV/IM; can repeat every 20–30 minutes. Crosses blood-brain barrier (treats CNS symptoms).
- **Supportive care:** Cooling measures; seizure precautions; IV fluids; urinary catheterization if retention.
- **Note:** Benzodiazepines for agitation; avoid further anticholinergic drugs.

### Opioid

**Mechanism:** Mu receptor agonism; depresses respiratory drive.

**Triad:**
- Respiratory depression
- Miosis (pinpoint pupils)
- Altered mental status (drowsiness, coma)

**Additional signs:** Bradycardia, hypotension, decreased bowel sounds, pulmonary edema.

**Management:**
- **Naloxone (Narcan):** 0.4–0.8 mg IV/IM; repeat every 2–3 minutes until responsive. Total dose may need to be 2–10 mg.
- **Ventilatory support:** Bag-valve-mask (BVM) or intubation if respiratory depression severe
- **Monitor:** Naloxone wears off in 30–90 minutes; opioid toxins are longer-acting. Patient may re-sedate.

### Stimulant (Cocaine, Amphetamine, Caffeine, Theophylline)

**Mechanism:** Increase catecholamine (norepinephrine, dopamine, epinephrine) activity.

**Signs:**
- Tachycardia, hypertension, arrhythmias
- Hyperthermia
- Dilated pupils (mydriasis)
- Tremor, agitation, seizures
- Coronary ischemia (chest pain)

**Management:**
- **Benzodiazepines:** Lorazepam 2–4 mg IV for agitation, seizures
- **Cooling measures:** For hyperthermia
- **Cardiac monitoring:** Watch for arrhythmias
- **Beta-blockers:** Cautiously, if hypertension severe; avoid isolated beta-blockade (unopposed alpha activity worsens hypertension)
- **Supportive care:** IV fluids, oxygen

### Sedative-Hypnotic (Benzodiazepines, Barbiturates, Alcohol)

**Mechanism:** Enhance GABA (inhibitory neurotransmitter) activity.

**Signs:**
- Respiratory depression
- Altered mental status (drowsiness, coma)
- Loss of reflexes
- Hypotension
- Bradycardia
- Pulmonary edema (in severe cases)

**Management:**
- **Flumazenil (benzodiazepine reversal agent, if available):** 0.2 mg IV; repeat every 1 minute up to 3 mg. Use with caution (may precipitate seizures if patient is benzodiazepine-dependent).
- **Supportive care:** Ventilatory support, oxygen, IV fluids, warming (if hypothermic)
- **Naloxone:** Not effective for benzodiazepines; only for opioid co-ingestion

### Serotonin Syndrome

**Mechanism:** Excess serotonergic activity, often from SSRIs + another serotonergic drug (MAOI, tramadol, linezolid).

**Routing anchors:** New or increased antidepressants, SSRI/SNRI/MAOI combinations, tramadol, linezolid, dextromethorphan cough/cold medicine, "serotonin medicine" overdose, changing meds, or a dangerous medication reaction plus shaking, sweating, diarrhea, clonus, leg jerking, muscle rigidity, fever, confusion, overheating, twitching, severe restlessness, or inability to stop moving should route here before panic, flu, menopause, routine GI illness, or generic anxiety. Without a medication trigger, do not use this section for ordinary fever/diarrhea, stomach flu, heat symptoms, or anxiety alone.

**First-answer boundary:** If medication concern is paired with clonus, jerking, twitching, rigidity, fever, overheating, sweating, diarrhea, confusion, or inability to stop moving, the first response should name possible serotonin syndrome and emergency toxicology escalation. Rehydration, flu assessment, panic calming, menopause thermoregulation, dementia agitation routines, and anticholinergic comparisons are secondary unless the medication trigger is absent or serotonin features are clearly not present.

**Signs:**
- Agitation, confusion
- Tremor, hyperreflexia, myoclonus (jerking)
- Hyperthermia
- Tachycardia
- Pupil dilation
- GI symptoms (diarrhea)

**Management:**
- **Cooling measures:** For hyperthermia
- **Hold suspected serotonergic exposure:** Say: "Do not take another dose of the suspected serotonergic medicine/source while contacting Poison Control, EMS, or a clinician." Do not generalize this into "stop all serotonergic medications," "cease any potential source," or "stop taking the combo immediately." Do not stop unrelated essential medicines indefinitely without medical direction.
- **Benzodiazepines:** Clinician/EMS-directed use for severe agitation or seizures; do not present this as a self-treatment step for a layperson.
- **Cyproheptadine (serotonin antagonist, if available):** clinician/Poison-Control-directed antidote for confirmed or strongly suspected serotonin syndrome. Do not self-dose from a guide; use only under trained medical direction.

## Decontamination Methods

### Ingested Poisons

**Activated charcoal:** Absorbs toxins in the GI tract.
- **Dose:** 50–100 g (1 g/kg for children) as a single dose
- **When:** Most effective within 1 hour of ingestion; up to 4 hours may still be beneficial
- **How:** Mix with water to slurry; administer PO or via NG tube
- **Caution:** Do NOT use if airway is unprotected; aspiration risk
- **Ineffective for:** Metals (iron, lithium), caustics, alcohols, hydrocarbons

**Whole bowel irrigation:** Polyethylene glycol (GoLYTELY) flushes the GI tract.
- **Indication:** Iron overdose, sustained-release medications, drug packets
- **Dose:** 25–40 mL/kg/hr until stool is clear
- **Caution:** Contraindicated in GI obstruction or perforation

**Gastric lavage (stomach pumping):** Mechanical removal of stomach contents.
- **When:** Within 1 hour of ingestion; limited benefit after 2–3 hours
- **How:** Pass large-bore NG tube; aspirate; rinse with normal saline until clear
- **Caution:** Risk of aspiration, esophageal/gastric perforation; not for caustics (reinjury to esophagus)
- **Modern trend:** Less commonly used; activated charcoal preferred

**Induced vomiting (ipecac):** Not recommended. Risk of aspiration, worsening of caustic injury, delayed other treatments.

### Inhaled Poisons

**Routing anchors:** Mixed-cleaner fumes, bleach plus vinegar, bleach plus ammonia, strong cleaner smell causing cough, unknown under-sink fumes, solvent or paint-thinner fumes making someone sick, solvent vapors in a shed or garage, and complaint-first prompts about breathing trouble after cleaning all belong here first, with reciprocal product-pair lookup in chemical-safety.md.

- **Scene isolation:** Stop the cleaning task, get everyone out of the room or shed, and keep others from re-entering. Do not add another cleaner or neutralizer to "fix" the smell.
- **Remove from exposure:** Move to fresh air immediately
- **Ventilation:** Open doors and windows if that can be done without remaining in the plume; otherwise stay out and wait for trained help
- **Oxygen:** Provide high-flow oxygen when available, especially if hypoxemia is present; if the exposure is actually smoke or carbon monoxide, use the dedicated smoke/CO guide instead
- **Escalate fast:** Wheeze, stridor, inability to speak full sentences, chest pain, blue lips, confusion, or worsening cough means emergency transport now
- **Supportive care:** Monitor for delayed pulmonary edema

### Dermal Exposure

**Routing anchors:** Cleaner on skin, burning hands after spill, burning hands after touching an unlabeled under-sink bottle, bleach splash on arms, lye or drain opener on skin, unknown household liquid on skin, and chemical splash to face or body belong here first. After rinsing, use chemical-safety.md for hazard-class-specific follow-up when the product is known.

If skin is burning after touching an unknown cleaner or unlabeled bottle, treat it as a possible chemical burn now. Do not wait to identify the product and do not route first to poison ivy, contact rash, or ordinary skin irritation.

- **No neutralizers:** Do not apply vinegar, baking soda, lemon juice, or other acids/bases to the skin
- **Remove contaminated clothing and jewelry:** Safely remove and bag anything that may hold chemical against the skin, including rings or watches.
- **Wash thoroughly:** Copious water rinse for ≥15 minutes; use soap
- **Escalate:** Call Poison Control or EMS during or immediately after flushing, especially for persistent burning, blistering, spreading pain, numbness, large-area exposure, face/genital exposure, or any acid/alkali/drain-cleaner/battery-fluid possibility.
- **Specific agents:**
  - **Caustic chemicals (acid, alkali):** Copious water rinse; neutralization can increase reaction heat, so water is safest
  - **Organophosphates:** Soap and water removes; skin decontamination staff should wear gloves
  - **Phenol:** Isopropyl alcohol or polyethylene glycol may be more effective than water

### Ocular Exposure

**Routing anchors:** Cleaner in eye, bleach splash in eye, drain cleaner in eye, unknown spray or mist to eye, and eye burning after household chemical use belong here first. Do not delay irrigation to identify the product.

- **No neutralizers:** Do not put vinegar, baking soda, or any other chemical into the eye
- **Immediate action:** Hold eyelid open; irrigate with water or saline for 15–20 minutes
- **Specific agents:** For alkali burns, prolong irrigation (alkali penetrates deeper than acid)

## Heavy Metal Poisoning

Heavy metals accumulate in tissues and cause organ damage over time. In austere environments, exposure may occur through contaminated water, dietary sources, occupational hazards, or accidental ingestion. Management focuses on reducing exposure and, if available, chelation therapy.

### Lead Poisoning

**Sources:** Battery recycling, lead-based paint, contaminated soil, improperly glazed ceramics, ammunition manufacturing, certain herbal remedies.

**Acute vs. Chronic:**
- **Acute:** Abdominal pain, vomiting, anemia, encephalopathy (rare, severe)
- **Chronic:** Anemia, cognitive decline, peripheral neuropathy, renal dysfunction, hypertension, reproductive harm

**Timeline of effects:**
- **Initial (hours–days):** GI upset, abdominal pain
- **Weeks–months:** Fatigue, weakness, headache, memory problems
- **Years:** Irreversible cognitive and kidney damage, especially in children

**Management:**
- **Remove from source:** Identify and eliminate lead exposure (dietary sources, water pipes, occupational site)
- **Supportive care:** Iron supplementation if anemic; treat anemia
- **Chelation therapy (if available):**
  - **EDTA (ethylenediaminetetraacetate):** 1–1.5 g daily IV/IM for 3–5 days; increases urinary lead excretion
  - **DMSA (meso-2,3-dimercaptosuccinic acid):** 10 mg/kg orally 3× daily for 5 days; oral alternative
  - **BAL (British Anti-Lewisite):** 3–5 mg/kg IM every 4–6 hours for 2–7 days (used for severe lead encephalopathy)
- **Monitor:** Renal function, hemoglobin, lead levels if possible

:::warning
Lead chelation in austere settings is limited. Remove the source of exposure and provide supportive care; chelators are hospital-level treatments.
:::

### Mercury Poisoning

**Types and sources:**
- **Elemental mercury:** Thermometers, barometers, old dental amalgam. Poorly absorbed from GI tract; inhalation of vapor causes respiratory damage.
- **Organic mercury (methylmercury):** Contaminated fish, bioaccumulated in predatory fish. Crosses blood-brain barrier; causes severe neurologic damage.
- **Inorganic mercury salts:** Corrosive to GI tract; causes acute kidney injury.

**Acute inorganic mercury poisoning:**
- Corrosive GI symptoms (bleeding, perforation)
- Acute kidney injury (oliguria, hyperkalemia)
- Death from renal failure or sepsis

**Chronic methylmercury poisoning (Minamata disease):**
- Tremor, ataxia, dysarthria (difficulty speaking)
- Paresthesias (burning sensations)
- Visual/hearing loss
- Peripheral neuropathy
- Cognitive decline
- Irreversible CNS damage at high exposures

**Management:**
- **Elemental vapor exposure:** Remove to fresh air; oxygen if respiratory symptoms
- **Inorganic salt ingestion:** Aggressive IV fluid resuscitation; monitor for acute kidney injury
- **Chelation (if available):** DMSA or BAL for acute inorganic mercury (similar dosing as lead)
- **No specific treatment for methylmercury:** Remove source; supportive care only
- **Monitor:** Renal function, neurologic status

:::danger
Organic mercury poisoning causes permanent neurologic damage. Prevention (avoiding high-mercury fish species) is essential.
:::

### Arsenic Poisoning

**Sources:** Contaminated groundwater (geothermal regions), occupational exposure (smelting, pesticide manufacturing), intentional poisoning (tasteless, odorless in water).

**Acute poisoning (hours–days):**
- Severe GI hemorrhage, diarrhea ("rice-water" stools similar to cholera)
- Hypovolemic shock, arrhythmias
- Altered mental status
- Pulmonary edema
- Death from dehydration and shock

**Chronic poisoning (weeks–years):**
- Skin changes: Hyperpigmentation, hyperkeratosis, warts (Bowen's disease), squamous cell carcinoma
- Peripheral neuropathy (motor and sensory)
- Cardiovascular disease
- Chronic kidney disease
- Increased cancer risk (lung, bladder, skin)

**Management:**
- **Acute ingestion:** Aggressive fluid and electrolyte resuscitation; treat arrhythmias
- **Decontamination:** Activated charcoal may absorb arsenic (though not well-bound)
- **Chelation (if available):**
  - **BAL:** 3–5 mg/kg IM every 4–6 hours; most effective in acute poisoning
  - **DMSA:** 10 mg/kg orally 3× daily for 5 days; oral alternative for chronic exposure
- **Supportive care:** Monitor for renal failure, arrhythmias, sepsis
- **Water testing:** In endemic areas, test and treat water supplies (well-water contamination is common in Bangladesh, India, Chile)

:::info-box
Arsenic in groundwater is a public health crisis in many developing regions. Mitigation via rainwater harvesting or arsenic-specific water treatment (iron oxide filters, activated alumina) is essential for communities with contaminated wells.
:::

### Cadmium Poisoning

**Sources:** Battery manufacturing, electroplating, welding fumes, contaminated rice (in industrial regions), some ceramics and paints.

**Acute exposure (inhalation of cadmium oxide fumes):**
- Severe pulmonary irritation, tracheobronchitis
- Chemical pneumonia, pulmonary edema
- Death from respiratory failure

**Chronic exposure:**
- "Itai-itai disease" (historical name): Severe bone disease with pain, osteoporosis, pathologic fractures
- Kidney damage (proteinuria, chronic kidney disease)
- Anemia
- Olfactory nerve damage (loss of smell)

**Management:**
- **Acute inhalation:** Remove to fresh air; oxygen; monitor for pulmonary edema
- **Chronic exposure:** Remove from source; no specific antidote
- **Supportive care:** Monitor renal function, bone density, hemoglobin
- **EDTA or DMSA:** May have marginal benefit in chronic cadmium poisoning; evidence limited

:::tip
Prevention is paramount for cadmium: avoid occupational exposure, do not smoke (tobacco accumulates cadmium), monitor for contaminated foods in industrial regions.
:::

</section>

## Mushroom Poisoning

Mushroom toxins vary widely by species. Only a few wild mushrooms are universally recognizable as safe. In austere environments, mushroom gathering is extremely high-risk. The most dangerous toxins are amatoxins (cyclopeptides) found in Amanita species.

### Amatoxin Syndrome (Cyclopeptide Poisoning)

**Deadly species:**
- **Amanita phalloides** (death cap): Found in temperate forests worldwide; often mistaken for edible species
- **Amanita virosa** (destroying angels): North American species; lethal
- **Amanita virosa sensu stricto:** European destroying angel
- **Lepiota species:** Some contain amatoxins

**Mechanism:** Amatoxins inhibit RNA polymerase II, preventing protein synthesis in hepatocytes and renal tubules.

**Classic biphasic course (very important for recognition):**

**Phase 1 (0–12 hours post-ingestion):** Asymptomatic or mild GI upset
- Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal cramps
- Patient may feel better, leading to false reassurance

**Phase 2 (12–48 hours): Hepatic and renal failure**
- Rising transaminases (ALT, AST)
- Coagulopathy (rising INR/PT)
- Jaundice
- Hypoglycemia
- Encephalopathy
- Acute kidney injury
- Metabolic acidosis

**Phase 3 (48–72 hours+): Fulminant hepatic failure**
- Coma, seizures
- Bleeding diathesis
- Organ failure
- Death in 50–90% of untreated cases (even with treatment, mortality is 10–20%)

**Management:**

1. **Immediate decontamination:**
   - **Activated charcoal:** 50–100 g within 2 hours if available; amatoxins are absorbed quickly, so early intervention is crucial
   - **Gastric lavage:** If within 1 hour, may reduce absorption
   - Do NOT induce vomiting (no benefit; worsens dehydration)

2. **Supportive care:**
   - **IV fluids:** Aggressive resuscitation; correct electrolyte abnormalities (hypoglycemia, hypokalemia)
   - **Glucose monitoring:** Hypoglycemia is a sign of severe liver damage
   - **Coagulation support:** Monitor INR/PT; transfuse fresh frozen plasma or vitamin K if bleeding

3. **Specific antidotes (if available):**
   - **Silibinin (silymarin, milk thistle extract):** 330 mg IV every 6 hours or 20–30 mg/kg daily. Strongest evidence for benefit; inhibits amatoxin uptake into hepatocytes
   - **High-dose penicillin G:** 1 million units/kg/day (increases renal excretion of amatoxins); used in European centers
   - **Activated charcoal (multiple doses):** 50 g every 4–6 hours for 24–48 hours. Blocks enterohepatic recirculation of amatoxins

4. **Liver support:**
   - **N-acetylcysteine (NAC):** 150 mg/kg IV loading, then 50 mg/kg, then 100 mg/kg (similar dosing to acetaminophen overdose); may improve outcomes
   - **Monitoring:** Serial liver function tests, PT/INR, glucose

5. **Dialysis:**
   - Hemodialysis or CRRT (continuous renal replacement therapy) may support kidney function; not a definitive treatment

:::danger
Amatoxin poisoning is a medical emergency requiring ICU-level care and specific antidotes. Immediate evacuation to a hospital with silibinin availability is essential. Without treatment, mortality exceeds 50%.
:::

### Other Mushroom Toxins

**Gyromitra (false morel, gyromitrin):**
- Delayed GI symptoms (4–24 hours): Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea
- Hepatotoxicity, hemolysis
- Seizures, coma
- **Management:** Activated charcoal, supportive care, benzodiazepines for seizures; no specific antidote

**Muscarine (Inocybe, Clitocybe species):**
- SLUDGE syndrome (excessive salivation, lacrimation, urination, defecation, GI upset, emesis) similar to organophosphate poisoning
- Miosis, bradycardia, bronchospasm
- **Management:** Atropine (2–5 mg IV bolus, repeat as needed); supportive care

**Ibotenic acid / muscimol (Amanita muscaria, fly agaric):**
- Sedation, euphoria, confusion, hallucinations
- Myoclonus, ataxia
- Usually self-limited (4–10 hours)
- **Management:** Supportive care; benzodiazepines for agitation; generally not fatal

### Prevention

:::warning
In austere settings, mushroom foraging should only be done by trained, experienced foragers who can positively identify safe species. When in doubt, do not eat. Poisoning risk far outweighs potential nutrition gain.

Key rule: If you cannot identify a mushroom with 100% certainty as edible, do not consume it. Many deadly mushrooms closely resemble edible species.
:::

### Botulism (Clostridium botulinum Toxin)

**Mechanism:** *Clostridium botulinum* produces botulinum toxin, a neurotoxin that cleaves SNARE proteins at the neuromuscular junction, blocking acetylcholine release and causing flaccid descending paralysis. Spores are heat-resistant; the preformed toxin is heat-labile (destroyed at 185°F / 85°C for 5 minutes).

**Symptom timeline:**
- **12–36 hours post-exposure (range 6 hours–10 days):** Cranial nerve palsies — ptosis, diplopia, dysphagia, dysarthria, blurred vision
- **Progression (hours):** Symmetric descending flaccid paralysis — neck, arms, respiratory muscles, legs
- **Sensory function preserved:** Patient remains alert and afebrile with intact sensation
- **Respiratory failure** is the primary cause of death

**Supportive care:**
- **Airway protection:** Intubation and mechanical ventilation often required; monitor forced vital capacity closely
- **NG tube feeding:** Dysphagia prevents safe oral intake
- **Avoid aminoglycosides and magnesium:** These worsen neuromuscular blockade

**Antitoxin (if available):**
- **Equine heptavalent botulinum antitoxin (HBAT):** Single IV dose; does not reverse existing paralysis but binds circulating toxin and halts further damage
- **Most effective when given early** — before paralysis reaches respiratory muscles
- **BabyBIG (infant botulism):** Human-derived antitoxin; single IV dose

**Prevention:** For safe canning practices that destroy botulinum spores, see <a href="../food-preservation.html#pressure-canning">Food Preservation — Pressure Canning</a>.

:::danger
Botulism is a medical emergency. Even with antitoxin and ICU care, recovery takes weeks to months as new nerve terminals regenerate. Immediate evacuation to a facility with mechanical ventilation and antitoxin is critical.
:::

</section>

## Carbon Monoxide Poisoning

Carbon monoxide (CO) is a colorless, odorless gas produced by incomplete combustion of carbon-containing fuels. In austere environments, CO poisoning occurs from enclosed or poorly ventilated fires (charcoal burners, wood stoves, generators, vehicle exhausts in caves or tents).

**Mechanism:** CO binds hemoglobin with affinity ~200× higher than oxygen, forming carboxyhemoglobin (COHb). This displaces oxygen and impairs oxygen delivery to tissues.

### Exposure Sources and Risk

- **Enclosed fires:** Charcoal burners in tents, closed rooms, caves
- **Generator or vehicle exhaust** in enclosed space
- **Malfunctioning heating systems** (stoves, furnaces)
- **Ice fishing shelters, survival shelters** heated by open flame with poor ventilation

### Severity by Carboxyhemoglobin Level

| COHb Level | Timeline | Symptoms |
|-----------|----------|----------|
| 10–20% | 2–3 hours | Headache, dizziness, weakness |
| 20–30% | 1–2 hours | Severe headache, dizziness, nausea, weakness, loss of judgment |
| 30–40% | 1–1.5 hours | Headache, dizziness, nausea, ataxia, confusion, syncope |
| 40–50% | 1 hour | Severe disorientation, seizures, coma |
| 50%+ | Minutes to hours | Rapid loss of consciousness, death |

**Symptoms:**
- Headache (often described as "pressure" or "band-like")
- Dizziness, vertigo
- Nausea, vomiting
- Weakness, fatigue
- Chest pain (angina in patients with cardiac disease)
- Confusion, poor judgment, impulsivity
- Syncope (fainting)
- Seizures, coma (severe exposure)

**Atypical presentations:**
- "Cherry-red" skin (actually rare; cyanosis more common)
- Confusion or bizarre behavior mistaken for intoxication
- Delayed neuropsychiatric syndrome (develops days–weeks after acute exposure)

### Management

1. **Remove from exposure immediately:**
   - Move to fresh air
   - Do not re-enter enclosed space without respiratory protection

2. **High-flow oxygen:**
   - Provide 100% oxygen via face mask (non-rebreather preferred)
   - Oxygen accelerates CO clearance by reducing half-life of COHb from ~4 hours (on room air) to ~40 minutes (on 100% O₂)
   - Continue until COHb level <5% (if testing available) or symptoms resolve

3. **Hyperbaric oxygen (if available):**
   - Compression to 2.4–3 atm of 100% oxygen further reduces COHb half-life to ~20 minutes
   - Preferred for severe poisoning (LOC, seizures, cardiac arrhythmias)
   - May reduce delayed neuropsychiatric sequelae

4. **Supportive care:**
   - Monitor cardiac rhythm (arrhythmias common)
   - IV fluids
   - Treat seizures with benzodiazepines
   - Warm passively if hypothermic (common in cold-weather rescues)

5. **Evacuation:**
   - Severe exposures require hospital-level care and hyperbaric oxygen facility access
   - Even mild-to-moderate exposures should be evacuated for definitive management

:::tip
Prevent CO poisoning by ensuring adequate ventilation in any enclosed space with a heat source. A small gap or crack is not sufficient; exhaust must exit the structure completely. In shelters, use external combustion with insulated piping, or use body heat only.
:::

</section>

## Methanol and Ethylene Glycol Poisoning

Both methanol (methyl alcohol) and ethylene glycol are sweet-tasting, colorless liquids that mimic ethanol's initial effects but cause severe metabolic acidosis and organ failure due to toxic metabolites.

**Sources:**
- **Methanol:** Windshield washer fluid, industrial solvents, improvised distilled alcohol ("moonshine" containing methanol)
- **Ethylene glycol:** Antifreeze, some brake fluids, hydraulic fluids, coolants

### Methanol Poisoning

**Mechanism:** Methanol is oxidized to formaldehyde, then formic acid, which causes metabolic acidosis and blindness.

**Timeline:**
- **0–12 hours:** Ethanol-like intoxication (CNS depression, slurred speech, ataxia)
- **12–48 hours:** Sudden deterioration with metabolic acidosis, visual symptoms, seizures
- **>48 hours:** Fulminant metabolic acidosis, coma, death

**Symptoms (biphasic):**
- Early: Intoxication, nausea, vomiting, headache (may be mistaken for ethanol intoxication)
- Late: Severe metabolic acidosis (Kussmaul respiration, fruity breath odor), visual disturbances (scotomas, blurred vision, blindness due to optic nerve necrosis), seizures, coma

**Classic finding:** Normal mental status with severe anion-gap metabolic acidosis (high osmolar gap: calculated osmolarity − measured osmolarity >10).

**Management:**

1. **Antidote (if available):**
   - **Fomepizole:** 15 mg/kg IV bolus, then 10 mg/kg IV every 12 hours for 4 doses, then 15 mg/kg every 12 hours until methanol level <20 mg/dL
     - Inhibits alcohol dehydrogenase; prevents formation of formic acid
     - **PREFERRED** over ethanol (fewer side effects)
   - **Ethanol (if fomepizole unavailable):** Loading dose 0.6–0.8 g/kg IV to achieve blood ethanol level 100–150 mg/dL; maintain with continuous infusion
     - Competes for alcohol dehydrogenase; blocks methanol metabolism
     - Carries risk of hypoglycemia, CNS depression, respiratory depression

2. **Supportive care:**
   - **Sodium bicarbonate:** 1–2 mEq/kg IV bolus; titrate to maintain pH >7.3 (alkalinization slows formic acid toxicity)
   - **Folinic acid (leucovorin):** 5–7 mg/kg IV every 4–6 hours (may help methylate formic acid; evidence weak)
   - **Glucose monitoring:** Maintain euglycemia; hypoglycemia worsens outcome

3. **Hemodialysis:**
   - Removes methanol and formic acid
   - Indicated if severe acidosis, renal failure, or visual disturbances

4. **Evacuation:** Methanol poisoning requires ICU care and antidote administration; urgent evacuation essential.

### Ethylene Glycol Poisoning

**Mechanism:** Ethylene glycol is oxidized to glycoaldehyde, glyoxal, and oxalic acid, causing metabolic acidosis and acute kidney injury (oxalate crystals deposit in renal tubules).

**Timeline:**
- **0–12 hours:** Intoxication phase (resembles ethanol intoxication)
- **12–24 hours:** Cardiopulmonary phase (pulmonary edema, myocarditis, arrhythmias)
- **24–72 hours:** Renal phase (oliguria, acute kidney injury, seizures)

**Symptoms:**
- Early: Intoxication, nausea, vomiting, headache, ataxia
- Cardiac: Chest pain, arrhythmias, myocarditis
- Renal: Oliguria, flank pain, acute kidney injury (can be dialysis-requiring)
- **Classic finding:** Calcium oxalate crystals in urine ("envelope" or "dumbell" shaped)

**Management:**

1. **Antidote (if available):**
   - **Fomepizole:** 15 mg/kg IV bolus, then 10 mg/kg every 12 hours for 4 doses, then 15 mg/kg every 12 hours
     - **PREFERRED** (same mechanism as for methanol)
   - **Ethanol:** Loading dose 0.6–0.8 g/kg to achieve 100–150 mg/dL; maintain with infusion (similar risks as above)

2. **Supportive care:**
   - **Sodium bicarbonate:** 1–2 mEq/kg IV bolus; maintain pH >7.3
   - **Calcium supplementation:** Cautiously; monitor for hypocalcemia (oxalate binds calcium)
   - **IV fluids:** Aggressive resuscitation; maintain urine output >200 mL/hr to prevent oxalate precipitation in kidney tubules

3. **Hemodialysis:**
   - Removes ethylene glycol and toxic metabolites
   - Indicated if severe acidosis, renal failure, or significant ingestion (>100 mg/kg)

4. **Evacuation:** Ethylene glycol poisoning causes acute kidney injury; ICU care and dialysis may be required.

### Osmolar Gap

In austere settings without lab capabilities, estimate poisoning severity using clinical osmolar gap:

**Calculated osmolarity** = 2(Na) + glucose/18 + BUN/2.8 + ethanol/4 (if known)

**Osmolar gap** = measured osmolarity − calculated osmolarity

Osmolar gap >10 suggests alcohol (methanol, ethylene glycol, or isopropanol) poisoning.

:::warning
Both methanol and ethylene glycol initially mimic ethanol intoxication. The critical clue is that the patient worsens after 12+ hours with severe acidosis and organ failure. Do not assume "just drunk"—obtain a careful timeline and maintain high suspicion.
:::

## Improvised Antidote Preparation

In austere environments without access to pharmaceutical antidotes, certain treatments can be improvised from available materials. These are NOT replacements for hospital-grade antidotes but may improve outcomes when nothing else is available.

### Activated Charcoal (from Wood)

**Principle:** Activated charcoal adsorbs many toxins in the GI tract, reducing absorption.

**Method:**

1. **Source material:** Hardwood (oak, birch, maple) burned completely to charcoal; avoid softwoods or treated wood
2. **Activation:**
   - Pulverize charcoal to fine powder (mortar and pestle, or wrapped in cloth and crushed with hammer)
   - Wash powder in water 3–4 times; discard water (removes ash)
   - Dry thoroughly in sun or by low heat; grind again to ensure fine consistency
3. **Dose:** Suspend 25–50 g (roughly 2–4 tablespoons) in 100–200 mL water to form a slurry
4. **Administration:** Mix with juice or water if possible to improve palatability; administer PO or via nasogastric tube

**Cautions:**
- Activated charcoal is black; patient may vomit from appearance
- Repeat doses every 4–6 hours for 24–48 hours if toxin absorption ongoing (e.g., some plant toxins)
- DOES NOT work for metals, alcohols, caustics, or strong acids/bases

### Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS)

**Principle:** Many poisonings (plant toxins, heavy metals, organophosphates) cause vomiting and diarrhea. ORS replaces fluids and electrolytes lost.

**Recipe (per liter of clean water):**
- 1 liter clean/boiled water
- 6 level teaspoons sugar
- 1/2 level teaspoon salt

**Alternative if salt unavailable:**
- Boil rice; drink the rice water (natural electrolytes)

**Administration:**
- Small, frequent sips (30–60 mL every 30 minutes) to prevent vomiting
- Continue until diarrhea stops and patient can drink normally

### Ethanol for Methanol/Ethylene Glycol Poisoning

**Principle:** Ethanol inhibits alcohol dehydrogenase, blocking conversion of methanol/ethylene glycol to toxic metabolites.

**Dosing (austere approximation, NOT ideal):**
- **Goal:** Achieve blood ethanol level 100–150 mg/dL (difficult without blood tests)
- **Loading dose:** 0.8 g/kg (roughly 1 mL of 80-proof vodka per kg body weight for 70 kg person ≈ 70 mL or ~2.4 oz)
- **Maintenance:** 10–15 mL of 80-proof alcohol every 2–4 hours to maintain level

**Routes:**
- **PO:** Dilute alcohol (e.g., vodka, whiskey) in juice or water; small frequent doses
- **IV:** If IV access available, normal saline or D5W with small ethanol aliquots (NOT standard; burns veins)

**Cautions:**
- Ethanol itself causes CNS depression; oversedation worsens outcome
- Risk of hypoglycemia; give dextrose if available
- This is a temporizing measure until fomepizole or hospital care available
- Frequent re-dosing needed (every 2–4 hours) to maintain levels

:::tip
If methanol or ethylene glycol poisoning is suspected AND the patient is conscious and can swallow, starting ethanol dosing may slow toxin metabolism and buy time for evacuation. However, this is NOT a cure—hospital-level care with fomepizole is essential.
:::

### Sodium Bicarbonate for Acidosis

**Principle:** Many poisonings (methanol, ethylene glycol, salicylates, tricyclic antidepressants) cause metabolic acidosis. Alkalinization may slow toxin effects and enhance excretion.

**Austere preparation:**
- Baking soda (sodium bicarbonate) available in many kitchens or commercial uses
- Typical dose: 1 teaspoon (5 g) baking soda in 200 mL water, given as oral solution
- Can repeat every 4 hours

**Concerns:**
- Oral bicarbonate tastes terrible; patient may vomit
- IV administration is FAR more effective but requires medical expertise; not recommended in austere settings
- Monitor for hypernatremia if large doses repeated

**Better in austere settings:** Focus on supportive care (fluids, oxygen) rather than attempting bicarbonate dosing without blood gas monitoring.

### Honey and Plant-Based Antimotility (Cautionary Note)

Some austere protocols suggest honey or plant extracts for GI upset from poisons. **Use extreme caution:**
- Antimotility agents (like loperamide) can INCREASE absorption of some toxins and prolong GI symptoms; NOT recommended
- Honey provides calories but does not reduce toxin absorption; use only for comfort/calories
- Plant remedies with unknown pharmacology may interact unpredictably with poisons

:::danger
Do not assume "natural = safe." Many plant remedies have their own toxins. Stick to decontamination (activated charcoal) and supportive care (fluids, heat/cold management).
:::

</section>

## Common Plant and Animal Toxins

### Dangerous Plants

For a detailed comparison table of medicinal plants versus their toxic lookalikes (with distinguishing leaf, stem, flower, and habitat features), see <a href="../sur-03-herbal-medicine.html#toxic-lookalikes">Herbal Medicine Guide — Dangerous Plant Lookalikes</a>.

| Plant | Toxin | Symptoms | Management |
|-------|-------|----------|-----------|
| **Ricin (castor bean)** | Ricin protein | Delayed (12–24 hr): GI distress, seizures, organ failure | Supportive care; no specific antidote |
| **Foxglove (digitalis)** | Cardiac glycosides | Nausea, arrhythmias, hyperkalemia | Digoxin-specific antibody; avoid hyperkalemia |
| **Oleander** | Cardiac glycosides | Nausea, arrhythmias | Similar to foxglove |
| **Nightshade (belladonna, datura, henbane)** | Tropane alkaloids (atropine) | Anticholinergic toxidrome | Benzodiazepines for agitation; supportive care |
| **Poison hemlock** | Furanocoumarin (phototoxic) | Severe dermatitis, blistering with UV exposure | Avoid sun exposure; supportive care |
| **Castor bean (ricin)** | Ricin | Severe GI, respiratory, multi-organ failure | Supportive care |
| **Strychnine tree** | Strychnine | Rigid muscles, "risus sardonicus" (facial grimace), lockjaw, opisthotonus (back arching), seizures | Benzodiazepines, muscle relaxants, supportive care |

### Snake Envenomation

**Signs vary by venom type:**
- **Neurotoxic:** Ptosis, diplopia (double vision), respiratory paralysis
- **Hemotoxic:** Coagulopathy (bleeding), tissue destruction at bite site, shock
- **Myotoxic:** Muscle pain, rhabdomyolysis

**Initial management (without antivenin):**
1. **Immobilize limb:** Splint; keep below heart if possible
2. **Remove constrictive items:** Rings, bracelets (reduce swelling complications)
3. **Reassess distal perfusion:** Check pulse, capillary refill, sensation below bite
4. **Elevate limb after 24–48 hours:** Once risk of systemic venom absorption is lower (paradoxically, initial elevation may increase absorption)
5. **Supportive care:** IV fluids, pain management, monitor for coagulopathy (watch for spontaneous bleeding, hematuria)
6. **Antivenin (if available):** Specific antivenin for regional snake species
7. **Evacuate urgently:** Even without antivenin, supportive care at hospital is essential (blood transfusion, clotting factor replacement, possible amputation if necrosis severe)

**Avoid:**
- Tourniquet (risk of tissue loss)
- Incision and suction (worsens wound, ineffective)
- Electrical shock (not proven effective)
- Ice (increases tissue damage)

### Insect Stings (Bee, Wasp, Hornet)

**Mild envenomation:** Local pain, swelling.
- Remove stinger (if present) by scraping (not tweezers, which may push venom)
- Ice pack
- Oral antihistamine and analgesic
- Monitor for secondary infection

**Anaphylaxis:** Urticaria, angioedema, stridor, hypotension, shock.
- **Epinephrine IM 0.3–0.5 mg immediately** (1:1000 solution)
- **IV access and fluids**
- **Antihistamine and corticosteroid**
- Observe for at least 4–6 hours (biphasic reactions possible)

</section>

<section id="supportive-care">

## Supportive Care and Monitoring

### Airway and Ventilation

- **Supplemental oxygen:** For any hypoxemia
- **Positioning:** Head of bed elevated to prevent aspiration
- **NG tube:** If unable to protect airway; useful for activated charcoal or gastric lavage
- **Intubation:** If respiratory depression, seizures, or inability to protect airway
- **Ventilatory parameters:** Maintain SaO₂ >94%, PaCO₂ 35–45 mmHg

### Circulation and Fluids

- **IV access:** Large-bore for rapid fluid resuscitation if needed
- **Fluid resuscitation:** 20 mL/kg bolus of normal saline for hypotension; repeat as needed
- **Vasopressors:** Epinephrine or norepinephrine for refractory hypotension
- **Cardiac monitoring:** For arrhythmia detection
- **Labs:** Electrolytes, renal function, liver function, coagulation studies if available

### Seizure Management

- **Benzodiazepines first-line:** Lorazepam 2–4 mg IV or diazepam 5–10 mg IV
- **Second-line:** Phenytoin or phenobarbital if seizures continue
- **Airway protection:** Be prepared for intubation if status epilepticus

### Hyperthermia Management

- **Cool environment:** Remove excess clothing
- **Tepid sponging:** Water at room temperature
- **Ice packs** to groin and axillae if severely hyperthermic (>41°C)
- **Treat underlying cause:** Cholinergic agents, stimulants, serotonin syndrome
- **Sedation:** Benzodiazepines reduce muscle activity and heat production

### Disposition and Evacuation

**Immediate evacuation:**
- Any severe toxidrome (respiratory depression, shock, seizures, severe hyperthermia)
- Unknown or complicated poisonings
- Toxins requiring specific antidotes not available locally
- Psychiatric risk (intentional overdose; suicide risk assessment needed)

<section id="summary">

## Summary

Poisoning management begins with rapid assessment, ABCs, and decontamination. Recognizing toxidromes guides empiric treatment. Specific antidotes are available for many poisons but not all; supportive care is the foundation. In austere settings without access to specific antidotes, benzodiazepines, oxygen, IV fluids, and careful monitoring are lifesaving. Know your local poisons (plants, animals, chemicals) and have contact information for poison control if available. When in doubt, evacuate to a facility with toxicology expertise and antidote availability.
