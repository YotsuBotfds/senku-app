## 1. Watercraft Design Selection

Choosing the right boat depends on available materials, intended use, and water conditions. Each design has distinct advantages and limitations.

### Design Options

**Dugout canoe:** A log hollowed out, providing a simple, durable, heavy vessel

**Plank canoe:** Wooden planks sewn or fitted together; lighter than dugout, more complex construction

**Coracle (hide boat):** A wooden frame covered with animal hide; ultralight, portable, excellent for rough water

**Skin-on-frame kayak:** A wooden frame covered with skin; efficient in calm to moderate water, difficult to self-rescue if capsized

**Raft:** Multiple logs or reeds lashed together; simple, unsinkable, slow

### Material Availability

**Dugout:** Requires a single large log (2-3 feet diameter, 20-40 feet long); high labor, minimal additional materials

**Plank:** Requires a sawmill or hand-sawn boards; 500-1000 board feet of wood

**Coracle:** Requires 1-2 animal hides, wooden frame materials (12-15 poles)

**Kayak:** Requires 12-20 light flexible poles, 1-2 seal or large animal hides

**Raft:** Requires 12-20 logs or bundles of reeds, rope

### Speed & Efficiency

| Design | Load (lbs) | Speed (mph) | Effort | Stability | Durability |
|--------|-----------|------------|--------|-----------|-----------|
| Dugout | 500-2000 | 2-3 | Medium | Very high | 20-30 yrs |
| Plank | 300-1500 | 3-4 | Medium-high | High | 15-20 yrs |
| Coracle | 200-400 | 1-2 | Low | Medium | 3-5 yrs |
| Kayak | 200-350 | 4-6 | Medium | Low | 5-10 yrs |
| Raft | 1000-5000 | 1-2 | Low | Very high | 2-5 yrs |

## 2. Dugout Canoe Construction

A dugout is the simplest watercraft to build. A properly made dugout lasts 20-30 years with minimal maintenance.

### Log Selection

- **Species:** Select straight-grained softwood or lightweight hardwood (pine, cedar, cottonwood, tulip poplar). Avoid oak (too heavy) and dense hardwoods (difficult to work).
- **Diameter:** 2-3 feet diameter; 25-40 feet long
- **Age:** Fresh cut is easier to work than aged dry wood; aged wood is durable for the finished vessel
- **Condition:** Select logs without major cracks, rot, or insect damage

### Felling & Preparation

1. **Fell the tree:** Use axe, saw, or wedge; minimize splintering at the cut ends
2. **Limb:** Remove branches completely
3. **Transport:** Roll to the building site (use ramps or water if available); a 30-foot log 2 feet diameter weighs ~10,000 lbs when fresh (very heavy)
4. **Support:** Place the log on supports (rocks, smaller logs) at waist height for comfortable working

### Hollowing Process

**Tool options:**

- **Axe:** Most basic; creates rough interior but functional
- **Adze:** Specialized tool with perpendicular blade; much faster than axe
- **Fire and scraping:** Burn the interior with coals and scrape the charred wood away (traditional method; slow but effective with limited tools)
- **Saw:** A frame saw or crosscut saw can be used to make cuts; combined with hand tools, speeds the process

**Process:**

1. **Mark the outline:** Draw a centerline along the top of the log, then mark a line 1-2 feet from each side (the wall thickness). Mark the interior hollowing area between these lines.
2. **Remove the top:** Using axes or saws, remove the top section of the log (12-18 inches of height) from the hollow area
3. **Hollow the interior:** Remove wood from the interior, working from the ends toward the middle. Aim for walls 2-3 inches thick (strength without excessive weight).
4. **Check symmetry:** The hollow should be roughly symmetrical to maintain balance
5. **Smooth the interior:** Use adzes, scrapers, or chisels to create an even surface

**Time estimate:** 100-300 hours for a 30-foot dugout with hand tools (varies with wood hardness and tool quality)

### Finishing

1. **Seal cracks:** Any longitudinal cracks risk water entry. Caulk with pitch, tar, or fiber-reinforced putty.
2. **Round the exterior:** Sharp edges catch on obstacles and create stress concentrations. Round the exterior edges with axes or planes.
3. **Carve gun'ales (rails):** Add raised edges along the top 2-3 inches to prevent water from flowing in while paddling. These are carved from the existing wood or added as separate rails.
4. **Waterproof:** Although wood is naturally water-resistant, coating with pitch, tar, or plant oil extends durability.

### Flotation & Stability

A solid wood dugout is inherently buoyant (wood floats). Stability is determined by:

- **Width:** Wider = more stable but slower
- **Depth:** Deeper = more stable but heavier
- **Freeboard:** Height above water = more stability against waves and water entry

A 2-foot diameter, 30-foot long dugout with 18-inch depth and 8-inch freeboard is stable in calm to moderate waves.

## 3. Plank Canoe Construction

A plank canoe is lighter and faster than a dugout but requires more sophisticated joinery.

### Materials

- **Hull planks:** 1-1.5 inch thick, 12-20 inches wide, cedar or pine (16-20 planks per canoe for a 20-foot boat)
- **Frame:** Ribs or skeleton structure of lighter wood (ash, birch) for internal bracing
- **Fastenings:** Wooden pegs, natural fiber lashing, or (if available) iron nails/screws
- **Sealing:** Pitch, tar, or plant oil for waterproofing

### Construction Steps

**Frame building:**

1. Construct a strongback (central longitudinal timber) and cross-members (supports at bow, stern, and midship)
2. Build a keelson (longitudinal member under the strongback) for additional strength
3. These form the skeleton; planks attach to this frame

**Plank laying (clinker or edge-set construction):**

1. Start with the garboard (first plank attached to the keel)
2. Lay succeeding planks in overlapping fashion (like shingles); each plank overlaps the one below by 1-2 inches
3. Fasten planks to the frame with pegs or nails; fasten planks to each other along the overlap with wooden pegs
4. Work upward toward the gunwale (top edge)

**Internal framing:**

1. Once the hull is planked, install internal ribs (arched cross-members) that tie the planks together and provide longitudinal strength
2. Space ribs 12-18 inches apart from bow to stern
3. Each rib is fitted individually to match the hull shape

**Finishing:**

1. Install gunwales (rails running the length of the canoe at the top) to protect the top edges and provide strength
2. Caulk seams between planks with fiber-reinforced pitch to seal against water entry
3. Waterproof the exterior
4. Install seats, paddler knees braces, and flotation

**Time estimate:** 300-500 hours of skilled labor

### Sealing & Waterproofing

Pitch or tar is applied:

1. Heat the pitch until liquid (150-200°C; very hot, handle carefully)
2. Apply with a brush or wooden paddle to all seams and the exterior
3. The pitch cools and hardens, creating a waterproof seal

Alternative: Plant oils (linseed oil, tree sap) or animal fats mixed with mineral substances can work but are less effective than pitch.

## 4. Coracle (Hide Boat) Construction

A coracle is an ancient design still used in parts of Wales, Ireland, and Asia. Ultralight and durable, a coracle fits in a person's hand when folded.

### Frame Construction

**Materials:**

- 12-15 thin flexible poles (hazel, willow, ash) 1 inch diameter, 10-12 feet long
- Wooden cross-members for the base
- Rope or cord for lashing

**Process:**

1. **Create an oval base:** Arrange 4-5 poles in an oval shape on the ground, crossing at the ends
2. **Build the skeleton:** Weave additional poles vertically around the base, creating a dome-like framework (approximately 5-6 feet in diameter, 2-3 feet deep)
3. **Cross-brace:** Add diagonal ribs to prevent the frame from collapsing; tie everything securely with rope
4. **Create seats:** Install a wooden cross-beam or seat support across the middle

### Hide Covering

**Hide preparation:**

1. Select a large animal hide (sheep, goat, deer, or larger). For a small coracle, one hide may be sufficient; larger boats require sewn hides.
2. Soak the hide in water for 24 hours to soften
3. Stretch the hide over the frame; it should be wet and pliable
4. Use wooden pegs, nails, or cord to attach the hide to the frame at intervals
5. As the hide dries (1-2 weeks), it shrinks and tightens, forming a weatherproof seal

**Sealing:** Apply pitch or plant oil to the hide exterior; this waterproofs and protects from sun damage (UV exposure cracks untreated hide).

### Characteristics

**Advantages:**

- Ultralight (30-50 lbs total weight)
- Portable; can be carried by one person
- Durable hide lasts 3-5 years with care
- Excellent for rough water (high freeboard, responsive to waves)

**Disadvantages:**

- Slow (paddled only)
- Limited load capacity (150-300 lbs)
- Hide requires periodic re-oiling
- Not suitable for extended ocean travel

## 5. Skin-on-Frame Kayak Construction

A kayak is an advanced design requiring precise frame fitting and hide work. Historical Inuit kayaks are exceptional examples of resource-efficient engineering.

### Frame Design

**Materials:**

- Frame poles (driftwood, ash, or willow): 12-15 poles, varying diameter
- Crossbeams and bracing: 10-20 pieces
- Lashing cord: seal sinew or plant fiber

**Dimensions (2-person kayak):**

- Length: 16-18 feet
- Beam (width): 18-22 inches
- Depth: 12-14 inches
- Internal volume: 60-80 cubic feet

**Construction process:**

1. **Build the gunwales:** Two long thin poles run the full length; they form the upper edge
2. **Build the keelson:** A central pole runs below, providing longitudinal strength
3. **Add cross-members:** Short ribs spaced 12-18 inches apart support the width and maintain shape
4. **Install sheerline braces:** Diagonal braces from gunwales to keelson prevent the frame from flexing excessively
5. **Tie joints:** Every intersection is lashed with fine cord or sinew; no nails are used (joints must be slightly flexible)
6. **Install a cockpit:** A hole 18-24 inches long, 12 inches wide, in the midship area where the paddler sits

### Hide Covering

**Hide selection:**

- Seal hide is traditional and superior (natural oils repel water; stretches and shrinks minimally)
- Whale hide is used where available
- Large land animal hides (moose, elk, deer) work but are heavier and less water-resistant

**Covering procedure:**

1. Soak and prepare hides as for coracles
2. Stretch hides over the frame, stitching them together with sinew thread at the seams
3. The stitching must be waterproof (traditional: stitch is sewn and then sealed with pitch)
4. Attach the hide to the frame with cord lashing through holes in the hide, not nails or pegs
5. Use an upstroke sewing technique (the stitch pulls the hides snugly together and the seam is naturally on the outside, away from the paddler)

**Cockpit edge:** The edges of the cockpit opening are reinforced with a wooden ring (the hatch ring) and sealed with pitch to prevent water from running down the outside of the skin.

### Waterproofing & Durability

- Seal all seams with pitch; gaps lead to water infiltration and eventual structural failure
- Apply plant oil or grease to the hide exterior; this prolongs life and improves water shedding
- Historical kayaks required annual re-oiling and maintenance

**Lifespan:** 5-10 years with proper care; hides eventually crack and must be replaced.

## 6. Paddle & Oar Construction

### Paddles (for kayaks, canoes, small boats)

A paddle has a blade, shaft, and grip. Balance and weight distribution affect efficiency.

**Materials:**

- **Blade:** Ash, birch, or spruce; straight-grained
- **Shaft:** Light flexible wood (ash, spruce); 4-5 feet long, 1.25-1.5 inches diameter

**Blade dimensions:**

- Length: 6-8 inches
- Width: 4-5 inches
- Thickness: 0.5-0.75 inches (thinner = lighter and more flexible)

**Construction:**

1. Select a piece of wood for the blade; saw or carve into shape
2. Attach the shaft to the blade with a mortise-and-tenon joint or lashing
3. Taper the transition from blade to shaft for ease of movement through water
4. Carve a grip at the top of the shaft; the grip should be slightly oval (allows the hand to find the correct orientation)
5. Apply waterproof finish (oil or light tar)

**Characteristics:**

- Length: 5-6 feet total (longer for larger boats)
- Weight: 2-3 lbs (lighter is better for sustained paddling)
- Balance: Weight should be forward of the midpoint (the blade should feel light in the hand)

### Oars (for rowing boats)

An oar is longer than a paddle and has a larger blade, designed for rowers sitting in a boat pulling oars through oarlocks (pivot points).

**Dimensions:**

- Total length: 8-10 feet (longer oars = more mechanical advantage but slower recovery)
- Blade length: 8-12 inches
- Blade width: 5-7 inches
- Shaft length: 6-8 feet

**Construction:** Similar to paddles but oars require stronger blades (experience higher forces) and a proper grip area (oarlock interface is typically 4-5 inches of the shaft wrapped with leather or cord for wear resistance).

**Characteristics:**

- A pair of oars rows significantly faster than paddling (3-4 mph vs. 1-2 mph paddling)
- Rowing is less tiring than paddling over long distances
- Oars require an oarlock mechanism; paddles do not

### Sail Rig (Simple Sprit Sail)

A basic sail can be added to larger canoes or small boats to augment paddling or rowing.

**Sprit sail components:**

- **Mast:** A vertical pole (10-15 feet); stepped into the boat and held by stays (guy wires)
- **Sail:** A triangular cloth; approximately 100-150 square feet for a 20-foot canoe
- **Sprit:** A diagonal pole that spreads the sail and transmits force to the mast
- **Boom:** A pole that extends the bottom edge of the sail

**Rigging:**

1. Install the mast with shrouds (side support cables) and a forestay (forward support cable)
2. Attach the sail with lines (fairleads); the sail can be adjusted by pulling lines to change its angle to the wind
3. The sprit connects the top-back corner to the sail; the boom extends from the bottom
4. Stays and braces allow the mast to flex slightly without breaking

**Sailing performance:**

- A sprit sail is efficient on downwind (running) courses
- Close-haul (into the wind) is difficult without a keel or centerboard (a fin under the boat that prevents sideslip)
- With practice, boats can be sailed 60-80 degrees from downwind direction

## 7. Stability & Self-Rescue

### Stability Assessment

**Righting moment:** The force resisting capsizing; depends on hull shape and weight distribution.

- **Wide, shallow boats:** High righting moment; stable but slow
- **Narrow, deep boats:** Low righting moment; fast but easily capsized

For any water crossing, stability is critical.

### Reducing Capsizing Risk

1. **Lower center of gravity:** Sit low in the boat; distribute weight evenly
2. **Know your boat:** Small boats respond quickly to weight shifts; avoid sudden movements
3. **Balance loads:** Equal weight on both sides; avoid loading one side more than the other
4. **Stay seated:** Sitting while paddling is more stable than kneeling or standing
5. **Practice:** Spend time in calm water learning how your boat responds to movements

### Self-Rescue If Capsized

**In cold water (under 60°F):**

Hypothermia (rapid body cooling) is the primary risk. Survival time is 15-30 minutes. Self-rescue must be fast.

**Procedure:**

1. **Stay with the boat:** An overturned boat provides flotation. A boat provides significantly more flotation than the human body.
2. **Establish a grip:** Grab the boat edge or, for larger boats, climb over the hull
3. **Get horizontal:** Minimize exposing your body to cold air; maintain contact with the floating boat
4. **Signal:** Make noise; wave; try to attract attention
5. **Do not attempt to swim:** Swimming in very cold water causes immediate incapacitation and cold shock (gasping and aspiration of water)

**For kayaks and narrow boats:** Self-rescue is very difficult. Historically, kayakers would never capsize (practiced extensively in calm water). If capsized, rely on rescue by other paddlers or reaching shore.

**For wider canoes/coracles:** Climbing back in is possible if you can get horizontal on the hull.

### Flotation & Life Wear

Add flotation to boats:

1. **Sealed compartments:** Sealed air-filled sections that provide buoyancy even if the boat is full of water
2. **Flotation bags:** Air-filled bags lashed inside the boat
3. **Life wear:** Wear a buoyant garment or lifejacket (if available) at all times on water

A boat with flotation that's half-filled with water can still support a swimmer; a boat without flotation sinks and cannot be used for rescue.

## 8. Maintenance & Storage

### Regular Maintenance

**After each use:**

1. Rinse with fresh water (salt water accelerates corrosion and hide degradation)
2. Allow to dry completely before storage
3. Inspect for damage

**Monthly:**

1. Check all fastenings (nails, pegs, lashing)
2. Inspect the hull for cracks or damage
3. Check hide for punctures or tears (even small holes allow water entry)
4. Tighten any loose joints

**Seasonally:**

- **Before water season:** Waterproof inspection and repair
- **After water season:** Deep cleaning, repairs, and protective coating
- **Before winter:** Drain any standing water; protect from freezing and thawing cycles

### Hide Maintenance

**Waterproofing hides:**

- Apply plant oil or animal fat annually to maintain water resistance
- Without maintenance, hides dry out and crack
- Historical Inuit maintained hides by regularly rubbing them with fat or oil

**Mold & mildew prevention:**

- Store in a dry location
- Never roll up a damp hide; always dry completely first
- In humid climates, periodic airing in sunlight helps prevent fungal growth

### Storage

**Best storage conditions:**

- Cool, dry location (basement, shed)
- Not in direct sunlight (UV damages hide and ages wood)
- Not in extreme temperature swings (freeze-thaw cycles degrade materials)
- Protected from rodents and insects
- Suspended on racks or straps (not resting on hard edges, which concentrate stress)

**Storage duration:** Well-maintained wooden boats last 10-20 years; hide boats last 5-10 years before requiring re-covering.

### Repair & Refurbishment

**Hull repair:**

- Small cracks are sealed with pitch-reinforced with cloth or fiber
- Larger holes require splinting or patching
- In emergencies, birch bark can be lashed over small holes as a temporary fix

**Hide repair:**

- Small holes are patched by sewing a new hide patch over the hole and sealing with pitch
- Large tears require extensive re-sewing
- After ~5 years, complete re-covering is more efficient than repeated patching
