## Overview: Doors and Windows in Building Systems

Doors and windows are high-performance building components. They must:
- Provide controlled access and egress
- Admit light and allow views
- Regulate thermal and moisture transfer
- Withstand wind loads and operational forces
- Resist weather degradation

![Window and door assembly reference diagram](../assets/svgs/windows-doors-assembly-1.svg)

In off-grid buildings, doors and windows are often handcrafted from local timber and materials, requiring solid joinery, proper hardware, and careful installation to function reliably for decades.

**Use this guide when the complaint is:**
- Drafts around a door or window opening
- A leaking threshold, sill, or rough opening
- A sagging, sticking, or poorly hung door
- Broken glazing, loose hardware, or a frame that does not fit cleanly
- Weatherstripping or air-sealing at moving openings

**Route elsewhere when the complaint is:**
- Roof, wall, or foundation waterproofing
- Boat seams, storage containers, or fabric needing a sealant choice
- Picking between wax, pitch, tar, oil, silicone, or epoxy for a material surface

For those cases, use [Waterproofing and Sealants](./waterproofing-sealants.md).

**This guide covers:**
- Door construction methods (plank, frame-and-panel, ledge-and-brace)
- Door hardware and hanging (hinges, pintles, latch mechanisms)
- Window types and operation (casement, double-hung, fixed)
- Glazing alternatives (glass, oiled paper, animal membranes, screens)
- Threshold, sill, and weatherstripping design
- Installation into timber and masonry walls

<section id="door-construction-types">

## Door Construction Types

### Ledge and Brace Door (Simplest)

Ledge-and-brace is the most basic door: vertical planks held together by horizontal ledges (crosspieces) and diagonal braces. No frame required.

**Construction steps:**

1. **Select boards:** Rough-sawn timber, 25–50mm thick, 100–150mm wide planks
2. **Arrange boards:** Stack vertically, edge-to-edge (flush or with slight rabbets for weathering)
3. **Install ledges:** Horizontal crosspieces (ledges) positioned top, middle, and bottom, mortised or bolted through boards
4. **Install braces:** Diagonal braces (typically 2–3 per door) mortised into ledges, running from top-hinge-side to bottom-latch-side (resists sagging)
5. **Hinge mounting:** Strap hinges (see section below) bolted through brace and board
6. **Latch:** Simple latch hook or thumb latch on outside face

**Advantages:** Quick, minimal skill, repair-friendly (replace individual boards). **Disadvantages:** Heavy, warps easily (all exposed faces must be sealed), poor insulation, acoustic privacy poor.

**Material estimate:** 2m × 1m door requires ~0.2 m³ timber at 25mm thickness.

### Frame-and-Panel Door

Frame-and-panel is a jointed frame with solid or semi-flexible panels infilled between frame members. More sophisticated and durable than ledge-and-brace.

**Components:**

1. **Frame:** Mortise-and-tenon jointed stiles (vertical) and rails (horizontal). Typical frame spacing:
   - Top rail to 2nd horizontal rail: 0.25m
   - 2nd rail to 3rd rail: 0.5m
   - 3rd rail to bottom rail: 0.3m

2. **Panels:** Solid wood boards (15–25mm thick) or smaller boards jointed together, fitted into grooves (rebates) cut into frame members. Panels float in grooves (not glued) to allow seasonal wood movement.

3. **Joinery:** Mortise-and-tenon (strongest) or bridle joints (faster).

**Assembly sequence:**

1. Mill stiles and rails to uniform cross-section (50mm × 80mm typical for 2m × 1m door)
2. Cut mortises in stile ends and rail ends (depth ~30mm, width ~12mm)
3. Cut tenons on rail ends (length ~30mm to fit mortises)
4. Cut grooves along inner edge of frame members to accept panels
5. Fit first panel into groove, then assemble frame around it
6. Fit remaining panels and complete frame
7. Clamp and check squareness (measure diagonals—should be equal)

**Advantages:** Lightweight, stable (wood movement absorbed by panels), attractive appearance, better insulation than ledge-and-brace. **Disadvantages:** Requires significant joinery skill, more material, longer assembly.

### Plank Door with Hardware Reinforcement

Midpoint between ledge-and-brace and frame-and-panel: plank door with bolted hardware straps, locks, and hinges to reinforce the joinery.

**Construction:**

1. Stack vertical planks, edge-to-edge, aligned top and bottom
2. Bolt strap hinges (see section below) on both faces, top and bottom
3. Bolt angle braces or flat bar stock diagonally across back face, extending from top-hinge to bottom-latch
4. Install substantial lock and latch mechanism (protects wood joinery from impact stress)

This hybrid approach provides strength without complex joinery.

<section id="hinges-and-hanging">

## Hinges and Hanging Hardware

### Strap Hinges (Traditional)

Strap hinges are long, flat metal hinges with extended leaves bolted through door face and frame. Most reliable for heavy wooden doors.

**Design features:**

| Feature | Purpose |
|---------|---------|
| Long leaves (0.3–0.5m) | Distributes load over larger area, prevents sagging |
| Pintles (vertical pins) | Allow door to swing; typically 12–20mm diameter |
| Bolt holes (3–4 per leaf) | Distribute fastener load, prevent tear-out |
| Material (wrought iron or steel) | Corrosion resistance, strength |

**Installation:**

1. Position hinge leaves on door and frame, centered on door edge thickness
2. Drill clearance holes through door and frame (typically 10–12mm holes for M8–M10 bolts)
3. Counter-bore bolt holes on frame side (so bolt heads don't protrude into wall)
4. Install bolts, washers, and nuts; tighten evenly (over-tightening can bend thin timber)

**Load calculations:** A 50kg door suspended on two strap hinges places ~25kg per hinge. Top hinge supports full door weight; bottom hinge resists tipping. For safety, use bolts rated for 50% more than calculated load (100% safety factor).

:::tip
**Pre-drill all fastener holes.** Wood splitting around bolt holes is the leading cause of hinge failure. Use clearance holes 1–2mm larger than bolt diameter.
:::

### Pintle and Eye Hinges

Pintles are vertical metal pins mounted on frame; eyes (large loops) are bolted to door top and bottom. Door hangs by eyes slipping over pintle pins.

**Advantages:** Simple installation, easy door removal (lift door off pintles). **Disadvantages:** Requires very accurate vertical alignment; poor load distribution compared to strap hinges.

**Pintle sizing:** 12–16mm diameter pins for residential doors; larger (20–25mm) for heavy doors.

### Butt Hinges (Modern Mortise Style)

Butt hinges are small, symmetric hinges mounted in mortised recesses (mortises cut into door and frame edge). Common in modern construction but require precise mortising.

**Mortise depth:** Typically 10–12mm (half the hinge leaf thickness).

**Sizing:** Standard residential hinge: 75–100mm length, rated for doors up to 50kg.

:::warning
Butt hinges are not recommended for heavy traditional doors (>50kg) in off-grid construction. Strap hinges distribute load better and tolerate rougher carpentry.
:::

<section id="window-types-and-operation">

## Window Types and Operation

### Casement Windows (Hinged)

Casement windows are single panes or frame-and-panel assemblies hinged on one vertical edge, swinging inward or outward. Hinges (usually butt hinges or strap hinges) are bolted to frame side.

**Design:**
- **Frame:** Mortise-and-tenon jointed stiles and rails, sized to accommodate glass or panels
- **Sash:** The moving part (hinged portion) fits into frame
- **Hinges:** Typically 2–3 per window, positioned at top, middle, and bottom of hinge side
- **Latch:** Simple hook or friction catch at opposite vertical edge

**Opening angle:** 90° swing typical for maximum ventilation.

**Weather sealing:** Caulk or felt weatherstrip along sash perimeter where it meets frame.

### Double-Hung Windows (Sliding Vertical)

Double-hung windows have two sashes (upper and lower) that slide vertically past each other in a frame. Both sashes open, allowing ventilation from both top and bottom simultaneously.

**Construction:**

1. **Frame:** Assembled stiles and rails mortised together
2. **Sashes:** Upper and lower sashes, each framed independently
3. **Guides:** Vertical grooves (or strips) on interior of frame stiles to guide sash movement
4. **Weights and pulleys (traditional):** Rope-and-weight system balances sash weight; sash cords pass over pulleys at top frame, connect to cast-iron weights inside frame cavities
5. **Sash stops:** Strips of wood or metal at top and bottom preventing sashes from falling out

**Modern alternative (without weights):** Friction sash locks or spring-loaded supports allow operation without counterweights.

**Glazing:** Each sash is glazed separately (typically with 4–6 panes of glass per sash for structural strength).

### Fixed Windows

Fixed windows are non-opening assemblies, simply framed and glazed. Typically used for views where ventilation is not needed, or paired with operable windows for additional light.

**Advantages:** Simplest construction, best weather seal, lowest draft. **Disadvantages:** No ventilation through window; requires other openings for air circulation.

<section id="glazing-methods">

## Glazing Methods and Alternatives

### Glass Glazing (Traditional)

**Process:**

1. **Cut glass:** Score and snap glass panes to fit rebates (grooves) in sash frame
2. **Bed glazing compound:** Press thick putty or glazing compound into rebate bottom
3. **Seat pane:** Press glass onto bedding compound, forcing out air bubbles
4. **Back putty:** Apply putty around pane perimeter, smooth at 45° angle
5. **Dry and paint:** Allow putty to set (7–14 days), then paint over joint to seal

**Glazing compound alternatives (if commercial putty unavailable):**
- Linseed oil + whiting (chalk powder): Simple putty substitute, slower to set
- Beeswax + rosin + pine tar: Medieval glazing compound, hardens well
- Lime mortar + linseed oil: Lower cost, slightly weaker but acceptable

**Pane sizing:** Larger panes (>1.5m × 1.0m) are prone to stress fractures from wind load. Subdivide large windows into smaller panes (0.5–0.8m on each side) with muntins (wooden glazing bars).

### Oiled Paper or Cloth Glazing

**Method:**

1. Cut strong paper (rice paper, linen cloth, or oilcloth) slightly larger than window opening
2. Apply adhesive around frame rebate (flour paste, hide glue, or pitch resin)
3. Press paper/cloth into adhesive, stretching slightly to eliminate wrinkles
4. Trim excess with sharp knife after adhesive sets
5. Optional: apply thin linseed oil or plant oil over paper to improve light transmission and weather resistance

**Properties:** Translucent (allows diffuse light but not clear views), durable (5–10 years with maintenance), cheap, easy to replace.

**Best for:** Storage areas, workshops, secondary light sources where views not needed.

### Animal Membrane Glazing

**Traditional intestinal casings (from cattle/pig slaughter):** Thin, translucent, very durable.

**Process:**

1. Obtain fresh intestines (soak in saltwater to preserve during processing)
2. Clean and remove mucous layer (scrape gently with dull knife)
3. Dry completely (hang in dry room 2–3 weeks)
4. Stretch over frame opening; secure edges with adhesive (pitch, rosin, or hide glue)
5. Smooth and allow to set

**Properties:** Nearly transparent, durable (10–15 years), water-resistant, stretches slightly (absorbs vibration).

**Disadvantage:** Requires access to slaughtered livestock; not practical for most modern situations.

### Screen Alternatives (Insect Protection)

If full glazing is not needed, screens allow ventilation while excluding insects:

1. **Woven fiber screen:** Jute, hemp, or flax rope woven into open mesh, stretched over frame
2. **Hair screen:** Fine animal hair (horsehair, cattle hair) woven into loose cloth
3. **Plant fiber:** Reed or bamboo slats woven with string, spaced 3–5mm apart

All require regular cleaning (dust clogs fibers, reducing airflow).

## Weatherstripping and Sealing

### Caulking Joints

**Traditional caulks:**
- **Linseed oil putty:** Linseed oil + chalk/whiting. Sets slowly (7–14 days), remains flexible. Re-workable before full cure.
- **Hide glue:** Protein-based adhesive. Fast-setting, rigid. Hygroscopic (absorbs/releases moisture).
- **Pitch or rosin:** Resinous materials heated and applied. Very durable but brittle when cold.
- **Lime mortar:** Lime + sand. Least effective at moving joints but affordable.

**Application technique:**
1. Clean joint of loose debris and old caulk
2. Prime if necessary (some materials require surface preparation)
3. Apply with caulking tool or putty knife, overfill slightly
4. Strike flush with frame surface
5. Allow to cure per material specifications before painting

### Weatherstripping Seals

Weatherstripping reduces infiltration at moving joints (door and window edges).

If the issue is not air leakage but water entering the surrounding wall, roof, or foundation assembly, use [Waterproofing and Sealants](./waterproofing-sealants.md) for the leak path and material choice.

**Materials and installation:**

| Material | Installation | Lifespan |
|----------|--------------|----------|
| Felt strips | Adhesive-backed or tacked to frame stop | 1–2 years |
| Rubber extrusion | Bolted or adhesive-backed | 3–5 years |
| Wool or animal hair | Hand-packed into groove, held by frame | 2–4 years |
| Cork-rubber strips | Adhesive or mechanical attachment | 3–5 years |
| Silicone sealant | Applied in continuous bead | 5–10 years |

**Installation:** Weatherstrip the perimeter of operable sashes/doors, focusing on moving edges (vertical edges of casements, top/bottom of double-hung sashes, door sides and bottom). Apply strips 2–3mm thick to compress slightly when sash closes.

### Door Threshold and Sill Construction

**Threshold:** Raised piece at doorway bottom, sheds water away from interior. Typically 50–100mm high, 100–150mm deep, sloped downward at 5–10° angle.

**Materials:** Timber (oak or hardwood resists wear), stone, or metal (zinc, aluminum).

**Installation:** Mortise threshold into door opening, allowing water to drain outward.

**Sill (window):** Horizontal member at bottom of window frame, sheds water away from wall below. Typically 30–50mm tall, sloped 5–15° downward on outer face.

**Installation:** Slope outward, extend 25–50mm beyond outer wall face to create drip edge (prevents water from running down wall).

## Frame Installation in Walls

### Installation in Timber-Framed Walls

**Timber-frame buildings:** Door/window frames sit between timber members (studs, posts). Frames are secured by:

1. **Wedge and shim method:** Insert tapered shims (wooden wedges) between frame and rough opening; adjust position; secure with nails driven through frame into studs
2. **Bolted connection:** For heavy frames or high wind areas, bolt frame to structural members via metal straps or brackets
3. **Gasket sealing:** Install compressible material (rope, wool, cork) between frame and rough opening to reduce drafts

**Shimming procedure:**

1. Position frame in rough opening
2. Insert shims at hinges and latch side of door frames (top, middle, bottom)
3. Adjust shims until frame is plumb (vertical) and level (horizontal)
4. Nail frame to studs through shims (nails hold shims in place)
5. Apply caulk or weatherstripping to exterior joint

If the real problem is wall flashing, roof runoff, or foundation seepage around the opening, switch to [Waterproofing and Sealants](./waterproofing-sealants.md) for the sealant and membrane decision.

### Installation in Masonry Walls

Masonry (stone, brick, or adobe) requires different anchoring.

**Methods:**

1. **Mortar bed:** Set frame in thick bed of mortar (50mm+), maintaining alignment while mortar cures. Simple but less adjustable.
2. **Mechanical anchors:** Install metal anchors or straps bolted through frame to masonry. Allows shimming and adjustment. Common approach.
3. **Embedded inserts:** Cast anchors into masonry during construction (planned frame locations).

**Flashing:** Install metal or felt flashing above frame to divert water outward; critical in rainy climates.

:::warning
Masonry installations are slower and less adjustable than timber-frame installations. Plan for longer lead times and more material prep.
:::

## Locks, Latches, and Hardware

### Latch Mechanisms

| Type | Operation | Use |
|------|-----------|-----|
| **Thumb latch** | Press thumb lever on outside; spring-loaded bolt retracts | Lightweight interior doors |
| **Norfolk latch** | Vertical lever locks bolt; mechanical connection | Exterior doors, barns |
| **Hook and eye** | Metal hook engaged over eye screw | Sheds, storage, temporary |
| **Friction catch** | Spring-loaded ball or magnetic mechanism | Lightweight cabinet doors |
| **Mortise lock** | Multi-mechanism lock with bolt and deadbolt | Main entry doors; requires mortising |

**Thumb latch installation (simplest):**

1. Cut recess in door face for latch bolt (bolt is simply a loose wooden peg in a leather strap loop)
2. Mount latch lever outside, connected via rod to bolt mechanism
3. Strike plate (bolted to frame) accepts bolt when door closes

### Heavy-Duty Bolts for Security

For sensitive areas (food storage, tool shed, workshop), use substantial padlocks or heavy bolts:

1. **Heavy hasp:** Bolted to door and frame, accepts padlock through loop
2. **Barrel bolt:** Long bolt slides through staple, held by rotating handle
3. **Chain and padlock:** Simple but effective, combines heavy chain (bolted to door/frame) with standard padlock

**Placement:** Position bolts near top and bottom of door to prevent forcing at center.

<section id="assembly-checklist">

## Door and Window Assembly Checklist

| Step | Details |
|------|---------|
| **Design phase** | Sketch door/window, calculate glass pane sizing (max ~1.5m × 1.0m before wind loads require subdivision) |
| **Material prep** | Mill timber to consistent thickness, cut all mortises and tenons |
| **Assembly** | Mortise-and-tenon joints glued and clamped; check squareness (measure diagonals) |
| **Hinge prep** | Drill clearance holes; install hinges on door face and frame |
| **Glazing** | Fit glass or alternative, apply weatherstripping and caulk |
| **Installation** | Position in rough opening, shim and wedge to plumb/level, secure to frame |
| **Final sealing** | Caulk all joints, paint, weatherstrip |
| **Testing** | Operate door/window smoothly; check weather seal (no drafts/light leaks) |
