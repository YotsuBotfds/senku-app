Combustion is a chemical reaction that releases heat and light. Understanding the chemistry of fire—what fuels burn, why they burn, and how to control burning—is essential for survival, heating, cooking, forging, and ceramic production. This guide covers the fire triangle, fuel chemistry, combustion hazards, and safe burning practices.

## The Fire Triangle: Three Requirements for Combustion

Fire requires three elements to exist and burn continuously. This is called the **fire triangle** or **combustion triangle**:

1. **Fuel** — A combustible substance (wood, coal, gasoline, etc.)
2. **Oxygen** — A gas that supports combustion (from air, ~21% of air)
3. **Heat** — Sufficient temperature to start and sustain the reaction

All three must be present simultaneously. Remove any one element, and the fire stops.

> **Deeper theory:** For flame temperatures, stoichiometric air-fuel ratios, draft pressure calculations, and combustion efficiency formulas, see [`combustion-science-flames.md`](combustion-science-flames.md). This guide covers the practical fire triangle, fuel types, and safety.

### Fuel

**Fuel** is any substance that can burn (oxidize rapidly). Common fuels include:

- **Solids:** Wood, coal, charcoal, paper, cloth, candle wax, plastic
- **Liquids:** Gasoline, kerosene, alcohol, oil, turpentine
- **Gases:** Hydrogen, methane (natural gas), propane, butane

Different fuels have different burning properties: some ignite easily (ignition point), some burn hot (energy content), some burn cleanly or with heavy smoke.

### Oxygen

**Oxygen (O₂)** is the oxidizer in normal combustion. It makes up about 21% of Earth's atmosphere. Combustion is fundamentally a rapid chemical reaction between a fuel and oxygen.

Oxygen can come from:
- Air (cheapest and most available)
- Compressed oxygen (for welding, oxy-acetylene torches)
- Oxidizers like potassium nitrate (used in explosives)

**Note:** Some substances contain their own oxidizer (like potassium permanganate). These can burn without atmospheric oxygen, but these are dangerous and unusual.

### Heat (Ignition Temperature)

**Heat** is energy that starts combustion. Every fuel has an **ignition temperature** (or **flash point**) — the minimum temperature at which it will catch fire:

| Fuel | Ignition Temperature |
|------|----------------------|
| Paper | ~450°C (840°F) |
| Wood | ~300°C (570°F) |
| Gasoline | -43°C (-45°F) — extremely flammable |
| Kerosene | ~38°C (100°F) |
| Diesel | ~52°C (125°F) |
| Charcoal | ~700°C (1290°F) |
| Coal | ~700°C (1290°F) |
| Methane gas | ~595°C (1100°F) |

**Flammable vs. Combustible:**
- **Flammable:** Ignites below 37.8°C (100°F) — very dangerous
- **Combustible:** Ignites above 37.8°C (100°F) — dangerous but less spontaneous

Fuels with low ignition temperatures ignite more easily and burn faster. Gasoline is extremely flammable (ignites well below room temperature). Wood is less flammable (requires substantial heat, like a match or lighter, to start).

### The Reaction Chain

Once burning starts, the heat from burning fuel heats surrounding fuel to ignition temperature, which creates more heat, which ignites more fuel. This **chain reaction** sustains the fire. That's why once a fire starts, it can sustain itself (if fuel and oxygen are available).

:::info-box
**Controlling fire means controlling the triangle:**
- Remove fuel: Starve the fire (separates fuel source)
- Remove oxygen: Smother the fire (blanket with non-flammable material)
- Remove heat: Cool the fire (water, cool sand, etc.)
:::

## Types of Combustion

### Complete Combustion

**Complete combustion** occurs when fuel fully reacts with abundant oxygen. The products are:

- **Carbon dioxide (CO₂)** — from the carbon in fuel
- **Water (H₂O)** — from the hydrogen in fuel
- **Heat** — released as thermal energy
- **Ash** — non-combustible minerals

**Example: Burning wood in a well-ventilated fireplace**

`2C (carbon) + 2O₂ → 2CO₂ + heat`

`2H₂ (hydrogen) + O₂ → 2H₂O + heat`

The flame is bright, clean, and hot. Complete combustion is what you want—it releases maximum energy and produces non-toxic products.

### Incomplete Combustion

**Incomplete combustion** occurs when fuel burns with limited oxygen. The products include:

- **Carbon monoxide (CO)** — toxic gas
- **Carbon dioxide (CO₂)** — less than in complete combustion
- **Water (H₂O)**
- **Smoke** — unburned carbon particles (soot)
- **Heat** — less than complete combustion

**Example: Burning wood in a poorly ventilated space, or burning charcoal indoors**

`2C (carbon) + O₂ → 2CO + heat` (incomplete, produces carbon monoxide)

The flame is dim, the smoke is heavy and dark, and dangerous **carbon monoxide** is produced.

:::warning
**Carbon Monoxide Poisoning:**

Carbon monoxide (CO) is a colorless, odorless, deadly gas. It binds to hemoglobin in blood, preventing oxygen transport to tissues.

- **Symptoms:** Headache, dizziness, nausea, confusion, loss of consciousness, death
- **Dangerous sources:** Burning charcoal indoors, poorly ventilated wood stoves, combustion heaters in enclosed spaces, car exhaust
- **Prevention:** Never burn fuel in enclosed, unventilated spaces. Always ensure adequate oxygen supply
- **If exposed:** Move to fresh air immediately. Severe exposure requires medical oxygen

For a full field protocol covering smoke inhalation, CO poisoning, airway burns, and delayed deterioration after fire-gas exposure, see [`smoke-inhalation-carbon-monoxide-fire-gas-exposure.md`](smoke-inhalation-carbon-monoxide-fire-gas-exposure.md).
:::

### Smoldering

**Smoldering** is very slow, low-temperature combustion without visible flame. The fuel glows red but doesn't produce bright flames.

- **Examples:** Burning cigarette coal, slow-burning charcoal
- **Temperature:** 300–600°C
- **Oxygen:** Limited
- **Products:** Heavy smoke, carbon monoxide, ash
- **Danger:** Can reignite if conditions change (fuel introduced, oxygen supply increases)

## Fuel Chemistry: Understanding Different Fuels

Different fuels have different chemical compositions, which affects how they burn. For safe storage, handling, and long-term inventory management of these fuels, see [`fuel-storage-handling.md`](fuel-storage-handling.md).

### Wood

**Composition:** Primarily cellulose (C₆H₁₀O₅) with lignin and other organic compounds.

**Burning:**
- Complete combustion: Produces CO₂, H₂O, heat, and ash (minerals)
- Produces visible flames (from hot carbon particles)
- Releases moderate energy (~15 MJ/kg)
- Burns best when dry (moisture slows combustion)

**Types of wood:**
- **Hardwoods** (oak, maple, hickory): Denser, higher energy content, burn hotter and longer
- **Softwoods** (pine, spruce): Less dense, lower energy content, ignite faster, produce more creosote and soot
- **Treated wood:** Never burn—releases toxic chemicals

**Best for:** Cooking fires, heating, charcoal production (hardwood preferred)

### Charcoal

**What it is:** Wood with water and volatile compounds removed. Charcoal is mostly pure carbon with minerals.

**Composition:** 85–90% carbon, 2–3% hydrogen, 2–3% oxygen, rest minerals

**Burning:**
- Burns hotter and cleaner than wood (fewer volatile compounds)
- More efficient energy release (~32 MJ/kg)
- Produces less smoke and creosote
- Complete combustion: Pure CO₂ (if adequate ventilation)

**Advantages:**
- Burns hotter
- Less smoke
- Easier to control temperature
- Better for metal forging

**Disadvantages:**
- Takes time to produce
- Incomplete combustion is deadly (carbon monoxide in enclosed spaces)
- More fragile than wood

**Best for:** Forges, heating, cooking (when ventilated), water filtration

### Coal

**Composition:** Ancient plant matter, compressed and fossilized. Complex hydrocarbons with minerals.

**Types:**
- **Bituminous coal:** Lower grade, more ash
- **Anthracite coal:** Higher grade, burns hotter, less ash
- **Lignite:** Lowest grade, more moisture

**Burning:**
- Very hot burn (higher energy than wood, ~24–32 MJ/kg)
- Produces heavy smoke and ash
- Contains sulfur (produces SO₂ toxic gas when burned)
- Requires good ventilation

**Advantages:**
- High energy content
- Long burn time
- Consistent temperature

**Disadvantages:**
- Produces toxic SO₂ gas
- Heavy ash production
- Requires substantial ventilation
- Sulfur content damages metal (unless using anthracite)

**Best for:** Large heating, forges (anthracite preferred), industrial processes

### Oils and Fats

**Composition:** Hydrocarbons and long-chain organic molecules.

**Examples:** Vegetable oil, animal fat (lard, tallow), mineral oil, kerosene, gasoline

**Burning:**
- High energy content (40+ MJ/kg for refined oils)
- Liquid or near-liquid state allows fine control
- Can burn clean (complete combustion) or smoky (incomplete)
- Requires heat to vaporize before burning (liquid itself doesn't burn; vapor does)

**Advantages:**
- High energy density
- Storable long-term (sealed containers)
- Precise temperature control possible
- Can create hot, concentrated flames

**Disadvantages:**
- Flammable and dangerous
- Require ventilation
- Smoke if burned inefficiently
- Expensive compared to wood/coal

**Best for:** Lamps, cooking (if refined), heating (if efficiently burned), engines

### Alcohol

**Composition:** Typically ethanol (C₂H₅OH) or methanol (CH₃OH).

**Burning:**
- Clean combustion: Produces CO₂ and H₂O
- Produces visible blue flame
- High energy content (~30 MJ/kg)
- Requires no oxygen from the air for some stages (carries some oxygen in molecule)

**Advantages:**
- Clean burn (no smoke or soot if complete)
- Blue flame is bright and controllable
- Renewable (from fermentation)

**Disadvantages:**
- Highly flammable
- Invisible flame (dangerous)
- Lower total energy than oils
- Evaporates quickly from open containers

**Best for:** Lamps (if non-denatured), cooking fires (where ventilated), portable stoves

## Combustion Byproducts and Hazards

### Carbon Dioxide (CO₂)

**Production:** Complete combustion of any fuel containing carbon produces CO₂.

**Properties:**
- Colorless, odorless gas
- Denser than air (sinks downward)
- Not toxic at normal concentrations
- Dissolves in water (slightly)

**At high concentrations (>10%):**
- Rapid breathing, dizziness
- Can displace oxygen in enclosed spaces

**Control:** Adequate ventilation removes CO₂ naturally.

### Carbon Monoxide (CO)

**Production:** Incomplete combustion (limited oxygen). Dangerous.

**Properties:**
- Colorless, odorless, extremely toxic
- Binds to hemoglobin with high affinity
- Prevents oxygen delivery to tissues
- Lethal at high concentrations

**Symptoms of exposure:**
- Low exposure: Headache, nausea after hours
- Medium exposure: Dizziness, confusion within minutes
- High exposure: Loss of consciousness and death within minutes

**Sources:**
- Charcoal burning in enclosed spaces
- Improperly ventilated stoves or heaters
- Car exhaust
- Incomplete fuel combustion (low oxygen)

:::warning
**Carbon Monoxide Safety:**
- Never burn charcoal, coal, or wood in enclosed, unventilated spaces
- Ensure stoves, heaters, and fireplaces have proper ventilation to outside
- Never block chimneys or vents
- If you feel dizzy while using heat source: Leave immediately, go to fresh air
- Severe exposure requires medical oxygen treatment
- Install CO detectors in heated living spaces

For medical recognition and treatment of CO exposure, see [`smoke-inhalation-carbon-monoxide-fire-gas-exposure.md`](smoke-inhalation-carbon-monoxide-fire-gas-exposure.md).
:::

### Smoke and Soot

**Composition:** Unburned carbon particles, ash, and compounds.

**Production:** Incomplete combustion, slow-burning fires, wet fuel burning.

**Effects:**
- Irritates eyes and lungs
- Carries other toxic compounds (tar, formaldehyde, etc.)
- Deposits on surfaces (creosote in chimneys)
- Reduces visibility

**Prevention:**
- Burn dry fuel
- Ensure adequate oxygen supply
- Use efficient stove design or fireplace
- Keep fires hot enough for complete combustion

### Sulfur Dioxide (SO₂)

**Production:** Burning coal or heavy oils containing sulfur.

**Properties:**
- Pungent, irritating gas
- Damages lungs
- Contributes to acid rain

**Sources:**
- Coal (especially bituminous and lignite)
- Heavy fuel oils
- Ore smelting

**Prevention:**
- Use anthracite coal if available (lower sulfur)
- Use clean fuels (oil, charcoal, hardwood)
- Ensure ventilation
- Avoid burning materials containing sulfur compounds

### Ash

**Composition:** Non-combustible minerals, silicates, and oxides left after fuel burns.

**Properties:**
- Usually sterile and stable
- Can be alkaline (useful for soil pH adjustment)
- Some ash is reusable (contains potassium for fertilizer)

**Dangers:**
- Ash that's still hot can reignite if fuel is present
- Very fine ash can irritate lungs if inhaled
- Ash can contain toxic elements if fuel was contaminated

**Handling:**
- Let ash cool completely before handling
- Dispose in water or sealed containers to prevent dust
- Use only uncontaminated ash for garden amendments

## Safe Combustion Practices

### Fire-Building Principles

**Adequate ventilation:**
- Never burn in completely enclosed spaces without exhaust
- Ensure at least one air inlet and one outlet
- Larger fires need more ventilation
- Chimneys and flues accelerate ventilation

**Fuel preparation:**
- Use dry fuel (wood should be seasoned, not fresh-cut)
- Split wood into uniform sizes for consistent burn
- Remove bark and moisture
- Use untreated wood (no paint, varnish, or chemicals)

**Fire arrangement:**
- Stack fuel loosely to allow airflow
- Don't smother fire with too much fuel at once
- Start with small, hot fire and gradually add larger fuel
- Arrange fuel to allow air circulation below

**Temperature control:**
- Monitor fire intensity
- Add fuel to increase heat
- Reduce airflow to decrease heat (close vents/dampers)
- Never leave burning fires unattended

### Carbon Monoxide Prevention

**Charcoal burning indoors:**
- Never use in closed rooms
- If necessary, ensure continuous fresh air supply
- Use only outside or in well-ventilated spaces
- Never use in tents, cars, or enclosed shelters

**Stove and heater safety:**
- Ensure stove is properly vented to outside
- Check vents and chimneys regularly (clear of blockages)
- Keep damper open (don't close it to save heat)
- Monitor for CO symptoms if heating with combustion

**Symptom recognition:**
- Headache with no other cause
- Nausea, dizziness, confusion
- Multiple people feeling sick simultaneously (indicator it's environmental)

### Fire Extinguishment Chemistry

The fire triangle guides extinguishment:

**Remove fuel:**
- Most effective long-term solution
- Clear combustible materials from around fire
- Starve the fire of additional fuel

**Remove oxygen (smother):**
- Use blanket, sand, dirt, or CO₂
- CO₂ displaces oxygen
- Blanket must cover completely and seal edges
- Sand/dirt smothers by blocking oxygen access
- Effective for liquid fires (oil, gasoline)

**Remove heat (cool):**
- Water is most effective (high heat capacity)
- Water cools fuel below ignition temperature
- Works well for wood fires
- Never use water on oil/grease fires (splatters)
- Never use water on chemical fires (may react)

**Type of extinguisher:**
- **Class A:** Water-based, for ordinary combustibles (wood, paper)
- **Class B:** Foam or CO₂, for flammable liquids (oil, gasoline)
- **Class C:** CO₂ or dry powder, for electrical fires
- **Class D:** Special dry powder, for metal fires
- **Class K:** Chemical foam, for cooking oil fires

:::warning
**Fire Safety Rules:**
- Always have water nearby when burning fuel
- Never leave fire unattended
- Extinguish completely before leaving (drown with water, stir ashes, drown again)
- Keep flammable materials away from fire (at least 10 feet)
- Never burn in wind without protection from sparks
- Wear appropriate clothing (not loose fabric that catches fire)
- Keep burn area clear of obstacles for emergency exit
:::

## Practical Applications

### Building a Forge Fire

Forge fires require:
- Intense, focused heat (900°C+ for most metalwork)
- Clean fuel (charcoal or coke, not coal with sulfur)
- Controlled airflow

**Setup:**
1. Create fire bed in forge (clay, sand, or iron)
2. Build small fire with charcoal
3. Supply air via bellows or blower beneath fuel
4. Heat metal in hottest part of fire
5. Adjust airflow to control temperature

**Fuel choice:** Charcoal is superior to coal for forging (cleaner, less sulfur damage to metal).

### Efficient Heating Stove

Efficient wood stoves extract maximum heat from fuel:
- Thick walls (mass to hold heat)
- Long, tortuous flue path (fuel transfers heat to walls before exiting)
- Damper control (controls combustion rate)
- Grate that allows ash removal and air circulation

**Result:** More heat output per unit of wood, less wood consumption.

### Pottery and Ceramic Firing

Kilns require sustained, controlled heat (800°C+):
- Fuel: Hardwood, charcoal, or coal (depending on kiln design)
- Ventilation: Critical (removes smoke, water vapor, CO)
- Temperature control: Via fuel rate and vent dampers
- Oxidation vs. reduction: Kiln atmosphere (high oxygen vs. low oxygen) affects glaze colors

### Charcoal Production

Charcoal is made by heating wood without oxygen (pyrolysis, not combustion):
- Build enclosed fire with controlled oxygen
- Heat drives off water and volatile compounds
- Remaining pure carbon is charcoal
- Requires skill to balance heat and oxygen

## Summary

Combustion is a chemical reaction between fuel and oxygen that releases heat and light. The fire triangle shows the three requirements: fuel, oxygen, and heat. Remove any one, and combustion stops.

Different fuels have different properties: wood is renewable but lower energy, charcoal burns hotter and cleaner, coal has high energy but produces toxic gases, and oils have high energy density but require careful handling.

Complete combustion produces CO₂ and H₂O (safe). Incomplete combustion produces carbon monoxide, a deadly gas. Adequate ventilation is essential. Never burn fuel in enclosed, unventilated spaces.

Understanding combustion chemistry lets you build better fires, forge metal safely, heat efficiently, and avoid dangerous carbon monoxide exposure. Respect fire as a powerful chemical reaction that requires knowledge and care to use safely.
