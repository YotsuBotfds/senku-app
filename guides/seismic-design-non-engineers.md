## Introduction

Earthquakes shake buildings in directions they were not designed to resist. In post-collapse scenarios, you may need to build or retrofit structures in seismically active regions without access to engineering software or seismic design codes. This guide provides practical, field-tested rules of thumb for earthquake-resistant construction.

:::warning
Earthquakes can destroy poorly built structures in seconds. These guidelines are a minimum baseline. When in doubt, build more robust than appears necessary. Overbuilding costs less than rebuilding.
:::

## Start Here

If you only remember a few things, make them these:

1. **Pick a better site first.** Choose firm, level ground if you can. Avoid steep slopes, loose fill, wet ground, and places where the soil is soft or likely to slide.
2. **Tie the building down.** Anchor the frame to the foundation, then add diagonal bracing and roof-to-wall ties so the building moves as one unit.
3. **Stop early when damage looks structural.** Call it serious if you see cracks wider than 1/4 inch in load-bearing walls, corners pulling apart, a leaning wall, or any foundation shift.
4. **Do not occupy it if damage is large or through the wall.** Cracks over 1/2 inch, separated corners, or cracks that go all the way through the wall mean the building needs inspection before use.

## How Earthquakes Damage Buildings

### Horizontal vs. Vertical Motion

**Vertical motion (up and down):** Least damaging; most structures handle vertical loads well.

**Horizontal motion (side to side):** Most damaging. Most buildings are designed for vertical loads (gravity) but not lateral loads (earthquake shaking side to side).

**Why horizontal motion is dangerous:**

1. Walls designed to support vertical load may crack and collapse when pushed sideways
2. Heavy roofs and upper floors act like pendulums, swinging and pulling walls outward
3. Connections between members may fail under shear forces they were never tested for
4. Unreinforced masonry (brick, adobe) is particularly vulnerable to horizontal shaking

### Soft-Story Failure

Buildings with large open spaces on lower floors (garages, shops) and solid walls above often collapse. The open floor acts like a hinge, allowing the building to fold.

**Example:** A building with a reinforced concrete garage on the first floor and residential apartments above may collapse as the garage floor provides no lateral support.

## Bracing Fundamentals

Bracing provides lateral resistance by creating triangles, which are inherently stiff and cannot deform without changing their geometry.

### Wall Bracing Patterns

**Diagonal bracing (most effective for earthquake):**

Two diagonal members crossing in an X create a triangular truss that resists lateral loads.

- One diagonal goes into compression (gets pushed together)
- One diagonal goes into tension (gets pulled apart)
- Both resist the sideways force

**Spacing:** Diagonal braces should be spaced no more than 16 feet apart horizontally. Longer spans require additional braces or stronger members.

**Connection:** Braces must be securely bolted (not just nailed) to the frame at both ends. Nail-only connections fail quickly in earthquakes.

### Foundation Anchorage

The most critical bracing point is at the foundation. A building can have perfect bracing, but if it's not tied to the foundation, the entire building can shift sideways and fall off its foundation.

**Anchor bolts:**

Install anchor bolts connecting the building frame to the concrete or stone foundation.

- **Spacing:** Maximum 6 feet apart around the building perimeter
- **Size:** Minimum 1/2 inch diameter for residential buildings; 5/8 inch for larger structures
- **Embedment:** Minimum 12 inches into concrete or 18 inches into stone
- **Washers:** Large washers (minimum 2-inch square) spread load and prevent bolts from pulling through wood

**Field installation:**

1. Drill holes through the sill plate (wooden base of structure)
2. Pre-drill the foundation for anchor bolts
3. Install anchor bolts with large washers and nuts
4. Tighten snugly (not excessively; can over-tighten wood)
5. Check tightness after first winter and after any settling

:::tip
Walk around the foundation perimeter and inspect every anchor bolt. Missing or loose bolts are the first failure point in earthquakes.
:::

## Shear Wall Design

Shear walls are the primary lateral load-resisting system in most buildings. They are walls reinforced to resist sideways forces.

### Identifying Good Shear Walls

A good shear wall:

1. **Extends from foundation to roof** (no breaks; continuous)
2. **Is braced on both sides** (with diagonal members or reinforcement)
3. **Has openings no larger than 4 feet wide** (doors, windows break the wall pattern)
4. **Is symmetrically placed** (walls on opposite sides create balanced load paths)
5. **Is well-connected** (bolted or welded to floors and roof)

### Shear Wall Spacing

Shear walls must be close enough to each other to brace the entire building.

**Rule of thumb:**

Maximum unsupported distance between shear walls = half the height of the building.

**Example:**
- Single-story building (10 feet tall): Shear walls should be no more than 5 feet apart
- Two-story building (20 feet tall): Shear walls should be no more than 10 feet apart
- Three-story building (30 feet tall): Shear walls should be no more than 15 feet apart

If buildings exceed this spacing, install additional bracing walls or increase diagonal member size.

### Construction Shear Wall Strengthening

For existing unreinforced walls:

**Method 1: Diagonal bracing with bolts**

1. Install 2-inch × 4-inch diagonal members from corner to corner
2. Bolt (not nail) to the frame at both ends
3. Use 1/2-inch bolts minimum, spaced 2 feet apart along each member
4. Install on both sides of wall if possible; if only one side, install on the side most visible (exterior)

**Method 2: Plywood sheathing**

1. Add 1/2-inch exterior-grade plywood to one or both sides of the wall
2. Nail plywood with 16d nails spaced 4 inches apart around perimeter, 8 inches in field
3. Nail all four edges completely
4. This creates a solid diaphragm that acts as a shear wall

**Method 3: Reinforced concrete overlay**

For critical walls in masonry or adobe buildings:

1. Apply a 2–3 inch reinforced concrete layer over existing wall
2. Install 1/2-inch steel reinforcement (#4 rebar) in both directions, 12 inches on center
3. Anchor reinforcement into existing wall with concrete anchors or by roughening the surface
4. This essentially creates a new reinforced concrete wall over the old wall

## Ring Beams (Bond Beams)

A ring beam is a reinforced concrete or steel beam running continuously around the perimeter of a building at the top of walls.

**Function:** Ties the entire structure together at the roof level, preventing walls from separating.

**For new construction:**

1. Build walls up to their full height
2. Place a reinforced concrete beam on top of the walls
3. Reinforce with #4 rebar (1/2-inch) in two continuous lines, one near top, one near bottom of beam
4. Connect vertical rebar from the walls into the ring beam
5. Pour concrete and let cure

**For retrofit (existing buildings):**

1. Install a continuous steel angle or pipe at the top of walls
2. Bolt the steel member to the walls (bolts through the wall, anchored on interior side)
3. Bolt spacing: maximum 4 feet apart
4. Steel member ties the entire perimeter together, restraining outward movement

**Roof connection to ring beam:**

The roof must be securely tied to the ring beam or wall.

- Install bolted connections between roof joists/trusses and the wall (minimum 1/2-inch bolts, maximum 4 feet apart)
- For rigid roofs (steel deck, reinforced concrete), embed anchors during construction
- For wood framed roofs, use hurricane ties or straps connecting roof members to walls

## Roof-to-Wall Connections

Roofs can literally lift off walls during earthquakes (especially when wind and seismic forces combine). Secure connections are critical.

### Wood Frame Roofs

**Connection method:**

1. Install metal roof clips or hurricane ties connecting each rafter or truss to the top plate of the wall
2. Clips are nailed to the rafter and bolted to the wall
3. Spacing: Maximum 4 feet apart; closer in high-wind/seismic areas (2 feet)

**For existing buildings without clips:**

1. Install through-bolts connecting roof members to the wall
2. Drill holes through the rafter/truss and through the wall top plate
3. Install 1/2-inch bolts with large washers (minimum 2-inch square)
4. Tighten snugly but not so tight as to crush the wood

### Reinforced Concrete Roofs

**Connection method:**

1. During construction, extend rebar from the walls into the roof slab
2. Roof slab is poured integrally with the walls
3. This creates a monolithic structure where roof and walls are one unit

**For retrofit:**

1. Install bolted connections around the perimeter (6-foot maximum spacing)
2. Use concrete anchors (epoxy-set, mechanical anchors) if the concrete is already cured

### Metal Roofs (Steel Deck)

**Connection method:**

1. Bolt the steel deck to the wall perimeter with bolted clips
2. Install bolts at each flange connection, maximum 2-foot spacing
3. Use large washers and lock washers to prevent loosening

## Lintel Reinforcement (Over Doors and Windows)

Lintels (horizontal beams over openings) must resist tension and shear during earthquakes.

### Masonry and Concrete Block Buildings

**Original design:** Lintels are often unreinforced wood or thin concrete.

**Retrofit:**

1. Install reinforced concrete lintels above all openings wider than 3 feet
2. Lintel width should be at least 12 inches longer than the opening on each side (6 inches minimum)
3. Lintel depth: 12 inches minimum for openings up to 8 feet wide
4. Reinforce with #4 rebar (1/2-inch) in the lower half (where tension occurs), minimum 2 bars
5. Reinforce with #3 rebar as stirrups (shear reinforcement) every 12 inches

**Installation:**

1. Remove existing lintel if it is cracked or weak
2. Build concrete forms underneath the opening
3. Install reinforcement and pour concrete
4. Let cure for 7 days before removing forms

### Wood Frame Buildings

**Original design:** Wood beams or box beams over openings.

**Retrofit:**

1. For large openings (over 8 feet), install steel lintels instead of wood
2. Steel lintels are much stiffer and less likely to crack
3. Bolt steel lintels to the walls on both sides (minimum 1/2-inch bolts, maximum 2-foot spacing)

## Soft-Story Prevention

Soft-story buildings (large open spaces below, solid structure above) are vulnerable to collapse.

### Identifying Soft Stories

- First story is a garage, shop, or open commercial space
- Upper stories are residential or office with solid walls
- First story has few or no interior walls for bracing

### Retrofit Solutions

**Option 1: Add shear walls**

1. Install reinforced concrete walls on the first floor to match the brace layout of upper floors
2. This requires relocating utilities, possibly reducing usable space
3. Most effective solution; creates strong vertical load path

**Option 2: Moment-resisting frame**

1. Install steel moment-resisting frames (columns and beams bolted together to resist bending)
2. Frames allow open spaces while providing lateral resistance
3. More expensive but preserves open space

**Option 3: Diagonal bracing**

1. Install diagonal X-bracing in the open story
2. Braces go from floor to roof in the corner bays
3. Less elegant but effective and least expensive

## Adobe and Unreinforced Masonry Strengthening

Adobe and unreinforced brick are particularly vulnerable to earthquakes.

### Assessment

Walk around the building and look for:

- Cracks wider than 1/4 inch (structural problem)
- Cracks that go all the way through the wall (very serious)
- Separated corners or walls pulling away from each other
- Leaning or out-of-plumb walls
- Deteriorated mortar joints (mortar crumbling when scraped)

### Strengthening Method 1: Vertical and Horizontal Reinforcement

1. Drill vertical holes through the walls (every 3 feet)
2. Install vertical rebar (#4, 1/2-inch diameter) in the holes
3. Pack reinforcement with hydraulic cement or epoxy
4. Drill horizontal holes and install horizontal rebar perpendicular to vertical bars (creates a grid)
5. This converts the wall into a reinforced masonry structure

**Practical challenge:** This is labor-intensive and requires drilling through thick adobe or masonry walls.

### Strengthening Method 2: External Reinforced Concrete Shell

1. Install a 3-inch reinforced concrete layer on the exterior of the building (or interior, if exterior is historic)
2. Reinforce with #4 rebar in both directions, 12 inches on center
3. Anchor concrete to existing wall with mechanical anchors or by roughening surface
4. This effectively creates a new reinforced concrete building over the old one

**Advantage:** Addresses all walls simultaneously and provides very strong reinforcement.

**Disadvantage:** Expensive and changes the appearance of the building.

### Strengthening Method 3: Ring Beam Only (Minimum Solution)

If budget is limited:

1. Install a reinforced concrete ring beam at the roof level (as described earlier)
2. Tie roof to ring beam
3. This prevents the walls from separating at the top but does not strengthen the walls themselves
4. **Limitation:** If walls are very weak, this is not sufficient; use with other reinforcement

## Connection Details

The details of how members connect are critical in earthquakes. Small, overlooked details can cause failure.

### Bolted Connections

**Rule:** All main connections should be bolted, not nailed.

- Nails pull out easily in earthquakes
- Bolts provide positive connection that resists both tension and shear
- Bolt spacing: Maximum 4 feet apart (2 feet for critical connections)

**Torque:**

- Hand-tighten bolts to snug-tight
- For critical connections, use a torque wrench
- Don't over-tighten wood bolts; can crush the wood

### Welded Connections

If welding is available:

- Welded connections are superior to bolted in earthquakes (no slip or loosening)
- Have welds inspected visually (no cracks, good penetration)
- Provide backup bolts if welds are the only connection

### Column to Foundation Connection

**Critical:** Column base must be securely anchored to the foundation.

1. Install anchor bolts (1/2-inch minimum diameter)
2. Spacing: Maximum 6 feet apart
3. Washers: Minimum 2-inch square (larger for wood base plates)
4. Install on all four sides of the column, or on all sides if round

## Post-Earthquake Assessment

After an earthquake, visual inspection identifies whether the building is safe to occupy.

### Immediate Safety Check

**Do not enter if:**

- Major cracks in structural members (walls, columns, beams)
- Separated corners or walls pulling away from each other
- Visible tilting or out-of-plumb walls
- Collapsed sections of the building
- Chimney partially collapsed and blocking exit routes
- Strong smell of gas (indicates ruptured gas lines; evacuate immediately)

**Safe to enter if:**

- Hairline cracks (under 1/8 inch) in non-structural walls only
- Minor cracks in concrete or masonry (under 1/4 inch) that do not go through the wall
- No visible movement or separation of major members

### Detailed Inspection Checklist

Walk the entire building systematically, checking:

**Exterior:**
- [ ] Major cracks in exterior walls
- [ ] Displaced foundation or cracks in foundation
- [ ] Separated corners
- [ ] Cracked or collapsed chimneys
- [ ] Roof damage or missing sections

**Interior:**
- [ ] Major cracks in interior walls
- [ ] Cracked connections between walls and roof
- [ ] Shifted floors or visible gaps between floor and walls
- [ ] Cracked columns or beams
- [ ] Damaged utility connections (gas, water, electrical)

**Roof:**
- [ ] Displaced or collapsed roof members
- [ ] Cracked roof deck (if concrete)
- [ ] Loose or missing roof coverings

### Crack Assessment

| Crack Type | Severity | Action |
|-----------|----------|--------|
| Hairline (< 1/16") in drywall or plaster | Cosmetic | Monitor; repair after building is checked by engineer |
| 1/8–1/4" in non-structural wall | Minor | Monitor; may indicate minor structural damage |
| 1/4–1/2" in structural wall | Serious | Limit occupancy; evaluate structure before full use |
| Over 1/2" or through wall completely | Critical | Do not occupy; evacuate and consult engineer |
| Stepped cracks in masonry (following mortar joints) | Very serious | Wall may be near failure; evacuate |

### Documentation

Photograph all damage:

1. Wide shots showing overall building condition
2. Close-ups of cracks (include ruler or coin for scale)
3. Photos of connections and joints showing separation or displacement
4. Document cracks on both sides of walls if possible

Keep photos and observations for later engineering review or insurance claims.

## Building Rules of Thumb for Earthquake Resistance

Apply these simple rules to any structure:

1. **Make it simple:** Complex, irregular shapes concentrate stresses. Simple rectangular buildings with even wall distribution are more resistant.

2. **Distribute walls evenly:** Shear walls should be roughly equally spaced on all sides of the building, not concentrated on one side.

3. **Make it symmetrical:** Buildings that are not symmetric sway and twist; symmetric buildings sway as a unit.

4. **Connect everything:** Every connection should be bolted or welded; nails are not adequate.

5. **Avoid long spans:** Long beams and open spaces increase sway. Keep spans under 30 feet; shorter is better.

6. **Reinforce corners:** Corners experience the highest stresses. Over-reinforce corner connections.

7. **Avoid heavy roofs:** Heavy roofs increase inertial forces. Use light roof materials when possible.

8. **Create redundancy:** Never depend on a single connection for safety. Provide multiple load paths.

9. **Minimize cantilevers:** Roof overhangs and balconies increase moment loads. Keep them small or very well-braced.

10. **Test everything:** Before considering a building complete, apply sideways force (by hand, if necessary) and observe—does it flex, or does it crack? Flex is better than cracking.

## Conclusion

Earthquake-resistant design does not require advanced engineering; it requires attention to load paths, connections, and redundancy. The most important steps are:

1. Anchor the building to the foundation
2. Connect all major components (roof, walls, floors)
3. Provide multiple bracing walls
4. Make connections robust (bolted or welded, not nailed)
5. Inspect after earthquakes for hidden damage

In post-collapse scenarios, buildings that survive earthquakes are those that are simple, over-designed, and built with craftsmanship. Save the fancy architecture for after stability is assured.
