## Why Seed Banking: Genetic Diversity as Survival Insurance

Seed banking is not nostalgia—it is active insurance against agricultural collapse. Modern food systems depend on industrial supply chains that are fragile, expensive, and controlled by a shrinking number of corporations. A community that cannot produce its own seeds is permanently vulnerable.

### The Crisis of Genetic Uniformity

Industrial agriculture has narrowed the genetic diversity of crops to dangerous extremes. Of the 7,000 crop species humans have cultivated, only 30 now provide 95% of global calories. Three crops—rice, wheat, and corn—account for 60% of human calorie intake. When your food depends on genetic monoculture, one pathogen or climate anomaly can devastate continental harvests.

The U.S. potato famine of the 1840s killed 1 million people because the population depended entirely on one variety: the Irish Lumper. Genetic diversity in the seed bank prevents this scenario from repeating.

### Commercial Seed Supply Will Collapse

Commercial seed companies are consolidating at an accelerating rate. Four corporations—Corteva, Bayer, BASF, and ChemChina—control 65% of the global seed market. Supply chains are strained, prices are climbing, and availability is shrinking. If that supply chain fails—whether through economic collapse, war, pandemic, or natural disaster—communities without seed reserves cannot plant.

Seed companies also frequently discontinue heirloom varieties because they are not profitable. If you wait for a crop you want to be back in stock, it may never be. Once a variety disappears from commercial seed lists and from all saved seeds, it is extinct forever.

### Heirloom & Open-Pollinated Varieties Breed True

Heirloom varieties (typically 50+ years old) and open-pollinated (OP) seeds are genetically stable. Save seed from a 'Brandywine' tomato and the offspring are 'Brandywine'. Save seed from an 'Anasazi' bean and the offspring are 'Anasazi'. This breeding-true property means that a farmer or gardener can maintain a variety indefinitely with zero external inputs—once you have the seed, you are independent.

This is revolutionary in a fragile world. It means:

-   No dependence on commercial suppliers
-   Seeds adapted to your local climate over generations
-   Free reproduction of productive varieties
-   Genetic diversity within a variety (unlike hybrids)

### One Season Without Saved Seed = Permanent Loss

Seed loss happens silently. A gardener skips saving beans one year because they were tired, or seeds were contaminated, or the season was bad. The next year, they buy seed from the company. But if the company has discontinued that variety, or the supply has broken down, or prices have tripled, it is too late. That genetic line is gone forever—not just from that farm, but potentially from human agriculture.

The only insurance against this loss is redundancy: seeds stored in multiple locations, tested for viability, and refreshed every few years. A personal seed vault combined with a community seed bank creates resilience.

### The Svalbard Vault Concept at Community Scale

The Global Seed Vault in Svalbard, Norway, preserves 1 million seed samples in a frozen mountain bunker. It is backup for the entire species. But a community cannot rely on a distant vault in the Arctic. What if that vault fails? What if shipping breaks down? What if the seeds you need are not there?

Every community should have its own seed vault:

-   **Personal vault:** Stored at home, contains varieties adapted to your land and preferences
-   **Community seed library:** Shared collection that members check out, grow, return with interest (seeds from the harvest)
-   **Regional backup:** Exchange with other communities to distribute risk geographically

This creates redundancy at three scales: personal, local, and regional. If one location fails, the genetic wealth is preserved elsewhere.

**Core Principle:** Every household that grows food should save seed from at least one crop. Every community should maintain a shared seed library. Every region should have backup vaults at separate locations. This is the only reliable insurance against agricultural collapse.

## Open-Pollinated vs Hybrid vs GMO: Understanding Your Seeds

Not all seeds are equal. The type of seed you plant determines whether you can save seed from the harvest—and whether you maintain agricultural independence.

### Open-Pollinated (OP) Seeds: The Foundation of Food Security

:::card
#### Definition

Open-pollinated seeds are fertilized by natural pollination: wind, insects, or self-pollination. The genetics are stable. Save seed from an OP plant and the offspring are essentially identical to the parent (with minor natural variation).
:::

-   **Breeds true:** Offspring match parent plant
-   **Stable genetics:** Can save seed indefinitely
-   **Genetic diversity:** Natural variation within the variety builds resilience
-   **Adapted varieties:** Over generations, your saved seeds adapt to local climate and soil
-   **Cost:** Higher initial seed cost, but zero cost after year one
-   **Best for seed saving:** ALWAYS choose OP varieties

Examples: 'Brandywine' tomato, 'Anasazi' bean, 'Scarlet Nantes' carrot, 'Walla Walla' onion, 'Butternut' squash

### F1 Hybrid Seeds: The Planned Obsolescence Trap

:::card
#### Definition

F1 Hybrids are deliberate crosses between two inbred parent lines, created in a laboratory. The first generation (F1) displays "hybrid vigor"—they grow fast, produce heavily, and are uniform. But they do not breed true.
:::

-   **Does not breed true:** Seed from an F1 hybrid produces a chaotic mix of plants, not the hybrid you planted
-   **Genetic regression:** F2 plants are often weaker, less productive, and unpredictable
-   **Requires new seed:** You must buy seed from the company every year
-   **Planned obsolescence:** This is intentional—the company profits from selling seed annually
-   **Short-term vigor:** Looks good in year one, but cannot be perpetuated
-   **Worst for seed saving:** NEVER use for genetic preservation

Examples: Most of the vegetables sold in big-box stores, commercial tomatoes, most squash varieties in seed catalogs. Many seed packets are labeled "Hybrid" or "F1"—read carefully.

:::warning
**WARNING:** If you save seed from an F1 hybrid expecting to grow the same plant next year, you will be shocked by the results. F2 plants will be weak, variable, and often unusable. Never rely on hybrids for food security.
:::

### Genetically Modified (GMO) Seeds: Avoid Completely

:::card
#### Definition

GMO seeds have had genes inserted from other species (bacteria, fish, animals) using laboratory techniques. The goal is usually herbicide resistance or insect resistance. GMOs are almost always patented.
:::

-   **Often sterile:** Many GMOs are engineered to produce sterile seeds (Terminator technology)
-   **Patented:** You do not own the seed. Saving or replanting is legally prohibited
-   **Corporate control:** Forces annual purchase and dependency
-   **Ecological risk:** Transgenes can escape into wild relatives, contaminating the genetic pool
-   **Least resilient:** NEVER use for seed saving. ALWAYS avoid

Major GMO crops: Corn, soybeans, cotton, canola, sugar beets. These are primarily in commercial agriculture, but home gardeners should avoid any seed labeled as GMO or transgenic.

:::warning
**CRITICAL:** For food security and independence, use ONLY open-pollinated and heirloom varieties. Hybrids and GMOs are dead-ends—you cannot propagate them. They are useful for commercial farmers who can buy seed annually, but they are unsuitable for a resilient food system.
:::

## Seed Saving Basics by Crop Family

Different crop families have different reproductive strategies. Understanding your crop's family determines how you will collect, isolate, and save its seed.

### Solanaceae (Nightshade Family): Tomato, Pepper, Eggplant

#### Reproduction & Isolation

-   **Pollination:** Self-pollinating (flowers pollinate themselves)
-   **Cross-contamination risk:** Low; can grow multiple varieties in same garden
-   **Isolation distance:** None required (they self-pollinate)
-   **Difficulty:** Easy

#### Seed Saving Process: Tomato

1.  Let fruit ripen fully on plant (even past eating ripeness)
2.  Cut fruit in half and scoop seeds and gel into a container
3.  **Fermentation (critical):** Cover loosely with a cloth. Leave at room temperature for 2-3 days. The gel coat will decompose. You will smell fermentation (slightly unpleasant smell is normal—that is the beneficial fermentation)
4.  Rinse thoroughly in water until all gel is removed
5.  Spread on plate or screen to dry
6.  Dry for 2+ weeks until completely hard
7.  Store in cool, dry place

**Why fermentation?** The gel coat contains germination inhibitors that prevent seeds from sprouting inside the fruit. Fermentation removes this inhibitor and kills seed-borne diseases. This single step is critical for seed viability.

#### Pepper & Eggplant

Same process as tomato: allow full ripeness, scoop seeds, ferment 2-3 days, rinse, dry. Peppers are easier than tomatoes (less gel), eggplants are similar to tomatoes.

#### Seed Yield & Variety

-   One tomato produces 100-300 seeds
-   One pepper produces 50-150 seeds
-   One eggplant produces 100-400 seeds

### Cucurbitaceae (Gourd Family): Squash, Melon, Cucumber

#### Reproduction & Isolation

-   **Pollination:** Cross-pollinated by insects (bees)
-   **Cross-contamination risk:** Very high; different varieties will cross and create unusable hybrids
-   **Isolation distance:** 1/2 mile minimum; 1 mile is safer
-   **Alternative:** Hand-pollination (removes need for isolation)
-   **Difficulty:** Moderate to hard

#### Critical Issue: Cross-Pollination

Different squash varieties cross very easily. If you grow 'Butternut' and 'Delicata' squash in the same season, pollen from one will pollinate the other. The fruit looks normal (it is the mother plant's genetics), but the seeds inside are hybrids. Next year, you will grow a useless squash that is neither parent.

Solutions:

1.  **Isolation distance:** Grow only one variety per season, or locate varieties 1/2 mile apart
2.  **Caging:** Build a mesh cage around flowering plants and manually pollinate with a small brush
3.  **Community arrangement:** Coordinate with neighbors—person A grows squash this year, person B grows different squash next year

#### Seed Saving Process: Squash (Butternut, Delicata, etc.)

1.  Allow fruit to fully mature on plant (longer than you would eat it)
2.  For winter squash, leave on vine until stem dies and skin hardens
3.  Cut fruit and scoop out seeds with pulp
4.  Rinse seeds thoroughly to remove all flesh (water flotation helps)
5.  Spread on screen to dry 2+ weeks
6.  Store in cool, dry place

#### Melon & Cucumber

Same process: cross-pollinated, require isolation or hand-pollination, seeds rinse clean, dry completely. Melons are easier (larger seeds), cucumbers are smaller and drying is finicky (they can develop mold).

#### Seed Yield

-   One squash produces 100-300 seeds
-   One melon produces 200-400 seeds
-   One cucumber produces 100-200 seeds

### Brassicaceae (Cabbage Family): Cabbage, Broccoli, Kale, Cauliflower

#### Reproduction & Isolation

-   **Pollination:** Cross-pollinated by insects
-   **Cross-contamination risk:** High; different brassicas can cross (cabbage × broccoli hybrids are possible)
-   **Isolation distance:** 1 mile minimum between different brassica varieties
-   **Growth pattern:** Biennial (takes two years)
-   **Difficulty:** Hard (requires space and patience)

#### The Biennial Challenge

Cabbage, broccoli, and kale do not produce seed in their first year. In year one, they store energy in the head (cabbage), florets (broccoli), or leaves (kale). To produce seed, the plant must survive winter and be planted again in spring (year two). This requires:

-   Space to overwinter the parent plants
-   Climate that does not kill them (mild winter or cold storage with protective covering)
-   Ability to protect plants from pests
-   Patience for 18+ months from seed to seed

#### Seed Saving Process: Cabbage

1.  Year 1: Grow cabbage normally to mature head size. Do not harvest
2.  Winter: Protect plant from frost (cold frame, mulch, or move to cool storage at 32-40°F if climate is too harsh)
3.  Spring (Year 2): Plant survives winter. Warm weather triggers bolting (flower stalk emergence)
4.  Summer (Year 2): Yellow flowers appear. Insects pollinate. Seed pods form
5.  Late Summer (Year 2): Pods dry on plant and turn brown
6.  Harvest: Cut flower stalks when mostly dry. Hang in cool place to finish drying. Thresh pods to release seeds

#### Broccoli & Kale

Same two-year cycle: grow first year for head/leaves, overwinter, bolt in spring of year two, produce seeds by summer. Isolation distance is 1 mile (brassicas cross promiscuously).

#### Seed Yield

-   One cabbage plant produces 3,000-10,000 seeds
-   One broccoli plant produces 5,000-10,000 seeds
-   One kale plant produces 5,000-10,000 seeds

### Fabaceae (Legume Family): Bean, Pea

#### Reproduction & Isolation

-   **Pollination:** Self-pollinating (mostly)
-   **Cross-contamination risk:** Very low; minimal crossing between varieties
-   **Isolation distance:** None required (rare crossing)
-   **Growth pattern:** Annual (one year)
-   **Difficulty:** Very easy

#### Why Beans Are Ideal for Beginners

Beans are self-pollinating and require no isolation. You can grow a dozen bean varieties in the same garden and they will not cross. The seed saving process is simple: let pods dry on the plant, thresh, and store. This is why beans are the first crop to save seed from.

#### Seed Saving Process: Dry Bean

1.  Allow pods to stay on plant until mature (pods turn brown/papery)
2.  Pick pods when mostly dry (not soaking wet)
3.  Shell pods: squeeze or crack open to remove beans
4.  Dry further on screen (1-2 weeks) if any moisture remains
5.  Test for dryness: bean should not dent when bent (should snap cleanly)
6.  Store in cool, dry place

#### Fresh/Snap Beans

If you want fresh snap beans, harvest pods when young and tender for eating. For seed, let beans mature fully and pods dry on plant. The mature seeds are not edible (too hard), but they are viable for planting.

#### Peas

Same process: grow, let pods mature and dry, shell, store. Peas are equally easy for seed saving.

#### Seed Yield

-   One bean plant produces 20-50 dry beans
-   One pea plant produces 10-30 dry peas

### Poaceae (Grass Family): Corn, Wheat, Rice

#### Reproduction & Isolation

-   **Pollination:** Wind-pollinated (pollen drifts on air)
-   **Cross-contamination risk:** Very high; different corn varieties cross easily over long distances
-   **Isolation distance:** 1 mile minimum (many recommend 2+ miles)
-   **Genetic diversity:** Requires 200+ plants minimum for genetic health
-   **Difficulty:** Hard (requires space and isolation)

#### Why Corn Is Complex

Corn is wind-pollinated and crosses at distance. Different corn varieties will cross, and you cannot control where pollen goes. Additionally, corn requires large populations (200+ plants) to maintain genetic diversity. This is intensive for home gardeners but essential for breeding true varieties.

#### Seed Saving Process: Corn

1.  Grow 200+ corn plants (minimum for genetic diversity)
2.  Isolate from other corn varieties by 1 mile (or 2 miles if possible)
3.  Allow ears to fully mature on plant (kernels harden)
4.  Harvest ears when stalks brown and kernels are hard
5.  Remove husks and allow ears to dry further in cool, dry place (2-4 weeks)
6.  Thresh: Remove kernels from cob (rub ear against cob or use hand thresher)
7.  Winnow: Pass kernels through air to remove chaff
8.  Store in cool, dry place

#### Wheat & Rice

Wheat is self-pollinating (not wind-pollinated as commonly believed) and requires no isolation. Rice is self-pollinating. Both are annuals and follow similar dry-seed process: mature, harvest, thresh, winnow, store.

#### Seed Yield

-   One corn ear produces 300-400 kernels
-   200 wheat plants produce 5+ lbs of seed
-   Rice is similar to wheat in yield

### Alliums (Onion/Garlic Family): Onion, Leek, Garlic, Shallot

#### Reproduction & Isolation

-   **Pollination:** Cross-pollinated (insects)
-   **Isolation distance:** 1 mile between onion varieties (leek and onion can cross)
-   **Growth pattern:** Biennial (two years)
-   **Garlic special case:** Propagated from cloves, not seed
-   **Difficulty:** Moderate

#### Onion & Leek

Onions and leeks follow the biennial pattern: year one grows the bulb, year two produces seed. Let bulbs overwinter, warm spring triggers bolting. Flowers produce seeds. Dry, thresh, store.

#### Garlic: Clove Propagation

Garlic is almost always propagated from cloves (vegetative propagation), not seed. This means:

-   Save the largest, healthiest cloves from each bulb
-   Store cloves in cool place over winter (50-60°F)
-   Plant cloves in fall (6-8 weeks before ground freezes)
-   Cloves produce genetically identical plants (clone, not hybrid)
-   Garlic seed takes longer and is less reliable, so avoid

#### Seed Yield

-   One onion flower stalk produces 1,000+ seeds
-   One leek plant produces 500-1,000 seeds
-   Garlic produces no seed; clove propagation only

### Apiaceae (Parsley/Carrot Family): Carrot, Parsley, Dill, Cilantro

#### Reproduction & Isolation

-   **Pollination:** Cross-pollinated (insects)
-   **Isolation distance:** 1 mile between carrot varieties (wild carrot can cross, complicating seed saving)
-   **Growth pattern:** Biennial (two years for carrot and parsley; annual for dill and cilantro)
-   **Challenge:** Tiny seeds, difficult to separate from debris
-   **Difficulty:** Hard

#### Carrot & Parsley (Biennial)

1.  Year 1: Grow root (carrot) or leaves (parsley) normally, do not harvest
2.  Winter: Protect or store at 32-40°F
3.  Spring (Year 2): Warm weather triggers bolting. Flower umbels (umbrella-shaped clusters) appear
4.  Summer (Year 2): Flowers produce seeds
5.  Harvest: Cut umbels when mostly brown/dry. Hang upside down to dry further. Seeds fall out. Winnow to remove chaff

#### Dill & Cilantro (Annual)

These bolt in first year. Let plants mature, seeds dry on plant, collect seeds. Much easier than biennials.

#### Seed Yield

-   One carrot plant produces 2,000-5,000 seeds (very small, hard to clean)
-   One parsley plant produces 2,000-3,000 seeds
-   One dill plant produces 500-1,000 seeds

## Harvesting Seed: Timing & Method

Seeds are mature before fruit is ripe for eating. A ripe tomato you would pick and eat has immature seeds inside. To collect viable seed, you must let fruit mature past eating ripeness. This is the first lesson of seed saving: patience.

### Dry Seeds: Mature on Plant

Dry seeds (beans, corn, squash, brassicas, alliums) should remain on the plant until dry. This reduces moisture content naturally and prepares seeds for storage.

**Process:**

1.  Do not harvest fruit/pod when it looks ripe for eating
2.  Leave on plant for additional 2-4 weeks (varies by crop)
3.  Pods, husks, or flower structures should brown and dry
4.  Seeds should feel hard and not easily dent with fingernail
5.  Pods should rattle when shaken (seeds loose inside)
6.  Pick fruit when dry but before it falls to ground (rain/rot risk)

#### Signs of Readiness:

-   Bean pods: brown, papery, rattle inside
-   Corn ears: kernels hard, husk brown, stalk dying
-   Squash: skin hard (cannot dent with fingernail), stem brown
-   Brassica flower stalks: pods brown, dried completely
-   Onion/garlic: leaves brown and wilting, bulbs hard

:::warning
**Timing Risk:** Waiting too long can mean seed falls to ground and is lost. Too early means poor viability. The window is usually 1-2 weeks. Monitor plants daily in late season and harvest when fully dry but not shattering.
:::

### Wet Seeds: Ferment or Rinse

Wet seeds (tomato, cucumber, melon, squash) have a gel coat that must be removed. There are two methods:

#### Method 1: Fermentation (For Tomato & Cucumber)

**Why ferment?** Fermentation dissolves the gel coat, kills seed-borne pathogens, and removes germination inhibitors. This is critical for seed viability.

1.  Scoop seeds and gel into container
2.  Add small amount of water (seeds should be barely covered)
3.  Cover loosely with cloth (not airtight)
4.  Leave at room temperature (65-75°F ideal) for 2-3 days
5.  Smell is important: you should detect fermentation (mildly unpleasant)
6.  Do not ferment longer than 5 days (seeds can rot)
7.  When gel is gone, rinse thoroughly in clean water multiple times
8.  Spread on screen or plate to dry

#### Method 2: Water Flotation (Alternative, Faster)

**For impatient seed savers (less ideal):**

1.  Scoop seeds and gel into container
2.  Add plenty of water
3.  Rub seeds between hands to loosen gel
4.  Pour off floating debris and dead seeds
5.  Refill with water, repeat until water is clear
6.  Spread on screen to dry

**Note:** This is faster but misses the disease-killing benefits of fermentation. Fermentation is preferred.

### Timing: Seed Maturity vs Fruit Ripeness

This is critical: seed maturity and fruit ripeness are different stages.

## Cleaning & Processing Seeds: Preparation for Storage

Raw seed from the plant contains debris: pods, chaff, dirt, non-viable seeds. Cleaning removes this debris and discards weak/empty seeds. Clean seed stores better and germinates more reliably.

### Winnowing: Separating Seed from Chaff

Winnowing uses air to blow away light chaff while heavy seeds fall. This is the oldest and simplest method.

**Method:**

1.  Pour seeds and chaff from one container to another in gentle breeze (outdoors, open window, or fan)
2.  Chaff (light) blows away downwind
3.  Seeds (heavy) fall into container below
4.  Repeat 3-5 times until chaff is minimal

**Gentle breeze is key:** Too much wind and seeds blow away. Too little and chaff does not move. An outdoor breeze or small fan works well.

### Screening: Size Separation

Screening uses mesh of different sizes to separate seeds by size and remove smaller debris.

**Method:**

1.  Use first screen with larger holes than seeds (removes large debris)
2.  Use second screen with holes smaller than seeds (seeds stay on top, fine debris falls through)
3.  Shake gently, moving in circular motion
4.  Inspect seeds: discard cracked, shriveled, or obviously non-viable seeds

**DIY screens:** Layer cheesecloth or window screen in wooden frames. Mesh size depends on seed size.

### Water Flotation: Density-Based Separation

Good, viable seeds are denser than empty hulls. Water flotation sorts them by floating away the bad seeds.

**Method:**

1.  Pour seeds into container of clean water
2.  Stir gently, let settle for 1 minute
3.  Empty/shriveled seeds float, viable seeds sink
4.  Skim floating seeds and debris from surface
5.  Pour off floating seeds (discard)
6.  Drain sinking seeds through strainer
7.  Spread on screen to dry immediately

**Critical:** Dry thoroughly after water flotation (within hours) to prevent mold.

### Threshing: Releasing Seeds from Pods

Threshing separates seeds from pods, husks, or flower structures.

**Methods:**

-   **Hand:** Rub pods between palms over cloth. Seeds fall out, pods stay
-   **Mortar & Pestle:** Gently pound dried pods (not seeds). Do not crush seeds
-   **Flail:** Bundle dried plant material and beat with stick over cloth
-   **Bag method:** Place dried material in burlap bag, stomp or beat to release seeds

**Gentle is important:** Goal is to release seeds, not crush them.

### Removing Mold & Contamination

If seeds were wet or stored improperly, mold or rot can develop.

-   **Surface mold:** Brush off gently with soft cloth or old toothbrush
-   **Heavy mold:** Discard seeds (internally contaminated)
-   **Insect contamination:** Freeze seeds for 48 hours at -10°F or lower to kill insects and larvae

:::warning
**Cleanliness:** Mold can develop quickly on damp seeds. Never store wet seeds. Clean all containers before storing. Store only fully dry seeds.
:::

</section>

## Drying Seeds: The Critical Step for Long Storage

Moisture is the enemy of seed storage. Seeds above 12% moisture content deteriorate rapidly, lose viability, develop mold, and rot. Dry seeds below 8% moisture can remain viable for years. Drying is not optional—it is essential for long-term seed viability.

### Why Drying Matters

-   **Viability:** Each 1% increase in moisture reduces storage life by 50% at room temperature
-   **Mold prevention:** Moisture triggers fungal growth
-   **Insect resistance:** Low moisture kills many insect pests
-   **Long-term storage:** Dry seeds can last 3-10+ years; wet seeds fail in months

### Method 1: Air Drying on Screens

This is the simplest method for most seeds.

**Process:**

1.  Spread seeds in single layer on screen or cloth
2.  Place in warm, dry location (65-75°F ideal, below 40% humidity)
3.  Ensure air circulation (fan or open window)
4.  Stir seeds daily (prevents packing)
5.  Dry for 1-2 weeks, longer for large seeds or humid climates
6.  Test: seed should snap cleanly when bent (not flex or bend)

**Ideal location:** Attic, basement, dry shed, or room with dehumidifier.

### Method 2: Silica Gel Drying (Faster, More Reliable)

Silica gel is a desiccant (moisture absorber) that can dry seeds rapidly and reliably.

**Process:**

1.  Place layer of silica gel in bottom of airtight container
2.  Place seeds in single layer on cloth above gel (not touching)
3.  Cover container (but leave slightly ajar for first 24 hours)
4.  Seal tightly after 24 hours
5.  Check daily: silica gel will change color (blue → pink/clear) as it absorbs moisture
6.  When gel is saturated, remove seeds and refresh gel
7.  Replace gel with fresh (or regenerate by heating in oven at 250°F for 1-2 hours)
8.  Continue process for 5-7 days until seeds are fully dry

**Reusable:** Silica gel can be regenerated and reused indefinitely.

### Method 3: Rice Bran or Flour Drying (Budget Alternative)

Powdered rice or flour can act as a desiccant in a pinch (less effective than silica gel but free).

**Process:**

1.  Lay seeds on cloth in airtight container
2.  Sprinkle rice flour or rice bran over seeds (do not mix directly)
3.  Seal container
4.  Check daily; refresh flour/bran every 2-3 days
5.  Takes longer than silica gel but works

**Drawback:** Flour can cake and clump. Silica gel is superior if available.

### Testing for Dryness: The Bend Test

Do not guess. Test seeds to confirm they are dry enough for storage.

**Method:**

1.  Take a seed and hold it between thumb and forefinger
2.  Bend with force (not gentle)
3.  **Dry:** Seed snaps cleanly and cracks open
4.  **Too wet:** Seed bends and flexes without breaking
5.  Test 10-20 seeds from different locations in batch
6.  All should snap, not bend

### Drying Timeline by Seed Type

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Seed Type</th><th scope="row">Size</th><th scope="row">Air Dry Time (Ideal Conditions)</th><th scope="row">Silica Gel Time</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Tomato, Pepper, Eggplant</td><td>Small</td><td>7-10 days</td><td>3-5 days</td></tr><tr><td>Bean, Pea</td><td>Large</td><td>10-14 days</td><td>5-7 days</td></tr><tr><td>Squash, Cucumber, Melon</td><td>Medium</td><td>10-14 days</td><td>5-7 days</td></tr><tr><td>Corn</td><td>Large</td><td>14-21 days</td><td>7-10 days</td></tr><tr><td>Tiny seeds (carrot, parsley)</td><td>Micro</td><td>7-10 days</td><td>3-5 days</td></tr></tbody></table>

:::tip
**Pro Strategy:** Air dry for initial drying (removes obvious moisture), then finish with silica gel (ensures deep dryness). This combines benefits of both methods.
:::

## Storage: Creating Your Seed Vault

A seed vault is only as good as its storage conditions. Cool, dry, dark, airtight storage can preserve seed viability for decades. Poor storage destroys seeds in months. **No seed batch should enter or remain in the vault without passing a germination test** — see [Seed Viability Testing & Storage](seed-viability-testing-storage.html) for the full testing protocol, interpretation thresholds, and longevity-by-crop tables.

### The Four Enemies of Seed Storage

-   **Moisture:** Above 12%, seeds absorb water and lose viability quickly
-   **Heat:** Each 10°F increase reduces storage life by half
-   **Light:** UV radiation degrades seed tissues
-   **Oxygen/Pests:** Air allows oxidation and insect infiltration

### Ideal Storage Conditions

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Parameter</th><th scope="row">Target</th><th scope="row">Acceptable Range</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td><strong>Temperature</strong></td><td>50°F (10°C)</td><td>35-55°F (2-13°C)</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Humidity</strong></td><td>30% RH</td><td>20-40% RH</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Moisture (Seed)</strong></td><td>&lt;8%</td><td>&lt;12%</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Light</strong></td><td>Total darkness</td><td>No direct light</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Container</strong></td><td>Airtight</td><td>Sealed glass or plastic</td></tr></tbody></table>

### Container Options

#### Option 1: Glass Jars with Rubber Seals (Recommended)

-   Airtight glass jars with rubber-gasket lids (Jar sealers)
-   Inert (does not react with seeds)
-   Reusable, durable, long-lasting
-   Easy to inspect (see contents without opening)
-   Cost: $0.50-$2 per jar

#### Option 2: Mylar Bags with Oxygen Absorbers

-   Food-grade mylar bags (blocks light, moisture)
-   Oxygen absorbers (small packets that remove air)
-   Heat seal with iron or sealer
-   Good for long-term storage (10+ years)
-   Cost: $0.10-$0.30 per bag

#### Option 3: Plastic Containers with Lids (Budget)

-   Food-grade plastic with tight-fitting lids
-   Less air-tight than glass or mylar, but acceptable short-term
-   Best if stored in cool place and refreshed every 2-3 years
-   Cost: $0.20-$1 per container

### Desiccants: Keeping Seeds Dry in Storage

Even after drying, humidity in the air can re-hydrate seeds over time. A desiccant in the storage container absorbs this moisture.

#### Option 1: Silica Gel Packets (Best)

-   Reusable, regenerate by heating
-   Place 1-2 packets per jar
-   Replace when saturated (color changes)

#### Option 2: Powdered Milk in Tissue

-   Wrap powdered milk in tissue or paper towel
-   Place in jar before sealing
-   Cheap, accessible alternative to silica gel
-   Replace annually

#### Option 3: Uncooked Rice

-   Place uncooked rice in small cloth bag
-   Include in jar
-   Less effective than silica gel, but functional

### Labeling: Critical Information

A seed is useless if you do not know what it is. Label every container with:

-   **Crop & Variety:** 'Brandywine' tomato (not just "tomato")
-   **Harvest Date:** Year saved (e.g., 2025)
-   **Source:** Where you got the seed or where you saved it from
-   **Germination Rate:** If tested (e.g., "85% germination")
-   **Storage Location:** Where this jar is kept (helps with inventory)

Use waterproof label or write on tape with permanent marker. Include all info even if it seems obvious—future you will forget.

### Temperature & Storage Life

Temperature is critical. The general rule: each 10°F temperature decrease doubles storage life.

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Storage Condition</th><th scope="row">Temperature</th><th scope="row">Typical Storage Life</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Room temperature (normal pantry)</td><td>70°F (21°C)</td><td>1-2 years</td></tr><tr><td>Cool closet, basement</td><td>55°F (13°C)</td><td>3-5 years</td></tr><tr><td>Refrigerator</td><td>40°F (4°C)</td><td>5-10 years</td></tr><tr><td>Freezer (-10°F to 0°F)</td><td>-4°F to 0°F (-20 to -18°C)</td><td>10-30 years</td></tr></tbody></table>

### Freezer Storage: Long-Term Preservation

Freezer storage is ideal for long-term preservation (10-30 years).

**Method:**

1.  Ensure seeds are fully dry (<8% moisture)
2.  Place in airtight container or mylar bag
3.  Add desiccant (silica gel or powdered milk)
4.  Seal completely
5.  Place in freezer at 0°F or below
6.  Do not remove and replace frequently (temperature fluctuations damage seeds)
7.  Label clearly

**Critical:** Allow seeds to warm to room temperature in sealed container before opening (prevents moisture condensation).

### Organized Vault Layout

A small shelf or cabinet organized by crop family is manageable:

-   **Shelf 1:** Solanaceae (tomato, pepper, eggplant)
-   **Shelf 2:** Brassicaceae (cabbage, broccoli, kale)
-   **Shelf 3:** Cucurbitaceae (squash, melon, cucumber)
-   **Shelf 4:** Fabaceae (beans, peas) + Poaceae (corn, wheat)
-   **Shelf 5:** Alliums (onion, garlic) + Apiaceae (carrot, parsley)
-   **Freezer:** Backup copies of everything

Maintain a log: written or digital list of what is in storage, quantities, and dates. Update when you add seeds or remove them.

:::tip
**Redundancy Strategy:** Keep primary vault at home (cool closet or basement). Keep backup in freezer. Share seeds with community members (living backup). If one location fails, others preserve genetic diversity.
:::

## Viability Testing: Ensuring Seeds Will Grow

Old seeds may not germinate. Before planting your vault for food production, test germination. A seed that does not sprout is wasted space and effort. Viability testing tells you which seeds to rely on. For the full germination test procedure, dormancy-breaking techniques, and troubleshooting, see [Seed Viability Testing & Storage](seed-viability-testing-storage.html).

### Why Test?

-   **Know what is viable:** Which varieties will grow
-   **Adjust seeding rate:** If 70% germinate, sow thicker
-   **Cull weak seeds:** Discard seeds with poor germination
-   **Plan succession:** Refresh seeds that are aging
-   **Confidence:** Know your vault is functional before it is critical

### Standard Germination Test

This is simple, fast, and reliable. It takes 7-14 days and requires only water and a plastic bag.

**Method:**

1.  Take 10 seeds of the variety to test (or 20-25 for precision)
2.  Place on moist paper towel (not soaking wet)
3.  Fold towel to enclose seeds loosely
4.  Place in plastic bag or plastic container (creates humid environment)
5.  Keep in warm location (65-75°F ideal)
6.  Check daily; keep paper towel moist (not soggy)
7.  Count sprouted seeds at day 7 and day 14
8.  Calculate germination percentage

#### Example:

If 10 seeds are tested and 8 sprout by day 7: 8/10 = 80% germination rate. This is good. 50-79% is acceptable. Below 50% means the batch is degraded and should be replaced or discarded.

### Interpreting Results

### Crop-Specific Germination Times

Different crops sprout at different rates. Know when to expect sprouts:

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Crop</th><th scope="row">First Sprout (Days)</th><th scope="row">Test at (Days)</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Tomato, Pepper</td><td>3-5</td><td>7-14</td></tr><tr><td>Eggplant</td><td>5-7</td><td>10-14</td></tr><tr><td>Bean, Pea</td><td>3-5</td><td>7-10</td></tr><tr><td>Squash, Melon, Cucumber</td><td>3-5</td><td>7-14</td></tr><tr><td>Cabbage, Broccoli, Kale</td><td>3-5</td><td>7-10</td></tr><tr><td>Carrot, Parsley</td><td>7-10</td><td>14-21</td></tr><tr><td>Onion, Leek</td><td>5-7</td><td>10-14</td></tr></tbody></table>

### Testing Schedule

Test seeds regularly to catch degradation:

-   **Year 1 (harvest year):** Test newly saved seed before storing (baseline)
-   **Year 2-3:** Test once per year (monitor storage effectiveness)
-   **Year 4+:** Test before planting if you plan to use stored seed
-   **Before critical use:** Always test seeds from vault before relying on them for food production

:::tip
**Pro Tip:** Keep a small sample of each variety separate specifically for testing. This prevents using seeds meant for testing. Label samples "Test Batch" and test them before growing for food.

<section id="vault-construction">

## Seed Vault Construction: From Personal to Community Scale

A seed vault is not just storage—it is a system. A good vault has redundancy, organization, accessibility, and community involvement.

### Personal Household Vault

Start with a personal vault in your home. This is the foundation of food security.

**Components:**

-   **Location:** Cool, dry, dark space (basement closet, cool pantry, root cellar ideal; avoid attic/kitchen)
-   **Temperature:** Below 60°F ideally (basement maintains 50-55°F naturally)
-   **Humidity control:** Small dehumidifier if necessary (<40% RH)
-   **Storage containers:** Glass jars or mylar bags, labeled, with desiccants
-   **Shelving:** Simple wire or wooden shelves organized by crop family
-   **Backup location:** Freezer for long-term copies
-   **Inventory log:** Written or digital list of what is stored

#### Minimum Setup Cost: $100-300

-   Glass jars: $30-50
-   Silica gel: $10
-   Small shelving: $30-50
-   Labels and supplies: $10
-   Seeds (first purchase): $50-150

This is a one-time investment for a resource that produces indefinitely. A single year of saved bean seeds can provide 100+ pounds of beans to plant.

### Underground Seed Vault (Community Scale)

For serious, long-term preservation, an underground chamber provides constant cool temperature and low humidity.

**Design Principles:**

-   **Location:** Below ground (basement, root cellar, buried chamber)
-   **Constant temperature:** Underground stays at 45-55°F year-round
-   **Low humidity:** 30-40% RH (drainage and ventilation control)
-   **Waterproofing:** Sealed chamber with drain or sump pump if water risk
-   **Airtightness:** Sealed door, minimal ventilation (prevents humidity exchange)
-   **Shelving:** Metal or wooden racks, labeled by crop family
-   **Accessibility:** Easy to navigate, add/remove seeds, inventory
-   **Security:** Lock if necessary, protection from unauthorized access

#### DIY Underground Vault Options:

-   **Basement corner:** Partition off cool section with plastic sheeting and seal. Simplest option
-   **Root cellar:** If you have one, use existing structure. Add shelving and organization
-   **Buried container:** Waterproof plastic shipping container buried 3+ feet deep. More complex, better insulation
-   **Purpose-built chamber:** Underground room with concrete walls and sealed entrance. Highest cost, best results

**Minimum viable underground vault: A cool, dry basement corner with shelving, dehumidifier, and sealed containers. Cost: $100-500.**

### Community Seed Library

A shared collection that members check out, grow, and return with interest (additional seeds from harvest).

**Organization:**

1.  **Host location:** Library, community center, garden, or member home
2.  **Storage:** Cool shelf or cabinet, organized by crop
3.  **Catalog:** List of varieties available with descriptions and planting info
4.  **Check-out system:** Simple log (who, what, when, date to return)
5.  **Return expectations:** Grow crop, save seeds, return with 2-3x original amount
6.  **Lost seed policy:** Member replaces if seeds are lost (purchase or donate)
7.  **Meeting/swap events:** Annual seed swaps where members share and discuss varieties

#### Seed Library Best Practices:

-   **Focus on easy-to-save:** Beans, tomatoes, peppers (self-pollinating, no isolation needed)
-   **Start small:** 5-10 varieties in year one. Expand as members participate
-   **Document:** Keep notes on what grows well in your climate
-   **Member education:** Provide simple saving guides (handout or digital)
-   **Redundancy:** Keep backup seeds in case member-saved seed fails
-   **Community building:** Make it social—seed swaps create connections

#### Minimum Viable Seed Library:

-   Start with 10-20 varieties of easy-to-save crops
-   One person volunteers to maintain (coordinate check-outs, inventory)
-   Cost: $50-100 to stock initial seeds
-   Growth: As members return seeds with surplus, library grows organically

### Regional Seed Backup Network

Distribute risk geographically by sharing seeds with other communities and networks.

-   **Connect with regional networks:** Seed Savers Exchange, local farm cooperatives, gardening clubs
-   **Trade seeds:** Exchange your surplus with other communities
-   **Multiple backups:** Your favorite beans stored in your vault, community library, friend's freezer, regional vault
-   **Communication:** Maintain contact list of network members who hold backup seeds
-   **Sustainability:** Each member/community grows some of the backup seeds to keep them fresh

If a disease, pest, or disaster destroys your local vault, your seeds survive in the regional network. This is the same principle as the Svalbard Vault but distributed and human-scale.

### Seed Swap Events

Annual or seasonal events where gardeners gather to share seeds and knowledge.

**Organizing a Seed Swap:**

1.  Set date in winter or early spring (before planting season)
2.  Advertise: community bulletin boards, social media, word of mouth
3.  Request: bring seeds to share (no cost to participate)
4.  Venue: community center, garden, library (tables, chairs)
5.  Format: people lay out seeds on table, others browse and take what they want (honor system or trading)
6.  Education: include talks on seed saving, variety recommendations, storage
7.  Social: light refreshments, conversation, building community

**Low-cost event:** $50-100 for venue and flyers. Value of seeds shared: potentially $500+.

## Seed Starting Calendar & Succession Planting

A seed vault is only useful if you grow it. A planting calendar ensures you use seeds strategically, maximize harvests, and produce seed sustainably.

### Understanding Your Climate Zone

All planting timing depends on your USDA hardiness zone and local frost dates. Find your zone:

-   Visit planthardiness.usda.gov or search "USDA hardiness zone \[your zip code\]"
-   Know your last spring frost date and first fall frost date
-   Count days between: this is your growing season

### Common Frost Dates by Zone

### Seed Starting Timing

#### Indoor Starting (6-8 weeks before transplanting)

Some crops (tomato, pepper, eggplant, brassicas) are started indoors under lights to get a head start.

**Process:**

1.  Count backward from last spring frost date
2.  Start seeds indoors 6-8 weeks prior (varies by crop)
3.  Keep under lights 14-16 hours per day
4.  Keep soil consistently moist, not soaking
5.  When seedlings have 2-3 true leaves, thin or transplant to larger pot
6.  Harden off: gradually expose to outdoor light and wind 1-2 weeks before transplanting
7.  Transplant after last frost date when soil warms

#### Direct Seeding (Plant directly outdoors)

Many crops are direct seeded after frost danger passes.

-   **Beans, peas:** Direct seed after last frost (soil must be warm, 60°F+)
-   **Corn:** Direct seed when soil reaches 60°F (typically 1-2 weeks after last frost)
-   **Squash, melon, cucumber:** Direct seed after last frost, soil 70°F+
-   **Root crops (carrot, beet, turnip):** Direct seed 2-3 weeks before last frost (cold hardy)
-   **Greens (spinach, lettuce):** Direct seed 4 weeks before last frost; again in late summer for fall

### Succession Planting: Multiple Harvests Per Year

Plant the same crop at intervals to stagger harvest throughout the season.

**Example: Lettuce & Greens (30-day harvest cycle)**

-   **May 1:** Plant batch 1 (harvest June 1)
-   **May 15:** Plant batch 2 (harvest June 15)
-   **June 1:** Plant batch 3 (harvest July 1)
-   **Continue:** Every 2-3 weeks until 8 weeks before first fall frost
-   **Fall succession:** Plant again in August for fall/early winter harvest
-   **Result:** Continuous fresh lettuce instead of 2-week glut then nothing

### Sample Year-Round Calendar (Temperate Zone, Zone 5)

Last frost: May 15 | First frost: October 1 | Growing season: ~140 days

**Spring (March-May):**

-   **Early March:** Start tomato, pepper, eggplant indoors
-   **Mid-April:** Direct seed spinach, lettuce, peas outdoors (4 weeks before frost)
-   **May 1:** Start brassicas (cabbage, broccoli) indoors
-   **May 15 (Last frost):** Plant tomato, pepper transplants; direct seed beans, corn
-   **May 25:** Transplant brassicas outdoors; direct seed squash, melon

**Early Summer (June):**

-   **June 1:** Second planting of beans, corn (3 weeks after first)
-   **Early greens harvest, peas reach peak**

**Mid-Summer (July):**

-   **July 1:** Third bean planting (if season allows)
-   **Squash, melon flowering, first tomato ripening**
-   **Succession plant lettuce for fall harvest (8-10 weeks before first frost)**

**Late Summer (August):**

-   **Corn, beans reaching maturity (let some mature for seed)**
-   **Tomato, pepper, eggplant peak production**
-   **Second lettuce/greens succession (for fall)**

**Fall (September):**

-   **Seed saving: allow beans, corn to mature on plant (stop harvesting for eating)**
-   **Tomato/pepper/eggplant seeds ferment and dry**
-   **Fall greens harvest**
-   **September 1 (8 weeks before frost):** Stop planting warm-season crops

**Harvest & Storage (October):**

-   **October 1 (First frost):** Final tomato, pepper harvest before frost kills plants
-   **Hard squash harvests before frost**
-   **Beans, corn fully mature and dried**
-   **Thresh, clean, dry, store seeds**
-   **Root crops (carrot, beet) can stay in ground with mulch 2-4 weeks past frost**

### Seed Saving Integration

Plan which crops to let mature for seed vs. harvest for eating:

-   **Plant 20% extra:** Of crops you will save seed from; let these mature while eating from rest
-   **Space separation:** Isolate seed crops from eating crops mentally (different areas if possible)
-   **Calendar reminder:** Mark planting date + expected seed maturity. Set reminder to stop harvesting for eating 2-3 weeks before maturity
-   **Biennial crops:** Plan overwintering location before planting (basement, cold frame, protective mulch)

:::tip
**Strategy:** Grow 1-2 crop families for seed each year, rotating. Year 1: save beans and tomato. Year 2: save squash and pepper. Year 3: save corn and cabbage. This distributes the work and ensures all your important varieties are refreshed every 3 years.
:::
