## Quick Triage: Life-Threatening Bleeding

If bleeding is heavy or spurting, apply a tourniquet high and tight for limb wounds, or use direct pressure plus wound packing, then evacuate urgently.

<section id="overview">

## Overview

Hemorrhage is the leading preventable cause of death in trauma patients. Modern trauma medicine prioritizes early control of life-threatening bleeding, which can mean the difference between survival and death. This guide covers systematic hemorrhage control from initial assessment through shock management in austere environments where immediate surgical intervention is unavailable. Master these techniques before attempting <a href="../surgery-field.html">Field Surgery & Emergency Procedures</a>, and follow up with <a href="../shock-recognition-resuscitation.html">Hemorrhagic Shock & Emergency Resuscitation</a> to manage the systemic effects of blood loss.

**Critical disclaimer:** This guide is educational. In any life-threatening emergency, call professional emergency services immediately. These techniques are intended for wilderness, disaster, or medical-scarce scenarios only.

The principles outlined here follow evidence from military combat medicine, civilian mass casualty protocols, and emergency medicine. The goal is to preserve life through the golden hour—the critical period when rapid intervention has maximum impact.

</section>

<section id="hemorrhage-assessment">

## Hemorrhage Assessment

Rapid assessment determines bleeding severity and guides treatment priority.

### Classification by Volume Loss

**Class I (up to 15% blood volume):** Minimal symptoms; heart rate <100; blood pressure normal; capillary refill normal. The body compensates without intervention.

**Class II (15–30% blood volume):** Anxiety, restlessness; heart rate 100–120; blood pressure normal or slightly low; pale skin; delayed capillary refill (>2 seconds). This is compensated shock—the patient appears relatively stable but is beginning to decompensate.

**Class III (30–40% blood volume):** Confusion, agitation; heart rate >120; systolic BP <100 mmHg; skin cold, clammy, mottled; capillary refill >3 seconds. This is decompensated shock—organ perfusion is severely compromised.

**Class IV (>40% blood volume):** Altered consciousness or unresponsiveness; barely palpable pulse; systolic BP <70 mmHg or absent; skin pale, cold, cyanotic. This is hemorrhagic shock—without immediate intervention, death is imminent.

:::warning
Class III and IV hemorrhage require aggressive volume replacement, tourniquets, and evacuation. Time is critical. Do not delay evacuation to perform non-life-saving interventions.
:::

### Rapid Assessment Protocol

1. **Scene safety first.** Ensure you and the victim are not in danger.
2. **Primary survey:** ABCs (Airway, Breathing, Circulation). Identify life threats.
3. **Hemorrhage check:** Expose the body, look for spurting blood or large pools.
4. **Shock assessment:** Check skin color, temperature, capillary refill, mental status, pulse.
5. **Anatomical bleeding source:** Determine if bleeding is from a limb, trunk, head, or internal.

External hemorrhage is visible and addressable. Internal hemorrhage (abdominal, thoracic, pelvic) is not directly controllable and requires rapid evacuation.

<section id="tourniquet-application">

## Tourniquet Application

A tourniquet is a mechanical device that stops blood flow to a limb. It is the most effective way to control severe extremity bleeding and save lives.

### When to Apply a Tourniquet

- Severe arterial bleeding from an arm or leg that cannot be controlled with direct pressure
- Mangled limb with vascular injury
- Unconscious patient with uncontrolled limb bleeding (too injured to apply pressure)
- Patient with severe burns limiting direct pressure. See <a href="../burn-treatment.html">Burn Treatment</a> for burn-specific management. See also <a href="../hydrotherapy-fever-management.html">Hydrotherapy & Fever Management</a> for temperature regulation in trauma patients.
- Delayed evacuation with continuing blood loss

:::tip
Do not hesitate to apply a tourniquet in life-threatening limb hemorrhage. Mark the time, keep it in place, and evacuate promptly once bleeding is controlled. Excessive bleeding is a greater threat than tourniquet complications.
:::

### Commercial Tourniquet Application

**High tourniquet (CAT, SOF-TT):** Place 2–3 inches above the wound, above the knee or elbow if possible.

1. Open the tourniquet to its widest setting.
2. Slide it onto the limb above the bleeding source.
3. Position so the tourniquet band is entirely on skin, not over joints.
4. Tighten the velcro strap firmly; the limb should pale below the tourniquet.
5. Locate the windlass (rod or lever).
6. Rotate the windlass until bleeding stops completely.
7. Tighten the locking mechanism.
8. Apply a second tourniquet 2 inches proximal to the first (backup measure for major thigh wounds).

### Improvised Tourniquet

If commercial tourniquets are unavailable:

1. **Material:** Use a strip of cloth, elastic bandage, bicycle inner tube, or belt that is 1.5–2 inches wide.
2. **Limb positioning:** Elevate the limb; assistant applies proximal pressure.
3. **Circumferential wrapping:** Pass the material around the limb 2–3 inches above the wound. Ensure no skin is bunched underneath.
4. **Windlass creation:** Tie a knot, leaving an end loop (or use a stick as the windlass rod).
5. **Tightening:** Twist the windlass until bleeding stops completely. If bleeding continues, tighten more or add a second tourniquet proximal to the first.
6. **Securing:** Wrap the windlass with additional cloth or tape to prevent unwinding.

### Marking and Time Documentation

Write the application time on the tourniquet band itself (or on tape attached to it) using a marker. This is critical for handoff to the next provider and guides evacuation priority. Keep the focus on rapid evacuation and definitive care rather than a fixed safe time window.

:::danger
**Do NOT loosen or remove tourniquets in the field.** Previous guidance recommended loosening tourniquets every 15-20 minutes — this is outdated and dangerous. Loosening releases pooled blood containing potassium, lactic acid, and myoglobin from ischemic tissue into systemic circulation, risking fatal cardiac arrhythmia, hypotension (reperfusion injury), and renewed hemorrhage. Once a tourniquet is applied, mark the time and leave it in place until the patient reaches surgical care.
:::

<section id="direct-pressure-and-wound-packing">

## Direct Pressure and Wound Packing

Direct manual pressure is the foundation of hemorrhage control. Wound packing is used for deep wounds, cavitary wounds, or junctional hemorrhage where a tourniquet cannot be applied.

### Direct Pressure Technique

1. **Don gloves if available,** but do not delay pressure to find protection.
2. **Apply firm, continuous pressure** with a clean cloth directly onto the wound.
3. **Do not remove the cloth to peek.** Removal disrupts clotting. If blood soaks through, apply fresh cloth on top (do not remove the bottom layer).
4. **Maintain pressure for 10–15 minutes.** Early removal allows rebleeding.
5. **Elevate the limb** above the heart to reduce blood pressure at the wound.
6. **Apply a pressure dressing** by wrapping snugly with elastic bandage or cloth strips.

:::warning
Never apply a tourniquet-like pressure dressing that cuts off all circulation. The goal is to control bleeding, not stop all blood flow to the limb.
:::

### Hemostatic Agents

Hemostatic agents (gauze impregnated with clotting promoters) dramatically reduce bleeding when packing deep wounds.

**Chitosan-based (Hemcon):** Activates the clotting cascade. Apply directly into the wound, pack firmly.

**Kaolin-based (QuikClot Combat Gauze):** Promotes clotting without thermal reaction. Pack gauze into the wound in layers; heating is minimal.

**Gelatin sponge (Surgifoam):** Provides scaffold for clot formation. Soak in saline or blood and pack tightly.

**Tranexamic acid (TXA):** Systemic antifibrinolytic that reduces bleeding. If available (usually IV form), give early in Class II or higher hemorrhage.

### Wound Packing Protocol

For deep lacerations, puncture wounds, or amputation stumps:

1. **Inspect the wound** to identify the source of bleeding (artery, vein, or general tissue ooze).
2. **Apply direct pressure** with a clean cloth for 1–2 minutes.
3. **If bleeding continues, apply hemostatic gauze:**
   - Soak the gauze in saline or apply dry.
   - Pack the wound cavity from the entrance, layer by layer, without probing the tract.
   - Pack layer by layer, filling the entire depth.
   - Ensure pressure is maintained throughout.
4. **Apply direct manual pressure** over the packed gauze for 3 minutes without lifting.
5. **Wrap with an elastic bandage** or cloth strips to maintain pressure.
6. **Mark the time** on the dressing or patient record.

### Junctional Hemorrhage

Junctional hemorrhage occurs in areas where a tourniquet cannot be applied: neck, axilla (armpit), groin, lower abdomen, and buttocks.

**Neck:** Apply firm direct pressure with both hands if possible. Pack only if the wound cavity is clearly open and accessible. Do not use carotid pressure-point compression or circumferential wraps. Monitor for airway swelling.

**Axilla (armpit):** This area is difficult to access. Pack wound tightly with hemostatic gauze; apply pressure dressing with the arm elevated and abducted (away from the body).

**Groin:** Apply firm, sustained direct pressure. Groin wounds often involve the femoral artery or vein; control is challenging. Pack deeply with hemostatic gauze; apply a pelvic binder if fracture or deep pelvic bleeding is suspected.

**Lower abdomen and buttocks:** Direct pressure and wound packing are limited. Evacuate urgently for surgical evaluation.

## Pelvic and Truncal Hemorrhage Control

Pelvic and abdominal bleeding are not directly compressible but require immediate tactical stabilization.

### Pelvic Binder

A pelvic binder is a fabric or elastic band that wraps around the pelvis at the level of the greater trochanters (hip bones). It stabilizes pelvic fractures and reduces pelvic volume, which slows ongoing bleeding.

**Improvised pelvic binder:**
1. Use a wide elastic bandage (4–6 inches), bed sheet, or sturdy cloth.
2. Position patient supine (flat on back).
3. Wrap the band horizontally around the pelvis at the hip level, just below the iliac crests.
4. Pull snugly but not so tight that breathing is compromised.
5. Secure with tape or pins.

**Clinical indicators for pelvic binder use:**
- Pelvic fracture with instability
- Blunt force trauma to the abdomen with shock
- Severe blunt trauma with signs of pelvic injury

### Truncal Hemorrhage

Hemorrhage from the chest, abdomen, or back is not amenable to direct control. Management focuses on:

1. **Positioning:** Place patient flat (supine) or in shock position (legs elevated 12 inches, head at 30 degrees).
2. **Airway management:** Ensure breathing is not compromised; manage vomiting risk.
3. **Evacuation:** Urgent surgical evaluation is required. Do not delay.
4. **Monitoring:** Assess for tension pneumothorax (severe shortness of breath, unilateral breath sounds absent, neck vein distension). If suspected, perform needle decompression at the 2nd intercostal space, midclavicular line.

## Shock Management and Fluid Resuscitation

Hemorrhagic shock develops when blood loss reduces oxygen delivery to vital organs. Early recognition and treatment are essential. For comprehensive shock assessment and management beyond hemorrhage control, see <a href="../shock-recognition-resuscitation.html">Hemorrhagic Shock & Emergency Resuscitation</a>.

### Recognition of Hemorrhagic Shock

**Compensated shock (Class II–III):** Rapid heart rate (>100 bpm), pale or mottled skin, delayed capillary refill (>2 seconds), restlessness or anxiety, decreased urination (if monitored), normal or low blood pressure.

**Decompensated shock (Class III–IV):** Severe altered mental status or unresponsiveness, barely palpable pulse, systolic BP <70 mmHg or unmeasurable, cold extremities, slow or absent capillary refill.

### Permissive Hypotension

In trauma patients with ongoing uncontrolled bleeding, controlled hypotension (keeping systolic BP at 80–90 mmHg) reduces continued blood loss and may improve survival. Avoid aggressive fluid resuscitation that increases bleeding.

**Exceptions:**
- Traumatic brain injury (maintain BP >90 mmHg)
- Elderly patients with cardiac disease (less tolerant of hypotension)

### Fluid Resuscitation

If IV access is available:

**Two-liter rule:** Start with 2 liters (2000 mL) of crystalloid (normal saline or Ringer's lactate) rapidly. Reassess bleeding control and shock status. If bleeding continues and shock persists, blood products (whole blood, packed RBCs, plasma) are superior to crystalloid but often unavailable in austere settings.

**Vital sign targets:**
- Heart rate <100 bpm
- Systolic BP ≥80 mmHg (or until bleeding is controlled and patient is alert)
- Capillary refill <2 seconds
- Urine output ≥0.5 mL/kg/hr (if catheterized)

### Position and Monitoring

1. **Supine positioning** optimizes venous return.
2. **Trendelenburg (legs elevated 30 degrees, head lower)** is controversial but may help in profound shock.
3. **Monitor continuously:** Recheck pulse, blood pressure, skin color, and mental status every 5–10 minutes.
4. **Reassess bleeding:** Determine if hemorrhage is controlled. If not, escalate measures (increase tourniquet tension, add second tourniquet, improve packing pressure, apply pelvic binder).

### When to Declare Massive Transfusion Protocol (MTP)

Massive transfusion is the transfusion of ≥10 units of RBCs in 24 hours or ≥4 units in 1 hour. Indicators include:

- Hemorrhage involving more than 40% blood volume (Class IV)
- Ongoing bleeding despite tourniquets and direct pressure
- Shock unresponsive to initial fluid resuscitation
- Anticipated need for urgent surgery

In hospital settings, MTP involves 1:1:1 ratio of RBCs:plasma:platelets. In austere settings, prioritize evacuation to a facility with blood products.

<section id="special-situations">

## Special Situations

### Amputation or Mangled Extremity

For traumatic amputation:

1. **Control hemorrhage** using tourniquet or direct pressure.
2. **Preserve the limb** if possible: wrap in clean cloth, place in plastic bag, keep cool with ice if available (do not freeze).
3. **Document exactly what happened** and the time of amputation.
4. **Evacuate urgently** to a center capable of replantation (usually requires surgery within 6–8 hours).

### Penetrating Wounds with Foreign Objects

**Do not remove foreign objects** (impaled knife, glass, etc.). Removal may precipitate massive bleeding. Stabilize the object and transport to surgery.

### Pregnancy-Related Hemorrhage

Pregnant patients are at high risk for shock due to normal physiologic increases in blood volume but reduced reserve. Tourniquet and direct pressure techniques apply. After 20 weeks, perimortem cesarean section may be life-saving if the patient is in cardiac arrest; perform only in setting where delivery and resuscitation of infant are possible.

### Anticoagulation and Antiplatelet Use

Patients on warfarin, DOACs (novel oral anticoagulants), or aspirin may have prolonged bleeding. Standard hemorrhage control applies. If reversal agents are available (vitamin K for warfarin, idarucizumab for dabigatran, fresh frozen plasma), use per protocol. Do not withhold hemorrhage control measures awaiting reversal.

<section id="evacuation-and-handoff">

## Evacuation and Handoff

Once hemorrhage is controlled, safe evacuation is the next priority.

### Preparation for Transport

1. **Stabilize dressings:** Ensure tourniquets and pressure dressings are secure and will not shift during movement.
2. **Monitor constantly:** Continue to assess pulse, breathing, and mental status.
3. **Notify receiving facility** of tourniquet application time, amount of fluid given, and current vital signs.
4. **Keep the patient NPO** (nothing by mouth) in case surgery is needed.
5. **Document everything:** Time of injury, interventions performed, times applied, fluid administered, vital signs.

### Communication to Next Provider

Clearly communicate:
- **Tourniquet application time** (write it on the tourniquet)
- **Type and location of hemorrhage control** (tourniquet vs direct pressure vs packing)
- **Hemostatic agents used** (if any)
- **Fluids administered** (volume and type)
- **Current vital signs and shock status**
- **Allergies and medications** (if known)

:::danger
Failure to communicate tourniquet time or location can result in missed or delayed evacuation to surgery, risking tissue loss or death.
:::

## Summary

Hemorrhage control follows a systematic approach: assess, apply tourniquets or direct pressure, pack wounds with hemostatic agents, manage junctional and truncal bleeding, resuscitate cautiously, and evacuate urgently. The key to survival is early recognition, rapid intervention, and escalation when needed.

In austere environments, you may be the difference between life and death. Mastery of these techniques can save lives.
