## Overview

Before modern engines, animal-powered transport moved goods, people, and armies across continents. Different species suit different roles: horses excel at speed; oxen excel at sustained heavy pulling; mules are tough hybrids combining horse strength with donkey resilience; donkeys are compact and efficient on rough terrain.

Selecting the right animal, training it properly, maintaining health, and fitting appropriate tack are the foundations of reliable transport. A well-trained, healthy animal can work 20–30 years; poor selection and management result in failure and animal suffering.

<section id="loading-travel-stress">

## Loading, Unloading, and Travel Stress

Use this section when the animal is physically sound but gets tense, resistant, or exhausted during transport.

- Prepare the route first: level footing, non-slip ramp or ground, clear lighting, and as little noise as possible.
- Load slowly and predictably. Give the animal time to look, step, and settle before asking for the next move.
- Never force an animal through panic. If the animal balks, stop, reset, and check for pain, fear, or bad footing.
- Watch during travel for rapid breathing, heavy sweating, trembling, stumbling, head tossing, refusal to eat or drink, or colic signs.
- After unloading, let the animal stand quietly, drink in small amounts, cool down, and inspect for rubs, swelling, or hoof damage before returning to work.
- If the animal collapses, cannot breathe normally, or shows severe colic, treat it as a veterinary emergency.

:::tip
If the problem is not transport-specific and is really about feeding, housing, breeding, or herd setup, hand off to [Animal Husbandry & Veterinary](animal-husbandry.html) instead.

<section id="daily-care-checklist">

## Daily Draft Animal Care Checklist

Use this as a morning check before hitching and an evening check after work.

**Before work:**
- Water available; animal should drink before and during work
- Feed appropriate ration for today's work level (see [Feeding and Work Capacity](#feeding-endurance))
- Check gait at walk — no limping, head-bobbing, or reluctance
- Inspect hooves for cracks, loose shoes, stones, or thrush odor
- Check harness or saddle fit — no worn spots, correct tension, clean padding
- Look at eyes and ears — alert and tracking

**After work:**
- Cool down gradually — walk the last 10 minutes; do not unhitch a lathered animal
- Offer water in small amounts at first; allow full access once cooled
- Remove harness or saddle; check for rubbing, hair loss, or heat on back and shoulders
- Inspect legs for swelling, heat, or new cuts
- Feed main ration; adjust upward if work was heavy

**Weekly:**
- Pick out and clean hooves; check shoe nails and wear
- Assess body condition — ribs and spine should not be prominent
- Groom thoroughly; check skin for sores, parasites, or rain rot
- Inspect all tack for wear, cracked leather, or bent hardware
- Schedule farrier if shoes are due (every 4-8 weeks depending on work)

## Animal Selection: Horses, Mules, Donkeys, Oxen

**Horses**

**Physical traits:** 14–17 hands tall (1 hand = 4 inches), 800–1,200 lbs, sleek muscular build.

**Strengths:**
- Speed: 3–6 mph trotting sustained; 10–12 mph galloping for short bursts.
- Responsiveness: Sensitive to rider cues; ideal for cavalry, courier routes, rapid deployment.
- Endurance: 20–30 miles per day over rough terrain with a rider and baggage.

**Weaknesses:**
- Load capacity: 150–200 lbs pack (less than oxen); fragile legs/hooves.
- Terrain limitations: Poor on swampy ground; muddy hooves require constant attention.
- Temperament: Prey animals; easily spook; require experienced handlers.
- Lifespan: 25–30 years if well-maintained.

**Cost:** $300–$1,000 in pre-industrial societies (expensive; owned by military, merchants, nobility).

**Mules**

Hybrid of male donkey (jack) and female horse (mare). Inherit horse size from dam and donkey toughness from sire.

**Physical traits:** 14–16 hands, 800–1,000 lbs.

**Strengths:**
- Hybrid vigor: Hardier than horses; fewer diseases.
- Load capacity: 200–250 lbs pack; stronger than horses.
- Surefootedness: Excellent on steep, rocky terrain; strong hooves with better footing than horses.
- Temperament: More docile and intelligent than horses; less prone to panic.
- Lifespan: 30–40 years.

**Weaknesses:**
- Speed: Slower than horses (2–4 mph), less responsive to cues.
- Sterility: Mules are sterile (cannot breed); must be purchased; higher cost.
- Stubbornness: Will refuse dangerous paths; requires respect for their judgment (advantage in hazardous terrain).

**Cost:** $400–$1,500 (more expensive than horses; require breeding specialized bloodlines).

**Donkeys (Asses)**

**Physical traits:** 10–14 hands, 500–1,000 lbs (larger draft donkeys are 1,000+ lbs).

**Strengths:**
- Load capacity: 150–200 lbs (smaller than horses/mules, but incredibly efficient).
- Feed efficiency: Eat 20–30% less than horses; survive on poor-quality forage.
- Toughness: Excellent disease resistance; long lifespan (40–50 years).
- Temperament: Intelligent, cautious; refuse dangerous paths; excellent for untrained users.
- Cost: Cheapest of the draft animals ($100–$400).

**Weaknesses:**
- Speed: 2–3 mph; slowest of the options.
- Size: Unsuitable for mounted cavalry or rapid deployment.
- Perception: Often underestimated; historical prejudice limits usage in some cultures.

**Oxen**

Castrated male cattle (bullocks); 5–10 years old for prime draft work.

**Physical traits:** 14–16 hands, 1,200–2,000 lbs, muscular build.

**Strengths:**
- Load capacity: 200–300 lbs sustained; can drag 2,000+ lbs with wheels; strongest of all draft animals.
- Cost: Cheapest to acquire (agricultural byproduct; castrated bulls less valuable for meat).
- Feed efficiency: Eat rough forage (hay, straw); don't require grain if pasture available.
- Temperament: Docile, predictable; easy to train.
- Lifespan: 15–20 years in draft work.

**Weaknesses:**
- Speed: 2–3 mph; slowest option.
- Training: Takes 2–3 years to train; requires patient, experienced teamster.
- Unsuitable for mounted work or rapid response.

**Selection comparison:**

| **Metric** | **Horse** | **Mule** | **Donkey** | **Ox** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Load capacity | 150–200 lbs | 200–250 lbs | 150–200 lbs | 200–300 lbs |
| Speed | 3–6 mph | 2–4 mph | 2–3 mph | 2–3 mph |
| Cost | $$$ | $$$$ | $$ | $$ |
| Terrain versatility | Moderate | Excellent | Good | Poor (flat only) |
| Feed efficiency | Moderate | Good | Excellent | Very good |
| Lifespan | 25–30 years | 30–40 years | 40–50 years | 15–20 years |

## Harness and Tack Design

Harness must distribute the load evenly to avoid injury and maximize power transmission.

### Riding Tack

**Saddle types:**

| **Type** | **Weight** | **Comfort** | **Load Capacity** | **Cost** | **Use** |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Bareback pad | 5 lbs | Poor | 25 lbs | $ | Padding only; no support |
| English saddle | 15–20 lbs | Moderate | 40–60 lbs | $$ | Riding with minimal baggage |
| Western/stock saddle | 25–40 lbs | Good | 60–150 lbs | $$$ | Roping, long-distance riding, cargo |
| McClellan military saddle | 20–25 lbs | Moderate | 60–100 lbs | $$ | Military standard; two-point suspension |
| Pack saddle | 20–30 lbs | N/A | 200+ lbs | $$ | Cargo only; no rider |

**Saddle fit critical:** A saddle that sits too far forward pinches the withers (shoulder); too far back pinches the loins. Poor fit causes sore backs, permanent nerve damage, and lameness.

**Proper fit test:** Two fingers should fit between saddle and withers when cinched; no pressure on loin area. The saddle should sit level (not tilted forward or backward).

### Draft Harness

**Collar and hames:**

A **collar** distributes pulling force over the animal's shoulders. Ill-fitting collars cause sores and reduce pulling power.

```
Proper fit: 2 fingers space between collar and neck when lifted up
Too tight: Chokes animal; restricts breathing; reduces endurance
Too loose: Slips forward; pulls on mane; unstable traction
```

**Harness geometry:**

| **Component** | **Function** | **Proper Angle** |
|---|---|---|
| Traces (pull lines) | Connect collar to vehicle | 30–40° downward from horizontal to distribute load forward and down |
| Breaching strap | Control downhill braking | Contacts rump; prevents wagon from overrunning animal |
| Back strap | Stabilize harness | Sits behind withers; prevents harness from shifting |
| Bellyband/girth | Holds harness in place | Snug (2-finger space) around ribs |

**Multi-animal teams:**

For heavier loads, multiple animals are harnessed together:

```
Tandem (in-line): Animals hitch one in front of the other. Good for narrow paths; each animal pulls full weight.

Side-by-side: Animals hitch next to each other. Good for wide vehicles; load shared between animals.

Example: 4-ox team pulling a plow
Each ox pulls ~30% of the load (100% / 4, with slight friction loss = 30%).
Total pulling force: 4 × 1,000 lbs per ox = 4,000 lbs (minus friction, ~3,500 lbs net).
```

## Shoeing and Health Monitoring

**Hooves wear:** At typical walking speeds (2–4 mph), working animals wear out their hoof walls in 4–8 weeks. Regular shoeing is essential.

### Shoeing Schedule

**Light work** (riding, light draft): Re-shoe every 6–8 weeks.

**Heavy work** (freight hauling, farming): Re-shoe every 4–6 weeks; inspect every 2 weeks.

**Extreme conditions** (rocky terrain, very heavy loads): Re-shoe every 3–4 weeks; pad the shoe for cushioning.

**Shoeing process:**

1. **Remove old shoe**: Drive out nails; carefully pry off shoe without damaging hoof.
2. **Trim hoof**: Rasp (file) the bottom and sides to proper angle (45–55° from ground, depending on breed and terrain).
3. **Fit new shoe**: Shoe is heated, shaped to match hoof contour, and nailed on with 6–8 nails per side.
4. **Clinch nails**: Nail ends are bent over and tapped flat against the hoof wall.

**Cost:** $100–$300 per animal per shoeing (material + labor), depending on region.

### Health Monitoring

**Daily checks:**

- **Lameness**: Observe gait; any unevenness indicates pain (hoof, leg, or joint injury).
- **Leg swelling**: Palpate (feel) legs for heat or swelling; indicates inflammation, strain, or infection.
- **Coat condition**: Rough, dull coat indicates poor nutrition or disease.
- **Eyes and ears**: Alert, responsive eyes; ears tracking sounds. Lethargy or drooping ears suggest illness.
- **Appetite**: Refusal to eat is a red flag for colic, infection, or systemic illness.

**Common ailments:**

| **Condition** | **Signs** | **Cause** | **Treatment** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Colic (belly pain) | Lying down, rolling, distress | Constipation, gas, twisted intestine | Rest, walk, water, veterinary evacuation if severe |
| Laminitis (hoof inflammation) | Lameness, heat in hooves, reluctance to move | Overfeeding grain, toxemia, injury | Shoe with bar support, reduce grain, cold water soaks |
| Thrush (hoof rot) | Black, foul-smelling discharge from frog | Wet, muddy conditions; poor hoof hygiene | Clean daily, apply iodine or copper sulfate |
| Azoturia (tying-up) | Stiffness, hindquarter paralysis after work | High grain diet without work; exhaustion | Rest, walk gently, ice, reduce grain |
| Spavin (hock joint arthritis) | Stiffness, lameness in hind leg | Age, heavy work, conformation | Rest, anti-inflammatory, reduce load |

## Feeding and Work Capacity

Draft animals must be fed differently based on work level. Underfeeding causes weight loss, weakness, and failure; overfeeding wastes resources and can cause colic.

### Feed Calculations

**Maintenance**: An animal at rest requires 1.5–2% of body weight in dry matter daily (forage-based).

```
Example: 1,000-lb horse at maintenance
Daily dry matter = 1,000 × 0.02 = 20 lbs

If feeding hay (10% moisture, 90% dry matter):
Daily hay = 20 ÷ 0.9 = 22 lbs fresh hay
```

**Work load**: Each hour of moderate work (trotting, light draft) requires additional 5–10% of maintenance calories.

```
Example: Same 1,000-lb horse working 8 hours per day (moderate draft)
Maintenance: 20 lbs dry matter
Work bonus: 20 × 0.08 (8 hours) = 1.6 lbs additional dry matter
Total: 21.6 lbs dry matter ≈ 24 lbs fresh hay
```

**Grain supplementation:** Working animals benefit from grain (oats, barley, corn) to meet energy demands. 1 lb of grain ≈ 3 lbs of hay in energy content.

```
Example: Horse needing 2 extra lbs dry matter for heavy work
Feed 2 ÷ 3 = 0.67 lbs grain (≈ 1 lb fresh), plus hay adjustment downward by same amount.

Sample ration:
- 20 lbs hay (maintenance)
- 2 lbs oats (work supplement)
- Total: 22 lbs forage-equivalent per day
```

### Work Capacity

**Sustainable work:** 4–6 hours per day, 5–6 days per week. Gives animals adequate recovery time.

**Daily distance:**

| **Terrain** | **Speed** | **Hours** | **Daily Distance** | **Days per Week** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Flat road | 4 mph | 6 | 24 miles | 5–6 |
| Rolling terrain | 3 mph | 6 | 18 miles | 4–5 |
| Mountain/rough | 2 mph | 5 | 10 miles | 3–4 |
| City/stop-and-go | 2 mph | 8 | 16 miles | 5–6 |

**Long-distance journeys** (weeks or months): Reduce daily distance by 20–30%, add rest days every 7 days.

```
Example: 30-day journey covering 300 miles
Average: 10 miles/day with 2–3 complete rest days
Actual routine: 12 miles/day, 25 days traveling, 5 days rest

Every 7 days: Take a day off to graze, recover, groom, and inspect.
```

:::warning
Heat exhaustion is a major cause of draft animal failure in summer. Provide water at least every 2–3 hours; allow grazing breaks in shade. Never work animals in extreme heat (>90°F) at full capacity; reduce load by 30–50% and extend travel to cooler hours (early morning, late evening).
:::

## Training Methods

**Young animals** (2–4 years old for horses/mules, 3–5 years for oxen) are broken to harness through patient, incremental training.

### Horse/Mule Training

**Phase 1** (1–2 months): Accustom to handler, saddle, bridle, and simple commands.

- Lead with halter; reward with treats and voice encouragement.
- Gradually introduce saddle, bridle, and light weight on back.
- Practice walk, trot, whoa (stop) commands.

**Phase 2** (2–4 months): Introduce rider and riding cues.

- Ride in enclosed area or very familiar terrain; avoid startling stimuli.
- Practice turning (leg pressure), stopping (rein pressure), and responding to voice commands.
- Build confidence with many short sessions (15–30 min) rather than few long sessions.

**Phase 3** (4–6 months): Draft work introduction.

- Hitch to light wagon or sled; practice pulling, turning, stopping.
- Gradually increase load over weeks.
- Always pair with experienced animal if possible (young animal learns by imitation).

### Ox Training

**Oxen require 2–3 years of training** (much longer than horses) because of their size, strength, and slow cognitive processing.

**Phase 1** (Years 1–2): Accustom to yoke, basic commands (gee [turn right], haw [turn left], whoa [stop]).

- Start with light yoke; practice walking with handler alongside.
- Pair young ox with trained ox; learn by imitation.
- Train for 2–3 hours daily, 5 days a week.

**Phase 2** (Year 2–3): Light draft work.

- Hitch trained pair (young + experienced) to light wagon.
- Gradually increase load.
- Practice response to commands without physical guidance.

**Three-year result:** Pair of oxen can work reliably at 2–3 mph for 8+ hours daily.

## Pack Saddle Fitting

Pack saddles distribute cargo weight over the animal's back. Improper fit causes sores, back injuries, and reduced capacity.

**Pack saddle types:**

| **Type** | **Best Animal** | **Weight Range** | **Load Distribution** | **Cost** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Aparejo (traditional Spanish) | Mule, horse | 200–300 lbs | Distributed across frame | $$$ |
| Pack frame (military, modern) | Horse, mule | 150–250 lbs | Concentrated on two points | $$ |
| Packsaddle tree (canvas/wood) | Donkey, small mule | 100–200 lbs | Distributed | $$ |
| Cargo net/throw-over | Any | 100–150 lbs | Simple, minimal structure | $ |

### Fitting Process

**Step 1:** Clean and dry the animal's back.

**Step 2:** Place saddle pad (4–6 inches thick felt or wool) on back; ensure no wrinkles.

**Step 3:** Place saddle; check that it sits level (not tilted).

**Step 4:** Tighten cinch gradually. Final tightness: 2-finger space under belly; snug but not choked.

**Step 5:** Load cargo symmetrically (equal weight left and right); total weight centered over withers and back (not loins or croup).

**Test:** Lead animal at walk; watch for shifting, unusual gait, or discomfort. A well-fitted load allows natural movement.

### Cargo Management

**Weight distribution:**

Maximum safe load = 20–25% of animal's body weight.

```
Example: 500-lb mule
Max load = 500 × 0.25 = 125 lbs

Laden mule weight: 500 + 125 = 625 lbs
```

**Long-distance packing** (weeks or months): Reduce load to 15–20% of body weight; provide 1 rest day per 5 days of travel.

```
Example: 6-week journey, 300 miles
Distance per day: 300 ÷ 30 days = 10 miles average
With 1 rest day per 5 days: 4 travel days + 1 rest day per cycle
4 × 10 = 40 miles per 5-day cycle = 8 miles average per travel day (moderate pace)

Load: 500 × 0.2 = 100 lbs (reduced from 125 lbs for long duration)
```

:::tip
Rotate pack animals every week if possible (give each animal 1 week off per month). This prevents chronic injury and extends useful lifespan from 15 to 25+ years. A sound animal is more valuable long-term than pushing it to failure.
:::

## Integrating Teams: Logistics and Deployment

**Small team** (2–4 animals): Direct owner control; minimal support staff.

**Large caravan** (20+ animals): Requires:
- 1 head teamster (oversees all animals).
- 1 helper per 5 animals (handling, repairs, veterinary care).
- Rest days every 7–10 days to inspect hooves, rest animals, and perform repairs.

**Multi-day journey itinerary:**

```
Day 1: 15 miles (acclimatization)
Days 2–5: 18 miles/day (normal pace)
Day 6: Rest day (hoof inspection, reshoe if needed, forage, water)
Days 7–11: 18 miles/day
Day 12: Rest day
Pattern repeats

Total progress: 5-day work + 1-day rest = 12 days for ~90 miles
```

**Animal rotation:** If surplus animals available (e.g., 10 animals for 5-day route), rotate animals:

```
Week 1: Team A travels (Team B rests)
Week 2: Team B travels (Team A rests)
Alternation allows animals to recover fully, extending lifespan and maintaining condition.
```
