## Overview

When food supply is disrupted, communities must transition quickly from "eat what you want" to "eat only what is allocated." Poorly managed rationing causes panic hoarding, malnutrition, social conflict, and even deaths. This guide provides practical methods for establishing calorie-fair, micronutrient-conscious rationing that keeps people healthy while stretching limited food supplies for weeks or months.

:::warning
Rationing failure in the first week creates hoarding behavior, black markets, and loss of community cohesion. Early, transparent, fair allocation prevents chaos and maintains morale. Delay and uncertainty are more dangerous than scarcity.
:::

## Part 1: Caloric Minimums by Activity Level

### Daily Caloric Needs (Baseline)

Basal metabolic rate (BMR)—energy needed at rest—varies by body size and age:

| Population Group | Weight | Activity Level | Daily Calories |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Children** | 40–60 lbs | Light | 1,400–1,800 |
| **Adolescents** | 100–130 lbs | Moderate–Heavy | 2,200–3,000 |
| **Adult women** | 130–150 lbs | Light | 1,800–2,200 |
| **Adult women** | 130–150 lbs | Moderate | 2,200–2,600 |
| **Adult women** | 130–150 lbs | Heavy labor | 2,600–3,200 |
| **Adult men** | 170–190 lbs | Light | 2,200–2,600 |
| **Adult men** | 170–190 lbs | Moderate | 2,600–3,200 |
| **Adult men** | 170–190 lbs | Heavy labor | 3,200–4,000 |
| **Elderly** (65+) | Varies | Light | 1,600–2,000 |
| **Pregnant women** | Variable | Any | +300–500 cal/day |
| **Lactating women** | Variable | Any | +500–800 cal/day |

### Activity Levels Defined

- **Light:** Sedentary work (administration, craft, teaching, watch duty)
- **Moderate:** Average farm/household work (gardening, food prep, maintenance, light field work)
- **Heavy:** Intense labor (field harvest, hauling water, mining, construction, intense foraging)

### Emergency Minimums

**Absolute survival** requires ~1,200–1,600 calories/day for most adults, but this is unsustainable beyond 2–4 weeks. Below these thresholds, weight loss accelerates and disease susceptibility rises.

**Recommended minimum rationing levels:**
- **Short-term (1–4 weeks):** 1,800–2,000 cal/day for adults (maintains function)
- **Medium-term (1–3 months):** 1,500–1,700 cal/day (modest deficit; weight loss ~1 lb/week)
- **Long-term (>3 months):** 1,800–2,000 cal/day (sustainable without deterioration)

:::info-box
A 170-lb adult losing 1 lb/week can sustain 3–4 months of rationing before becoming severely underweight and malnourished. Plan for alternative food sources (foraging, hunting, new harvest) before entering a 3+ month deficit.
:::

## Part 2: Micronutrient-Priority Food Lists

When calories are limited, prioritize foods that deliver nutrition density.

### Highest-Priority Foods

| Food | Calories/lb | Key Nutrients | Storage Life | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Legumes (dried)** | 1,500 | Protein, iron, B vitamins | 5–10 years | Pair with grains for complete protein |
| **Grains (whole)** | 1,600 | Carbohydrate, B vitamins, fiber | 5–30 years | If refined (white rice), add vitamins |
| **Fats/oils** | 4,000 | Calories, fat-soluble vitamins | 1–5 years | Dense calorie source; prevents deficiency |
| **Dried fruit** | 1,200 | Carbohydrate, fiber, minerals | 2–5 years | Vitamin C (modest), potassium |
| **Canned vegetables** | 150 | Vitamins A, C, minerals | 3–10 years | Retain 50–80% of fresh nutritional value |
| **Eggs** (fresh or powdered) | 150 | Protein, B12, choline, vitamin A | 2–4 weeks (fresh); 10 years (powdered) | Complete protein |
| **Dairy (powdered)** | 1,600 | Calcium, protein, B vitamins | 5–10 years | Milk powder or cheese |
| **Fish oil or cod liver** | 4,000 | Vitamin A, D, omega-3 | 1–3 years | Prevents vitamin A/D deficiency |
| **Salt** | 0 | Sodium, iodine | Indefinite | Essential for health and preservation |
| **Sugar/honey** | 1,750 | Quick carbohydrate | Indefinite (honey) | Low nutrition, but morale boost |

### Secondary Priority

- Canned meat (higher calorie density, protein) – 500–800 cal/lb
- Nuts & seeds (if available) – 2,400 cal/lb
- Vinegar or fermented foods – microbiome support, flavor

### Foods to Ration Carefully

- **Fresh produce (if stored root cellar):** Allocate extra to at-risk populations; spoilage risk
- **Nuts/expensive items:** Hoard tendency; distribute in small, controlled portions
- **Sugar/treats:** Morale is important, but don't waste calories on pure treats; prioritize nutrition

## Part 3: Weekly & Daily Rationing Schedules

### Sample Weekly Ration (Per Person, Moderate Activity)

**Goal:** 1,800 calories/day, basic micronutrient coverage

| Item | Qty/Week | Cal/day | Nutrients |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Grains** (rice, wheat, oats) | 3 lbs | 900 | B vitamins, carbs, fiber |
| **Legumes** (beans, lentils) | 1.5 lbs | 300 | Protein, iron, B vitamins |
| **Oil/fat** | 0.3 lbs (1.3 oz/day) | 300 | Calories, fat-soluble vitamins |
| **Dried fruit or dried vegetables** | 0.5 lbs | 150 | Carbs, minerals, some vitamins |
| **Canned vegetables or fresh (if available)** | 2 cans or 3 lbs fresh | 100 | Vitamins A, C, minerals |
| **Salt** | 0.2 oz | 0 | Sodium, iodine (if iodized) |
| **Sugar/honey** (optional) | 0.5 lbs | 0–150 | Morale, quick carbs |
| **TOTAL** | — | 1,750–1,900 | Adequate |

### Daily Distribution Method

**Breakfast:** Grain + fat + small portion of dried fruit
- 1 cup oatmeal (cooked) with 1 tsp oil
- Handful of dried fruit
- Hot water (tea, if available)

**Lunch:** Grain + legume + canned vegetable
- 1 cup rice with 1/2 cup beans
- Canned vegetables (tomato, carrot, greens)
- Small amount of salt for flavor

**Dinner:** Grain + legume + fat + dried vegetable
- Grain-and-bean stew with oil for cooking
- Root vegetable (if fresh available) or dried vegetable
- Hot water

### Weekly Ration Cards

Print or hand-write ration cards for each household:

```
═════════════════════════════════════════════════
WEEKLY RATION CARD — Week of [DATE]
Household: [NAME]    Size: [#] people    Activity: [Light/Moderate/Heavy]

ALLOCATED RATIONS:
  ☐ Grains (rice/wheat/oats): ___ lbs
  ☐ Legumes (beans/lentils): ___ lbs
  ☐ Oil/fat: ___ oz
  ☐ Dried fruit/veg: ___ lbs
  ☐ Canned goods: ___ items
  ☐ Salt (iodized): 0.2 oz
  ☐ Sugar (optional): ___ oz

COLLECTED: ___ [Administrator initials]
DATE: ___

NOTES:
• Rations are not tradeable
• Each family cooks their own meals
• Any surplus returns Friday for redistribution
═════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

## Part 4: At-Risk Populations & Priority Allocation

Some groups have higher nutritional needs. Allocate food strategically.

### Tier 1: Highest Priority

| Group | Why | Allocation Increase |
|---|---|---|
| **Pregnant women** | Fetal development, blood volume expansion | +300–500 cal/day |
| **Lactating women** | Milk production demands | +500–800 cal/day |
| **Children <5** | Brain development, rapid growth | +400–600 cal/day above baseline |
| **Adolescents 10–18** | Rapid growth, high activity | Adult allocation or +20% |

### Tier 2: Secondary Priority

- **Elderly 70+** with chronic illness (infection risk if malnourished)
- **Laborers in heavy roles** (field harvest, hauling water) — +400 cal/day
- **Nursing mothers** (lactating, see above)

### Tier 3: Standard Allocation

- Healthy adults in moderate activity
- Healthy children 5–10
- Elderly in good health

### Tier 4: Minimum Allocation (If Severe Shortage)

- Non-working adults, sedentary roles (1,200–1,400 cal/day)
- Accept temporary weight loss to preserve working population

:::danger
Do NOT reduce rations for infants or young children, even if shortage is severe. Malnutrition in early childhood causes permanent cognitive and physical damage. Reduce adult rations before reducing children's allocations.
:::

## Part 5: Inventory Management & Distribution

### Daily Inventory Ledger

Track food in and out:

| Date | Item | Qty In | Qty Out | Balance | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Feb 20 | Rice | 100 lbs | — | 100 | Received shipment |
| Feb 20 | Rice | — | 35 | 65 | Weekly rations to 35 households |
| Feb 21 | Beans | — | 8 | 42 | Daily kitchen use for communal meals |

### Secure Storage

- **Lock supplies in central location** (warehouse, cellar, church)
- **Assign 2+ people to manage** (prevent theft, ensure transparency)
- **Weekly count** of remaining inventory
- **Monthly projection:** "At current consumption, supplies last X weeks"

### Distribution Method

**Weekly (Preferred):**
- Set a distribution day (e.g., Friday)
- People come to distribution point with ration cards
- Pre-weighed/pre-packaged portions handed out
- Recipient signs card confirming receipt
- Keeps portions fresh, prevents spoilage

**Daily (If supplies unstable):**
- Administer 1-day ration each morning
- Prevents hoarding, encourages just-in-time consumption
- Higher labor, but tighter control

### Anti-Hoarding Measures

1. **No accumulation:** Rations distributed only for current period
2. **Ration cards non-transferable:** No trading or selling (publicly stated, enforced)
3. **Transparent allocation:** Publish how many rations each household receives (by size)
4. **Spot checks:** Random checks of homes for hoarded food (conducted respectfully)
5. **Community pressure:** Publicly celebrate compliance; shame hoarding (carefully, to maintain cohesion)

## Part 6: Psychological Coping with Hunger

Rationing is stressful. Communities with poor morale become fragmented and may resist or hoard.

### Morale-Boosting Strategies

- **Communal meals:** 1–2 meals/week cooked together. Improves nutrition (economies of scale), builds solidarity
- **Flavor emphasis:** Use salt, herbs (dried), small amounts of sugar to make rations taste acceptable
- **Variety:** Rotate grain types (rice, wheat, oats, corn) weekly to prevent monotony
- **Celebration foods:** One meal/month with a small treat (jam, honey, extra oil) to mark time and maintain hope
- **Transparent communication:** Weekly announcements of food situation—this builds trust
- **Work incentives:** Extra rations for community work (harvesting, repairs, cooking) acknowledges contribution

### Managing Theft & Conflict

- Hunger drives people to desperation. Theft increases as calorie deficits deepen.
- **Social response > punishment:** Understanding that theft signals hunger and need prevents escalation
- **Increase rations if widespread theft:** May indicate baseline is too low
- **Community courts:** Peer judgment (if theft occurs) often more accepted than authority punishment

:::tip
A community with adequate nutrition, transparent leadership, and shared purpose maintains cohesion. One with hunger, secrecy, and perceived unfairness fragments. Invest in morale as much as rationing.
:::

## Part 7: Transitioning from Rationing to Sufficiency

### Indicators You Can Ease Rationing

1. **Local harvest begins** (spring gardens producing, trees fruiting)
2. **New protein sources secured** (hunting, fishing, livestock births)
3. **Caloric deficit is no longer growing** (consumption is sustainable)
4. **At-risk populations show stable health** (no new deficiency cases, weight stable)

### Transition Timeline (Typical)

| Phase | Duration | Rationing Level | Goal |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Crisis rationing** | Weeks 1–4 | 1,200–1,400 cal/day | Prevent panic, stabilize consumption |
| **Extended rationing** | Weeks 5–12 | 1,500–1,700 cal/day | Bridge gap to first harvest |
| **Supplemented rations** | Weeks 13+ | 1,800–2,000 cal/day | Mix stored food + new production |
| **Transition out** | Months 4–6 | Open access (managed) | Return to normal eating patterns |

### Easing Rations Gradually

Don't suddenly remove rationing when food becomes available—this causes malabsorption (especially in people who were severely deficient). Increase calories slowly:

- **Week 1 of transition:** +200 calories
- **Week 2:** +300 calories
- **Week 3:** +400 calories
- Continue until fully unrestricted

### Identifying People Who Took Most Harm

- **Weight loss >15 lbs:** May take 4–6 weeks to regain
- **Tooth decay, hair loss:** Long-term effects; nutrition now prevents further damage
- **Edema in elderly:** May take weeks to resolve as protein intake rises
- **Children showing growth stunting:** Catch-up growth happens, but previous growth is permanently lost

## Part 8: Contingency Scenarios

### If Supplies Run Out Before Harvest

- Shift to **foraged foods** (<a href="../foraging.html">Foraging for Wild Foods</a>)
- **Hunting/fishing** increases urgently
- **Reduce rations by 10–20%** to extend remaining stores
- **Communal meals become mandatory** (better calorie utilization)
- **Temporary work-for-food programs** (people labor in fields for supplemental rations)

### If Unexpected Population Influx

- **Rapid recalculation:** Divide remaining food by new population size
- **Intake period:** Define how long newcomers receive minimum rations (2 weeks?) before full access
- **New arrivals contribute labor:** They eat only if they work
- **Communication:** Explain fairly that new people reduce per-capita rations; prevents resentment

### If Rationing Fails (Hoarding, Theft, Corruption)

- **Reboot with transparency:** Public inventory count, all ration cards reissued
- **Leadership change:** If distributors are found to be corrupt, replace immediately
- **Return to daily distribution:** Tighter control, less opportunity for gaming the system
- **Community courts:** Open, transparent judging of theft or distribution violations

## Key Takeaways

1. **Act early:** Establish rationing in first week of shortage, not after panic
2. **Be fair:** Transparent allocation by household size/need prevents conflict
3. **Prioritize calories, then micronutrients:** Use dense foods (grains, legumes, fats) for survival; supplement with vegetables/fruit when possible
4. **Protect the vulnerable:** Children, pregnant women, lactating mothers, elderly need extra nutrition
5. **Maintain morale:** Communal meals, variety, and honest communication prevent hoarding and fragmentation
6. **Plan exit:** From week one, know how you'll transition to normal eating (first harvest, hunting success, etc.)
7. **Keep records:** Inventory, distribution, and at-risk population tracking prevent errors and detect theft

## Part 10: Black Market Prevention Strategies

As soon as rationing begins, an underground food economy emerges. People trade rations, hoard, or steal. Preventing black markets requires enforcement, fairness, and adequate rations.

### Why Black Markets Form

**Causes:**
- **Insufficient calories**: If rations are below 1,500 kcal/day, people are desperate and will risk trading rations for survival
- **Perceived unfairness**: If people believe allocations are biased, they lose faith and turn to black markets
- **Hoarding temptation**: If someone perceives scarcity, they hoard to prevent future starvation
- **Inequality**: If officials receive larger rations while poor people starve, resentment drives black market activity

### Prevention Strategies

**1. Keep Rations Adequate (>1,500 kcal/day)**

When baseline rations are adequate, the desperation motive disappears. People will not risk their ration card for a few extra calories if they are already fed.

**2. Make Allocation Completely Transparent**

Publish exactly how rations are calculated and verify math so everyone trusts the system.

**3. Officials Eat the Same Rations**

When people see leaders sharing hardship equally, support for rationing increases dramatically.

**4. Implement Anti-Hoarding Enforcement**

Monthly surprise inspections detect illegal hoarding. Legal reserve is 1 week extra; anything more is confiscated and the family loses 1 week of rations (severe penalty).

**5. Establish Informant Networks**

Encourage anonymous reporting of black market activity with small rewards. Confidentiality is protected.

**6. Enforce Harsh Penalties**

- **First offense**: Loss of rations for 1 week
- **Second offense**: Loss of rations for 2 weeks + name posted publicly
- **Third offense**: Loss of rations for 1 month + possible exile

## Part 11: Spoilage Accounting and Reserve Planning

Food spoils naturally. Accurate accounting prevents accusations of theft and enables realistic supply projections.

### Spoilage Loss Rates by Food Type

| Food Type | Monthly Loss | Annual Loss |
|---|---|---|
| **Grain (cool, dry, sealed)** | 0.5–1% | 6–12% |
| **Legumes (cool, dry, sealed)** | 1–2% | 12–24% |
| **Root vegetables (cellar, humid)** | 3–8% | 36–96% |
| **Oil (cool, dark, sealed)** | 2–5% | 24–60% |
| **Canned goods (cool, dry)** | 0.5–1% | 6–12% |

### Planning with Spoilage

Example: 5,000 lbs grain, 100 people, 1% monthly spoilage, 1,500 lbs consumption/month

| Month | Start | Spoilage | Consumption | End |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 5,000 | 50 | 1,500 | 3,450 |
| 2 | 3,450 | 35 | 1,500 | 1,915 |
| 3 | 1,915 | 19 | 1,500 | 396 |

Grain lasts 3 months. By month 3, secure new supply or increase production.

### Spoilage Documentation

Record all losses to prevent theft accusations:

| Date | Food | Amount | Reason | Witnesses | Disposal |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Feb 18 | Grain | 5 lbs | Mold detected | Chen, Wong | Composted |
| Feb 20 | Oil | 0.5 lbs | Container leak | Lee, Smith | Discarded |

## Part 12: Nutritional Validation & Monitoring

Even if calorie targets are met, malnutrition can occur if nutrient ratios are wrong.

### Nutrient Targets (for 1,500 cal/day ration)

| Nutrient | Requirement | Deficiency Timeline | Key Sources |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Protein** | 50+ g | 2–4 weeks | Legumes, powdered milk, nuts |
| **Vitamin C** | 75+ mg | 8–12 weeks | Dried fruit, sprouted seeds, canned tomato |
| **Iron** | 8+ mg | 4–8 weeks | Beans, lentils, dark vegetables |
| **Calcium** | 400+ mg | 12+ weeks | Powdered milk, leafy greens, bones |
| **Iodine** | 150 mcg | 12+ weeks | Iodized salt only |

Before implementing a ration plan, verify it meets these targets.

### Monitoring for Deficiency Signs

| Symptom | Deficiency | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Bleeding gums, poor healing** | Vitamin C (scurvy) | Add dried fruit, sprouted seeds |
| **Extreme fatigue, shortness of breath** | Iron (anemia) | Increase legumes, dark greens |
| **Bone/muscle pain** | Vitamin D/Calcium | Increase sunlight; add dairy |
| **Swollen neck, lethargy** | Iodine (goiter) | Verify salt is iodized; use seaweed |
