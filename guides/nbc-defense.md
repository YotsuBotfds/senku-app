## 1\. Nuclear Survival

### Fallout Basics

Nuclear fallout consists of irradiated dust and debris created by the detonation. This is the primary survival threat outside the blast radius, especially in the first 24-48 hours.

:::info-box
**What is Fallout?** Neutron activation of soil, building materials, and air creates radioactive isotopes. These particles range from large (deposit locally) to extremely fine (travel hundreds of kilometers). Most dangerous isotopes have half-lives of days to weeks.
:::

#### Rule of Seven (Decay Rate)

For every seven-fold increase in time after detonation, radiation intensity decreases by a factor of ten:

-   **7 hours after:** 1/10 of initial intensity
-   **49 hours (7²) after:** 1/100 of initial intensity
-   **343 hours (7³, ~2 weeks) after:** 1/1000 of initial intensity
-   **2400+ hours (~14 weeks) after:** 1/10,000 of initial intensity

**Critical Timeline:** After 2 weeks of sheltering, radiation exposure drops to approximately 1/1000th of initial levels in most areas. This makes 14 days the minimum safe sheltering period for areas with significant fallout.

:::tip
Use the Rule of Seven to predict radiation decay without instruments. If you shelter in place for 2 weeks, multiply any initial estimate by 1/1000 to find expected radiation levels—this provides rough guidance for when it may be safe to emerge for brief outdoor tasks.
:::

### Shelter Requirements

Protection from fallout is measured in Protection Factors (PF). A PF of 40 means you receive 1/40th the external radiation dose. Effective sheltering requires 10 Half-Value Layers (HVL) of shielding material for a PF of approximately 1,000.

:::warning
Inadequate shielding during peak fallout hours (first 24-48 hours) dramatically increases radiation dose. If you cannot achieve PF 40 minimum, shelter deeper underground or in basement rather than ground-level rooms. Even basement shielding is far better than no shelter.
:::

### Expedient Shelter Construction

#### Basement Improvement

If a basement is available, it provides a starting point. Increase protection by:

-   Sealing doors with plastic and duct tape
-   Stacking earth, books, or other dense materials against exterior walls to 2-3 feet height
-   Sealing basement windows with plastic and board
-   Using the most interior corner as primary shelter
-   Creating ventilation without outside air entry (covered basement window, sealed ductwork)

#### Trench Shelter

For areas without basements:

1.  Dig a trench 3-4 feet deep, 2.5 feet wide, long enough for your group
2.  Construct a roof of logs or branches (minimum 12 inches diameter)
3.  Cover roof with plastic sheeting
4.  Layer earth over roof at least 18 inches thick (preferably 24-36 inches)
5.  Create entrance with multiple bends to block radiation
6.  Install ventilation shaft at opposite end from entrance, covered with plastic to limit fallout entry
7.  Reinforce walls with branches or boards if soil is unstable

#### KFM (Kearny Fallout Meter) Construction

An improvised dosimeter that requires no batteries or electronics. Instructions:

1.  **Container:** Empty tin can (soup can size), rinsed thoroughly
2.  **Leaf electrodes:** Cut aluminum foil into thin strips 1 cm × 8 cm
3.  **Support:** Suspend one foil strip from the can's rim using thread, allowing it to hang freely inside without touching sides
4.  **Charging:** Remove the cap and use a plastic comb (rubbed on wool or hair) to charge the foil negatively, bringing it near the suspended foil to induce repulsion
5.  **Calibration:** Mark the initial position of the suspended foil. As radioactive ionization occurs, the foil will gradually lose charge and drop
6.  **Reading:** The distance the foil drops (measured with a ruler) indicates accumulated ionization dose
7.  **Interpretation:** Requires pre-calibration or reference data; indicates relative dose accumulation over time

:::warning
**KFM Limitations:** The KFM is a crude device and requires specific calibration to interpret readings as actual dose. However, it provides relative measurement of radiation exposure and confirms whether radiation levels are dropping (foil movement stops) over time, which is critical information.
:::

### Decontamination Procedures

Decontamination immediately after exposure removes 80-90% of external contamination:

1.  **Remove outer clothing:** This single step removes ~90% of external contamination. Bag clothing in plastic and set aside for decay
2.  **Shower or wash:** Use soap and warm water. Wash hair multiple times. Pay attention to skin folds and areas with cuts
3.  **Dry with clean towel:** Avoid reusing towels across multiple people
4.  **Don clean clothing**
5.  **Monitor exposure:** Use any available dosimeter or KFM. If contamination persists, repeat washing

:::tip
Decontamination is most effective in the first 24 hours after exposure. Prioritize this immediately—removing contaminated clothing alone eliminates 90% of the hazard. Even with limited water, remove clothing and splash wash at minimum before entering living space.
:::

### Potassium Iodide (KI)

Potassium iodide protects the thyroid gland ONLY. It is not a general radiation protective agent and must be taken before or immediately after exposure to be effective.

-   **Adults:** 130 mg per dose
-   **Children (3-18 years):** 65 mg per dose
-   **Infants and young children:** 32.5 mg per dose
-   **Timing:** Most effective within 24 hours of exposure; some protection up to 3 hours after
-   **Frequency:** Once per day for 7-10 days after exposure
-   **Limitations:** Does NOT protect against internal ingestion of other radioactive isotopes or external whole-body radiation

**Storage:** KI loses potency over time. Store in cool, dark, dry location. If tablets are over 5 years old, obtain fresh supply. Do not use if color has changed significantly.

:::note
Potassium iodide is not a catch-all radiation protection. It only blocks iodine-131 uptake by the thyroid. Many other radioactive isotopes (cesium, strontium, cobalt) pass through the body and deposit in bones and organs—KI provides no protection against these. Use it to protect the thyroid specifically, but do not rely on it as primary defense.
:::

### Long-Term Nuclear Survival

#### Food Chain Contamination

After fallout has decayed to safe levels, continue avoiding foods that may contain long-lived isotopes:

-   **First 2 weeks:** Do not consume outdoor-grown foods. Use stored food or greenhouse-grown food only
-   **Weeks 2-6:** Root vegetables grown indoors are safe. Avoid aboveground crops and leafy greens
-   **Leafy greens:** Do not consume outdoor-grown leafy greens for minimum one growing season; they concentrate radioactive material
-   **Milk:** Dairy animals consuming contaminated grass produce contaminated milk. Use stored milk or indoor-grown hay for livestock
-   **Water:** Deep well water is safest; surface water may be contaminated. Distillation is recommended

#### Safe Farming After Fallout

Gradual reintroduction of food production after extended sheltering:

1.  Test soil with KFM if available, or establish safety through observation of animal behavior
2.  Begin with underground greenhouse production to avoid aerial contamination
3.  Peel all root vegetables and discard skins
4.  First year above-ground crops: consider emergency use only (very high risk of contamination)
5.  Second-year crops: acceptable risk for non-leafy vegetables after rainfall has further reduced fallout
6.  Livestock: Provide stored feed (hay, grain) for minimum 30 days before consuming

#### Well Water vs Surface Water

-   **Deep wells (200+ feet):** Most protected from fallout; use immediately after safety confirmed
-   **Shallow wells (under 50 feet):** May be contaminated initially; wait 2-3 weeks minimum
-   **Springs and streams:** Highly contaminated from fallout; do not use for 2+ weeks
-   **Rainwater:** Contaminated during and immediately after fallout event; avoid collection first 48 hours
-   **Decontamination:** Distillation removes most particulate contamination; boiling does not remove dissolved radioactive ions

## 2\. Radiation Measurement Without Instruments

### Acute Radiation Syndrome (ARS) Recognition

Clinical symptoms of whole-body radiation exposure follow predictable patterns based on dose received. Symptom onset and severity indicate dose level and prognosis.

#### ARS Progression Phases

##### Phase 1: Prodromal (Initial Onset)

-   Timing of onset is the key indicator: earlier onset = higher dose
-   Symptoms: nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, loss of appetite, fatigue
-   Dose estimation based on vomiting onset:
    -   Less than 10 minutes: 8+ Gy (lethal)
    -   10-30 minutes: 6-8 Gy (lethal)
    -   30-60 minutes: 4-6 Gy (lethal without intervention)
    -   1-2 hours: 2-4 Gy (severe; survival possible with care)
    -   4+ hours: 1-2 Gy (moderate; high survival rate)

##### Phase 2: Latent Period

-   Duration: Hours to days depending on dose
-   Individual may appear to recover, feel normal
-   Higher doses result in shorter latent periods
-   This is the period of maximum damage to bone marrow and GI tract

##### Phase 3: Manifest Illness

-   Onset indicates extent of internal organ damage
-   Hematopoietic syndrome (bone marrow damage):
    -   Severe infection risk due to low white blood cells
    -   Uncontrolled bleeding due to low platelets
    -   Onset 2-3 weeks post-exposure
-   Gastrointestinal syndrome:
    -   Bloody diarrhea, internal bleeding
    -   Severe dehydration and electrolyte loss
    -   Onset 1-2 weeks post-exposure (indicator of very high dose)

:::danger
Vomiting within minutes of exposure indicates lethal radiation dose. Focus on comfort care and pain management rather than expecting recovery. Rapid symptom onset (under 1 hour) indicates severe damage that will likely prove fatal even with supportive care. Do not exhaust resources attempting advanced treatment if dose appears extremely high.
:::

### Time-Distance-Shielding Principles

Without instruments, limit exposure through these three factors:

#### Time

-   Minimize time spent in contaminated areas
-   Measure your activities: eating, water collection, repairs
-   Assign shifts to distribute exposure across multiple people if possible
-   Example: 2 people spending 1 hour each receives half the dose of 1 person spending 2 hours

#### Distance

-   Inverse square law: doubling distance reduces exposure by 75%
-   Maintain maximum safe distance from fallout sources (outdoors, surface water, contaminated food)
-   Work from the periphery of a contaminated area toward the clean center, not vice versa

#### Shielding

-   Remain in protective shelter during highest fallout periods (first 24-48 hours)
-   Use available shielding materials during necessary outdoor work
-   Wear protective clothing (removed immediately after outdoor exposure)
-   Create improvised barriers for work areas (water drums, packed earth)

### Symptom Timeline Documentation

Maintain detailed notes of symptom onset for exposed individuals:

-   Record time of exposure (if known)
-   Note time of first nausea, first vomiting, diarrhea onset, other symptoms
-   This information helps medical personnel assess dose and prognosis if care becomes available
-   Early symptom onset (minutes) indicates need for comfort care only; survival unlikely
-   Delayed symptom onset (hours to days) indicates survival is possible with supportive care

## 3\. Biological Threats

### Natural Epidemic vs Deliberate Release

Distinguishing between natural disease outbreaks and intentional bioweapon release helps determine appropriate response:

#### Natural Epidemic Characteristics

-   Gradual geographic spread (person-to-person transmission)
-   Expected pathogen for region (seasonality, known animal vectors)
-   Normal age and gender distribution of cases
-   Cases appear in healthcare workers at expected rates
-   Symptom onset follows known incubation period

#### Deliberate Release Characteristics

-   Sudden onset in large numbers over short time period
-   Unusual pathogen for region (exotic agent, unusual strain)
-   Atypical age or gender distribution (affects military-age men, specific populations)
-   Abnormal geographic clustering (concentrated in specific building, area)
-   Healthcare workers heavily affected (suggests high inoculum)
-   Simultaneous infections in animals and humans (if environmental contamination)

### Key Biological Agents

#### Anthrax (Bacillus anthracis)

**Forms:** Cutaneous (skin), inhalation (respiratory), gastrointestinal

**Spore Detection and Properties:**

-   Anthrax spores are highly stable and can survive environmental conditions for decades
-   Non-pathogenic spores appear identical to dangerous spores under visual inspection
-   White powder appearance is NOT diagnostic (could be talc, flour, or truly dangerous anthrax)
-   Any white powder exposure in context of biological threat must be assumed dangerous

**Symptoms (Inhalation Form):**

-   Initial: fever, chills, malaise, myalgia (resembles influenza)
-   Phase 1 (days 1-3): respiratory symptoms, cough, chest discomfort
-   Phase 2 (days 3-5): acute respiratory distress, shock, possible death
-   Untreated mortality: 90%+

**Decontamination:**

-   Soap and water effectively inactivates anthrax spores
-   Bleach solution (0.5% sodium hypochlorite, 1:100 dilution) kills spores
-   Contaminated surfaces should be scrubbed with brush and bleach, allowed to sit 10+ minutes, then rinsed
-   Contaminated clothing can be incinerated or exposed to bleach solution (requires full soaking)

:::warning
White powder suspects must be treated as hazardous. Even if the powder is harmless (flour, talc, baking soda), responding as if it is dangerous is the only safe approach. Isolate the area, minimize disturbance of particles, and use bleach decontamination regardless of suspected identity.
:::

#### Smallpox (Variola)

**Recognition:**

-   Incubation: 7-19 days (average 10 days)
-   Prodrome: fever, chills, malaise, myalgia (3-4 days)
-   Rash onset: starts on face and forearms, spreads to trunk and legs
-   Lesions: fluid-filled (pustules), NOT flat like chickenpox, all in same stage of development
-   Centrifugal pattern: highest density on face and arms, least on trunk
-   Mortality: 30% even with supportive care

**Quarantine Setup (See Medical Reference for extended guidance):**

-   Isolate infected individual(s) in separate space if possible
-   Minimize contact to essential care only
-   Use dedicated equipment (utensils, bedding) if available
-   All caregivers wear improvised PPE (see Improvised PPE section)
-   Duration: Until all scabs have fallen and new skin is visible (3-4 weeks minimum)

:::note
Smallpox is highly contagious via respiratory droplets for the first 7-10 days of illness. Patients should be isolated before symptom appearance if close contact occurs. Any suspected smallpox case requires immediate isolation and external notification if communication is possible—this is a reportable disease with severe public health implications.
:::

#### Plague (Yersinia pestis)

**Forms:** Bubonic (lymph nodes), pneumonic (respiratory), septicemic (bloodstream)

**Flea Control (Critical):**

-   Plague spreads primarily through fleas that feed on infected rats
-   Eliminate flea breeding areas: remove debris, close rat entry points
-   Diatomaceous earth (food-grade) applied to pet areas kills fleas
-   Avoid handling dead animals; if necessary, wear gloves and burn or bury far from inhabited areas
-   Isolate infected persons to prevent flea access (protective barrier between person and fleas)

**Symptoms (Bubonic):**

-   Incubation: 1-7 days (usually 2-4 days)
-   Sudden onset: fever, chills, weakness, myalgia
-   Buboes: extremely painful, swollen lymph nodes near flea bite (groin, armpit, neck)
-   Untreated mortality: 60%

**Symptoms (Pneumonic):**

-   Incubation: 1-6 days (usually 2-3 days)
-   Rapid onset: fever, cough, chest pain, hemoptysis (coughing blood)
-   Untreated mortality: 90%+
-   Highly contagious: respiratory droplets transmit person-to-person

### Water and Food Safety During Bio Events

#### Water Safety

-   Boiling for 1+ minute kills vegetative bacteria and viruses, does NOT kill some bacterial spores
-   For biological threats, use boiling PLUS chemical disinfection or filtration
-   Bleach: 2 drops per liter of clear water, let stand 30 minutes minimum before drinking
-   Filtration: cloth + sand + gravel removes some bacterial particles but not viruses
-   Best approach: Boil + bleach disinfection for biological contamination scenarios

#### Food Safety

-   Stored, sealed foods are safe if packaging is not contaminated
-   Fresh foods grown in contaminated area should be thoroughly washed and cooked
-   Raw produce (fruit, vegetables): wash in clean water, avoid if source water is contaminated
-   Milk from potentially exposed animals: heat to 63°C (145°F) for 30 minutes (pasteurization) if possible
-   Wild game/meat: cook thoroughly to internal temperature of 74°C (165°F)

### Improvised PPE (Personal Protective Equipment)

#### Improvised N95-Equivalent Mask

HEPA vacuum bag material provides filtration equivalent to N95 masks:

1.  Cut rectangular strip from used HEPA vacuum bag (minimum 4"×6")
2.  Fold in half lengthwise to create a flat, double-layer mask
3.  Punch holes with needle or punch tool on each side for ear loops
4.  Thread elastic (string, rubber band, or cloth strip) through holes to create ear loops
5.  Shape the top (against nose) to fit contours and prevent air leakage
6.  Tape edges with medical tape or duct tape to create seal
7.  Replace after 8 hours or if visibly soiled/wet

#### Eye Protection

-   Glasses or goggles: protect against splashing contaminated water or blood
-   Improvised goggles: cut eyeholes from plastic sheeting, secure with duct tape and elastic
-   Seal around eyes with duct tape if handling highly contaminated materials

#### Hand and Body Protection

-   Gloves: latex, nitrile, or plastic bags over hands
-   Change gloves between individuals and contaminated items
-   Body coverage: long sleeves, pants, boots to minimize skin exposure
-   Apron or gown: old clothing or plastic sheeting can be removed after contaminated work

#### Decontamination Zones

Establish three-zone areas during outbreak response:

-   **Hot Zone:** Area with confirmed or suspected contamination. All personnel wear full PPE. Strict decontamination on exit.
-   **Warm Zone:** Buffer area. Personnel remove outer PPE here, wash hands and exposed skin before proceeding
-   **Cold Zone:** Clean area. No contamination expected. Standard hygiene only

## 4\. Chemical Threats

### Chemical Agent Categories

#### Nerve Agents (Organophosphate Compounds)

**Examples:** Sarin (GB), VX, Tabun (GA), Soman (GD)

**Mechanism:** Block acetylcholinesterase enzyme, causing overstimulation of nerves and muscles

**Recognition Symptoms (SLUDGE):**

-   **S** alivation: excessive drooling
-   **L** acrimation: excessive tearing
-   **U** rination: involuntary urination
-   **D** efecation: involuntary defecation
-   **G** astrointestinal distress: nausea, vomiting, cramping
-   **E** mesis: vomiting

**Additional symptoms:** Pinpoint pupils (miosis), muscle twitching/paralysis, difficulty breathing, loss of consciousness, seizures

**Onset:** Seconds to minutes; severity depends on exposure amount and route

#### Blister Agents (Mustards)

**Example:** Sulfur mustard (mustard gas)

**Recognition:**

-   Garlic or mustard odor (not always present at dangerous concentrations)
-   Effects are DELAYED: may not appear for 6-24 hours after exposure
-   Skin: erythema (redness) progressing to fluid-filled blisters
-   Eyes: pain, redness, tearing; corneal damage possible
-   Respiratory: damage to throat and lungs; chemical burns to airways
-   Long-term: risk of cancer from mustard exposure

**Danger:** Delayed symptoms mean individuals may not realize they're exposed and may contaminate others

:::warning
Delayed effects of blister agents create false sense of safety. Individuals exposed to mustard gas may feel fine for hours then develop severe burns. Assume any suspected mustard exposure is dangerous—decontaminate immediately even if symptoms haven't appeared.
:::

#### Blood Agents (Cyanide Compounds)

**Examples:** Hydrogen cyanide (AC), Cyanogen chloride (CK)

**Recognition:**

-   Almond odor (not all people can smell this; about 40% of population cannot detect it)
-   Cherry-red skin color (due to oxygen saturation of venous blood)
-   Symptoms: rapid breathing, dizziness, confusion, seizures, loss of consciousness
-   Onset: seconds to minutes
-   Death can occur within 5-10 minutes of exposure

#### Choking Agents (Respiratory Irritants)

**Examples:** Chlorine (Cl₂), Phosgene (COCl₂)

**Chlorine Recognition:**

-   Distinctive strong acid smell
-   Yellow-green gas
-   Immediate burning of eyes, throat, respiratory tract
-   Coughing, wheezing, difficulty breathing
-   Symptoms appear immediately upon exposure

**Phosgene Recognition:**

-   Sweet smell (like corn, green hay, or mown grass)
-   Danger: initial symptoms are MILD; severe pulmonary edema develops over hours
-   Initial: mild cough, throat irritation, eye tearing
-   Latent period: 1-24 hours during which person may seem to recover
-   Manifest illness: acute pulmonary edema (fluid in lungs), respiratory failure, death
-   High mortality even with treatment due to delayed pulmonary damage

:::danger
Phosgene is particularly dangerous because initial symptoms are mild. Exposed individuals may feel recovered after 2-4 hours then suddenly deteriorate with pulmonary edema. If phosgene exposure is suspected, monitor continuously for 24 hours minimum and provide supplemental oxygen if available, even if symptoms appear to improve.
:::

### Detection Without Instruments

#### Smell

-   Garlic/mustard = blister agent (delayed effects)
-   Almond smell = blood agent (cyanide) — CAUTION: not all people detect this
-   Pungent acid (chlorine-like) = choking agent (chlorine)
-   Sweet smell (corn, hay) = phosgene (choking agent with delayed pulmonary effects)
-   Note: Nerve agents (sarin, VX) are often colorless and odorless

#### Animal Indicators

-   Dogs, birds, insects dying or acting abnormally in an area = possible chemical contamination
-   Absence of insects/birds = potential chemical hazard (they're more sensitive than humans)
-   If animals are avoiding an area, humans should also avoid it

#### Vegetation Changes

-   Dead plants, discolored leaves, or sudden wilting = possible chemical or biological contamination
-   Pattern of dying vegetation (concentrated in specific area, wind direction) = clue to dispersal pattern
-   Multiple species affected simultaneously = more likely chemical than natural disease

:::tip
Animals are better early warning systems than humans for chemical and biological agents. Monitor pet behavior and wildlife activity—unusual clustering of dead birds or insects, avoidance of specific areas, or behavioral changes precede human symptom onset. Trust animal warnings more than your own perception.
:::

### Improvised Protection

#### Shelter-in-Place with Chemical Filtration

-   **Sealed room:** Plastic sheeting + duct tape over all windows and doors
-   **Filtered ventilation:** If air intake is needed, route through improvised filter:
    -   Layer 1: Activated charcoal (2-4 inches) — removes vapors
    -   Layer 2: HEPA material from vacuum bags (2 inches) — removes particulates
    -   Layer 3: Cloth pre-filter (to prevent clogging)
-   **Positive pressure:** If possible, create slight positive pressure (air flowing out) by forcing filtered air into room with small fan powered by batteries or hand-crank

#### Wet Cloth Over Face (Limited Protection)

-   Wet cloth over nose and mouth provides minimal protection (~10% filtration)
-   Slightly reduces inhalation of heavy particulates and aerosols
-   More effective for choking agents (water reacts with chlorine) than for nerve agents
-   Not recommended as primary defense; useful only for evacuation through contaminated area

#### Activated Charcoal Filter

-   Activated charcoal absorbs organic vapors (nerve and blister agents)
-   Ineffective against blood agents (cyanide) or some choking agents without pre-treatment
-   Improvised filter: fill cloth bag or stockings with activated charcoal, secure over intake vent
-   Limitations: requires air flow (insufficient for complete protection), saturates over time (not reusable)

### Decontamination from Chemical Exposure

#### Large Volume Water Flushing

-   Remove all contaminated clothing immediately and place in separate area
-   Flush skin with large volumes of water for minimum 15 minutes
-   Use soap to help remove oil-based chemical agents
-   Pay special attention to: hair, skin folds, underarms, groin, between toes
-   Eyes: flush with clean water for minimum 15 minutes
-   If water is contaminated with chemical, the water itself is safer than chemical (dilution is effective)

#### Upwind Positioning

-   Determine wind direction (leaves, smoke, dust)
-   Position yourself upwind (so wind blows away from you)
-   Move upwind away from suspected release area
-   Advise others to move upwind; downwind position is dangerous for all remaining exposure time

#### Equipment Decontamination

-   If tools or equipment were exposed, scrub with soap and large volume of water
-   For nerve/blister agents: bleach solution (0.5%) accelerates decontamination
-   Allow equipment to air-dry in open area before reuse

### Industrial Chemical Awareness

Many hazardous chemicals are used industrially and could be released accidentally or deliberately:

#### Chlorine

-   **Sources:** Water treatment plants, swimming pools, bleach manufacturing
-   **Risk:** Tank rupture or line rupture could release large volume into air or water
-   **Protection:** Evacuate downwind; seek shelter upwind of release

#### Ammonia

-   **Sources:** Fertilizer production, refrigeration systems, industrial processes
-   **Hazard:** Respiratory irritant; forms toxic aerosol when mixed with water
-   **Protection:** Shelter indoors; wet cloth over face provides minimal protection

#### Hydrogen Sulfide

-   **Sources:** Sewers, manure pits, petroleum processing, decaying organic matter
-   **Hazard:** "Rotten egg" smell; rapidly fatal at high concentrations; nerve damage at lower levels
-   **Protection:** Evacuate upwind; do not re-enter area where smell detected until safe confirmation

## 5\. Shelter-in-Place

### Decision Tree: Shelter vs Evacuate

The correct response depends on the threat and your location:

:::card
**Shelter in Place if:**

-   Nuclear fallout is likely (explosion detected, civil alert issued)
-   Chemical agent release upwind is confirmed (shelter keeps contaminated air away)
-   Evacuation routes are blocked or dangerous
-   You are in a well-constructed building with some protection
-   You cannot quickly reach a safer location
:::

:::card
**Evacuate if:**

-   You are downwind of a chemical or biological release and can safely move upwind
-   You are in a mobile home or vehicle without structural protection
-   Your building is severely damaged
-   Local authorities issue evacuation orders
-   You are within blast radius and can move to greater distance
:::

### Sealing a Room

#### Materials Needed

-   Clear plastic sheeting (4-6 mil thickness)
-   Duct tape (strong adhesive, works on most surfaces)
-   Scissors or utility knife
-   Caulk or spackling compound (optional, for gaps)

#### Procedure

1.  **Select a room:** Interior room with fewest windows and doors; bathroom or interior bedroom preferred
2.  **Close all doors and windows:** Shut interior doors to eliminate air exchange between rooms
3.  **Block all openings:**
    -   Windows: Cut plastic to size, cover entirely, seal all edges with duct tape (minimum 3 inches wide tape)
    -   Doors: Seal gap under door with plastic and tape, or fill with rolled towels/duct tape
    -   Vents and ducts: Cover with plastic; do not seal heating system vents (see ventilation section)
4.  **Seal all gaps:** Use duct tape around plastic edges, overlapping edge onto wall/window frame
5.  **Test for leaks:** Smoke from a cigarette or incense should move away from sealed area, not toward it. If smoke is drawn inward, leak exists — find and seal

#### Positive Pressure (if possible)

-   With ventilation system enabled, room maintains slight positive pressure (air inside pushes out)
-   Requires functioning HVAC or makeshift fan with filtered intake
-   Positive pressure prevents outside air from being pulled inward if room is breached

### Oxygen Consumption in Sealed Room

A sealed room has limited oxygen. Calculate endurance:

-   **Oxygen consumption:** ~0.84 cubic feet per person per hour (at rest)
-   **Room volume example:** 12 ft × 10 ft × 8 ft (height) = 960 cubic feet
-   **Usable oxygen:** ~204 cubic feet (21% of 960)
-   **Duration for 1 person:** 204 ÷ 0.84 = ~240 hours (~10 days) at rest
-   **Duration for 2 people:** ~120 hours (~5 days)
-   **Duration for 4 people:** ~60 hours (~2.5 days)

**Important:** This calculation assumes no carbon dioxide removal. In practice, CO₂ buildup makes the space uncomfortable much earlier. Safe occupancy is typically 24-48 hours maximum in a sealed, unventilated room.

### Ventilation with Improvised Filter

#### Basic Filtered Ventilation

If staying sheltered for more than 24 hours, some ventilation is needed to remove CO₂:

1.  **Create intake filter:** Assemble a box or duct with layers:
    -   Pre-filter layer: old cloth or furnace filter (prevents clogging)
    -   Activated charcoal layer: 2-4 inches thickness in cloth bag or filter media
    -   HEPA layer: vacuum bag material or furnace filter (removes fine particles)
2.  **Install intake:** Run intake duct from outside through filter into room. Seal connection with duct tape
3.  **Create exhaust:** Run duct from room to outside, or allow pressure relief around sealed doors/windows
4.  **Power the system:** Use battery-powered fan (12V car fan with battery), hand-crank fan, or gravity ventilation (if exhaust is higher than intake)
5.  **Maintain air flow:** Test that intake air is entering room (use smoke test)

#### Duration Limits for Different Scenarios

-   **Nuclear fallout:** Minimum 2 weeks sheltered; 24-48 hours minimum for severe scenario to allow fallout to settle
-   **Chemical agent:** 24-48 hours until weather changes or all-clear is given; ventilation critical
-   **Biological (external exposure):** 48 hours minimum to ensure weather disperses agent and personal quarantine is in effect

## 6\. Decontamination Procedures

### Three-Zone Decontamination Layout

Organize decontamination activities into three zones to prevent spread of contamination:

Decontamination Zone Layout

![NBC Defense diagram 2](../assets/svgs/nbc-defense-2.svg)

### Personal Decontamination

1.  **Strip outer layer:** Remove all potentially contaminated clothing and place in designated area. This removes 80-90% of contamination immediately
2.  **Discard protective equipment:** Remove gloves, masks, outer PPE into contaminated waste container
3.  **Wash from head to toe:**
    -   Wash hair multiple times with soap and water
    -   Wash face, ears, and neck thoroughly
    -   Wash arms, underarms, hands (between fingers)
    -   Wash torso, back, abdomen
    -   Wash legs, feet, between toes
    -   Special attention to any cuts or abrasions
4.  **Rinse thoroughly:** Ensure all soap is removed; residual soap can irritate skin
5.  **Dry with clean towel:** Do not reuse towel for multiple people
6.  **Don clean clothing:** Put on clothing that has not been exposed to contamination
7.  **Monitor:** If any remaining contamination is detected (using KFM or other method), repeat washing

:::warning
Contamination must be removed before entering clean areas. Do not bypass decontamination steps—contaminated individuals will spread hazards to others and to living spaces. Use three-zone decontamination even if it requires extra time and resources.
:::

### Equipment Decontamination

#### Radioactive Contamination (Fallout)

-   Physical removal: Brush loose contamination from surfaces
-   Wash: Scrub with soap and water, paying attention to cracks and crevices
-   Rinse: Remove all soap residue
-   Dry: Air dry in open area
-   Verify: Use KFM or monitoring device to confirm decontamination

#### Chemical Contamination

-   For nerve agents or blister agents: Use bleach solution (0.5% sodium hypochlorite)
-   Scrub contaminated surfaces with bleach solution and brush
-   Allow 10-15 minutes contact time for bleach to neutralize agent
-   Rinse thoroughly with water and dry

#### Biological Contamination

-   Bleach is highly effective against biological agents
-   Use 0.5% bleach solution (1:100 dilution of household bleach)
-   Scrub surfaces and allow 10+ minutes contact time
-   Rinse and dry

### Area Decontamination

#### Radiation (Fallout) Decontamination

-   **Top soil removal:** The most effective method. Remove top 2-4 inches of soil where fallout settled
-   **Disposal:** Place contaminated soil in sealed containers or dig it into a pit far from inhabited area and water sources
-   **Replacement:** Use clean soil or gravel to backfill the area
-   **Decaying fallout:** After initial top-soil removal, remaining radiation will decay naturally (follow Rule of Seven)

#### Biological Decontamination

-   **Lime treatment:** Quicklime (calcium oxide) or slaked lime (calcium hydroxide) neutralizes biological agents and speeds decomposition
-   **Application:** Spread lime over contaminated area at rate of 50-100 lbs per 1000 sq ft
-   **Duration:** Allow 24+ hours contact time
-   **Removal:** Shovel treated material into sealed containers; lime remains hazardous until neutralized with acid

#### Chemical Decontamination

-   **Bleach solution:** Spray or scrub contaminated area with 0.5% bleach solution
-   **Contact time:** 15-30 minutes for complete neutralization of most agents
-   **Ventilation:** Use fans to improve air circulation during decontamination
-   **Disposal:** Allow bleach to evaporate or neutralize before cleanup; residual bleach is less hazardous than chemical agents

### Water Decontamination

#### Radioactive Contamination

-   **Distillation:** Boil contaminated water and collect condensed steam. This removes dissolved radioactive particles
-   **Process:**
    -   Boil water in covered pot
    -   Run tube from steam outlet to collection container (submerged in cold water to condense)
    -   Collect distilled water only
-   **Effectiveness:** Removes ~99% of particulate contamination; some dissolved isotopes (iodine) may persist

#### Chemical Contamination

-   **Filtration:** Use activated charcoal (2-4 inches) plus sand filter plus cloth to remove organic chemicals
-   **Activated charcoal:** Most effective method for organic chemical removal
-   **Boiling:** Does NOT remove dissolved chemicals effectively; may be counterproductive
-   **Best practice:** Filtration + activated charcoal for chemical threats

#### Biological Contamination

-   **Boiling:** 1+ minute boiling kills most bacteria and viruses
-   **Chemical disinfection:** Bleach (2 drops per liter) after boiling for redundancy
-   **Bacterial spores (anthrax):** May survive boiling; filtration through cloth + activated charcoal helps
-   **Best practice:** Boiling + bleach for biological contamination

## 7\. Emergency Communications

### EBS/EAS (Emergency Broadcast System / Emergency Alert System)

Official emergency information may be broadcast through radio and television:

-   **EAS alert:** Special tone followed by official announcement on radio/TV
-   **Frequency:** All licensed radio and TV stations in USA required to participate
-   **AM/FM radio:** Frequency of local stations varies; NOAA Weather Radio (162.55 MHz) also broadcasts EAS
-   **Information provided:** Type of emergency, affected areas, instructions for survival/evacuation
-   **Limitations:** May be delayed; assumes functioning power, transmitters, and radio infrastructure

### Shortwave Monitoring

International shortwave stations continue broadcasting even if local infrastructure is damaged:

-   **BBC World Service:** 3.98, 5.97, 7.325, 9.41 MHz (broadcast times vary)
-   **Voice of America:** Multiple frequencies in 5-17 MHz range
-   **Radio Canada International:** 5.95, 6.14, 7.235, 9.60 MHz
-   **Monitoring:** Use battery-powered or hand-crank shortwave radio
-   **Advantage:** Long-range reception; functions even if local infrastructure damaged

### Reference: Ham Radio

See ham-radio.html for extended emergency communications guidance including amateur radio frequencies, licensing, and equipment setup for extended off-grid communication.

### Signal for Help

-   **Visual signals:** Bright colored cloth on high point (roof, tall tree) signals distress
-   **Smoke:** Large fires visible from distance; green branches create smoke
-   **Sound:** Three 'bursts' of signal (three whistle blasts, three gunshots, three horn honks) is universal distress signal
-   **SOS:** Radio transmission "SOS" is international distress signal (though less relevant without functioning infrastructure)

### Convoy Communication

If traveling in group or convoy:

-   **Hand signals:** Predetermined hand signals for stop, turn, danger, all-clear
-   **Radio:** FRS (Family Radio Service) radios or CB radio for vehicle-to-vehicle communication (limited range, ~1 mile)
-   **Runners:** Fastest communication in group is verbal; designate runners for information relay between vehicles
-   **Scheduled communication:** Establish times for radio check-ins to maintain group cohesion

## 8\. Post-Event Recovery

### When Safe to Emerge

#### Nuclear Fallout Scenario

-   **Minimum sheltering:** 2 weeks in protected shelter for areas with heavy fallout
-   **After 14 days:** Radiation has decayed to ~1/1000th of initial levels (Rule of Seven)
-   **Lighter fallout areas:** 7 days sheltering reduces dose to ~1/100th of initial
-   **Verification:** If KFM or dosimeter shows stable readings (no further movement for several hours), radiation is likely decayed enough for emergence
-   **Caution:** Remain alert to areas of heavier contamination (low spots where fallout settled)

#### Biological Event Scenario

-   **Quarantine period:** 2 incubation periods after last known case is safe (usually 2-3 weeks)
-   **Verification:** Absence of new cases for extended period indicates agent is no longer circulating
-   **Reemergence:** Gradual reentry into public activities, starting with solitary work progressing to small groups

#### Chemical Event Scenario

-   **Volatility:** Chemical agents dissipate relatively quickly (hours to days depending on agent and weather)
-   **Weather change:** Wind and rain disperse chemical agents; monitor weather patterns
-   **Verification:** If no symptoms develop after 24-48 hours in shelter, chemical threat likely has dispersed
-   **Caution:** Some areas may have pooled liquid agent; avoid standing water and low areas

### Food Safety

#### Wash All Surfaces

-   Wash exterior of all food containers, cans, and packaging before opening/consuming
-   Wipe down utensils, dishes, cookware with clean cloth before use
-   Clean countertops, tables, and food preparation surfaces with soap and water

#### Peel Root Vegetables

-   Carrots, beets, potatoes, turnips: peel thick outer layer to remove surface contamination
-   Underground vegetables are more protected than surface vegetables
-   Residual peeling removes ~70-80% of any remaining fallout particles

#### Discard Leafy Greens

-   First year after major fallout event: do not consume outdoor-grown lettuce, spinach, kale, cabbage, other leafy greens
-   These vegetables concentrate radioactive material and pose high risk
-   Greenhouse-grown leafy greens are safe if external contamination did not occur
-   Second-year crops: leafy greens become progressively safer as contamination decays and is diluted by soil

#### Livestock Feed

-   Feed dairy animals stored hay/grain for minimum 2 weeks before producing milk for consumption
-   During this period, iodine contamination (primary milk contaminant) will be excreted and decayed
-   Monitor first milk produced for any unusual appearance or taste

### Water Safety

#### Deep Wells (200+ feet)

-   Most protected source immediately after contamination event
-   Natural filtration through earth removes fallout particles
-   Use immediately after safety confirmation

#### Shallow Wells (50-200 feet)

-   More vulnerable to surface contamination than deep wells
-   Wait 2-3 weeks after event before reopening
-   First water pumped may be contaminated; pump extensively (500+ gallons) before collection for consumption

#### Surface Water (Springs, Streams)

-   High risk of contamination; wait 2+ weeks minimum
-   Even after waiting period, use distillation or aggressive filtration + activated charcoal
-   Monitor animal behavior: if wildlife avoids source, humans should also

#### Rainwater Collection

-   Contaminated during and immediately after fallout event
-   Do not collect rainwater for first 48 hours after event
-   After 48+ hours, first rainfall will have carried most fallout; collection becomes safer
-   Use distillation or activated charcoal filtration for extra safety

### Psychological Impact and PTSD

NBC events cause severe psychological trauma. Recognition and support are critical for recovery.

#### Recognition of PTSD Symptoms

-   **Intrusive thoughts:** Unwanted memories, flashbacks, nightmares of the event
-   **Avoidance:** Avoiding places, people, or activities that trigger memories
-   **Hyperarousal:** Excessive startle response, difficulty sleeping, irritability, difficulty concentrating
-   **Negative mood:** Persistent negative beliefs about self, guilt, shame, emotional numbing
-   **Duration:** Symptoms persist for weeks to months after traumatic event

#### Immediate Support

-   Maintain routine: Regular sleep, meals, activity provides structure and safety
-   Social connection: Maintain contact with family and community (isolation worsens PTSD)
-   Physical activity: Exercise reduces anxiety and improves mood
-   Limit discussion: Repeated discussion of trauma can intensify symptoms; limit but don't completely avoid

See psychology-morale.html for comprehensive guidance on recognizing and treating psychological effects of extreme survival situations, including depression, anxiety, grief, and long-term trauma recovery.
