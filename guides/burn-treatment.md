**Severity ladder — start here:**

| If the burn is… | Key signs | What to do |
|---|---|---|
| **1st degree (minor)** | Red, dry, painful, no blisters. E.g. mild sunburn. | Cool water 10-20 min, aloe/OTC pain relief. Self-care. See Burn Classification → 1st Degree. |
| **2nd degree (moderate)** | Blisters, pink/red, weeping, very painful. | Do **not** pop blisters at home. Cool water, sterile non-stick dressing, seek care if face/hands/genitals/joints or >10% TBSA. See Blister Management; Burn Classification → 2nd Degree. |
| **3rd degree (severe)** | White/brown/black, dry, leathery, **painless** (nerves gone). | Emergency. Do not apply home remedies. Cover with clean dry dressing, seek immediate professional care. See Initial Management (ABCDE). |
| **4th degree (critical)** | Charred, muscle/bone visible or exposed. | Emergency. Same as 3rd degree — immediate transport. See Initial Management (ABCDE). |

## Overview and Epidemiology

Burn injuries represent a significant cause of morbidity and mortality worldwide. Burns are classified as a major trauma requiring systematic assessment and treatment. The severity depends on:

-   **Depth:** Extent of tissue damage (epidermal through full-thickness)
-   **Surface area:** Percentage of Total Body Surface Area (TBSA) involved
-   **Location:** Face, hands, genitals, joints carry higher mortality/morbidity
-   **Patient factors:** Age, comorbidities, inhalation injury
-   **Time since injury:** Critical for treatment planning

Burns >15-20% TBSA in adults (>10% in children/elderly) require hospitalization and fluid resuscitation. Burns >40% TBSA carry significant mortality risk.

## Burn Classification by Depth

### Understanding Burn Depth

Burn depth is classified from superficial to full-thickness, correlating with anatomical layers of skin and underlying tissues.

Burn Depth Cross-Section

![🔥 Burn Treatment Guide diagram 1](../assets/svgs/burn-treatment-1.svg)

:::card
#### 1st Degree (Superficial)

**Depth:** Epidermis only

**Appearance:** Erythematous (red), intact

**Sensation:** Very painful

**Healing:** 3-5 days, no scarring

**Example:** Sunburn
:::

:::card
#### 2nd Degree (Partial Thickness)

**Depth:** Epidermis + part/all dermis

**Appearance:** Blistering, pink-red, moist

**Sensation:** Very painful

**Healing:** 2-3 weeks, may scar

**Subdivisions:** Superficial vs. deep
:::

:::card
#### 3rd Degree (Full Thickness)

**Depth:** All skin layers destroyed

**Appearance:** White/charred, leathery, fixed

**Sensation:** Painless (nerves destroyed)

**Healing:** Requires grafting, significant scarring

**Eschar:** Non-viable tissue
:::

:::card
#### 4th Degree (Deep Tissue)

**Depth:** Muscle, bone, organs involved

**Appearance:** Charred, mummified

**Sensation:** Painless

**Healing:** Severe tissue loss, amputation likely

**Mortality:** High with systemic involvement
:::

### Palmar Method

The patient's open palm (including fingers) equals approximately 0.5% TBSA in adults and children. This is useful for:

-   Small scattered burns
-   Irregular burn patterns
-   Quick estimation without drawing

**Method:** Count how many palms fit over the burned area and multiply by 0.5%. For example, 20 palms = 10% TBSA.

### Lund-Browder Chart

The gold standard for TBSA assessment, especially in children. Uses a detailed body map accounting for age-related variations. More accurate than Rule of Nines for:

-   Precise surgical planning
-   Pediatric patients
-   Large burns requiring exact fluid calculations

:::info-box
**Critical Point:** Exclusions from TBSA calculation include erythema (1st degree) unless accompanied by blistering. Only count 2nd degree and deeper burns unless treating for fluid resuscitation purposes.
:::

## Initial Management: Primary ABCDE Survey

### Pre-Hospital and Emergency Department Management

Treat burn victims using the same trauma protocol as other major injuries:

#### A - Airway with cervical spine protection

-   Assume cervical spine injury if trauma mechanism unclear
-   Remove constrictive clothing and jewelry from neck
-   If inhalation injury suspected, prepare for difficult airway
-   Early intubation considered if facial burns, stridor, or respiratory distress
-   **Critical:** Intubate based on clinical signs, not just carboxyhemoglobin levels

#### B - Breathing and ventilation

-   Assess for circumferential chest burns causing eschar restriction
-   Oxygen delivery: maintain SpO2 >94% (aim for 100% initially to displace CO)
-   Monitor for signs of inhalation injury: singed nasal hairs, carbonaceous sputum, stridor
-   Prepare for emergent escharotomy if respiratory compromise develops
-   If ventilated, use low tidal volumes (6-8 mL/kg) due to increased airway pressure risk

#### C - Circulation and hemorrhage control

-   Establish two large-bore IV lines (14-16 gauge) through non-burned skin if possible
-   Start fluid resuscitation immediately in burns >15-20% TBSA
-   Monitor urine output as indicator of adequate perfusion
-   Remove rings, bracelets, watches from burned extremities (risk of compartment syndrome)
-   Do NOT apply ice to large burns (risk of systemic hypothermia)
-   Cover burns with clean, dry dressings to prevent contamination

#### D - Disability and neurological examination

-   Assess Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) score
-   Consider carbon monoxide poisoning if unconscious at scene
-   Check for signs of smoke inhalation toxicity (cyanide, hydrogen sulfide)
-   Evaluate for other traumatic injuries masked by burn severity

#### E - Exposure and environmental control

-   Remove all clothing and jewelry to assess total burn extent
-   Prevent heat loss: apply warm blankets, give warm IV fluids, warm environment
-   Perform full body examination including back, genitals, areas under jewelry
-   Insert Foley catheter for monitoring urine output (key resuscitation endpoint)
-   Insert nasogastric tube for burns >20% TBSA (risk of ileus)

:::warning
**CRITICAL:** Do NOT delay fluid resuscitation waiting for transfer to burn center. Start immediately based on TBSA estimation. Underresuscitation leads to organ failure; overresuscitation causes compartment syndrome and abdominal compartment syndrome.
:::

## Fluid Resuscitation: Parkland and Modified Brooke Formulas

### Indications for Fluid Resuscitation

-   Burns >15-20% TBSA in adults
-   Burns >10% TBSA in children
-   Burns >10% TBSA in elderly
-   Inhalation injury
-   Electrical burns (risk of myoglobinuria)

### Parkland Formula (Original)

Total 24-hour fluid = 4 mL × Body Weight (kg) × %TBSA Given as Lactated Ringers (LR) - First half given over 8 hours - Second half given over 16 hours

**Example calculation:**

-   70 kg patient with 30% TBSA burn
-   4 × 70 × 30 = 8,400 mL total
-   First 8 hours: 4,200 mL = 525 mL/hour
-   Remaining 16 hours: 4,200 mL = 262 mL/hour

### Modified Brooke Formula (Preferred Modern Approach)

Total 24-hour fluid = 2 mL × Body Weight (kg) × %TBSA Given as Lactated Ringers (LR) - First half given over 8 hours - Second half given over 16 hours

**Advantages:** Reduces fluid overload and associated complications (abdominal compartment syndrome, acute respiratory distress syndrome). Equally effective with lower morbidity.

**Same example with Modified Brooke:**

-   70 kg patient with 30% TBSA burn
-   2 × 70 × 30 = 4,200 mL total
-   First 8 hours: 2,100 mL = 262 mL/hour
-   Remaining 16 hours: 2,100 mL = 131 mL/hour

### Important Considerations

#### Fluid Selection

-   **Lactated Ringer's Solution:** Preferred choice - physiologic electrolyte composition
-   **Normal Saline:** Acceptable alternative but risks hyperchloremic acidosis with large volumes
-   **Dextrose-containing solutions:** Avoid initially (risk of hyperglycemia and worsening edema)
-   **Colloids:** Limited role in first 24 hours (increased mortality in some studies)

#### Resuscitation Endpoints (Titration is Key)

Formulas provide initial guidance - adjust based on clinical response:

<table><thead><tr><th scope="col">Patient Type</th><th scope="col">Target Urine Output</th><th scope="col">Other Endpoints</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td><strong>Adult</strong></td><td>0.5 mL/kg/hr</td><td>Systolic BP &gt;90, alert, peripheral perfusion</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Child</strong></td><td>1.0 mL/kg/hr (0.5 if &lt;30 kg)</td><td>Age-appropriate parameters</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Electrical burn</strong></td><td>200-300 mL/hr (until myoglobin clears)</td><td>Urine color clears, CK normalizes</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Inhalation injury</strong></td><td>0.5 mL/kg/hr or higher</td><td>May require higher rates due to pulmonary edema</td></tr></tbody></table>

#### Monitoring Parameters

-   Monitor urine output: most reliable indicator (requires Foley catheter)
-   Monitor blood pressure and heart rate trends (may be falsely reassuring)
-   Assess mental status and sensorium
-   Assess peripheral perfusion and extremity warmth
-   Check electrolytes (K+, Na+, Cl-) at 4-6 hour intervals initially
-   Check arterial blood gas: assess for metabolic acidosis
-   Track serial lactate: marker of tissue perfusion

### Fluid Overload Prevention (Fluid Creep)

"Fluid creep" describes excessive fluid administration beyond formula recommendations, leading to:

-   Abdominal compartment syndrome (ACS)
-   Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS)
-   Pneumonia
-   Increased mortality and morbidity

**Prevention strategies:**

-   Use Modified Brooke over Parkland
-   Titrate to urine output, not formulas
-   Monitor abdominal compartment and bladder pressures
-   Consider vasopressors (dopamine, norepinephrine) if inadequate perfusion despite appropriate fluids
-   After initial 24 hours, switch to hypotonic fluids (D5½NS) and reduce rate based on ongoing losses

:::warning
**Abdominal Compartment Syndrome:** Clinical signs include abdominal distension, elevated bladder pressure (>25-30 cmH2O), oliguria, tachycardia, and elevated peak airway pressures. May require urgent decompressive laparotomy.
:::

### Second 24 Hours and Beyond

-   Reduce infusion rate by 10-20% every 2-4 hours if urine output adequate
-   Switch to hypotonic solutions (D5½NS or D5¼NS) after first 24 hours
-   Continue addressing ongoing losses (evaporation from burns)
-   Add dextrose and potassium as needed for hypoglycemia and hypokalemia
-   Maintain adequate nutrition support (see Nutrition section)

## Wound Care and Debridement

### Goals of Burn Wound Care

-   Remove devitalized tissue
-   Prevent infection
-   Promote epithelialization
-   Preserve viable tissue and function
-   Minimize pain and scarring

### Initial Wound Cleaning

-   Gently remove loose debris and clothing
-   Irrigate with lukewarm water or normal saline
-   Avoid vigorous scrubbing of partial-thickness burns (risk of converting to full-thickness)
-   Sterile technique essential
-   Pain control crucial during cleaning (topical anesthetics, systemic analgesia, anxiolytics)

### Blister Management

Controversial but general approach:

-   **Unroofing recommended:** Remove blister fluid to:

-   Reduce contamination risk
-   Decrease inflammatory mediators
-   Improve topical agent penetration

-   **Preserve blister roof:** If epidermis intact and clean - provides biologic dressing
-   In practice: unroof large blisters, leave small intact blisters

### Debridement Techniques

#### Tangential Excision

-   Sequential removal of necrotic tissue in layers
-   Performed with anesthesia (typically OR)
-   Continued until viable tissue encountered (bright red bleeding)
-   Preferred method - maintains maximum viable tissue
-   Can be done bedside or in OR

#### Fascial Excision

-   Excision down to fascia
-   More rapid than tangential
-   Greater blood loss
-   Used for deep burns or massive burns with time constraints

#### Enzymatic Debridement

-   Bromelain (Nexobrid) - FDA approved debriding agent
-   Collagenase - enzymatic preparation
-   Slower than surgical but useful in resource-limited settings
-   Less blood loss than surgical debridement

#### Autolytic Debridement

-   Use of wet dressings and moist environment
-   Body's own enzymes remove dead tissue
-   Slow process - takes days to weeks
-   Used for small wounds or when surgery unavailable

### Debridement Frequency and Timing

-   **Deep burns:** Early excision (within 0-3 days) preferred
-   **Partial-thickness:** Can wait for demarcation (7-10 days) then selective excision
-   **Daily bedside:** Remove loose tissue daily during dressing changes
-   **Frequency:** Surgical debridement planned every 2-3 days until eschar removed

### Topical Wound Care Agents

#### Silver Sulfadiazine (SSD)

-   **Mechanism:** Broad-spectrum antimicrobial
-   **Application:** Sterile, 1-2 mm layer, 1-2 times daily
-   **Advantages:** Effective, low cost, tolerable pain
-   **Disadvantages:** Risk of leukopenia, argyria (gray discoloration), sulfonamide allergy
-   **Monitoring:** Weekly CBC for WBC count

#### Mafenide Acetate (Sulfamylon)

-   **Mechanism:** Broad-spectrum, penetrates eschar well
-   **Application:** 1-2 mm layer, 1-2 times daily
-   **Advantages:** Better eschar penetration than SSD, useful for deep burns
-   **Disadvantages:** Painful on application, metabolic acidosis risk, hypersensitivity
-   **Cost:** More expensive than SSD

#### Honey-Based Dressings

-   **Mechanism:** Osmotic, enzymatic, antimicrobial (hydrogen peroxide generation)
-   **Application:** Medical-grade honey on gauze, changed daily
-   **Advantages:** Natural, effective, low cost, reduced pain, promotes granulation
-   **Disadvantages:** Limited availability of medical-grade, stickiness, moisture management
-   **Ideal for:** Resource-limited settings, partial-thickness burns

#### Bacitracin Ointment

-   **Use:** Small partial-thickness burns, face, genitals
-   **Advantages:** Non-toxic, low cost, allows visualization
-   **Disadvantages:** Frequent reapplication needed, limited deep penetration

#### Iodine-Based Products

-   Povidone-iodine or cadexomer iodine
-   Broad-spectrum antimicrobial
-   Risk of iodine toxicity with large burns
-   Monitor thyroid function

#### Biosynthetic and Biologic Dressings

-   **Biobrane:** Nylon-collagen composite, temporary coverage
-   **Integra:** Collagen-GAG matrix, allows dermal regeneration
-   **AlloDerm:** Acellular donor dermis
-   **Use:** Expensive, for major burns in well-resourced settings

### Dressing Change Protocol

-   **Frequency:** Daily to twice daily depending on wound type
-   **Pain control:** Opioids 20-30 minutes before; topical anesthetics (lidocaine)
-   **Procedure:**

-   Remove old dressing gently
-   Inspect wound for signs of infection, healing
-   Clean gently with normal saline
-   Pat dry with sterile gauze
-   Apply topical agent
-   Cover with sterile gauze (plain or impregnated)
-   Secure with tape or bandaging

:::info-box
**Dressing Choice:** In resource-limited settings with limited topical agents, honey-based dressings combined with daily cleaning have demonstrated excellent outcomes for partial-thickness burns.
:::

## Escharotomy and Fasciotomy

### Indications for Escharotomy

Circumferential full-thickness burns create a non-elastic eschar that restricts expansion. Escharotomy is indicated for:

#### Extremity Escharotomy

-   Circumferential burns of arms or legs
-   Evidence of compartment syndrome:

-   Pain out of proportion to examination
-   Pain with passive stretch of muscles
-   Paresthesias
-   Pulselessness (late finding)
-   Compartment pressure >30-40 mmHg

-   Absence of capillary refill in non-burned tissue distal to burn

#### Thoracic/Truncal Escharotomy

-   Circumferential chest burns restricting respiration
-   Rising peak airway pressures on ventilator
-   Decreased chest wall compliance
-   Hypoxemia despite adequate ventilation settings

#### Abdominal Escharotomy

-   Circumferential abdominal burns
-   Elevated abdominal pressure/compartment syndrome
-   Oliguria unresponsive to fluid resuscitation

### Escharotomy Incision Sites and Technique

Escharotomy Incision Lines - Extremities and Torso

![🔥 Burn Treatment Guide diagram 3](../assets/svgs/burn-treatment-3.svg)

#### Upper Extremity Escharotomy

-   **Incisions:** Medial and lateral forearm and upper arm
-   **Landmarks:**

-   Medial incision: Along medial arm (avoid ulnar nerve and artery)
-   Lateral incision: Along lateral arm (avoid radial nerve)

-   **Technique:** Cut through eschar only, to but not into viable tissue
-   **Depth:** Through eschar and any constrictive edema

#### Lower Extremity Escharotomy

-   **Incisions:** Medial and lateral thigh and leg
-   **Landmarks:**

-   Medial incision: Medial thigh (avoid saphenous vein) and medial leg
-   Lateral incision: Lateral thigh (anterior to vastus lateralis) and lateral leg (anterior to fibula)

-   **Modifications:** May extend to feet (dorsal and plantar surfaces)

#### Thoracic Escharotomy

-   **Incisions:** Two mid-axillary lines from shoulder to groin
-   **Landmarks:** Mid-axillary line (midway between anterior and posterior axillary lines)
-   **Supplemental:** Horizontal incisions across chest if needed for improved mobility

#### Abdominal Escharotomy

-   **Incisions:** Along lateral rectus margins
-   **Alternative:** Transverse incisions if circumferential
-   **Care:** Avoid fascial incision (prevents compartment release)

### Fasciotomy

Surgical incision through fascia to release compartment pressure when compartment syndrome develops despite escharotomy:

-   **Indication:** Compartment pressure >30-40 mmHg despite escharotomy
-   **Timing:** Urgent, within hours of diagnosis
-   **Technique:** Surgical incision through fascial layer of affected compartments
-   **Arms:** Separate incisions for anterior and posterior compartments
-   **Legs:** May require four compartment fasciotomy (anterior, lateral, superficial posterior, deep posterior)
-   **Reopening:** Wounds left open, covered with dressings, closed secondarily or grafted

### Post-Escharotomy Care

-   Cover open incisions with topical agents and dressings
-   Monitor for signs of compartment syndrome (re-check frequently)
-   Reassess limb perfusion and function
-   Prevent infection of open wounds
-   Plan for fasciotomy if compartment pressures remain elevated

:::warning
**Bedside Procedure:** Escharotomy can be performed bedside without anesthesia (eschar is non-viable), but pre-medication with opioids and anxiolytics recommended. Some sources advocate for local anesthesia under the eschar edge where viable tissue begins.
:::

## Skin Grafting Basics

### Principles of Skin Grafting

Skin grafts provide:

-   Permanent wound coverage
-   Prevention of infection and water loss
-   Pain relief
-   Functional and cosmetic improvement
-   Reduced length of hospitalization

### Types of Skin Grafts

#### Autografts (Patient's Own Skin)

-   **Source:** Harvested from unburned areas (donor sites)
-   **Advantages:** Permanent, best outcome, no rejection
-   **Disadvantages:** Limited donor skin, additional wound created, requires multiple operations
-   **Timeline:** First autograft typically 3-5 days after burn

#### Split-Thickness Skin Graft (STSG)

-   **Composition:** Epidermis and part of dermis (0.010-0.015 inches thick)
-   **Healing:** Donor site re-epithelializes in 10-14 days
-   **Contraction:** Moderate (20-40% shrinkage)
-   **Function:** Good but less ideal elasticity and sensation
-   **Scarring:** Minimal to moderate
-   **Advantages:** Multiple grafts possible, better take rate, faster donor healing
-   **Disadvantages:** More contraction, less aesthetic result long-term

#### Full-Thickness Skin Graft (FTSG)

-   **Composition:** Entire epidermis and dermis
-   **Healing:** Donor site must be closed primarily (stitches) or with STSG
-   **Contraction:** Minimal (<10%)
-   **Function:** Better sensation, elasticity, cosmesis
-   **Scarring:** Better long-term appearance
-   **Disadvantages:** Limited donor sites (face, neck, antecubital fossa, groin), lower take rate, requires primary closure of donor site
-   **Use:** Face, hands, joints for better functional outcomes

#### Cultured Epithelial Autografts (CEA)

-   **Process:** Grow patient's own keratinocytes in culture (3-4 weeks)
-   **Coverage:** Massive areas from small biopsies
-   **Advantages:** Permanent, extensive coverage possible
-   **Disadvantages:** Fragile, delayed (slow culture), expensive, high failure rate, requires specialized lab
-   **Use:** Massive burns where donor skin severely limited

#### Allografts (Cadaver Skin)

-   **Source:** Human cadaver skin
-   **Duration:** Temporary (3-4 weeks), eventual rejection
-   **Function:** Biological dressing, reduces pain, prevents infection
-   **Use:** Coverage while awaiting autografts, temporary protection of excised wounds
-   **Disadvantages:** Limited availability, cost, disease transmission risk (rare with proper screening)

#### Xenografts (Porcine Skin)

-   **Source:** Porcine (pig) skin, most common xenograft
-   **Duration:** Temporary (7-10 days), faster rejection than allograft
-   **Function:** Biological dressing, pain relief
-   **Advantages:** Readily available, cost-effective
-   **Disadvantages:** Foreign tissue reaction, shorter duration than allograft
-   **Use:** Temporary coverage in resource-limited settings

### Graft Application Timing

-   **Early excision and grafting:** Preferred within 0-7 days
-   **Benefits:** Reduces infection risk, shortens hospital stay, improves outcomes
-   **Challenges:** Requires multiple OR trips, anesthetic risk, blood loss
-   **Wound preparation:** Excise eschar completely before grafting

### Graft Application Technique (Basic)

-   **Recipient site:** Must be clean, viable tissue with good blood supply
-   **Hemostasis:** Careful attention - bleeding will disrupt graft take
-   **Graft placement:** Applied with dermal side down, epidermal side up
-   **Fixation:** Sutures, staples, or biological adhesives
-   **Meshing:** Often STSG meshed 1.5:1 or higher to cover larger areas (creates pattern, allows drainage)
-   **Bolster dressing:** Gauze bolster held pressure for 5-7 days to promote graft adherence

### Graft Take and Failure

-   **Take rate:** Expect 60-100% with STSGs, 50-80% with FTSGs
-   **Timeline:** Graft incorporates over 3-5 days
-   **Causes of failure:**

-   Seroma/hematoma (collection under graft)
-   Infection
-   Poor graft immobilization
-   Inadequate recipient bed vascularity

-   **Assessment:** Graft take visible by day 5-7 (graft adheres, becomes vascularized)

:::info-box
**Resource-Limited Settings:** When cultured epithelial autografts and advanced dressings unavailable, STSG from unburned areas remains the gold standard for permanent coverage, supported by topical agents and frequent dressing changes.
:::

## Infection Prevention and Treatment

### Infection in Burn Wounds

Burn wounds are highly susceptible to infection due to:

-   Loss of skin barrier
-   Immune suppression
-   Eschar provides ideal bacterial growth medium
-   Impaired neutrophil function
-   Altered wound vascularity

### Common Causative Organisms

### Infection Prevention Strategies

#### Topical Prophylaxis

-   **Silver sulfadiazine:** Broad-spectrum, standard choice
-   **Mafenide:** Better eschar penetration, reserve for zones of eschar
-   **Honey-based:** Effective, natural antimicrobial
-   **Iodine-based:** Broad-spectrum but risk of toxicity with large burns
-   **Bacitracin:** For small, superficial wounds
-   **Frequency:** Once or twice daily depending on agent

#### Systemic Prophylaxis

-   **Not recommended:** No benefit from prophylactic systemic antibiotics
-   **Reserve:** Systemic antibiotics for documented infection only
-   **Exception:** Some use penicillin for strep prophylaxis in first 48 hours (controversial)

#### Wound Care

-   Daily debridement removes necrotic tissue that harbors bacteria
-   Early excision of eschar eliminates bacterial growth medium
-   Frequent dressing changes with topical agents
-   Sterile technique during all wound procedures

#### Infection Control

-   Isolation precautions (contact isolation)
-   Hand hygiene critical
-   Dedicated equipment for burn patient
-   Prevent cross-contamination to other wounds

### Diagnosis of Burn Wound Infection

-   **Clinical signs:** Increased erythema, edema, purulent drainage
-   **Systemic signs:** Fever, tachycardia, leukocytosis
-   **Sudden deterioration:** Increased organ dysfunction, sepsis
-   **Wound culture:** >10^5 organisms/gram tissue indicates infection (quantitative culture)
-   **Biopsy:** Histology showing organisms invading viable tissue (gold standard)
-   **Blood cultures:** Positive in invasive infection/sepsis

### Treatment of Burn Wound Infection

#### Fever/Sepsis in Burn Patient

-   **Assume wound infection:** Until proven otherwise
-   **Evaluate:** Blood cultures, wound culture/biopsy, imaging for other sources
-   **Management:**

-   Increase topical agent frequency or change agent
-   More aggressive debridement
-   Start empiric broad-spectrum antibiotics
-   Urgent surgical debridement if significant eschar involvement

#### Empiric Antibiotic Choice

Until culture results available:

-   **Common regimen:** Fluoroquinolone (ciprofloxacin) + Gram-positive coverage (vancomycin or cephalosporin)
-   **Alternative:** Anti-pseudomonal penicillin (piperacillin-tazobactam) + vancomycin
-   **Dosing:** Often require higher doses due to altered pharmacokinetics in burns
-   **Monotherapy options:** Piperacillin-tazobactam, carbapenems

#### Antifungal Therapy

-   **Candida:** Topical agents (nystatin, clotrimazole) typically sufficient
-   **Systemic infection:** Fluconazole (first-line) or echinocandins (if fluconazole resistant)
-   **Timing:** Often later in hospital course with prolonged antibiotic use

:::warning
**Invasive Burn Wound Infection:** Characterized by bacterial invasion of viable tissue beneath eschar. Presents with rapid eschar discoloration, tissue necrosis, and systemic toxicity. Requires urgent extensive debridement and aggressive antibiotics. High mortality if untreated.
:::

### Monitoring During Treatment

-   Serial wound appearance and culture
-   Temperature curve trends
-   WBC count
-   Inflammatory markers (CRP, procalcitonin)
-   Organ function (renal, hepatic, coagulation)
-   Repeat imaging if clinical deterioration

## Nutrition Support in Burn Patients

### Hypermetabolic Response to Burn Injury

Major burns trigger profound metabolic changes:

-   **Increased metabolic rate:** 20-100% above baseline (varies with burn size and stage)
-   **Protein catabolism:** Massive nitrogen loss from wound drainage and urine
-   **Stress hormones:** Catecholamines, cortisol, glucagon drive metabolism
-   **Immune dysfunction:** Malnutrition worsens immunosuppression
-   **Impaired wound healing:** Protein/nutrient deficiency delays grafting and epithelialization

### Curreri Formula for Caloric Needs

Daily Calories = 25 × Body Weight (kg) + 40 × %TBSA Burned Alternative (Toronto Formula): Daily Calories = 4920 × %TBSA + 1500 kcal (baseline) Additional adjustments: - Add 10% for sepsis - Add 20% for multiple operations - Subtract 10-15% after epithelialization begins

**Example calculation:**

-   70 kg patient with 30% TBSA burn
-   Curreri: 25 × 70 + 40 × 30 = 1,750 + 1,200 = 2,950 kcal/day
-   If septic: 2,950 × 1.10 = 3,245 kcal/day

### Protein Requirements

-   **Standard requirement:** 0.8 g/kg/day (non-burned population)
-   **Burn requirement:** 1.5-3.0 g/kg/day (severe burns require highest intake)
-   **General guideline:** 1.5-2.0 g/kg/day for most burns
-   **Assessment:** Monitor nitrogen balance (24-hour urinary urea nitrogen), wound drainage losses

### Nutritional Assessment

### Route of Nutrition

#### Enteral Nutrition (Preferred)

-   **Advantages:** Maintains GI integrity, reduces translocation, more physiologic, cost-effective
-   **Timing:** Start within 6-12 hours of injury
-   **Method:**

-   Nasogastric tube (most common)
-   Nasojejunal (if gastric intolerance)
-   Postpyloric feeding (reduces aspiration risk)

-   **Feeding:** Continuous infusion often better tolerated than bolus
-   **Formula:** High-protein formulas preferred (e.g., Trauma, Pulmocare, proprietary burn formulas)
-   **Complications:** Diarrhea (common), aspiration, tube occlusion, abdominal distension

#### Parenteral Nutrition (TPN)

-   **Indication:** When enteral route unavailable or inadequate
-   **Advantages:** Delivers nutrients regardless of GI function
-   **Disadvantages:** Hepatic steatosis, line complications, expensive, metabolic derangements
-   **Timing:** Consider after 7-10 days if unable to achieve goal enterally
-   **Composition:** Balanced carbohydrate/lipid, high amino acids (2-2.5 g/kg)

#### Combination Approach

-   Start enteral feeding early
-   Supplement with TPN if enteral insufficient
-   Transition to TPN alone if enteral intolerant
-   Goal: 80%+ calories enterally if possible

### Specific Nutrient Considerations

#### Carbohydrates

-   Primary fuel source (40-50% of calories)
-   Dextrose preferred in early phase (improves wound healing)
-   Monitor for hyperglycemia (common in burns)
-   Goal: Glucose 150-200 mg/dL

#### Lipids

-   20-30% of total calories
-   Essential for cell membrane, hormones, immune function
-   Early high-dose lipids may impair immune function (limit to 1-1.5 g/kg/day)

#### Micronutrients

-   **Vitamin C (Ascorbic acid):** 500 mg BID-QID (wound healing, collagen synthesis)
-   **Vitamin A:** 10,000 IU daily (epithelialization, immune function)
-   **Zinc:** 25-50 mg daily (protein synthesis, immune function, wound healing)
-   **Selenium:** 100-200 micrograms daily (antioxidant, immune support)
-   **Iron:** Supplementation for replacement of losses
-   **Calcium/Vitamin D:** Critical for bone health during prolonged immobility

### Monitoring and Adjustment

-   **Daily weights:** Expected 0.5-1 kg/day weight loss early
-   **Intake/output:** Track feeding volume delivered vs. ordered
-   **Tolerance:** Monitor for diarrhea, gastric residuals, abdominal distension
-   **Labs (weekly):** Albumin, prealbumin, glucose, electrolytes, trace elements
-   **Adjust calories:** Based on weight loss trajectory and metabolic markers
-   **End of resuscitation:** Reduce calories by 10-15% to prevent overfeeding

:::info-box
**Early Feeding:** Early enteral feeding (within 6-12 hours) reduces sepsis risk, maintains GI integrity, shortens hospital stay, and improves survival in major burns.
:::

## Pain Management Strategies

### Pain in Burn Injuries

Burn pain is complex and often severe:

-   **Types:** Background pain, procedural pain, breakthrough pain
-   **Factors:** Burn depth, extent, procedures (dressing changes, debridement)
-   **Impact:** Impairs wound care, delays healing, increases infection risk
-   **Challenge:** Severe pain despite high opioid doses (opioid tolerance)

### Analgesic Agents

#### Opioids

-   **First-line for severe burn pain**
-   **IV administration:** Morphine or hydromorphone
-   **Dosing:** Often requires higher doses than non-burned population
-   **Titration:** Dose to effect before procedures
-   **Background pain:** Long-acting opioids (extended-release morphine) for baseline pain
-   **Breakthrough:** Short-acting IV opioids for acute exacerbations
-   **Tolerance:** Develops rapidly - rotation and dose escalation often necessary
-   **Side effects:** Respiratory depression, constipation, ileus (common), nausea

#### Non-Opioid Analgesics

-   **NSAIDs:** Useful adjuncts (ibuprofen, ketorolac)
-   **Limitation:** Ceiling effect, renal/GI concerns in burn setting
-   **Acetaminophen:** Useful for mild to moderate pain

#### Regional Anesthesia

-   **Epidural analgesia:** Excellent for lower extremity burns
-   **Peripheral nerve blocks:** Useful for specific areas
-   **Advantages:** Targeted pain relief, opioid-sparing, improved perfusion
-   **Disadvantages:** Technical expertise required, catheter management

#### Ketamine

-   **Emerging role in burn pain management**
-   **Advantages:** Maintains airway reflexes, dissociative analgesia, opioid-sparing
-   **Dosing:** Low-dose infusion (0.5-2 mcg/kg/min) or bolus dosing
-   **Use:** Particularly valuable for procedural pain

#### Benzodiazepines

-   **Anxiety relief and amnesia:** Midazolam or lorazepam
-   **Adjunct to opioids**
-   **Caution:** Risk of tolerance, dependence, respiratory depression when combined with opioids

#### Other Adjuncts

-   **Gabapentin/Pregabalin:** Useful for neuropathic pain, anxiety
-   **Topical agents:** Lidocaine spray, EMLA cream for minor procedures
-   **Nitrous oxide:** Useful for dressing changes in acute phase

### Pain Management by Phase

#### Acute Phase (First 24-48 Hours)

-   IV opioids (morphine/hydromorphone) continuously or as needed
-   NSAIDs if no contraindications
-   Benzodiazepines for anxiety
-   Regional anesthesia if appropriate

#### Procedural Pain (Dressing Changes, Debridement)

-   Opioid dose increase 20-30 minutes before
-   Benzodiazepines for anxiety/amnesia
-   Topical anesthetics under dressings
-   Consider ketamine for major debridement
-   Epidural/nerve blocks for limb procedures
-   Background opioid infusion with boluses for procedure-related pain

#### Intermediate Phase (Week 2+)

-   Transition to extended-release opioids for baseline pain
-   Short-acting for breakthrough/procedural pain
-   Continue adjunctive agents (gabapentin, NSAIDs)
-   Psychological support and coping strategies

#### Rehabilitation Phase

-   Gradual opioid taper as wounds heal
-   Focus on non-opioid and psychological interventions
-   Manage withdrawal symptoms carefully

### Non-Pharmacological Approaches

-   **Psychological support:** Reduce anxiety and catastrophizing
-   **Cognitive behavioral therapy:** Coping strategies
-   **Relaxation techniques:** Breathing exercises, imagery
-   **Distraction:** Music, virtual reality
-   **Hypnosis:** Useful for some patients
-   **Physical therapy:** Maintains function despite pain

:::warning
**Opioid Tolerance:** Severe burns often result in rapid opioid tolerance. Patients may require very high opioid doses. Rotation between opioids, combination with NMDA antagonists (ketamine), and adjunctive agents crucial. Plan for difficult weaning in rehabilitation phase.
:::

## Rehabilitation and Contracture Prevention

### Goals of Burn Rehabilitation

-   Restore function and mobility
-   Prevent contractures and deformities
-   Optimize cosmetic outcome
-   Psychological reintegration
-   Return to activities of daily living (ADLs)

### Pathophysiology of Contracture Formation

-   **Mechanism:** Myofibroblasts in granulation tissue contract, reducing scar size
-   **Timeline:** Begins during healing, progresses for months to years post-injury
-   **Factors:** Depth of burn (deeper = more contraction), wound size, location
-   **Problem areas:** Joints (flexion contractures), neck (if circumferential), axilla, groin

### Early Mobilization

-   **Timing:** Begin as soon as hemodynamically stable and pain controlled
-   **Goals:**

-   Maintain joint range of motion
-   Prevent muscle atrophy
-   Improve pulmonary function
-   Psychological benefit

-   **Activities:** Passive range of motion (ROM) during acute phase
-   **Progression:** Active-assisted then active ROM
-   **Frequency:** Multiple sessions daily (often coordinated with dressing changes)

### Pressure Garments (Compression)

-   **Mechanism:** External pressure (15-25 mmHg) reduces collagen deposition and scar thickness
-   **Timing:** Begin after wound closure or skin grafting (typically week 2-3)
-   **Duration:** Continue for 6-12 months post-injury or until scars mature
-   **Types:**

-   Custom-made elastic garments (most effective)
-   Splints and braces for joints
-   Face masks (if facial burns)

-   **Compliance:** Critical - often poor due to discomfort, itching, aesthetic concerns
-   **Care:** Laundering, regular replacement as scar matures

### Splinting and Positioning

-   **Static splints:** Maintain optimal position during sleep/rest
-   **Dynamic splints:** Provide low-load prolonged stretch during day
-   **Positioning:**

-   Neck: Neutral or extension (avoid flexion)
-   Shoulders: Abduction (avoid adduction)
-   Elbows: Extension (avoid flexion)
-   Wrists: Extension (avoid flexion)
-   Hands: Intrinsic plus position (MCPs flexed, IPs extended)
-   Hips: Abduction (avoid flexion/adduction)
-   Knees: Extension (avoid flexion)
-   Ankles: Dorsiflexion (avoid plantarflexion)

-   **Duration:** Should be worn consistently, especially at night

### Physical Therapy Programs

-   **Stretching:** Low-load prolonged stretch most effective
-   **Strengthening:** Resistance exercises as healing progresses
-   **Scar tissue mobilization:** Massage, ultrasound
-   **Functional training:** ADLs, work simulation, return to activities
-   **Frequency:** Often multiple daily sessions in acute phase, tapering in rehabilitation phase

### Surgical Management of Contractures

-   **Indications:** Contractures preventing function despite conservative therapy
-   **Procedures:**

-   Z-plasty: Scar repositioning for linear scars
-   Scar excision and grafting: For hypertrophic/contracted scars
-   Serial excision: Multiple procedures to release contractures
-   Tissue expansion: Preparation for scar revision

-   **Timing:** After scar maturation (typically 6+ months)

### Scar Management

-   **Timeline:** Scars continue changing for 12-24 months
-   **Early phase:** Hypertrophic scars common, improve with pressure
-   **Treatments:**

-   Pressure garments/splints (primary)
-   Topical silicone gels/sheeting
-   Intralesional steroid injections (for persistent hypertrophy)
-   Laser therapy (pulsed dye laser for erythema)
-   Chemical peels (for superficial pigmentation)
-   Microdermabrasion

### Psychological Support

-   **Acute phase:** Anxiety, depression, pain management
-   **Rehabilitation:** Body image concerns, return to activities
-   **Long-term:** PTSD, depression, social reintegration
-   **Interventions:** Counseling, cognitive-behavioral therapy, support groups
-   **Family support:** Education, involvement in therapy

:::info-box
**Key Point:** Contracture prevention is much easier than treatment. Start early mobilization, pressure garments, and splinting immediately after wound closure for best outcomes.
:::

## Inhalation Injury

### Types of Inhalation Injury

#### Thermal Injury

-   **Mechanism:** Direct heat to airway
-   **Location:** Typically upper airway (heat dissipates)
-   **Manifestations:** Stridor, voice changes, difficulty swallowing
-   **Risk:** Airway obstruction, requires monitoring for swelling

#### Smoke Inhalation (Chemical)

-   **Mechanism:** Inhalation of toxic combustion products
-   **Common products:** Carbon monoxide (CO), cyanide, aldehydes, ammonia
-   **Location:** Typically lower airway/lung parenchyma
-   **Manifestations:** Cough, dyspnea, respiratory distress, pulmonary edema

### Carbon Monoxide (CO) Poisoning

-   **Mechanism:** Binds hemoglobin with affinity 200-250x greater than O2
-   **Result:** Carboxyhemoglobin (COHb) formation, reduced oxygen delivery
-   **Symptoms:** Headache, confusion, loss of consciousness
-   **Diagnosis:** Serum carboxyhemoglobin level (normal <2%, symptoms at >10%)
-   **Treatment:** High-flow oxygen (100% O2 reduces half-life from 4-5 hours to 60 minutes)
-   **Hyperbaric oxygen:** Consider for COHb >25%, neurologic symptoms, or pregnancy (though evidence debated)

### Cyanide Toxicity

-   **Source:** Combustion of synthetic materials (plastics, wool, silk)
-   **Mechanism:** Inhibits cytochrome c oxidase, prevents aerobic metabolism
-   **Presentation:** Rapid loss of consciousness, cardiac dysrhythmias, acidosis
-   **Diagnosis:** Clinical suspicion (fire in enclosed space, unconscious despite low COHb)
-   **Treatment:** Hydroxocobalamin (preferred antidote) or sodium thiosulfate
-   **Prognosis:** Rapid deterioration if untreated; survival possible with prompt recognition and treatment

### Clinical Assessment of Inhalation Injury

#### Immediate Indicators

-   Fire in enclosed space
-   Facial burns or singed nasal hairs
-   Carbonaceous sputum
-   Stridor (thermal injury)
-   Altered mental status (CO/cyanide)

#### Diagnostic Tools

-   **Arterial blood gas:** Assess metabolic acidosis, CO2 retention
-   **Carboxyhemoglobin level:** Specific for CO exposure
-   **Cyanide levels:** Not always available acutely; treatment based on clinical suspicion
-   **Flexible fiberoptic bronchoscopy:** Gold standard for diagnosis; visualizes airway injury
-   **Chest X-ray:** May be normal early; check for pulmonary edema

### Airway Management in Inhalation Injury

-   **High suspicion = Intubate early:** Airway swelling progresses, making later intubation difficult/impossible
-   **Do NOT wait for:** Stridor, hypoxemia, or respiratory failure
-   **Timing:** Within first 6-12 hours if signs present
-   **Technique:** Prepare for difficult airway; may need smaller endotracheal tube
-   **Post-intubation:** Frequent suctioning for carbonaceous material; maintain airway clearance

### Pulmonary Management

-   **Oxygen therapy:** High-flow oxygen (100% initially)
-   **Ventilator strategy:** Protective ventilation (low tidal volumes 6-8 mL/kg)
-   **Secretion management:** Aggressive bronchial hygiene (suctioning, nebulizers)
-   **Mucolytics:** Nebulized heparin and N-acetylcysteine to thin secretions
-   **Infection prophylaxis:** Risk of bacterial infection from damaged mucosa

### Complications

-   **Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS):** From direct chemical injury
-   **Bacterial pneumonia:** Common superinfection
-   **Ventilator-associated pneumonia (VAP):** With prolonged intubation
-   **Pulmonary edema:** May appear 24-72 hours post-injury

:::warning
**Cyanide Poisoning Alert:** Consider in fire survivors who are unconscious or have severe acidosis despite low CO levels. Treatment should not be delayed for confirmatory blood levels. Hydroxocobalamin is preferred (safer than sodium nitrite/thiosulfate combination).
:::

## Chemical and Electrical Burns

### Chemical Burns

#### Assessment

-   **Identify agent:** Critical for specific treatment
-   **Duration of exposure:** Longer exposure = deeper injury
-   **Concentration:** Higher concentration = more severe
-   **Mechanism:** Acid vs. alkali (alkali often deeper)

#### Acid Burns

-   **Examples:** Sulfuric acid, hydrochloric acid, nitric acid
-   **Mechanism:** Coagulation necrosis (eschar formation limits depth)
-   **Characteristics:** Usually demarcated, limited depth
-   **Treatment:** Copious irrigation with water
-   **Neutralization:** Generally NOT recommended (exothermic reactions can worsen injury)

#### Alkali Burns

-   **Examples:** Sodium hydroxide, potassium hydroxide, lime
-   **Mechanism:** Liquefaction necrosis (penetrates deeply)
-   **Characteristics:** Progressive, poorly demarcated, often deeper than thermal burns of similar appearance
-   **Treatment:** Prolonged copious irrigation (may need >30 minutes)
-   **Timeline:** Ongoing damage possible for hours; reassess depth at 24 hours

#### Special Chemicals

-   **Hydrofluoric acid:** Causes systemic toxicity (hypocalcemia, hyperkalemia); treat with calcium gluconate (topical and IV)
-   **Lime (calcium oxide):** Reacts with water generating heat; brush off particulate first, then irrigate
-   **Phenol:** Can cause systemic toxicity; some recommend alcohol wash before irrigation
-   **Phosphorus:** Spontaneously ignites when exposed to air; keep wet with saline-soaked dressings

#### Treatment Protocol

-   Remove contaminated clothing immediately
-   Brush off particulate matter (wear gloves)
-   Immediate copious irrigation with water (gold standard)
-   Continue irrigation for 15-60 minutes depending on chemical
-   Cover with dressings after irrigation complete
-   Observe for systemic effects (some chemicals absorbed)
-   Treat as thermal burn (size assessment, fluid resuscitation, infection prevention)

### Electrical Burns

#### Pathophysiology

-   **Voltage:** Determines severity

-   **Low voltage:**<1000V (household current)
-   **High voltage:** >1000V (industrial)

-   **Current type:** Alternating current (AC) more dangerous than direct current (DC)
-   **Duration of contact:** Longer = deeper injury
-   **Tissue resistance:** Varies by tissue type (bone > fat > muscle > blood)
-   **Pathway:** Current path through body determines injury pattern

#### Clinical Manifestations

-   **Skin:** Entry and exit wounds ("contact burns")
-   **Deep tissues:** Often more severely damaged than appearance suggests
-   **Muscle:** Rhabdomyolysis → myoglobinuria → acute kidney injury
-   **Cardiac:** Dysrhythmias (ventricular fibrillation, asystole)
-   **Nervous:** Altered mental status, respiratory paralysis (if current traverses chest)
-   **Vessels:** Thrombosis from heat injury

#### Initial Management

-   **Scene safety:** Turn off power source; do not enter if power lines may still be live
-   **ACLS/BLS:** May need CPR; some victims of cardiac arrest can be resuscitated despite prolonged downtime
-   **IV access:** Two large-bore IVs
-   **Fluid resuscitation:** Use electrical burn formulas (often higher than thermal)
-   **Foley catheter:** Essential for monitoring urine output (myoglobinuria indicator)
-   **ECG:** Screen for cardiac dysrhythmias, myocardial infarction
-   **Admission:** All significant electrical burns require hospitalization

#### Complications to Monitor

-   **Myoglobinuria:** Dark brown urine; treat with aggressive fluids to maintain urine output 200-300 mL/hr
-   **Acute kidney injury:** From rhabdomyolysis; may require dialysis
-   **Compartment syndrome:** Often bilateral in electrical burns; high threshold for fasciotomy
-   **Dysrhythmias:** Can occur immediately or hours later
-   **Aspiration:** If patient lost consciousness; intubate if concern
-   **Spinal cord injury:** From muscle contractions; immobilize until cleared

#### Fluid Resuscitation in Electrical Burns

-   **Usually requires higher rates:** Than thermal burns of equivalent surface area
-   **Titrate to urine output:** 200-300 mL/hr (versus 0.5 mL/kg/hr for thermal)
-   **Monitor for overload:** As fluid requirements decrease, taper cautiously
-   **Alkalization:** Some advocate sodium bicarbonate in fluids (maintain urine pH >6.5) to prevent myoglobin precipitation
-   **Duration:** May need several days of aggressive hydration

#### Specific Electrical Burn Scenarios

-   **High-voltage injury:** Entry and exit burns usually obvious; deep tissue damage often severe
-   **Alternating current (household):** Can cause tetanic muscle contraction, respiratory paralysis from chest current
-   **Lightning strike:** Often massive high-voltage; frequently fatal or result in severe disability

:::warning
**Electrical Burns Rule:** The skin manifestation typically underestimates the depth of internal injury. Assume significant deep tissue damage even if skin injury appears mild. Aggressive fluid resuscitation and high threshold for fasciotomy essential.
:::

## Common Mistakes in Burn Care

### Hot-Water Forearm Burn: First Aid Ladder

For a fresh scald or hot-water burn on the forearm, use this quick first-aid
sequence:

1. **Cool first:** Hold the burned area under cool running water for 10-20
   minutes as soon as possible. Remove rings, bracelets, watches, or tight
   clothing near the forearm before swelling traps them.
2. **Cover after cooling:** Pat around the area gently and cover the burn with
   a clean, non-stick sterile dressing or clean cloth. Keep it loose; do not
   wrap tightly around a swelling arm.
3. **Do not do these:** Do not apply ice directly. Do not put butter, oil,
   toothpaste, herbs, powders, or other home remedies on the burn. Do not pop
   or peel blisters.
4. **Escalate care:** Seek medical help for any deep, white, brown, black,
   leathery, numb, circumferential, large, worsening, infected, face/hand/
   genital/joint, chemical, electrical, or breathing-related burn. Also seek
   care if pain, redness, swelling, drainage, fever, or function worsens.

### Fluid Resuscitation Errors

#### Under-Resuscitation

-   **Problem:** Insufficient fluid leads to shock, organ failure, death
-   **Cause:** Using formulas as maximum rather than starting point; inadequate reassessment
-   **Prevention:** Titrate to urine output and clinical parameters
-   **Signs:** Oliguria, tachycardia, hypotension, metabolic acidosis

#### Over-Resuscitation ("Fluid Creep")

-   **Problem:** Excessive fluid causes abdominal compartment syndrome, ARDS, increased infection
-   **Cause:** Using Parkland in modern era, not titrating, continuous adjustments upward
-   **Prevention:** Use Modified Brooke, strict titration to urine output only
-   **Signs:** Abdominal distension, elevated bladder pressure, respiratory compromise

#### Using Wrong Fluids

-   **Problem:** Normal saline causes hyperchloremic acidosis; dextrose solutions worsen hyperglycemia
-   **Prevention:** Use Lactated Ringers for burn resuscitation

### Airway Management Errors

#### Delayed Intubation in Inhalation Injury

-   **Problem:** Waiting for stridor or hypoxemia means airway already severely compromised
-   **Consequence:** May become impossible to intubate; emergency tracheostomy required
-   **Prevention:** Intubate early (within 6-12 hours) if any sign of inhalation injury

#### Inadequate Tube Size

-   **Problem:** Smaller tubes required due to edema; standard-size tubes may not fit
-   **Prevention:** Have multiple sizes available; prepare for difficult airway

### Wound Care Errors

#### Inadequate Debridement

-   **Problem:** Leaving nonviable tissue promotes infection, prolongs hospital stay
-   **Prevention:** Aggressive early excision (within 0-7 days)

#### Overly Aggressive Debridement

-   **Problem:** Removing viable tissue causes unnecessary blood loss and increases TBSA
-   **Prevention:** Use proper technique (tangential excision to viable tissue)

#### Wrong Topical Agent

-   **Problem:** Some agents not indicated for certain burn depths or areas
-   **Example:** SSD on face (cosmetic staining); mafenide for shallow burns (unnecessary toxicity)
-   **Prevention:** Know properties and indications of each agent

#### Infrequent Dressing Changes

-   **Problem:** Allows bacterial overgrowth, increases infection risk
-   **Prevention:** Daily to BID dressing changes with pain control

### Nutrition Errors

#### Delayed Feeding

-   **Problem:** Waiting days to feed allows malnutrition, worsens outcome
-   **Prevention:** Start enteral feeding within 6-12 hours of injury

#### Inadequate Protein/Calories

-   **Problem:** Underestimating needs leads to malnutrition, poor wound healing
-   **Prevention:** Use Curreri formula as starting point; increase as needed

#### Using Wrong Route

-   **Problem:** Relying only on TPN when enteral possible causes more complications
-   **Prevention:** Maximize enteral feeding; use TPN only when necessary

### Pain Management Errors

#### Inadequate Analgesia

-   **Problem:** Severe pain impairs wound care, immune function, healing
-   **Prevention:** Aggressive titration of opioids before procedures

#### Opioid Phobia

-   **Problem:** Withholding opioids due to addiction concerns; severe burns warrant large doses
-   **Prevention:** Understand that tolerance and physical dependence in context of severe pain is expected and manageable

### Infection Control Errors

#### Prophylactic Antibiotics

-   **Problem:** No benefit from systemic prophylaxis; promotes resistance
-   **Prevention:** Use topical agents only; reserve systemic for documented infection

#### Missed Infection Diagnosis

-   **Problem:** Treating fever as non-infectious when wound infection present
-   **Prevention:** High index of suspicion; culture wounds, adjust topical agents, debride if concern

### Rehabilitation Errors

#### Delayed Mobilization

-   **Problem:** Contractures form quickly; once established, difficult to reverse
-   **Prevention:** Start passive ROM on day 1-2; active as tolerated; aggressive splinting

#### Pressure Garments Not Applied

-   **Problem:** Hypertrophic scars form without pressure; difficult to manage later
-   **Prevention:** Custom garments ordered as soon as wound healed/grafted; patient education on compliance

### Overall System Errors

#### Transfer Delays

-   **Problem:** Severe burns need specialized care; delays worsen outcomes
-   **Prevention:** Identify early, call burn center immediately, arrange transfer

#### Failure to Reassess Burn Extent

-   **Problem:** Initial TBSA estimate wrong; affects resuscitation planning
-   **Prevention:** Use Lund-Browder chart; reassess at 24 hours as edema resolves

#### Failure to Recognize Comorbidity

-   **Problem:** Underlying disease masked by burn; may change management
-   **Prevention:** Full history and physical; consider other injuries from trauma mechanism

:::warning
**Learning Point:** Most errors are preventable through systematic protocols, frequent reassessment, and adherence to evidence-based practices. Communication with specialized burn center essential for complex cases.
:::
