Metalworking begins with understanding metals themselves. Before you forge, cast, or repair anything, you need to know what you're working with—what metals are made of, why they behave differently, and where to find them. This guide covers the fundamentals that make metalworking practical and safe.

<section id="what-metals-are">

## What Metals Are

Metals are elements—the simplest chemical substances. Unlike wood or stone, which are composites of many elements, a pure metal is one element in its elemental form. On Earth, metals are rarely found completely pure; they're usually bound with other elements in ore (rock that contains valuable metal compounds).

### The Atomic Picture (Simplified)

Every element is made of atoms. Metals have a special structure: their outer electrons are loosely bound and can move freely. This "electron sea" is what makes metals conductive, malleable (bendable), and ductile (drawable into wire). Non-metals, by contrast, hold their electrons tightly.

**Why this matters for your work:**
- Free electrons = conducts heat and electricity
- Flexible atomic structure = can be reshaped without breaking (unlike brittle materials)
- Reactive = can rust, oxidize, or combine with other elements

### Alloys

Pure metals are rare in practical work. Most metals you'll encounter are **alloys**—intentional combinations of a base metal with other elements. Steel is iron mixed with carbon. Bronze is copper mixed with tin. Brass is copper mixed with zinc.

**Why alloys?** Pure metals are often too soft for practical use. Adding other elements changes properties: harder, stronger, more corrosion-resistant, or easier to work.

<section id="common-metals">

## Common Metals and Their Properties

### Iron and Steel

**Where found:** Rust-colored ore (magnetite, hematite); scrap metal; old tools; structures

**Pure iron:** Soft, very malleable, rusts quickly in moist air

**Steel (iron + 0.1–2% carbon):**
- Harder and stronger than iron
- Still malleable when hot
- Can be heat-treated to adjust hardness
- Rusts without protection (paint, oil, rust-resistant alloys)

**Common uses:** Tools, nails, bolts, structural material, cookware

**In practice:** Steel is your workhorse metal. It's abundant in salvage, affordable, and forgiving to work with.

### Copper

**Where found:** Electrical wire, plumbing pipes, old coins, decorative items, ore (green or blue minerals)

**Properties:**
- Excellent electrical and heat conductor
- Soft and very malleable
- Reddish color, polishes to bright shine
- Oxidizes to green patina (protective coating)
- Does not rust like iron

**Common uses:** Electrical wiring, plumbing, decorative items, cookware, joints/brazing

**In practice:** Soft and easy to shape; deforms permanently when work-hardened (becomes brittle if hammered repeatedly). Can be annealed (heated) to soften.

### Brass (Copper + Zinc)

**Where found:** Cartridge casings, door hardware, decorative items, plumbing fittings

**Properties:**
- Harder than pure copper
- Gold-colored
- Good electrical conductor
- Does not rust
- Lower melting point than steel (easier to cast or melt)

**Common uses:** Ammunition reloading, decorative work, valves, fittings

**In practice:** Easier to machine than steel. Poisonous fumes if overheated excessively; work in ventilation.

### Bronze (Copper + Tin, sometimes with other elements)

**Where found:** Bells, bearings, statues, ancient tools, decorative items

**Properties:**
- Harder and stronger than pure copper
- Excellent fatigue resistance
- Does not rust
- Lower melting point than steel
- Rings with clear tone (bells, gongs)

**Common uses:** Bearings, bells, statues, corrosion-resistant components

**In practice:** More difficult to obtain than brass or iron in modern salvage, but highly valued for its durability and low rust rate.

### Aluminum

**Where found:** Beverage cans, foil, windows, structural parts, aircraft components, automotive parts

**Properties:**
- Very light (one-third the density of steel)
- Soft and malleable
- Excellent heat conductor
- Oxidizes quickly to white aluminum oxide (protective)
- NOT magnetic (useful for identification)
- Lower melting point (~660°C vs 1500°C for steel)

**Common uses:** Lightweight structures, cookware, automotive, aerospace

**In practice:** Can be forged, but requires lower temperatures and careful technique. Joins poorly with most adhesives; requires specialized welding.

### Lead

**Where found:** Old paint, solder, plumbing, car batteries, radiation shielding

**Properties:**
- Very soft and heavy
- Low melting point (~330°C)
- Highly toxic if ingested or inhaled (as dust or fume)
- Corrosion-resistant

**In practice:** Useful for casting and soft soldering, but HANDLE WITH EXTREME CAUTION. Never inhale lead fumes. Wash hands thoroughly. Not recommended for beginners.

:::warning
**Lead Safety:** Lead is toxic. Avoid prolonged contact. Never melt lead indoors or without proper ventilation. Do not use in applications where it contacts food or water. Never remove lead paint by sanding or heating—professional remediation required.
:::

### Tin

**Where found:** Solder, old tin cans (actually steel with tin coating), decorative items

**Properties:**
- Soft and malleable
- Low melting point (~230°C)
- Corrosion-resistant
- Silver color

**In practice:** Mostly used as solder (mixed with lead traditionally, or with silver/copper in modern alloys). Not commonly used alone.

### Nickel and Stainless Steel (Iron + Chromium + Nickel)

**Where found:** Coins, decorative items, cookware, cutlery, car parts

**Properties:**
- Very corrosion-resistant
- Harder than plain steel
- Silvery appearance
- Does not rust or patina

**Common uses:** Cookware, cutlery, decorative items, marine components

**In practice:** Requires higher temperatures to work and is more difficult to forge than plain steel, but the corrosion resistance is worth it for certain applications.

<section id="properties-beginners-care">

## Properties That Matter in Practical Work

### Hardness vs. Brittleness

**Hard = resistant to scratching or deformation** (you can't easily dent or bend it)

**Brittle = breaks or shatters under stress** (rather than bending)

These are opposite qualities in many cases:
- **Steel:** Can be hard AND tough (bends slightly before breaking)
- **Cast iron:** Hard but brittle (breaks sharply)
- **Copper:** Soft but tough (bends without breaking)

### Ductility and Malleability

**Ductility:** Ability to be drawn into wire (pulled without breaking)

**Malleability:** Ability to be reshaped by hammering or pressure without breaking

Most metals are malleable when heated. Cold work (at room temperature) also works, but the metal work-hardens:
- Repeated cold hammering makes the metal harder and more brittle
- Solution: Annealing (heating to specific temperature, then slow cooling) restores malleability

### Melting Point

Different metals melt at different temperatures. This matters for:
- **Forging:** You need heat to soften metal without melting it
- **Casting:** You need to melt it completely to pour it into molds
- **Joining:** Soldering uses metals that melt easily; brazing uses higher temps; welding melts the base metal itself

**Common melting points:**
- Lead: 330°C (easy to melt in a household fire)
- Tin: 230°C (very easy)
- Brass: 900°C (moderately easy, requires forge)
- Copper: 1080°C (difficult, requires forge or furnace)
- Steel: 1500°C (very difficult, requires serious furnace)

### Corrosion and Oxidation

**Corrosion** is chemical damage, usually by water and oxygen.

**Oxidation** is combination with oxygen, forming an oxide layer.

- **Iron:** Oxidizes to rust (iron oxide), which is porous and flakes off, exposing fresh metal beneath. Rusts quickly.
- **Copper:** Oxidizes to green patina, which is protective and does not flake. Does not "rust" in the traditional sense.
- **Aluminum:** Oxidizes to white aluminum oxide, which is protective. Does not rust.
- **Stainless steel:** Chromium forms a protective oxide layer that prevents further oxidation.

## Where to Find Metals

### Salvage Sources

**Scrap metal yards:** Organized sources of iron, steel, copper, brass, aluminum. Often have sorted bins.

**Old structures:** Demolished buildings contain beams, pipes, hardware, electrical wiring, fixtures.

**Automotive parts:** Cars and machinery contain steel, aluminum, copper, brass components. Lead-acid batteries contain lead.

**Electronics:** Copper wiring, gold plating on circuit boards (small amounts), and aluminum heat sinks.

**Household items:** Pots, pans, decorative items, old tools, hinges, brackets.

**Farm and industrial salvage:** Equipment, machinery, implements often contain useful metals.

### Identifying What You Have

See the related guide **Tool Identification and Metals** for detailed identification techniques using simple tests (magnet, spark, color, weight, sound).

### Processing Salvage

**Sorting:**
1. Separate by type (magnetic iron/steel vs. non-magnetic)
2. Remove fasteners and hardware
3. Group by intended use (scrap, decent pieces, finished items)

**Cleaning:**
- Light rust: Wire brush or grinding
- Heavy rust: Longer soaking in vinegar or wire-wheel grinding
- Oil/grease: Wash with degreaser or hot water

**Storage:**
- Keep dry (prevent rust formation)
- Organize by type and size for easy access
- Cover piles in dry locations

## Basic Concepts for Your First Projects

### Grain Structure

Metals are made of crystals arranged in a lattice. The arrangement and size of these crystals affects strength and workability.

- **Hot working** (forging) aligns grains favorably and keeps them small (stronger)
- **Cold working** (hammering at room temperature) distorts grains and work-hardens the metal (becomes brittle)
- **Annealing** (controlled heating and cooling) resets grain structure, restoring malleability

### Work Hardening

When you hammer or bend a metal repeatedly at room temperature, the internal structure deforms. The metal becomes harder and more brittle. Eventually, it will crack.

**Solution:** Periodically heat the metal (to specific temperatures depending on the alloy) and cool it slowly. This resets the structure.

This is why metalworking alternates between work (hammer) and annealing (heat).

### Strength vs. Weight

Different metals offer different advantages:
- **Steel:** Strong, moderately heavy, affordable, forgiving
- **Aluminum:** Light, moderate strength, easy to work cold, lower forging temps
- **Copper/Brass:** Heavy, soft, excellent conductivity, excellent appearance
- **Bronze:** Heavy, strong, extremely durable, excellent for bearings

Choose based on what you're making and what you have available.

<section id="safety-first">

## Safety Fundamentals

:::warning
**Hot Metal:** Hot metal does not look obviously hot. Assume all metal in or near a forge is dangerously hot. Always use tongs or tools to handle it. Bare hands touching hot metal causes severe burns instantly.
:::

:::warning
**Sparks and Ash:** Forge work produces sparks and hot particles. Wear eye protection and protective clothing (leather apron, long sleeves, closed-toe shoes). Keep loose clothing away from flames and hot metal.
:::

:::warning
**Fumes:** Some metals produce toxic fumes when heated excessively (especially brass, galvanized steel, and lead). Always work in ventilated spaces. If melting or casting, use outdoor ventilation or a fume hood.
:::

:::warning
**Storage:** Store flammable materials away from forges. Store lead, cadmium, and other toxic materials safely away from children and food. Clearly label hazardous materials.
:::
