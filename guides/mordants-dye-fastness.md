## Overview: Why Dyes Without Mordants Wash Out

An untreated dye molecule is weakly attracted to fiber. In water, the dye dissolves and washes away. A **mordant** acts as a chemical bridge, creating a stable complex that bonds dye tightly to fiber.

**Simple analogy:** Dye alone is like a weak handshake (easily broken). Mordant-dye-fiber complex is like a locked embrace (dye cannot escape).

**Historical motivation:** Before synthetic dyes (post-1850s), all color came from natural sources (plants, minerals). The difference between a color that lasted generations and one that faded after one washing was mordant technique. Medieval textile guilds jealously guarded mordant secrets; they were passed as family trade secrets and protected by guild regulations.

**Post-collapse relevance:** Mordants ensure dyed textiles remain useful through years of washing and sunlight exposure. A community that masters mordanting can produce valuable, durable goods (trade, bartering).

This guide explains the chemistry, common mordants achievable with post-collapse resources, procedure protocols, and quality testing.

## Mordant Chemistry Fundamentals

### The Dye-Fiber-Mordant Complex

**Three components interact:**
1. **Dye molecule** (usually organic; contains color)
2. **Fiber** (wool, cotton, linen; composed of proteins or cellulose)
3. **Mordant** (metal salt; acts as chemical bridge)

**Process:**
1. Mordant binds to fiber (metal cations form chemical bonds with fiber molecules)
2. Dye binds to mordant (dye molecule forms complex with metal ion)
3. Result: Dye-mordant-fiber complex is now locked into place (resistant to water and light)

**Simplified formula:**
```
Fiber-OH (hydroxyl groups) + Metal^n+ (mordant) → Fiber-O-Metal (bound)
Fiber-O-Metal + Dye → Fiber-O-Metal-Dye (stable complex)
```

### Metal Ions as Mordants

Different metal ions produce different colors from the same dye (because the metal affects how dye absorbs light).

<div class="chemistry-box">

**Example: Madder dye (from madder plant root)**
- With alum: Red/orange
- With iron: Dark brown/black
- With copper: Purple/violet
- With tin: Bright orange/scarlet

Same dye, different metal, different color. This is why mordant selection is critical not just for fastness but for desired color shade.

</div>

### Why Fastness Improves With Mordant

**Mechanism:**
- **Dye alone:** Held by weak physical adsorption; water molecules compete with dye for fiber binding sites; dye washes away
- **Dye + mordant:** Dye is covalently bonded to metal; metal is bonded to fiber; complex is far more stable
- **Result:** Wash fastness improves from "fades after 5 washings" to "stable after 50+ washings"

### Alum Preparation from Clay

**Most accessible mordant post-collapse:**

**Procedure:**
1. Identify alum-rich clay (white/light gray color; slightly chalky feel; found in many regions)
2. Soak clay in water (1 kg clay : 2 L water)
3. Let settle 24 hours; clay particles sink
4. Carefully pour off clear liquid (alum-rich water)
5. Boil down liquid to reduce volume by 50% (concentrate alum)
6. Cool; use as mordant (slight turbidity is acceptable)

**Dosage:** ~1% by weight of fiber (10 g alum powder per 1 kg fiber)

### Iron Preparation from Rust or Vinegar

**Procedure:**
1. Collect iron filings (filing steel) or gather rust-coated objects (old nails, machinery)
2. Place in glass jar; cover with vinegar (white or apple cider)
3. Seal jar; let sit 1–2 weeks (rust dissolves in acid)
4. Solution turns brown (ferrous sulfate formed)
5. Filter to remove undissolved rust; dilute solution if too concentrated
6. Store in sealed bottle (oxidizes over time; use within months)

**Dosage:** ~1–2% by weight of fiber (start with 1%; increase if color not dark enough)

### Tannin from Bark & Leaves

**Procedure:**
1. Harvest oak bark (scrape from branches; do not girdle trees), oak galls (small round growths on oak twigs), or black walnuts
2. Dry material or use fresh
3. Boil in water (1 kg plant material : 3 L water) for 1–2 hours
4. Strain through cloth; liquid will be golden/brown
5. Use as tannin bath (no further concentration needed)

**Dosage:** Use full strength tannin bath; soak fiber 30–60 minutes before dyeing

**Note:** Tannin is often used as a pre-mordant with alum (tannin first, then alum) to deepen colors

## Mordanting Procedures by Fiber Type

**Critical principle:** Fiber must be clean and free of oils/finishes before mordanting. Scour fiber in hot water with soap; rinse thoroughly.

### Wool Mordanting (Pre-Mordant Method)

**Wool is protein-based; most responsive to mordants; highest color uptake**

<div class="recipe-step">

**Procedure:**
1. **Prepare fiber:** Wet wool gently in warm water (no hot shocks; prevents felting). Drain.
2. **Dissolve mordant:** Mix alum (10 g per kg wool) in warm water (50°C); stir until dissolved. Pour into large pot.
3. **Submerge wool:** Add wet wool slowly to alum bath; stir gently for first 5 minutes to ensure even saturation. Do not squeeze or wring.
4. **Heat gently:** Over 1 hour, slowly raise temperature to 85–90°C (185–195°F). Do not boil; prevents damage.
5. **Hold temperature:** Maintain 85–90°C for 45–60 minutes. Stir occasionally.
6. **Cool slowly:** Remove from heat; allow to cool to room temperature (2–4 hours) in the bath.
7. **Remove and drain:** Lift wool gently; allow to drain. Squeeze gently (do not wring).
8. **Dry or dye immediately:** Can dry and store (mordant-treated wool stable for months), or proceed to dye bath within hours.

**Result:** Wool now has alum bound to fiber; ready to accept dye.

</div>

**Adding iron (after alum, to deepen color):**
- After alum-mordanted wool has cooled and drained
- Prepare weak iron solution (1–2 tsp iron vinegar per 1 L water)
- Add wool to iron bath; heat to 70°C for 15–20 minutes (not longer; prevents brittleness)
- Cool; drain; proceed to dye bath

### Cotton & Linen Mordanting (Pre-Treatment + Mordant)

**Cellulose fibers do not hold dyes as easily as protein fibers. Require tannin pre-treatment.**

<div class="recipe-step">

**Step 1: Tannin pre-bath**
- Prepare tannin bath (boiled oak bark/gall water)
- Submerge wet cellulose fiber; heat to 85°C; hold 45 minutes
- Cool; drain; rinse in cool water
- Tannin coating now on fiber (helps fiber accept dye)

**Step 2: Alum bath**
- Prepare alum solution (8 g alum per kg fiber; slightly lower than wool)
- Submerge tannin-treated fiber; heat to 85°C; hold 45 minutes
- Cool; drain
- Fiber now has tannin + alum complex

**Step 3: Optional iron for dark colors**
- If deep color desired, add iron bath (same as wool procedure)

**Result:** Cotton/linen now mordanted; ready for dye bath. Color will be less intense than wool, but more durable than unmordanted cellulose.

</div>

**Duration:** Tannin + alum + optional iron takes 3–4 hours total

### Simultaneous Mordanting (Mordant + Dye in One Bath)

**Faster than separate steps; less effective but viable when time is critical**

**Procedure:**
1. Dissolve mordant in dye bath from the start
2. Add fiber; heat as per dye bath procedure
3. Mordant and dye occur simultaneously
4. Color uptake is ~80% of pre-mordant method
5. Suitable for : Quick dyeing for practical fabrics (not high-color-demand items)

### Post-Mordanting (Dye First, Then Mordant)

**Used when specific color shade is desired; or when dye bath cannot be altered**

**Procedure:**
1. Dye fiber in standard dye bath (without mordant)
2. Remove fiber; drain
3. Prepare mordant bath (lukewarm)
4. Submerge dyed fiber; heat slowly to 85°C; hold 30 minutes
5. Cool; drain; dry
6. Color may shift; fastness improves but less than pre-mordant method

**Result:** Color is altered by mordant addition (reds may shift to orange/brown); fastness moderate

## Dye-Bath Preparation & Critical Ratios

### Water Hardness Effects

**Water quality critically affects mordant and dye uptake.**

**Soft water (few minerals; preferred):**
- Mordant dissolves completely
- Dye dissolves evenly
- Fiber accepts color uniformly
- Result: Even color tone

**Hard water (calcium, magnesium ions):**
- Mordant ions precipitate (form compounds with Ca/Mg; become unavailable)
- Dye may not dissolve fully (dye molecules cling to mineral particles)
- Fiber cannot absorb mordant or dye efficiently
- Result: Patchy, uneven color

**Solution in hard water:**
- Pre-treat water: Boil to remove some Ca/Mg; or add wood ash (alkaline; precipitates some minerals); filter
- Use more mordant to compensate for loss (increase by 20–30%)
- Soften water: Add bran (contains compounds that bind minerals); simmer, filter

### Dye-Fiber Ratio

**Dye concentration affects color intensity and uptake:**

**Light colors:** 10–15% dye by weight of fiber
- Example: 1 kg fiber + 100–150 g dried dye plant material
- Produces pale tints; multiple washings over several hours

**Medium colors:** 15–25% dye by weight
- Example: 1 kg fiber + 150–250 g dye material
- Produces standard hues; typical recipes use this range

**Dark colors:** 25–50% dye by weight
- Example: 1 kg fiber + 250–500 g dye material
- Produces deep, saturated colors; longer dye time; sometimes requires multiple dye baths

### Dye-Bath Temperature & Timing

**Most natural dyes release color best at 85–95°C (185–205°F)**

**Procedure (example: madder dye):**
1. Prepare dye bath: Boil dye material in water (separate pot) to extract color; strain
2. Transfer dye liquid to dyeing pot
3. Add pre-mordanted fiber (wet or dry; both acceptable)
4. Heat slowly to 85°C over 30 minutes (faster heating = uneven color)
5. Hold at 85–95°C for 30–60 minutes (longer = more color uptake)
6. Remove from heat; cool slowly (2–4 hours) in the dye bath
7. Remove fiber; drain; rinse in cool water until rinse water runs clear

**Duration:** Total time in dye bath: 2–4 hours for medium colors

### Fiber Movement in Dye Bath

**Critical for even color:**

**Do:** Gently move fiber with wooden stick every 5–10 minutes to ensure all areas contact dye evenly

**Don't:** Squeeze, wring, or allow fiber to sit bunched in one area (causes blotchy color)

**Especially important if dyeing a large mass (>2 kg); may need to split into smaller batches**

## Wash Fastness & Light Fastness Testing

Proper fastness testing determines if mordant technique was successful.

### Wash Fastness Test

**Procedure:**
1. Dye small swatch (10 cm × 10 cm) using same technique as full batch
2. Record original color (photograph or color reference card)
3. Wash swatch in hot water (60°C) with soap, agitating gently
4. Rinse thoroughly in cool water; drain
5. Dry
6. Repeat wash cycle 2–5 times (increase for high-use textiles)
7. Compare to original color: Is fading <5%? <10%? >20%?

**Interpretation:**
- **Excellent (>5 washes, <5% fading):** Mordant technique successful; fabric suitable for everyday wear
- **Good (3–5 washes, 5–10% fading):** Acceptable; sufficient for moderately-used items
- **Poor (1–2 washes, >10% fading):** Mordant inadequate; increase mordant dosage or dye time in future batches

### Light Fastness Test

**Procedure:**
1. Dye swatch same as above
2. Place swatch in sunlight (south-facing window) or under UV light
3. Cover half of swatch with opaque material (to compare faded vs. protected area)
4. Expose for 2–4 weeks (accelerated; or 3–6 months in normal sunlight)
5. Remove cover; compare faded area to protected area
6. Measure color loss (use color-matching cards if available)

**Interpretation:**
- **Excellent (minimal color loss after 4 weeks intense sunlight):** Dye + mordant combination is very light-fast
- **Good (moderate fading; still usable color after 4 weeks):** Acceptable for indoor use or items not in constant sunlight
- **Poor (significant fading in 2 weeks):** Not suitable for outdoor or high-light-exposure use

**Practical note:** Most natural dyes are weaker in light fastness than in wash fastness. Indigo and madder are excellent; weld (yellow) is poor.

### Blending Dyes for Custom Colors

**Layered dyeing technique (overlay):**
1. Dye fiber in first dye bath (e.g., weld for yellow)
2. Remove; dry
3. Re-dye in second dye bath (e.g., madder for red)
4. Result: Blended color (yellow + red = orange-brown)
5. Fastness combines both dyes' properties

**Advantage:** Achieves intermediate colors without pure pigments

**Disadvantage:** Requires multiple dye baths; time-consuming

## Troubleshooting Uneven Dyeing

Common problems and solutions:

**Problem: Blotchy/streaky color (some areas dark, others pale)**
- Cause: Uneven mordant application or fiber bunching in dye bath
- Solution: Ensure gentle fiber movement in mordant and dye baths; spread fiber to avoid clumps; if re-dyeing, pre-treat all areas evenly

**Problem: Color too pale even after long dye bath**
- Cause: Insufficient mordant; or dye material weak (old/degraded); or hard water
- Solution: Increase mordant by 20%; ensure dye material is fresh; soften water if available; increase dye time to 2+ hours

**Problem: Color shifted (expected red, got brown)**
- Cause: Wrong mordant (iron instead of alum); or post-mordanting shift
- Solution: Use specified mordant; if color incorrect, accept as variant or re-dye with correct mordant

**Problem: Fiber feels stiff/harsh after mordanting**
- Cause: Over-concentration of mordant; or insufficient rinsing
- Solution: Rinse thoroughly in cool water after mordanting; reduce mordant concentration in next batch (lower than recommended); ensure water is soft

**Problem: Dye bath mold/smell after storage**
- Cause: Dye extract contains nutrients; microbes grow in stored dye
- Solution: Use dye extract fresh (same day); or boil before use to kill microbes; or preserve with salt (add salt to stored dye to inhibit microbial growth)

## Safety Protocols for Metal Mordants

<div class="safety-box">

**Alum (potassium aluminum sulfate):**
- Low toxicity
- Safe to handle without gloves
- Disposal: Rinse down drain or use on garden soil (not accumulative)
- Avoid: Breathing powder dust (use water or wet handling)

**Iron (ferrous sulfate from rust/vinegar):**
- Moderate toxicity (excess iron can cause liver damage if ingested)
- Wear gloves when handling concentrated solution
- Avoid: Splashing in eyes
- Disposal: Rinse down drain or absorb on soil
- Note: Ferrous iron oxidizes to ferric over time (solution color changes from green to brown); still usable

**Tannin (natural extract):**
- Low toxicity
- Safe to handle without protection
- Disposal: Compost or soil (natural compound breaks down)
- Avoid: Ingestion (high tannin intake can irritate stomach; but not acutely poisonous)

**Copper (copper sulfate, verdigris):**
- **TOXIC—requires extreme caution**
- Copper accumulates in body; chronic low-dose exposure causes neurological damage
- Always wear gloves
- Avoid breathing dust or fumes (work outdoors or in ventilated area)
- Wash hands thoroughly after handling
- Do not prepare or dye if pregnant (fume exposure risk to fetus)
- Disposal: Do not pour down drain (bioaccumulates in water); absorb on soil in isolated area; or seal in container
- Post-collapse: Use only when absolutely necessary (deep purple dyes); minimize exposure

**Tin (stannous chloride):**
- Caustic and toxic
- Not recommended for austere settings
- If used: Extreme ventilation required; protect skin/eyes completely
- Disposal: Hazardous waste; not suitable for home environments

</div>

## Mordant Mastery: Quick Checklist

1. **Choose mordant:** Alum (accessible, low-risk, most versatile) or iron vinegar (deep colors; more toxic)
2. **Prepare fiber:** Scour in hot soapy water; rinse until clean
3. **Pre-mordant:** Soak fiber in mordant bath at 85°C for 45–60 minutes; cool slowly
4. **Add dye:** Prepare dye bath; add pre-mordanted fiber; heat gently to 85°C; hold 30–60 minutes
5. **Cool & drain:** Cool in dye bath slowly; drain gently (avoid wring)
6. **Rinse thoroughly:** Cool water rinses until water runs clear
7. **Dry:** Air dry or use gentle heat
8. **Test:** Wash small swatch 3–5 times; check for fading <5%
9. **Document:** Record mordant type, dye source, fiber type, temperature, time for future reference
