## Cart & Sled Construction for Hauling

The ability to move large quantities of salvage separates sustainable scavenging from subsistence-level picking. Well-built hauling systems multiply the value extracted per trip and reduce injury risk from manual carrying.

### Wheel-Based Carts

A functional cart can be constructed from salvaged components or adapted from existing equipment:

-   **Bicycle Wheel Carts:** Two or four 20" bicycle wheels can support 200-250 lbs on a frame of pipe conduit (3/4" or 1" steel pipe) bolted together. Mount wheels on 3/8" steel axles. Keep wheels aligned perpendicular to direction of travel to reduce drag. Pneumatic bicycle tires provide better shock absorption but require maintenance; solid tires eliminate flats but create more vibration.
-   **Shopping Cart Reinforcement:** Existing shopping carts offer 120-150 lbs capacity without modification. Reinforce with metal bands welded to frame joints, especially at corners where stress concentrates. Replace wheels if worn; add a bar across the front at knee height to prevent tripping. Two carts lashed together side-by-side create a wider platform.
-   **Garden Cart Conversion:** Four-wheeled garden carts (available at most hardware stores pre-collapse) handle 300-400 lbs with proper weight distribution. The angled sides may need plywood panels to contain smaller items. Grease axle points monthly to maintain smooth operation.
-   **Plywood Platform Carts:** A 3/4" plywood base (4'x3' maximum) mounted on four wheels creates a stable, load-bearing platform. Weld or bolt frame members at bottom corners. This design accommodates irregular shapes better than enclosed carts.

### Winter Sled Systems

In snowy climates, sleds often outperform wheeled carts for hauling salvage:

-   **HDPE Sheet Sleds:** High-density polyethylene (HDPE) plastic—common in dunnage pallets and water tanks—creates an excellent sled base. Cut a 4'x3' sheet and curve the front edge upward by applying heat and cooling against a curved form. Attach 2"x4" lumber runners underneath with bolts or screws. This design slides smoothly on packed snow and ice, reducing pulling effort by 50% compared to wheeled carts.
-   **Toboggan-Style Sleds:** Three or four curved wooden slats (1"x8" lumber) lashed together with rope or wire create a flexible sled that rides over broken snow and obstacles. The curved prow lifts rather than digs into snow. Capacity: 300-400 lbs depending on construction. Add a rope bridle at the front for pulling.
-   **Lumber Runner Sleds:** A plywood platform (3/4", 4'x2.5') mounted on two 4"x4" timber runners 8 feet long distributes weight exceptionally well. The large contact area prevents sinking into deep snow. Requires two people to pull efficiently but handles 500+ lbs. Useful for long-distance supply hauls.
-   **Hitch Systems:** For sleds pulled by vehicles: attach a steel ring or clevis to the sled's front bridle. Design vehicle attachment points for quick-release function—in deep snow, you need to disconnect rapidly if the sled becomes stuck. Test hitch systems under load before relying on them operationally.

### Load Capacity and Safety

Realistic capacity estimates prevent failure in the field:

-   Wheel-based cart: 200-400 lbs (depends on wheel quality and axle strength)
-   HDPE sled on packed snow: 300-400 lbs
-   Toboggan sled on compact snow: 250-350 lbs
-   Hand-carry per person: 40-60 lbs (less for children, elderly, or weak individuals)
-   Two-person carry (balanced load): 80-120 lbs

:::warning
Never load carts or sleds to maximum capacity on maiden runs. Test equipment under 60% load first. A cart failure 3 miles from home with 350 lbs of salvage becomes a disaster—assess the consequence before pushing equipment to limits. Equipment failure in the field leaves you stranded and reduces carrying capacity for the return trip.
:::

## Staging Area Setup

A well-organized staging area multiplies scavenging efficiency by creating centralized sorting, storage, and inventory control. The staging area is where raw salvage becomes processed inventory.

### Location Selection

Choose a staging area with these criteria:

-   **Defensibility:** Position between your base and scavenging zones to minimize transport time. Ensure line of sight to approaches; avoid being cornered by terrain features. A location with 360-degree visibility is ideal.
-   **Weather Protection:** A roof or tarp covering prevents spoilage and makes work possible during rain or snow. An abandoned garage, covered loading dock, or sturdy lean-to works. In extreme climates, a full enclosure with ventilation is necessary.
-   **Accessibility:** Must accommodate carts and sleds. Smooth, level ground reduces labor. If situated on sloped terrain, position it lower than the approach routes to use gravity for unloading.
-   **Water Access:** Proximity to water for washing salvage and refilling hydration containers. 100+ yards away is acceptable; more than 400 yards becomes inefficient.
-   **Size:** Minimum 20'x30' open area. Larger is better—you'll expand faster than anticipated. Secondary storage (less frequent access) can be positioned 50-100 yards away if necessary.

:::info-box
Ideal staging area location: An abandoned structure (garage, warehouse, covered barn) that provides weather protection, defensibility, and room for organized storage. Natural camouflage (tree cover, low visibility from roads) is a plus.
:::

### Sorting Zone Organization

Divide the staging area into functional zones:

-   **Unload Zone:** First position for incoming carts and sleds. Keep clear for rapid turnover. Adjacent to this, position sorting tables.
-   **Keep Zone:** Items moving into the supply chain (immediate or near-term use). Organize by category: food, medical, tools, construction materials, textiles, fuel, etc.
-   **Redistribute Zone:** Useful items surplus to current needs. Either trade goods for allied groups or excess items held for future needs (seasonal goods, specialty items). Position these items for visible inspection by trade partners.
-   **Discard Zone:** Contaminated, damaged, or worthless items awaiting disposal. Keep separate to avoid contaminating other zones. Some items (metal, cardboard, wood scraps) may have salvage value later.
-   **Wash/Prep Zone:** For items requiring cleaning or preparation (canning bottles, tool cleaning, etc.). Position near water. Include drain area for waste water.

### Temporary Shelving and Storage

Pallet-based shelving is the fastest solution:

-   Stack two standard pallets to create 48"x40" base with 4-6" height between pallets
-   Lay a third pallet on top, creating a two-tier system
-   Stack no more than 3-4 pallet levels high (unstable above that; risk of collapse)
-   Stagger pallet positions on successive levels to distribute weight
-   Use straps or rope to bind stacks together, preventing shifting
-   Store heavy items (metal, tools) on lower shelves; lighter items above
-   Cover top tier with tarp to prevent rain intrusion from above

:::tip
Ensure pallets are level before stacking. Uneven ground can cause shifting. Place shims (flat stones, wood scraps) under pallets to level them before stacking multiple tiers.
:::

### Inventory Marking System

Implement a marking system that works without electricity:

-   **Paint Codes:** Use spray paint (white, yellow, red) on item containers or pallet fronts. White = keep for community use; Yellow = trade goods; Red = hazardous or discard pending.
-   **Chalkboard Labels:** Attach to pallets listing contents, date received, and status. Use permanent chalk or marker for readability in outdoor conditions.
-   **Card System:** Write item descriptions and quantities on 3"x5" cards, attach to pallet with clothespin. Simple, durable, and easily updated.
-   **Grid Mapping:** Assign each pallet a grid position (A1, A2, B1, etc.) and maintain a master map so anyone can locate items quickly.

### Security and Guard Considerations

Staging areas attract attention and represent concentrated value. Security must balance protection with operational accessibility:

-   **Visibility Strategy:** Hide high-value, small items (medicine, ammunition, tools) in inconspicuous containers. Place obvious low-value items (scrap metal, wood) in visible locations to create the impression that the staging area contains only bulk, low-value salvage.
-   **Guard Rotation:** If the staging area is remote, position a lookout with clear sightlines. A single person on a 4-hour shift can prevent most opportunistic theft. Rotate guards to prevent fatigue.
-   **Obscured Entrance:** Position the staging area so it's not visible from main roads or approaches. A screen of salvaged lumber, tarps, or natural vegetation reduces casual discovery.
-   **Accountability:** Require sign-out for items removed. A simple log prevents internal theft and helps track consumption rates.

:::danger
Staging areas are theft magnets. Concentrated value in a single location is irresistible to opportunistic thieves and organized scavenging teams. Adequate security is not optional—it's survival. Communities have lost entire seasons of salvage work to theft in a single night.
:::

## Mapping & Cataloging Salvage Sites

Systematic mapping transforms scavenging from random exploration into strategic resource extraction. Maps become institutional knowledge that your community can reference years after an initial survey.

### Grid Mapping System

Create or obtain a large map of your operational area (city/town map or drawn-to-scale version). Overlay a grid system:

-   Use a 1000' x 1000' grid (or 400m x 400m) as the primary unit
-   Label rows with numbers (1-10), columns with letters (A-J) for rapid reference
-   Within each grid square, mark individual buildings with numbers
-   Mark grid squares on the map with different colors or symbols to indicate search status
-   Keep the master map in a waterproof container at your staging area
-   Create a paper copy for field teams to reference during operations

### Building-by-Building Status Tracking

For each structure, maintain a record with the following status codes:

-   **Unsearched (U):** Building not yet explored. Priority for new teams.
-   **Partially Searched (P):** First-level search completed, but deep storage (attics, basements, locked rooms) not accessed. Note what areas were checked and what remains.
-   **Fully Searched (F):** Complete exploration including locked rooms, attics, and subsurface areas. Likely to have minimal useful items remaining, though seasonal goods or forgotten caches may be found on re-visits.
-   **Hazardous (H):** Structural damage, chemical/biological contamination, or unstable conditions present. Avoid unless specifically tasked and protected. Update status if conditions change (fire damage spreads, structure stabilizes, hazmat cleaned).
-   **Collapsed/Inaccessible (C):** No viable entry or structure integrity too compromised for safe entry. Mark with date when status confirmed.

### Information Recording

For each building, record on a 3"x5" card or in a ledger:

-   **Address:** Street address or grid location reference
-   **Building Type:** Residential/Commercial/Industrial/Specialized (hospital, warehouse, etc.)
-   **Date Searched:** When the most recent search occurred
-   **Major Items Found:** What was extracted (food, tools, furniture, fixtures, etc.)
-   **Hazards:** Structural damage, contamination, environmental hazards noted
-   **Locks/Barriers:** Type of entry difficulty (locked, boarded, debris blocked)
-   **Secondary Items:** Lower-priority items still available (books, clothing, electronics, etc.)
-   **Notes:** Any unusual features, owner occupation at time of search, other groups active in area

### Sharing Intelligence with Allied Groups

If you have established trade relationships or mutual defense agreements with other communities:

-   **Exchange Maps:** Share grid maps showing which areas your group has searched, hazards you've identified, and locations of particularly valuable buildings. This prevents overlap and reduces conflict.
-   **Status Updates:** Communicate when buildings are searched or hazardous conditions are discovered. This allows allied groups to adjust their own operations.
-   **Hazard Warnings:** Immediately alert all known groups if you discover biological hazards, chemical contamination, or unstable structures that could endanger their teams.
-   **Resource Agreements:** Establish "dibs" agreements where one group leads search of a particular zone, but allied groups can request specific item searches within that zone for fair trade value.

:::info-box
Information sharing with allied groups prevents tragic duplicate efforts and deaths. If another group enters a building your team assessed as unsafe, and people die as a result of hazards you knew about but didn't communicate, that's a community relationship breach with serious consequences.
:::

### Paper-Based Systems

Digital systems fail; paper systems persist:

-   Laminate critical maps using clear tape or plastic covers to protect from weather
-   Maintain multiple copies of master maps at different locations (staging area, primary base, secondary cache)
-   Use pencil for status markings so maps can be updated as situations change
-   Keep a bound ledger (journal-style) with complete records. Bind sheets with brads or cord; don't use loose pages that can be lost.
-   Archive ledgers when full and store in waterproof containers; maintain a narrative index listing contents of each ledger
-   Train 2-3 people in the mapping system so knowledge doesn't concentrate in one individual

## Team Roles & Communication

Coordinated teams operate safely and efficiently. Role clarity prevents confusion that leads to accidents or missed objectives.

### Minimum Team Size

Never send solo scavengers into potentially hazardous buildings. Minimum team: 3 people

-   2 team members conduct interior search
-   1 team member maintains security/lookout position outside
-   This provides safety redundancy and external communication/rescue capability

### Core Team Roles

**Team Lead (Lead):** Overall decision-maker for the operation. Responsible for safety decisions, route planning, and priority adjustments. Lead should be experienced and decisive. Typically positions themselves to see both interior and exterior operations.

**Interior Searcher 1 (Scout):** First person entering buildings. Assesses structural integrity, identifies hazards, and marks cleared areas. Moves deliberately and methodically. Responsible for spotting threats (human, animal, structural) before they become emergencies.

**Interior Searcher 2 (Porter):** Follows behind Scout, gathering items and beginning basic organization. Carries heavier items, manages the hauling equipment inside the building, and prepares loads for exit. Strength and stamina are important for this role.

**Lookout/Security (Guard):** Stationed outside with clear sightlines of all approaches. Watches for approaching people, vehicles, or animals. Communicates with interior team regarding external threats or time management. Does not abandon post unless emergency. In high-threat areas, may be armed; maintains distance so interior team can exit and rally if necessary.

### Hand Signals for Silent Communication

When radio silence is necessary or to avoid alerting potential threats:

-   **All Clear (Thumbs Up):** Building interior safe to enter or progress as normal
-   **Danger/Stop (Raised Fist):** Cease movement, freeze position, take cover
-   **Retreat (Beckoning Arm):** Return to entry point immediately and exit building
-   **Approach (Forward Wave):** Move toward specified position or direction
-   **Guard Alert (Point Outward):** Threatening presence outside staging area, increase vigilance
-   **Medical Issue (Wave Arms):** Injury or health emergency requiring immediate assistance

Practice these signals until all team members respond automatically without hesitation.

### Whistle Codes

For situations where hand signals aren't visible or sound is preferable:

-   **One Long Blast:** Attention/Listen—important announcement incoming
-   **Two Short Blasts:** All Clear—conditions normal
-   **Three Short Blasts Rapid:** Danger/Retreat immediately
-   **Series of Four Blasts:** Injury/Emergency—initiate rescue procedures

Agree beforehand on which signals apply to your operations. Not all situations warrant whistles (noise discipline in hostile areas). Test the specific whistle you'll carry—plastic referee whistles often sound identical and can cause confusion.

### Radio Protocol

If radios are available:

-   Establish a primary channel for your team and a secondary "emergency only" channel
-   Use brief radio calls: "Scout to Lead: interior clear, proceeding to second floor"
-   Establish check-in intervals (every 15 minutes is typical) so absence of communication triggers alarm
-   Use predetermined callsigns instead of real names on radio (reduces intelligence leakage if others monitor)
-   Establish a "code word" that indicates being under duress. Example: "The building needs a survey" means emergency rescue needed.
-   Set a dead-time limit—if a check-in doesn't happen when expected, Guard initiates abort/rescue sequence

### Check-in Schedule

Establish timing for the operation:

-   **Pre-Operation Brief:** 15 minutes before entering building. Confirm roles, hand signals, time limit, abort triggers, and rally points.
-   **Initial Entry:** Scout enters first, waits 2 minutes for structural confirmation. Guard and Porter wait at threshold. Once Scout signals all clear, Porter enters.
-   **Check-in Intervals:** Every 15-30 minutes depending on building complexity. Guard confirms team members still functioning. If no response after 1 minute, Guard initiates emergency sequence.
-   **Time Limit:** Predetermined maximum (45-60 minutes typical). Even if productive, exit when time expires. Fatigue and complacency increase accident risk near the end of operations.
-   **Final Exit:** Scout exits first (last person to leave is last to confirm area clear). Porter exits with load. Guard confirms all personnel out before moving position.

## Weight Estimation for Trip Planning

Accurate weight estimation prevents equipment failure, injury, and exhaustion. Many new scavengers dangerously overestimate their capacity for laden travel.

### Material Weight Reference

Use these benchmarks for common items:

-   **Water:** 8.3 lbs per gallon (62.4 lbs per 7.5 gallons or 5-gallon bucket)
-   **Steel:** 490 lbs per cubic foot; 20.2 lbs per linear foot of 1" pipe
-   **Copper:** 555 lbs per cubic foot; scrap copper 8-12 lbs per pound of loose wire
-   **Aluminum:** 170 lbs per cubic foot; much lighter than steel for equivalent volume
-   **Lumber (varies by species and moisture):** Dry softwood 35 lbs/cubic foot; green/wet lumber 50-60 lbs/cubic foot
-   **Plywood (3/4" sheet):** 55-60 lbs; water-damaged plywood gains 20-40% additional weight
-   **Concrete:** 145 lbs per cubic foot; blocks (8"x8"x16") weigh 35-50 lbs each
-   **Glass:** 160 lbs per cubic foot; a large window or pane can weigh 15-50 lbs
-   **Canned Food:** 12-oz can averages 1 lb; one case (24 cans) weighs 24-25 lbs

### Volume-to-Weight Estimation

Before entering a site, estimate what you can realistically move:

-   Measure the dimensions of items you intend to extract (length x width x height in feet)
-   Divide by 10 to estimate cubic feet (rough formula)
-   Multiply cubic feet by estimated density (use reference values above)
-   Account for water weight if items are wet or damp
-   Example: A wooden cabinet 3'x2'x2' = 12 cubic feet; dry wood at 35 lbs/cf = 420 lbs. This requires your wheeled cart and good axles—not a hand-carry item.

### Degradation and Hazard Multiplier

Certain materials become heavier or more hazardous when saturated with water:

-   Water damage multiplies weight by 1.2-1.5x, making items 20-50% heavier than expected
-   Wet drywall is nearly impassable; water damage in basements creates heavy, contaminated items of minimal value
-   Rotted wood loses structural integrity rapidly; don't attempt to carry loads through it
-   Corrosion and rust on metal increases difficulty of extraction; calculate extraction cost vs. value before committing to removal

### The Overloading Injury Rule

An injury sustained during an overloaded trip has cascading consequences:

-   The injured person becomes a liability to transport and care for
-   Other team members are diverted from productive work for rescue/support
-   Medical resources are consumed treating the injury
-   The injured person loses productivity for weeks to months during recovery
-   If the injury is permanent (crushed foot, spinal damage, nerve damage), capacity is permanently reduced

:::warning
Conservative load planning that extracts 80% of theoretical capacity per trip is far superior to aggressive planning that risks 30% of trips ending in injury. Plan for sustainable, repeatable operations, not maximum single-trip extraction. An injury to a productive team member reduces your community's salvage capacity far more than a missed opportunity for extra salvage.
:::

## Load Prioritization

Not all salvage is equal. Effective prioritization ensures your team extracts maximum value within physical and temporal constraints.

### Value Density Analysis

Value density = (Market Value in Trade Goods) / (Weight in Pounds). Prioritize high-value-density items.

A medicine bottle weighing 0.5 lbs might trade for 50 trade units (100 units/lb), making it 200x more valuable than scrap steel (0.5 units/lb).

### Degradation Urgency

Some items deteriorate if not recovered promptly:

-   **Perishable Food:** Most immediate urgency (hours to days). If not retrieved quickly, will spoil and create waste.
-   **Medicine/Pharmaceuticals:** High urgency; effectiveness degrades over time, especially if exposed to temperature extremes or light.
-   **Seeds:** Urgency depends on storage conditions; if discovered in dry storage, medium priority; if exposed to moisture, high priority.
-   **Electronics:** Will corrode if wet; low urgency if dry, high urgency if moisture is present or imminent (weather forecast showing rain).
-   **Structural Materials:** No urgency; these are stable indefinitely. Plan retrieval around other trips for space efficiency.

### Community Needs Assessment

Align extraction with actual community requirements:

-   Maintain an active "wish list" of items community leadership has identified as needed
-   Prioritize these items when found, even if other items have higher value density
-   Example: Your community needs glass jars for food preservation; even though jars have low value density, their high utility makes them priority finds
-   Cross-reference wish list with items on-site during interior search; this guides team decisions about what to extract first

### Caching vs. Full Extraction

Sometimes it's better to leave items temporarily cached and return later:

-   **Cache Strategy:** If a building contains more salvage than your cart capacity, move high-value items off-site on this trip. Leave remaining items in a secure, marked cache location within the building. Return with fresher team and empty cart 1-3 days later to extract cached items.
-   **Cache Location:** Upper floors are safer (less likely to be discovered by vagrants); secure caches in locked rooms or concealed behind false walls if possible.
-   **Cache Marking:** Mark with a simple symbol (chalk mark, broken stick) visible to your team but not obvious to outsiders. Record cache location in your building status card.
-   **Risk Assessment:** Caching works in stable, relatively secure areas. In high-threat zones or areas with active competing groups, extract everything immediately—leaving cached items behind risks losing them to theft.

:::tip
Caching is particularly effective for bulk, low-density items (lumber, metal scrap, concrete blocks) that take up cart space but have low immediate trade value. Cache them and return with a vehicle or additional team when you have capacity.
:::

## Route Planning & Security

The route between base, staging area, and salvage sites determines success and safety. Systematic route planning reduces travel time and encounter risk.

### Primary and Alternate Routes

Never have only one way in or out:

-   **Primary Route:** Shortest, most direct path. Use when no threats are detected. Maximizes efficiency.
-   **Alternate Route 1:** 20-40% longer but avoids a specific choke point (bridge, narrow street, guard checkpoint). Use if primary route is compromised.
-   **Alternate Route 2:** Longer still (30-50% more distance) but completely independent; uses different terrain/street grid. Fallback if primary and Alternate 1 are compromised.
-   **Emergency Rally Point:** Defensible location 1/4 to 1/2 mile from primary operating area. If team becomes separated, all members head to rally point and wait for others. Mark rally point on all team members' mental map.

### Choke Points to Avoid

Identify locations that restrict movement and create vulnerability:

-   Bridges (single crossing, limited alternative routes)
-   Narrow valleys or canyon passages
-   Dense urban blocks with few exit streets
-   Areas with known hostile populations or gang territories
-   Road sections with poor visibility for observation of approaches

When possible, route operations to avoid choke points. If a choke point must be transited, time it for maximum visibility (daylight hours) and send a scout ahead to confirm it's clear.

### Time-of-Day Considerations

Timing affects risk and visibility:

-   **Early Morning (6 AM - 9 AM):** Low activity from vagrants and hostile groups (night watchers are exhausted, day activity hasn't started). Good visibility. Cooler temperatures. Recommended for initial site surveys.
-   **Mid-Morning to Early Afternoon (9 AM - 3 PM):** Highest visibility. Many other people/groups active (increased encounter risk but also more "cover" from casual observation). Weather usually stable.
-   **Late Afternoon (3 PM - 5 PM):** Declining light; shadows complicate vision. Activity increasing again. Weather may degrade toward evening. Avoid unless necessary.
-   **Dusk/Dawn Transitions:** Worst time for movement. Visibility poor, shadows mask threats, and timing changes can strand you in darkness. Avoid.
-   **Night Movement:** Only with specific objectives and careful planning. Requires lights (flashlights, lanterns) or night vision. Risk of accidents increases dramatically. Avoid as standard practice.

### Noise Discipline

Sound carries and attracts attention:

-   Move slowly; running makes noise and signals panic
-   Don't drag carts or sleds; carry them over obstacles if possible
-   No talking during approach; hand signals and whistle codes only until objective is reached
-   Minimize metal-on-metal contact when loading (cushion items to prevent clanging)
-   No radios on high volume; use low volume or earpieces for discretion
-   If operating in the dark, minimize flashlight use; allow eyes to adjust to moonlight when possible

:::warning
Excessive noise attracts competing scavenging groups, hostile individuals, and draws attention to your staging area location. Quiet operations protect not just the current team but the staging area security long-term.
:::

### Signs of Other Groups

Detect presence before direct contact:

-   **Fresh Trash/Waste:** Food scraps, discarded packaging, human waste indicate recent habitation. Smell (human odor, smoke) is most obvious marker.
-   **Cleared Paths:** Trails through brush or accumulated debris show frequent passage. Fresh disturbance (broken twigs, displaced items) indicates very recent use.
-   **Temporary Shelters:** Tarps, lean-tos, or cleared areas with sleeping gear indicate established camps.
-   **Marks/Signs:** Graffiti, marks on trees, or other territorial indicators show group presence. Different symbols/styles indicate different groups.
-   **Observation Points:** High locations with clear sightlines that are cleaned or maintained for observation indicate active surveillance.

### Conflict Avoidance Strategies

If you detect other groups or people:

-   **First Sighting:** Stop immediately. Do not approach. Observe from distance. Assess if the group is aware of you.
-   **If Undetected:** Retreat quietly using alternate route. Abort the current operation. Report to leadership; reassess area threat level.
-   **If Detected/Confronted:** Stop, keep hands visible, remain calm. The Team Lead speaks; other members remain silent. Establish peaceful intent ("We're collecting supplies, not looking for trouble"). Back away slowly if instructed. Do not attempt escape run unless directly attacked.
-   **If Attacked:** Fight or flee depending on tactical situation. Guard role member provides covering fire or distraction. Interior team exits immediately with whatever load is available. Regroup at rally point. Do not counterattack unless clearly able to win.

## Record Keeping & Inventory

Inventory systems transform raw salvage data into community knowledge. Good records enable efficient planning and support trade relationships with allied groups.

### Community Inventory Tracking

Maintain a comprehensive inventory of all salvage:

-   **Item Category:** Food, Medical, Tools, Construction Materials, Textiles, Fuel, Seeds, etc.
-   **Item Description:** "Canned tomatoes (15 oz), 47 cans" or "Ball canning jars (quart size), 35 count"
-   **Quantity:** Count or weight, depending on item
-   **Date Received:** When item was added to inventory
-   **Location:** Where in staging area or primary storage
-   **Source:** Building location where item was found (useful for pattern analysis)
-   **Quality/Condition:** Excellent/Good/Fair/Poor/Damaged
-   **Notes:** Expiration dates, required conditions (refrigeration, ventilation), hazard warnings

### Site-Specific Tracking

Record what was found at each location and when:

-   **Building Address/Grid ID:** Unique identifier
-   **Date Searched:** YYYY-MM-DD format
-   **Items Extracted:** Specific items and quantities
-   **Items Remaining:** Available but not extracted (and why—too heavy, lower priority, etc.)
-   **Estimated Remaining Value:** Rough assessment of uncollected items
-   **Hazards Documented:** Any dangers that should be noted for future teams
-   **Recommended Future Action:** Re-visit in X months, avoid entirely, investigate further, etc.

### Consumption Rate Analysis

Track what your community uses to predict future needs:

-   For staple items (grain, canned goods, salt, sugar), calculate consumption per day/week/month
-   Multiply consumption rate x expected scavenging interval (days between major salvage runs) to determine target acquisition quantity
-   Example: If community consumes 15 lbs of grain per week and you do major scavenging runs every 14 days, prioritize finding 30-40 lbs per run (accounting for 25-33% buffer for shortfall)
-   Track seasonal variations (heating fuel in winter, preservation supplies in harvest season)
-   Review consumption trends monthly; adjust acquisition targets based on actual vs. estimated usage

### Sharing Data with Trade Partners

If you establish trade relationships with allied communities:

-   Create summary ledgers showing what items you have available for trade (without revealing total quantity for security purposes)
-   Share consumption data to coordinate supply chains (you have excess grain, they have excess preserved vegetables—establish regular trade route)
-   Exchange building status maps so both communities can avoid redundant searches
-   Maintain fairness in trade pricing; establish agreed-upon exchange rates to prevent disputes

### Simple Ledger Systems

Paper-based inventory that survives failure of technology:

-   **Master Inventory Ledger:** Large bound journal, one entry per item or per day of receipts. Columns: Date | Item | Quantity | Quality | Location | Source | Notes
-   **Site Record Cards:** 3"x5" cards filed alphabetically by address. Record every visit, every item found, status changes, and estimates of remaining value.
-   **Consumption Log:** Track items used from inventory. Record date, item, quantity used, purpose. Identify consumption patterns.
-   **Archive System:** When a ledger is full, store it in waterproof container with index listing contents. Maintain active ledger for current records only.
-   **Trained Staff:** Assign 2-3 people to maintain records and train replacements. If institutional knowledge concentrates in one person, that person's illness or death causes data loss.

### Buddy System and Extraction Plans

Never work alone inside hazardous structures. The buddy system is non-negotiable.

**Buddy pair requirements:**

- Stay within visual or verbal contact at all times
- Check on each other every 5-10 minutes (verbal "okay" or hand signal)
- One buddy never proceeds beyond the other without communication
- If one buddy is injured, the other immediately stops work and provides assistance
- Never leave an injured buddy alone; extract together or call for rescue team

**Extraction procedures:** Plan before entering:

- Identify primary exit (door you entered through) and secondary exit (window, alternate door) if available
- Mark exit routes with reflective tape or chalk arrows so you can navigate back in darkness or smoke
- Keep extraction route clear of obstacles (do not stack items in doorways or hallways)
- In multi-story buildings, identify stairwells as you ascend; descend same way you came up
- If trapped, move to highest floor and signal from windows (louder and more visible than ground level)

**Communication with exterior team:** Establish check-in intervals:

- Every 15 minutes: exterior Guard signals interior team to report status (two whistle blasts = check in)
- Interior team responds (two whistle blasts back or verbal "okay")
- If no response after 30 seconds, Guard escalates to emergency protocol (three rapid blasts = all exit immediately)
- Interior team, upon hearing emergency signal, immediately exits and rallies at rally point (designated meeting location outside building)

### Advanced Radio Procedures

Where radios are available, use them strategically to enhance safety:

**Frequency discipline:** Establish a primary frequency. Change frequency weekly (prevents monitoring by hostile groups). Memorize backup frequency and emergency frequency (if operating with multiple teams).

**Call signs:** Use code names instead of real names (e.g., Team 1 = "Reaper," Team Lead = "Shepherd"). Change call signs monthly to prevent pattern recognition by hostile actors.

**Communication protocol:**

- **Check-in:** "Shepherd to Reaper One, checking in, position basement southwest, no issues, proceeding to next sector."
- **Status update:** "Reaper One, objects located, estimate 200 lbs useful salvage, need extraction assistance. Requesting Reaper Two assist with lift-out. Guard, status external?"
- **Threat alert:** "Guard to all, unknown vehicle approaching from north at distance 300 meters, currently stationary. Recommend radio silence and visual-only observation."
- **Situation resolved:** "Guard to all, vehicle departed heading east, all clear, resume communications normal."
- **Exit signal:** "Shepherd to all, 10 minutes to extract and load. Prepare final sweep and load-out sequence."

**Duress protocol:** Establish a "duress code word" that signals emergency without alerting anyone listening to radio:

- Normal language: "We're finding good material here, lots of intact items on second floor. Shepherd, shall we continue?"
- Duress message (sounds natural): "We're finding deteriorated material here, not much salvageable. Shepherd, should we call it?"
- On hearing duress code, Guard immediately executes extraction protocol (signals interior team emergency exit, prepares for rescue, alerts base for support)

**Radio silence:** In high-threat situations (hostile group in area, military/police patrols nearby), avoid transmissions entirely. Use hand signals and whistle codes only. Radio transmissions can reveal position and attract hostile attention.

### Load Sharing and Distribution

Distribute weight fairly among team members to prevent overexertion injuries and fatigue-related accidents:

**Weight guidelines per person:**

- 25-30% of maximum carrying capacity = sustainable over 1-2 hours without excessive fatigue
- Exceeding 40% capacity causes rapid fatigue, reduces situational awareness, increases accident risk
- Elderly, pregnant women, children, weak individuals: reduce to 15-20% of their maximum capacity
- Injured individuals: 5-15% maximum (light duties only until healed)

**Load distribution method:**

- Heaviest single item on strongest member (but not overloading that person to the point of instability)
- Distribute total weight across team (if 250 lbs total salvage and team of 3: target 80-85 lbs per person)
- Reassess load every 30 minutes; rotate who carries heaviest item if fatigue is evident in any team member
- Stop and rest every hour (prevents cumulative fatigue injuries that develop over multi-hour operations)

**Item placement on body:**

- Backpack: center weight between shoulders, not pulling backward (misalignment causes back injury over time)
- Hand-carry: distribute between hands; never carry all weight in one arm (causes muscle fatigue and joint strain)
- Two-person carry: balance the load (both people carry similar weight; do not have one person supporting 60%+ of load)
- Unbalanced loads: if item must be carried by one person due to size/shape, reduce other load for that person

**Fatigue warning signs:** Stop operation if team member shows:

- Excessive breathing difficulty (unable to speak in full sentences)
- Stumbling or loss of balance
- Complaints of dizziness, vision changes, or chest pain
- Extreme slowness (person who was keeping pace now falling behind)
- Irritability or personality changes (sign of severe fatigue and decision-making impairment)

### Decontamination Procedures

Upon returning from scavenging operations in contaminated areas (abandoned hospitals, industrial sites, agricultural chemical-treated areas, pest control-treated buildings):

**Personal decontamination:**

- Change clothes immediately upon return; place worn clothing in sealed container for washing separately
- Wash hands, arms, and all exposed skin with soap and water (removes chemical or biological residue)
- Wash face and neck carefully (avoid contact with eyes/mouth during washing)
- If possible, shower with clean water before entering living areas or handling food
- Do not touch face, eat, drink, or smoke until decontaminated (prevents ingestion of residual contamination)

**Equipment decontamination:**

- Wipe tools, containers, and hauled items with damp cloth to remove surface contamination
- Allow to air dry in designated decontamination area away from living spaces and food storage
- If chemical exposure suspected, rinse multiple times with water
- Do not store contaminated equipment in main supply areas until decontamination complete and items verified safe

**Medical observation:** Anyone showing symptoms after contaminated-site work (rash, respiratory irritation, nausea, chemical smell on clothing) should be isolated and monitored. Seek medical attention if symptoms persist >4 hours or worsen.

### Route Planning and Navigation

Scavenging teams covering large areas need systematic route planning to maximize efficiency and prevent getting lost:

**Route types:**

- **Grid sweep:** Divide operational area into rectangular sections (1000'x1000' or 400m x 400m). Team systematically searches each grid from west to east or north to south. Ensures complete coverage, reduces duplicate effort, prevents wasted trips.
- **Landmark-based:** Use natural or man-made features (rivers, roads, large buildings) as route markers. Navigate from landmark to landmark. Useful in terrain with clear geography or familiar features.
- **Radial search:** Start from base and move outward in expanding circles or spiral pattern. Useful for areas around established settlement; reduces transport time as you begin with nearest locations.
- **Targeted search:** Identify specific building types (hospitals, pharmacies, warehouses) from pre-operation reconnaissance. Navigate directly to priority targets, then survey secondary targets as time allows.

**Navigation method:**

- Carry paper map (electronic GPS fails without power/satellites, and digital systems are vulnerable)
- Mark current position and route on map with pencil (allows updates without permanent marks)
- Note landmarks on map as you pass them (verifies position and helps with orientation)
- If lost, return to last known landmark rather than continuing blindly into unfamiliar territory
- Establish time checkpoints: "We should reach grid D-4 by noon. If not by 12:30pm, we reassess route and return."

**Return route planning:** Plan return journey differently from approach:

- Prevents ambush on predictable route (if hostile group is shadowing you)
- Creates opportunity to scout different areas on return (increases salvage yield without additional trips)
- If approach route becomes compromised, team has alternate exit option already planned
- Secondary route also serves as contingency if primary route is blocked or unsafe

### Hazard Communication and Pre-Operation Safety Briefing

Before each scavenging operation, conduct a 5-10 minute safety briefing. This ensures all team members understand objectives, hazards, and procedures:

**Briefing includes:**

1. **Objective:** Clear statement of what the team is looking for (food, medical supplies, metal, electronics, textiles, etc.)
2. **Target location:** Map location, grid reference, street address, distinctive features ("the white brick building with boarded windows," "three-story structure with collapsed north wall")
3. **Estimated duration:** Expected time in field and estimated return time (helps team avoid complacency as time limit approaches)
4. **Known hazards:** Structural damage, contamination (chemical/biological), wild animals, hostile groups reported in area, water hazards, etc.
5. **Roles and responsibilities:** Who is Lead (decision-maker), who is Scout (first entry), who is Porter (hauling), who is Guard (security). Clarify expectations for each role.
6. **Rally point:** Where team gathers if separated (e.g., "100 meters north of target building, east side of large oak tree," "the fire station, north entrance"). Make it distinctive and defensible.
7. **Extraction plan:** How to exit if threatened; which exits are safe, which should be avoided due to hazards or exposure
8. **Communication protocol:** Check-in frequency (every 15-30 minutes), radio call signs, hand signals to use, duress code word, what to do if communication fails

## Troubleshooting & Common Logistics Mistakes

### Problem: Cart Breaks Down in the Field

**Issue:** Wheeled cart develops problems—broken axle, damaged wheel, or structural failure—3+ miles from base.

**Solution:** Have a backup plan. Carry repair materials (bolts, spare wheel, tools) on longer trips. If repair is impossible, redistribute load among team members and abandon cart if necessary. Never stay with broken equipment in hostile territory.

### Problem: Staging Area is Discovered by Outsiders

**Issue:** Unknown group finds your staging area and begins stealing salvage.

**Solution:** This is a security breach. Immediately increase guard rotation and consider relocating high-value items. Document what was stolen. Alert other community members to investigate the unknown group's identity and intentions. Plan for escalating security measures (defensive positioning, armed guards, relocation to new site).

### Problem: Consumption Exceeds Projections

**Issue:** Community uses supplies faster than planned. Salvage runs cannot keep up with demand.

**Solution:** Either increase scavenging frequency (more runs per week) or reduce community consumption. Have leadership discussion about consumption vs. salvage capacity. Prioritize highest-value-density items to maximize acquisition.

### Problem: Too Much Salvage, Limited Cart Capacity

**Issue:** Building contains more valuable salvage than you can transport. Team wants to take more than cart capacity allows.

**Solution:** Prioritize by value density (highest-value items first). Use caching strategy for lower-density items. Return with additional cart/vehicle on next trip. Never overload cart beyond safe capacity—equipment failure is worse than leaving salvage behind.

### Problem: Mapping System Becomes Disorganized

**Issue:** Multiple people update maps differently, ledgers become inconsistent, status records conflict.

**Solution:** Designate one person as "map keeper" responsible for all status updates. Others report findings to map keeper; keeper records it. Regular team meetings review map status and resolve inconsistencies. Archive old maps; never discard them.
