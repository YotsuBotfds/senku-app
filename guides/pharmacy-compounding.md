<section id="overview">

## Overview

Pharmacy compounding is the art and science of preparing customized medication formulations from raw pharmaceutical ingredients. In survival and post-collapse scenarios, compounding becomes critical when commercial supply chains fail and pharmaceutical manufacturing ceases. This guide covers techniques to stretch limited medication supplies, adapt formulations for different patient populations (pediatric, geriatric, patients unable to swallow), and prepare medications from raw powders and plant materials.

**Key competencies in this guide:**
- Crushing tablets and preparing suspensions safely
- Reconstituting powdered medications
- Calculating accurate doses across populations
- Compounding solutions, ointments, and tinctures
- Quality control and contamination prevention
- Storage and shelf-life extension

In normal times, pharmacists perform nearly all compounding under strict protocols. Post-collapse, these skills ensure medication access when pills are unavailable and communities depend on improvised formulations.

:::info-box
### When Compounding Becomes Essential

- Commercial medications unavailable or expired
- Pediatric doses required (tablets too large, liquid forms needed)
- Patient cannot swallow (dysphagia, unconscious)
- Specific strength unavailable (need 25mg but only have 500mg tablets)
- Raw pharmaceutical powders stockpiled require reconstitution
- Extract and dose plant-based medicines
:::


<section id="recognition">

## Recognition & Assessment

### When to Compound vs. Use Pre-Made Medications

**Use pre-made tablets/capsules if:**
- Patient can swallow normally
- Exact dose available in tablet form
- Medication not expired and properly stored
- Aseptic technique/equipment unavailable
- Time pressure exists (prepared formulations faster)

**Compound new formulation if:**
- Tablets expired but powder active ingredient available
- Pediatric patient requires liquid (tablets cannot swallow)
- Available dose too high (need to dilute 500mg tablet to 50mg dose)
- Specific route needed (IV/IM unavailable in pre-made form)
- Intolerance to binders/fillers in tablets (can use pure drug powder)

:::tip
### Pre-Compounding Checklist

Before beginning any compounding, verify:
1. **Drug identity:** Confirm correct medication (check label twice)
2. **Expiration status:** Powder/tablets within expiration window?
3. **Patient factors:** Weight, age, kidney/liver function, allergies, current medications
4. **Stability:** Know how long formulation will remain potent (hours/days)
5. **Storage:** Appropriate container, temperature, light protection available?
6. **Aseptic requirements:** Is sterile technique necessary (injectable vs oral)?
:::

### Assessment Indicators

**Safe to compound with basic technique:**
- Oral tablets being crushed for immediate use
- Pediatric dose requiring dilution of existing solution
- Topical ointments or creams
- Non-sterile tinctures from plant material

**Requires careful aseptic technique:**
- Reconstituting injectable powder (antibiotics, insulin)
- Preparing IV solutions
- Compounding for immunocompromised patients
- Compounding for open wounds/burns

## Equipment & Materials (Expanded)

### Compounding Workspace Setup

Establish dedicated compounding area: clean table, protected from dust/animals, good lighting. For injectable preparation: separate sterile workspace if possible. Steps: (1) Wipe down table with clean cloth and isopropyl alcohol (70%) if available, or soap and water. (2) Organize materials in work zone. (3) Work quickly to minimize exposure to contamination. (4) Clean workspace immediately after completing batch.

:::warning
### Cleanliness Requirements by Preparation Type

**Oral/topical compounding:** Standard kitchen cleanliness acceptable. Wash hands, wear clean clothes. Disinfection preferred but not mandatory.

**Injectable/parenteral compounding:** Strict aseptic technique required. Disinfect workspace with 70% alcohol. Wear gloves, sterile if available. Work in clean environment away from open windows/fans. Maintain this heightened standard to prevent potentially fatal infections.

**Immunocompromised patient:** Treat all formulations as requiring aseptic technique, even oral preparations.
:::

## Step-by-Step Procedures

### Procedure 1: Crushing Tablets for Pediatric Administration

**Time:** 5-10 minutes
**Equipment:** Mortar & pestle (or cup + spoon), small cup for mixing, gloves
**Medication stability post-crush:** 15-30 minutes (use immediately)

**Steps:**
1. Verify tablet identity and expiration date
2. Perform hand hygiene; don gloves
3. Place ONE tablet in mortar
4. Apply steady pressure with pestle, grinding in circular motions for 20-30 seconds until fine powder
5. Add 2-5mL liquid (water, juice, breast milk): use smallest amount patient will accept to minimize volume
6. Stir for 10 seconds to create uniform suspension/solution
7. Draw into small syringe or administer via spoon directly into mouth
8. Give immediately; do not pre-prepare

**Caution:** Check tablet packaging—do NOT crush: sustained-release tablets, enteric-coated tablets, or capsules with extended-release beads.

### Procedure 2: Reconstituting Injectable Powder (Antibiotic Example)

**Time:** 2-3 minutes
**Equipment:** Vial of powder, sterile water or specified diluent, 3mL syringe, alcohol pad
**Stability:** 7-14 days refrigerated (2-8°C), 1-4 hours room temperature (check manufacturer instructions)

**Steps:**
1. Check powder vial label: confirm drug name, volume of diluent required, expiration date
2. Prepare workspace; wipe bottle top with alcohol pad, let dry 30 seconds
3. Draw up specified volume of sterile water into syringe (e.g., 4.8mL for 500mg vial)
4. Insert needle into vial's center (rubber stopper)
5. Push diluent in SLOWLY while GENTLY swirling vial—do NOT shake (creates bubbles/froth)
6. Remove syringe and needle
7. Let vial stand 1-2 minutes for complete dissolution
8. Verify: keep solution clear and colorless (unless otherwise noted—check instructions)
9. Label vial with date/time reconstituted, expiration date/time, reconstituting technician initials
10. Store appropriately (refrigerated or room temperature per instructions)

:::danger
### Do NOT Use If:

- Powder vial already pre-filled (don't mix); it is ready to use
- Diluent specified cannot be obtained (use only specified diluent; substitution risks precipitation/inactivation)
- Solution is cloudy, discolored, or shows particles after reconstitution
- Expiration time exceeded
- You are not confident in aseptic technique (contaminated injectable = infection risk)
:::

### Procedure 3: Preparing Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS) for Acute Diarrhea

**Time:** 10 minutes
**Equipment:** Clean container, measuring spoons, water
**Stability:** 24 hours at room temperature, 48 hours refrigerated

**Steps:**
1. Measure 1 liter (4 cups) clean water into container
2. Add 6 teaspoons granulated sugar (or honey if sugar unavailable)
3. Add 1/2 teaspoon table salt (sodium chloride)
4. If available, add 1/2 teaspoon potassium chloride (salt substitute) or use 4 oz coconut water for potassium
5. Stir thoroughly for 30 seconds
6. Label with contents, date prepared, expiration time (24 hours room temp or 48 hours refrigerated)
7. Administer: give small sips frequently (5-15mL every 5-10 minutes) to maximize absorption

**Modified formula if standard ingredients unavailable:**
- Substitute rice water (boil rice, strain liquid) for plain water (enhanced absorption)
- Substitute honey for sugar (1 tablespoon honey per liter)
- Omit potassium chloride if unavailable (suboptimal but acceptable)

### Procedure 4: Pediatric Dosing from Adult Medications

**Time:** 5-10 minutes (calculation + preparation)
**Equipment:** Scale or balance, syringe, graduated cylinder, calculator
**Stability:** Use immediately

**Pediatric Dosing Methods:**

**Method 1: Weight-Based Dosing (Most Accurate)**
Formula: Child dose (mg) = Adult dose (mg) × [Child weight (kg) / 70 kg]

Example: Amoxicillin adult dose 500 mg; child weighs 20 kg.
- Child dose = 500 mg × (20/70) = 500 × 0.286 = 143 mg

**Method 2: Clark's Formula (Simpler)**
Formula: Child dose = Adult dose × [Child weight (lbs) / 150 lbs]

Example: Acetaminophen adult dose 650 mg; child weighs 44 lbs.
- Child dose = 650 × (44/150) = 650 × 0.293 = 191 mg

**Method 3: Young's Rule (Age-Based, Less Accurate)**
Formula: Child dose = Adult dose × [Child age / (Child age + 12)]

Example: Ibuprofen adult dose 400 mg; child age 4 years.
- Child dose = 400 × (4 / 16) = 400 × 0.25 = 100 mg

**Special Considerations:**
- **Neonates (0-3 months):** Use 1/8 to 1/6 adult dose; organ systems immature
- **Infants (3-12 months):** Use 1/6 to 1/4 adult dose
- **Toddlers (1-3 years):** Use 1/4 to 1/3 adult dose; liquid formulations preferred
- **Children (4-8 years):** Use 1/3 to 1/2 adult dose
- **Older children (9-12 years):** Use 1/2 to 2/3 adult dose or weight-based calculation

:::danger
### Pediatric Dosing Errors Are Lethal

Common mistakes:
- Confusing liquid concentration (5 mg/mL vs. 50 mg/mL)—can cause 10-fold overdose
- Rounding doses incorrectly (round DOWN for pediatric patients)
- Using adult formulations in young children (some drugs inappropriate under certain ages)
- Forgetting to convert weight units (pounds vs. kilograms)
- Not accounting for kidney/liver immaturity (clearance reduced)

**Best practice:** Calculate, write down the dose, have second person verify calculation independently. Do NOT rely on mental math.
:::

### Procedure 5: Preparing Simple Alcohol-Based Tincture

**Time:** 5 minutes preparation + 4 weeks extraction
**Equipment:** Clean glass jar with lid, dried plant material, alcohol (40%+ ethanol)
**Stability:** 2-5 years in sealed, dark container

**Steps:**
1. Harvest/obtain dried plant material (use only known medicinal plants: willow bark for fever, yarrow for wounds, etc.)
2. Fill clean glass jar 1/3 full with dried plant material (chopped finely)
3. Cover completely with alcohol—aim for 1:10 ratio (1 part plant : 10 parts alcohol)
4. Seal jar tightly with lid
5. Store in cool, dark location away from sunlight
6. Shake jar every 2-3 days for 4 weeks minimum
7. After 4 weeks: strain through fine cloth or coffee filter into clean container
8. Discard spent plant material
9. Label with plant name, date prepared, storage instructions
10. Administer: typically 5-30mL per dose depending on tincture potency

:::info-box
### Tincture Dosing Guidance

- **Weak tincture (herbal, low toxicity):** 20-30mL per dose, 2-3 times daily
- **Strong tincture (potent herbs):** 5-10mL per dose, 2-3 times daily
- **Pediatric:** 1/4 to 1/2 adult dose depending on age
- Administer mixed in water if taste objectionable
- Storage: amber glass bottles in cool, dark location
:::

### Dose Calculation for Weight-Based Medications

**Formula:** Patient weight (kg) × Dose per kg = Total dose

**Example:** 8-year-old (25 kg) with infection. Amoxicillin dosed at 25mg/kg.
- 25 kg × 25 mg/kg = 625 mg total dose
- Divide into 3 doses (every 8 hours) = ~208 mg per dose
- If have 250 mg tablets: give 1 tablet per dose (slight overdose acceptable, or crush tablet and give 4/5)

:::tip
### Rounding Doses for Practicality

In survival scenarios, perfect dosing rarely possible. Acceptable rounding:
- If calculated dose is 183 mg and have 250 mg tablets: give 1 tablet (37% over, acceptable for most antibiotics)
- If calculated dose is 475 mg and have 500 mg tablets: give 1 tablet (5% over, within margin)
- If calculated dose is 25 mg and have 500 mg tablets: crush and divide into 20 pieces (⬇️ risk of uneven distribution; prefer liquid if available)

**Goal:** Stay within +/- 20% of calculated dose when possible.
:::

## Topical Preparations (Ointments, Creams, Poultices, Wound Powders)

### Ointment Preparation

**Time:** 15-20 minutes
**Equipment:** Heat source (water bath), mortar/pestle, clean containers
**Stability:** 1-2 months room temperature if stored cool/dry

**Steps:**
1. **Choose base:** Petroleum jelly (vaseline) is easiest—inert, stable, waterproof. Alternatives: lanolin, coconut oil, lard
2. **Prepare active ingredient:** If starting with powder (e.g., sulfur, activated charcoal), grind to fine powder in mortar
3. **Melt base (if needed):** Warm petroleum jelly in water bath (never direct heat) to ~50-60°C until semi-liquid
4. **Combine:** Add active ingredient powder to melted base (10-30% by weight typical); stir thoroughly for 2-3 minutes
5. **Cool:** Pour into clean jars while still warm; allow to cool to room temperature and solidify (1-2 hours)
6. **Label:** Include active ingredient, concentration (%), date prepared, and storage instructions

**Common Topical Preparations:**

- **Sulfur ointment (5-10%):** For scabies, ringworm, acne; mix sulfur powder into petroleum jelly
- **Activated charcoal ointment (5%):** For infected wounds, absorbs odor/toxins; mix charcoal powder with lanolin
- **Turmeric ointment (5%):** Anti-inflammatory; mix turmeric powder with coconut oil
- **Plantain ointment (10%):** Wound healing; infuse fresh plantain leaves in hot oil, strain, mix with petroleum jelly

### Cream Preparation

Creams are lighter and more water-absorbent than ointments:

**Steps:**
1. Prepare oil phase: Heat oil (coconut, almond, or mineral oil) to 40-50°C
2. Prepare water phase: Dissolve active ingredient in hot water (40-50°C); may include alcohol or glycerin
3. Emulsify: Slowly add water phase to oil phase while stirring vigorously (creates emulsion)
4. Cool: Stir as mixture cools to prevent separation; continues to 25°C
5. Store: Creams separate easily; keep in sealed jars, refrigerate if possible

**Caution:** Without proper emulsifiers (lecithin, beeswax), water and oil separate. For survival, simpler oil-based ointments are more reliable.

### Poultice Preparation

A poultice is a moist paste applied directly to skin (useful for drawing infections, reducing inflammation):

**Steps:**
1. Obtain active ingredient: Powdered herbs (plantain, comfrey, turmeric), activated charcoal, or clay
2. Mix with liquid: Add water, broth, or oil to powder until paste consistency (spreadable but not runny)
3. Apply: Spread directly on clean skin; cover with cloth to contain
4. Duration: Leave 4-6 hours or overnight; refresh as needed
5. Disposal: Discard used poultice; wash skin after removal

**Common Poultices:**
- Plantain + water: For infected cuts, insect bites
- Turmeric + honey: For inflammation, bruises
- Activated charcoal + water: For abscess drainage

### Wound Powder Preparation

Powders for open wounds absorb drainage and provide antimicrobial action:

**Steps:**
1. Choose base powder: Talc (traditional but harmful—avoid), clay (bentonite), or diatomaceous earth (food-grade)
2. Add active ingredient: Mix antimicrobial powder (sulfur, activated charcoal) or herb powder (yarrow, sage) into base
3. Ratio: 5-20% active ingredient by weight
4. Dry storage: Keep in sealed container in cool, dry place
5. Application: Dust directly onto clean, dry wound; mix it with small amount of oil if needed for adherence

**Caution:** Never use on actively bleeding wounds (powder prevents hemostasis—let blood clot first). Use only on clean, dry wounds.

<section id="complications">

## Complications & When to Stop

### Signs of Compounded Preparation Failure

**Stop using and discard immediately if:**

1. **Cloudiness/particulates in solution** (non-suspension): Indicates contamination or degradation
2. **Color change:** Brown/yellow tint in clear liquid = oxidation or bacterial growth
3. **Mold/growth visible:** Any fuzzy growth on surface = fungal contamination (fatal if injected)
4. **Foul/unusual odor:** Indicates bacterial overgrowth
5. **Separation/caking:** In suspensions, excessive settling that won't re-suspend = degradation
6. **Crystallization:** Crystals forming in liquid = solute precipitating out (concentration wrong)

:::danger
### Contaminated Injectable Solutions

If injectable preparation shows ANY sign of contamination, discard completely. Do not attempt to "fix" by filtering. Administering contaminated injectable solution can cause:
- Sepsis (bloodstream infection)
- Abscess at injection site
- Tissue necrosis
- Death

Even if you are uncertain, discard and prepare fresh. The risk is not worth conserving materials.
:::

### Dosing Complications

**Underdose (too little medication):** Treatment ineffective; infection worsens, pain inadequately controlled. Symptoms persist beyond expected timeframe. Response: increase dose if within safe range, or switch to stronger medication if available.

**Overdose (too much medication):** Acute toxicity, nausea/vomiting, CNS effects (dizziness, confusion), organ damage (liver/kidney). For some drugs (acetaminophen, aspirin), overdose causes specific syndromes. Response: assess for toxicity signs, supportive care, activated charcoal if oral ingestion recent (within 2 hours).

### Allergic/Adverse Reactions

**Minor reactions:** Rash, mild itch, gastrointestinal upset (nausea, cramping). Management: give antihistamine (diphenhydramine 25-50mg), monitor, continue medication if tolerable.

**Serious reactions:** Anaphylaxis (difficulty breathing, throat swelling, severe hypotension), Stevens-Johnson syndrome (severe blistering rash), serum sickness (fever, joint pain, severe rash). Response: STOP medication immediately, treat anaphylaxis with epinephrine if available, provide supportive care.

### Stability Window Exceeded

If compounded preparation is beyond its documented stability time:
- Efficacy reduced (medication degraded)
- Toxicity risk increased (degradation products may be harmful)
- Microbial contamination possible (if beyond refrigeration/preservation)

**Action:** Discard and prepare fresh batch. Do not attempt to use expired compounded medication for serious infections or critical care.

### When to Seek Professional Help (if available)

- Severe allergic reaction to medication
- Signs of systemic infection (fever, chills, swelling)
- Overdose with concern for toxicity
- Complications during administration (respiratory difficulty, loss of consciousness)
- Medication not working as expected despite correct dosing

<section id="aftercare">

## Recovery & Aftercare

### Patient Monitoring After Administration

**For oral medications (systemic):**
- First 30 minutes: monitor for allergic reaction (rash, difficulty breathing, throat tightness)
- 1-2 hours: assess for adverse effects (nausea, dizziness, unusual symptoms)
- Ongoing: track symptom improvement (fever reduction, infection clearing, pain control)

**For injections (IV/IM):**
- Immediately after: assess for immediate reactions (anaphylaxis, severe local reaction)
- 15-30 minutes: monitor for delayed allergic responses, local tissue reaction
- Every 1-2 hours: check injection site for infection signs (redness spreading, warmth, drainage)
- If IV line: monitor for infiltration (swelling around catheter), phlebitis (vein inflammation—red line), or occlusion (fluid not running)

**For topical preparations:**
- 15-30 minutes: assess for burning, stinging (expected minor irritation acceptable)
- 1-2 hours: evaluate effect on wound/rash
- Daily: monitor for allergic contact dermatitis (spreading redness, itching, blistering)

### Documenting Treatment

Record for each patient:
- Drug name and dose given
- Route (oral, IM, IV, topical)
- Date and time administered
- Batch number or preparation date (enables traceability)
- Patient response (good tolerance vs. adverse effects)
- Any complications or notes

Simple format: notebook entry with patient name, date, medication, dose, route, outcome.

### Pediatric Post-Treatment Care

**For children receiving compounded liquid medications:**
- Encourage fluid intake (medication + water dilution aids absorption)
- Monitor for vomiting (if vomits within 30 minutes of dose, may need to re-dose)
- Watch for signs of toxicity specific to drug (tremor, excessive urination, behavioral changes)

**For crushed tablets:**
- Ensure complete ingestion (child doesn't spit out residue)
- Give small amount of food after if tolerated (improves palatability, reduces nausea)

### Disposal of Unused Compounded Medication

- **Solid waste:** Wrap leftover compounded powders/tablets in paper, dispose with general waste (if non-toxic)
- **Liquid waste:** Pour excess solution down drain followed by water (acceptable in survival, assumes wastewater processing)
- **Injectable vials:** Blunt needle or leave needle sterile, place in rigid sharps container (or glass jar sealed with tape), bury away from water sources if no other option
- **Never reuse compounded medication** from one patient for another (contamination risk, inadequate dosing)

## Injectable Medication Reconstitution & Quality Control

### Aseptic Technique for Injectables

Injectable medications carry the highest contamination risk. Parenteral administration (intravenous, intramuscular) bypasses skin barrier, introducing bacteria directly into bloodstream or muscle tissue.

**Critical Aseptic Steps:**

1. **Hand hygiene:** Wash with soap and water; dry thoroughly (water on hands introduces contamination)
2. **Disinfect workspace:** Clean table with 70% isopropyl alcohol (or diluted bleach 1:10); allow to air dry
3. **Don gloves:** Sterile latex or nitrile gloves; if unavailable, thoroughly washed hands acceptable but less ideal
4. **Prepare vials:** Remove foil cap from vial; disinfect rubber septum (stopper) with alcohol pad; let dry completely
5. **Sterile components:** Use only sterile syringes, needles, and alcohol pads from sealed packages. Do NOT reuse needles between vials.
6. **Minimize air exposure:** Work quickly; prepare and administer without delays
7. **Discard immediately:** After injection, discard needle in rigid sharps container (glass jar, plastic sharps box)

:::danger
### Injectable Contamination Consequences

Bacterial infection from contaminated injectable:
- **Local:** Abscess at injection site (pus-filled swelling, warmth, pain)
- **Systemic:** Bacteremia → sepsis (fever, chills, shock, organ failure, death)
- **Fungal:** More rare but possible; fungal sepsis is extremely difficult to treat without IV antifungal drugs

Even minor contamination (bacteria or fungi from a fingertip) can cause life-threatening infection. If ANY doubt about sterility, prepare fresh batch.
:::

### Stability Testing for Injectables

Injectable solutions degrade over time. Proper testing ensures medication safety:

1. **Visual inspection:** Clear solutions should remain crystal clear. Cloudiness, particles, or color change = do not use. Suspensions are expected to be cloudy; shake before use, but should resuspend easily.

2. **Sterility assessment (field):** No simple test without lab culture media. Preventive: use aseptic technique, assume contamination if:
   - Solution was exposed to non-sterile environment
   - Vial was opened and left uncovered
   - Visible particle in solution
   - Foul odor (indicates bacterial overgrowth)

3. **pH testing (if available):** Most injectable antibiotics are neutral to slightly acidic (pH 6.5-7.5). Extreme pH indicates degradation. Use pH paper (matches to color chart).

4. **Potency retention:** Over time, active ingredient degrades. Reconstituted antibiotics lose potency faster at room temperature. Typical:
   - Reconstituted penicillin: 7-14 days refrigerated; 1-4 hours room temperature
   - Reconstituted cephalosporin: 7-10 days refrigerated; 2-4 hours room temperature
   - Exact times depend on drug; consult package insert if available

### IV Solution Preparation (Saline, Dextrose)

Simple IV solutions can be compounded for fluid replacement in dehydration or blood loss:

**Normal Saline (0.9% NaCl) for IV:**
- Dissolve 9 grams sodium chloride in 1 liter of sterile water
- Final concentration: 0.9% (isotonic—safe for IV)
- Sterilization: If using boiled water (not sterile), filter through 0.22 micron filter if available; otherwise accept higher infection risk
- Storage: Room temperature, sealed container; expires 24-48 hours without preservatives

**5% Dextrose in Water (D5W):**
- Dissolve 50 grams dextrose (glucose) in 1 liter sterile water
- Provides calories (5% dextrose = ~170 kcal/liter) but minimal electrolytes
- Use: Rehydration when no electrolyte imbalance; safer than plain water for IV
- Storage: Room temperature; clear solution; expires 24 hours

**Administration:**
- Warm IV fluids to body temperature if possible (room-temperature IV causes hypothermia)
- Use sterile tubing and catheter; disinfect insertion site with alcohol pad
- Monitor for signs of infection (redness at site, fever, sepsis)
- Discontinue if contamination suspected

<section id="troubleshooting">

## Troubleshooting Compounding Problems

### Problem: Tablet Won't Crush to Fine Powder

**Causes:**
- Tablet is sustained-release (hard coating designed to resist crushing)
- Tablet is too hard (aged, humidity exposure)
- Mortar/pestle inadequate for force needed

**Solutions:**
- Use tablet breaker/pill cutter if available (splits tablet, easier to crush halves)
- Try soaking tablet in small amount of warm water 2-3 minutes (softens), then crush
- If liquid formulation available, substitute (do not force crush of sustained-release)
- Place tablet in small plastic bag, crush with hammer (less elegant, but effective)

### Problem: Liquid Won't Mix Evenly (Still Cloudy After Mixing)

**Causes:**
- Powder not dissolving (solubility issue—drug incompatible with liquid)
- Insufficient mixing time
- Particles too fine (floating longer)

**Solutions:**
- Let vial/container sit undisturbed 5-10 minutes (particles settle naturally)
- Shake/stir again for another 30-60 seconds
- If still cloudy after 15 minutes: this is a suspension (expected), shake before each dose
- If cloudiness accompanied by foul odor/color change: discard (contamination)

### Problem: Reconstituted Antibiotic Looks Wrong (Crystal/Particles)

**Causes:**
- Incompatible diluent used (should have used sterile water, not normal saline)
- Diluent temperature too cold (crystals forming)
- Contamination introduced during reconstitution

**Solutions:**
- Check original vial instructions: what diluent was specified?
- If wrong diluent used: discard vial, cannot salvage
- If diluent too cold: warm vial in hands or warm water (not boiling) for 1-2 minutes, particles may dissolve
- If unsure of cause: discard and prepare fresh

### Problem: Tincture Cloudy After 4-Week Extraction

**Causes:**
- Inadequate straining (fine plant particles remaining)
- Water contamination in plant material
- Temperature fluctuation causing precipitation

**Solutions:**
- Re-strain through finer cloth or coffee filter multiple times
- For cloudiness caused by water: acceptable to use (minor efficacy reduction); strain again if possible
- Store in cool, dark location (prevents further precipitation)
- If unacceptable: discard and restart with dried plant material

### Problem: Cannot Obtain Specified Diluent for Injectable Powder

**Cause:** Sterile water unavailable for antibiotic reconstitution

**Solutions:**
- Use boiled then cooled water (not ideal; infection risk higher, but acceptable if sterile powder)
- Use normal saline (0.9% NaCl) only if specifically allowed on package (check first)
- Contact original medication manufacturer if instructions available
- If neither available: crush tablet and compound as suspension instead (if tablet formulation available)

:::warning
### Critical Diluent Issue

Never guess on diluent type for injectables. Wrong diluent causes:
- Precipitation (unusable)
- Inactivation of drug
- Toxicity from precipitation particles

If unsure, do not attempt. Wait for correct diluent or use alternative route.
:::

### Problem: Patient Refuses Bitter-Tasting Compounded Liquid

**Causes:**
- Natural unpleasant taste (tinctures, antibiotics in solution)
- Child refusal due to taste

**Solutions:**
- Mix with flavor (juice, honey, vanilla extract if available)—small amount to minimize volume
- Administer in applesauce or other thick food (if absorption not affected)
- Give as "shot" to minimize taste exposure (quick administration)
- Consider enteral feeding tube if available (bypasses taste)
- For pediatric: smaller, more frequent doses of lower volume (less taste exposure)

### Problem: Medication Expired but Still Needed

**Cause:** Only medication available is past expiration date

**Assessment:**
- How long past expiration? (1 month = higher retained potency; 1 year = degraded)
- Storage conditions? (Cool/dark = better retention; warm/humid = faster degradation)
- Visual inspection: color/clarity normal?
- Is this critical (life-saving) or non-critical use?

**Decision framework:**
- **Non-critical (pain, cold):** Do not use if expired >6 months
- **Critical (infection, severe pain):** Can use if <1 year past expiration AND visually acceptable
- **Life-or-death (sepsis, anaphylaxis):** Use even if expired >1 year

**Before using:** Inspect for color change, particles, odor. If any abnormality: discard. Do not compound expired medication.

</section>

<section id="aseptic-technique-reference">

## Aseptic Technique Reference: Sterile Preparation Procedures

Aseptic technique is the systematic approach to preventing contamination during preparation of medications intended for parenteral (injectable) administration. Unlike oral compounding where the stomach's acid environment provides some protection against contamination, injectable preparations bypass all natural barriers. A single bacterium or fungal spore can cause sepsis, abscess formation, or death. Aseptic technique is not optional—it is the foundation of safe injectable compounding.

### Step-by-Step Sterile Preparation Procedures

#### Step 1: Hand Hygiene (Critical Foundation)

Hand hygiene is the most important element of aseptic technique. Your hands are a major source of bacterial contamination.

1. **Wash hands with soap and clean running water:**
   - Wet hands with warm water
   - Apply soap and scrub for 20 seconds, covering palms, backs of hands, between fingers, under nails, and wrists
   - Special attention: areas under fingernails harbor bacteria and fungal spores
   - Rinse thoroughly with clean water
   - Dry hands with a clean cloth or paper towel (not a shared towel)

2. **If available, perform antiseptic hand preparation:**
   - After washing, apply 70% isopropyl alcohol, surgical scrub (chlorhexidine or iodine), or sterile hand sanitizer
   - Rub hands together vigorously until dry (at least 30 seconds)
   - This further reduces bacterial count on skin surface

3. **When preparing injections:**
   - Never touch the prepared area of syringes, needles, or vial interiors after washing
   - If you must touch the preparation area, rewash hands before continuing
   - Avoid sneezing or coughing near the work area
   - Do not eat, drink, or apply cosmetics during compounding

#### Step 2: Clean Room Setup (Environmental Contamination Control)

The workspace itself is a major source of contamination. Environmental surfaces, dust, air currents, and other people's shed skin cells can all introduce bacteria.

1. **Workspace preparation:**
   - Select a clean, dedicated area away from foot traffic and activities
   - Avoid compounding in kitchen areas, bathrooms, or near animals
   - If possible, designate a separate room for injectable preparation
   - Remove clutter, papers, and unnecessary items from the workspace

2. **Disinfection of surfaces:**
   - Wipe down work surface (table, bench, or counter) with:
     - 70% isopropyl alcohol (preferred), OR
     - Household disinfectant spray, OR
     - Diluted bleach (1 part bleach : 10 parts water), OR
     - Boiled water with soap if nothing else available
   - Allow to air dry completely (do not wipe dry)
   - Drying time is critical: alcohol air-drying kills residual bacteria

3. **Work zone isolation:**
   - Create a "clean zone" on the prepared surface
   - Keep all non-essential items outside this zone
   - Arrange equipment and supplies within arm's reach
   - Minimize hand movements in and out of the work area

#### Step 3: Equipment Sterilization and Preparation

Sterilize all equipment contacting the injectable preparation. In survival scenarios, "sterile" means boiled, and use sterile packages if available.

1. **Sterilization methods (in order of preference):**
   - **Autoclave (if available):** 15 minutes at 15 psi, 121°C—gold standard for sterilization
   - **Boiling:** 15-20 minutes rolling boil at sea level; add 1 minute for each 1000 feet elevation
   - **Pressure cooker:** 15 minutes at 15 psi if autoclave unavailable
   - **Dry heat oven:** 2 hours at 160°C (too slow in emergency situations)

2. **Syringes and needles (if reusing):**
   - Boil for 15-20 minutes
   - Cool on a clean towel without touching the plunger or needle tip
   - Allow to cool completely before use (hot instruments burn when handling)
   - Use only boiled syringes/needles, never unwashed equipment
   - **Preferred:** Use pre-sterilized, individually packaged syringes and needles if available (purchased pre-collapse)

3. **Bottles and containers:**
   - Wash thoroughly with soap and water
   - Boil for 15-20 minutes if empty containers
   - If compounding a multi-dose vial, boil empty vial before filling
   - Allow to cool; do not wipe interior dry (residual moisture is acceptable)

#### Step 4: Glove Use and Aseptic Handling

Gloves provide a critical barrier between hand flora and the preparation.

1. **Sterile glove selection:**
   - **Preferred:** Sterile latex or nitrile gloves from sealed packages (purchased pre-collapse)
   - **Acceptable:** Thoroughly washed clean (not sterile) gloves if sterile unavailable
   - **Minimum acceptable:** Bare hands with rigorous hand washing and antiseptic preparation

2. **Donning (putting on) gloves:**
   - After hand hygiene and antiseptic prep, let hands air dry completely
   - Open sterile glove package carefully (if using sterile gloves)
   - Pinch the cuff, slide hand in, being careful not to touch the exterior surface
   - Slide cuff up over wrist
   - Put on second glove using first gloved hand on the outside of the second glove cuff
   - Do not let bare skin touch anything outside the gloves

3. **During compounding:**
   - Maintain a "sterile field"—the area where sterilized equipment and materials will be handled
   - Do not touch your face, clothes, or non-sterilized areas with gloved hands
   - If glove becomes contaminated (touches non-sterile surface, tears), remove and replace immediately
   - Avoid touching vial rubber septa with ungloved hands before needle insertion

#### Step 5: Vial Disinfection and Preparation

The rubber septum (stopper) of a vial is the entry point for bacteria. Disinfect it properly.

1. **Visual inspection before preparation:**
   - Examine vial for cracks, cloudiness in contents, or particles (signs of contamination)
   - Check expiration date
   - Verify correct medication and concentration
   - If any abnormality noted: discard vial and prepare fresh

2. **Disinfection of vial rubber septum:**
   - Hold vial firmly with non-dominant hand
   - Using dominant hand, grasp an alcohol pad (70% isopropyl alcohol)
   - Vigorously wipe the rubber septum in circular motions for at least 10 seconds
   - Approach is important: wipe outward from center in spiral pattern (prevents re-contaminating center)
   - Allow alcohol to air dry completely—at least 30 seconds
   - **Critical point:** Do not fan or blow on vial to speed drying. Drying allows alcohol to kill remaining bacteria

3. **Never touch disinfected surface:**
   - Once septum is disinfected and dried, do not touch it with fingers
   - Do not re-wipe unless contamination suspected
   - Proceed immediately to needle insertion

#### Step 6: Needle and Syringe Preparation

Contamination can occur during syringe-needle assembly.

1. **Assembly (if using separate needle and syringe):**
   - Remove syringe from sterile package
   - Remove needle from sterile package
   - Attach needle to syringe by twisting (firm seal important)
   - Do not touch needle tip or syringe barrel interior with fingers

2. **Filling the syringe (for diluent or medication):**
   - Hold syringe and vial so vial is upside down (mouth facing down)
   - Insert needle through disinfected septum at slight angle
   - Slowly pull back plunger to fill syringe with desired volume
   - Remove syringe and needle from vial

3. **Needle handling:**
   - Never recap needle with bare hands (high injury risk and re-contamination risk)
   - If needle must be changed (e.g., filled needle used for drawing up diluent, fresh needle used for vial injection), use a needle-capping device or carefully place needle on sterile field
   - Use each needle for single puncture only: do not reuse needle between vials

#### Step 7: Sterile Field Maintenance

A sterile field is an area where only sterilized equipment and materials contact a clean surface.

1. **Preparing the sterile field:**
   - Use a clean, disinfected surface (aluminum tray, clean towel, or sterile drape if available)
   - Arrange all needed equipment and supplies before beginning work
   - Create clear boundary between sterile and non-sterile areas

2. **Maintaining sterility:**
   - Minimize movement of hands in/out of sterile field
   - Keep fingers away from items that will contact injectable medication
   - Do not reach over sterile field with ungloved hands
   - Keep sterile equipment separated from dirty items
   - If any doubt about sterility: consider it contaminated and discard

#### Step 8: Reconstitution and Mixing (Minimal Contamination)

Once prepared, injectable solutions are vulnerable to contamination through minimal handling.

1. **Reconstitution procedure (antibiotic powder example):**
   - Verify correct medication and diluent volume
   - Draw up specified volume of sterile diluent into syringe
   - Inject diluent slowly into powder vial (do not shake—creates bubbles, air incorporation)
   - Gently swirl vial to facilitate dissolution
   - Allow to stand 1-2 minutes for complete dissolution

2. **Visual confirmation:**
   - Keep solution clear (unless noted otherwise in instructions)
   - No particles, cloudiness, or color abnormalities
   - If cloudiness or particles present: contamination or precipitation occurred—discard vial

#### Step 9: Contamination Indicators and Detection

Field sterilization has limitations. Recognize signs of contamination.

1. **Visual indicators of likely contamination:**
   - **Cloudiness in clear solutions:** Bacterial growth, fungal contamination, or precipitation
   - **Particles visible:** Undissolved material, skin cells, dust, bacterial aggregates
   - **Color change:** Oxidation, degradation, or bacterial byproducts
   - **Separation/caking in suspensions:** Degradation or contamination with water

2. **Odor indicators:**
   - **Foul or unusual odor:** Bacterial overgrowth (produces volatile organic compounds)
   - **Putrid smell:** Advanced sepsis of the preparation—do not use

3. **Absence of obvious contamination does NOT guarantee sterility:**
   - Bacteria and fungi may be present without visible signs
   - Incubation time required for growth (typically 24-48 hours)
   - Always use aseptic technique; assume any non-sterile preparation is contaminated

### Injectable Preparation Risks: Why Contamination Is Fatal

:::danger
### High-Risk Contamination Scenarios

**Scenario 1: Contamination from skin flora**
- Even with hand washing, skin flora (Staphylococcus aureus, Staphylococcus epidermidis) persist
- If needle passes through skin before disinfection, bacteria enter vial
- Risk: local abscess at injection site progressing to sepsis within 24-72 hours

**Scenario 2: Contamination from environmental sources**
- Dust containing Aspergillus (fungal spores) settles on vial during preparation
- Fungal contamination difficult to detect visually; grows slowly (days)
- Risk: fungal sepsis—extremely difficult to treat without IV antifungal therapy

**Scenario 3: Contamination from insufficiently sterilized equipment**
- Spore-forming bacteria (Bacillus, Clostridium) survive boiling <15-20 minutes
- Pressure cooker may not reach adequate temperature/pressure
- Risk: late onset abscess (days to weeks post-injection)

**Scenario 4: Contamination from vial manipulation**
- Repeated needle insertions into multi-dose vial introduce bacteria
- Each insertion risks introducing contamination
- Risk: increasing bacterial load with each use; cumulative infection risk

**Consequences of Injectable Contamination:**
- **Localized:** Abscess (pus collection) at injection site; requires surgical drainage
- **Systemic:** Bacteremia → sepsis (fever, chills, shock, organ failure)
- **Fungal sepsis:** Extremely difficult to treat; often fatal without antifungal therapy
- **Endocarditis:** Infection of heart valve; leads to heart failure
- **Mortality rate:** Even with antibiotics, untreated sepsis is 30-40% fatal; without antibiotics, nearly 100% fatal
:::

### Acceptable Risk Acceptance Framework

In true emergency scenarios where injectable medication is necessary for survival:

1. **Life-threatening infection (sepsis, severe pneumonia):** Risk of sepsis from contaminated preparation is outweighed by benefit of antibiotic therapy
2. **Severe injury with hemorrhage:** IV fluid replacement necessary; risk of infection is secondary to hemorrhage prevention
3. **Diabetic coma:** Injectable insulin necessary; risk of infection secondary to life-saving treatment

**Key principle:** Only accept infection risk when benefit clearly outweighs risk. Non-critical medications should never be given via contaminated injectables.

### Post-Injection Monitoring for Contamination

After administration, monitor closely for signs of infection:

1. **First 24 hours:**
   - Fever (>38°C or 100.4°F)
   - Severe pain at injection site (beyond normal soreness)
   - Rapid spreading redness or swelling
   - Warmth to touch at site

2. **Days 2-7:**
   - Abscess formation (pus-filled swelling, fluctuance)
   - Lymphadenopathy (swollen lymph nodes draining area)
   - Streaking (red lines extending from injection site toward heart)

3. **Any sign of infection: immediate action**
   - Stop using the preparation
   - Discard remaining vial
   - Treat with alternative antibiotics if available
   - Perform local wound care (warm compresses, drainage if abscess forms)
   - Monitor for systemic infection (fever, chills, hypotension)

## Common Mistakes (Expanded)

### Mistake 1: Crushing Sustained-Release Tablets

**What happens:** Destroys the coating that prevents rapid absorption. Drug absorbed all at once into bloodstream = toxicity (overdose effect).

**Examples:** Extended-release opioids (morphine ER, oxycodone ER), extended-release antidepressants, some blood pressure medications.

**Prevention:** Check tablet imprint/packaging for "ER," "XR," "Extended Release," "Sustained Release." If unclear: do not crush. If must dose pediatric patient: use immediate-release formulation or liquid if available.

### Mistake 2: Confusing Concentration Units (mg vs mL, % vs mg/mL)

**Confusion example:** "5% solution" vs. "5 mg/mL."
- 5% w/v = 5g per 100mL = 50 mg/mL (vastly different doses)
- Writing "5 mL of 5%" is meaningless without specification (w/w, w/v, v/v)

**Prevention:** Always specify both amount AND concentration. Example: "Give 5 mL of 500mg/5mL amoxicillin suspension" (clear). Not: "Give 5 mL" (ambiguous if you don't know what it's 5mL OF).

### Mistake 3: Insufficient Aseptic Technique for Injectable Medications

**What happens:** Bacteria/fungal spores contaminate vial. Patient develops abscess, sepsis, or death.

**Examples of poor technique:**
- Not disinfecting vial top before needle insertion
- Touching sterile field with ungloved hands
- Preparing injection in dirty environment
- Reusing needle between vials

**Prevention:** Before any injectable preparation: (1) Clean workspace with alcohol or disinfectant. (2) Wear gloves. (3) Disinfect vial top with alcohol pad, wait 30 seconds dry. (4) Use sterile needle per vial. (5) Prepare quickly, minimize air exposure.

:::danger
### High-Risk Mistakes for Injectable Medications

- Using non-sterile diluent
- Preparing in contaminated environment
- Using vial/needle multiple times
- Reusing preparation if contamination suspected

Any of these risks life-threatening infection. If uncertain about sterility: prepare fresh, discard questionable batch.
:::

### Mistake 4: Measuring Errors (Guessing vs. Using Calibrated Equipment)

**Error:** Using unmarked spoon for "1 teaspoon" dose. Actual volume varies 3-8mL depending on spoon.

**Impact:** Patient gets 2-4x intended dose or 1/8 intended dose.

**Prevention:** Always use calibrated equipment. Syringe (most accurate), measuring spoon (acceptable), graduated cylinder (good for liquids). Never eyeball volume.

### Mistake 5: Not Documenting Preparation Details

**Risk:** If adverse event occurs, cannot determine:
- What was actually given
- When it was prepared (stability status)
- Who prepared it (training level)
- Batch source

**Prevention:** Label EVERY compounded medication with: (1) Drug name, (2) Concentration/dose, (3) Date prepared, (4) Expiration time, (5) Route (oral/IV/topical), (6) Preparer initials.

### Mistake 6: Exceeding Stability Window

**Example:** Reconstituting antibiotic on Monday for Monday-Tuesday use (acceptable). Using same vial Friday (stability exceeded).

**Impact:** Medication degraded/contaminated; ineffective or toxic.

**Prevention:** Know stability window for every compounded medication:
- Reconstituted injectable: 7-14 days refrigerated (check package)
- Homemade liquid (no preservative): 4-8 hours room temperature, 24-48 hours refrigerated
- Topical ointment: 1-2 months if stored cool/dry
- Tinctures: 2+ years in sealed dark bottle

Mark expiration time on label. When time reached: discard, prepare fresh.

### Mistake 7: Using Wrong Diluent Type

**Example:** Package says "reconstitute with sterile water"; you use normal saline.

**Result:** Drug may precipitate, become inactivated, or cause toxicity.

**Prevention:** Read reconstitution instructions BEFORE starting. If unsure of diluent type: ask another pharmacist, check reference materials, or wait for correct diluent. Do not guess.

### Mistake 8: Administering Wrong Dose Due to Calculation Error

**Example:** Child needs 10mg/kg. Weighs 15kg. Should get 150mg total. You calculate 10mg × 15mL = 150mg (wrong formula—mixing units).

**Prevention:**
- Write out calculation step-by-step
- Verify units match (weight in kg, dose in mg/kg)
- Have second person double-check calculation
- Use dose charts if available (reference material)

<section id="field-adaptations">

## Field Adaptations (Low-Resource Scenarios)

### Compounding Without Graduated Cylinders

**Problem:** No precise measuring equipment

**Solutions:**
- **Syringes (best substitute):** Mark in mL, accurate to ±0.5mL for 3mL+ volumes
- **Improvised marks on container:** Fill cup with known volume (e.g., 100mL), mark line with permanent marker. Repeat for 50mL, 25mL, etc.
- **Volumetric calibration:** Use water (1mL water ≈ 1g). Weigh water on simple balance, know volume
- **Household equivalents:** 1 teaspoon ≈ 5mL, 1 tablespoon ≈ 15mL (acceptable for non-critical doses)

### Compounding Without a Proper Scale

**Problem:** Cannot measure powdered medications by weight

**Solutions:**
- **Balance scale (field-expedient):** Create equal-arm balance using stick, string. Calibrate using known weights or water
- **Relative measures:** Use seeds (grain of rice ≈ 20mg), coins (penny ≈ 2.5g), or stones calibrated against known object
- **Volumetric substitution (limited):** Some drugs have consistent bulk density. 500mg amoxicillin ≈ 1/4 teaspoon (rough, not ideal)

### Compounding Without Refrigeration

**Problem:** Medications degrade rapidly without cool storage

**Solutions:**
- **Evaporative cooling:** Wet cloth around container, place in breeze (reduces temperature 5-15°F)
- **Root cellar/underground storage:** Cool, stable temperature, darkness
- **Small batch preparation:** Make only what will be used within 4-8 hours (room temperature stability for most oral liquids)
- **Choose long-shelf-life formulations:** Powders (pre-reconstitution) last months/years; liquids only hours-days

### Compounding Without Sterile Technique Equipment

**Problem:** No gloves, alcohol, sterile supplies for injectable compounding

**Caveat:** This is high-risk; avoid if possible.

**Makeshift approach (last resort for life-threatening infection):**
1. Wash hands thoroughly with soap and clean water
2. Disinfect workspace and vial top with boiled-then-cooled water
3. Use cleanest container available (glass preferred)
4. Boil needle/syringe if possible (15 minutes rolling boil = sterilization)
5. Prepare quickly, minimize air exposure
6. Monitor injection site closely for infection (redness spreading, warmth, drainage within 24-48 hours = stop use)

**Acceptance:** This carries high infection risk. Use only if infection/death otherwise certain.

### Tincture Preparation Without Alcohol

**Problem:** Cannot obtain ethanol (fermented or distilled)

**Alternatives:**
- **Vinegar (4-8% acetic acid):** Weaker extraction, but acceptable for mild tinctures. Use 1:5 ratio (1 part plant : 5 parts vinegar), soak 2-3 weeks
- **Honey (preservation via osmotic pressure):** Mix finely-ground plant material with honey 1:1, let sit sealed for 2-4 weeks. Creates thick preparation; dilute with water before dosing
- **Glycerin (if available):** Similar to alcohol; use 1:5 ratio, 3-4 week soak
- **No extraction:** Use plant material fresh/dried as tea (simmer in water 10-15 minutes). Lower potency, limited shelf life (use within 1-2 days)

:::info-box
### Priority Medications to Stockpile for Compounding

In survival scenarios, prioritize these raw pharmaceutical ingredients (pre-collapse):
1. **Amoxicillin/ampicillin powder** (most common infection treatment)
2. **Acetaminophen/ibuprofen powder** (pain, fever)
3. **Sterile water (large containers)** (diluent for injectables, saline)
4. **Binders/excipients** (starch, gelatin, gum arabic)
5. **Alcohol (40%+ ethanol)** (tinctures, antiseptic)
6. **Weighing scale** (most critical equipment)
7. **Syringes/needles** (accurate dosing, injectables)
:::

## Compounding Principles

### Compounding vs Manufacturing

**Manufacturing:** Large-scale production by pharmaceutical companies under strict GMP (Good Manufacturing Practice). Standardized, quality-assured. **Compounding:** Preparation of custom medication doses/formulations for individual patients. Legal in survival scenarios (pharmacist typically performs in normal settings). Can adapt available medications for different routes/strengths.

### Survival Pharmacology Needs

Limited medication availability requires: liquid formulations from tablets (for children, difficulty swallowing), higher/lower doses from available stock, alternative routes (liquid injection-grade medication from tablets), preservation of medications for extended shelf life. Compounding allows improvisation with available materials.

### Compounding Safety Rules

-   Aseptic technique: prevent contamination with bacteria, fungi, particles
-   Accurate measurements: pharmaceutical compounding requires precision; errors cause toxicity or inefficacy
-   Proper storage: maintain stability, prevent degradation
-   Documentation: record what was made, when, by whom, expiration
-   Training: ensure proper technique before attempting complex formulations

<section id="history">

## Historical Context & Evolution

### Traditional Compounding Era (Pre-1950s)

All medications hand-compounded by pharmacists. Mortar and pestle, scales, measuring vessels. Pharmacists trained for years to master techniques. All modern pharmacists had compounding training; now less common as mass manufacturing dominates. Survival advantage: if manufacturing fails, compounding skills resume pharmaceutical production.

### Industrial Shift (1950s-Present)

Pharmaceutical manufacturing moved to factories. Compounding relegated to special cases (pediatric, geriatric, formulation adjustments). Pharmacists' compounding skills atrophied. Survival realization: supply chain breakdown necessitates resurrecting compounding knowledge. In post-collapse scenarios, local pharmacists become invaluable for custom formulation.

### Resurgence (Modern Survival Thinking)

Preparedness communities now stockpile raw pharmaceutical ingredients (bulk powders, excipients, binders). Knowledge of compounding techniques ensures medication availability when commercial supplies fail. Single pharmacist can serve community of thousands with appropriate raw materials and equipment.

<section id="dosage-forms">

## Dosage Forms

### Tablets and Capsules

Solid dosage forms; portable, stable. Tablets can be crushed (if not sustained-release) for administration to those unable to swallow. Some medications enteric-coated (dissolve in intestine, not stomach); crushing destroys coating. Capsules: separate halves, mix powder with liquid/food if needed. Advantages: stable, portable, accurate dosing. Disadvantages: require swallowing, more difficult for pediatric patients.

### Liquids (Solutions and Suspensions)

**Solutions:** Drug dissolved in liquid (clear). **Suspensions:** Drug particles suspended (cloudy, requires shaking). Liquids: easier swallowing, flexible dosing, faster absorption. Disadvantages: shorter shelf life (especially suspensions), larger volume needed for same dose, require storage space. In survival: crucial for pediatric medication administration.

### Powders and Injectables

**Powder:** Dry form, long shelf life. Reconstitute (mix with liquid) before use. **Injectables:** Sterile solutions for IV/IM/SC administration. Allow bypassing GI tract (useful if GI upset/inability to swallow). Risk: infection if not sterile, needle injury. In survival: reserve for serious conditions requiring IV access.

### Topical Preparations

Creams, ointments, gels: applied to skin/mucous membranes. Advantages: localized effect, minimal systemic absorption (lower toxicity risk), familiar preparations. Disadvantages: slower effect, limited penetration. In survival: useful for wounds, infections, inflammation where systemic effects unnecessary.

### Tinctures and Extracts

Alcohol-based preparations of plant materials or drugs. Tincture: 1:10 ratio (1 part plant/drug : 10 parts alcohol). Extract (more concentrated): 1:1 or 2:1 ratios. Preparation: soak plant material in alcohol 4 weeks minimum (extraction), then strain. Shelf life: years to decades in sealed containers. Advantages: potent, long-lasting, flexible dosing. Disadvantages: requires alcohol source, tastes terrible (important for patient compliance).

## Basic Compounding Techniques

### Tablet Crushing and Suspension

1.  Identify tablet (ensure crushable, not sustained-release)
2.  Perform hand hygiene, don gloves
3.  Place tablet in clean cup/mortar
4.  Crush with pestle or spoon handle into powder
5.  Add small amount liquid (water, juice if flavor desired) to dissolve/suspend
6.  Stir thoroughly to ensure even distribution
7.  Draw up into syringe if volume allows, or use spoon/cup for administration
8.  Administer immediately to minimize degradation

### Powder Reconstitution

Many medications supplied as powder (especially antibiotics) requiring reconstitution before use. Steps: 1) Read instructions (amount of liquid, type). 2) Draw up specified volume of sterile water. 3) Add to powder vial slowly while swirling (don't shake—creates bubbles). 4) Let stand 1-2 minutes for complete dissolution. 5) Label with date/time of reconstitution, expiration time (typically 7-14 days refrigerated, hours at room temp—check instructions). 6) Store appropriately.

### Dilution Calculations

Formula: C₁V₁ = C₂V₂ (concentration₁ × volume₁ = concentration₂ × volume₂). Example: Have 500mg/5mL suspension, need 250mg. (500mg/5mL) × V₁ = (250mg) × (final volume). V₁ = 2.5mL of the 500mg/5mL suspension. Can dilute further with liquid for smaller doses.

![Dilution calculation example showing serial dilution steps](../assets/svgs/pharmacy-compounding-1.svg)

### Solution Mixing

For medication solutions that need dilution: use sterile water (or normal saline for injection solutions). Mix in sterile container using sterile technique. For liquids requiring compounding (flavor, preservation): use clean containers, measure accurately, mix thoroughly. Label immediately with contents, concentration, date prepared.

### Raw Material Sourcing (Post-Collapse)

**Pharmaceutical powders:** Pre-purchased bulk powders (amoxicillin, acetaminophen, ibuprofen, aspirin, etc.). Store in cool, dry place. Shelf life 5-10 years if properly sealed. Sources: online pharmaceutical suppliers (purchase pre-collapse), salvaged from commercial stockpiles, or local synthesis (for simple drugs like aspirin).

**Scale substitution:** No gram scale? Use balance scale (equal-arm balance) with known weights. Can calibrate using water (1mL water ≈ 1g). Improvised weights: coin (penny ≈ 2.5g), grain of rice (~20mg), or make stone weights and calibrate against known items.

## Measurements and Calculations

### Metric System (Standard)

<table><thead><tr><th scope="row">Unit</th><th scope="row">Abbreviation</th><th scope="row">Equivalent</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Kilogram</td><td>kg</td><td>1000g</td></tr><tr><td>Gram</td><td>g</td><td>1000mg</td></tr><tr><td>Milligram</td><td>mg</td><td>1000mcg (or µg)</td></tr><tr><td>Microgram</td><td>mcg</td><td>0.001mg</td></tr><tr><td>Liter</td><td>L</td><td>1000mL</td></tr><tr><td>Milliliter</td><td>mL</td><td>1mL (1 cubic cm)</td></tr></tbody></table>

### Volume Measurement

Syringes (1mL, 3mL, 5mL, 10mL common): mark in mL or sometimes in minims (older). For accuracy: use smallest syringe that accommodates dose (1mL syringe more accurate for small volumes than 10mL). Spoons: unreliable (vary in size); use measuring spoons (teaspoon = 5mL, tablespoon = 15mL) if syringes unavailable.

### Concentration Calculations

Percent solutions: % w/w (weight/weight) = grams solute/100g total. % w/v (weight/volume) = grams solute/100mL total. % v/v (volume/volume) = mL solute/100mL total. Example: 5% solution means 5g in 100mL for w/v, or 5mL in 100mL for v/v.

### Dose Calculation Example

Patient weighs 70kg, needs amoxicillin 25mg/kg. Total dose = 25mg/kg × 70kg = 1750mg. Divided into 3 doses = 583mg per dose (or round to 600mg). If have 500mg tablets = give 1.2 tablets (or 1 tablet + 1/5 tablet by crushing and dividing).

<section id="solutions">

## Solutions and Suspensions

### Saline Solution (for injection/irrigation)

Sterile normal saline: 0.9% sodium chloride (9g NaCl in 1L sterile water). For: IV fluids, wound irrigation, inhalation. Preparation: dissolve 9g NaCl in 1L sterile water, autoclave or filter-sterilize. In survival: boil and cool water, dissolve salt (ratio: 1/2 teaspoon salt per 8 oz water approximates 0.85%, acceptable alternative).

### Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS)

Critical for diarrheal illness. WHO formula: 1L water + 6 teaspoons sugar + 1/2 teaspoon salt (sodium chloride) + 1/2 teaspoon potassium chloride (if available, otherwise omit). Mix thoroughly. Can substitute: coconut water (natural electrolytes), broth with added sugar/salt. Administer: sips frequently (small amounts tolerated better than large volumes). Continue until diarrhea stops.

This is the canonical ORS recipe block for the nutrition family. Mirror any wording changes here rather than duplicating ORS wording elsewhere.

### Suspension Stability

Particles settle over time; requires shaking before each use. Shelf life reduced compared to solutions (particles may cake, become difficult to resuspend). Preservatives added (in commercial preparations) prevent microbial growth; without preservatives, must use within hours (room temperature) or days (refrigerated). Document date prepared, expiration time.

<section id="quality">

## Quality Control

### Visual Inspection

Before administering any compounded preparation: examine for color changes (indicate degradation), precipitate/cloudiness in solutions (contamination/degradation), crystal formation (salt out of solution), mold/growth on surface (contamination). If any abnormality: discard, do not use.

### Clarity and Particulates

Solutions should be clear. Suspensions cloudy (expected). Look against light for particles (especially for injectable solutions—particles can cause emboli). Filter through clean cloth if particles present and not suspension-type. For injectable: use 0.22 micron sterile filter if available.

### pH Testing (if available)

Some medications require specific pH for stability. pH paper (strips, meters) can assess. Extreme pH (very acidic/alkaline) indicates degradation/contamination. Most injectable solutions neutral (pH 6.5-7.5); if significantly different, suspect degradation.

### Microbial Sampling

In normal practice: cultures on appropriate media confirm sterility. In survival: impossible to perform. Preventive: strict aseptic technique, use sterile components, proper storage. Assume: anything prepared without aseptic technique may be contaminated; use only for serious conditions when benefit outweighs risk.

### Refrigeration in Survival

Limited access to refrigeration complicates stability. Mitigations: prepare small quantities (for use within 8-12 hours), use non-refrigerated formulations when possible, consider root cellars/underground storage (cooler, more stable temperature than above ground). Evaporative cooling (water-soaked cloth, clay pot) can reduce temperature 5-15°F if no mechanical refrigeration.

<section id="scaling">

## Scaling Guidelines

### Individual vs Community Production

**Individual scale (household):** 10-50 doses prepared at once. Equipment: mortar/pestle, measuring spoons, basic scale. Time: 30 minutes to 2 hours. Cost: minimal. Quality: adequate for family use.

**Community scale (50-500 people):** 500-5000 doses produced. Equipment: proper pharmaceutical scale, mortar and pestle sets, bottling/labeling stations. Time: 4-8 hours per batch. Cost: moderate. Quality: must meet hygiene/accuracy standards for public health credibility.

**Regional scale (500+ people):** Large batches (5000-50000 doses). Equipment: mechanical mill for grinding, multiple mortar/pestle sets, bottling line, quality testing lab. Time: 1-2 days per batch. Cost: significant. Quality: must match commercial standards.

### Batch Production Record Keeping

Essential for any scale: record ingredients (source, batch number, expiration), dates, compounding technician, amount produced, testing results, storage conditions. Enables: (1) traceability if problems occur, (2) recall capability, (3) quality assurance documentation, (4) training new staff. Simple notebook adequate; spreadsheet better for larger operations.

<section id="safety">

## Safety Warnings

:::warning
### Critical Compounding Safety

-   Calculation errors = dose errors causing toxicity/inefficacy. Double-check all calculations; verify against patient weight/age.
-   Contaminated preparation = introduces infection risk. Aseptic technique essential; discard if contamination suspected.
-   Drug stability compromise = reduced effectiveness or toxicity. Don't use expired/discolored preparations; prepare fresh as possible.
-   Allergen/interaction oversight = serious adverse reaction risk. Obtain full medication history, check for interactions before administering.
-   Measurement errors = incorrect dose. Use calibrated equipment (syringes, graduated cylinders); avoid guessing volumes.
-   Undocumented preparations = no traceability if adverse event occurs. Label EVERY compounded dose with drug name, concentration, date, expiration time.
:::

:::tip
### Best Practices for Accuracy

-   Always work in clean, organized space (reduces errors)
-   Use smallest syringe/measuring device suitable for volume (better accuracy)
-   Double-check calculations before and after preparation (prevents math errors)
-   Have second person verify measurement (reduces single-operator errors)
-   Document everything in real-time (not from memory later)
-   Prepare only what will be used within stability window (reduces waste)
:::

### Common Compounding Errors

-   **Using wrong liquid:** Some drugs require specific diluents (saline vs water, sterile vs boiled); confirm before mixing.
-   **Insufficient mixing:** Suspensions/solutions must be thoroughly mixed; inadequate mixing causes uneven dosing.
-   **Confusing concentration:** 5% not same as 5mg/mL; verify units before calculation.
-   **Crushed sustained-release:** Destroys mechanism, causes rapid absorption = toxicity risk. Only crush immediate-release tablets.
-   **Exceeding stability window:** Using preparation beyond expiration (especially injectable/suspension) risks infection/toxicity.
