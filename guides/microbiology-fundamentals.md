Microorganisms—bacteria, fungi, viruses, and parasites—are invisible to the naked eye yet profoundly affect human health, food safety, soil fertility, and survival. This guide covers the fundamental classification, identification, practical testing, and applications of microbes in low-resource settings.

![Microbiology Fundamentals Overview](../assets/svgs/microbiology-fundamentals-2.svg)

<section id="microbial-world-overview">

## Microbial World Overview

**The four kingdoms of microorganisms:**

1. **Bacteria:** Single-celled prokaryotes (no nucleus); typically 0.5–5 micrometers (µm); reproduce by binary fission (splitting). Most are harmless or beneficial; some cause disease.
2. **Viruses:** Acellular; much smaller (0.02–0.3 µm); require host cells to reproduce; cause infection by hijacking cellular machinery.
3. **Fungi:** Eukaryotes (with nucleus); typically 5–100 µm; include molds (filamentous) and yeasts (round cells); reproduce by spores or budding. Essential for decomposition; some produce toxins.
4. **Parasites:** Eukaryotic organisms visible to the naked eye or barely visible under low magnification; include protozoa (single-celled, 10–100 µm) and helminths (worms, 1–30 cm).

**Size comparison:**
- Red blood cell: ~7 µm (visible under microscope)
- Typical bacterium: 1–5 µm (requires microscope at ×400 magnification)
- Virus: 0.05–0.3 µm (requires electron microscope for visualization)
- Parasite cyst (e.g., *Giardia*): 10–14 µm (visible under ×100 microscope)

**Understanding microbial ecology—how different organisms compete, cooperate, and thrive under specific conditions—is the foundation of practical survival microbiology.**

<section id="bacteria-classification-identification">

## Bacteria Classification & Identification

Bacteria are classified by shape, staining properties, and function.

### Shape

- **Coccus (cocci):** Spherical cells. Examples: *Staphylococcus aureus*, *Streptococcus pyogenes*.
- **Bacillus (bacilli):** Rod-shaped cells. Examples: *Escherichia coli*, *Bacillus anthracis*.
- **Spirillum (spirilla):** Spiral/helical cells. Examples: *Vibrio cholerae*, *Spirillum volutans*.
- **Vibrio:** Curved rod. Example: *Vibrio cholerae*.

### Gram Staining Classification

**Principle:** Gram staining differentiates bacteria into two groups based on cell wall composition.

**Gram-positive bacteria:** Thick peptidoglycan layer; retain crystal violet (stain purple).
- Examples: *Staphylococcus*, *Streptococcus*, *Clostridium*.
- Generally less dangerous; more susceptible to antibiotics.

**Gram-negative bacteria:** Thin peptidoglycan layer with outer lipid membrane; do not retain crystal violet (stain pink/red with safranin counterstain).
- Examples: *Escherichia coli*, *Vibrio cholerae*, *Salmonella*.
- Often more dangerous; more resistant to antibiotics.

### Other Bacterial Tests

**Acid-fast staining** (for *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*, *Mycobacterium leprae*):
- Uses carbol fuchsin dye and heat.
- Acid-fast bacteria appear red; others are blue (methylene blue background).
- Complex waxy cell wall retains the dye.

**Oxygen preference:**
- **Aerobic:** Require oxygen; grow on the surface of culture plates.
- **Anaerobic:** Cannot tolerate oxygen; grow only in the absence of oxygen.
- **Facultative:** Can grow with or without oxygen.

### Beneficial Bacteria

- *Lactobacillus* species — Produce lactic acid in fermentation; preserve foods and enhance gut health.
- *Bacillus subtilis* — Soil-dwelling; helps decompose organic matter and fix nitrogen.
- *Acetobacter* — Convert ethanol to acetic acid (vinegar fermentation).
- Cyanobacteria — Photosynthetic; fix atmospheric nitrogen in rice paddies and wetlands.

### Pathogenic Bacteria (Primary Concern)

- *Vibrio cholerae* — Waterborne; causes acute diarrheal disease.
- *Salmonella* species — Foodborne; contaminates poultry, eggs, and unpasteurized dairy.
- *Escherichia coli* (pathogenic strains) — Fecal indicator; O157:H7 causes hemorrhagic colitis.
- *Clostridium botulinum* — Anaerobic; produces deadly botulotoxin in improperly canned foods.

:::warning
**Critical:** *Clostridium botulinum* produces spores that survive boiling. Safe preservation requires either pressure canning (121°C, 100 kPa for 20 minutes), acidification to pH <3.8, or salt/sugar curing to reduce water activity below 0.85. Never rely on water-bath canning for low-acid foods.
:::

### Bacterial Growth Requirements

All bacteria require:
1. **Carbon source** — Organic matter (glucose, amino acids, etc.)
2. **Nitrogen source** — Amino acids, proteins, or inorganic nitrates
3. **Minerals** — Phosphorus, sulfur, potassium, iron, magnesium
4. **Appropriate pH** — Most thrive at pH 6–8; some tolerate extremes
5. **Moisture** — Cannot divide below water activity (Aw) ~0.85
6. **Temperature** — Mesophiles (20–45°C), thermophiles (45–70°C), psychrophiles (<20°C)
7. **Oxygen** — Aerobes require O₂; anaerobes do not; facultative anaerobes tolerate both

In survival contexts, controlling these conditions preserves food (removing water, lowering pH, reducing oxygen) and prevents unwanted bacterial growth.

## Gram Staining: Standard & Field Methods

### Standard Gram Staining Procedure

1. **Prepare a smear:** Spread a loop of bacteria-containing liquid thinly on a glass slide.
2. **Heat fix:** Pass the slide through a flame 3–5 times to kill and fix bacteria.
3. **Crystal violet stain:** Apply for 1 minute; rinse gently with water.
4. **Gram's iodine:** Apply for 1 minute (acts as mordant); rinse.
5. **Alcohol rinse:** Apply acetone or 70% alcohol for 30 seconds; rinse immediately.
6. **Safranin counterstain:** Apply for 30 seconds; rinse and blot dry.
7. **Examine:** Under ×1000 magnification (oil immersion), Gram-positive appear purple; Gram-negative appear pink.

### Improvised Gram Staining (Field Method)

**Gram staining with kitchen substitutes:**

1. **Specimen preparation:** Smear bacteria on a clean glass slide; air-dry and gently heat-fix over a flame.
2. **Crystal violet (primary stain):** Apply for 1 minute. **Substitute:** Food coloring or ink diluted 1:10 with water.
3. **Gram's iodine (mordant):** Apply for 1 minute to bind the stain. **Substitute:** Weak iodine solution (Lugol's iodine if available).
4. **Alcohol/acetone (decolorizer):** Rinse briefly. **Substitute:** Ethanol (70%) or dilute acetic acid.
5. **Safranin (counterstain):** Apply for 1 minute. **Substitute:** Dilute methylene blue or cheap red pen ink diluted 1:100.
6. **Observation:** Gram-positive cells retain purple; Gram-negative cells appear pink/red.

**Alternative staining indicators:**
- **Methylene blue (simple stain):** Stains all bacteria blue; easier than Gram but provides less information.
- **Malachite green (spore stain):** Heat the stain to penetrate spore coats, then counterstain with safranin.

<section id="culture-media-lab-improvised">

## Culture Media: Lab & Improvised

Growing microorganisms in culture allows identification, testing, and preservation.

### Lab Culture Media Preparation

**Nutrient agar (general-purpose bacterial medium):**
- Ingredients: Beef extract (3 g), peptone (5 g), sodium chloride (5 g), agar (15 g), distilled water (1 liter).
- **Beef extract:** Source of vitamins, minerals.
- **Peptone:** Nitrogen source.
- **NaCl:** Maintains osmotic balance.
- **Agar:** Gel that solidifies at 37°C, allowing colonial growth.

**Preparation:**
1. Dissolve all ingredients in 1 liter distilled water.
2. Heat to 121°C (250°F) at pressure (15 psi) for 15 minutes to sterilize.
3. Cool to ~50°C; pour into sterile petri dishes (~20 mL per dish).
4. Allow to solidify at room temperature (~1 hour); plates are ready for inoculation.

**Potato dextrose agar (for fungi):**
- Boil 200g potatoes, filter; add 20g sugar, 15g agar, 1L water.
- Sterilize as above.

**Broth culture (liquid medium):**
- Same ingredients as agar but omit agar. Useful for growing large quantities; lacks the solid surface for colonial observation.

### Improvised Kitchen Culture Media

**Nutrient broth (liquid medium):**
- 5 g beef bouillon powder (or gelatin-free stock)
- 2.5 g sodium chloride (table salt)
- 1 L water
- pH should reach ~6.8 (neutral)

**Method:** Dissolve ingredients in water, heat gently, cool, and inoculate. Cultures grow turbid within 24–48 hours at room temperature.

**Nutrient agar (solid medium from kitchen ingredients):**
- Nutrient broth (above)
- 15 g gelatin or agar powder (if available; agar is superior)
- pH ~6.8

**Method:** Dissolve agar in hot water, cool to ~50°C, pour into sterilized containers (boiled petri dishes or small jars), and allow to set. Inoculate with sterile loop or needle. Colonies appear within 24–72 hours.

:::warning
**Gelatin vs. agar:** Gelatin melts at body temperature and is ideal only for room-temperature incubation. Agar has a much higher melting point (82°C) and is superior for all applications. If neither is available, use cornstarch suspension for very crude colony observation.
:::

**Fermentation media (for yeasts and LAB):**
- 50 g sugar (glucose or honey)
- 5 g peptone or yeast extract
- 1 L water
- Inoculate with fruit juice, whey, or prior culture
- Incubate anaerobically (sealed container) at room temperature; observe foam/bubbles within 24 hours

**High-salt media (for salt-tolerant bacteria):**
- Add 100–300 g NaCl per liter to nutrient broth
- Used for fermenting vegetables and meat

### Streak Plating (Isolation Method)

To isolate a pure culture from a mixed sample:

1. **Sterilize a wire loop:** Heat over a flame until glowing red; cool for 10 seconds in the air (do not touch anything).
2. **Load the loop:** Touch the loop to the sample (liquid or colony).
3. **Streak the plate:** Drag the loop across the agar surface in a zig-zag pattern, covering 1/3 of the plate.
4. **Re-sterilize the loop:** Flame again; cool briefly.
5. **Streak from the first region:** Drag the loop through the edge of the first streak into the next 1/3 of the plate.
6. **Repeat:** Flame, cool, and streak from the second region into the final 1/3.
7. **Incubate:** Place plate upside down (agar on top, condensation on lid) at 37°C for 24–48 hours.
8. **Observe:** Isolated colonies should appear in the final streak region; each colony is a pure culture from a single cell.

<section id="pathogenic-organisms">

## Pathogenic Organisms

### Bacteria That Pose Critical Health Risks

**Foodborne pathogens:**

| Pathogen | Source | Symptoms | Prevention |
|----------|--------|----------|-----------|
| *Salmonella* | Poultry, eggs, dairy | Diarrhea, fever (1–3 days) | Cook to safe temp; store cold |
| *Clostridium botulinum* | Improperly canned foods | Paralysis, respiratory failure | Pressure-can low-acid foods |
| *Listeria monocytogenes* | Soft cheeses, deli meats | Fever, neurological symptoms | Avoid high-risk foods when pregnant |
| *Campylobacter* | Poultry, contaminated water | Diarrhea, cramping | Cook poultry thoroughly; treat water |

### Waterborne Parasites

**Giardia lamblia:**
- Size: 10–14 µm (visible under ×100 microscope)
- Causes giardiasis (chronic diarrhea, malabsorption)
- Waterborne; cysts survive chlorination
- Require 1 micrometer filtration or 1-minute boiling for inactivation

**Cryptosporidium:**
- Waterborne; causes severe watery diarrhea
- Highly resistant to chemical disinfection
- Requires 0.5 micrometer filtration or boiling
- Oocysts may require vigorous rolling boil for full inactivation

**Entamoeba histolytica:**
- Fecal-oral transmission
- Causes dysentery
- Eliminated by boiling and proper sanitation

:::warning
**Boiling effectiveness:** Boiling for 1 minute at sea level kills most pathogens including bacteria and viruses. At high elevation (>2000m), extend to 3 minutes. Cryptosporidium oocysts may require vigorous rolling boil for full inactivation.
:::

### Protozoa and Parasites Summary

**Protozoa (single-celled parasites):**
- Size: 10–50 micrometers
- Examples: *Giardia lamblia* (giardiasis), *Entamoeba histolytica* (amoebic dysentery), *Plasmodium* (malaria)
- Identification: Microscopic examination of stool, blood, or body fluids

**Helminths (parasitic worms):**
- Visible to naked eye (1–30 cm) or under microscope
- Examples: Roundworms, tapeworms, hookworms, schistosomes
- Identification: Eggs visible in stool under microscope; adult worms sometimes in vomit or feces

**Field identification of parasites:**
1. **Stool examination:** Fix a small sample (pea-sized) in 10% formalin. Centrifuge and examine under microscope at ×100–400.
2. **Blood smear:** For malaria—take a drop of blood, spread thinly, dry, stain with Giemsa, and examine for parasite-infected red blood cells.
3. **Visual inspection:** Large tapeworm segments in stool are visible to naked eye.

## Beneficial Microbes: Fermentation & Applications

Fermentation leverages beneficial microbes to preserve food, enhance nutrition, and produce flavor.

### Lactic Acid Fermentation

*Lactobacillus* species produce lactic acid, lowering pH below 3.8 and inhibiting pathogens.

**Mechanism:**
1. Salt (typically 2–5% by weight) creates osmotic stress, inhibiting pathogens but allowing LAB to thrive.
2. LAB metabolize sugars to lactic acid, dropping pH over 1–3 weeks.
3. Low pH preserves the product and inhibits spoilage.

**Common ferments:**
- Sauerkraut (cabbage + 2% salt)
- Kimchi (vegetables + 3% salt + spices)
- Pickles (cucumbers + 5% brine)
- Miso (soybeans + salt + koji mold, fermented 6–12 months)

**Conditions:**
- Temperature: 15–25°C ideal; slower at cooler temps, risk of contamination if too warm.
- Anaerobic: Use weighted lids or airlocks to prevent mold.
- Duration: 1–4 weeks for vegetables, months for miso/tempeh.

### Alcoholic Fermentation

*Saccharomyces cerevisiae* ferments sugars to ethanol and CO₂.

**Mechanism:**
1. Yeast consumes glucose under anaerobic conditions.
2. Produces ethanol (~12–15% ABV as yeast tolerance limit) and CO₂.
3. High alcohol inhibits most bacteria, preserving the product.

**Applications:**
- Wine (fruit juice + yeast, 2–4 weeks)
- Beer (grain mash + hops + yeast, 2–4 weeks)
- Cider (apple juice + yeast, 3–6 weeks)
- Distilled spirits (fermented liquid + heating)

**Starter culture:** Dried yeast, whey, or a previous successful ferment inoculates a new batch. Ensure the inoculum is from a known safe source.

:::info-box
**Fermentation as preservation:** Products like sauerkraut, kimchi, and fermented beverages are shelf-stable without refrigeration if properly sealed, and improve in flavor over months. Fermentation also increases bioavailable nutrients (B vitamins, amino acids) and population of beneficial bacteria.
:::

### Probiotic Foods and Gut Health

Probiotic foods contain live beneficial bacteria, primarily *Lactobacillus* and *Bifidobacterium* species, which colonize the gut and support digestion, immune function, and nutrient absorption.

**Common probiotic foods:**
- **Sauerkraut and kimchi:** Fermented vegetables rich in LAB; stable at room temperature if kept anaerobic.
- **Yogurt and kefir:** Fermented milk; requires dairy and starter culture or prior batch.
- **Miso paste:** Fermented soybean; adds umami flavor and probiotics to soups.
- **Tempeh:** Fermented soybean cake; higher protein than tofu, easier to digest.
- **Kombucha:** Fermented tea; requires a SCOBY (symbiotic culture of bacteria and yeast) and careful technique to avoid contamination.

**Cultivation and storage:**
- **Maintain diversity:** Consume multiple fermented foods to ensure varied beneficial bacteria strains.
- **Store cool if possible:** Refrigeration slows fermentation but extends shelf life; room-temperature storage is acceptable if the product is properly sealed and anaerobic.
- **Avoid heat:** Cooking fermented foods kills the bacteria; consume raw or add after cooking.

:::info-box
**Gut dysbiosis recovery:** In scenarios with limited food variety, fermented foods help restore a healthy microbiota after antibiotic use or diarrheal disease. Offer gradual introduction to avoid digestive upset.
:::

### Fungi for Food Production

Fungi are eukaryotic decomposers essential for nutrient cycling. They break down dead wood, plant matter, and other organisms, returning nutrients to soil.

**Yeasts** — Single-celled fungi; *Saccharomyces cerevisiae* ferments sugars to alcohol and CO₂.
- Used for bread leavening, beer/wine production, and distillation.
- Anaerobic fermentation produces alcohol; aerobic growth produces more biomass.

**Molds** — Filamentous fungi with visible mycelium (thread-like networks).
- *Aspergillus niger* — Used for citric acid production in fermentation.
- *Penicillium roqueforti* — Blue-veining in cheese; produces penicillin-class antibiotics.
- *Rhizopus* — Rapid decomposer; useful for tempeh (soybean cake) fermentation.

**Mushrooms** — Macroscopic fruiting bodies of fungal mycelium.
- Some are edible and nutritious; many are highly toxic.
- Cultivation requires sterile spawn, proper humidity (85–95%), and temperature control.

:::tip
**Fungal food production:** Grow mushrooms on straw, logs, or grain substrate. Most species fruit within 4–12 weeks at room temperature. Oyster mushrooms (*Pleurotus* species) are the most forgiving and tolerate poor conditions and variable temperatures.
:::

### Soil Microbiology and Agriculture

Soil teems with microorganisms—bacteria, fungi, protozoa, nematodes—that govern nutrient cycling, plant health, and carbon sequestration.

**Key soil processes:**

**Nitrogen fixation:** Cyanobacteria and *Rhizobium* bacteria in legume root nodules convert atmospheric nitrogen (N₂) to bioavailable ammonia (NH₃). Grow legumes (beans, clover, alfalfa) and leave residue to enrich soil.

**Decomposition:** Fungi and bacteria break down dead plant/animal matter, releasing nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium. A healthy soil with organic matter supports robust microbial populations.

**Mycorrhizal networks:** Fungal partners colonize plant roots, extending the plant's nutrient uptake in exchange for plant sugars. Avoid fungicides and excessive tilling, which disrupt these networks.

**Building soil health:**
- **Add compost:** Microbe-rich compost inoculates soil with beneficial organisms.
- **Minimize tillage:** Preserve soil structure and fungal networks.
- **Grow cover crops:** Legumes (clover, vetch) and grasses (rye) add organic matter and fix nitrogen.
- **Mulch:** Organic mulch (straw, leaves) feeds decomposers and conserves moisture.
- **Avoid pesticides/fungicides:** Broad-spectrum chemicals eliminate beneficial microbes; use targeted approaches if needed.

:::tip
**Quick compost:** Layer green materials (grass, food scraps) and brown materials (straw, leaves) in a bin. Maintain moisture and turn weekly; decomposition completes in 4–8 weeks with active microbial colonization.
:::

## Mycotoxin Safety

Certain molds produce mycotoxins—carcinogenic or neurotoxic compounds—if contamination occurs during storage.

**Common mycotoxins and sources:**

- **Aflatoxins** — Produced by *Aspergillus flavus* on peanuts, grains, and nuts in humid conditions. Highly carcinogenic; associated with liver cancer.
- **Ochratoxin** — Found in moldy coffee, grains, and dried fruits. Nephrotoxic and potentially carcinogenic.
- **Ergot** — Produced by *Claviceps* fungus on rye and wheat. Contains ergotamine (vasoconstrictor); causes ergotism (gangrenous or convulsive). Historical cause of "St. Anthony's Fire."
- **Trichothecenes** — Produced by *Fusarium* species on wheat and barley. Immunosuppressive and potentially teratogenic.

**Prevention strategies:**
- Store grains in airtight containers with moisture content <13%
- Ensure good air circulation
- Discard visibly moldy food
- Monitor for musty odors
- Inspect nuts and grains before consumption
- In high-humidity areas, use desiccants (salt, silica gel) in grain storage

:::danger
**Do not attempt to salvage moldy foods** by cutting off the visible mold. Mycotoxins often permeate the surrounding tissue. Discard entire affected portions.
:::

## Water Testing Methodology

### Visual and Sensory Assessment

Inspect water for:
- **Turbidity (cloudiness):** Indicates suspended particles and potential pathogens.
- **Color:** Brown/yellow often indicates organic matter; clear does not guarantee safety.
- **Odor:** Musty smell suggests algal blooms or decaying organic matter.
- **Taste:** Do not taste untreated water; any off-flavor suggests contamination.

### Simple Turbidity Test

Fill a clear glass with water and hold it over a white background. You should be able to read text through it. If not, filter before treating.

### Total Coliform Count (Most Probable Number, MPN)

Coliforms are Gram-negative bacteria found in the intestines of warm-blooded animals. Their presence in water indicates fecal contamination.

**Field method:**
1. **Prepare serial dilutions:** From 100 mL water, dilute to 10⁻¹, 10⁻², 10⁻³, etc.
2. **Inoculate media:** Add 1 mL of each dilution to tubes of lactose broth.
3. **Incubate:** 35°C for 24–48 hours.
4. **Observe:** Gas production (bubbles in inverted tubes) indicates coliform fermentation.
5. **Count positives:** Determine MPN from the number of positive tubes at each dilution.
6. **Result:** Safe drinking water should have 0 CFU/100 mL.

### Parasite Testing (Stool Examination)

1. **Collect sample:** Fresh stool (~5 grams); place in a container with 10% formalin as preservative.
2. **Prepare a smear:** Mix a small portion with water on a glass slide.
3. **Examine under microscope** (×100–400 magnification):
   - **Giardia cysts:** Oval, 10–14 µm, distinctive "falling leaf" motility if fresh.
   - **Roundworm eggs:** Oval, 40–60 µm, with smooth shell.
   - **Hookworm eggs:** Similar to roundworm but smaller (40–50 µm).
   - **Tapeworm proglottids/eggs:** Segments visible in stool; eggs are round, 40–50 µm.

### Field Indicators and Water Treatment

**Indicator organisms:**
- *Escherichia coli* presence indicates fecal contamination and suggests presence of pathogenic bacteria and viruses.
- Coliforms (broader group) include *E. coli* and related species; presence indicates recent or remote contamination.

**Improvised testing (no equipment):**
- Boil all water used for drinking and cooking.
- Use 0.1 micrometer ceramic or sand filters if available; clean by back-flushing or boiling.
- UV exposure (direct sunlight for 6 hours in clear bottles, or dedicated UV lamps) inactivates bacteria and viruses but does not remove organic matter.

:::warning
**Multiple barriers:** Combine filtration (removes protozoa, some bacteria), chemical treatment (iodine kills bacteria/viruses, bleach is broad-spectrum), and boiling (final safety step) for maximum safety. A single method alone is insufficient for water of unknown origin.
:::

## Virus Transmission & Prevention

### Virus Structure and Characteristics

Viruses are obligate intracellular parasites—bundles of genetic material (DNA or RNA) surrounded by protein coats. They cannot be cultured on standard media and require living host cells to replicate.

- **Size:** 0.02–0.3 micrometers (invisible to standard microscopy; require electron microscope)
- **Composition:** Genetic material (RNA or DNA) + protein coat (+ lipid envelope in some species)
- **Reproduction:** Cannot reproduce independently; hijack host cell machinery
- **Field identification:** Viruses cause specific disease patterns (symptoms) and can be tested via serological tests (blood tests detecting antibodies) or antigen tests (rapid detection in saliva, nasal secretions)
- **Common viruses:** Influenza, measles, chickenpox, polio, hepatitis, COVID-19

### Transmission Routes and Prevention

**Respiratory (airborne):**
- Examples: SARS-CoV-2, influenza, measles, tuberculosis
- Spreads via aerosol droplets during coughing/sneezing
- **Prevention:** Ventilation, hand hygiene, respiratory isolation, vaccination (if available)

**Fecal-oral:**
- Examples: Rotavirus, norovirus, hepatitis A, enteroviruses
- Spreads through contaminated food/water
- **Prevention:** Sanitation, handwashing, water treatment

**Bloodborne:**
- Examples: HIV, hepatitis B and C
- Spreads via contact with infected blood or body fluids
- **Prevention:** Blood safety protocols, needle hygiene

**Vector-borne:**
- Examples: Dengue, yellow fever, Zika (mosquitoes); plague (fleas)
- **Prevention:** Insect control, protective clothing

### Viral Control Strategies

Viruses are sensitive to:
- **Heat** — Most inactivated by 56°C for 30 minutes or 70°C for 5 minutes
- **UV light** — 254 nm wavelength damages RNA/DNA; requires direct exposure
- **Chemical disinfectants** — 70% ethanol, bleach (0.5%), iodine (2%) are effective
- **pH extremes** — Most enveloped viruses (influenza, corona) inactivated at pH <3 or >11

Unlike bacteria, viruses do not grow on culture media; diagnosis and monitoring rely on clinical observation or, in better-equipped settings, serology or PCR.

<section id="sanitation-sterilization">

## Sanitation and Sterilization

In low-resource settings, preventing contamination is more practical than eliminating established infection.

### Heat Sterilization

- **Boiling (100°C):** Kills vegetative bacteria and viruses within 1 minute; requires 10 minutes for protozoan cysts and higher at altitude.
- **Pressure cooking (121°C, 100 kPa):** Destroys bacterial spores; essential for canning and sterilizing equipment. Time depends on volume and density.
- **Dry heat (160°C for 2 hours):** Sterilizes metal instruments and glass; requires an oven or controlled flame.
- **Flaming:** Hold metal loops/needles in a flame until red-hot; kills all microorganisms but does not sterilize liquids.

### Chemical Disinfection

- **Bleach solution (0.5% sodium hypochlorite):** Mix 1 part household bleach (~5% chlorine) with 10 parts water. Wipe or soak for 10 minutes. Effective for surfaces, not ideal for large water volumes (can cause off-flavors).
- **Iodine (2% Lugol's iodine):** Add 8–10 drops per liter of water; wait 30 minutes. Effective against most pathogens except Cryptosporidium.
- **Vinegar (5% acetic acid):** Spray or soak; slower than bleach but safer for food contact.
- **Alcohol (70%):** Excellent for hand and equipment disinfection; evaporates quickly.

:::tip
**Hand hygiene protocol:** Wash hands with soap and water (friction for 20 seconds removes >99% of transient bacteria), then dry with clean cloth or air. In resource-scarcity, rinse with water and wipe on clean cloth before food handling. Alcohol-based hand sanitizer works if soap is unavailable.
:::

### Environmental Disinfection

- Sweep or wipe surfaces with cloth dampened in disinfectant.
- Focus on high-touch areas: door handles, food-prep surfaces, eating utensils.
- Allow disinfectant to air-dry; do not rinse afterward (allows re-contamination).

### Infection Control Principles

**Hand hygiene:**
- Wash with soap and water for at least 20 seconds.
- Alcohol-based sanitizer if water unavailable (requires 60%+ alcohol).
- Critical times: Before eating, after toileting, after handling sick person.

**Food safety:**
- Cook thoroughly to internal temperature (71°C / 160°F for meat).
- Store at <4°C (refrigerate) or >65°C (hot hold).
- Avoid cross-contamination (use separate cutting boards for meat and vegetables).

**Isolation of infected persons:**
- Separate from healthy individuals when possible.
- Use separate eating/drinking utensils.
- Restrict contact with vulnerable people (elderly, infants, immunocompromised).

## Improvised Microscopy

For cultures without access to a commercial microscope, observation is possible using improvised tools.

### Water-Drop Lens

A simple water droplet can function as a lens, magnifying ~3–10×.

**Construction:**
1. Cut or punch a 2–3 mm hole in a piece of black cardstock or thin plastic.
2. Place a water droplet on one side of the hole, held in place by surface tension.
3. Position the specimen on the opposite side of the hole.
4. Hold up to light and observe through the droplet.

This provides enough magnification to spot larger microorganisms (protozoa, fungal spores) but not bacteria.

### Simple Water Droplet Microscope

1. Place a droplet of liquid sample on a glass slide.
2. Hold a drinking glass (or water-filled round bottle) above the droplet as a lens.
3. Adjust the distance until the image magnifies (approximately 10–20×).
4. Microscopic organisms may be visible as shadows or as mobile particles.

### Better Improvised Scope (Using a Magnifying Glass)

1. Mount a magnifying glass (~10× magnification) in a fixed position above a glass slide.
2. Use a light source (sunlight or lamp) positioned below.
3. Prepare a thin wet mount slide (sample + drop of water covered with a glass cover slip).
4. Observe through the lens.

**Limitations:** Low magnification (10–20×) allows viewing of large parasites and fungi but not bacteria or viruses.

### Salvaged Optics

If binoculars, magnifying glasses, or camera lenses are available, they can be repurposed:

- **Magnifying glass + eyepiece:** Stack a magnifying glass and a strong eyepiece (or even a cell-phone camera lens) to increase magnification.
- **Fresnel lens:** Salvaged from old LCD screens or overhead projectors; focus to create a strong lens.
- **Microscope objective repair:** If a broken microscope exists, salvage undamaged objective lenses and rebuild using improvised tube and eyepiece.

:::info-box
**Improvised stage lighting:** Use a white paper or aluminum foil reflector to redirect sunlight onto the specimen from below, or use a candle/oil lamp for transmitted light. Dark-field (backlighting) makes flagella and cilia visible on moving organisms.
:::

### Morphology Observation Without Staining

Even without staining, observe:
- **Shape:** Cocci (round), bacilli (rod), spirilla (spiral), vibrio (comma-shaped)
- **Arrangement:** Single, pairs (diplo-), chains (strepto-), clusters (staphylo-)
- **Motility:** Observe living cells under magnification; motile cells indicate flagella or pili
- **Size:** Estimate relative to known bacteria (~0.5–5 micrometers)

</section>

## Antibiotic Resistance and Long-Term Health

Antibiotic resistance emerges when bacteria survive antibiotic exposure and pass resistance genes to offspring. In a scenario with limited pharmaceutical supply, minimizing resistance is critical.

### Resistance Mechanisms

- **Enzymatic degradation:** Bacteria produce enzymes (e.g., beta-lactamase) that break down antibiotics.
- **Efflux pumps:** Membrane transporters expel the antibiotic faster than it accumulates.
- **Target alteration:** Mutations change the antibiotic's binding site, reducing efficacy.

### Prevention Strategies

1. **Use antibiotics sparingly:** Reserve for serious infections with clear bacterial etiology (not viral colds).
2. **Complete the course:** Even if symptoms resolve, finish prescribed antibiotics to prevent resistance selection.
3. **Source control:** Drain abscesses, remove foreign bodies, clean wounds—this reduces bacterial burden more than antibiotics alone.
4. **Sanitation:** Prevent infection through good hygiene, water treatment, and wound care.
5. **Local application:** Use antibiotic salves (neomycin, bacitracin) on minor wounds to minimize systemic use.

:::warning
**Antibiotic salves expire:** Homemade or salvaged antibiotics lose potency over time. Prioritize prevention and sanitation over reliance on old medications.
:::

### Antibiotic Sensitivity Testing (Kirby-Bauer Method)

Determines which antibiotics are effective against a bacterial isolate.

**Procedure:**
1. **Prepare a lawn:** Spread a pure bacterial culture thinly across an entire agar plate (creates a uniform surface).
2. **Place antibiotic discs:** Using sterile forceps, place antibiotic-impregnated paper discs on the lawn (do not press).
3. **Incubate:** 37°C for 24 hours.
4. **Measure zones:** Clear zones around discs indicate antibiotic sensitivity. Resistant bacteria grow all the way to the disc.
5. **Interpret:** Zone diameter determines sensitivity (varies by antibiotic and bacteria; see reference tables).

**Example:** If a tetracycline disc shows a 20 mm clear zone around it, the bacterium is sensitive to tetracycline.

</section>

<section id="spoilage-pathogens">

## Spoilage vs. Pathogenic Microbes

**Spoilage bacteria** (*Lactobacillus, Bacillus*) produce off-flavors and odors but usually are not pathogenic to healthy individuals. They indicate decay but not necessarily immediate danger.

**Pathogenic bacteria** (*Salmonella, Listeria*) often grow without visible spoilage, making them dangerous. Rely on proper cooking, canning, and refrigeration rather than sensory assessment.

### Safe Food Handling

1. **Cook:** Heat food to safe internal temperatures (74°C for poultry, 63°C for ground meat, 71°C for fish).
2. **Cool:** Refrigerate cooked food within 2 hours; store at <4°C.
3. **Separate:** Use different cutting boards for raw meat and vegetables to prevent cross-contamination.
4. **Store:** High water activity (>0.95) promotes bacterial growth; preserve via canning, drying, salting, fermentation, or freezing.
