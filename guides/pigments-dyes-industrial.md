:::danger
**Toxic Chemical Exposure**
Industrial pigment and dye production involves acutely toxic substances. Mineral pigments (lead, mercury, chromium, arsenic compounds) cause cumulative organ damage through inhalation and skin contact. Mordanting chemicals (chrome alum, copper sulfate, tin chloride) are poisonous and corrosive. Indigo vat dyeing produces alkaline solutions that cause chemical burns. Always work in well-ventilated areas, wear gloves, eye protection, and a respirator when handling dry pigments or mordants. Never dispose of dye baths into waterways. This guide is for educational reference — not a substitute for formal chemistry training and proper hazmat protocols.
:::

Pigments and dyes provide color to paints, fabrics, inks, and other materials. Understanding sources, properties, and application creates vibrant, lasting colors.

<section id="pigment-types">

## Pigment Types

### Mineral Pigments (Inorganic)

**Ochre (Iron Oxide)**
- Source: Clay minerals containing iron oxide (naturally occurring)
- Color: Yellow, red, brown (depends on oxidation state of iron)
- Preparation: Extract, roast (oxidizes and darkens color), grind fine
- Properties: Permanent, inexpensive, good covering power
- Uses: Paint, plaster, cosmetics, ceramics

**Umber (Iron and Manganese Oxide)**
- Source: Brown iron-manganese oxide clay
- Color: Dark brown, rich tone
- Preparation: Extract, roast (darkens), grind
- Properties: Very durable, darker than ochre
- Uses: Paint, stain, ceramics

**Malachite Green (Copper Carbonate)**
- Source: Copper ore (natural mineral) or chemical synthesis
- Color: Bright green
- Preparation: Crush, grind fine
- Properties: Vibrant but can fade in light
- Uses: Paint, cosmetics (historical)

**Iron Oxide Blacks**
- Source: Iron oxide, natural or synthetic
- Color: Deep black
- Properties: Very permanent, non-toxic
- Uses: Paint, ink, rubber

**Titanium White (Titanium Dioxide)**
- Source: Mineral rutile (TiO₂)
- Color: Pure white; excellent coverage
- Properties: Very permanent, completely opaque
- Uses: Modern paint (replaced lead white)

### Earth Pigments (Natural Mineral Deposits)

Raw ochres, umbers, siennas extracted and minimally processed.

**Raw vs Calcined:**
- Raw: Ground as-mined color (lighter)
- Calcined: Roasted to darken and intensify
- Calcining: Heat raw pigment to 200–400°C (oxidizes iron, darkens color)

**Advantages:** Completely natural, safe, permanent
**Disadvantages:** Color varies by deposit; limited range

<section id="plant-dyes">

## Plant-Based Dyes

### Indigo (Blue)

**Source:** Leaves of *Indigofera tinctoria* plant

**Historical extraction:**
1. Harvest leaves; ferment in water
2. Oxidize (stir, expose to air): Leaves become blue
3. Settle; skim blue precipitate
4. Dry to powder (indigo blue dye)

**Chemistry:**
- Indigo is insoluble in water (must reduce — add reducing agent)
- Reduced indigo dissolves (indigo white)
- Dye fabric in reduced solution
- Oxidize in air: Indigo re-precipitates in fiber, fixing color

**Properties:**
- Extremely fast (colorfast to light, washing, rubbing)
- Deep, rich blue color
- Used for centuries (blue jeans still use indigo)

### Madder (Red)

**Source:** Roots of *Rubia tinctorum* plant

**Extraction:**
1. Dig roots (3+ years old have most dye)
2. Dry completely
3. Grind to powder
4. Soak in hot water to extract dye
5. Add mordant (aluminum salt) to fix color

**Chemistry:**
- Main dye compound: Alizarin (red color)
- Requires mordant (mordant binds dye to fiber)
- With different mordants: Different colors (red, purple, brick)

**Properties:**
- Moderately fast (good colorfastness)
- Rich warm reds, purples, oranges depending on mordant
- Used historically for expensive textiles

### Weld (Yellow)

**Source:** *Reseda luteola* plant (leaves and flowering tops)

**Preparation:**
1. Harvest flowering tops
2. Dry thoroughly
3. Extract with hot water
4. Simmer fabric in dye bath

**Properties:**
- Produces golden yellow
- Fast to light (good permanence)
- Combined with indigo = green (blue + yellow)

### Woad (Blue, Less Intense than Indigo)

**Source:** *Isatis tinctoria* plant leaves

**Extraction:** Similar to indigo; historically older but less valued (weaker blue)

### Cochineal/Carmine (Red)

**Source:** Cochineal insect (not plant, but natural dye)

**Preparation:**
- Dry insects
- Boil in water; extract red pigment
- Extremely expensive; historically luxury dye

**Properties:**
- Brilliant red color
- Very fast (permanent)
- Used for finest textiles, cosmetics (still used)

<section id="mordants">

## Mordant Chemistry

Mordants are chemical compounds that bond dyes to fibers, fixing color.

### Common Mordants

**Alum (Aluminum Potassium Sulfate)**
- Most common, safest
- Increases dye uptake 30–40%
- With madder: Red colors
- Cost: Inexpensive, food-safe

**Iron (Ferrous Sulfate)**
- Darkens colors (saddens)
- Creates: Blacks, browns, greens
- Excess iron can weaken fiber (use sparingly)
- Cost: Inexpensive

**Copper (Copper Sulfate)**
- Shifts colors: Blues → greens, reds → browns
- Toxic (use carefully)
- Cost: Moderate; hazardous

**Tannin (from Gall Nuts, Pomegranate Rind)**
- Pre-mordant (apply before dye)
- Increases alum effectiveness
- Shifts colors toward browns
- Cost: Expensive if sourced; free if using plant scraps

**Washing Soda (Sodium Carbonate)**
- For direct dyes (indigo)
- Alkaline (helps dye dissolution)
- Cost: Very inexpensive

### Mordanting Process

<div class="dyeing-process">

**Standard alum mordanting (cotton/linen fabric):**
1. Fill pot with water; add alum (1 oz per pound fabric)
2. Dissolve alum; heat to 140–160°F (not boiling)
3. Add pre-wetted fabric (no air pockets)
4. Simmer 1 hour; stirring occasionally
5. Cool slowly; rest overnight
6. Rinse gently (remove excess alum)
7. Ready for dyeing

**After dyeing:**
- Can post-mordant with iron if darker color desired
- Or use tannin before alum for improved results

</div>

### Dye Chemistry (Simplified)

**Dye-fiber bonding:**
- Dye molecules have affinity for fiber (hydrogen bonding, van der Waals forces)
- Mordant creates "bridge" between dye and fiber
- Strengthens bond; increases colorfastness

**Example: Madder + Alum:**
- Alizarin (red dye) + Al³⁺ (mordant) + Fiber = Stable red color
- Without mordant: Dye washes out quickly
- With mordant: Color permanent to light, washing, rubbing

</section>

<section id="lake-pigments">

## Lake Pigments

Dyes precipitated onto inert substrate; combines dye color with pigment properties.

**Process:**
1. Dissolve natural dye in water
2. Add inert substrate (aluminum oxide, chalk, clay)
3. Add base (alkali) to precipitate dye onto substrate
4. Filter, dry, grind to fine powder

**Result:**
- Dye color on stable particle
- Pigment particle (suspended in paint, ink, etc.)
- Color and permanence of natural dye
- Handling/application of pigment

**Example: Madder Lake Red**
- Madder dye precipitated on aluminum oxide
- Brilliant red color
- Permanent to light (like original dye)
- Suspends easily in oil or water

**Advantages:**
- Extend expensive dyes (less dye needed)
- Combine dye color with pigment application
- Improve lightfastness of some dyes

</section>

<section id="paint-formulation">

## Paint Formulation with Pigments

### Basic Paint Composition

- **Pigment:** Color + hiding power (10–20% by weight typical)
- **Binder:** Holds pigment, hardens (oil, resin, water-based polymer)
- **Solvent:** Thins to paintable consistency (water, mineral spirits, etc.)
- **Additives:** Improve handling, drying, durability

### Pigment Selection for Paint

**Hiding power (opacity):**
- Titanium white: Excellent (opaque)
- Iron oxides: Good (opaque)
- Earth pigments: Good (opaque)
- Lake pigments: Fair to good (depends on density)
- Natural dyes (unsupported): Poor (transparent; tints, not covers)

**Lightfastness (fade resistance):**
- Mineral pigments (ochre, umber, oxides): Excellent
- Lake pigments from natural dyes: Good to very good (depends on dye)
- Modern synthetic pigments: Excellent

### Formulation Example (Oil Paint)

<div class="dyeing-process">

OIL PAINT RECIPE (artist quality):
- 100 parts Linseed oil (binder)
- 10-15 parts Pigment (iron oxide, ochre, etc.)
- 5 parts Mineral spirits (thinner)
- 1 part Drier (cobalt or manganese soap, accelerates hardening)

Procedure:
1. Grind pigment very fine in mortar if clumpy
2. Mix pigment with small amount of oil to paste
3. Add remaining oil slowly, stirring constantly
4. Thin with mineral spirits to desired consistency
5. Add drier if desired (optional; slows yellowing)
6. Store in airtight container

Results in creamy oil paint similar to commercial products.
</div>

**Variations:**
- Replace linseed with walnut, safflower for less yellowing
- Increase drier for faster drying (reduces yellowing but may crack)
- Add wax (beeswax) for matte finish

</section>

<section id="ink-formulation">

## Ink Formulation

Inks are similar to paints but designed for finer distribution.

### Indian Ink (Traditional)

**Composition:**
- Carbon black (soot or bone black): Pigment
- Gum arabic: Binder
- Water: Vehicle
- Ammonia (sometimes): Improves flow

**Making:**
1. Collect carbon black (burn oil, resin; collect soot)
2. Dissolve gum arabic in water
3. Mix carbon black into gum solution
4. Thin to ink consistency with water

**Properties:**
- Very permanent (carbon doesn't fade)
- Flows well (thin enough)
- Water-based (easy cleanup; not waterproof once dry)

### Colored Inks

**Lake pigments work well:**
- Madder lake red
- Indigo (reduced form)
- Ochre yellow
- Suspended in gum arabic + water

**Application:**
- Dilute concentrated lake pigment suspension
- Mix with gum arabic and water to proper consistency
- Test on paper; adjust color/flow as needed

</section>

<section id="dye-fastness">

## Colorfastness and Dye Testing

### Fastness Ratings (Textile Industry)

**Light fastness:** Fade resistance to sunlight (1–8 scale, 8 = excellent)
- Grade 1–2: Poor (noticeable fade in weeks)
- Grade 3–4: Moderate (fade over months)
- Grade 5–6: Good (minimal fade over years)
- Grade 7–8: Excellent (permanent appearance)

**Wash fastness:** Color loss in laundering
- Grade 1–2: Poor (color bleeds in water)
- Grade 3–4: Moderate (some bleeding/fading)
- Grade 5–6: Good (minimal color loss)
- Grade 7–8: Excellent (no color change)

**Crocking (rubbing fastness):** Color rubs off on contact
- Related to inadequate dyeing/fixing
- Poor mordanting increases crocking
- Lake pigments in paint don't have this issue (particle-bound)

### Testing

**Simple light fade test:**
- Dye sample fabric in known mordant/dye
- Expose half to sunlight; cover other half (control)
- Periodically assess color match
- Track time to noticeable difference

**Wash test:**
- Dye fabric
- Wash in hot water (detergent optional)
- Assess color loss; note any bleeding

<section id="safety">

## Safety in Pigment and Dye Work

**Hazardous pigments to avoid:**
- Lead-based pigments (white lead, red lead): Toxic accumulation
- Mercury-based: Very toxic
- Cadmium: Carcinogenic
- Chromium VI: Carcinogenic

**Safe alternatives (modern):**
- Titanium white (titanium dioxide): Safe
- Iron oxides: Safe
- Earth pigments: Safe
- Synthetic organic pigments: Mostly safe (check MSDS)

**Natural dyes (mostly safe):**
- Madder: Generally safe
- Indigo: Safe
- Weld, woad: Safe
- Avoid some historical dyes (arsenic-containing)

**Copper sulfate (mordant):**
- Moderate toxicity
- Wear gloves; avoid ingestion
- Ventilate (fumes)
- Dispose safely (environmental hazard if dumped)

**Iron oxide (mordant):**
- Low toxicity
- Handle normally; wash hands

</section>

## Conclusion

Pigments and dyes create color in materials. Natural mineral pigments are permanent and safe; plant dyes are chemically interesting and historically important, requiring mordants to fix to fiber. Understanding pigment properties, chemistry, and application allows you to formulate paints and dyes, troubleshoot color problems, and appreciate the science behind color permanence and vibrancy.
