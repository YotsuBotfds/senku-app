<section id="overview">

## Overview

When civilization's supply chains collapse, feeding a family—or a community—depends entirely on what you grow. This guide provides the agricultural data and planning tools needed to calculate exactly how much land, seeds, and effort are required to keep people alive year-round using only field crops, legumes, and home vegetables.

Staple crop nutrition planning bridges three critical gaps:

1. **Caloric sufficiency** — How many acres of grains and roots are needed to prevent starvation?
2. **Nutritional completeness** — Can your crop mix provide protein, fats, and micronutrients, or will deficiencies emerge?
3. **Scalability** — What does planning look like for 5 people vs. 50 vs. 500?

This guide is written for post-collapse agriculture: low input, high resilience, no commercial fertilizers or pesticides. All yields assume heirloom/open-pollinated varieties and moderate soil fertility.

</section>

<section id="caloric-targets">

## Caloric Targets for Communities

The first step is knowing what you're feeding. Daily caloric needs vary by age, sex, and labor intensity.

:::info-box
**Daily Caloric Requirements (post-collapse lifestyle, moderate physical labor)**

- **Adult male:** 2,400–2,800 kcal/day
- **Adult female:** 1,800–2,200 kcal/day
- **Child (6–12 years):** 1,400–1,800 kcal/day
- **Child (1–5 years):** 1,000–1,400 kcal/day
- **Heavy labor (farming, construction):** +500 kcal/day

For planning, assume **2,000 kcal/person/day** as a sustainable baseline (240 calories per person per hour of moderate work).
:::

**Community scale calculations:**

- **5-person household:** 5 × 2,000 = 10,000 kcal/day = 3.65 million kcal/year
- **50-person community:** 50 × 2,000 = 100,000 kcal/day = 36.5 million kcal/year
- **100-person community:** 100 × 2,000 = 200,000 kcal/day = 73 million kcal/year

These figures must account for **seed reserve** (save 10–15% of grain harvest to plant next year) and **spoilage/animal loss** (assume 15–20% in storage, rodents, mold).

**Adjusted yearly need:**
- Actual harvest required = (Daily need × 365) ÷ 0.85 (accounting for 15% loss)

For a 5-person household: 3.65M ÷ 0.85 = **4.3 million kcal of crops needed per year**.

### Wheat (Winter Wheat)

Winter wheat is the gold standard for post-collapse survival agriculture. It's cold-hardy, stores indefinitely, and provides ~2.5 million kcal per acre (1.3 tons × 453.6 lb/ton × 1,650 kcal/lb).

**Profile:**
- Plant in late summer/early fall (August–October in temperate zones)
- Overwinters dormant
- Harvest late spring (May–June)
- Requires ~15 inches annual rainfall or irrigation
- Protein: 12–14% (marginal for complete nutrition without legumes)

**Post-collapse advantage:** Extremely reliable, stores in sealed containers for 10+ years, winnows easily with hand tools, and ground grain makes bread with no additional ingredients.

### Rice

Rice yields 2.5–3.3 million kcal per acre. In flooded lowland paddies (the traditional method), it is the highest-yielding staple crop.

**Profile:**
- Requires standing water 4–6 months
- Needs warm growing season (minimum 120 days, 60°F+)
- Labor-intensive (flooding/draining management, hand harvest in survival contexts)
- Protein: Only 7–8% — must be paired with legumes
- Stores well when dry in cool/dry conditions

**Climate:** Limited to warm climates. Upland (rainfed) rice is possible in temperate zones but yields drop 30–50%.

### Corn (Field Corn, Not Sweet Corn)

Mature field corn provides 2.2–3.1 million kcal per acre. It's hardy, flexible, and produces a multipurpose crop (grain, fodder, cobs).

**Profile:**
- Plant spring after last frost
- 110–140 days to mature grain (ears harden by late fall)
- Moderate drought-tolerance with established root system
- Nitrogen-loving: benefits from legume rotation or compost input
- Stores well dried on the cob for many years

**Survival advantage:** Corn cobs are excellent kindling/fuel, husks provide animal bedding, stalks can be composted or used for thatching.

### Potato

Potatoes are unique: they yield the highest calories per acre of any staple crop (6–10 tons fresh = 2.1–3.5 million kcal/acre) and grow in moderate climates.

**Profile:**
- Plant spring, harvest fall (80–120 days)
- Require consistently moist, loose, well-draining soil
- High input: need seed pieces, pest management, hilling labor
- Store at 35–40°F with high humidity for 4–6 months
- Protein: Only 2–3% — strictly a carbohydrate source

**Limitation:** Unlike wheat or rice, potatoes spoil after months in storage. Suitable for year-round consumption but inadequate as sole winter staple without root cellar.

### Sweet Potato

Sweet potatoes offer better storage and nutrition than potatoes (more vitamin A, slightly more protein).

**Profile:**
- Requires warm climate (frost-sensitive vine crop)
- 100–150 days to harvest
- Yields 4–8 tons fresh (1.5–3 million kcal/acre)
- Store at 55–60°F, high humidity, for 4–8 months
- Can be propagated from slips (shoots) or root pieces

**Climate consideration:** Requires 20+ frost-free weeks. Excellent for warm/tropical regions; marginal in cool temperate.

### Cassava (Manioc)

Cassava is the most drought-hardy, poverty-soil-tolerant staple crop available. In tropical/warm climates, it's nearly foolproof.

**Profile:**
- Woody shrub; perennial (harvest year-round or once per year)
- Yields 3–6 tons fresh roots per acre (1.35–2.7 million kcal/acre dry weight)
- Requires only 30–40 inches rain annually (thrives where other crops fail)
- Takes 12 months for roots to fully mature
- Protein: 1–2% (severe; must be supplemented with legumes)
- Leaves are edible and nutritious (6% protein, vitamin A-rich)

**Processing:** Fresh cassava roots cannot be eaten raw (contain cyanogenic compounds); must be peeled, dried, or fermented. In survival contexts, drying takes 2–3 weeks or can be sun-dried into cassava flour.

### Dry Beans (Legumes)

Beans are irreplaceable for nutrition: 20–25% protein, lysine (amino acid lacking in grains), and soil-fixing nitrogen.

**Common post-collapse varieties:**
- **Pinto, kidney, black beans:** 0.8–1.2 tons/acre, 150 days
- **Lentils:** 0.6–0.9 tons/acre, 100–110 days (cool-season)
- **Chickpeas:** 0.7–1 ton/acre, 120 days (warm-season, semi-arid)

**Profile:**
- Require moderate fertility (less demanding than grains after legume rotation)
- Mature pods dry on plant; harvest when crispy
- Store indefinitely when dry (10+ years)
- Hand-harvest is feasible at small scale

:::info-box
**Nutritional Complementarity:** Grains lack lysine; legumes lack methionine. Eating beans + grains = complete protein profile identical to meat. This is the survival farmer's key to avoiding protein deficiency.
:::

## Crop Combinations for Complete Nutrition

Growing a single staple crop, no matter how high-yielding, will eventually cause nutritional deficiencies. The solution is polyculture: combining crops that fill each other's nutritional gaps.

### The "Three Sisters" and Grain+Legume Model

Traditional indigenous agriculture across the Americas, Africa, and Asia used intercropping:

**Three Sisters (corn + beans + squash):**
- Corn provides vertical structure for beans to climb
- Beans fix nitrogen, enriching soil for corn and squash
- Squash leaves shade soil, suppress weeds, and retain moisture
- Together: grain (carbs) + legume (protein) + gourd (calories, vitamins, minerals)

**For post-collapse survival, the better model is sequential rotation:**

1. **Year 1:** Grain crop (wheat, corn, or rice) on field A; legume on field B; vegetables on field C
2. **Year 2:** Legume on field A (soil enriched by previous grain); grain on field B; vegetables on field C
3. **Year 3:** Vegetables on field A; grain on field B; legume on field C

This cycle eliminates synthetic fertilizer need while maintaining soil fertility.

### Complete Nutrition Targets

A sustainable diet in post-collapse conditions requires:

- **Calories:** 2,000 kcal/person/day = ~730,000 kcal/person/year
- **Protein:** 50 g/person/day (women: 46 g, men: 56 g)
- **Fat:** 44–77 g/person/day (for hormone/vitamin absorption)
- **Micronutrients:** Iron, iodine, vitamin A, vitamin C, calcium

**Optimal crop combination for a 100-person community:**

| Crop | Acres | Annual Yield | Calories/Year | Protein (lbs) |
|------|-------|--------------|---------------|---------------|
| Wheat | 40 | 50 tons | 165M kcal | 13,200 |
| Beans (dry) | 15 | 18 tons | 56M kcal | 8,800 |
| Potatoes | 10 | 80 tons | 28M kcal | 1,100 |
| **Root Vegetables** (carrots, beets) | 3 | 18 tons | 3.2M kcal | 180 |
| **Leafy Greens** (kale, chard, fast-growing) | 2 | 12 tons | 2M kcal | 600 |
| **TOTAL** | **70** | — | **254.2M kcal** | **23,880 lbs protein** |

For 100 people eating 2,000 kcal/day: 73 million kcal/year needed. The above yields ~254M, providing 3.5× surplus for seed saving (12%), animal feed, processing losses, and trade/exchange.

:::tip
**Seed Saving Calculation:** Save 15% of grain harvest for replanting. From 50 tons of wheat, preserve 7.5 tons as seed. This requires separate, rat-proof storage and germination testing before spring planting.
:::

## Acreage Calculator: How Much Land Per Person?

This is the question every post-collapse community faces first.

**Baseline minimum** (grain + legume only, no surplus):

| Scenario | Grain (lbs/person/year) | Legumes (lbs/person/year) | Acres/person |
|----------|------------------------|---------------------------|--------------|
| Wheat alone | 500 | — | 0.4 |
| Wheat + beans (50/50 mix) | 250 | 250 | 0.5 |
| Corn + beans (50/50 mix) | 250 | 250 | 0.5 |
| Rice + fish/legumes | 400 | 150 | 0.6 |

**With vegetables, root crops, seed saving, and spoilage buffer:**

Add **0.15–0.2 acres per person** for vegetables, medicinal herbs, and diversity. Add another **0.1 acres per person** for seed saving and loss.

**Practical rule of thumb:**
- **Subsistence, no surplus:** 0.5–0.7 acres/person
- **Subsistence + modest surplus/trade:** 0.8–1.0 acres/person
- **Comfortable surplus (regional trade, animal fodder):** 1.2–1.5 acres/person

**Examples:**
- **5-person household:** 2.5–5 acres (3–5 acres recommended)
- **50-person community:** 40–75 acres (60 acres practical target)
- **100-person community:** 80–150 acres (120 acres practical target)

:::warning
These calculations assume moderate soil fertility (2–3% organic matter, pH 6–7). Poor soil or marginal climate will require 30–50% more acreage. Start with a soil test and compost amendment strategy.
:::

<section id="seasonal-calendar">

## Seasonal Planting Calendar

Post-collapse agriculture must respect local climate. Below is a temperate-zone (USDA zones 5–7) calendar; adjust dates based on your last/first frost dates.

### Spring (March–May)

- **Early March:** Sow peas, cool-season greens, spinach indoors/outdoors (35°F minimum soil)
- **Late March–Early April:** Sow potatoes, onions, early carrots (after frost danger passes)
- **Late April–May:** Sow corn (soil 60°F+), beans, warm-season squash, cucumbers

### Early Summer (June–July)

- **June:** Transplant warm-season crops (tomatoes, peppers, basil)
- **Late June–July:** Summer succession plantings of beans, lettuce, radishes every 2 weeks

### Late Summer/Early Fall (August–September)

- **August:** Sow fall/winter crops—kale, chard, brassicas, root vegetables (carrots, beets, turnips)
- **Early September:** Sow winter wheat (ideal window: 5–6 weeks before first frost)

### Fall/Winter (October–February)

- **October–November:** Harvest potatoes, sweet potatoes, root vegetables; prepare fields
- **December–January:** Winter rest; maintain dormant wheat/legume crops
- **February:** Begin season planning, soil amendments, seed germination testing

:::info-box
**Frost Dates Are Critical:** For your region, find your first and last frost dates. Local extension offices, almanacs, and longtime neighbors are reliable sources. Planting before the last frost or after the first kills crops outright—no flexibility.
:::

</section>

## Protein Sources: Legumes, Livestock, and Aquaculture

Grain alone provides 9–12% protein (wheat is best). Community health requires 50+ grams protein per person per day. Here's how to achieve it without meat:

### Legumes: The Foundation

Dry beans, lentils, and peas are the sole renewable protein source available to small-scale farmers without livestock.

**Protein yield per acre:**

| Crop | Yield (tons/acre) | Protein % | Protein lbs/acre |
|------|------------------|-----------|------------------|
| Dry beans | 1.2 | 23% | 553 |
| Lentils | 0.8 | 26% | 416 |
| Chickpeas | 1.0 | 19% | 380 |
| Field peas | 1.5 | 24% | 720 |

**Practical protein math:** 1 acre of beans feeds 11 people their entire protein requirement (550 lbs ÷ 50 lbs/person/year).

For a 100-person community, **8–10 acres of legumes** (alternated with grain) ensures complete protein security without animals.

### Livestock Integration (Small Scale)

If resources permit, chickens and goats provide protein without grain competition:

- **20 chickens** produce ~180 eggs/week (36 g protein/egg) — 6.5 lbs protein/week
- **5 dairy goats** produce ~2.5 lbs milk/day (8 g protein/lb) — 40+ lbs protein/week

Both are low-input after initial infrastructure (coops, fencing). Manure feeds compost for crop production.

### Fish Ponds and Aquaculture

If your community has reliable water, tilapia, carp, or catfish ponds are protein gold:

- **1 acre pond, well-managed:** 1,000–2,000 lbs of fish/year (160–320 lbs protein)
- Feeds 3–6 people their entire protein requirement

This requires water source, oxygen management (aeration or flow-through systems), and initial fingerling stock.

:::tip
**Easiest Legume for Beginners:** Field peas (not the sugar snap variety) are robust, nitrogen-fixing, and tolerate poor soil. Grow them as a catch crop (quick 90-day summer planting) for green manure or dry harvest.
:::

<section id="micronutrient-crops">

## Micronutrient Crops: Preventing Deficiency Diseases

Staple grains and beans provide calories and protein but lack critical micronutrients. A post-collapse diet without vegetables leads to predictable deficiency diseases:

- **Vitamin C deficiency (scurvy):** Bleeding gums, poor wound healing, weakness
- **Vitamin A deficiency:** Blindness, immune collapse
- **Iron deficiency:** Anemia, fatigue, reduced work capacity
- **Iodine deficiency:** Goiter, cognitive impairment

**Micronutrient-dense crop list:**

| Crop | Key Nutrient | Yield | Storage |
|------|--------------|-------|---------|
| Kale, chard | Vitamin A, C, calcium | 10 tons/acre | 1–2 weeks fresh; dry for winter |
| Carrots, sweet potato | Vitamin A (beta-carotene) | 15 tons/acre | 4–6 months, cool storage |
| Beets | Iron, folate | 20 tons/acre | 4–6 months |
| Cabbage | Vitamin C (stores naturally) | 15 tons/acre | 4–6 months in cool storage |
| Sea vegetables (kelp, nori) | Iodine, trace minerals | 1–2 tons/acre (dried) | Indefinite |

**For a 100-person community:**

- **Leafy greens:** 2–3 acres rotation (succession plant for continuous harvest)
- **Root vegetables:** 5–6 acres (carrots, beets, turnips, parsnips)
- **Cabbage/brassicas:** 2 acres
- **Sea vegetable harvest/drying:** 0.5 acre equivalent (if coastal; otherwise skip or trade for iodized sea salt)

All must be preserved: dried, fermented, or root-cellared for winter consumption.

:::info-box
**Fermentation for Vitamin C:** Sauerkraut (fermented cabbage) provides vitamin C year-round and improves digestibility. A 100-person community should ferment 2–3 tons of cabbage in fall for winter supply.
:::

<section id="storage-prevention">

## Storage & Loss Prevention

Growing food means nothing if 30% rots before consumption. Post-collapse storage is primitive but proven.

### Grain Storage (Wheat, Rice, Corn)

**Best practice: sealed, cool, dry, pest-proof**

- **Containers:** Food-grade buckets (5-gallon), sealed glass jars, or traditional clay pots sealed with beeswax
- **Desiccant:** Dried lime powder or diatomaceous earth (food-grade) mixed into grain prevents moisture and insects
- **Temperature:** 50°F or below; every 18°F temperature drop doubles storage life
- **Expected shelf life:** Wheat, rice, corn in sealed containers = 10–30 years if kept dry and cool

**Community-scale solution:** Buried root cellar (55–65°F, high humidity) for grain in bulk wooden bins with tight-fitting lids.

### Root Vegetable Storage

- **Cool, humid cellar:** 35–40°F, 90–95% humidity
- **Separate produce by type** (store apples away from potatoes — ethylene gas hastens spoilage)
- **Beds of sand or sawdust** insulate and reduce moisture loss
- **Expected shelf life:** Potatoes 4–6 months; carrots, beets, turnips 5–8 months

### Legume Storage

- **Fully dried pods:** Place in sealed containers with desiccant
- **Keep away from humidity:** Moisture causes mold
- **Expected shelf life:** 10–20 years (lentils slightly shorter than beans)

### Loss Prevention Strategy

**Rodent control:**
- Cats (essential for a farm community—train them to live in grain stores)
- Traps and poison (pre-collapse advantage if available)
- Sealed containers (most effective long-term)

**Mold prevention:**
- Sun-dry all grains and legumes to <12% moisture before storage
- Use desiccants liberally
- Inspect stored grain monthly; remove any moldy sections

**Spoilage during growing season:**
- Assume 15% crop loss to weather, pests, and disease
- Plant 20% surplus to account for this

:::warning
**Aflatoxin (fungal toxin):** If stored grain smells musty or shows visible mold, do not eat it—aflatoxin can cause liver damage. Discard and compost safely away from food areas.
:::

<section id="scaling-community">

## Scaling: Household to Community Planning

Feeding a family is different from feeding a village. Here's how planning changes at each scale.

### Household Scale (5–10 People, 3–5 Acres)

**Realistic approach:**
- Grow 60–70% of calories (grains + potatoes + beans)
- Buy/trade 30–40% (salt, cooking fat, sugar, luxury items)
- Focus: reliability over maximum yield

**Layout:**
- 1–2 acres grain/legume rotation
- 0.5–1 acre potatoes (rotation every 3 years)
- 0.5 acre beans
- 0.5–1 acre garden (vegetables, herbs)
- 0.5 acre orchard/perennials (apples, berries, nuts—3–5 year payoff)

**Labor:** 1–2 adults, seasonal peaks (planting, harvest) need 40+ hours/week

### Small Community (25–50 People, 30–50 Acres)

**Organizational shift:**
- Central grain/legume fields managed by rotating work crews
- Individual household gardens (essential for morale and diversity)
- Shared storage facility and processing equipment (thresher, mill, root cellar)

**Land allocation:**
- 15–20 acres grain (wheat, corn)
- 6–8 acres legumes
- 8–10 acres root crops (potatoes, sweet potatoes)
- 4–5 acres vegetables
- 2–3 acres orchard/perennials
- 2–3 acres fallow (rotation buffer)

**Labor:** Assign crop responsibilities by skill. Rotate labor-intensive tasks (harvest, threshing) among households based on family strength.

:::tip
**Rotating Harvests Reduce Bottlenecks:** If all grain ripens simultaneously, harvest becomes impossible. Plant early, mid, and late varieties of wheat/corn so ripening staggered over 4–6 weeks.
:::

### Large Community (100+ People, 120+ Acres)

**Institutional structure:**
- Dedicated grain production (40–50 acres wheat/rice)
- Legume production (10–15 acres)
- Starchy roots (12–15 acres potatoes/sweet potatoes/cassava)
- Distributed vegetable zones (5–8 acres, managed by family gardens + communal plots)
- Pasture for livestock, if animal protein planned (20+ acres)

**Specialization:**
- Master farmers oversee grain/legume rotations
- Gardeners manage vegetables and preservation
- Herders manage livestock/poultry
- Millers/processors manage grain milling, preservation, seed saving

**Redundancy and resilience:**
- Plant same crop across 2–3 separate fields (weather/disease doesn't wipe out all seed)
- Maintain 2-year surplus storage (seeds and emergency rations)
- Cross-train so no single person is irreplaceable

**Storage infrastructure:**
- Central grain silo or buried root cellar for 20,000+ lbs grain
- Multiple root cellars (distributed storage = fire/flooding insurance)
- Fermentation facility for vegetables
- Drying racks for herbs, vegetables, fruits

<section id="climate-adaptation">

## Climate Adaptation: Crop Selection for Your Region

Staple crop viability depends entirely on climate. Choose wrong, and you grow nothing.

### Cool Temperate (USDA Zones 4–5, <150 frost-free days)

**Best crops:**
- Winter wheat (all)
- Potatoes (excellent; stores well for long winters)
- Lentils (100–110 days, reliable)
- Brassicas (kale, cabbage, turnips)
- Peas (spring/summer)

**Avoid:**
- Rice (insufficient heat)
- Corn (risky; needs short-season variety)
- Cassava (will not mature)

**Strategy:** Focus on wheat + potatoes + legumes. Root cellar is critical for winter.

### Warm Temperate (USDA Zones 6–8, 150–200 frost-free days)

**Best crops:**
- Winter wheat (fall/spring crop, excellent)
- Corn (mainstream 120-day varieties)
- Beans (all types)
- Potatoes + sweet potatoes
- Rice (upland varieties; requires summer water management)

**Avoid:**
- Cassava (borderline; needs protected microclimates)

**Strategy:** Rotate wheat (winter) + corn (summer) on same field with spring bean crop. Three crops in 18 months.

### Subtropical/Tropical (Frost-free year-round, >250 frost-free days)

**Best crops:**
- Rice (flooded lowland paddies or rainfed upland)
- Cassava (perennial, drought-tolerant, versatile)
- Sweet potatoes (high yield, long harvest window)
- Corn (multiple plantings per year)
- Tree crops (breadfruit, mango, coconut, cacao)

**Avoid:**
- Winter wheat (cannot overwinter easily; spring plantings risky)
- Potatoes (disease pressure, short storage life in heat)

**Strategy:** Cassava + rice + beans polyculture. Year-round planting windows allow flexibility. Focus on preservation (drying, fermentation) for dry season.

### Semi-Arid / Dry Climate (15–25 inches rain/year)

**Best crops:**
- Wheat (early, short-season varieties)
- Millet/sorghum (if locally adapted; high drought tolerance)
- Beans (early-maturing, small-seeded varieties)
- Cassava (if warm enough; extremely drought-hardy)

**Must-have:** Irrigation source (wells, rainwater capture, rivers) for reliability.

**Strategy:** Conserve every drop of water. Mulch heavily. Plant in depressions to capture runoff. Rotate fields to rest and allow natural moisture recovery.

:::info-box
**Finding Your USDA Hardiness Zone:** Visit planthardiness.usda.gov or contact your local Cooperative Extension. Know your zone—it's the foundation of all planning.
:::

<section id="crop-failure-contingency">

## Crop Failure Contingency Protocols

Total crop loss—from drought, flood, disease, or pest—can occur even with perfect planning. Communities survive if replacement crops are known and seeds available.

### Defining Total Loss Scenarios

**Total loss = <10% harvest of planned yield.** This differs from poor harvest (50%) or moderate loss (20–30%).

**Triggers for total crop loss:**

| **Event** | **Crops Affected** | **Recovery Strategy** |
|---|---|---|
| **Drought (>6 weeks no rain, <1 inch/month)** | Grains (wheat, corn), legumes; potatoes vulnerable | Switch to drought-hardy crops (millet, cassava); reduce animal populations |
| **Flooding (fields underwater >2 weeks)** | All summer crops; winter wheat survives if not flowering | Replant fast-maturing varieties; salvage any grain from margins |
| **Early/Late Frost (freeze outside normal window)** | Tender crops (beans, corn, squash); late frost kills emerging wheat | Fall-planted winter crops; spring replanting if space available |
| **Epidemic Disease (late blight, ergot, rust)** | Entire variety susceptible; entire field lost | Switch to disease-resistant variety mid-season if available; abandon field if infection severe |
| **Pest Infestation (locusts, armyworms, weevils)** | Locust swarms destroy everything; grain weevils devastate stored crops | Forage and preserve any surviving food; deploy grasshopper traps if infrastructure available |

**Survival principle:** Plan now what to plant if your main crop fails.

| **Millet** | 70–90 | 40–50% | 9–11% | June sowing (drought-hardy) |

**Lost crop: Spring corn (planted but killed by flood/frost in June)**

| **Crop** | **Days to Harvest** | **Yield vs. Corn** | **Planting Window** |

| **Crop** | **Days to Harvest** | **Yield vs. Original** | **Notes** |

**Best bet:** Plant winter wheat in August (long recovery season). Simultaneously plant beans for fall harvest. Winter wheat provides major caloric recovery next spring.

#### Subtropical/Tropical (Frost-free, Year-Round Planting)

**Advantage:** Can plant replacement crops anytime year-round.

**Best bet:** Cassava provides fastest caloric recovery. Plant immediately after loss; begin harvesting 4–6 months later. Fills the gap while replanting main crop.

**If you have abundant water but limited labor:**

## Practical Checklist: Building Your Nutrition Plan

1. **Know your numbers:**
   - Population to feed
   - Caloric/protein targets (use 2,000 kcal/day baseline)
   - Available acreage and soil quality

2. **Choose crops:**
   - Primary staple (wheat, rice, corn, or potatoes based on climate)
   - Legume crop (beans, lentils, peas)
   - Secondary starch (second grain or root crop)
   - Micronutrient crops (leafy greens, root vegetables, cabbage)

3. **Calculate acreage:**
   - Use yields table in this guide
   - Add 20% for seed saving
   - Add 15% for spoilage/loss
   - Round up; surplus is insurance

4. **Plan rotation:**
   - Grain → Legume → Vegetable (3-year cycle minimum)
   - Document in a simple table: Field A (year 1, 2, 3), Field B, Field C

5. **Design storage:**
   - Root cellar, grain bins, desiccant containers—all must be rodent-proof
   - Temperature and humidity targets by crop type
   - Capacity = 1.5× annual consumption (seed + emergency reserve)

6. **Test soil:**
   - Send samples to extension office before year one
   - Amend based on results (compost, lime, phosphate rock)

7. **Source seeds:**
   - Heirloom, open-pollinated varieties only
   - Order in winter for spring planting
   - Save 15% of each harvest as next year's seed

8. **Build redundancy:**
   - Plant same crop in 2+ separate fields (weather insurance)
   - Maintain 2-year stored seed reserve
   - Cross-train 2+ people in critical skills (grain milling, seed saving)

<section id="conclusion">

## Closing: Nutrition Planning Is Community Planning

Staple crop and nutrition planning is not just agriculture—it is the foundation of community stability, health, and resilience. A community fed on grain alone will develop deficiencies within a year; a community without seed reserves cannot plant next season; a village that loses its harvest to inadequate storage faces starvation.

The numbers in this guide are achievable with moderate effort and knowledge. A 100-person community on 120 well-managed acres can feed itself completely, save seed for next year, and even trade surplus for items it cannot produce (salt, tools, medicines). A 5-person household on 4 acres can achieve 70–80% self-sufficiency, supplementing with trade goods and wild foraged foods.

The key is **starting early**: before collapse, or in its early stages when resources are still available. Once shortages begin, soil quality, seed stocks, and knowledge compound into either surplus or crisis.

Begin with your household, expand to your neighborhood, and scale from there. The math is simple; the execution demands patience, learning, and community cooperation. This guide provides the foundation. The rest depends on your climate, soil, labor, and will to sustain yourself and those you choose to support.
