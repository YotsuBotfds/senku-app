## Introduction

Concrete quality determines structural integrity, durability, and safety. In post-collapse scenarios without access to commercial testing labs, you must rely on field testing and observation to ensure concrete meets minimum strength and durability standards. This guide covers practical tests that can be performed on site with basic tools and materials.

## Concrete Composition and Water-Cement Ratio

The strength and durability of concrete depends primarily on the water-cement ratio (W/C)—the weight of water divided by the weight of cement in the mix.

**Key relationship:**
- **W/C = 0.40:** High strength (4,000+ psi at 28 days), low permeability, durable
- **W/C = 0.50:** Moderate strength (3,000–4,000 psi), moderate durability
- **W/C = 0.60:** Lower strength (2,500–3,000 psi), higher permeability, acceptable for non-critical work
- **W/C > 0.65:** Weak concrete (below 2,500 psi), unsuitable for structural work

The lower the water-cement ratio, the stronger and more durable the concrete.

:::warning
Never exceed a water-cement ratio of 0.65 for structural concrete. The most common concrete failure in post-collapse rebuilding is excessive water in the mix, creating weak concrete that fails under load.
:::

### Controlling Water Content

In field conditions, water-cement ratio is controlled by:

1. **Weigh cement and water** — Use a scale or volumetric containers (5-gallon buckets). For every 50 pounds of cement, use no more than 20 pounds of water (W/C = 0.40) for structural concrete, or up to 32.5 pounds of water for acceptable concrete (W/C = 0.65).

2. **Account for water in aggregates** — Sand and gravel contain moisture. After heavy rain, aggregates may contain 5–15% additional water by weight. **Dry aggregates before mixing when possible, or reduce added water accordingly.**

3. **Measure water carefully** — Use marked buckets. Common error: overwatering by 10–20% to improve workability. This drastically reduces strength.

4. **Check slump immediately after mixing** — If slump exceeds 4 inches, water content is too high; reduce water in subsequent batches.

## Slump Testing

Slump measures the workability and consistency of fresh concrete. The test is simple, fast, and reveals water content problems immediately.

### Equipment Needed

- Slump cone: Metal or plywood cone, 12 inches tall, 8 inches diameter at top, 4 inches at bottom
- Straight ruler or measuring tape
- Tamping rod (metal rod, 5/8 inch diameter, 24 inches long)
- Smooth, level surface

### Procedure

1. **Prepare the surface:** Place cone on a smooth, damp (not wet) surface. Support the cone so it cannot slip.

2. **Fill the cone:** In three equal layers, fill the cone with fresh concrete.

3. **Tamp each layer:** Use the tamping rod to tamp each layer 25 times with moderate force. The rod should penetrate into the layer below.

4. **Strike off excess:** After the third layer is tamped, strike off concrete level with the top of the cone using the ruler.

5. **Lift the cone:** Rapidly lift the cone straight up without twisting. Do not hold the concrete down.

6. **Measure subsidence:** Using the ruler, measure the vertical distance from the original height of concrete (12 inches) down to the highest point of the slumped concrete.

### Slump Interpretation

| Slump (inches) | Consistency | W/C Likely | Suitability |
|----------------|-------------|-----------|------------|
| 0–2 | Very stiff | 0.35–0.40 | Requires vibration; strong concrete but difficult to place |
| 2–4 | Stiff | 0.40–0.50 | Good for reinforced concrete structures; easy placement with vibration |
| 4–6 | Plastic | 0.50–0.60 | Good workability; acceptable W/C for most applications |
| 6–8 | Fluid | 0.60–0.70 | Easy to place but higher W/C; weak concrete for structural use |
| Over 8 | Runny | Over 0.70 | Unacceptable; too much water; will be weak and non-durable |

:::tip
Perform slump tests immediately after mixing and at intervals during concrete placement. If slump increases during the day (concrete becomes wetter), water is being added—this is a common site error. Stop adding water and investigate the mixer.
:::

## Concrete Cube and Cylinder Testing

To estimate concrete strength without laboratory testing, make test cubes and cylinders from the same batch used in the structure.

### Making Test Specimens

**Materials:**
- Wooden or plastic molds (4×4×4 inch cubes or 4-inch-diameter, 8-inch-tall cylinders)
- Concrete from the same batch as the structure
- Tamping rod or vibrator
- Oil or release agent to coat molds

**Procedure:**

1. **Coat molds:** Apply oil to the interior to allow easy removal.

2. **Fill molds:** Pour concrete into molds in layers (typically two equal layers for cubes, three for cylinders).

3. **Consolidate:** Tamp each layer 25 times with a rod or use a vibrating table if available. Vibration is superior to tamping, producing denser, stronger concrete.

4. **Strike off excess:** Level the top of the specimen with a straight edge.

5. **Label and cure:** Mark each specimen with the date, time, location, and batch number. Store in the same location as the structural concrete (exposed if outdoors, protected if indoors).

### Curing and Storage

Concrete strength develops over time through hydration. Proper curing is critical:

- **Temperature:** Concrete cures faster at higher temperatures. At 70°F, strength development is normal (28-day design strength). At 50°F, it is slowed by 30–50%. Below 40°F, curing is very slow or halted.

- **Moisture:** Concrete must remain moist to hydrate properly. Keep curing test specimens damp by:
  - Storing under burlap or wood covers that are kept wet
  - Placing in a plastic bag sealed with a stone on top
  - Submerging in a water tank
  - Spraying daily with water (less reliable but functional)

- **Duration:** Test specimens at 7 days and 28 days to track strength development:
  - **7-day strength:** Typically 60–70% of 28-day strength
  - **28-day strength:** Design strength; used for structural decisions

### Field Crushing Test (No Equipment)

Without a compression testing machine, you can estimate concrete strength using simple tools.

**Hardness test:**

1. Once concrete is fully cured (7–28 days), hammer a hardened nail against the surface with moderate force. If the nail does not dent the surface, concrete strength is likely adequate (above 2,500 psi). If the nail leaves a dent, concrete is weak (below 2,500 psi) and unsuitable for structural use.

**Breaking test:**

1. At 28 days, carefully remove a test cube from curing
2. Place it in a vise or between heavy concrete blocks on the ground
3. Load with heavy weights (add weight gradually):
   - Sandbags (each ~50 lbs)
   - Concrete blocks (each ~35 lbs)
   - Logs and blocks
4. Increase load until the specimen breaks
5. Record the total weight at failure

**Strength estimation:**

Divide total load (in pounds) by footing area (4×4 = 16 square inches):

- **Less than 1,000 psi:** Weak; likely W/C over 0.65; unsuitable for structural use
- **1,000–2,000 psi:** Acceptable for non-structural work; marginal for structures
- **2,000–3,000 psi:** Acceptable for most structures; adequate with proper design
- **Over 3,000 psi:** Good strength; likely W/C below 0.50; suitable for all structural uses

:::warning
If field testing reveals concrete weaker than 2,000 psi, do not use it in structural elements. Concrete can be removed and replaced, or doweled in place with new concrete to increase strength.
:::

## Aggregate Evaluation

Aggregate (sand and gravel) comprises 70–80% of concrete by volume. Poor aggregate quality directly reduces concrete strength and durability.

### Sand Quality Assessment

**Perform these tests on sand before mixing:**

1. **Silt content test:**
   - Fill a glass jar halfway with sand
   - Add water until jar is 3/4 full, add a drop of dish soap
   - Seal and shake vigorously for 3 minutes
   - Let settle for 1 hour; observe silt layer on top of sand
   - Acceptable: silt layer is less than 1/8 inch high on 6 inches of sand
   - Unacceptable: silt layer over 1/4 inch; wash sand before using

2. **Grading test:**
   - Fill a bucket with a representative sample of sand (1 gallon)
   - Grade should include fine particles (dust-like) and coarser particles (pea-sized), not uniform size
   - Uniform fine sand produces weak, unworkable concrete
   - Uniform coarse sand separates during placement (segregation)

3. **Organic content test:**
   - Fill a jar 1/3 with sand, 2/3 with water, add a drop of dish soap
   - Seal and shake for 3 minutes
   - Let settle for 1 minute, observe water color
   - Clear water: clean sand, acceptable
   - Brown or dark water: organic material present; may weaken concrete
   - Dark water and smell: do not use; wash thoroughly or obtain new sand

### Coarse Aggregate (Gravel) Assessment

1. **Size and uniformity:** Gravel should include stones ranging from 1/2 inch to 1 inch for optimal packing. Uniform size (all same) is weaker.

2. **Cleanliness:** Dust, dirt, or clay coating prevents cement paste from bonding. Wash gravel if questionable.

3. **Weakness:** Look for soft stones that break easily with a hammer. If more than 5% of stones break easily, the aggregate is too weak; obtain better gravel.

4. **Soundness:** In areas with freeze-thaw cycles, use aggregate without flat, porous stones (slate, weathered shale) that shatter when frozen.

## Mix Design Verification in the Field

Without laboratory facilities, verify concrete mix proportions by weight or volume.

### Standard Concrete Mix by Volume

These are nominal proportions for general-purpose concrete:

**Standard mix (W/C ~0.60, about 3,000 psi at 28 days):**
- 1 part cement
- 2 parts sand
- 3 parts gravel
- 0.6 parts water (by weight)

**Strong mix (W/C ~0.45, about 4,000 psi at 28 days):**
- 1 part cement
- 1.5 parts sand
- 2.5 parts gravel
- 0.45 parts water (by weight)

### Proportioning by Volume (Buckets)

For a standard concrete batch:

1. Measure cement: 5 gallons (93 lbs typical)
2. Measure sand: 10 gallons (clean, damp sand)
3. Measure gravel: 15 gallons (1/2–1 inch stones)
4. Water: 2.5–3 gallons (adjust for sand moisture)

### Proportioning by Weight (Scales)

For a standard concrete batch:

1. Cement: 50 lbs
2. Sand: 100 lbs (dry weight; add 10–20% for moisture)
3. Gravel: 150 lbs (dry weight)
4. Water: 20–25 lbs (depending on aggregate moisture and slump)

**Verify water-cement ratio:** 22.5 lbs water ÷ 50 lbs cement = 0.45 W/C (good)

### Correcting Poor Mix Results

If concrete is too stiff (low slump under 2 inches):
- Add a small amount of water and remix
- Maximum: increase W/C to 0.60

If concrete is too fluid (high slump over 6 inches):
- Add more cement and aggregates in the same ratio (1:2:3)
- Do not add more cement alone, as it changes the mix

:::tip
Always test the first batch from a new mixer or new aggregate source by making slump tests and test cubes. This identifies problems before placing concrete in the structure.
:::

## Curing Monitoring and Requirements

Concrete requires 7–28 days of proper curing to develop design strength. Improper curing creates weak, permeable, non-durable concrete.

### Curing Methods in the Field

**Method 1: Wet burlap and plastic (most reliable)**
- Immediately after concrete sets (6–12 hours), dampen burlap and lay over surface
- Cover with plastic sheet to retain moisture
- Keep burlap damp by spraying with water daily
- Maintain for 7–14 days minimum

**Method 2: Continuous water spray**
- Spray concrete surfaces with water 3–4 times daily for 7 days
- Effective but labor-intensive; water must be clean (fresh water, not salty or acidic)

**Method 3: Plastic sheeting**
- Seal concrete with clear or white plastic sheeting, sealed at edges
- Minimizes evaporation and maintains moisture
- Less reliable than wet burlap but better than no protection

**Method 4: Curing compounds (if available)**
- Apply spray-on curing compound immediately after final finishing
- Seals surface and prevents evaporation
- Follow manufacturer instructions

### Curing in Cold Weather (Below 50°F)

- Cover with insulation (blankets, straw, plastic domes) to retain heat
- Add 10% extra cement to the mix to accelerate hydration
- Use warm water (up to 160°F) in mixing
- Allow curing time to extend; concrete cures 50% slower at 40°F than at 70°F

### Curing in Hot Weather (Above 85°F)

- Begin water curing immediately after initial set
- Provide shade or white plastic covering to reduce surface temperature
- Spray frequently to keep surface damp
- Use cooler water if available
- Reduce W/C slightly (0.45 instead of 0.50) to offset evaporation

## Common Concrete Defects and Diagnosis

### Cracking (Fine Surface Cracks)

**Causes:**
- Plastic shrinkage: Rapid drying of surface while concrete is still plastic (within 24 hours)
- Thermal cracking: Differential temperature between surface (cold) and interior (warm, still hydrating)
- Drying shrinkage: Concrete drying after set, especially in hot, dry climate

**Prevention:**
- Keep surface wet during curing
- Avoid placing concrete in extreme heat
- Avoid rapid temperature changes
- For slabs, cut control joints every 10–12 feet to direct cracks

**Repair:** Small surface cracks (under 1/16 inch) are cosmetic and not structural. Wider cracks may indicate deeper problems; investigate by tapping (hollow sound = void; solid sound = concrete okay).

### Dusting (Weak Surface Layer)

**Cause:** Poor curing or low water-cement ratio with excess cement that was not properly hydrated

**Prevention:**
- Proper wet curing for 7+ days
- Do not trowel surface while concrete is still bleeding water (water rising to surface)
- Use proper W/C (0.40–0.60)

**Repair:** Remove weak surface layer by grinding or light sandblasting, then seal with epoxy coating.

### Spalling (Surface Breaking Away)

**Causes:**
- Freeze-thaw cycles in concrete containing air pockets or moisture
- Corrosion of reinforcing steel expanding outward
- Salt damage in areas exposed to road salt or seawater

**Prevention:**
- Use properly entrained air (air-entraining admixture) in freeze-thaw regions
- Provide adequate cover over reinforcement (1.5–2 inches)
- Avoid salt exposure

**Repair:** Remove spalled area, clean surface, and patch with patching concrete. For larger areas, remove back to sound concrete and reinstall reinforcement before patching.

### Honeycomb (Visible Voids and Porous Areas)

**Causes:**
- Inadequate vibration during placement
- Concrete too stiff to consolidate (low slump, high coarse aggregate)
- Air pockets trapped around reinforcement

**Prevention:**
- Adequate vibration (mechanical vibrators preferred over manual tamping)
- Proper slump (2–4 inches for most reinforced concrete)
- Proper spacing of reinforcement (min 6 inches clear space)

**Repair:** Small honeycomb may be left as-is if appearance is not critical. Larger areas: chip out, clean, apply bonding agent, and patch with concrete.

### Discoloration (Color Variations)

**Causes:**
- Inconsistent mixing (cement content varies)
- Different batches, brands, or types of cement
- Efflorescence (white powder): salts being transported to surface by moisture
- Mold or algae growth

**Prevention:**
- Use same cement source and brand
- Consistent mixing and proportioning
- Proper curing to minimize efflorescence

**Repair:** For efflorescence, brush with stiff brush; for mold, wash with dilute bleach solution. For color variations, recoat surface with clear sealer or thin overlay.

## Strength Estimation Without Testing

If you cannot make test cubes or perform field crushing tests, estimate concrete strength from observation:

| Observation | Estimated Strength | Suitability |
|-------------|-------------------|------------|
| 28+ days old; nail does not dent surface; solid sound when tapped | 2,500+ psi | Acceptable for most structures |
| 14 days old; moderately hard; nail dents slightly | 2,000–2,500 psi | Acceptable for non-critical structures |
| 7 days old; can scratch with fingernail; dull thud when tapped | 1,500–2,000 psi | Marginal; requires reinforcement |
| Fresh or 3 days old | Under 1,500 psi | Do not use; continue curing |

## Quality Control Checklist

Before and during concrete placement, verify:

- [ ] Aggregates tested for cleanliness, gradation, and soundness
- [ ] Cement stored dry and not expired (bag date within 3 months)
- [ ] Water is clean (fresh water, no salt or acid)
- [ ] Water-cement ratio measured and controlled
- [ ] Slump tested immediately after mixing
- [ ] Test cubes made from every batch and properly cured
- [ ] Surface kept wet during curing for 7–14 days
- [ ] Temperature monitored; curing time adjusted for cold weather
- [ ] Final concrete inspected for cracks, spalling, or other defects

## Conclusion

Field testing and quality monitoring ensure concrete strength and durability without laboratory access. The most critical steps are controlling water content, testing slump, making test specimens, and maintaining proper curing. Concrete is only as strong as the weakest batch—a single poorly made batch can compromise an entire structure. Take time to test, monitor, and verify.
