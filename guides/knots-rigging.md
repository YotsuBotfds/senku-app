<section id="rope-fundamentals">

## Rope Fundamentals

For detailed rope-type properties, breaking-strength tables by material and diameter, rope-versus-cable choice, and rope-condition guidance, see [Rope and Cable Utilities](/guides/rope-cable-utilities).

### Rope Types

Understanding rope construction is essential for selecting the right rope for your task:

-   **Laid (Twisted) Rope:** Consists of three strands twisted together. Most common, flexible, easy to splice. Good grip. Can rotate under load.
-   **Braided Rope:** Fibers braided in a pattern. More flexible than laid, doesn't rotate, but harder to splice. Better for slings and halyards.
-   **Kernmantle Rope:** Core (kern) surrounded by protective sheath (mantle). Very strong, minimal stretch. Used in climbing and rescue. Difficult to splice.

### Rope Materials

Natural Fibers

-   **Manila:** Golden color, excellent grip, 1500+ ft/lb per diameter
-   **Sisal:** Lighter color, less expensive, similar strength to manila
-   **Hemp:** Historic standard, superior handling, natural rot resistance
-   **Cotton:** Soft, comfortable, weaker, used for sash and seine rope

Synthetic Fibers

-   **Nylon:** Very strong, stretchy, good shock absorption, absorbs water
-   **Polyester:** Strong, less stretch, resists UV and water, maintains strength when wet
-   **Polypropylene:** Lightweight, floats, weaker, good for water applications
-   **Paracord:** Braided nylon, inner cores, 550 lbs breaking strength (standard)

### Safety Factors

:::info-box
Always apply a **safety factor** to rope work. Never load a rope beyond its safe working load:

-   **5:1 ratio:** General purpose, human loads, low-risk activities
-   **6:1 ratio:** Hoisting, moderate risk, loaded lines
-   **10:1 ratio:** Critical applications, climbing, rescue, life-safety

Example: A 1/2" nylon rope with 7,000 lbs breaking strength should only support 700 lbs (10:1) to 1,400 lbs (5:1) maximum.
:::

### Rope Care and Storage

-   **Coil properly:** Use figure-eight or figure-of-nine coil to prevent tangles and kinks
-   **Keep dry:** Moisture promotes rot in natural fiber rope. Store in dry location
-   **Avoid sunlight:** UV rays degrade both natural and synthetic rope over time
-   **Check regularly:** Inspect for fraying, fuzzing, stiffness, color changes, or odor
-   **Prevent abrasion:** Keep rope away from sharp edges and rough surfaces
-   **Don't store under load:** Permanent tension weakens rope fibers
-   **Maintain temperature:** Extreme heat weakens synthetic fibers, extreme cold brittles natural fiber

## Rope Making

Understanding how rope is constructed helps you appreciate its properties and make repair assessments. Traditional rope-making follows this progression:

### From Fiber to Rope

1
**Fiber:** Raw plant fiber (flax, hemp, cotton, sisal, etc.) or animal fiber
2
**Yarn:** Fibers twisted together into thread
3
**Strand:** Multiple yarns twisted together
4
**Rope:** Strands twisted together (typically 3 or 4)

### 3-Strand Laid Rope Construction

3-Strand Rope Structure (Cross-Section)

![Knots, Rope &amp; Rigging diagram 1](../assets/svgs/knots-rigging-1.svg)

### Natural Fibers for Rope Making

-   **Hemp (Cannabis sativa):** Superior fiber length and strength. Bark fibers separated and spun into yarn. Historic standard before synthetics.
-   **Flax:** Fine, strong fibers. More brittle than hemp. Excellent for fine cordage.
-   **Milkweed:** Silky fibers from seed pods. Weaker but workable for cordage in survival situations.
-   **Stinging Nettle:** Surprisingly strong fibers in the stem. Can produce fine cordage with proper processing.
-   **Dogbane:** Native American use. Produces strong, fine cordage when properly processed.
-   **Bark fibers:** Inner bark of willow, cottonwood, basswood, elm. Immediate emergency cordage source.
-   **Milkweed floss:** The silky down from seed pods. Can be carded and spun into yarn.

### Traditional Rope Walk Method

The "rope walk" was the primary method of rope manufacture before industrial production. A rope maker would walk backward while twisting fibers:

1
Secure three bundles of prepared fiber to a fixed point (the "head")
2
Attach each bundle to a separate spinning hook (powered by hand crank or foot treadle)
3
Walk backward while turning the crank, maintaining tension and controlling twist
4
Each strand rotates, and the three strands lay around each other to form rope
5
Walk the full length of the rope walk (100+ feet) to complete the rope

## Essential Knots

### Overhand Knot (Stopper Knot)

Overhand Knot

![Knots, Rope &amp; Rigging diagram 2](../assets/svgs/knots-rigging-2.svg)

**Use:** Simplest stopper knot to prevent rope from passing through a hole or pulley. Minimum knot size.

### Figure-Eight Knot

Figure-Eight Knot (Better Stopper)

![Knots, Rope &amp; Rigging diagram 3](../assets/svgs/knots-rigging-3.svg)

**Use:** Preferred stopper knot. Creates a larger, more reliable knob than overhand. Won't slip through lines or pulleys.

### Square Knot (Reef Knot)

Square Knot (Joining Equal Ropes)

![Knots, Rope &amp; Rigging diagram 4](../assets/svgs/knots-rigging-4.svg)

**Use:** Joining two ropes of **equal diameter** . Simple, fast, and looks neat. NOT suitable for different rope sizes.

### Sheet Bend

Sheet Bend (Joining Ropes of Different Sizes)

![Knots, Rope &amp; Rigging diagram 5](../assets/svgs/knots-rigging-5.svg)

**Use:** Joining ropes of **different sizes** . The sheet bend uses the larger rope as a "loop" and the smaller rope passes through. Double it for extra security.

### Bowline (King of Knots)

Bowline (Fixed Loop - Won't Slip)

![Knots, Rope &amp; Rigging diagram 6](../assets/svgs/knots-rigging-6.svg)

**Use:** Creates a fixed loop that **cannot slip** regardless of load. Essential for hoisting, securing, and anchor points. Sometimes called the "king of knots."

### Clove Hitch

Clove Hitch (Quick Attachment to Pole)

![Knots, Rope &amp; Rigging diagram 7](../assets/svgs/knots-rigging-7.svg)

**Use:** Quick and effective attachment to posts, poles, or rings. Two wraps cross each other. Easy to tie and untie. Can slip if not under tension.

### Two Half Hitches

Two Half Hitches (Securing to Ring/Post)

![Knots, Rope &amp; Rigging diagram 8](../assets/svgs/knots-rigging-8.svg)

**Use:** Secure way to attach rope to a ring, hook, or post. Two sequential wraps around the standing line. Very reliable under load.

### Taut-Line Hitch (Adjustable Loop)

Taut-Line Hitch (Adjustable Tension)

![Knots, Rope &amp; Rigging diagram 9](../assets/svgs/knots-rigging-9.svg)

**Use:** **Adjustable friction loop** for tent guy-lines. Slides freely when you want to adjust tension, but locks solid under load. Essential for camping.

### Timber Hitch

Timber Hitch (Dragging Logs)

![Knots, Rope &amp; Rigging diagram 10](../assets/svgs/knots-rigging-10.svg)

**Use:** Ideal for securing and dragging logs or heavy cylindrical objects. The wrapping grips tighter as you pull. Simple but very effective.

### Trucker's Hitch (Mechanical Advantage)

Trucker's Hitch (3:1 Mechanical Advantage)

![Knots, Rope &amp; Rigging diagram 11](../assets/svgs/knots-rigging-11.svg)

**Use:** Provides **3:1 mechanical advantage** for tensioning loads with one-third the force. Used for securing cargo, tightening lines, and vehicle recovery.

## Binding & Lashing Knots

Lashing techniques join poles and timber together to create structures. These are fundamental to shelter building, bridges, and scaffolding.

### Square Lashing (4 Poles at 90°)

Square Lashing Pattern (Top View)

![Knots, Rope &amp; Rigging diagram 12](../assets/svgs/knots-rigging-12.svg)

1
**Start:** Take-up around one pole with clove hitch
2
**First pass:** Wrap around both poles 3-4 times (going over first pole, under second)
3
**Second pass:** Make diagonal wraps (frapping turns) to tighten
4
**Finish:** Clove hitch to secure the end

**Use:** Joining two poles at **right angles** (90°). Creates very strong joint for building frameworks, towers, and scaffolding. Most common lashing type.

### Diagonal Lashing (Angles Other than 90°)

**Use:** Joining poles at **acute or obtuse angles** (not 90°). Used for X-frame structures and angled supports. Rope wraps in spiral pattern between poles.

### Shear Lashing (Joining Parallel Poles)

**Use:** Tying two parallel poles together (e.g., creating a wider base). Rope spirals around both poles together, creating a unified structure. Common in tripod construction.

### Tripod Lashing

Tripod Lashing (Three Poles)

![Knots, Rope &amp; Rigging diagram 13](../assets/svgs/knots-rigging-13.svg)

**Use:** Lashing three or more poles at their apex to form a tripod or pole stand. Foundation for camp structures, drying racks, and portable shelters.

<section id="fishing-trapping">

## Fishing & Trapping Knots

### Improved Clinch Knot

Improved Clinch Knot (Hook Attachment)

![Knots, Rope &amp; Rigging diagram 14](../assets/svgs/knots-rigging-14.svg)

**Use:** Strong, reliable knot for attaching fishing line to hooks and lures. 5-7 wraps of line around hook shank. Rated at 95%+ of line strength.

### Palomar Knot

**Use:** Very strong and simple fishing knot. Double the line, loop through hook, tie overhand knot, pass hook through loop. Easier than clinch knot, nearly as strong.

### Surgeon's Knot (Joining Lines)

**Use:** Join two fishing lines of different weights. Essentially a double overhand knot with both line ends. Holds well even with disparate line weights.

### Snare Loop (Trapping)

Snare Loop (Fixed Loop for Trapping)

![Knots, Rope &amp; Rigging diagram 15](../assets/svgs/knots-rigging-15.svg)

**Use:** Creates a fixed loop with a slip knot for trapping small game. Loop is set at animal level along a run. When animal passes through, it tightens around the neck or body.

<section id="splicing">

## Splicing Techniques

Splices are permanent joints that are **stronger than knots** because they don't create stress points like knots do. Use this section when the practical question is "how do I make the eye, join, or finish this rope?" rather than "what rope should I buy or is this rope still serviceable?" For rope condition, retirement signs, or strength planning before you splice, see [Rope and Cable Utilities](/guides/rope-cable-utilities). For hauling and tie-down jobs where maximum strength or smooth passage through hardware matters, choose a splice when the task allows it.

### Why Splices Are Superior to Knots

:::info-box
-   **Stronger:** Splice retains 90-100% of rope strength. A knot reduces strength to 50-70%.
-   **Smoother:** No bulky knob reduces friction through pulleys and fairleads.
-   **Neater:** Blends seamlessly into rope line.
-   **No twisting:** Unlike laid rope knots, splices don't cause rotation.
:::

### Eye Splice (3-Strand Rope)

Eye Splice Tuck Pattern

![Knots, Rope &amp; Rigging diagram 16](../assets/svgs/knots-rigging-16.svg)

1
**Unlay:** Untwist the three strands about 6 inches
2
**Form eye:** Bend the rope to form desired loop size
3
**First tuck:** Tuck one strand under a strand of the main rope
4
**Continue tucking:** Work the three strands around, each under the next main rope strand
5
**Complete passes:** Typically need 3 full passes (9 individual tucks)

**Use:** Creates a permanent eye loop at the end of a rope. Used for anchor points, rigging attachment, and mooring. Can be worked by hand without special tools.

### Short Splice (Joining Two Ropes)

**Use:** Joins two ropes end-to-end with a permanent joint. Both ropes are unlaid 6 inches and interlaced, with strands tucked under opposing strands. Creates a slightly thicker joint than either rope alone.

### Back Splice (Finishing Rope Ends)

**Use:** Prevents a rope end from fraying by tucking strands back along the rope. Creates a tapered, neat finish. Takes three complete passes. Less bulk than whipping.

## Block & Tackle Systems

Block and tackle systems use pulleys to create mechanical advantage, allowing you to lift or pull heavy loads with less force.

### Understanding Mechanical Advantage

Single, Double, and Triple Pulley Systems

![Knots, Rope &amp; Rigging diagram 17](../assets/svgs/knots-rigging-17.svg)

### Block and Tackle Rigging

1
**Fixed pulley:** Attached to overhead anchor (roof beam, tree branch, or tripod)
2
**Moving pulley(s):** Attached to the load being lifted
3
**Rope routing:** Pass rope through pulleys following the desired mechanical advantage configuration
4
**Tie off:** Secure the standing end of rope (the non-pulling end) to prevent load dropping
5
**Pull gradually:** Maintain tension while lifting. Use 4:1 safety factor (divide max load by 4)

## Net Making

Nets are useful for fishing, game capture, and storage. Traditional net-making uses simple knots to create mesh patterns.

### Sheet Bend Netting Technique

Net Mesh Pattern (Square Netting)

![Knots, Rope &amp; Rigging diagram 18](../assets/svgs/knots-rigging-18.svg)

### Mesh Gauge (Determining Mesh Size)

Mesh size is measured as the **distance between knots** when the net is laid flat:

-   **Fine mesh (1-2 inch):** For small fish, shrimp, bait capture
-   **Medium mesh (2-4 inch):** General-purpose fishing
-   **Coarse mesh (4-6 inch or larger):** Large fish, game capture

### Casting Net

A circular casting net is thrown to spread over fish, then retrieved. Traditional design uses radial mesh emanating from center, with a weighted perimeter.

-   Made from 12-20 feet of 15-25 pound test line
-   Diameter when spread: 4-8 feet
-   Sheet bend knots connect the mesh throughout
-   Lead weights sewn into the perimeter edge
-   Rope hand line attached to center (wrist strap)

### Gill Net

A gill net hangs vertically in water. Fish swim in but can't back out because gills catch the mesh.

-   Top line floats; bottom line anchored
-   Mesh size selected for target fish species
-   Length varies from 25-1000 feet depending on application
-   Depth typically 4-8 feet for most freshwater use

### Hammock Construction

A basic hammock is an excellent net-making project for beginners:

1
Create a continuous net rectangle approximately 5 feet x 8 feet using sheet bend netting technique
2
Attach a rope header line along the two short ends (reinforced suspension line)
3
Create suspension points (typically 2 per end, rated for 400+ lbs each)
4
Attach carabiners or rope loops to suspension anchor points
5
Hang with approximately 30° angle from anchor points for optimal comfort

## Whipping & Seizing

Whipping prevents rope ends from unraveling. Seizing binds ropes together. Both are traditional maritime skills.

### Common Whipping (Preventing Rope Fraying)

Whipping Thread Pattern (End View)

![Knots, Rope &amp; Rigging diagram 19](../assets/svgs/knots-rigging-19.svg)

1
**Prepare:** Cut rope straight across. Use heavy thread or light line for whipping.
2
**Start:** Lay a loop of thread alongside the rope end
3
**Wrap:** Wrap thread tightly around rope, going from rope toward the end (toward the frayed fibers)
4
**Continue:** Make 5-10 tight wraps, depending on rope diameter. Final wrap should reach close to rope end.
5
**Finish:** Thread the end through the loop and pull tight to lock the wraps in place

**Use:** Prevents the end of a laid rope from unwinding and fraying. Essential maintenance. Takes 5-10 minutes per rope end. Dramatically extends rope life.

If the rope is already badly abraded, softened, heat-damaged, chemically damaged, or otherwise suspect, whipping is not a substitute for retirement; check the inspection and retirement guidance in [Rope and Cable Utilities](/guides/rope-cable-utilities).

### West Country Whipping (Faster Variation)

An alternative whipping technique using figure-eight wraps instead of spiral wraps. Slightly less refined but faster to apply. Uses the same thread and concept.

### Seizing (Binding Two Ropes Together)

**Use:** Permanently binds two ropes together side-by-side. Similar to whipping but applied around two ropes instead of one rope end. Creates a strong, neat joint that's narrower than splicing.

<section id="practical-applications">

## Practical Rigging Applications

### Raising a Ridgepole (Simple A-Frame Shelter)

1
**Prepare poles:** Select two straight poles 12-16 feet long. Lay parallel on ground.
2
**Create tripod:** Lash three shorter poles together at apex using tripod lashing. Create two tripod sets.
3
**Set tripods:** Space tripods 12 feet apart, standing upright.
4
**Raise ridgepole:** Use block and tackle (2:1 or 3:1 system) to hoist the ridgepole between the two tripod apexes.
5
**Secure:** Lash ridgepole firmly to both tripods with square lashing. Remove rope from block and tackle.
6
**Add rafters:** Lean poles against ridgepole and lash them. Cover with bark, cloth, or thatch.

### Hauling Heavy Logs

-   **Timber hitch:** Secure one end of rope to log with timber hitch. Rope grips tighter as you pull.
-   **Multiple ropes:** For larger logs, use 2-3 timber hitches spaced along the length
-   **Clearing ground:** Clear obstacles before dragging. Use block and tackle for mechanical advantage if needed.
-   **Team pulling:** Distribute force among multiple people. Use trucker's hitch for ratchet-style tensioning.

### River Crossing (Tyrolean Traverse)

A Tyrolean traverse uses a rope suspended across a river to move personnel and supplies across.

1
**Anchor upstream:** Secure a rope upstream on both banks using bowlines and tree anchors (5:1 safety minimum)
2
**Main rope:** String a second rope from bank to bank with slight downstream sag. This is the traversing line.
3
**Safety line:** Attach a backup safety rope along the main line for redundancy.
4
**Pulley rig:** Person or cargo crosses on a pulley/carabiner assembly clipped to the main rope.
5
**Haul across:** Pull person across using a separate haul line. Maintain constant tension on safety line.

### Clothesline Rigging

-   **Anchor points:** Trees, posts, or purpose-built anchors on both ends
-   **Tension:** Use trucker's hitch to maintain constant tension. Allows for clothesline that won't sag even when fully loaded.
-   **Height:** Install at 5-6 feet height for comfortable hanging and clearing ground
-   **Maintenance:** Inspect regularly for fraying. Rewhip rope ends annually.

**Multiple contact points:** Secure load at minimum 4 points (front, rear, each side)
2
**Trucker's hitch:** Use 3:1 mechanical advantage for tensioning
3
**Check tension:** Each rope should require firm push with thumb to depress (not too loose, not crushing)
4
**Cross-brace:** Use diagonal ropes from corner to corner to prevent load shifting
5
**Inspection:** Stop every 50-100 miles (or equivalent) to verify tension is still adequate

### Improvised Stretcher

Creating a stretcher for transporting an injured person using rope and poles:

-   **Poles:** Two sturdy poles 6-8 feet long, 2-3 feet apart
-   **Lashing:** Square lash 5-6 cross-pieces perpendicular to the poles
-   **Canvas:** Stretch cloth or tarp over the frame and lash securely
-   **Handles:** Create rope handles at each end for carrying
-   **Safety:** Secure the patient with additional rope to prevent falling

### Rope Bridge Basics

A simple suspension rope bridge requires substantial engineering and safety consideration:

-   **Cables:** Main support cables must be extremely strong, rated at 10:1 safety minimum
-   **Anchor points:** Both banks must have unquestionably solid anchor points (large trees, rock anchors, dead-man anchors)
-   **Decking:** Create a walkway using lashed poles or boards beneath the cables
-   **Handrails:** Essential for safety. Rope railings on both sides.
-   **Sag:** Allow slight sag (5-10%) in main cables for load distribution
-   **Testing:** Test thoroughly with sandbag loads equal to expected human load before use

## Net Making for Fishing and Cargo

### Overview

Nets have been essential tools for millennia. Fishing nets, cargo nets, and seine nets can be constructed entirely by hand using cordage and basic tying techniques.

### Basic Net Mesh (Square Knot Method)

#### Materials

-   **Cordage:** Natural fiber rope (3-6mm diameter suitable for fishing nets), twisted or braided
-   **Netting needle:** Wooden shuttle or carved needle with eye large enough for rope
-   **Frame:** Two wooden stakes or dowels spaced to desired net width
-   **Twine:** Thin cordage for spacing nodes during construction

#### Net Knot (Clove Hitch Based)

Each mesh is created using a modified clove hitch, tightening a loop around a spacer, then sliding to next position.

#### Construction Steps (Hand Method)

1.  **Create foundation:** Tie a series of knots along a cord stretched between two stakes (foundation row). Space knots 2-4 inches apart depending on desired mesh size.
2.  **Loop and knot:** Take netting needle with cordage. Create a loop hanging below the foundation. Knot the loop around a spacer (wooden dowel same diameter as desired mesh hole).
3.  **Advance horizontally:** Move to next foundation knot. Create loop, secure around spacer, tighten.
4.  **Repeat vertically:** Complete one row across the width. Move spacer to next row beneath completed row.
5.  **Continue building:** Each new row is knotted to loops of previous row, creating mesh. Net grows downward from foundation.
6.  **Desired length:** Continue until net reaches desired depth. Knot and trim remaining cordage.

#### Mesh Spacer Sizes

-   **Small mesh (1/2 inch spacer):** Catches small fish and aquatic insects. Useful for seine nets.
-   **Medium mesh (1-2 inch spacer):** General fishing nets, balanced for various fish sizes
-   **Large mesh (3-6 inch spacer):** Cargo nets, coarse fishing nets, trap bags

### Seine Net Construction

A seine net is a large fishing net with weighted bottom and floating top, used to surround fish and draw them in.

#### Components

-   **Net bag:** Central mesh pocket where fish are trapped (50-100 feet long, 10-20 feet deep)
-   **Wings:** Tapered mesh on each side, extending from net bag to leads
-   **Lead line:** Weighted line (bottom), keeps net bottom on lake/river bottom
-   **Float line:** Buoyant line (top), keeps upper edge at water surface
-   **Lead weights:** Attached to bottom line at regular intervals (3-6 feet apart)
-   **Floats:** Cork or wood floats attached to top line, spacing determines water depth net can fish

#### Operating a Seine Net

1.  **Deployment:** Two people wade with boat, rowing/dragging net in semicircle perpendicular to shore
2.  **Encirclement:** Complete circle around fish school, bringing both ends back to shore
3.  **Retrieval:** Pull both ends toward shore. Weights keep bottom closed, floats keep opening above water.
4.  **Purse:** As net comes ashore, the mesh bag naturally purses (closes), trapping fish
5.  **Collection:** Fish are collected from mesh bag, net is reset for next cast

### Fish Trap (Fyke Net)

#### Simple Box Trap Design

-   **Frame:** Wooden box frame, 12 x 12 x 24 inches
-   **Mesh:** Net covering box opening, with funnel-shaped entrance narrowing to 2-inch hole
-   **Bait chamber:** Separate compartment inside trap holds bait (fish scraps, grain)
-   **Door:** Hinged or removable door for fish collection
-   **Anchor:** Trap anchored to shore or river bottom with rope

#### Operation

-   **Placement:** Set in river channel or along shoreline where fish naturally travel
-   **Bait refresh:** Replace bait daily to maintain attractiveness
-   **Collection:** Check traps twice daily (dawn and dusk are peak activity times)
-   **Catch:** Fish enter through funnel but rarely find exit, accumulating in trap

### Hammock Construction

#### Basic Design

A hammock is a suspended bed using rope or webbing for support and comfort. Easy to construct from available materials.

#### Simple Rope Hammock

-   **Frame:** Two anchor points (trees, posts) 12-15 feet apart
-   **Support ropes:** Two lengths of strong rope (1/2 inch diameter minimum), each 20-30 feet long
-   **Sleeping bed:** Woven fabric or canvas (6 x 9 feet) tied to support ropes at multiple points
-   **Height:** Ropes angle 30° from horizontal (not vertical) to reduce stress on anchor points
-   **Sag:** Allow 18-24 inches sag in middle when occupied—too tight stresses ropes, too much sag uncomfortable

#### Anchor Requirements

-   **Tree selection:** Healthy trees at least 6 inches diameter
-   **Height:** Anchor 5-7 feet up the tree to angle ropes properly
-   **Wrapping:** Never use knots that cut bark. Use webbing straps wrapped around tree with knots on strap, not bark.
-   **Safety factor:** Anchors must support 5x the hammock weight (person + hammock = 200-250 lbs, anchor must support 1000+ lbs)

#### Step-by-Step Construction

1.  **Cut fabric:** Heavy canvas or linen, 6 feet x 9 feet. Reinforce corners with extra stitching (stress points)
2.  **Create corner loops:** Sew reinforced corner attachment points (metal D-rings or heavy stitched loops)
3.  **Thread support ropes:** Pass support rope through each corner loop, creating two main support points per rope
4.  **Create ridge support:** Mid-point attachments along the length (every 18 inches) for weight distribution
5.  **Angle lines to anchors:** Rope should angle downward 30° from horizontal to anchor points
6.  **Secure to anchors:** Use bowline or other secure knots to attachment points on trees or posts
7.  **Test:** Add weight gradually, check rope tension and anchor stability before full use

### Cargo Net Construction

#### Grid Pattern Net

A square-mesh cargo net with 2-4 inch holes carries heavy loads, distributes weight across multiple rope strands.

#### Basic Design

-   **Dimensions:** 10 x 10 feet common size, 2-inch mesh
-   **Rope diameter:** 3/4 inch diameter rope, strong twisted or braided construction
-   **Perimeter:** Heavy border rope around all edges (1 inch diameter) provides structural integrity
-   **Corner loops:** Large rope loops at four corners for tie-down or lifting
-   **Weight capacity:** 500-1000 lbs depending on rope quality and knot integrity

#### Knot Pattern for Cargo Net

Each mesh intersection uses a double half hitch or modified clove hitch, creating secure locking between strands.

#### Applications

-   **Cargo transport:** Securing loads on carts or wagons
-   **Climbing:** Vertical cargo nets for training or climbing (low impact)
-   **Fencing:** Temporary enclosures for animals or garden protection
-   **Drying:** Hanging food or materials for air-drying (high airflow)
-   **Storage:** Wall-mounted nets for tool or equipment organization
