## Overview

While hand-held flaked stone tools (described in the stone-tools guide) are effective for cutting and basic processing, hafting—attaching a stone blade to a handle—dramatically increases tool versatility and efficiency. A hafted axe can fell trees; a hafted spear can hunt large game; a hafted scraper can process hides. This guide covers hafting principles, composite tool construction, specialized tool forms (axes, chisels, harpoons, hooks, and specialized hunting implements), and maintenance techniques to extend tool life.

Composite tools represent a significant evolutionary step in tool technology. A carefully constructed hafted tool can outlast the user when properly maintained and can be repaired when damaged, allowing a single tool to remain in service for years or decades.

## Hafting Principles and Materials

Hafting attaches a stone blade or point to a handle (haft), typically made of wood, bone, or antler. The connection must be secure, resilient to impact, and maintainable.

### Hafting Methods

**Adhesive hafting (glue or resin only):**
- Stone blade is glued into a slot or socket on the handle
- Fastest method but least durable long-term
- Works best for lighter tools; often supplemented by binding

**Mechanical hafting (binding with cordage):**
- Stone blade is lashed to the handle using plant fiber cordage or sinew
- Strong, resilient, and repairable
- Allows the blade to flex slightly (absorbs impact)
- Takes more time but produces durable tools

**Combination hafting (glue plus binding):**
- Stone is glued and then bound with cordage
- Most effective long-term approach
- Combines the quick setup of glue with the durability of binding

### Adhesive Materials

**Plant-based adhesives:**
- **Birch tar/pitch:** Boiled birch bark yields a tar that hardens on cooling; excellent grip, waterproof
- **Pine resin:** Raw or slightly heated pine sap; sets hard but brittle
- **Tree sap and gums:** Various tree exudates; effectiveness varies by species
- **Starch paste:** Boiled plant material (grains, tubers) creates temporary adhesive; requires binding to hold permanently

**Animal-based adhesives:**
- **Hide glue:** Boiled animal hide or bone collagen; strong when set but water-soluble
- **Hoof/horn glue:** Similar to hide glue but from harder tissues
- **Fish glue:** Boiled fish skin; effective but has strong odor

**Mixing adhesives:** Many cultures combined glues with fine mineral powders (sand, ash) to extend coverage and improve grip.

### Handle (Haft) Materials and Design

**Wood:**
- **Best for hafts:** Hardwoods with straight grain (ash, hickory, oak) are strongest; softwoods (pine, spruce) are easier to shape
- **Haft shapes:** Tapered to fit the user's hand; narrower grip, wider upper section for blade mounting
- **Durability:** Hardens with age; lasts decades with maintenance

**Bone and antler:**
- **Advantages:** Very hard; naturally strong; don't rot; easy to carve fine details
- **Disadvantages:** More brittle than wood; require careful hafting to avoid breaking at stress points
- **Common:** Split and straightened antler shafts (thin, strong, uniform)

**Handle Construction:**
1. Haft length depends on tool purpose: 20–30 cm for hand tools (chisels, small axes); 50–150 cm for hunting spears and larger axes
2. Grip diameter: 20–30 mm for comfortable hand fit
3. Blade-mounting section: tapered or with a socket to receive the stone blade
4. Balance: the blade weight should be near the front, with the grip balanced in the hand

### Socket vs. Slot Design

**Socket (end-mounted blade):**
- Stone blade sits in a carved or drilled socket at the end of the haft
- Good for axes, chisels, adzes (with blade perpendicular to haft)
- Handles high impact forces; blade can be replaced

**Slot (side-mounted blade):**
- Stone blade sits in a slit along the side of the haft
- Good for scrapers, knives (blade parallel to haft length)
- Less impact resistance but good for precision work

**Barbed slot (specialized for harpoons):**
- Multiple barbed stone blades arranged around a bone harpoon shaft
- Creates a multi-pronged spear head that holds prey
- Blades tied with cordage to allow flex and prevent loss

## Specialized Axes and Chopping Tools

The hafted axe is one of the most significant tools in human history, enabling tree felling and wood processing at scale.

### Hand Axe (Small Chopping Axe)

**Specifications:**
- Blade weight: 300–700 g (roughly the size of a fist)
- Haft length: 20–35 cm
- Haft material: hardwood (ash, hickory)
- Blade material: stone (basalt, granite, or high-quality flint)

**Blade shape:**
- One flat side (resting against the haft)
- One curved or beveled side (the striking edge)
- Cutting edge runs perpendicular to the haft
- Blade width: 4–6 cm at the widest point

**Hafting method:**
1. Carve a socket at the haft end, slightly larger than the blade's thickness
2. Insert the blade and secure with hide glue
3. Bind tightly with cordage (sinew or plant fiber), wrapping around the blade and haft multiple times
4. The binding should be tight enough to prevent rotation or sliding

**Applications:**
- Chopping small branches and saplings
- Processing firewood
- Basic carpentry
- Food preparation

### Large Felling Axe

**Specifications:**
- Blade weight: 1–2 kg
- Haft length: 60–80 cm (long enough for two-handed swing)
- Blade material: stone (basalt strongly preferred) or iron (if available)
- Balance: critical; blade should not be so heavy that the tool feels unwieldy

**Blade shape:**
- Wider than the hand axe (6–8 cm)
- Cutting edge is slightly curved (makes contact at the bottom of the swing)
- Bevel angles: 20–30° on each side of the blade (creates a sharp, durable edge)

**Hafting:**
- Similar to hand axe but with a more pronounced socket to handle the greater impact forces
- Binding must be very tight; reinforce with multiple wraps
- Some cultures used a wooden or bone wedge driven through the haft above the blade to prevent slipping

**Two-handed swing technique:**
1. Hands grip the haft near the end (both hands, 20–30 cm apart)
2. Raise the axe overhead with a smooth motion
3. Swing downward in a wide arc, letting gravity and momentum do the work
4. Strike with the lower part of the blade edge (optimal impact)
5. Repeat; don't force the blade; let each swing do clean work

:::warning
Large axes require significant strength and coordination. Practice on dead wood first; avoid swinging near others. An out-of-control swing can cause serious injury. Wear footwear and keep hands and feet clear of the cutting line.
:::

### Adze (Side-Cutting Axe)

An adze is similar to an axe but with the blade perpendicular to the shaft (blade to the side).

**Specifications:**
- Blade weight: 200–600 g
- Haft length: 25–40 cm
- Blade orientation: perpendicular to haft

**Use:**
- Hollowing wood (canoe carving, bucket making)
- Shaving wood surfaces
- Removing bark
- Detail woodwork

**Technique:**
- Stand with feet shoulder-width apart
- Blade points toward you; swing down and forward in a scooping motion
- Creates a peeling action rather than a chopping action

## Specialized Hunting and Fishing Implements

Hunting tools require precision, balance, and reliable attachment mechanisms. A poorly hafted spear head or arrow point can fail at a critical moment.

### Harpoon with Detachable Head

A harpoon (particularly for fishing or water hunting) uses a detachable head to allow the rope or cord to trail behind, preventing the prey from escaping.

**Components:**
1. **Shaft:** Long pole (wood, 1–2 meters, 2–3 cm diameter)
2. **Head:** Bone or stone, barbed or multi-pointed
3. **Fastening:** The head is attached via a socket, but held by a single line of cordage
4. **Trailing rope:** 10–50 meters of strong cordage attached to the head via a loop
5. **Mechanism:** When the harpoon head strikes prey and embeds, the shaft often detaches (held only by friction), while the rope prevents the prey from diving or swimming away

**Multi-pronged head design:**
- Three to five bone or stone points arranged around a central bone shaft
- Each point barbed (curved hook at the tip)
- Points hold the prey in the spaces between them
- Shaft: bone or wood, 15–20 cm long

**Assembly:**
1. Carve a socket in the end of the main haft
2. Insert the harpoon head and secure with glue and binding
3. Attach the trailing rope to a ring or hole in the head (not the shaft)
4. Test: shaft should separate easily from head when pulled; head should remain attached to rope

### Atlatl (Spear-Thrower)

An atlatl is a lever mechanism that extends the human arm, allowing a spear to be thrown with greater force and distance than hand-thrown.

**Components:**
1. **Handle:** 30–40 cm hardwood, carved for comfortable grip
2. **Shaft:** 15–20 cm long, narrower (3 cm diameter)
3. **Hook:** At the end of the shaft, a hook (carved, bone, or stone) that catches the spear butt
4. **Finger loops:** Woven cordage loops on the handle for grip stability

**Operation:**
1. Place the spear butt into the atlatl hook
2. Grip the handle with fingers in the loops
3. Raise the atlatl to shoulder height, arm bent
4. Extend arm and snap forward in a single motion (straightening the elbow powerfully)
5. This lever action accelerates the spear to much higher velocity than hand-throwing

**Advantages:** Longer range; greater force; less arm fatigue for sustained hunting
**Disadvantages:** Requires practice for accuracy; difficult in confined spaces

### Specialized Arrow and Dart Points

Stone or bone arrow points must be small, sharp, and securely hafted to a shaft.

**Socket hafting (most common):**
- The shaft (usually wood, 1–2 cm diameter) is sharpened or has a socket carved at the end
- The point is inserted into this socket
- Adhesive and binding (sinew wrap) secure it permanently
- Sinew dries and shrinks, creating a very tight bond

**Functional designs:**
- **Broadhead:** Wide, flat blade (2–3 cm wide); for large game or fishing
- **Leaf-shaped:** Symmetrical point; good for general hunting
- **Barbed:** Angled barbs prevent withdrawal; for fish or small game
- **Blunt:** Rounded point; for small game and birds (minimizes penetration, allowing reuse)

### Fish Hooks and Fishing Implements

Stone or bone hooks are carved with a sharp curve and barb.

**Hook design:**
1. Material: bone or antler (carved into a curve with a sharp inward barb)
2. Size: varies; large hooks (3–4 cm) for fish; small hooks (1 cm) for minnows
3. Attachment: drilled hole for cordage, or a notch with a tie-on point
4. Sharpness: barb must be sharp enough to penetrate fish skin when weight of the fish hooks it against the barb

**Multi-hook fishing line:**
- Main line: strong cordage (2–3 mm diameter)
- Droppers: short lines (15–20 cm) attached at 20–30 cm intervals
- Hooks: one hook at the end of each dropper
- Bait: small fish or flesh attached to each hook
- Use: cast into the water and wait; as fish bite, multiple hooks can be set simultaneously

:::info-box
**Hook geometry is critical:** The inner angle of the hook (between the shaft and the curve) should be 60–90°. Too tight and it won't work; too loose and the fish can slip free. Test your hook design before relying on it for food.
:::

## Chisels and Detail Working Tools

Fine woodworking and bone carving require sharp, precisely hafted chisels and burins (engraving tools).

### Chisel Design

**Specifications:**
- Blade width: 1–3 cm (depending on task)
- Blade thickness: 5–10 mm
- Handle length: 10–20 cm
- Hafting: socket in the handle, binding with cordage

**Blade shapes:**
- **Flat chisel:** Straight edge perpendicular to the handle; for general work
- **Gouged chisel:** Curved cutting edge; for carving concave shapes
- **Pointed chisel:** Sharp point; for engraving and detail work

**Hafting method:**
1. Carve a tapered socket in the handle end
2. Insert the blade and secure with glue
3. Wrap cordage tightly around the socket area (2–3 wraps, very tight)
4. Test by striking gently with a wooden mallet; blade should not move

### Using Chisels Safely

1. Always work with the blade angled away from your body
2. Use a wooden mallet (never a stone hammer; they shatter)
3. Keep both hands on the tool; remove one hand only after completing the stroke
4. Work on a stable surface (piece of wood in a vise, or held by a partner)
5. Sharpen regularly; a dull chisel is more dangerous (requires excessive force)

## Tool Maintenance and Repair

Well-maintained tools outlast their makers. Regular care keeps tools sharp, secure, and reliable.

### Edge Maintenance

**Sharpening stone tools:**
- Use a finer-grained stone to rub along the blade edge
- Maintain the original bevel angle (20–30° for axes; 30–45° for scrapers)
- Periodic light sharpening maintains the edge better than waiting until the tool is very dull
- Obsidian and high-quality flint can be resharpened many times

**When sharpening won't work:**
- If the blade is very dull or damaged, you may need to reknap it (flake a new edge)
- This requires skill; it's often better to replace the blade entirely

### Handle Repairs

**Crack in the handle:**
- Small cracks may be sealed with resin or glue; wrap with cordage for reinforcement
- Large cracks may require replacing the entire handle (remove blade, carve new handle, re-haft)

**Loose blade:**
- Tighten binding by adding additional cordage wraps
- If binding is deteriorated, carefully remove old cordage, clean the socket, reglue, and rebind

**Splintered handle:**
- Shave smooth with a knife or scraper
- If the damage is structural, repair may require removing the blade and regrafting

### Blade Replacement

**When a blade becomes irreparably dull or broken:**
1. Carefully remove the old binding (cut cordage, scrape glue)
2. Clean the socket area thoroughly
3. Carve or select a new blade of similar size and shape
4. Apply glue to socket and blade
5. Insert blade fully and allow glue to cure (varies by adhesive type)
6. Bind tightly with fresh cordage
7. Test carefully before using in demanding applications

### Storage

- Keep tools in a dry location (damp promotes rust and glue deterioration)
- Store blades horizontally or point-down in a protected sheath
- Inspect regularly for loose bindings or glue deterioration
- Keep spares of frequently-used tools (blades break in use; having a backup prevents work delays)

## Troubleshooting and Tool Failure

**Problem: Blade rotates in the socket during use**
- Cause: Insufficient binding; glue failure
- Fix: Retighten cordage binding; if glue is failed, rehaft entirely

**Problem: Blade is dull and sharpening doesn't help**
- Cause: Edge is chipped or damaged; resharpening won't restore full sharpness
- Fix: If minor chips, rework edge carefully; if severe damage, consider blade replacement

**Problem: Handle breaks during use**
- Cause: Wood defect; excessive force; repeated impacts
- Fix: Inspect before next use; if the break is clean, you may glue and bind it; if the wood is splintered or cracked, replace the handle

**Problem: Binding becomes loose over time**
- Cause: Normal shrinkage; cordage deterioration; moisture swelling/shrinking
- Fix: Periodically retighten binding (every 6–12 months); replace cordage if frayed or weakened

:::tip
Tool care is an ongoing practice. Spend 15–30 minutes weekly inspecting and maintaining tools. This prevents failures during critical work and keeps your tools sharp and reliable for years or decades of use.
:::

## Conclusion

Hafted and composite tools represent a major leap in capability over hand-held flakes. A well-constructed hafted axe or spear extends human reach and strength dramatically. The key to success is using high-quality materials (particularly stone), careful and secure hafting, and consistent maintenance. Master these techniques, and you'll have access to tools capable of felling trees, hunting large game, and processing materials at scale—transforming your ability to survive and thrive in a low-technology environment.
