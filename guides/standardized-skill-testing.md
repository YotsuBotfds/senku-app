## 1\. Overview

In collapse conditions, incompetent practitioners cause harm: medical mistakes kill patients, engineering failures cause injuries, failed food preservation causes illness. Formal certification systems verify competency and protect communities from danger.

This guide covers designing and administering standardized competency exams, establishing certification levels, managing credentials, and preventing fraud. These systems enable communities to identify capable practitioners and prevent unqualified people from performing critical roles. Certification works in tandem with <a href="../labor-organization.html">labor organization</a> (hiring and assigning certified people to projects) and <a href="../economics-trade.html">economics and trade</a> (compensation and specialization of skilled workers).

### Purpose of This Guide

Learn to:

-   Design competency exams for critical skills
-   Assess both written knowledge and practical ability
-   Establish certification levels and standards
-   Issue and verify credentials
-   Manage continuing education requirements
-   Prevent credential fraud and imposters
-   Document competency for community decision-making


<section id="why-certification">

## 2\. Why Certification Matters (Preventing Harm from Incompetence)

Without formal systems, people make claims about their knowledge. In critical fields, unverified claims can be deadly:

### Medical Consequences

An unqualified person dispensing medication might cause death through drug interactions, allergic reactions, or overdose. A person claiming to know surgery but lacking knowledge could kill a patient. Without certification, communities can't distinguish between expert healers and dangerous imposters.

### Engineering & Construction Failures

A building designed by someone lacking structural knowledge might collapse, injuring or killing people. Water systems designed by unqualified people cause disease outbreaks. Bridges built by incompetent engineers fail under load. Engineering certification prevents these catastrophes.

### Food Safety Disasters

Improper food preservation causes botulism and other deadly illnesses. Without certification, people preserving food for the community might use methods that create poisonous conditions. Certification ensures food handlers follow proven protocols.

### Educational Impact

Unqualified teachers perpetuate misinformation that harms the next generation. Students who learn incorrect science, math, or technical skills face lifelong disadvantages. Teaching certification ensures educational quality.

### Trust in Critical Roles

People need to trust their medical practitioners, engineers, and food handlers. Without formal certification, trust relies on reputation alone—which can be fraudulent. Certification provides objective verification.

:::tip
Certification is not elitist gatekeeping—it's protection from harm. It ensures people performing critical work have demonstrated competency. In collapse conditions where medical errors are often fatal, this protection is essential.
:::

## 3\. Medical Competency Exams

Medical practitioners must pass standardized exams to verify competency. Testing both knowledge and practical skills prevents dangerous practitioners from treating patients.

### Certification Levels for Medical Practitioners

<table><thead><tr><th scope="col">Level</th><th scope="col">Requirements</th><th scope="col">Scope of Practice</th><th scope="col">Testing Focus</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td><strong>First Aid Provider</strong></td><td>40 hours training + exam</td><td>Wound care, CPR, basic trauma</td><td>Recognition of emergency signs, basic response procedures</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Community Health Worker</strong></td><td>200 hours training + exam</td><td>Common illnesses, health education, basic assessment</td><td>Disease recognition, treatment protocols, when to escalate</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Midwife</strong></td><td>500+ hours training + exam</td><td>Pregnancy care, normal delivery, postpartum</td><td>Pregnancy assessment, delivery procedures, complication recognition</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Herbalist/Pharmacist</strong></td><td>400+ hours training + exam</td><td>Herb identification, medicine preparation, dosing</td><td>Plant identification, active constituents, drug interactions, safety</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Physician/Doctor</strong></td><td>2000+ hours training + comprehensive exam</td><td>Diagnosis, prescription, complex treatment, surgery</td><td>Anatomy, physiology, pathology, pharmacology, all treatment domains</td></tr></tbody></table>

### Medical Competency Exam Structure

**Written Exam (60%):**

-   Multiple choice: Recognition of diseases and appropriate treatment
-   Short answer: Explanation of medical reasoning
-   Case scenarios: Decision-making under realistic conditions
-   Content areas: Anatomy, physiology, disease recognition, treatment protocols, medication knowledge, complication prevention

**Practical Exam (40%):**

-   Patient assessment: Perform physical exam, take history, identify problems
-   Procedures: Demonstrate wound care, blood pressure measurement, injection techniques
-   Case presentations: Diagnose condition from patient presentation, explain treatment reasoning
-   Emergency response: Handle simulated medical emergency with appropriate prioritization

## 4\. Engineering Certification

Engineers design structures, water systems, and mechanical devices. Certification prevents catastrophic failures that injure or kill people.

### Engineering Certification Levels

<table><thead><tr><th scope="col">Certification</th><th scope="col">Training Required</th><th scope="col">Authorized Projects</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td><strong>Builder Apprentice</strong></td><td>500+ hours mentored work</td><td>Simple repairs, assist master builder</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Master Builder</strong></td><td>5000+ hours + advanced exam</td><td>Design and build residential structures</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Structural Engineer</strong></td><td>Complex structures course + exam</td><td>Bridges, public buildings, load-bearing structures</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Water Systems Engineer</strong></td><td>Water engineering course + exam</td><td>Wells, irrigation, sanitation systems</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Mechanical Engineer</strong></td><td>Mechanics + machinery course + exam</td><td>Machinery design, power systems</td></tr></tbody></table>

### Engineer Competency Testing Focus

**Written Exam (50%):**

-   Structural principles: Load distribution, material strength, safety factors
-   Building codes: Community standards for safe construction
-   Materials: Strength, durability, appropriate applications
-   Design scenarios: Design project to specifications, justify design decisions
-   Failure analysis: Identify why structures fail, prevent failures

**Practical Exam (50%):**

-   Design a small structure to given specifications
-   Calculate load-bearing requirements
-   Specify materials and construction methods
-   Prepare drawings with correct proportions and details
-   Defend design choices

### Critical Engineering Exam Questions

STRUCTURAL ENGINEERING EXAM SAMPLES: 1. A wooden bridge must span 20 meters and carry ox cart traffic. - What type of wood? Why? - What's the minimum height of support beams? - How far apart should support posts be? - What could cause bridge failure? - How would you test beam strength? 2. A stone building is cracked. The crack widens over time. - What causes this? - How would you assess severity? - What repairs would you recommend? - When should the building be evacuated? 3. A water cistern made of wood is leaking. - Why might wood leak? - How would you test for leaks? - What repairs are possible? - When should it be replaced? 4. You're asked to design a roof for a building in a region with heavy snow. - What slope (pitch) would you specify? - What support structure is needed? - What materials would you use? - How would you prevent roof collapse? 5. A community wants a dam to create water storage. - What measurements must you take? - How is dam height determined? - What safety concerns exist? - How would you design spillways?

:::tip
Engineering exams should include real community projects. Testing someone's ability to design for local conditions, available materials, and community needs is more valuable than abstract theory.
:::


<section id="trade-testing">

## 5\. Blacksmith & Machinist Certification

Skilled craftspeople (blacksmiths, machinists, carpenters) must demonstrate practical competency. Testing focuses on quality of work and safety knowledge.

### Trade Certification Standards

**Blacksmith/Smith Certification:**

-   3000+ hours mentored apprenticeship
-   Produce working examples: simple tool, hinges, fasteners, repair job
-   Exam: Forge unknown item from specifications without instruction
-   Tool knowledge: Identify materials, temperature by color, techniques
-   Safety: Proper forge management, burn prevention, tool handling

**Machinist Certification:**

-   2000+ hours training + apprenticeship
-   Ability to operate lathe, milling machine, grinder safely
-   Produce working parts: bearing, gear, custom fastener
-   Measurement precision: Use calipers, micrometers, gauges
-   Failure analysis: Identify defective parts, explain causes

## 6\. Teaching Certification

Teachers educate the next generation. Certification ensures teaching competency, knowledge accuracy, and classroom management ability.

### Teacher Certification Requirements

**Primary/Elementary Teacher:**

-   High school equivalency or better
-   400 hours teaching methodology course
-   Competency in: basic math, reading, science, history, practical skills
-   Supervised teaching: 300 hours classroom time under mentor
-   Final exam: Written knowledge + classroom demonstration

**Subject Specialist (Math, Science, Trades, etc.):**

-   Advanced knowledge in subject area (apprenticed or trained)
-   Teaching methodology course (200 hours)
-   Supervised teaching: 200 hours classroom time
-   Content expertise exam + teaching demonstration

Apprenticeships train skilled people through hands-on practice. Establish clear standards so apprentices know what competency they must achieve.

## 8\. Written vs Practical Testing

Different skills require different testing approaches. Understand when written tests are appropriate and when practical demonstration is essential.

### Written Tests Are Best For:

-   **Knowledge assessment:** Does person know facts, principles, procedures?
-   **Decision-making:** Can person reason through complex scenarios?
-   **Systems understanding:** Does person grasp how components fit together?
-   **Diagnostic ability:** Can person identify problems from information?
-   **Safety knowledge:** Does person understand hazards and prevention?

### Practical Tests Are Best For:

-   **Technical skill:** Can person actually perform procedures?
-   **Problem-solving under pressure:** What happens with time constraints?
-   **Quality of work:** Does produced work meet standards?
-   **Safety behavior:** Does person actually work safely, not just know safety?
-   **Tool mastery:** Can person use equipment effectively?

### Effective Test Combinations

<table><thead><tr><th scope="col">Skill Area</th><th scope="col">Written Component</th><th scope="col">Practical Component</th><th scope="col">Weighting</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Medical Care</td><td>Disease recognition, treatment decisions</td><td>Patient assessment, procedures, emergency response</td><td>50/50</td></tr><tr><td>Engineering</td><td>Principles, calculations, design reasoning</td><td>Design project, specification documents</td><td>50/50</td></tr><tr><td>Blacksmithing</td><td>Material knowledge, technique, safety</td><td>Produce objects to specifications</td><td>30/70</td></tr><tr><td>Teaching</td><td>Content knowledge, methodology</td><td>Teach lesson, design curriculum</td><td>50/50</td></tr><tr><td>Food Preservation</td><td>Methods, safety, spoilage prevention</td><td>Preserve food, verify results</td><td>40/60</td></tr></tbody></table>

:::warning
Don't test only knowledge when skill matters. A person might know all food preservation theory but produce spoiled food in practice. Practical results matter more than test answers.
:::

## 9\. Test Design Principles

Well-designed tests accurately measure competency. Poor tests don't distinguish capable from incapable people, endangering communities.

### Testing Best Practices

### 1\. Test What Matters

-   Identify critical competencies for safe practice
-   Test those competencies thoroughly
-   Don't test irrelevant knowledge (e.g., history of medicine on medical competency exam)
-   Focus on judgment and decision-making, not just facts

### 2\. Use Multiple Methods

-   Written test: Assess knowledge and reasoning
-   Practical: Assess actual performance
-   Oral: Assess explanation and judgment
-   Case study: Real-world problem-solving
-   Combination shows complete competency

### 3\. Clear Standards

-   Define what passing looks like before test
-   Publish passing score and grading criteria
-   Don't change standards based on results
-   Maintain consistent standards across test administrations

### 4\. Qualified Examiners

-   Use master practitioners with real expertise
-   Ensure examiners are fair and unbiased
-   Train examiners on evaluation criteria
-   Don't let friendship/family bias affect scores

### 5\. Realistic Conditions

-   Test in actual working conditions when possible
-   Include time pressure (realistic deadlines)
-   Provide tools/equipment that practitioner will actually use
-   Test with realistic problems and scenarios

### Test Security & Fairness

-   **No advance answer sharing:** Maintain test confidentiality
-   **Consistent administration:** All candidates take same test under same conditions
-   **No coaching during test:** Examiner observes without assisting
-   **Double-blind grading:** Examiner doesn't know candidate identity if possible
-   **Documented grades:** Maintain records of test performance

:::note
#### Field Notes: Test Administration

Create detailed grading rubric BEFORE administering test. Example: "Medical exam—wound care assessment: student properly cleans (2 pts), applies appropriate dressing (2 pts), explains infection prevention (1 pt) = 5 points max." Grade all answers using this rubric. This prevents bias and ensures consistency. Document everything.
:::

<section id="fraud-prevention">

## 10\. Preventing Credential Fraud

People sometimes claim credentials they don't have. Implement verification systems to prevent imposters from endangering communities.

### Common Fraud Schemes

-   **False credentials:** Claim to have passed exam without doing so
-   **Inflated experience:** Claim years of experience they don't have
-   **Buying credentials:** Pay someone else to take exam
-   **Credential theft:** Use another person's credential
-   **Outdated credentials:** Practicing with expired or revoked credentials

### Credential Verification Systems

### 1\. Official Certificate

**Requirements:**

-   Issued by recognized certifying authority
-   Contains signature and official seal
-   Unique number for tracking
-   Expiration date (credentials must be renewed)
-   Specific scope of practice listed
-   Date of issuance

### 2\. Credential Registry

Maintain official list of all certified practitioners:

-   Name, credential type, issue date, expiration date
-   Unique credential number
-   Any restrictions or limitations
-   Available for public verification

### Anti-Fraud Measures

-   **Unique credential numbers:** Impossible to forge completely
-   **Official seals:** Difficult to replicate; seal broken if tampered
-   **Expiration dates:** Force periodic renewal; keeps credentials current
-   **Registry database:** Easy to verify by checking official list
-   **Spot checks:** Periodic verification that practitioners claim legitimately
-   **Public access to registry:** Anyone can verify credentials
-   **Penalties for fraud:** Consequences for falsifying credentials

### Credential Revocation

Remove credentials for:

-   Demonstrated incompetence (failed competency re-examination)
-   Harmful practice (injury to patient/community due to negligence)
-   Credential fraud (falsified test, paid for exam, identity fraud)
-   Violation of safety standards (practicing dangerously)
-   Refusal to engage in continuing education

:::warning
Fraudulent credentials endanger lives. Have clear procedures for investigating fraud, revoking false credentials, and preventing imposters from practicing. This is not about punishment—it's about community safety.
:::

<section id="continuing-education">

## 11\. Continuing Education Requirements

Knowledge advances and practitioners become rusty. Require ongoing education to keep credentials current and practitioners competent.

### Continuing Education Framework

### Continuing Education Options

-   **Formal classes:** Workshops, courses, seminars
-   **Self-study:** Reading, research, literature review
-   **Mentoring others:** Teaching apprentices deepens knowledge
-   **Collaborative learning:** Study groups, peer discussion
-   **Practical projects:** Work on complex projects to advance skills
-   **Conference/meetings:** Participate in professional gatherings

### Continuing Education Documentation

-   Require documentation of hours completed
-   Keep record of topic, date, hours, instructor/source
-   Verify completion (certificate from instructor, testimonial from mentor)
-   Submit documentation with credential renewal
-   If insufficient, extend credential renewal deadline or require re-testing

:::tip
Frame continuing education as professional development, not punishment. Practitioners appreciate opportunity to deepen expertise. Make education accessible—online, local, self-paced when possible.

<section id="common-mistakes">

## 12\. Common Mistakes in Skill Testing & Certification

### Test Design Mistakes

-   **Tests too easy:** Everyone passes, certification becomes meaningless. Set high standards.
-   **Tests too theoretical:** Testing knowledge but not practical ability. Include hands-on assessment.
-   **Inconsistent standards:** Some candidates graded strictly, others leniently. Use objective rubrics.
-   **No practical component:** Medical exam with only written test might pass incompetent practitioner. Always test practical skill.

### Administration Mistakes

-   **Biased examiners:** Friends pass easily, outsiders fail harshly. Use objective criteria, blind grading when possible.
-   **Advance answers shared:** Test becomes about memorization, not competency. Keep test confidential.
-   **No written standards:** Standards exist only in examiners' heads, inconsistent. Document all criteria.
-   **No appeal process:** Unfair failures with no recourse breed resentment. Allow appeals with different examiner.

### Verification Mistakes

-   **No registry:** Can't verify who's actually certified. Maintain official list.
-   **Easy to forge certificates:** Fraudulent credentials indistinguishable from real. Use seals, unique numbers.
-   **No expiration dates:** Outdated certifications never expire. Require renewal with re-testing.
-   **No revocation process:** Can't remove incompetent practitioners. Have clear revocation procedures.

### Scope Mistakes

-   **Vague scope of practice:** Unclear what certified person can do. Specify exactly.
-   **Over-certifying:** Giving broad certification for limited training. Match scope to actual competency.
-   **No restrictions:** Medical apprentice certified to do any medical work. Specify limitations.

:::warning
The most dangerous mistake: Passing incompetent people to avoid conflict. A person you deny certification stays safe; an unqualified person with certification harms people. Be willing to fail people for community safety.
:::

## 13\. Conflict Resolution & Appeals

Even well-designed certification systems can produce unfair results. Establish appeals processes and conflict resolution mechanisms to maintain legitimacy and correct errors.

### Why Appeals Matter

A candidate who fails an exam may believe the decision was unfair. Without an appeals process:
- Resentment builds toward certifying authority
- Competent people may be excluded due to examiner bias
- System loses community trust
- Mistakes go uncorrected

Appeals processes protect both the community (preventing unqualified people from practicing) and individuals (preventing unfair exclusion).

### Appeal Procedures

**Right to Appeal:**
- Any candidate may appeal within 30 days of receiving results
- Appeal based on: examiner bias, test administration error, grading error, new evidence of competency
- Appeal does not automatically overturn decision; it triggers review

**Appeal Review Board:**
- Convene panel of 3+ qualified practitioners (different from original examiner)
- Review all test materials and grading
- Interview examiner about grading decisions
- May require re-examination in disputed areas
- Make binding decision within 60 days

**Appeal Grounds (examples):**
- Examiner demonstrated bias against candidate
- Test administered differently than standard procedures
- Grading inconsistent with rubric
- Technical error in scoring
- Evidence of examiner fraud or misconduct

:::info-box
Appeal processes are not "second chances." They correct legitimate errors and prevent abuse. A good appeals system actually strengthens certification credibility by showing fairness and transparency.
:::

### Conflict Resolution Between Practitioners

Certification disputes sometimes arise between practitioners in same field. Example: Two blacksmiths disagree about whether a third person's work meets standards.

**Resolution Options:**
1. **Joint evaluation:** Both practitioners evaluate work together
2. **Independent third-party:** Bring in certified practitioner from another community
3. **Arbitration:** Submit disagreement to certifying authority or council
4. **Community decision:** If system failure is suspected, have community council review process

:::tip
Establish dispute resolution procedures BEFORE conflicts arise. Written procedures prevent ad-hoc decisions and maintain system credibility.
:::

## 14\. Adaptation & System Reform

As communities change and knowledge advances, certification systems must adapt. Build mechanisms for improvement while maintaining standards.

### Reviewing Certification Standards

Every 2-3 years:
- Analyze which certified practitioners succeed and which fail
- Review test pass rates (too high? too low?)
- Survey practitioners about test relevance
- Check for demographic biases (does system unfairly exclude groups?)
- Identify areas where standards are outdated

**Questions to ask:**
- Are we testing what actually matters for safe practice?
- Have new tools or techniques emerged requiring new competencies?
- Are practical requirements realistic for available resources?
- Do certification paths match community needs?
- Have we excluded competent people due to institutional bias?

### Making Changes

**Small adjustments:**
- Add or remove test questions
- Adjust passing scores based on performance data
- Add continuing education requirements
- Update equipment or materials list

**Major revisions:**
- Completely redesign test
- Change certification levels
- Add new credential types
- Establish new prerequisite skills

<!-- SVG-TODO: System adaptation timeline diagram showing regular review cycles -->

:::warning
Avoid power abuse when revising standards. Officials sometimes tighten certification to exclude competitors or reward allies. Base changes on evidence (test data, practitioner feedback) not politics. Publicize changes before implementing and allow transition period.
:::

### Historical Learning

Study how certification systems failed in history:

**Pre-modern guild systems** in medieval Europe:
- Masters artificially limited journeyman advancement to preserve high wages
- Created shortage of skilled people
- Community suffered from lack of practitioners

**Licensing barriers** (various countries):
- Used to exclude women, minorities from professions
- Tests covered irrelevant material to filter out unwanted groups
- Harmed communities by reducing practitioner diversity

**Lessons:**
- Keep certification standards transparent and documented
- Monitor for systematic exclusion of groups
- Focus on actual competency, not family connections or wealth
- Allow multiple pathways to credentialing
- Review decisions for bias

:::info-box
Good certification systems can be corrupted by those holding power. Stay vigilant for function creep—when systems designed for safety become tools for gatekeeping. Regular external audits help prevent this.
:::

## 15\. Case Studies & Historical Examples

### Case Study 1: Medieval Blacksmith Guild System

**Context:** 15th century European cities established guild systems for craftspeople. Masters trained apprentices and controlled market access.

**How it worked:**
- Apprentice works 7 years under master with no pay
- Journeyman works 5 years for wages
- Journeyman must produce "masterpiece" to become master
- Masters control who can work in the city

**Results:**
- High quality work maintained (testing was rigorous)
- Apprentices exploited (long hours, no compensation)
- Shortage of skilled workers (masters limited acceptance to keep wages high)
- Innovation slowed (masters resisted new techniques)
- Women and outsiders excluded systematically

**Lessons:**
- Rigorous testing protects quality
- BUT—systems must balance certification with access
- Power imbalance in apprenticeship led to exploitation
- Limiting practitioner supply harms communities
- Transparent standards and timeframes essential

### Case Study 2: Modern Medical Licensure Failures

**Context:** Medical licensing in some countries became barrier to access rather than quality assurance.

**Problems that emerged:**
- Licensing exams designed to exclude rural/poor practitioners
- Documentation requirements impossible without formal schooling access
- Foreign-trained doctors couldn't practice despite competency
- Resulting doctor shortage in underserved areas
- Deaths from preventable diseases due to lack of practitioners

**Reform solutions:**
- Create multiple pathways to qualification
- Accept alternative training documentation
- Allow competency testing without formal degree
- License community health workers separately
- Focus exams on actual clinical skills

**Lessons:**
- Certification can become tool of exclusion
- Systems must balance safety with access
- Over-restrictive standards harm communities
- Alternative credentialing pathways valuable
- Regular review prevents institutional sclerosis

### Case Study 3: Hypothetical Collapse Scenario

**Scenario:** Community in collapse conditions attempting to establish medical practitioner certification.

**Initial mistakes made:**
- Only certified doctors (from old system) allowed to treat
- Old licenses required on file, not renewed—credentials were based on credentials, not current competency
- Only 3 certified doctors in community of 2,000
- No way to quickly certify competent healers
- People died from treatable conditions

**Corrections implemented:**
- Created new competency exam for traditional healers/midwives
- 40-person emergency medical training program
- Practical testing emphasis (can you actually assess and treat?)
- Tiered certification: first aid providers, community health workers, physician-level
- Continuing education through community meetings

**Results:**
- Medical coverage increased dramatically
- Some deaths from incompetent practitioners, but fewer than before
- System adapted as knowledge increased
- Practitioners mentored each other

**Lessons:**
- Old credentials may not indicate current competency
- Emergency situations require rapid alternative certification
- Multiple credential tiers meet diverse needs
- Practical testing more important than formal credentials
- Community input improves system acceptance

:::note
#### Field Notes: Real-World Implementation

These cases show that certification systems balance competing needs: safety (don't certify incompetent people), access (make credentialing achievable), fairness (transparent standards), and adaptation (change systems as conditions change). No perfect system exists—all require ongoing attention.
:::

### Static Certification Worksheet

When no custom testing form is available, use a plain written record for each candidate. Keep the standard narrow, observable, and tied to the actual work the person will be allowed to perform.

**Minimum record:**
- Candidate name and contact or household identifier
- Skill being tested and certification level requested
- Date, location, and names of examiners
- Required tools, materials, or patients/simulations used
- Written score, practical score, and pass/fail decision
- Any restrictions, supervision requirements, or retest date

**Practical test structure:**
- State the task and safety limits before the test begins
- Require the candidate to explain hazards and stop conditions
- Observe the candidate performing the task, not only describing it
- Score against a checklist agreed on before testing
- Record critical failures separately from minor mistakes
- Give the candidate written feedback and an appeal path

For high-risk skills such as medical treatment, structural work, electrical repair, food preservation, or water treatment, do not certify from reputation alone. Require direct observation by at least two competent reviewers and limit the certificate to the tested scope.
