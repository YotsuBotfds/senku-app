## Overview

A pit saw is a long, narrow saw worked by two people—one above the log (topman) and one below in a pit (pitman). For millennia, pit saws were the primary tool for converting round logs into squared timber and boards. Even with modern mechanical saws widely available, pit saws remain viable for off-grid sawmilling, repair of large timbers, and detailed work where mechanical saws are overkill.

This guide covers pit design, safe two-person sawing technique, log preparation, blade maintenance, and historical context. With proper setup and practice, two people can saw 200–400 board feet per day by hand—adequate for small-scale construction projects.

</section>

<section id="pit-design-safety">

## Pit Design and Safety

A sawing pit is an excavated trench allowing the pitman (bottom person) room to move the saw. Proper design ensures safety and work efficiency.

**Pit dimensions:**

For a standard pit saw (blade 1.5–2 m long):

- **Length:** 2.5–3.5 m (longer than the longest log expected; typically oriented perpendicular to the building for access)
- **Width:** 0.8–1.2 m (wide enough for the pitman to move comfortably; narrower pits are cooler in summer)
- **Depth:** 1.5–2 m (deep enough that the topman's feet are at or above eye level; allows full arm extension for the pitman)
- **Sides:** Sloped at 1:2 (45°) or vertical with timber bracing, depending on soil type

**Construction:**

1. **Excavate the trench:** Use a shovel and pick. Compact walls if soil is loose (clay and silt are stable; sandy soil requires shoring).

2. **Install shoring (if needed):** For loose or sandy soil, install wooden bracing:
   - Vertical posts at 1 m intervals
   - Horizontal beams secured to posts with bolts or pegs
   - Planking between beams to support the wall

3. **Log support:** Install two parallel wooden rails (logs or heavy beams) across the pit opening, 0.3–0.5 m apart, to support the log being sawed.

4. **Drainage:** Slope the pit floor toward one end and install a small sump pit or drain to collect water. A wet pit is slippery and hazardous.

5. **Ladder or stairs:** Install a sturdy wooden ladder or stairway for the pitman to exit safely.

**Safety measures:**

- **Railing:** Install a railing (waist-height) around the pit opening to prevent falls
- **Bracing:** Ensure the log support rails are secure and cannot rotate
- **Non-slip:** Keep the pit floor clean and dry; use sand or straw for traction
- **Protective gear:** Wear eye protection (pit saws throw fine sawdust) and gloves to prevent splinters and blisters
- **Communication:** Establish clear signals between topman and pitman (whistle or bell for stop/start)

:::warning
A pitman working below is at serious risk if the log shifts or the saw binds. Never leave the pit unattended while a log is in position. Always brace the log securely before starting. If the saw binds, both workers stop immediately—forcing it will snap the blade or splinter the log.
:::

<section id="log-preparation">

## Log Preparation and Marking

Preparing logs correctly ensures straight, efficient cuts and minimizes waste.

**Log selection:**

- **Straightness:** Avoid logs with excessive crook or sweep. A 1–2% curve is acceptable; anything more will result in wedged or binding cuts.
- **Diameter:** 150–300 mm is ideal (requires force but is manageable by two people). Larger logs (>400 mm) are extremely difficult; smaller logs (<100 mm) waste material and are hard to control.
- **Length:** Pit saws typically handle 2–4 m lengths. Longer logs require additional support or multiple cuts.
- **Species:** Hard, dense wood (oak, ash) is slower to saw but produces superior lumber. Softwood (pine, fir) saws quickly.

**Debarking:**

Remove bark completely before sawing:
- Use a drawknife or debarking spade to strip bark
- Removes insect hiding places and makes layout easier
- Prevents dulling the blade with mineral-laden bark

**Log mounting:**

1. Position the log on the support rails (centered between them)
2. Check that the log is level along its length (use a straightedge or level)
3. Wedge the log securely with wooden blocks under the rails to prevent rolling
4. For large logs, install additional supports (posts) every 1–2 m along the length

**Marking lines:**

1. **Snap chalk lines** for each cut:
   - Square lines (perpendicular to the log axis) for cross-cuts
   - Parallel lines for ripping (lengthwise cuts to produce boards)

2. **For ripping:** Mark two parallel lines ~25–50 mm apart (thickness of the board plus blade kerf). This margin allows for minor drift without re-marking.

3. **Use a carpenter's square** to verify squareness of layout

4. **Mark height:** Chalk the top surface and along both sides so the pitman can reference the line

**Sawing sequence:**

- Start by squaring the ends (cross-cuts at 90° to the log axis)
- Make one side face (large ripping cut) first, establishing a reference surface
- Square one edge (smaller ripping cut) next
- Make all remaining cuts based on the squared surfaces

This produces a squared log with minimal waste.

<section id="pit-saw-technique">

## Pit Saw Technique and Operation

Successful pit sawing requires coordination, stamina, and proper technique.

**Saw anatomy:**

- **Blade:** 1.5–2 m long, ~10–15 cm wide, 1–2 mm thick (thinner blade = smoother cut, but less rigid)
- **Frame:** Wooden or iron frame holding the blade under tension
- **Handles:** Wooden grips at each end; the topman operates the upper handle, the pitman the lower
- **Tension:** Rope or iron screw mechanism to tension the blade; tension is crucial for straight cutting

**Blade sharpness check:**

The saw should cut with light pressure. Dull blades require excessive force and produce ragged cuts. If the saw "stalls" (does not progress despite pressure), the blade likely needs sharpening.

**Grip and posture:**

- **Topman:** Stand with feet wide apart, pulling the saw toward himself with arm extended. Allow the weight of the saw to provide downward force; let the pitman do much of the work on the downstroke.

- **Pitman:** Stand with knees slightly bent, pulling the saw upward and pushing on the return stroke. The pitman's position is harder and more dangerous; take breaks frequently.

**Sawing rhythm:**

1. **Establish a rhythm:** Pull steadily, not jerking. Each stroke should be smooth and controlled.

2. **Start the cut:** The first few strokes are critical. Lean the blade forward slightly; use the topman's weight to press down. Once a kerf forms (2–3 cm deep), the cut usually goes straight.

3. **Maintain alignment:** Watch both sides of the blade to ensure it's tracking true. If it drifts, adjust by tilting the frame slightly toward the correct side.

4. **Pace:** A sustainable pace is 40–60 strokes per minute (roughly one stroke per second). Faster sawing is tiring and less effective.

5. **Duration:** Saw for 30–45 minute periods, then take a 10–15 minute break. Fatigue leads to mistakes and accidents.

**Common problems:**

- **Blade drifting sideways:** The blade is dull or misaligned. Check sharpness and tension.

- **Blade stalling (no progress):** Increase downward pressure from the topman. Check for knots or grain reversals in the wood. These may require extra force or a different path.

- **Ragged cut:** Blade is dull or too loose. Tighten tension first; if that doesn't help, sharpen the blade.

- **Pitman getting tired quickly:** This is normal at first. Share the work by alternating roles every 30 minutes, or reduce the stroke pace.

:::tip
A dull saw is far more tiring than a sharp one. If the saw feels difficult, stop and sharpen immediately rather than persisting with a dull blade. Sharpening every few hours of use is typical.
:::

<section id="blade-materials">

## Blade Materials and Specifications

Pit saw blade quality directly determines cutting efficiency and longevity. Understanding steel selection, tempering, tooth geometry, and sharpening angles enables both proper blade selection and DIY blade fabrication when salvage alternatives aren't available.

### Steel Types for Saw Blades

**Spring steel (common for pit saws):**
- Composition: Iron + 0.8-1.2% carbon + 0.3-0.5% manganese
- Properties: Good toughness (resists breakage), moderate hardness
- Hardness: Rockwell C 45-50 (reasonable cutting edge, can be sharpened by hand)
- Source: Old leaf springs from vehicles, abandoned industrial machinery, salvaged flat bars
- Best for: Hand-operated pit saws where shock and vibration are common

**High-carbon steel (superior for pit saws):**
- Composition: Iron + 1.0-1.5% carbon (1.2-1.3% optimal for saw blades)
- Properties: Higher hardness, holds edge longer, more brittle if mishandled
- Hardness: Rockwell C 52-58 (excellent cutting edge, requires skill to sharpen)
- Sources: Old files, precision tools, high-quality industrial blades
- Best for: Production sawmills or well-maintained pit saws with careful sharpening

**Tool steel (advanced option):**
- Composition: Iron + 1.2% carbon + tungsten/molybdenum additions
- Properties: Exceptional hardness, holds edge through extended use, expensive
- Hardness: Rockwell C 60-65 (superior cutting, difficult hand sharpening)
- Sources: Salvaged tool bits, old dental or precision tools
- Best for: Commercial or high-volume production situations only

:::warning
Never use stainless steel for saw blades. Stainless is too soft to hold a cutting edge and too tough to sharpen. Cast iron is brittle and unsuitable. Use only carbon steel or tool steel.
:::

### Tempering Requirements

Proper heat treatment dramatically affects blade performance:

**Hardness vs. Brittleness Trade-off:**
- Under-tempered (heated to only 300-400°C): Very hard, cuts well, but brittle—breaks under shock
- Properly tempered (400-500°C for high-carbon, 350-450°C for spring steel): Good cutting edge + toughness
- Over-tempered (>500°C): Too soft, dulls quickly, loses hardness

**Tempering procedure (if blade needs rehardening):**
1. Heat blade to cherry-red color (~750°C) using forge or open flame
2. Quench quickly in clean water or oil (not too violently—sudden cooling can cause warping)
3. Allow blade to cool slightly; wipe clean to reveal color
4. Heat again carefully to straw-yellow or light-blue color (350-450°C) depending on steel type
5. Quench again when desired color is reached
6. Allow to cool slowly in air

Visual color guide during tempering:
- Pale yellow (150°C): Too soft
- Straw yellow (220°C): Optimal for saw blades
- Brown (300°C): Harder but more brittle
- Purple-blue (400°C): Very hard, suitable for high-carbon steel
- Dark blue (500°C+): Too soft for cutting edges

### Tooth Geometry Specifications

Pit saw tooth design determines cutting speed, smoothness, and resistance to dulling.

**Hook angle (rake angle):**
- Definition: Angle of tooth face relative to the cutting direction
- Optimal for pit saws: 25-35° positive hook (tooth pulls into the wood)
- Effect: Larger hook = faster cutting but more prone to chattering; smaller hook = slower but smoother
- Measurement: Use protractor on tooth side profile

**Clearance angle (escape angle):**
- Definition: Angle behind the tooth tip, allowing it to escape without binding
- Optimal: 40-60° clearance
- Effect: Too little clearance = binding; too much = weak tooth tip prone to breakage
- Measurement: Measure angle at rear of tooth profile

**Gullet (tooth spacing):**
- Definition: Space between teeth for sawdust discharge
- Optimal size: 15-20 mm depth between teeth (relative to tooth height)
- Effect: Larger gullet = more sawdust clearance but weaker structure; cramped gullet = sawdust packing
- Specification: Typically 10-12 teeth per foot (6-8 cm pitch) for pit saws

**Tooth set (lateral bend):**
- Definition: Each tooth bent slightly left or right alternately
- Purpose: Creates kerf (blade width) wider than blade thickness, preventing binding
- Amount: 2-4 mm bend per tooth (depends on blade thickness and wood hardness)
- Set configuration: Alternating left-right-center or left-right-left pattern

### Sharpening Angles

Correct sharpening angle maintains cutting efficiency:

**Filing angle (stroke direction):**
- Approach angle: 45° to the blade perpendicular (sharpening stone/file drawn diagonally across tooth)
- This angle maintains the hook angle during filing
- Stroke direction: Consistent direction each pass (all strokes same way—unidirectional sharpening)

**Tooth-by-tooth procedure:**
1. Secure blade in bench vise at comfortable height
2. Use triangular file matched to gullet size (typically 5-6 mm for pit saws)
3. Position file at 45° angle across each tooth
4. File from the gullet toward the tooth tip with 3-5 medium-pressure strokes
5. Lift file on return stroke (don't drag backward)
6. File every tooth the same number of strokes for even sharpness
7. After every 5-8 teeth, rotate blade and visually inspect teeth for consistent geometry

### Blade Specifications Summary Table

| Specification | Value | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Length | 1.5-2.0 m | Longer = covers thicker logs; shorter = more control |
| Width | 100-150 mm | Wider blade = straighter cuts; narrower = less drag |
| Thickness | 1.0-1.5 mm | Thinner = smoother cuts, less kerf waste; thicker = more rigid |
| Steel type | High-carbon 1.2-1.3% C or spring steel 0.9-1.1% C | Avoid stainless or cast iron |
| Hardness (Rockwell C) | 48-56 | 52-54 optimal for pit saws |
| Hook angle | 25-35° | 30° typical for balanced cutting speed |
| Clearance angle | 40-60° | 50° standard |

| Tension force | 50-100 kg for 2m blade | Higher tension = straighter cuts but harder to control |

## Blade Maintenance and Sharpening

The blade is the heart of the pit saw. Proper maintenance ensures longevity and performance.

**Sawing teeth anatomy:**

Pit saw teeth are large (1–2 cm long) with:
- **Hook angle:** ~30°, pulling the saw forward as it cuts (aggressive, efficient)
- **Clearance angle:** ~50°, allowing the tooth to escape the wood
- **Gullet:** Space between teeth for sawdust discharge

**Sharpening procedure:**

1. **Mount the blade:** Remove the blade from the frame (unscrew the tension mechanism) and secure in a vice or clamp, teeth up.

2. **File each tooth:** Use a 5–6 mm triangular file matched to the tooth size.
   - Position the file parallel to the blade's side
   - File at a ~45° angle across the tooth
   - Stroke from the gullet toward the tip; lift the file on the return stroke
   - File each tooth evenly; count strokes to ensure consistency

3. **Check depth:** Every 3–4 teeth, examine the gullet. If the file no longer bites the tooth top, the tooth is sharp enough. Stop filing.

4. **Jointing (optional, but improves performance):** Run a flat file along the tooth tips to level them. This ensures all teeth cut at once rather than skipping.

5. **Reinstall and test:** Put the blade back in the frame, tension it, and test on a scrap log. The blade should cut smoothly with light pressure.

**Blade tensioning:**

Pit saw blades must be under tension (typically 50–100 kg tension force for a 2 m blade). Tension is adjusted via a rope or screw at the frame end.

- **Too loose:** Blade wanders and produces uneven cuts
- **Too tight:** Blade flexes in the frame and may snap

Test tension by plucking the blade midway along its length; it should vibrate at a high pitch and return quickly to center.

**Blade replacement:**

Good pit saw blades last 5–10 years with proper maintenance. Replacement blades are expensive but can be sourced from specialty tool makers or salvaged from antique saws. Blade width and length must match your frame exactly.

## Water-Powered Saw Mills

A water-powered saw mill automates the sawing process, allowing one person (or a small team) to process logs at high speed.

**Key components:**

1. **Water wheel:** Captures falling or flowing water to produce rotational power. Power = ρ × g × Q × h, where h is the head (vertical drop) and Q is flow rate. Typical small mills: 2–10 kW.

2. **Gear train:** Reduces the water wheel's slow rotational speed (5–20 RPM) to match the saw speed (200–600 saw cuts per minute).

3. **Crankshaft:** Converts rotational motion into reciprocating (back-and-forth) motion.

4. **Saw carriage:** A frame that holds the blade and can move forward and backward.

5. **Log feed mechanism:** Manual or automatic; feeds the log into the blade after each stroke.

**Design considerations:**

- **Head:** Minimum 1 m; 2–5 m is typical. Higher head means more power available.

- **Flow rate:** Even a small stream (0.1 m³/s = 100 liters/second) provides usable power at a 2 m head.

- **Saw speed:** A typical reciprocating saw mill cuts at 200–400 strokes per minute. Blade length is 400–600 mm.

- **Production rate:** A small water-powered mill can saw 500–2000 board feet per day, depending on log size and power available.

**Historical sash saw mills:**

Before modern circular saws, sash saws (similar to pit saws but powered) were common. These remain practical where electricity is unavailable but water power is.

**Limitations:**

- Setup requires significant capital investment and site development (dam, water channel)
- Only viable where consistent water flow is available
- Output is limited by water availability; mills cannot operate in drought
- Requires skilled operation and regular maintenance

For off-grid locations with reliable streams, a small water-powered mill can be transformative, allowing efficient log processing.

## Lumber Grading and Quality

Once logs are sawn into boards, they must be evaluated and sorted by quality.

**Visual grading criteria:**

- **Defects:** Knots (size, location, loose vs. tight), checks (surface cracks), wane (missing wood on the edge)
- **Appearance:** Color consistency, grain figure, surface roughness
- **Structural properties:** Straightness (curvature), slope of grain (affects strength)

**Grading standards vary by country and species.** In the US, common softwood grades (from best to worst) are:

- **Clear (1st & Better):** Nearly defect-free; premium price
- **Select (A & B):** Minor defects; suitable for visible finish work
- **Common (No. 1 & 2):** Visible knots, minor checks; acceptable for construction
- **Economy (No. 3):** Larger defects; suitable for rough construction or packing

**Pit-sawn lumber** is often graded by eye (no standardized testing). Sellers typically mark boards:
- "Clear" or "Select" if knot-free or near-knot-free
- "No. 1" if 1–3 tight knots
- "No. 2" if 4+ knots or visible defects

**Yield calculation:**

From a log, expect:
- **Sawing efficiency:** 50–70% (rest is sawdust, bark, checks, waste)

A 300 mm diameter, 3 m long oak log yields approximately:

```
Volume = π × (0.15 m)² × 3 m ≈ 0.212 m³
Board feet = 0.212 m³ × 424 bf/m³ ≈ 90 board feet (rough estimate)
Assuming 60% yield: ~54 board feet of sellable lumber
```

This varies greatly by species, log quality, and sawing technique.

## Lumber Seasoning and Storage

Green (freshly sawn) lumber contains 50–100% moisture by weight. It must be dried before use to prevent cupping, warping, and checking.

**Air drying:**

1. **Stack lumber:** Place boards horizontally with wooden spacers (stickers) between each layer.
   - Stickers should be 25–50 mm thick, aligned vertically to prevent sagging
   - Sticker spacing: typically 600–1000 mm apart along the board length

2. **Orientation:** Cover the stack with a roof (to prevent rain) but allow airflow on all sides.

3. **Timeline:** Softwood dries in 6–12 months; hardwood in 12–24 months (thicker stock takes longer).

4. **Target moisture content:** 15–20% for construction wood (measure with a pin-type moisture meter).

**Kiln drying (if available):**

Much faster (2–8 weeks) but requires fuel and equipment. Results in wood at 8–12% moisture, suitable for fine woodwork.

**Drying defects to watch for:**

- **Checking (surface cracks):** Reduces strength slightly; cosmetically unacceptable for finish work
- **Cupping (concave warping):** Makes boards unsuitable for flooring or panels
- **Twisting:** Makes boards unsuitable for structural use

Minimize defects by:
- Avoiding exposed end-grain (coat with sealant)
- Stacking with proper sticker alignment
- Protecting from sun and rain
- Allowing good airflow on all sides

:::info-box
Properly seasoned pit-sawn lumber often ages better than kiln-dried lumber. The slow, gradual drying allows the wood to relieve stress evenly, producing less checking and warping. This is why reclaimed timber from old buildings is highly prized.
:::

## Working as a Pit Saw Team

Pit sawing is a team activity requiring communication, shared responsibility, and mutual respect.

**Role clarity:**

- **Topman:** Primary force; pulls the saw with arm and upper body. Responsible for line of sight and ensuring the blade tracks true.
- **Pitman:** Provides upward pull and return-stroke push. Most physically demanding; needs frequent breaks.

**Communication:**

- Start and stop with clear signals (verbal call or whistle)
- Call out problems ("Binding left!", "Knot ahead!")
- Discuss pace at the start of each cut ("Slow steady pace, OK?")

**Pace and rest:**

- Work in 30–45 minute blocks; take 10–15 minute breaks
- In hot weather, rotate roles frequently to distribute fatigue
- Stop immediately if the saw binds—do not force it

**Common mistakes to avoid:**

- **Pushing on a dull saw:** Exhausting and ineffective. Stop and sharpen.
- **Rushing:** Haste leads to binding and mistakes. Steady pace is faster overall.
- **Ignoring back pain:** Pit sawing is hard on the back. Use proper posture; take breaks frequently.
- **Overworking when tired:** A tired worker is a dangerous worker. Rest when needed.

**Social aspect:**

Historically, pit sawing was a social event—sometimes involving singing or storytelling to keep rhythm. Modern pit sawing can be meditative or social, depending on preference.

## Historical Saw Types

Understanding the history of saws provides context for pit saw design and variations.

**Pit saw:** The dominant form from the Middle Ages through the 19th century. Some regions and specialized uses continue pit sawing today.

**Bow saw (frame saw):** Shorter blade (600–900 mm) in a wooden or metal bow frame. Used for crosscutting logs and boards. Still common in Europe and Scandinavia.

**Sash saw mill:** Powered pit saw (1700–1900). Much faster than hand pit saws, allowing industrial-scale production.

**Circular saw:** Invented in the late 1700s but not practical until the 1850s (industrialization). Now dominant; requires electricity or mechanical power.

**Band saw:** Continuous loop blade; very efficient but requires modern precision manufacturing. Dominant in modern mills.

**Veneer saw (thin kerf):** Extremely thin blade (~1 mm) producing minimal kerf waste. Requires tension and guiding mechanisms.

Pit saws remain relevant because they:
- Require minimal tool investment
- Produce thick, high-quality kerf (clean cuts without fine dust)
- Allow customization (blade size, tooth pattern, frame design)
- Work in off-grid settings

<section id="safety-protocol">

## Safety Protocol and Hazard Prevention

Pit sawing involves high forces, sharp blades, and risks of serious injury. Systematic safety protocols prevent accidents and ensure rapid response if incidents occur.

### Kickback Hazards

Kickback occurs when the saw blade suddenly stops or changes direction, often due to binding in grain reversals or knots.

**Causes of kickback:**
- Grain reversal (wood grain direction changes mid-board): blade suddenly stops, can twist out of control
- Knots or hard inclusions: extremely hard wood resists blade, causes sudden resistance
- Uneven pressure from topman or pitman: imbalanced force causes blade to wander and bind
- Dull blade: requires excessive force, creates heat and friction, more likely to bind

**Kickback indicators:**
- Saw suddenly "stalls" (stops making progress despite applied pressure)
- Grinding or squealing sound instead of smooth cutting sound
- Blade deviates from marked line
- Excessive vibration or shaking

**Prevention:**
1. Keep blade sharp (dull blades are primary kickback cause)
2. Maintain consistent, even pressure from both workers
3. Watch for grain direction changes; adjust angle slightly if grain reverses
4. Apply light pressure if knot is encountered; never force the blade through hard spots
5. Never tighten the saw blade beyond recommended tension (over-tensioned blades are more brittle)

:::danger
Kickback can cause the blade to suddenly twist, striking the topman's hands or body. A blade stopping suddenly can throw occupants off-balance, causing them to fall into the blade. During kickback, both workers must immediately release pressure and stabilize the saw. Never attempt to fight a binding blade by forcing harder—release pressure, assess the problem, and restart carefully.
:::

### Body Positioning & Ergonomics

Proper positioning reduces strain and improves safety.

**Topman positioning (above the log):**
- Stand with feet wide apart for stability (shoulder width or wider)
- Allow the saw to move primarily with arm motion and body sway, NOT pure arm strength
- Lean into the stroke slightly on the downstroke; relax on the return stroke
- Keep elbows close to body (wide elbow position creates leverage problems)
- Never reach across the blade or work above it
- Keep hands clear of the saw blade path—hands should guide the upper handle, not pull across the blade

**Pitman positioning (below the log):**
- Position in the pit with adequate room to move (pit must be at least 1 meter deep)
- Stand with knees slightly bent (shock absorption)
- Use leg muscles more than back muscles (bend knees to pull upward, not your back)
- Maintain neutral spine position (don't slouch or hyperextend backward)
- Keep hands clear of the blade; guide the lower handle only
- Never reach into the blade path or attempt to touch the blade during operation

**Common mistakes:**
- Topman pulling downward with pure arm strength (should use body weight and gravity)
- Pitman pushing upward with back muscles (should use leg muscles)
- Either worker getting tired but continuing at same pace (fatigue leads to accidents)
- Workers standing too close to the blade (should maintain 10cm clearance on sides)

### Emergency Procedures

**If blade binds (sudden stall):**
1. Both workers immediately release all pressure and step back from the pit
2. Do NOT force the blade backward or forward
3. Assess the binding: determine if a knot, grain reversal, or log shift caused it
4. If log has shifted: use wedges to re-stabilize before restarting
5. If knot or grain reversal: attempt to carefully work the blade free with gentle rocking motion (push-pull small increments)
6. If blade cannot be freed: Stop work, assess damage, sharpen if dulled by binding
7. Never attempt to force a bound blade; blade breakage can cause injury

**If worker is injured:**
1. Stop all work immediately
2. Assess injury: if blood loss, chest/abdominal trauma, loss of consciousness, or severe pain—get emergency help
3. Minor cuts/splinters: treat in-field with first aid kit
4. Back/strain injuries: rest and avoid further work that day; assess next morning before resuming
5. Keep first aid kit at pit location; include: bandages, antiseptic, pain relievers, tourniquet (for severe bleeding)

**If log shifts or props fail:**
1. Immediately alert other worker verbally ("LOG SHIFTING!" or similar clear signal)
2. Step away from beneath or beside the log
3. Do not attempt to stabilize a falling log with hands/body
4. After log settles, inspect the support props and rails for damage
5. Reset support props before resuming work

### Respiratory & Eye Protection

Sawing produces fine sawdust that poses respiratory and eye hazards.

**Eye protection:**
- Wear safety glasses or protective goggles at all times (pit sawing produces many splinters)
- Sawdust can embed in eyes causing infection and pain
- Splinters travel at high speed; eye protection prevents serious eye injury

**Respiratory protection:**
- Pit sawing creates fine wood dust (particularly from softwoods)
- Dust accumulates in the pit, creating high concentrations
- Extended exposure can cause respiratory irritation and chronic dust inhalation issues
- Wear P100 respirator (particulate mask, not cloth) if working in enclosed pit or high-dust conditions
- Ensure adequate ventilation: pit should have air flow (avoid still-air pits where dust accumulates)
- Clear dust from the pit periodically (every 30-45 minutes of work) by sweeping or ventilating

### Log Restraint & Wedging Safety

Improperly restrained logs create crush and entanglement hazards.

**Proper log restraint:**
- Use substantial wooden blocks or stone wedges under the log (not movable objects like rope)
- Wedges should be solid and wedge-shaped; blocks should be at least 5cm thick
- Position wedges/blocks directly under the support rails and every 1-2 meters along the log
- Test stability by hand-pushing the log; it should not roll or shift
- Never rely on a single wedge; use at least 2-3 per support rail

**Wedge extraction safety:**
- Always remove wedges from the side, NOT from beneath the log (prevents the log from falling and crushing hands)
- Use a lever or pry-bar to extract wedges; never hammer on wedges or attempt to pull them by hand
- Ensure no workers are in the pit or beneath the log when removing wedges
- Test log stability after each wedge removal before removing the next

:::danger
A log shifting suddenly can crush a pitman in the pit or trap their leg. Never work beneath a log that feels unstable. Stop work immediately if log movement is detected, and do not resume until the log is absolutely secure.
:::

### Blade Guard & Equipment Safety

**Blade protection:**
- Cover any exposed blade sections (beyond the log) with wooden guards or cloth
- Prevents accidental contact with the blade
- Guards should be removable but always in place when not actively sawing

**Tool maintenance:**
- Inspect frame bolts before each work session; tighten any that are loose
- Check the tension mechanism (rope or screw) for damage or fraying
- Replace rope tension mechanism if rope is frayed or worn (risk of breakage under load)
- Oil the frame and joints monthly to prevent rust and binding

### First Aid Essentials

**Minimal first aid kit for pit sawing:**
- Sterile gauze pads (4x4 inch, 10 pack)
- Elastic bandages (2-3 sizes for sprains)
- Antiseptic solution (70% isopropyl alcohol or povidone-iodine)
- Pain relief (ibuprofen or aspirin tablets)
- Tourniquets (for severe bleeding): improvise from cloth strips if needed
- Tweezers (for splinter removal)
- Wound closure strips (for lacerations without stitches)

## Safety Reminders

Pit sawing can result in serious injury if safety is neglected.

**Hazards:**

- **Pinch points:** Fingers can be caught if the blade binds and moves unexpectedly
- **Falls:** Falling into the pit or from high log positions
- **Fatigue:** Tired workers make mistakes
- **Splinters:** Large splinters can cause deep wounds
- **Eye injury:** Fine sawdust is irritating; splinters can embed in the eye

**Precautions:**

- **Eye protection:** Always wear safety glasses
- **Gloves:** Protect hands from splinters and blisters
- **Secure footing:** Keep the pit floor clean and dry
- **Stable log:** Never begin sawing without the log fully secured
- **Blade guards:** Cover unused portions of the blade
- **Stop signals:** Establish and follow clear stop procedures
- **First aid:** Keep a first aid kit nearby
