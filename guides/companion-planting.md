## Overview

Companion planting is the strategic arrangement of plant species to create mutual benefits—pest suppression, nutrient enhancement, improved pollination, and increased yields. Rather than monoculture rows, companion planting mimics natural ecosystem relationships where diverse plant communities support each other.

The practice rests on several mechanisms: (1) pest confusion—aromatic herbs mask crop smells from pests; (2) predator support—flowers attract beneficial insects that feed on crop pests; (3) nitrogen fixation—legume root nodules supply nitrogen to neighboring plants; (4) allelopathy—some plants emit compounds that inhibit competing species; (5) structural support—tall plants shelter short ones from wind and excessive sun.

This guide covers the science behind these relationships, the most reliable companion combinations, spacing requirements, and troubleshooting strategies for a diverse vegetable and herb garden.

<section id="pest-confusion-and-masking">

## Pest Confusion and Masking

Many insect pests locate crops by smell. When you interplant aromatic herbs alongside vulnerable crops, the herb's volatile compounds mask the crop's chemical signature, making it harder for pests to find their target hosts.

**Classic combinations:**
- **Tomatoes + basil + parsley.** Basil repels whiteflies, spider mites, and aphids that attack tomatoes. Parsley attracts hoverflies (whose larvae consume aphids). Plant basil at the base of tomato stakes; space 12 inches apart.
- **Brassicas (cabbage, broccoli, kale) + thyme + dill.** Thyme deters cabbage moths; dill attracts parasitic wasps that lay eggs in caterpillar hosts. Interspace thyme every 3 plants; scatter dill seed along bed edges.
- **Carrots + onions.** Onion smell deters carrot root flies. Interplant onion sets every 6 inches between carrot rows (carrots: 4 inches apart). Both tolerate 16-18 month rotations.
- **Squash + nasturtiums.** Nasturtiums attract squash bugs and cucumber beetles, drawing them away from the squash. Plant nasturtiums around the perimeter of squash hills; sacrifice some to pests to protect the crop.
- **Lettuce + chervil.** Chervil's anise-like smell masks lettuce. Space chervil 8 inches apart among lettuce rows.

:::tip
Aromatic herb effectiveness depends on volatile release. Brush or lightly crush leaves during warm afternoons to increase pest-confusing volatile release. Morning dew and cooler evenings reduce volatility.
:::

## Nitrogen Fixation and Heavy-Feeder Guilds

Legumes (beans, peas, clover, alfalfa, vetch) form symbiotic relationships with Rhizobium bacteria in soil. These bacteria colonize root nodules and convert atmospheric nitrogen (N₂) into ammonia, available to the plant. When legumes are grown alongside heavy nitrogen-demanders, the fixed nitrogen leaches into soil and is available to neighboring roots.

**Nitrogen fixation rates (under ideal conditions):**
- Bush beans: 40–80 lbs N per acre per season
- Peas: 60–150 lbs N per acre per season
- Alfalfa: 100–300 lbs N per acre per season
- Clover: 80–200 lbs N per acre per season

**Heavy-feeder crops** (corn, squash, tomatoes, brassicas) should be interplanted with or follow legumes in rotation. The nitrogen fixed by legumes will be available to the heavy feeder's roots.

**Three Sisters system (Mesoamerican model):**
The most documented companion guild—corn, beans, and squash.
- **Corn** grows vertically, providing a trellis for beans and shade for squash roots
- **Pole beans** climb corn stalks and fix nitrogen in the soil
- **Squash** sprawls around the base, shading soil and preventing weed growth (and reducing water loss through evaporation)

Spacing for a 10-foot bed: Plant corn 12 inches apart in 2 rows; space rows 24 inches apart. Two weeks after corn reaches 6 inches, plant pole beans 6 inches from the base of each stalk. At the same time, plant squash seeds 3 feet apart between the corn rows.

:::warning
The Three Sisters only works with pole beans (not bush beans). Bush beans grow too short to climb corn effectively. Also, this system demands full sun (8+ hours) and rich soil; poor soil will limit corn vigor, which weakens the entire structure.
:::

**Other nitrogen-feeder combos:**
- Tomatoes + bush beans (undersown at 4-week intervals for succession)
- Brassicas + hairy vetch (vetch grown as a cover crop, cut back before brassica planting)
- Cucumber + crimson clover (clover as living mulch around bases)

<section id="pollinator-attraction">

## Pollinator Attraction

Crops with small flowers or heavy pollen demands benefit from pollinators—bees, flies, butterflies, and beetles. Companion flowers provide nectar and pollen to sustain pollinator populations year-round.

**Pollinator-attracting flowers to interplant:**
- **Alyssum** (sweet alyssum): Low-growing, blooms continuously, attracts small flies and bees. Seed around bed edges or undersow in squash beds.
- **Borage**: Blue flowers, attractive to bumblebees. Self-seeds. Plant 2–3 plants per 100 sq ft.
- **Cornflower**: Tall, daisy-like, attracts bees and hoverflies. Plant in corners or back rows.
- **Calendula** (pot marigold): Yellow-orange flowers, attracts bees and hoverflies. Space 12 inches apart; deadhead to extend bloom.
- **Cosmos**: Tall, feathery foliage, daisy-like flowers. Attracts pollinators and beneficial wasps. Space 18 inches apart.
- **Fennel**: Umbel flowers, attracts parasitic wasps and flies. Space 18 inches apart; allow some to flower.
- **Marigold**: Strong scent, attracts pollinators, may repel some pests (though evidence is weak). Space 12 inches apart.

**Crop-specific pollinator needs:**
- **Beans and peas**: Mostly self-pollinating, but bees improve pod set by 10–20%.
- **Cucumber and melon**: Require pollinator visits for fruit development. Plant alyssum or borage every 10 feet along rows.
- **Squash and pumpkin**: Heavy pollen demand. Plant pollinator flowers around the perimeter of squash patches.
- **Tomato**: Mostly self-pollinating (wind and vibrations), but bee visits slightly improve fruit size.

## Allelopathy: Plants That Inhibit Others

Allelopathy is the release of chemical compounds by one plant that inhibit growth in neighboring species. A few companion plants use this defensively (to suppress competitors) or to prevent specific pest establishment.

**Allelopathic plants to be cautious with:**
- **Walnut trees**: Release juglone, which stunts or kills many herbaceous plants. Avoid planting near walnut trees entirely. If unavoidable, plant beans, carrots, corn, and tomatoes (relatively tolerant) at least 15 feet away.
- **Tomato roots**: Release compounds that inhibit some root pathogens but can stunt certain understory plants (though this is weak and often overstated).
- **Fennel**: Releases coumarin compounds that inhibit seed germination of many plants. Keep fennel isolated to its own corner or border; do not interplant with other crops.
- **Rye and other small grains** (when grown as cover crops): Release compounds that suppress weed seeds. Incorporate rye 2–3 weeks before planting to allow these compounds to break down and nitrogen to be released.

**Using allelopathy defensively:**
- Grow rye as a winter cover crop, then incorporate in early spring (allow 21 days before planting), to suppress spring weeds.
- Plant fennel as a monoculture border to deter wandering pests without interfering with nearby crops.

:::info-box
Allelopathy is often overstated in popular companion planting guides. In well-managed garden soil with diverse microbes, the allelopathic effect is usually minimal. Focus instead on proven pest-suppression and nutrient-fixation benefits.
:::

## Spacing and Density Modifications

Standard spacing recommendations assume single-crop rows. Companion planting allows tighter spacing because the diversity prevents pest and disease buildup.

**Density increases in companion systems:**
- **Tomato + basil**: Standard tomato spacing is 24–36 inches. With basil at 12 inches from the stem, you can reduce tomato spacing to 30 inches (a 17% increase in plant density) without sacrificing yield.
- **Three Sisters**: Standard corn spacing is 12 inches apart in rows 24 inches apart. The beans and squash add no extra spacing requirement; they fill vertical and horizontal gaps. Yield per square foot is 30–50% higher than monoculture corn.
- **Brassica + thyme border**: Standard brassica spacing is 18–24 inches. Thyme planted at 12-inch intervals around the bed adds minimal density; you lose no brassica plants.

**Density decreases to avoid competition:**
- **Tall + short pairs**: If a tall companion (corn, fennel) shades a short one (lettuce, carrot), increase spacing of the shade-sensitive crop by 20–30% or relocate it to the eastern side of the tall plant to catch morning sun.
- **Deep-rooting + shallow-rooting**: Fennel, asparagus, and fruit trees have deep roots (18–36 inches). Shallow-rooted companions (lettuce, radish, alyssum) can grow closer (6–12 inches) without root competition.

:::tip
Use a measuring tape to mark companion spacing before planting. Eyeballing distances leads to overcrowding. Overcrowded plants compete for water and nutrients, negating companion benefits.
:::

## Multi-Season Planning and Succession

Companion relationships work best when planned across the entire growing season. Spring, summer, and fall crop rotations offer different companion opportunities.

**Spring (cool season):**
- Peas + carrots + lettuce + onion
- Asparagus (perennial) + thyme + parsley (understory)

**Early summer (warm season transition):**
- Plant pole beans to climb pea trellises (after peas finish)
- Underplant squash in bean beds
- Sow alyssum and calendula around bed edges for pollinator support

**Mid-late summer (heat-tolerant crops):**
- Tomatoes + basil + parsley
- Eggplant + basil + thyme
- Succession-sow beans every 2 weeks for continuous harvest

**Fall (cool-season return):**
- Plant brassicas in summer beds (after tomatoes/beans removed) with thyme and dill companions
- Underplant with spinach and lettuce

**Year-round perennials:**
- Asparagus (under-plant with rotating annuals)
- Strawberry (edge planting with thyme and borage)
- Perennial herbs (oregano, thyme, sage) in permanent border beds

<section id="troubleshooting">

## Troubleshooting Companion Plantings

**Problem: Companion plant outcompetes the main crop**
- *Cause*: Overplanting, too-close spacing, or wrong season
- *Solution*: Reduce companion density by 30%. For prostrate companions (alyssum, nasturtium), plant at the periphery, not around the main crop's stem. For tall companions (corn), ensure they're north of shade-sensitive crops.

**Problem: Pests still present despite companion plantings**
- *Cause*: Insufficient aromatic mass, pollinator flowers flowering too late, or pest population already established
- *Solution*: Increase companion plant density by 50%. Start companion flowers from seed 4 weeks before main-crop planting. Scout early and remove pest clusters by hand before populations explode.

**Problem: Nitrogen fixation not improving growth (beans + heavy feeders)**
- *Cause*: Soil lacks Rhizobium bacteria, or beans are nitrogen-saturated and not forming nodules
- *Solution*: Inoculate bean seeds with Rhizobium at planting (available as a seed coating). Avoid adding nitrogen fertilizer alongside legumes; it suppresses nodulation. Wait until fall to assess nitrogen benefit; nitrogen fixation peaks mid-season.

**Problem: Main crop flowering but not setting fruit**
- *Cause*: Insufficient pollinator activity
- *Solution*: Plant more pollinator flowers (double the alyssum or borage). Avoid insecticides (kills pollinators). Ensure flowers are blooming; if they've finished, seed more every 2 weeks.

**Problem: Allelopathic inhibition (fennel, walnut proximity)**
- *Cause*: Allelopathic compounds in soil or root exudates
- *Solution*: Remove fennel to a separate bed or border. For walnut trees, plant only allelopathy-tolerant crops (corn, beans, carrots, tomatoes) at least 15 feet away. Till soil annually to break up accumulating allelopathic compounds.

<section id="getting-started">

## Getting Started: Your First Companion Garden

For a beginner:

1. **Choose a 100 sq ft bed** (10 ft × 10 ft or equivalent).
2. **Select one main crop** (tomatoes, brassicas, or squash).
3. **Add two companions**: one aromatic herb (basil for tomatoes, thyme for brassicas) and one pollinator flower (alyssum or calendula).
4. **Use the companion chart** to check for avoided plants.
5. **Measure spacing** before planting; mark with stakes.
6. **Scout every 3 days** for pests and harvest opportunities.
7. **Allow companions to mature**: basil leaves for harvest at 8–10 weeks, calendula flowers at 10–12 weeks.
8. **Record results** in a garden journal: companion ratios, pest pressure, yield. Adapt next season.

The evidence for companion planting is strongest for pest suppression (aromatic herbs) and nitrogen fixation (legumes). Pollinator attraction is secondary but significant. Allelopathy is real but often overstated. Start with proven combinations and experiment from there.
