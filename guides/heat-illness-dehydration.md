:::danger
**LIFE-THREATENING CONDITION:** Heat stroke is a medical emergency. Core body temperature above 104°F (40°C) causes irreversible organ damage and death. Cooling must begin immediately—do not wait for medical transport. If someone is conscious, confused, lacks sweat despite heat, or has a temperature above 103°F, begin aggressive cooling now. Severe heat illness kills within hours. Do not assume someone will recover on their own.
:::

<section id="overview">

## Overview

**Start here if your question sounds like:**
- "feeling dizzy or faint in the heat" -> heat exhaustion, see Stage 2
- "dizzy or lightheaded after working outside in hot weather" -> heat exhaustion, see Stage 2
- "almost passed out while working in the sun" -> heat exhaustion or heat stroke; check mental status, see cooling protocols
- "confused or stopped sweating despite the heat" -> heat stroke, cool immediately
- "muscle cramps after working in hot conditions" -> heat cramps, see Stage 1
- "dark urine after a long hot day" -> dehydration, see dehydration assessment
- "headache and nausea after hot-weather work" -> heat exhaustion, see Stage 2
- "feeling weak and sweaty after exertion in the heat" -> heat exhaustion, see Stage 2
- "my stove or heater is making people dizzy" or "the room smells smoky" -> this is not heat illness; use the stove or smoke guide first

Heat illness is a spectrum of conditions ranging from minor (heat cramps) to fatal (heat stroke). The progression is not always linear—a person can jump from heat exhaustion directly to heat stroke without intermediate stages. Severe heat illness causes permanent neurological damage and death.

First step: this guide is for environmental heat stress. If the problem is smoke, carbon monoxide, a stove, a chimney, or indoor air from a heater, route to the cookstoves guide or the smoke exposure guide instead.

![Heat illness and dehydration management reference diagram](../assets/svgs/heat-illness-dehydration-1.svg)

Core concept: **Heat illness occurs when your body's heat loss (sweating, radiation) cannot match heat generation (metabolism, sun exposure, ambient temperature).** This guide covers recognizing the stages of heat illness, performing field cooling (which is often superior to waiting for medical evacuation), managing dehydration, restoring electrolytes, and preventing heat illness through work-rest protocols and acclimatization.

The key insight is that dehydration and heat illness are related but distinct problems. You can be severely dehydrated with normal core temperature (mild danger). You can have normal hydration and dangerously elevated core temperature (severe danger). Both must be managed.

</section>

## Heat Illness Spectrum: Recognition and Differential Diagnosis

Heat illness progresses through recognized stages. Each stage has distinct signs and requires different management.

### Stage 1: Heat Cramps

**What**: Painful muscle cramps, typically in legs or abdomen. Core temperature remains normal (98.6°F/37°C).

**Cause**: Electrolyte depletion (sodium loss) from sweating, combined with muscle fatigue.

**Recognition**:
- Sharp, involuntary muscle contractions
- Cramping stops with rest and electrolyte replacement
- Person is alert, sweating normally, no confusion
- Core temperature normal by touch (skin warm but not hot)

**Management**:
- Stop activity immediately
- Drink water with salt/electrolytes (see electrolyte recipes below)
- Massage cramped muscles gently
- Rest in shade for 15-30 minutes
- If cramps persist after 30 minutes or return frequently, suspect dehydration or heat exhaustion—escalate care

**Risk**: Heat cramps are often a warning sign. Without intervention, heat exhaustion follows.

### Stage 2: Heat Exhaustion

**What**: Dehydration + heat stress without severe core temperature elevation. Core temp 99-103°F (37.2-39.4°C).

**Cause**: Excessive sweating, inadequate fluid/electrolyte replacement.

**Recognition**:
- Heavy, profuse sweating (this is KEY—sweating is present and visible)
- Weakness, dizziness, nausea
- Headache
- Rapid heartbeat (pulse >100 bpm at rest)
- Cool, clammy skin despite heat
- Possible vomiting
- Person is alert but may seem anxious or complain of feeling weak
- Core temperature warm but not hot to touch
- No mental status change (person is oriented, can answer questions)

**Management**:
1. Move to shade or air conditioning immediately
2. Lie down and elevate legs (improves circulation)
3. Drink cool water with electrolytes (small sips if nausea present)
4. Apply cool water to skin (wetting clothing, pouring water over head)
5. Use fans to evaporate moisture (accelerates cooling)
6. Monitor for deterioration—if person's mental status changes or sweating stops despite heat, escalate to heat stroke protocols
7. Rest for at least 1-2 hours; do not return to activity same day

**Prognosis**: Heat exhaustion responds rapidly to cooling and hydration. With proper management, recovery is complete within hours.

**Red flag**: If sweating stops despite continued heat exposure, core temperature is likely rising dangerously. This is heat stroke.

### Stage 3: Heat Stroke (Exertional vs Classic)

**What**: Dangerously elevated core temperature (>104°F / 40°C) with thermoregulatory failure. This is a medical emergency.

**Two types**:

**Exertional heat stroke**: Occurs during physical work in heat. Onset is rapid (hours). Sweating may still be present early.

**Classic (non-exertional) heat stroke**: Occurs in sedentary people in extreme heat (infants, elderly, people with chronic illness). Sweating is often absent from onset.

**Recognition**:
- Core temperature elevated (may feel hot to touch on core/forehead)
- Altered mental status: confusion, irritability, aggression, delirium, slurred speech, irrational behavior
- Loss of consciousness or unresponsiveness
- Absence of sweat (classic sign in non-exertional; may be present early in exertional)
- Hot, flushed skin
- Rapid breathing
- Possible seizures
- No improvement with rest; condition worsens

**Why it's dangerous**: Heat above 104°F denatures proteins in the brain, heart, and muscles. Even brief exposure to 106-107°F causes permanent neurological damage. Every minute counts.

**Management** (See aggressive cooling section below)

**Prognosis**: Mortality is 10-20% even with aggressive cooling. Survivors often have permanent cognitive deficits or organ damage. This is preventable—recognition and cooling are the only effective interventions.

## Core Temperature Measurement in Field Conditions

Accurate core temperature assessment is critical. You can measure core temperature without instruments using field methods.

### Field-Expedient Core Temperature Assessment

<div class="core-temp-method">

**Method 1: Rectal Temperature (Most Accurate)**
- Use a standard thermometer (even plastic ones work)
- Insert 2-3 inches into rectum
- Wait 2-3 minutes for reading
- Rectal temperature is 0.5°F higher than oral, so adjust expectations
- This is the most reliable field measurement

</div>

<div class="core-temp-method">

**Method 2: Oral Temperature**
- Place thermometer under the tongue
- Keep mouth closed
- Wait 3-5 minutes (longer than usual because heat may slow mercury rise)
- Oral readings are slightly lower than rectal
- Avoid if person has vomited or consumed hot/cold liquids recently

</div>

<div class="core-temp-method">

**Method 3: Tactile Assessment (No Thermometer)**
- Feel the person's core: forehead, neck, armpits, lower back
- Compare to your own skin temperature
- Extremely hot core (too hot to comfortably hold your hand) suggests dangerous elevation
- This is crude but better than guessing
- A person who is hot to touch across core (not just extremities) needs cooling regardless of exact temperature

</div>

<div class="core-temp-method">

**Method 4: Symptom-Based Escalation**
- If person is confused or irrational → assume temperature >102°F, begin cooling
- If person is alert but weak, complaining of nausea → assume temperature 100-103°F
- Mental status is the most reliable field indicator of dangerous heat

</div>

### Temperature Thresholds and Action

- **Below 101°F**: Symptomatic care, hydration, rest. Monitor every 15 minutes.
- **101-103°F**: Heat exhaustion. Aggressive cooling, hydration. Do not return to activity today.
- **103-104°F**: Borderline heat stroke. Begin aggressive cooling. Monitor mental status closely.
- **Above 104°F**: Heat stroke. Aggressive cooling now. Seek medical help. Do not delay cooling for transport.

</section>

<section id="cooling-protocols">

## Aggressive Cooling Protocols for Heat Stroke

If core temperature exceeds 103°F or mental status is altered, begin cooling immediately. Do NOT wait for medical help—cooling in the field is often more effective than hospital transport.

### Goal: Cool to 101-102°F Rapidly

The objective is to lower core temperature by 2-4°F in the first 10-30 minutes. Effective cooling can mean the difference between full recovery and permanent brain damage.

<div class="cooling-steps">

#### Cooling Methods (in order of effectiveness):

**1. Cold Water Immersion (Most Effective)**
- Immerse the person in ice water, cold lake, or cold stream if available
- Core cooling rate: 0.25-0.3°F per minute (can cool from 108°F to 102°F in 20 minutes)
- Duration: Leave in water until temperature drops to 101-102°F, then remove (avoid overcooling)
- **Limitation**: Not always available; requires a body of cold water nearby

**2. Ice Packs to Core Areas (Second Most Effective)**
- Apply ice packs to: groin (femoral vessels), armpits, sides of neck
- These areas have large blood vessels close to surface; cooling here drops core temperature fastest
- Wrap ice in cloth to prevent frostbite—do not place ice directly on skin
- Core cooling rate: 0.1-0.15°F per minute (slower than immersion but often more practical)
- Use all available ice; make ice packs from whatever cold substance you have

**3. Evaporative Cooling**
- Spray or pour cool water over the person's entire body
- Use fans to blow over wet skin (accelerates evaporation)
- This works in dry climates but is ineffective in humid environments
- Core cooling rate: 0.05-0.1°F per minute

**4. Cool Water Ingestion (Least Effective for Core, But Essential)**
- Have the person drink cool (not cold) water
- This helps with internal cooling but is slower than external methods
- Core cooling rate: ~0.05°F per minute
- Limits: Cannot give water to unconscious person (aspiration risk)

</div>

### Aggressive Cooling Procedure

1. **Remove from heat source**: Move to shade, air conditioning, or cooler environment immediately
2. **Remove excess clothing**: Remove unnecessary layers to allow evaporation and cooling
3. **Position**: Lay flat or head slightly elevated (improves circulation, reduces dizziness)
4. **Apply ice/cold**: Immerse in water if possible, or apply ice to core vessels (groin, armpits, neck)
5. **Spray water**: Continuously wet the person with cool water
6. **Monitor temperature**: Measure core temperature every 5-10 minutes
7. **Stop cooling at 101-102°F**: Once temperature drops to this range, stop aggressive cooling (can overshoot)
8. **Monitor for rebound**: Core temperature can rise again if person returns to heat. Keep in cool environment, monitor for 1-2 hours

**Unconscious person protocol:**
- Do not attempt to give water (risk of aspiration)
- Position on side to prevent airway obstruction if vomiting occurs
- Aggressive cooling is still the priority
- Seek medical help immediately (call/radio for emergency transport)

### Post-Cooling Management

After temperature drops to normal range:
- Continue monitoring temperature (rebound heating can occur)
- Provide oral rehydration once person is fully conscious and able to swallow
- Do not return to activity for 24-48 hours
- Watch for delayed symptoms (some organ damage manifests hours later)

</section>

Dehydration is loss of total body water. It's independent of heat illness but often occurs together.

### Field Assessment of Dehydration Severity

**Urine color test**: This is the most practical field assessment.
- **Clear/pale yellow**: Well hydrated (0-1% loss)
- **Light yellow**: Mild dehydration (1-5% loss)
- **Dark yellow/amber**: Moderate dehydration (5-10% loss)
- **Brown/tea-colored**: Severe dehydration (>10% loss)

**Skin turgor test**: Pinch skin on forearm and release.
- Normal skin springs back immediately
- Dehydrated skin "tents" (stays pinched, bounces back slowly)
- The more severe the dehydration, the longer the skin takes to return

**Capillary refill**: Press a fingernail until it blanches, then release.
- Normal: Color returns in <2 seconds
- Dehydrated: Slower return (indicates reduced blood flow)

**Thirst perception**: Thirst is not a reliable indicator in field conditions. Many dehydrated people lose the sensation of thirst, especially in extreme heat or after prolonged deprivation.

## Oral Rehydration Therapy (ORT)

The gold standard for dehydration treatment is drinking fluid with the right balance of water, salt, and sugar.

### WHO Oral Rehydration Solution (Gold Standard)

The World Health Organization formula has been proven effective across millions of dehydration cases:

<div class="electrolyte-recipe">

**WHO-ORS Recipe (per liter of clean water):**
- **Sodium chloride (table salt): 2.6 grams (1/2 teaspoon)**
- **Potassium chloride (salt substitute) or banana, orange juice: 1.5 grams**
- **Glucose (sugar, honey, or any carbohydrate): 13.5 grams (3 teaspoons)**
- **Sodium bicarbonate (baking soda): 2.9 grams (1/2 teaspoon)**

**Simplified WHO-ORS (without measuring equipment):**
- 1 liter clean water
- 1/2 teaspoon salt (table salt)
- 6 teaspoons sugar or honey
- Optional: pinch of baking soda (helps with acidosis from dehydration)

**Taste**: The solution should taste slightly salty-sweet (like a mild sports drink). If it tastes too salty or too sweet, adjust.

</div>

### Field-Expedient Electrolyte Solutions

If you lack WHO-ORS ingredients, use these alternatives:

<div class="electrolyte-recipe">

**Honey + Salt + Water:**
- 1 liter clean water
- 1 tablespoon honey (provides glucose)
- 1/4 teaspoon salt
- Stir well; drink small sips

</div>

<div class="electrolyte-recipe">

**Sports Drink Equivalent (if available):**
- Most sports drinks are formulated similarly to ORS
- Gatorade, Powerade, etc. are acceptable alternatives
- If too concentrated, dilute 1:1 with water

</div>

<div class="electrolyte-recipe">

**Broth + Sugar:**
- 1 liter of boiled/cooled water
- Add salt (sodium from salt crystals or animal stock)
- Add sugar or honey
- This provides sodium and carbohydrate

</div>

### How to Rehydrate

**Amount**: 500-1000 mL (16-32 oz) over the first hour. Adjust based on severity.

**Method**:
- Drink in small sips (5-10 mL every few minutes)
- Avoid large gulps (can cause nausea and vomiting)
- If nausea occurs, pause for 10-15 minutes, then resume sipping
- Continue until urine color improves (goal: pale yellow)

**Continue for 4-6 hours** after initial rehydration. Dehydrated tissues reabsorb water slowly.

**Monitoring**: Check urine color every 30-60 minutes. Once urine is pale yellow, switch to normal hydration (drink when thirsty).

### Intravenous Rehydration (If Available)

If someone is severely dehydrated and unable to drink (unconscious, vomiting uncontrollably), and you have IV supplies:
- Standard IV solution: Normal saline (0.9% NaCl) or Ringer's lactate
- Flow rate: Depends on severity, but generally 1-2 liters over the first hour
- Monitor urine output and mental status

If you lack IV supplies and the person cannot drink, seek emergency medical help immediately.

</section>

<section id="electrolyte-imbalance">

## Electrolyte Imbalance: Hyponatremia and Hypernatremia

Dehydration and rehydration create electrolyte imbalances, each with dangerous effects.

### Hyponatremia (Low Sodium)

**Cause**: Drinking excessive pure water (especially in hot conditions) without electrolyte replacement. Common in endurance athletes.

**Recognition**:
- Nausea, vomiting
- Confusion, altered mental status
- Headache, weakness
- Swelling of hands/feet (fluid shifts into tissues)
- Seizures (in severe cases)

**Prevention**: Always add salt to drinking water in survival situations. Use ORS or salty foods.

**Management**:
- Restrict fluid intake (counterintuitive, but necessary)
- Increase salt intake
- Do not give IV hypotonic fluids (this worsens it)
- If severe (seizures, altered mental status), seek medical help for hypertonic saline

**Key lesson**: In hot conditions, you need salt + water, not just water.

### Hypernatremia (High Sodium)

**Cause**: Salt intake without adequate water, or severe dehydration from fluid losses exceeding water replacement.

**Recognition**:
- Extreme thirst
- Dry mucous membranes, dry tongue
- Lethargy, weakness
- Confusion in severe cases
- Possible seizures

**Prevention**: Maintain adequate fluid intake. Limit salt-only snacks without water.

**Management**:
- Increase free water intake (not electrolyte solution, just water)
- Avoid high-salt foods until rehydrated
- If severe, seek medical help

**Key lesson**: In arid conditions, drink enough water even if you lack food/salt.

### Hypokalemia (Low Potassium)

**Cause**: Diarrhea, vomiting, or excessive sweating without potassium replacement.

**Recognition**:
- Muscle weakness, cramps
- Irregular heartbeat (can be life-threatening)
- Fatigue, lethargy
- Muscle paralysis in severe cases

**Prevention**: Include potassium-rich foods (fruits, vegetables, nuts) in your diet. Use ORS (contains potassium).

**Management**:
- Eat potassium-rich foods: bananas, coconuts, leafy greens, beans
- Include in ORS recipe (salt substitute or fruit juice)
- If severe (arrhythmia, paralysis), seek medical help

</section>

## Work-Rest Cycles and WBGT Index

The key to heat illness prevention in hot environments is managing work intensity through rest cycles.

### Concept: WBGT (Wet Bulb Globe Temperature)

WBGT accounts for humidity, solar radiation, and air temperature—a better indicator of heat stress than temperature alone.

**How to estimate WBGT** (without instruments):
- In full sun with high humidity (sweat doesn't evaporate): Add 10-15°F to air temperature
- In full sun with low humidity (sweat evaporates): Add 5-10°F to air temperature
- In shade with breeze: Subtract 5°F from air temperature

**Example**: Air temperature is 95°F in humid conditions. Estimated WBGT is 95 + 10 = 105°F.

**Key principle**: As heat increases, rest periods must be longer than work periods. Above 100°F WBGT, you're almost entirely resting.

### Acclimatization Protocol (7-14 Days)

Unacclimatized people have worse heat tolerance. Acclimatization improves sweating efficiency and cardiovascular stability.

**Week 1 Acclimatization Schedule**:
- **Days 1-2**: 20% normal work intensity, 80% rest
- **Days 3-4**: 40% intensity, 60% rest
- **Days 5-6**: 60% intensity, 40% rest
- **Days 7+**: 80-100% intensity (full work capacity)

**What happens during acclimatization**:
- Sweating starts earlier (more efficient cooling)
- Sweat is more dilute (conserves salt)
- Plasma volume expands (better cardiovascular stability)
- Core temperature rises less for the same work

**Duration**: 10-14 days of progressive exposure. You lose acclimatization if you spend 3+ weeks in cool environments.

### Activity Restrictions in Extreme Heat

**Above 105°F WBGT**: Avoid exertional activity entirely. Rest in shade, focus on hydration and cooling.

**Above 110°F WBGT**: Stay indoors or in shade. Limit movement to essential tasks only. Heat stroke risk is extreme.

**At night in desert heat**: Core temperature may drop enough to allow normal activity. Work during evening/night, rest during daytime.

<section id="prevention">

## Heat Illness Prevention Strategies

Prevention is far more effective than treatment.

<div class="prevention-checklist">

**Pre-Activity Preparation**:
- Acclimatize for 7-14 days before major work in heat
- Start activities well-hydrated (drink 500 mL water 2-3 hours before activity)
- Wear light-colored, loose-fitting clothing (reflects solar radiation)
- Wear a wide-brimmed hat (reduces solar load on head by 15-20%)
- Apply sunscreen (sunburn impairs sweating)

**During Activity**:
- Drink small amounts frequently (don't wait until thirsty)
- Follow work-rest cycles based on WBGT
- Monitor urine color (stay pale yellow)
- Watch teammates for early heat illness signs (confusion, weakness, loss of coordination)
- Take electrolyte breaks (salt with water, not just water)
- Soak clothing or pour water over head periodically (evaporative cooling)

**Environmental Factors**:
- Work in morning/evening when cooler (avoid peak sun 11 AM - 3 PM)
- Use shade whenever possible (reduces environmental heat load)
- Seek breezy areas (air movement aids evaporation)
- Create artificial shade with tarps/umbrellas if natural shade unavailable
- In high humidity, seek dry environments (sweat doesn't evaporate in 100% humidity)

**Food and Hydration Maintenance**:
- Eat light meals during heat stress (heavy meals increase metabolic heat)
- Include salty foods with meals (salt + food aids retention)
- Avoid caffeine and alcohol (both increase diuresis and dehydration)
- Maintain consistent hydration throughout the day

**Medical/Personal Factors**:
- Inform others of heat sensitivity (some medications, medical conditions, or prior heat illness increase risk)
- Take breaks if you have a fever (illness + heat = severe risk)
- Avoid heat activity if you have new-onset diarrhea or vomiting (fluid loss)
- Increase hydration if you're taking diuretics (blood pressure meds, some supplements)

</div>

### Heat Illness in Special Populations

**Children**: Smaller body mass-to-surface-area ratio = less efficient cooling. Use shorter work cycles and more frequent breaks. Monitor more closely.

**Elderly**: Blunted sweating response and less effective cardiovascular adaptation. Shorter work cycles, frequent monitoring, earlier intervention needed.

**Obese individuals**: More body mass to cool, less surface area for heat loss. Significantly increased risk. Earlier intervention, more frequent breaks, lower work intensity.

**People with prior heat illness**: Permanent increased susceptibility. May have lower heat tolerance threshold and worse prognosis if affected again.

</section>

## Summary: Field Management Decision Tree

**Person complains of heat-related symptoms or looks unwell in heat:**

1. **Is person confused, irrational, or has altered mental status?**
   - YES → Heat stroke. Begin aggressive cooling immediately. (See cooling protocols)
   - NO → Proceed to 2

2. **Is person sweating profusely but weak/dizzy?**
   - YES → Heat exhaustion. Move to shade, cool with water, hydrate with electrolytes. Rest 2 hours.
   - NO → Proceed to 3

3. **Does person have severe muscle cramps?**
   - YES → Heat cramps. Rest, drink electrolyte solution. Massage muscle gently.
   - NO → Proceed to 4

4. **Is person thirsty with dark urine?**
   - YES → Dehydration. Drink ORS slowly, rest, monitor urine color.
   - NO → Not heat-related. Assess for other causes.

**After treatment**:
- Monitor for 2-4 hours
- Do not return to activity same day
- Continue hydration with electrolytes
- Watch for delayed symptoms

### Boundary Triage: Heat Illness

Use this static check instead of a calculator when someone may be overheated.

- **Confusion, collapse, seizure, fainting, or worsening behavior in heat is an emergency.** Stop work, move to shade or a cooler place, begin cooling with available water, airflow, and shade, and seek emergency help.
- **Do not wait for an exact temperature reading.** A thermometer can help, but mental-status change in heat is enough reason to cool and escalate.
- **Give fluids only when the person is awake, cooperative, and able to swallow.** Use small sips; do not force water into someone confused, vomiting, unconscious, or hard to wake.
- **Cooling comes before return-to-work decisions.** Anyone who needed help for heat illness should stay out of heat and exertion while symptoms are present or worsening.
- **Route non-heat causes separately.** Smoke, carbon monoxide, chemical exposure, poisoning, infection, chest pain, or stroke-like symptoms need their own emergency owner, even if the day is hot.
