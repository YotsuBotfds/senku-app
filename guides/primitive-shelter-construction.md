## Overview

Shelter is one of the primary survival priorities. Exposure to cold, wind, rain, and sun causes body temperature dysregulation, hypothermia, heat stroke, and death within hours to days. This guide covers construction of practical, low-technology shelters using natural materials: debris huts (insulated leaf shelters), A-frames, lean-tos, tipis, wickiups, and snow shelters. Topics include site selection (avoiding hazards, proximity to water and resources), structural principles (weight distribution, wind resistance), rainproofing (thatching, bark shedding), insulation (using leaves, fur, and dead air), placement of fire (heat transfer without burning the shelter), and construction methods using available tools and skills.

Shelter selection depends on climate, season, available materials, and construction time available. A debris hut takes 30 minutes; a semi-permanent tipi takes several hours.

## Site Selection and Site Preparation

Choosing a good location prevents disaster and makes shelter construction easier.

### Hazard Avoidance

**Avoid these locations:**
- Riverbeds and low areas (flash floods sweep through during rain)
- Dead trees or branches overhead (widow makers; can fall and crush occupants)
- Below steep slopes (snow slides, rockfalls)
- Dense evergreen thickets (accumulate carbon dioxide and moisture)
- Exposed ridges or open areas (maximum wind exposure)
- Marshy or boggy ground (cold, wet, mosquito habitat)
- Direct downwind from prevailing smoke (from fires, other camps)

**Look for these features:**
- Slight elevation (above standing water, away from wind gusts at ground level)
- Tree cover (protection from wind and rain)
- Proximity to water (less than 100 m walk)
- Proximity to fuel (dead wood for fire)
- Open space nearby for signaling (fires, mirrors) in a search scenario
- Rock outcrops or dense vegetation for wind break

### Microsite Optimization

Within a chosen location:
- **Aspect:** south-facing slope (if building semi-permanent shelter) catches sun warmth in winter
- **Wind break:** position the shelter behind a dense thicket or downwind of a hill
- **Fire placement:** position shelter so smoke drifts away from the entrance
- **Insulation:** if on sloped ground, build with the opening downslope and the insulated back upslope
- **Weather-first order:** build roof and wind-break first (protection from rain and wind before walls); orient the open end away from prevailing storm direction; test drainage with water before sleeping

### Site Preparation

1. **Clear vegetation:** remove low brush and dead wood from the shelter footprint (no lumps or splinters to sleep on)
2. **Level the ground:** rake smooth; remove roots and rocks
3. **Arrange rocks or logs:** create a perimeter to define the shelter and keep it clear of debris
4. **Drainage:** if on slopes, dig a small drainage channel upslope to divert water away from the shelter

## Debris Hut (Emergency Shelter)

A debris hut is quickly built, thermally efficient (due to dead-air insulation from leaves), and suitable for one person.

### Construction Steps

1. **Main frame:**
   - Find or cut a strong ridgepole (1.5–2 meters long, 3–5 cm diameter, straight)
   - Supports: prop one end 60–80 cm high (on a Y-shaped stick or between two trees); the other end rests on the ground (creating a ~20° slope)
   - The ridgepole is the structural foundation; everything else is built on or around it

2. **Rib frame:**
   - Lean branches (3–4 cm diameter) across the ridgepole at 30 cm intervals
   - Angled down, creating a framework that looks like an upside-down canoe or A-frame shape
   - Use about 8–12 ribs depending on the hut's length

3. **Cross-bracing (optional but reinforcing):**
   - Thin branches woven through the ribs horizontally, for stability

4. **Debris covering:**
   - Pile leaves, pine needles, and straw heavily over the rib frame
   - Layer should be 30–50 cm thick (dead air in the leaves provides insulation)
   - Pile higher at the upslope end; less at the downslope opening

5. **Entrance and door:**
   - Leave one end open for entry (usually the downslope end)
   - Construct a loose door of branches and bark that can be pulled across to reduce heat loss

6. **Interior preparation:**
   - Clear the interior floor and line with dry leaves or pine needles (at least 10 cm thick for insulation from ground cold)
   - Arrange so the sleeper can lie along the ridgepole's length

**Construction time:** 30–45 minutes

**Capacity:** 1–2 people (tight fit for two)

**Insulation quality:** Excellent (dead air in the leaf layer is highly insulative)

**Rain protection:** Good if properly thatched; water drains off the sloped roof

<section id="a-frame">

## A-Frame Shelter

An A-frame is more spacious than a debris hut and suitable for 2–3 people or longer occupancy.

### Construction Steps

1. **Ridge structure:**
   - Two trees spaced 2–3 meters apart, or two vertical posts lashed together at the top (A-shape)
   - The apex (top) should be 1.5–2 meters high

2. **Ridgepole:**
   - Run a main pole or rope horizontally between the two apexes (or rest a pole across two A-frames)

3. **Frame:**
   - Lean branches diagonally from the ridgepole to the ground on both sides
   - Spacing: 30–45 cm apart
   - Create a tent-like triangular profile

4. **Covering:**
   - Option A (bark/thatch): Layer large pieces of bark or branches to create a shingled roof
   - Option B (hide/canvas): If available, drape hides or cloth over the frame (more water-tight)
   - Option C (debris): Layer leaves and straw (less water-proof but highly insulative)

5. **Entrance:**
   - Leave one end (typically the south-facing end) open for entry
   - Can construct a hinged or slidable door frame if semi-permanent

6. **Flooring:**
   - Layer branches as a raised platform (keeps people off the damp ground)
   - Cover with leaves or hay for insulation and comfort

### A-Frame Shelter Diagram

**Construction time:** 1–2 hours

**Capacity:** 2–4 people

**Advantage over debris hut:** More headroom; easier to enter and move around inside

**Disadvantage:** Less insulation (depends on covering material chosen)

## Lean-To Shelter

A lean-to is the simplest two-person shelter, ideal for mild climates or temporary camps.

### Construction Steps

1. **Ridgepole:**
   - Rest a strong pole horizontally between two trees or two vertical posts (typically 1.5–2 meters high)

2. **Lean-to frame:**
   - Lean branches or poles at 30–45° angle from the ridgepole to the ground
   - Spacing 30–45 cm apart
   - Extends 2–3 meters out from the ridgepole

3. **Covering:**
   - Layer bark, branches, or thatch over the frame to create a roof
   - Should slope downward toward the open side for water drainage
   - Layer must be thick enough to repel rain (30+ cm of thatch)

4. **Sides (optional):**
   - For wind protection, build up walls on the two sides using branches, bark, or brush
   - Leave the front open for ventilation and fire access

5. **Flooring:**
   - Raise the floor slightly with branches and leaves for insulation

6. **Fire placement:**
   - A small fire outside the lean-to (near the opening) provides warmth
   - Heat radiates into the shelter
   - Smoke vents upward and away

**Construction time:** 30–60 minutes

**Capacity:** 1–3 people

**Best use:** Mild weather, temporary camps, or areas with abundant thatch material

**Weakness:** Not suited for heavy rain or snow (water drips onto occupants if coverage isn't perfect)

</section>

## Tipi and Wickiup Construction

Tipis and wickiups are more substantial structures suitable for semi-permanent occupation or seasonal camps.

### Tipi (Conical Frame)

**Construction:**
1. **Poles:** Gather 15–20 straight poles (5–8 meters long, 5–10 cm diameter)
2. **Apex bundle:** Lash 3–4 of the tallest poles together at one end (using sinew or cordage)
3. **Tripod:** Stand the lashed poles upright, spreading the bases to form a tripod (creates the main framework)
4. **Additional poles:** Lean the remaining poles against the tripod to create a conical framework
5. **Covering:** Drape large animal hides or bark sheets over the frame (tied or pinned in place)
6. **Smoke hole:** Leave an opening at the apex for smoke ventilation; cover with adjustable flaps
7. **Entrance:** Create an opening at ground level (typically facing away from prevailing winds)

**Capacity:** 4–6 people depending on diameter

**Advantages:**
- Excellent wind resistance (aerodynamic conical shape)
- Strong fire in center vents through smoke hole
- Spacious interior
- Can be reused (frame is semi-permanent; hide covering can be moved to a new frame)

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires many long poles and hides (significant material and labor)
- Takes 3–4 hours to construct

### Wickiup (Dome Frame)

**Construction:**
1. **Bent frame:** Bend 8–12 saplings (3–4 cm diameter, 3–4 meters long) into a dome shape
2. **Framework:** Weave the bent poles together (crossing them to form a lattice)
3. **Thatch covering:** Layer branches, bark, and leaves over the framework (30–50 cm thick)
4. **Entrance:** Leave a small opening at ground level for entry

**Capacity:** 2–4 people

**Advantages:**
- Quicker to build than a tipi (1–2 hours if materials are abundant)
- Excellent insulation (thick debris layer)
- Good for winter (dome holds heat well)
- Can be made from bent branches (no need to fell trees)

**Disadvantages:**
- Heavy thatch can collapse if not properly supported
- Interior is cramped
- Requires very dense thatch material to shed rain effectively

<section id="snow-shelter">

## Snow Shelter (Quinzhee)

In snow-covered regions, a quinzhee (shelter carved from a single snow mound) is quicker and easier than traditional structures.

### Construction Steps

1. **Build the mound:**
   - Shovel or pile snow into a mound (2–3 meters diameter, 1.5–2 meters high)
   - Pack the snow down by walking on it repeatedly
   - Let it settle for 30+ minutes (helps bond the snow crystals)

2. **Hollow out the interior:**
   - Dig a tunnel entrance at ground level (angled upward slightly, ~60 cm long)
   - From the tunnel, hollow out a dome-shaped interior chamber
   - The floor should be slightly higher than the entrance tunnel (warm air rises)
   - Ceiling should be ~1.5 meters above the floor (provide headroom)

3. **Ventilation:**
   - Poke 2–3 holes in the roof with a stick (allows CO₂ to escape if a candle or stove is used)
   - The holes should be ~5 cm diameter

4. **Interior finishing:**
   - Smooth the interior walls and ceiling (prevents dripping)
   - Line the floor with insulating material (pine boughs, foam pad, or dried grass)
   - The snow itself provides insulation (poor conductor of heat)

5. **Door:**
   - Use a block of snow, branches, or a stuffed pack to loosely block the entrance
   - Leave room for air circulation

**Construction time:** 45 minutes to 2 hours (depending on snow availability and depth)

**Capacity:** 1–4 people (depends on mound size)

**Insulation quality:** Excellent (snow is a poor conductor of heat; internal temperature can be 10–20°C warmer than outside air)

**Advantages:**
- Quick to construct
- Excellent thermal insulation
- Uses available material (snow itself)
- Can be left standing and used for multiple nights

**Disadvantages:**
- Only works in deep-snow regions
- Roof can drip if temperature rises near freezing
- Ceiling snow blocks some light (dark interior)

## Insulation Principles and Heat Loss Prevention

Effective shelter keeps occupants warm by reducing heat loss to the environment.

### Heat Loss Mechanisms

1. **Conduction:** Heat flowing through materials (through walls, floor)
   - Prevention: Use insulative materials (leaves, straw, fur, air gaps)
   - The thicker the insulation, the better (30+ cm of leaves is ideal)

2. **Convection:** Heat lost to moving air inside the shelter
   - Prevention: Minimize interior air circulation; still air is better than drafty air
   - Interior baffles or hangings can reduce air movement

3. **Radiation:** Heat radiating outward from the body and fire
   - Prevention: Reflective surfaces (back wall of the shelter can reflect heat toward occupants)
   - Fire placement (position fire to radiate heat toward occupants)

4. **Evaporation:** Moisture from breath and perspiration cools the shelter
   - Prevention: Adequate ventilation (prevents moisture buildup and condensation)
   - A balance between insulation and ventilation is needed

### Insulation Methods

**Floor insulation (critical):**
- Layer insulation between the sleeper and the cold ground
- 10–20 cm of leaves, straw, pine boughs, or fur
- Ground can conduct heat away rapidly if the body is in direct contact

**Wall insulation:**
- Thick covering material (thatch, bark, debris)
- 30–50 cm thickness is ideal for cold climates
- Air pockets in the insulation are key; loose piling is better than compacted material

**Roof insulation:**
- Sloped so rain/snow drains off
- Thick covering (same as walls)
- Slightly pitched interior ceiling (helps shed moisture)

**Entrance insulation:**
- Reduce draft at the entrance; narrow the opening or create a door
- A small baffle or wall inside the entrance reduces wind entering

## Fire Placement and Heat Transfer

A shelter is more comfortable when heated by fire, but improper fire placement causes burns or smoke inhalation.

### Fire Location

**Inside the shelter (tipi or wickiup with smoke hole):**
- Place fire in the center, under the smoke hole
- Heat radiates in all directions; occupants are warmed
- Requires ventilation (smoke hole)
- Risk: fire can spread to the shelter covering

**Outside the shelter, near the entrance:**
- Place fire 1–2 meters away, in front of the opening
- Reflects heat toward the shelter interior
- Heat transfer is less efficient (more heat is lost)
- Lower burn risk

**Reflected fire (reflector wall):**
- Build a wall of rocks behind the fire (between the fire and shelter)
- The wall reflects heat back toward the shelter
- Makes exterior fires more efficient

:::danger
**CARBON MONOXIDE POISONING — DEADLY RISK:** Never use any combustion heat source (open fire, charcoal, coal, camp stove, candles) inside an enclosed shelter without adequate ventilation. Carbon monoxide (CO) is colorless and odorless — you cannot detect it. CO displaces oxygen in the blood and causes unconsciousness and death within minutes in a sealed space. Even in extreme cold, you MUST maintain a ventilation opening (minimum 10-15 cm diameter) at both the top and bottom of the shelter to allow airflow. A shelter with a fire inside requires a dedicated smoke hole (as in a tipi) plus a low-level air intake. If using a fire near a debris hut, lean-to, or snow shelter, keep the fire OUTSIDE the shelter with the entrance open. Symptoms of CO poisoning — headache, dizziness, nausea, confusion — may be mistaken for fatigue or altitude sickness. If anyone in the shelter develops these symptoms, evacuate to fresh air immediately.
:::

### Safety Measures

- **Always maintain ventilation** — even in extreme cold, keep an opening for fresh air exchange when any combustion source is present
- Maintain clearance around the fire (at least 2 meters from any shelter covering)
- Never leave a fire unattended
- Have water available to douse the fire if needed
- Line the shelter interior floor with rocks (reduces burn risk if embers fall)

## Rainproofing and Water Shedding

Effective rainproofing prevents water from entering the shelter.

### Thatching Technique

Proper thatch sheds water like a roof.

1. **Layer direction:** Arrange bark, branches, or thatch so each layer overlaps the one below (like shingles)
2. **Slope:** Ensure the shelter roof slopes downward at 30–45°; water runs down and off
3. **Eaves:** Extend the thatch 30+ cm beyond the shelter walls; prevents water from dripping down the sides
4. **Thickness:** Minimum 30 cm; 50+ cm is better for sustained rain

### Bark shingling

Large pieces of bark can be individually overlapped:
1. Attach the bottom edge of each piece (using knots or pegs)
2. Overlap the next piece so it covers 2/3 of the previous piece
3. Water runs from piece to piece and off the edge

### Testing

Build a test shelter and expose it to rain (or use a hose) to verify water-tightness before occupation in bad weather.

## Wood Quality Evaluation for Shelter Construction

Shelter structural integrity depends on wood quality. Selecting appropriate wood species, evaluating green vs. seasoned wood, and identifying load-bearing capacity prevents shelter collapse and reduces construction time.

### Wood Species Identification for Structural Use

Not all trees provide suitable structural wood. Species selection determines load capacity, durability, and ease of working.

**Ideal structural species (hardwoods):**

**Oak (Quercus spp.):**
- Characteristics: Very hard, strong, dense
- Workability: Difficult to cut/split (requires sharp tools, significant effort)
- Durability: Excellent; resists rot and insect damage for decades
- Weight: Heavy (complicates moving/positioning)
- Suitability: Best for primary support beams, ridgepoles; avoid for quick shelters
- Identification: Large lobed leaves, acorns, pale wood when freshly cut

**Ash (Fraxinus spp.):**
- Characteristics: Strong, flexible, excellent shock-resistance
- Workability: Splits easily along grain; easier than oak
- Durability: Moderate; benefits from shading and drying
- Best for: Ridgepoles, support members (handles vibration and movement)
- Identification: Opposite leaflets, diamond-patterned bark, light wood

**Maple (Acer spp.):**
- Characteristics: Very hard, stable, excellent shock-resistance
- Workability: Difficult to split; requires persistent effort
- Durability: Good if protected from ground moisture
- Best for: High-load support structures, rafters
- Identification: Palmate (hand-shaped) leaves, paired winged seeds

**Elm (Ulmus spp.):**
- Characteristics: Very strong, excellent water resistance
- Workability: Splits with difficulty; binds saw blades
- Durability: Excellent in damp conditions (naturally rot-resistant)
- Best for: Ground-contact members, supports in wet environments
- Identification: Serrated leaves, asymmetrical leaf bases, corky bark

**Acceptable structural species (softwoods):**

**Pine (Pinus spp.):**
- Characteristics: Moderate strength, lightweight, easy to work
- Workability: Cuts and splits readily with moderate effort
- Durability: Moderate; benefits from shelter from rain and sun
- Best for: Quick shelters, non-critical supports (can build rapidly)
- Limitation: Lower strength-to-weight; cannot carry heavy loads unsupported
- Identification: Needle-like leaves in bundles (2-5 per bundle depending on species), reddish-brown bark

**Fir (Abies/Pseudotsuga spp.):**
- Characteristics: Good strength-to-weight, straight grain, workable
- Workability: Cuts and splits cleanly; moderate effort
- Durability: Good if elevated and sheltered; acceptable for semi-permanent structures
- Best for: Pole frames, rafters, ribs (not critical supports)
- Identification: Needle-like leaves (individual, not bundles), smooth gray bark (young) or furrowed brown (old)

**Spruce (Picea spp.):**
- Characteristics: Light, strong, straight grain
- Workability: Easy to fell and work; recommended for quick structures
- Durability: Moderate; more rot-resistant than pine if sheltered
- Best for: Emergency shelters, ribs, non-load-bearing supports
- Identification: Needle-like leaves (single, attached individually), pendulous cones

**Avoid for structural use:**
- Aspen/Poplar: Too soft, weak (suitable only as ribs/framing, not load-bearing)
- Birch: Moderate strength but prone to splitting along grain (unreliable)
- Willow: Very weak, soft (suitable only for lightweight, non-structural members)
- Dead wood: Compromised strength (brittle, may fail suddenly)

### Green vs. Seasoned Wood Properties

**Green wood (freshly felled):**
- Moisture content: 50-100% (measured as percentage of dry weight)
- Weight: Heavy (water adds significant mass)
- Strength: Paradox—green wood is actually stronger in toughness (won't snap as easily from shock) but weaker in bending strength
- Workability: Easier to cut (less dense) but very heavy to position; splits and binds saws during processing
- Drying behavior: Will shrink and warp significantly during drying (6 months to 2 years depending on size and wood type)

**Seasoned wood (dried 6+ months):**
- Moisture content: 12-20% (equilibrium with ambient humidity)
- Weight: 30-50% lighter than green wood (dramatic difference for large members)
- Strength: Maximum strength in bending and compression; less prone to shock fracture
- Workability: Requires sharp tools (harder to cut) but easier to position due to lighter weight
- Stability: Minimal further shrinking; won't warp significantly if sheltered from rain
- Advantage: Lighter poles can span longer distances with less sag

**Practical decision:**
- Emergency shelter (hours): Use green wood (readily available, easier to cut)
- Semi-permanent shelter (weeks-months): Use seasoned wood if available (lighter, more stable)
- If forced to use green wood for semi-permanent shelter: Allow extra support members (compensate for lower bending strength) and accept some settling/sag over time

:::tip
**Quick drying technique:** If green wood must be used for semi-permanent structure, reduce moisture loss rate variation by: (1) avoiding direct sun (prevents checking/splitting from rapid surface drying), (2) keeping wood shaded and ventilated, (3) allowing slow, even drying over 2-4 weeks. This reduces warp and cracking compared to sun-dried or rain-exposed wood.
:::

### Strength Indicators in Timber

Visual inspection reveals wood quality before cutting/using it.

**Grain straightness:**
- Straight grain (parallel to log axis): Best strength; load follows grain path with minimal stress concentration
- Spiral or irregular grain: Reduced strength (~10-30% lower); grain wraps around wood, complicating load path
- Visual test: Sight along log from end; look for visual spiral pattern or waviness

**Knot size and frequency:**
- Tight knots (grown-in, concentric rings): Minimal strength loss (~5-10%)
- Loose or missing knots (hole in wood): Severe weakness (~30-50% strength loss)
- Knot frequency: Count knots in length of pole
  - Few knots (<1 per meter): Acceptable for structural use
  - Many knots (>2 per meter): Poor quality; avoid for load-bearing
- Location matters: Knot at critical load point (top of arch, center of span) is worse than edge knot

**Wood density (visual/touch):**
- Dense wood: Feels heavy for size, sinks in water immediately
- Light wood: Floats or barely sinks, feels light
- Field test: Attempt to indent wood with thumbnail; dense wood resists indentation; soft wood dents easily
- Density indicator: Heavy wood (~50+ lbs for 2-meter pole) suggests high strength; light wood suggests lower strength

**Color and discoloration:**
- Uniform color: Healthy wood with no disease
- Blue/purple staining: Fungal discoloration (weakens wood 20-30%; use for non-critical members only)
- Black/dark spots: Possible rot (severely weakened; avoid structural use)
- Yellow/orange: Sap staining; normal; no strength loss
- Pale/silvered: Sun-bleached surface only; interior OK

**Checks and cracks (radial splits):**
- Small checks (hairline cracks radiating from center): Normal drying; acceptable if <2mm wide
- Large checks (>5mm wide): Significant weakness; may split further under load
- Longitudinal splits (along grain length): Severe weakness; avoid for structural members
- Rule: Avoid wood with splits longer than 10cm or wider than 5mm

### Rot and Insect Damage Detection

**Visual rot identification:**
- Soft spots: Press tool/knife point; if tool sinks >5mm, wood is rotted (avoid)
- Color changes: Dark staining (varies by rot type) indicates decomposition
- Texture: Soft, crumbly, or powdery texture = advanced rot
- Smell: Earthy, musty smell = fungal decomposition

**Insect damage indicators:**
- Bark beetles: Small holes (<2mm) in bark (doesn't affect structural wood within)
- Wood-boring beetles: Larger holes (3-5mm) in wood; powder-like "frass" (insect waste) around holes
- Termites: Galleries (tunnels) inside wood; visible as lighter wood streaks; wood sounds hollow when tapped
- Severity: Scattered holes acceptable; heavy damage (>5 holes per meter) indicates weakened wood; avoid

**Prevention:**
- Use wood elevated off ground (allows air circulation, prevents soil contact where rot/insects thrive)
- Keep shelter roof waterproof (prevents water accumulation that promotes rot)
- Avoid wood that's been in contact with soil for extended periods before use
- Inspect wood before bringing into semi-permanent structures; reject heavily damaged specimens

### Load-Bearing Capacity Estimates by Species and Diameter

Shelter design requires knowing how much weight a pole can support. Estimates by species and diameter enable safe design:

**Rough load capacity for horizontal spans (ridgepoles, beams):**
- Unsupported span length = diameter in cm × 10-15 (depending on load)
- Example: 10 cm diameter oak pole can span 100-150 cm before unacceptable sag
- Example: 10 cm diameter pine pole can span 70-100 cm
- Hardwoods: 50% higher capacity than softwoods of same diameter

**Vertical load capacity (support posts):**
- Each vertical post rated at roughly: diameter (cm)² × 30-40 lbs per post for hardwood
- Example: 10 cm diameter oak post = 10² × 35 = 3,500 lbs safe load
- Example: 8 cm diameter pine post = 8² × 25 = 1,600 lbs safe load
- Safety factor already built in; these are conservative estimates

**Practical application for 4-person shelter:**
- Total load (4 people + supplies + roof): ~1,000 lbs (4.5 kN)
- Single ridgepole spanning 3 meters: Requires hardwood 12+ cm diameter or softwood 14-15 cm diameter
- Vertical supports: Four posts of 8-10 cm diameter (hardwood) comfortably handles load with safety margin

:::tip
**Quick field estimate:** If pole diameter equals 1 inch, assume 100 lbs per post safe load (conservative, field-friendly). A 4-inch diameter post = 1,600 lbs capacity (hardwood). This rough rule prevents over-engineering while maintaining safety margin.
:::

<section id="common-mistakes">

## Common Construction Mistakes and Recovery

| Mistake | Shelter Type | Symptom | Fix |
|---------|-------------|---------|-----|
| Ridgepole too thin or sagging | Debris hut, A-frame | Roof sags under debris load | Replace with thicker pole (≥5 cm hardwood) or add center prop |
| Insulation too thin (<20 cm) | Debris hut, lean-to | Cold at night, wind penetration | Double the debris layer; aim for 30-50 cm minimum |
| Flat or low-angle roof | Lean-to, A-frame | Water pools and leaks through | Re-pitch to ≥30°; add ridge pole to increase slope |
- Thatch laid bottom-up instead of top-down | All thatched shelters | Water runs under layers, not over | Remove and re-layer from bottom to top (shingle fashion) |
| Fire too close to covering | All types with fire | Scorching, smoke inhalation risk | Move fire to 2+ meters from covering; use reflector wall |
| No ventilation in enclosed shelter | Tipi, wickiup, quinzhee | CO symptoms (headache, nausea) | Open vent holes immediately; evacuate until air clears |
| Using rotten or insect-damaged wood | All types | Structural collapse under load | Replace with sound wood; press-test with knife tip (>5 mm penetration = reject) |
| Building in dry wash or valley bottom | All types | Flash flood during rain | Abandon and relocate to elevated ground above high-water marks |

:::warning
If a shelter collapses or floods during occupancy, do not re-enter until the structure is assessed. Salvage materials and rebuild on better ground rather than risking a second failure on the same flawed site.
:::

Shelter is a life-saving necessity in most environments. The variety of designs and materials allows adaptation to specific climates, seasons, and available resources.
