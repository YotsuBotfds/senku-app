## Overview

The bow is one of humanity's most enduring hunting and defense tools, capable of delivering lethal force over 50+ meters with minimal sound. Unlike crossbows (which require metal and complex mechanisms), traditional bows can be built entirely from natural materials found in most temperate regions. A capable 40–60 lb draw weight bow takes 40–100 hours of work but yields a reliable, repeatable weapon that can be maintained and repaired indefinitely.

This guide covers self-bows (made from a single stave of wood) and backed bows (reinforced with sinew or rawhide). Both are practical for survival and hunting contexts, achievable without metallurgy, and deadly at ranges of 20–50 meters.

:::info-box
**Which guide do you need?** This guide covers building a bow (self-bow, backed bow, flatbow) and arrows from natural materials — the quickest path to a working ranged weapon without metal mechanisms. If your goal is to **build a crossbow** (a mechanized bow with a trigger, stock, and prod), see <a href="../archery-crossbows.html">Archery & Crossbows</a>. For arrow construction, fletching, and point hafting, stay here.

**This guide owns:** arrow shaft construction, spine matching, fletching, arrow nocks, arrow points and hafting, stave splitting, tillering, limb bending corrections, bowstring making, and backed-bow sinew/rawhide application. See <a href="../archery-crossbows.html">Archery & Crossbows</a> for crossbow prods, rolling nut triggers, crossbow bolts, and cocking mechanisms.
:::

:::info-box
**Key dimensions for a 50 lb draw bow:**
- Total length: 64–68"
- Draw length: 28–30"
- Handle width: 1.5–2"
- Limb depth at handle: 1.5–2", thinning to 0.5" at tips
- Bowstring length: 60–64" (bow length minus 4")
:::

<section id="quick-material-string-reference">

## Quick Material & String Reference

Shared selection criteria for all bow types (self-bow, backed bow, flatbow) and crossbow prods. For crossbow-specific string and prod details, see <a href="../archery-crossbows.html">Archery & Crossbows</a>.

**Wood selection priority (all bow types):**

| Priority | Wood | Draw Weight | Notes |
|----------|------|-------------|-------|
| 1 | **Yew** | 60–100+ lb | Premier bow wood; heartwood in compression, sapwood in tension; rare, slow to season (2+ years) |
| 2 | **Osage Orange** | 60–80 lb | Excellent density and durability; difficult to work; naturally rot-resistant |
| 3 | **Ash** | 40–60 lb | Best beginner wood; straight grain, clean splitting; readily available |
| 4 | **Hickory** | 50–70 lb | Extreme strength and flexibility; handles shock well; harder to work |
| 5 | **Elm** | 40–60 lb | Good flexibility for heavier draws; less common |
| 6 | **Maple** | 30–50 lb | Dense and fine-grained; good for backed bows |

Emergency substitutes: white birch, Douglas fir, red oak — require heavier staves and have shorter lifespan.

**String tuning principles (bows and crossbow prods):**

- String length = bow/prod length minus 3–4 inches
- Brace height target: 4–7" (lower = faster arrow speed, higher = smoother release)
- Strand count scales with draw weight: 8–10 strands for 30 lb, 12–14 for 50 lb, 16–18 for 75 lb, 20–30 for crossbow prods
- Wax all strings with beeswax monthly; replace when fibers separate or brace height drops more than 1" from original
- Flemish twist construction is the universal method for both bowstrings and crossbow prod strings

<section id="wood-selection">

## Wood Selection

Not all woods are suitable for bows. The best bow woods share these properties: straight grain, good bending strength-to-weight ratio, moderate stiffness, and resistance to splinter propagation. Availability varies by region.

| Wood Type | Availability | Strength | Workability | Notes |
|-----------|--------------|----------|-------------|-------|
| **Yew** | Europe, North Africa | Excellent (back in tension, belly in compression) | Moderate — very hard to work | Gold standard; rare in many regions; extremely long lifespan |
| **Osage Orange** | Central/Southern USA | Excellent | Difficult — very hard and brittle | Dense and rot-resistant; very slow to dry |
| **Ash** | North America, Europe | Very good | Easy — splits cleanly | Straight grain; fairly available; good for longer bows |
| **Hickory** | Eastern North America | Very good | Moderate — tough and flexible | Handles shock well; excellent backup wood |
| **Elm** | Temperate regions (wet) | Good | Moderate — tends to check | Less forgiving than others but workable; strong in lateral loads |
| **Maple** | North America, Europe | Good | Difficult — very hard | Suitable for backed bows; workability compensated by strength |

**Selection rules:**

- **Grain direction matters most:** A straight grain that runs from handle to tip without spiraling is non-negotiable. Reject any wood with grain deviation >15°.
- **Age and seasoning:** Freshly felled wood is too wet and weak. Ideal age is 3–12 months air-seasoning (covered from rain) in 50°F–70°F, 40–60% humidity. High-speed drying via kilns or radiant heat can reduce this to 2–4 weeks but risks checking/cracking.
- **Diameter selection:** For a self-bow, select a stave 1.5–2.5" in diameter. For a backed bow, slightly thinner stock (1–1.5") is acceptable since the sinew backing adds strength.
- **Visual inspection:** Split a sample end to examine grain alignment; grain should be nearly parallel to the long axis. Reject any wood with knots, large rays, or wavy annual rings.

:::tip
If traditional bow woods are unavailable, white birch, Douglas fir, and red oak are adequate emergency substitutes, though they require heavier staves to reach the same draw weight and will not have the longevity of yew or osage.
:::

</section>

<section id="stave-preparation">

## Stave Preparation

Once wood is selected, it must be converted into a workable stave (a single piece forming the bow's main body).

**Splitting vs. sawing:**

- **Splitting:** Better for self-bows because it preserves grain alignment and produces minimal waste. Split along the grain using a driven wedge; the wood naturally breaks along growth rings. Takes 2–4 hours for a 24" section.
- **Sawing:** Necessary if you have only a log and no splitting tools. Produces more waste (1/4 to 1/3 of the log), but sawing through the center of the log and working outward can yield useful pieces.

**Initial drying:**

After splitting/sawing, the stave must be air-dried in a stable environment. Moisture content should drop from ~50% (fresh wood) to 10–15% (bow-ready).

- Stack horizontably, supported at both ends, in a covered shelter.
- Maintain 50–70°F and 40–60% humidity if possible.
- Place stickers (thin spacers) between pieces to allow airflow.
- Check daily for checking (radial cracks). If minor cracks appear, cover the ends with wax or cloth to slow end-grain drying.
- Test dryness with a moisture meter or knife test: a knife should slice the outer 0.5" without crushing the fibers.

**Grain assessment:**

Once dry, study the stave end and side carefully:

- Annual rings should be nearly parallel to the curved surfaces (you are working "quarter-sawn," with growth rings vertical relative to the bow's thickness).
- Grain line on the side should run continuously and straight from handle to tip.
- If grain lines spiral >15° or deviate, reject the stave—it will fail catastrophically under load.

:::warning
**Checking risk:** If large radial checks develop (cracks radiating from the center), the stave is compromised and may fail suddenly. Small checks (<1/8" wide) at the ends can be painted over with epoxy or sealed with wax, but deep checks require rejection of that section.
:::

</section>

<section id="self-bow-construction">

## Self-Bow Construction

A self-bow is carved from a single piece of wood. This section covers a 50–60 lb bow, ~66" long.

**Layout:**

1. Select the straightest, strongest grain direction as the "belly" (the inside of the curve when drawn). The opposite side is the "back."
2. Mark the handle zone (center 6–8") in pencil.
3. Mark tip positions 33–34" from the handle center on each end.
4. Eyeball a gentle curve from handle to tips (like a relaxed "C" shape); mark this curve on the belly side with a curved guide or string.

**Roughing out:**

1. Use a drawknife, spokeshave, or plane to remove wood from the back, establishing the basic curve. Work evenly; the curve should be symmetrical top and bottom.
2. Remove wood until the stave bends easily by hand—this is the "floor tiller" position (fully drawn, the bow should not exceed a ~90° bend angle; sharper bends concentrate stress).
3. Target thickness at this stage: 1.25–1.5" at the handle, tapering to 0.75" at mid-limb, and 0.375–0.5" at the tips.
4. Keep the belly rounded and the back flat or slightly crowned to match the growth ring structure.

**Tiller shaping:**

The goal is an even bend throughout both limbs—no flat spots, no kinks.

1. Make a simple tiller board: a 6' plank with a hook at one end and a fixed peg near the other.
2. Hook the bow's handle on the hook, hang a weight (~50 lbs) from the other end.
3. Sight along the back of the bow from each end. Stiff spots will appear as flat regions; limbs that bend too sharply will show kinks.
4. With the drawknife or rasp, thin the stiff spots progressively, removing thin shavings until the bend is even.
5. Avoid removing wood from the belly (the tension side).
6. Repeat tiller testing every 2–3 passes until the bow is floor-tillers with ~50 lbs applied.

**Handle shaping:**

1. The handle (grip zone) should be slightly narrower (~1.25–1.5" wide) than the adjacent limbs and shaped to fit the hand comfortably.
2. Round the top and bottom edges; leave the sides flat enough for a firm grip.
3. Shape grip tapering into the limbs gradually over ~6", not abruptly.

**Tip nocks:**

1. At each tip (33–34" from handle center), carve a small nock groove 0.375–0.5" deep, 0.25" wide, perpendicular to the back.
2. The nock should cradle the string loop without snagging.

**Draw-weight testing:**

1. Brace the bow with a temporary string (~2 lbs tension).
2. Hang a scale from the handle and pull the string to your draw length (28–32") while measuring force.
3. Typical targets: 45 lbs at 28" draw. If your target is 50 lbs, shoot for 50.5–51 lbs (peak force during draw; it relaxes as the archer settles).

:::tip
If the bow is too stiff, remove wood evenly from the back of both limbs (especially the thicker mid-limb zone). If too soft, you've worked too hard; a limb cannot be re-thickened without grafting.
:::

</section>

<section id="tiller-and-bending">

## Tiller & Bending Corrections

Even after roughing out, the bow may need refinement to achieve the correct tiller profile and account for wood irregularities.

**The ideal tiller:**

A well-tillered bow bends in a smooth, even curve from the handle outward. When drawn, the inner 6" (near the handle) should be stiff and the outer 24" should curve progressively. If one limb bends sharply while the other is flat, they will produce unequal force, causing the bow to shoot left or right (depending on dominance).

**Tiller tree method:**

1. Drive a peg into a beam at the desired draw length (e.g., 28" from a fixed point).
2. Hang the unstrung bow vertically from its handle.
3. Pull down on the lower limb tip to the peg; observe the bend curve from the side.
4. The curve should be smooth, with no flat spots or sharp kinks.
5. Mark stiff regions and thin them with a rasp or drawknife.
6. Re-test every 2–3 passes.

**Heat correction:**

If one limb is naturally shorter, stiffer, or has an inherent curve, gentle heat (from steam or a heat gun, ~180°F) can straighten or lengthen it:

1. Soak a towel in hot water and wrap the curved limb.
2. Hold or weight it straight for 10–15 minutes.
3. Allow it to cool while held.
4. Repeat 2–3 times as needed.

Do not overheat; above 200°F, wood loses strength. Steam is safer than direct heat.

**Testing draw weight at multiple draw lengths:**

1. Hang the scale and pull the string to 26", 28", 30", and 32".
2. Record the force at each distance.
3. The draw-weight curve should be smooth and steady (not spiking or dropping sharply).
4. If the force is low at 28" but high at 32", the limbs are too short; if high at 28" and dropping steeply, the limbs are too flexible.

</section>

<section id="backed-bow">

## Backed Bow (Sinew or Rawhide)

A backed bow has a reinforcing layer (sinew or rawhide) glued to the back. This adds strength, especially along the compression side (the back under draw), and allows a thinner, more efficient stave.

**Why back a bow:**

- Increases draw weight without proportional weight gain.
- Distributes compression stress, reducing chance of failure.
- Extends the lifespan significantly (some sinew-backed bows last centuries).
- Allows use of slightly inferior wood.

**Materials:**

- **Sinew:** Dried animal tendon (deer, elk, moose). Pre-soak it; pound it into fibers 0.5–1" wide.
- **Rawhide:** Untanned hide, cut into strips 1–2" wide. Requires soaking to activate adhesion.

**Glue preparation:**

Use animal-hide glue (from hooves, antler, or collagen):

1. Soak hide pieces in water for 24 hours.
2. Heat in a double boiler (not direct flame) to 140–160°F until liquid.
3. The glue should be thick but brushable (consistency of honey).

**Application:**

1. Brush a thin, even layer onto the back of the bow.
2. Lay down sinew or rawhide strips, overlapping slightly (1/4–1/2"), and press firmly.
3. Wipe away excess glue with a damp cloth.
4. Wrap the whole bow tightly with rawhide strips or cloth; this creates pressure as it dries.
5. Allow 2–4 weeks of drying in a warm, dry place (60–75°F, <50% humidity).

**Finishing:**

Once dry, remove the wrapping and trim any overhang with a knife. The backing should be fully adhered. If any edge is lifting, re-glue and re-wrap that section.

:::warning
Hide glue is vulnerable to moisture and mold. In humid environments, apply a protective sealant (wax or oil) over the backing and handle area to prevent deterioration.
:::

</section>

<section id="bow-finishing">

## Bow Finishing

A finished bow needs a protective and aesthetic coating to prevent moisture intrusion and wear.

**Sanding:**

1. Smooth all surfaces with progressively finer grades (60, 80, 120, 150 grit).
2. Pay special attention to the grip zone (where wear occurs most).
3. Leave the back slightly textured to prevent slipping.

**Oil or wax finish:**

1. **Linseed oil:** Classic choice. Apply thin coats (3–5), allowing each to soak in and dry overnight. Takes 2 weeks to cure fully.
2. **Beeswax/plant oil mixture:** Melted beeswax (1 part) mixed with warm linseed oil (2 parts). Brush on, wipe dry after 10 minutes. Gives a harder, more water-resistant finish.
3. **Modern option:** Spar urethane or marine varnish (if available) offers better water resistance than oil alone.

**Protective wrapping:**

Wrap the grip and lower handle zone with cloth, leather, or rawhide strips to protect against hand wear and improve grip security. This also protects the wood from sweat and moisture.

**String nock wrapping:**

Wrap the nock areas with thread or thin sinew to prevent fraying and provide a smoother surface for the string loops.

</section>

<section id="bowstring-making">

## Bowstring Making

A bowstring must be strong, elastic enough to store and release energy smoothly, and straight enough to not induce torque on the arrows.

**Materials:**

| Material | Strength | Stretch | Durability | Notes |
|----------|----------|---------|------------|-------|
| **Plant sinew** | Good | Moderate | 2–5 years | Traditional; requires pounding; locally available in many regions |
| **Animal sinew** | Excellent | Low | 5+ years | Dried tendon; most reliable; requires soaking |
| **Linen thread** | Good | Low | 3–7 years | If available (salvage or grow flax); wax-treat to improve |
| **Modern Dacron** | Excellent | Low | 10+ years | Ideal if available; minimal stretch minimizes bow-induced error |

**Twisted sinew/plant string:**

1. Pound dried sinew into 0.5–1" wide fibers.
2. Split into 4–6 strands, each roughly 1/16" thick.
3. Twist strands tightly together, using your hands or a spindle.
4. For a 60" bowstring, you need ~70–80" of final cord (accounts for knots).
5. Result: a cord about 1/8" diameter, quite strong.

**Flemish twist method (3-strand):**

This creates a sophisticated, low-creep string from any cordage:

1. Take 3 parallel strings (each 20–28" of thin cordage).
2. Separate the outermost 4–6" of one end; this creates a loop.
3. Twist the three strings together, but alternate the twisting direction (clockwise, then counter-clockwise) every 1–2 inches.
4. The result is a helical braid that resists unraveling and distributes load evenly.

**Loop construction:**

A bowstring must have a small loop at each end to hang from the bow nocks. Two methods:

- **Whip splice:** Wind thread tightly around the cord 1/2" from the end, then fold the cord back on itself and wind over both layers. Cut off the short end. Creates a bulge ~3/8" long but very strong.
- **Simple knot:** A double or triple overhand knot works but can slip; reinforce with wax or glue.

**String length:**

For a bow that is 66" long with nocks 33" from handle center, the string should be 62–64" long (measured from loop to loop). When braced (under ~5 lbs tension), it should hang 5–7" from the handle at the deepest point (brace height).

**Waxing:**

Coat the string with beeswax or plant wax to improve weather resistance and reduce creep. Warm the wax slightly and rub it onto the string evenly.

</section>

<section id="arrow-shaft-construction">

## Arrow Shaft Construction

An arrow must be straight, properly weighted, and spined (flexible) to match the bow's draw weight and the archer's draw length.

**Materials:**

- **Straight-grained wood:** Ash, spruce, or birch are ideal. Diameter 5/16–3/8" for hunting bows.
- **Bamboo:** Exceptionally straight when split; grows in many warm regions.
- **Cane or reed:** Acceptable if truly straight; more brittle than wood.

**Straightness assessment:**

1. Place the shaft on a flat surface and sight along its length.
2. Rotate it; straightness should be visible from all angles.
3. Even 1/8" deviation over 28" will cause poor accuracy and may cause the arrow to stick in the bow (paradox).

**Straightening wood shafts:**

1. If slightly curved, heat a damp cloth around the curve and hold it straight for 10 minutes.
2. Allow to cool while straight.
3. For stubborn curves, gentle steam pressure (from a kettle) can help.
4. Repeat 2–3 times as needed.

**Spine matching:**

Spine is the amount a shaft bends under a standard weight (usually 2 lbs hung from the center, supported at both ends). Arrows must be matched to your bow and draw:

- Lighter arrows (thinner) bend more (higher spine number, e.g., .400 = higher bend).
- Heavier arrows (thicker) bend less (lower spine number, e.g., .300 = less bend).

For a 50 lb draw weight bow with 28" draw, arrows should typically have a spine of .500–.600 (measured in traditional units) or 0.30–0.35" wall thickness if solid wood.

**Length:**

Measure from the nock end (base of the arrow) to 1/8" past the bowstring's rest position. Typical: 28–30" for adult hunters.

**Cutting and smoothing:**

1. Cut to final length with a sharp saw.
2. Sand all rough edges smooth (100–150 grit).
3. Taper the front 2" very slightly (1/32" reduction in diameter) to improve aerodynamics.

</section>

<section id="fletching">

## Fletching

Fletching (feathers) stabilize the arrow in flight by creating drag and spin. Three feathers are standard; four feathers are used for heavier arrows.

**Feather selection:**

- **Turkey feathers:** Gold standard; large, durable, and readily available from wild or domestic turkey.
- **Goose feathers:** Slightly smaller; workable but less durable than turkey.
- **Hawk or eagle feathers:** Excellent but often illegal to harvest or use; check local regulations.

**Preparation:**

1. Split a large primary feather (wing feather, 8–10" long) along the shaft.
2. Trim to 4.5–5.5" length for hunting arrows; 5.5–6.5" for target arrows.
3. Shape the trailing edge into a roughly triangular profile (the back of the feather narrower than the front).
4. Trim the base to 0.5–0.75" length; this is the part that glues to the shaft.

**Attachment:**

**Sinew wrapping method (traditional):**

1. Coat the base of the feather with hide glue.
2. Press it onto the arrow shaft, aligned with the grain (or offset for slight spin).
3. Wrap fine sinew thread tightly around the base, using 2–3 tight wraps at the front and back, with several in between.
4. Allow to dry 24 hours.

**Adhesive method (if available):**

1. Use hide glue, pitch-based adhesive, or modern epoxy.
2. Align the feather; clamp or wrap loosely until dry.

**Fletching pattern:**

- **Helical (spiraling):** Offset each feather slightly along the shaft (~30°apart) to create spin. Improves stability but harder to manufacture.
- **Straight:** All three feathers aligned along the same plane. Simpler to make; adequate for hunting.

**Three-feather spacing:**

Space them 120° apart around the circumference. One feather (the "cock feather") is often a different color or left slightly proud to make nocking easier (the archer feels it and orients the arrow consistently).

:::tip
If feathers are unavailable, lightweight cloth strips (linen or canvas) glued and wrapped can serve as primitive fletching, though they are less stable and require heavier arrows.
:::

</section>

<section id="arrow-points">

## Arrow Points

The point (head) is what kills game and defines the arrow's penetration and weight balance.

**Knapped stone points:**

1. Use flint, chert, or obsidian (see the <a href="../stone-tools-flaking.html">Stone Tools & Flaking</a> guide for detailed knapping).
2. Target thickness: 1/8–1/4", width: 0.75–1.5" for hunting.
3. Sharp points are lethal; blunt points are used for practice or small game.

**Bone points:**

1. Carve from large bones (deer, elk, moose antler) using sharp stone or metal tools.
2. Shape a narrow point 2–3" long with a tanged base (a narrower section that fits into a socket).
3. Sand smooth on stone.

**Forged metal points:**

If you have blacksmithing capability (see <a href="../blacksmithing.html">Blacksmithing</a>), forge simple triangular or leaf-shaped points from iron or steel:

1. Hammer a nail-like tang (0.75–1" long) and a broad point (1.5–2" long).
2. Quench and temper to be hard but not brittle.
3. Sharpen on a stone.

**Hafting methods:**

**Socketed:** The tang slides into a hollow carved at the arrow's tip. Glue with hide adhesive or epoxy. This is strong and allows easy point replacement.

**Tanged (split shaft):** Split the arrow shaft 1" at the front and insert the tang between the two halves. Wrap with sinew. Lower holding power than sockets but simpler.

**Glued butt:** Flatten the point's base and glue it directly to the arrow tip. Least reliable for hunting but acceptable for blunt practice points.

**Point weight:**

- Heavy points (>150 grains) are slower but penetrate well and suit shorter draw weights.
- Light points (<100 grains) are faster and flatter-shooting, suited to longer draws.
- For a 50 lb bow, aim for 100–150 grains total (point + shaft).

</section>

<section id="nock-cutting">

## Nock Cutting

The nock is the groove or slot at the back of the arrow that engages the bowstring.

**Notch style:**

1. Carve a small V-groove perpendicular to the fletching grain (typically on the belly side if there is a natural variation).
2. Depth: 1/8–3/16", width: 3/16–1/4" (sized to the string).
3. Smooth and round the interior edges so the string doesn't catch.

**Reinforcement:**

If the arrow is made of weak wood (ash can split), wrap the nock area with thread to prevent splitting, or glue a small bone ring around the shaft just behind the nock.

</section>

<section id="assembly-testing">

## Assembly & Testing

Once all components are finished, assemble and test before use.

**Assembly checklist:**

- [ ] Shaft is straight and correct length.
- [ ] Fletching is secure (tug gently; no movement).
- [ ] Point is sharp and securely hafted.
- [ ] Nock is smooth and correct size.
- [ ] Total weight is balanced (heaviest point forward).

**Initial testing:**

1. Nock the arrow on the bowstring with the cock feather pointing away from the bow.
2. Draw to full length 2–3 times to ensure the arrow nocks smoothly and doesn't stick.
3. Aim at a safe butt (hay, straw, or a wood target) 10 yards away.
4. Shoot 3–5 arrows.
5. Observe where they land and how they fly (erratic, veering, dropping).

**Tuning:**

If arrows fly erratically:

- **Veering left/right:** The bow may not be tillered evenly, or the arrow may not be spined correctly. Re-check tiller and consider lighter/heavier arrows.
- **Dropping:** The arrow is too heavy or the point is too far forward. Rebalance weight distribution.
- **Fishtailing (side-to-side oscillation):** Fletching may be damaged or not secure. Inspect and re-glue if needed.

</section>

## Safety

:::warning
**Archery is dangerous.** Untrained use can cause severe injury or death. Follow these rules always:

- **Never aim at people.** Ever.
- **Always know what is beyond your target.** Arrows travel far; a 50 lb bow can penetrate 200+ yards.
- **Wear an arm guard** (leather or heavy cloth on the inside of the drawing arm's bicep) to prevent bowstring bruising.
- **Use a finger guard or glove** on the drawing hand.
- **Inspect arrows before every use.** Cracked or damaged arrows are unsafe and unpredictable.
- **Never overdraw.** Pulling beyond your bow's design length (e.g., 30" if the bow is designed for 28") can cause failure.
- **Store bows unstrung** in a cool, dry place. A strung bow loses its temper and weakens over time.
- **Retrieve arrows safely.** Always move behind a retreating arrow; never chase one forward into unknown terrain.
:::

## Maintenance

A well-maintained bow can last decades.

**Storage:**

- Unstring after use if the bow will sit idle for >1 week.
- Store in a cool (50–70°F), dry (40–60% humidity) location, away from direct sunlight.
- Hang vertically or lay flat; never lean against a wall (can develop permanent set).

**Regular inspection:**

- Check bowstring tension weekly; it should feel taut but not rock-hard.
- Look for cracks or splinters on the back and belly.
- If the bow becomes stiff or takes permanent set, reheat and straighten (see Heat Correction above).

**String replacement:**

Bowstrings wear out; replace when:

- Fraying appears at nocks.
- Fibers separate or show breakage.
- Brace height drops significantly (>1" below original).

Typically, a natural-fiber string lasts 2–5 years with regular use.

**Repair:**

- **Small cracks:** Seal with wax or glue.
- **Separated backing:** Re-glue and re-wrap.
- **Bent limb:** Apply steam and weight to straighten; if permanent set develops, the bow is compromised and should be retired.
