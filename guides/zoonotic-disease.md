## Overview

Zoonotic diseases are infections transmitted from animals to humans through direct contact, vector bites, contaminated food/water, or respiratory exposure. In low-connectivity and survival environments where veterinary services and pharmaceutical supply chains are disrupted, these diseases pose significant mortality risk. Understanding transmission pathways, early recognition, rapid response, and prevention becomes paramount—aggressive infection prevention can reduce disease incidence dramatically, while delayed treatment leads to catastrophic outcomes.

An estimated 1.7 billion people experience zoonotic diseases globally; 29 million animal-to-human spillover events occur annually. In collapse scenarios, zoonotic disease incidence typically increases due to disrupted animal control programs, poor sanitation, increased hunting/butchering, and unreliable treatment access.

## Rabies

### Rabies Virus Transmission

Transmission: saliva (bite/scratch/mucous membrane contact). Animals: dogs (90%+ human deaths worldwide), bats, raccoons, skunks, foxes, coyotes, livestock (if bitten by rabid animal). Incubation: typically 1-3 months but highly variable (weeks to years; longer incubation = slower progression). No treatment once symptomatic (nearly 100% fatal); prevention critical via post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP).

:::danger
Once clinical rabies symptoms appear (hypersalivation, hydrophobia, encephalitis), fatality is nearly 100%. The only documented survival cases involved experimental "Milwaukee Protocol" (induced coma, antiviral drugs)—not feasible in survival settings. Post-exposure prophylaxis within 24 hours is 99%+ effective; delay beyond 10-14 days yields questionable protection. Every animal bite must be treated as potential rabies exposure.
:::

### Clinical Presentation

**Prodromal phase (2-10 days):** Fever, malaise, headache, sensory changes at bite site (tingling, burning, hypersensitivity). **Active disease phase:** Encephalitis symptoms (agitation, aggression, hypersalivation, hydrophobia \[fear of swallowing/water\], hallucinations, delirium, behavioral changes). OR paralytic rabies: descending paralysis from bite site, progressive weakness. **Terminal phase:** Coma, death typically from respiratory failure (paralysis of respiratory muscles). Once clinical symptoms appear: progression to death within 7-10 days without life support.

:::tip
Early warning signs of rabies in an exposed person: excessive salivation, difficulty swallowing, hallucinations, agitation, unusual fear of water. If any of these appear weeks to months after exposure, rabies is progressing—seek palliative care immediately. Inform family/caregivers: isolation recommended (saliva infectious) for last days of life.
:::

### Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP)

Start immediately after exposure. Components: 1) Wound washing (vigorous with soap and water, reduces risk 50%+). 2) Rabies immunoglobulin if available (20IU/kg, infiltrate around wound). 3) Vaccine series (5 doses: Day 0,3,7,14,28 or 4-dose if supplies limited). Efficacy >95% if started <24 hours; drops rapidly with delay. Beyond 10-14 days questionable benefit.

:::warning
Rabies immunoglobulin should be infiltrated into the wound itself (not only systemic injection). If RIG limited: infiltrate as much as possible into wound, remainder IM at distant site. Standard dose is 20 IU/kg body weight. For 70kg adult = 1,400 IU minimum. Insufficient immunoglobulin dose increases risk of PEP failure.
:::

### Animal Assessment

If caught: quarantine 10 days (dogs/cats). No symptoms after 10 days = likely not rabid. Symptoms/death = assume rabid, administer PEP. Cannot capture = assume rabid, give PEP. Document animal characteristics when possible.

:::info-box
**Rabies Assessment Quick Decision Tree:**
- Provoked exposure (handled aggressive animal): assess animal for symptoms; if unavailable/dead, assume rabid
- Unprovoked bite (animal bit without apparent reason): high suspicion for rabies—treat exposure as rabies risk
- Bat/skunk/raccoon exposure: assume rabid (high prevalence in wildlife; even "healthy-looking" wildlife high-risk)
- Domestic dog/cat with 10-day observation clear: low risk (rabies animal would show signs within 10 days)
:::

### Rabies Prevention and Post-Exposure Protocol

Hierarchy: 1) Avoid exposure (distance from wildlife). 2) Pre-exposure vaccination (high-risk persons). 3) Post-exposure prophylaxis (immediately). 4) Palliative care if symptomatic (fatal without PEP). Survival: stockpile vaccine if possible, prioritize wound care, train community on risk assessment.

## Leptospirosis

### Transmission and Reservoir

Transmission: urine from infected animals in water/wet environments via skin breaks, mucous membranes. Common in tropical/subtropical areas, post-flooding, among occupations with animal contact. Risk: contaminated water exposure, infected tissue handling. Typical environmental transmission: wading in contaminated water for 10+ minutes, swimming in flooded areas, occupational exposure (farmers, slaughterhouse workers, veterinarians working with rodent-exposed livestock).

:::warning
Post-flooding leptospirosis risk is dramatically elevated (5-10x normal incidence). Floodwaters collect rodent urine, creating concentrated exposure risk. Any fever + water exposure history post-flooding = probable leptospirosis until proven otherwise. Recommend empiric doxycycline (100mg BID x 7 days) if exposure confirmed, even before laboratory confirmation.
:::

### Clinical Presentation (Biphasic)

**Leptospiremic phase (Days 1-7):** Fever (39-40°C), severe headache, myalgia, chills, weakness. **Immune phase (Days 8-30+):** Meningitis symptoms, hepatic/renal injury (jaundice), pulmonary hemorrhage. Severe form (Weil's disease): jaundice, renal failure, mortality 5-15% untreated.

:::tip
Leptospirosis biphasic pattern is distinctive: fever breaks around day 7, appears recovered, then worsens into immune phase. High index of suspicion required—relapsing fever pattern in water-exposed patient strongly suggests leptospirosis. Early doxycycline (Days 1-3) is highly effective; wait-and-see approach risks progression to severe disease with organ failure.
:::

### Diagnosis and Treatment

Diagnosis: serology, blood culture, PCR if available. Treatment: doxycycline (100mg BID x 7 days) if mild/early stage (first 7 days); severe cases IV ceftriaxone (2g every 12 hours) or penicillin G (1.5 million units IV every 6 hours for 7-10 days). Support: careful fluid management (renal failure risk—hypotonic fluids contraindicated), monitoring kidney/liver function. Mild cases resolve well; severe with renal failure has high mortality (>50% if oliguria develops). Dialysis if available dramatically improves outcomes in severe renal failure.

:::danger
Leptospirosis with pulmonary hemorrhage (hemoptysis) or acute renal failure (oliguria, serum creatinine >6 mg/dL) = medical emergency. Mortality >60% without ICU-level support. Focus on fluid resuscitation (careful—can worsen pulmonary hemorrhage; use colloids if available) and vasopressor support if in shock. In survival scenario: aggressive hydration and supportive care only option.
:::

### Prevention

Avoid contaminated water (especially floodwaters—post-flooding leptospirosis risk high), maintain rodent control (reduce reservoir), wear protective footwear in high-risk areas, treat water (boiling, chlorination). In survival: avoid open water sources if rodent activity high, cover cuts before water exposure, maintain sanitation to reduce rodent population.

<section id="brucellosis">

## Brucellosis

### Transmission

Transmission: contact with infected animals/products (unpasteurized dairy, undercooked meat, placental tissue from infected animals). Occupations: herders, slaughterhouse workers, veterinarians, farmers. Incubation: 1-8 weeks variable. Route: inhalation (aerosol from infected tissue), ingestion (dairy/meat), skin contact (cuts/abrasions).

### Clinical Features

Fever (undulating pattern), night sweats, fatigue, myalgia, arthralgia. Chronic: arthralgia, depression, relapsing fever. Diagnosis: serology, blood culture, agglutination test. Treatment: doxycycline (100mg BID x6 weeks) + rifampin (600mg x6 weeks); gentamicin for endocarditis. Support: fluids, fever management, NSAIDs.

### Chronic Disease Management

Some patients develop chronic form (chronic fatigue, arthralgia, depression) lasting months to years. Requires prolonged antibiotic courses (doxycycline 100mg BID x 4-6 weeks or longer), supportive care (physical therapy, psychological support). Prognosis: generally good if treated early; chronic form more difficult to resolve, may require extended therapy or residual symptoms persist.

:::info-box
**Brucellosis vs Leptospirosis Differentiation:**
- **Brucellosis:** Undulating fever pattern (rises/falls repeatedly over weeks), night sweats prominent, occupational exposure (herders, dairy workers), joint pain pronounced early
- **Leptospirosis:** Biphasic pattern (breaks then returns), myalgia predominant, water/flooding exposure history, potential organ compromise (renal/hepatic)
- **Treatment:** Brucellosis = doxycycline + rifampin (6 weeks); Leptospirosis = doxycycline monotherapy (7 days for mild)
:::

### Livestock Brucellosis Management

Identify infected via reproductive failure, abortions, milk reduction. Isolate infected animals immediately; pasteurize all milk (63°C for 30 minutes or 72°C for 15-20 seconds). PPE for handling: double gloves, eye protection/face shield, N95 respirator (aerosol risk during milking/birthing). Safe disposal of infected animals (burial 6+ feet deep with lime, or incineration). Vaccinate herd with live vaccine (B19 or S19) if available and calves <4 months old. Monitor regularly (monthly), isolate new animals before herd mixing (2-week quarantine minimum).

:::warning
Brucella can survive in unpasteurized dairy for weeks at room temperature, months if refrigerated. Pregnant women are at HIGH risk for infection and miscarriage—recommend strict avoidance of unpasteurized dairy in endemic areas. Aerosol transmission risk highest during birthing season (spring) when infected females giving birth; use respiratory protection during livestock births in endemic areas.
:::

## Anthrax

### Anthrax Routes and Presentations

**Cutaneous (skin):** Most common (95% of natural cases). Black necrotic eschar (hence "anthrax" = coal-black), painless, surrounded by edema. Starts as itchy bump, progresses to necrotic ulcer. **Pulmonary (inhalation):** Severe; initial flu-like symptoms (fever, cough, malaise) progressing to severe respiratory distress, stridor, cyanosis, mediastinal widening, shock. Mortality >50% untreated. **Gastrointestinal:** Diarrhea, abdominal pain, hematemesis, high mortality if untreated. **Inhalation risk:** Spore-based bioterrorism concern (aerosol dispersal in post-collapse scenario could occur from environmental contamination or deliberate release).

### Treatment

Cutaneous: ciprofloxacin (500mg BID x 7-10 days) or doxycycline (100mg BID x 7-10 days). Pulmonary/systemic: IV antimicrobials (ciprofloxacin 400mg IV every 8-12 hours for 2 weeks, or doxycycline IV 100mg every 12 hours), plus supportive care (fluids, oxygen, vasopressors if shock). Early treatment critical (cure rate >90% for cutaneous if treated promptly within days of symptom onset; pulmonary much worse prognosis, 50%+ mortality even with treatment). Vaccination available (for high-risk persons); no vaccine for post-exposure usually (antibiotics sufficient for PEP). Post-exposure prophylaxis uncertain benefit; treat clinically confirmed cases promptly.

:::danger
Pulmonary anthrax is a medical emergency. Symptoms (dyspnea, chest pain, stridor) progress rapidly to respiratory failure within 24-48 hours of symptom onset. Mortality >50% even with IV antibiotics started in hospital setting. In survival scenario: high-dose IV fluoroquinolone (if available) + supportive oxygen/ventilation best possible—prognosis poor regardless. Consider early palliative care discussion if pulmonary anthrax suspected and treatment capacity limited.
:::

### Biosafety Concerns

Anthrax spores highly stable; contaminated soil remains infectious decades. Proper decontamination/burial (6+ feet deep, lime powder) essential. Natural transmission rare; post-collapse concern relates to environmental spores.

### Avian Influenza Monitoring

H5N1, H7N9 transmitted from poultry/waterfowl via direct contact or contaminated environment. Symptoms: severe fever, respiratory distress, high mortality (>50%). Prevention: avoid wild birds, restrict contact if present, cull flocks, PPE during handling. Treatment: antivirals if available; early critical. Assume severe poultry illness = avian flu; avoid handling without PPE.

## Recognition & Assessment Framework

### Clinical Assessment Protocol

When a patient presents with fever + recent animal exposure, systematically evaluate for zoonotic disease:

1. **Exposure history (critical):** What animal contact? Duration? Bite/scratch/mucous membrane? Date of exposure? Geographic location (endemic area for specific diseases)?
2. **Symptom onset timeline:** When did fever start relative to exposure? Days/weeks post-exposure (helps narrow differential diagnosis)?
3. **Symptom pattern:** Gradual vs. sudden onset, constant vs. relapsing fever (undulating = brucellosis), biphasic pattern (leptospirosis), presence of rash (tick-borne disease, anthrax cutaneous)?
4. **Risk factors:** Pregnancy (risk for brucellosis miscarriage), immunocompromised (more severe disease), occupational exposure (higher pre-test probability)?
5. **Geographic context:** Which zoonotic diseases are endemic to region? Tick prevalence/types, rodent populations, livestock farming, wildlife risk?

:::warning
Missing zoonotic disease diagnosis in early stages is high-risk (most diseases progress rapidly without treatment). If fever + ANY animal exposure history = empiric treatment often justified (especially for rabies PEP, doxycycline for leptospirosis/Lyme if exposure confirmed). "Better to over-treat than under-treat" principle applies when survival stakes are high and diagnostic capability limited.
:::

<section id="historical">

## Historical Context and Principles

### Pre-Antibiotic Zoonotic Disease Management

Before antibiotics (pre-1940s), zoonotic disease management relied on: prevention (quarantine, sanitation, animal control), supportive care (fluids, temperature management), and acceptance of high fatality rates. Understanding this history frames survival approach: prevention becomes paramount (antibiotics unavailable), early recognition essential (enables PEP if rabies, treatment if bacterial), quarantine/isolation critical for transmission control (especially for communicable zoonoses).

### Disease Transmission Principles

Most zoonotic diseases NOT person-to-person transmissible (exception: plague if pneumonic form, or respiratory pathogens if severe). Focus shifts to: preventing animal-to-human transmission (hygiene, handling precautions, safe food prep), early treatment (prevents severe disease), managing complications (sepsis, organ failure). This informs survival strategy: isolate infected animals, educate community on safe handling, prioritize treatment resources.

### Historical Pandemic Parallels

Black Death (14th century): 50-60% mortality, flea-borne with rare pneumonic spread. Lesson: vector control + quarantine critical. 1918 Flu pandemic: zoonotic origin, 50 million deaths. Lesson: respiratory precautions, isolation, early treatment reduce mortality. Secondary bacterial pneumonia often fatal; antibiotics would have saved many. Modern survival emphasis: prevention, early recognition, supportive care.

<section id="tick-borne">

## Tick-Borne Zoonotic Diseases

### Lyme Disease (Borrelia burgdorferi)

Transmission: Ixodes tick (small, difficult to detect). Symptoms: erythema migrans (expanding red rash from tick site, classic bullseye appearance in 80%), fever, arthralgia, fatigue. Later manifestations: Bell's palsy, carditis (myocarditis, conduction blocks), arthritis (chronic). Diagnosis: serology (late stage), culture (early, rare), PCR if available. Treatment: doxycycline (100mg BID x 14-21 days early stage) if documented within first 30 days; amoxicillin if pregnant/pediatric (500mg TID x 21 days). Late stage (arthritis, carditis): longer courses (28 days) or IV ceftriaxone if severe.

### Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever (R. rickettsii)

Transmission: American dog tick, Rocky Mountain wood tick, brown dog tick. Symptoms: sudden fever (38-40°C), severe headache, myalgia, rash (initially macular on wrists/ankles, becomes petechial, characteristic on palms/soles). Mortality: 20% untreated, drops to 5-10% with early doxycycline treatment. Diagnosis: serology, immunostain of rash. Treatment: doxycycline (100mg BID) IMMEDIATELY if suspected (do NOT wait for serological confirmation—delay increases mortality). Clinical diagnosis sufficient to start treatment. Continue for 3+ days after fever resolves (typical course 7-10 days).

:::danger
Rocky Mountain spotted fever mortality is 5-20% depending on treatment timing. CRITICAL: treat on clinical suspicion alone—do not wait for test confirmation. Delay of even 3-5 days significantly increases mortality risk. Rash typically appears Day 3-6 of illness (often after fever/headache); classic presentation is rash on palms/soles. If fever + headache + myalgia in endemic area (especially post-tick exposure), assume RMSF and start doxycycline immediately.
:::

### Tick Fever (Q Fever)

Transmission: Coxiella burnetii (extremely infectious, can survive environmental conditions, respiratory droplet transmission). Acute: fever, headache, fatigue, pneumonia (atypical, often without productive cough). Chronic: endocarditis (in patients with valvular disease pre-existing), high mortality if untreated. Diagnosis: serology, PCR. Treatment: doxycycline + fluoroquinolone (for chronic Q fever to prevent relapse). Prevention: tick avoidance crucial, vaccination available for occupations at high-risk (veterinarians, livestock workers).

### Tick Bite Prevention and Removal

Wear long sleeves/pants in brush/grass (dense vegetation), tuck pants into socks (prevents ticks crawling up legs). Use insect repellent (DEET 20-30% most effective, permethrin 0.5% spray on clothing—reapply after 5-6 hours or laundry). Perform daily body checks, especially groin, armpits, hair (ticks crawl for hours before attaching—catch them before attachment). Removal: grasp tick as close to skin as possible with fine tweezers, pull straight out steadily for 1-2 minutes (don't twist/crush—risks releasing pathogens into bite site). Avoid bare-hand handling; use gloves or tools. Disinfect bite site (iodine, alcohol, or salt water). Dispose of tick safely (flush down toilet or crush in sealed bag with bleach, not bare-handed).

:::tip
Tick attachment duration matters: Lyme disease transmission risk increases significantly if tick attached >36 hours (risk ~1% at 24h, ~3% at 36-48h). For Rocky Mtn Spotted Fever, transmission occurs within 6-24 hours of attachment. Remove ticks promptly—check daily during high-risk season (April-October in temperate zones). If tick embedded and head stuck: leave it, allow body to work out over days (extraction risk is secondary infection, not pathogen transmission).
:::

<section id="transmission-pathways">

## Transmission Pathway Types

**Direct Contact:** Animal saliva/tissue to human via bites, scratches (rabies, brucellosis, anthrax). Prevention: gloves, protective clothing, immediate wound care. **Vector-Borne:** Ticks, mosquitoes transmit pathogens (Lyme, Rocky Mountain spotted fever). Prevention: DEET repellent, long clothing, daily tick checks. **Foodborne:** Unpasteurized dairy/undercooked meat (brucellosis, anthrax). Prevention: pasteurize milk, cook meat to 165°F. **Airborne:** Respiratory droplets (avian influenza, anthrax, Q fever). Prevention: N95 respirators during animal handling. **Waterborne:** Animal urine in water (leptospirosis). Prevention: boil/treat water, distance water sources from animals.

</section>

<section id="major-vectors">

## Major Zoonotic Diseases by Vector Type

**Tick-Borne:** Lyme disease (Northeast US), Rocky Mountain spotted fever (South-central US, 20% mortality untreated), Babesiosis (hemolytic anemia), Anaplasmosis, Ehrlichiosis. Prevention: repellent, long clothing, daily checks, early removal. **Mosquito-Borne:** Avian influenza (bird-to-human spillover), West Nile virus (neurologic complications possible), Dengue (high fever), Zika (teratogenic). Prevention: eliminate standing water, repellent, protective clothing, avoid peak hours (dusk/dawn). **Rodent-Borne:** Hantavirus (40% mortality), Leptospirosis (water-borne), Plague (highly virulent), LCMV (aseptic meningitis). Prevention: rodent control, avoid dead rodent handling without PPE, maintain sanitation. **Livestock-Associated:** Brucellosis (herders/slaughterhouse staff risk), Q fever (birthing season), Anthrax (spores infectious decades), Rabies (from wild animal bites). Prevention: vaccinate livestock, restrict wildlife access, PPE for handling, rapid isolation of sick animals.

</section>

<section id="surveillance">

## Disease Surveillance Methods

### Animal Monitoring

Regular observation of domesticated animals: note behavioral changes (aggression, lethargy, excessive drooling), reproductive issues (abortion rates, stillbirths), production decline (milk output, growth rate). Illness signs by disease: rabies (neurologic: aggression/paralysis), brucellosis (abortion storms in pregnant females), Q fever (milk-shedding animals—often asymptomatic), anthrax (sudden death, blackened blood from orifices). Structured monitoring: daily animal checks by trained staff, recording observations (date, animal ID, symptoms, progression), documentation enables early detection and intervention.

### Sample Collection and Testing

Blood sampling: sterile collection technique, proper labeling, refrigeration until testing. Serology: antibody testing (detects past/current infection), affordable and practical for survival scenario. Blood culture: slow-growing organisms (Brucella), requires specialized media (often unavailable post-collapse). Tissue samples: from dead animals (autopsy), look for organ changes (splenomegaly in plague/brucellosis, hemorrhage in anthrax). Environmental samples: water testing (leptospirosis risk), soil samples (anthrax spore detection if possible). PCR testing: highly sensitive/specific, rarely available in survival scenario.

### Reporting and Response Triggers

Establish reporting system: any person with acute fever + animal exposure history = probable zoonotic infection (trigger testing/treatment). Animal illness cluster: multiple animals in herd with same signs = investigate (test, consider outbreak response). Environmental concern: dead wildlife accumulation, unusual animal behavior (rabies risk) = community alert. Response measures: quarantine affected animals, restrict animal movement, test exposed persons, treat high-risk exposures (rabies PEP, tick-borne disease prophylaxis), alert community to risks.

</section>

<section id="materials">

## Materials & Sourcing

### Post-Exposure Prophylaxis Supplies

**Rabies vaccines/immunoglobulin:** If pharmacy accessible, stockpile rabies vaccine (likely unavailable post-collapse; focus on prevention). **Antibiotics:** Doxycycline (broad-spectrum, covers many zoonotic infections), fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin), cephalosporins (ceftriaxone for severe infections). **Supportive care:** NSAIDs (pain/fever management), IV fluids (if available), oxygen (if respiratory compromise). **Wound care:** Soap, clean water, disinfectants (iodine, alcohol, chlorhexidine), bandages.

### Post-Collapse Substitutions

**Antiseptics:** Salt water rinse (1 teaspoon salt per cup water), herbal antiseptics (tea tree oil if available, honey for antibacterial effect). **Fever management:** Herbal (willow bark decoction, turmeric), cooling measures (cool cloths, tepid water sponging). **Pain relief:** NSAIDs if stocked, herbal (turmeric, ginger). **Antibiotics:** Survival relies on stockpiled pharmaceuticals or herbal alternatives (effectiveness variable; prioritize human-critical infections for antibiotic use).

### Diagnostic Materials

Serological testing: blood serum, test kit (likely unavailable post-collapse). Survival relies on: clinical diagnosis (symptom recognition, exposure history), empiric treatment if high suspicion, supportive care. Culture: requires specialized media (unavailable in field); not practical for survival scenario. Rapid diagnostic tools: lateral flow assays (if stockpiled) can provide quick results for some pathogens; expensive but valuable if available.

## Prevention Strategies

### Animal Contact Precautions

-   Avoid contact with wild animals; use barriers (gloves, thick clothing)
-   When handling domestic animals: assume disease risk, use care, watch for illness signs
-   Hunting/butchering: wear gloves, eye protection, avoid splashing blood/tissue. Wash hands and exposed skin immediately post-handling. Cook meat thoroughly (internal temperature 165°F/74°C minimum)
-   Avoid bites/scratches; treat any wound immediately (wash, disinfect, apply antiseptic). Consider tetanus prophylaxis if not current.
-   Educate children: no wild animal contact, report unusual animal behavior (stumbling, aggression, excessive drooling = potential rabies)

### Waterborne Transmission Prevention

Boil water from potentially contaminated sources (leptospirosis, giardia from wildlife urine), avoid swimming in flood-affected areas (leptospirosis risk high), protect water supplies from animal contamination (cover well, fence off livestock from water source), maintain sanitation distance from water (prevent animal waste runoff). In survival: assume water contamination risk; boil or chemically treat (bleach, iodine) before drinking.

### Meat and Dairy Safety

Cook meat thoroughly (internal temperature 165°F/74°C minimum, verified with thermometer if available), avoid unpasteurized dairy (brucellosis, tuberculosis, Q fever), proper butchering hygiene (separate cutting surfaces for raw/cooked, wash hands, no cross-contamination), store meat properly (cold chain maintained, prevent thawing/refreezing). In survival: prioritize food safety over convenience; foodborne illness compounds survival challenges.

### Environmental Safety

Maintain animal housing sanitation (remove urine/feces regularly, reduce rodent habitat), dispose of dead animals safely (burial 6+ feet deep, lime powder to accelerate decomposition, distance from water source), protect food from rodent contamination (sealed containers, elevated storage), manage waste (prevent water contamination from decomposing animal tissue).

<section id="ppe">

## Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) for Zoonotic Handling

### Glove Selection and Use

**Latex/nitrile gloves:** Standard for most handling (animal contact, butchering, tissue contact). Thickness: at least 5-mil for durability. Double-gloving recommended for high-risk activities (anthrax tissue, rabid animal handling). Change gloves between tasks to prevent cross-contamination. Proper removal: peel off first glove turning it inside-out, then remove second glove same way, dispose safely. In survival: cloth or leather gloves if latex unavailable; less effective barrier but better than bare hands.

### Respiratory Protection

**N95 respirators:** Minimum for airborne zoonotic risks (anthrax, avian influenza, Q fever aerosols). Proper fit: test fit required for effectiveness. Change when saturated or damaged. **Higher protection (P100, PAPR):** For high-risk procedures (butchering infected animals, anthrax-suspected tissue). Full-face shield preferred over goggles (protects eyes/mucous membranes). In survival: improvise with cloth masks if N95 unavailable (minimal protection, better than nothing); multiple layers (cotton, linen), snug fit. Highest priority: respiratory protection when handling respiratory-risk pathogens.

### Body Protection

**Protective gowns/aprons:** Fluid-resistant for butchering/tissue work. Long-sleeved, covering legs if possible. **Footwear:** Waterproof boots, easily decontaminated. **Head/face:** Hair covering (prevents contamination), face shield or goggles (eye protection). In survival: use available clothing (apron, long sleeves, covered shoes); avoid contaminating regular clothes by changing into designated "work clothes" for animal handling.

### Safe Removal and Disposal

**Order of removal:** Gloves first (highest contamination risk), then gown, respiratory protection, footcover. Perform hand hygiene between steps. Dispose in designated biohazard container if available; in survival scenario, burn or bury contaminated materials separately from regular trash. Decontaminate reusable PPE: wash gowns/aprons in hot water, boil or autoclave if possible, air-dry in sunlight (UV sterilization). Footwear: wash with soap/water, air-dry in sunlight.

<section id="animal-handling">

## Animal Handling Safety Protocols

### Safe Animal Restraint

**Approach techniques:** Move slowly, avoid sudden movements, use verbal calm tones. **Restraint methods:** Proper technique varies by species (ropes for livestock, nets for wildlife, secure containers for small animals). **Two-person minimum:** For large/dangerous animals; one person restrains, second performs procedure. **Escape routes:** Always position to allow safe exit if animal becomes aggressive. **Documentation:** If capturing wild animal, photograph/describe (enables rabies risk assessment even if animal not secured).

### Sick Animal Isolation

**Quarantine area:** Separate enclosure if possible (prevents exposure to other animals, reduces transmission risk). **Duration:** Minimum 10 days for rabies (dogs/cats), longer if other diseases suspected. **Staff assignments:** Dedicated caregivers if possible (prevents cross-contamination). **PPE protocols:** Full protection for all interactions with quarantined animals. **Monitoring:** Daily observations (recovery signs, disease progression), record symptoms, test if available.

### Euthanasia Decisions and Methods

**Indications:** Symptomatic rabies, uncontrollable aggression, severe injury/suffering, confirmed dangerous pathogen (anthrax). **Methods:** Firearm (rapid, relatively painless), lethal injection (humane, requires expertise/drugs), suffocation (least humane, avoid if alternatives exist). **Precautions:** Full PPE required, especially respiratory protection (avoid blood/tissue aerosolization). **Post-mortem handling:** Do not perform autopsy on confirmed/suspected rabid animal (risk of infection). Tissue/head retention for testing (if available) requires special containment.

### Carcass Disposal Methods

**Burial:** Minimum 6 feet deep in non-permafrost soil, distance from water sources (>100 feet), secure against scavenger exhumation. Lime powder (calcium oxide) accelerates decomposition, reduces odor, disinfects. Mark burial site for future reference (prevent accidental disturbance). **Incineration:** Burn carcass completely if fire resources available (requires sustained high heat, complete consumption of remains). **Rendering:** If community has rendering facility, process carcass for safe disposal. **Avoid:** Open air exposure, shallow burial, water-adjacent sites (contamination risk).

<section id="procedures">

## Detailed Procedures

### Rabies PEP Administration

1.  **Wound care first:** Thoroughly wash with soap and water (or saline if no soap). Scrub for 1-2 minutes minimum. Rinse thoroughly. Air dry or pat dry with clean cloth.
2.  **Rabies immunoglobulin (if available):** Dose: 20 IU/kg. Infiltrate directly into and around wound (if available, saves systemic dose). Remainder given IM at site distant from vaccination site (opposite arm if arm bitten).
3.  **Rabies vaccine:** Series varies (traditional 5 doses, newer 4-dose schedules). Administer per protocol. Days 0, 3, 7, 14, 28 (classic) or 0, 7, 21, 28 (newer, if supplies limited).
4.  **Documentation:** Record bite details (date, time, animal type/characteristics), exposure type (bite depth, contamination), PEP administration (date, vaccine lot, immunoglobulin dose if given).
5.  **Follow-up:** Monitor for adverse reactions (myalgia, fever, headache common post-vaccine). Inform patient: infection will develop if PEP delayed >14 days; compliance critical.

### Exposure Assessment for Treatment Initiation

1.  **Identify exposure type:** Bite (highest risk), scratch (moderate risk), mucous membrane exposure (lower risk), contact with infected material without break in skin (very low risk).
2.  **Identify animal:** Domestic (dog, cat, livestock—assess for rabies), wild (assume rabid), unknown (assume rabid unless proven otherwise).
3.  **Exposure timing:** Recent (within 24 hours = urgent), delayed (>24 hours = still treat if high-risk animal, though efficacy reduced).
4.  **Risk assessment:** Rabies in region? Animal behavior unusual? Patient immunocompromised (may need enhanced PEP)? Pregnancy (some vaccines contraindicated)?
5.  **Decision:** If any doubt = TREAT (rabies is nearly 100% fatal once symptomatic; over-treatment far preferable to missing exposure).

<section id="quarantine">

## Quarantine Procedures for Disease Control

### Quarantine Zone Layout

**Three-zone model:** Hot zone (infected/most contaminated area; only essential personnel with full PPE), buffer/intermediate zone (partial protection zone; transition area), clean/cold zone (safe area for non-contaminated staff/supplies). **Physical barriers:** Marked boundaries (tape, signage), doors/gates (control access), one-way flow of personnel/supplies when possible. **Decontamination stations:** At each zone boundary; handwashing station (soap, clean water), shower/wash area (if available), PPE removal area (with disposal containers).

### Quarantine Duration and Conditions

**Rabies:** 10 days observation for dogs/cats (may extend to 3 months if exposure significant). **Other zoonoses:** Duration based on incubation period (leptospirosis 2 weeks, brucellosis 8 weeks, Q fever 3+ weeks). **Housing conditions:** Separate, secure enclosure preventing escape; shade, water, minimal stress. **Feeding:** Separate feeding from non-quarantined animals (prevent disease spread through shared resources). **Monitoring:** Daily observations (fever, behavior, illness progression), record results, test if available.

### Decontamination Station Operations

**Setup:** Dedicated area with supplies (soap, clean water, towels, disinfectant, waste containers). **Personnel flow:** All staff exiting hot/buffer zones must pass through decontamination. **Hand washing:** Minimum 20 seconds with soap and running water. **Shower if possible:** Full body wash for staff with high contamination risk. **PPE disposal:** Contaminated items in sealed biohazard bags (burn or bury safely). **Equipment decontamination:** Spray with disinfectant (bleach 1:10 dilution, iodine, or quaternary ammonium), allow contact time (10-15 minutes), rinse.

### Movement and Escape Prevention

**Fence specifications:** Minimum 6 feet high (prevents jumping/climbing), buried 1-2 feet (prevents burrowing), no gaps (prevents squeezing through). **Gate security:** Double gates (prevent quick escape), latches operable only from outside hot zone. **Roof:** If aerial escape risk (birds, bats), cover top of enclosure. **Regular checks:** Inspect for breaches (damage, weakened areas) daily. **Escape protocol:** If animal escapes, notify community (assume disease risk), search for animal, capture if possible and return to quarantine.

<section id="exposure-response">

## Post-Exposure Response Protocol

### Immediate Actions After Exposure

1.  Wash wound aggressively with soap and water (removes surface pathogens)
2.  Determine exposure type: bite, scratch, mucous membrane exposure, contact with infected material
3.  Identify animal (species, if possible capture for observation)
4.  Consider which zoonotic diseases relevant (geography, animal type, illness signs if any)
5.  Assess need for rabies PEP (high-risk animal, significant exposure = treat immediately)
6.  Assess need for other prophylaxis/treatment (depends on disease risk for region, animal type, exposure intensity)

### Disease-Specific PEP/Treatment

**Rabies:** PEP immediately (describe procedure above). **Leptospirosis:** Doxycycline 100mg BID x 7 days if significant exposure (immersion in contaminated water, direct urine contact) in endemic area. **Brucellosis:** No PEP; treatment only if infection develops (doxycycline + rifampin). **Lyme disease:** Doxycycline 100mg BID x 20 days if tick attached >36 hours in endemic area. **Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever:** No PEP; treatment if symptoms develop (doxycycline immediately, don't wait for confirmation).

### Rabies PEP Timeline and Decisions

Administer as soon as possible after exposure. Efficacy drops significantly if delayed >24 hours. Beyond 10-14 days: questionable benefit (disease may already incubating; PEP unlikely to help). In survival scenario: treat immediately if animal bite/exposure (do not delay waiting for confirmation). Rabies PEP unlikely harmful if given unnecessarily; risk of NOT treating when indicated is 100% mortality. Cost/risk calculation: over-treat probable exposures.

## Seasonal Disease Patterns

### Spring/Summer Peak Zoonoses

**Tick-borne diseases:** Lyme disease, Rocky Mountain spotted fever peak as ticks become active (April-September, peak June-August). **Leptospirosis:** Peaks post-flooding (spring rain/summer flooding), warm/wet conditions favorable. **Anthrax:** Can occur year-round; seasonal peaks when animal deaths increase (drought stress, winter). **Vector control measures:** Peak seasons require intensified surveillance, increased PPE availability, community education campaigns. Survival planning: prepare for high-risk seasons 3 months in advance (stockpile supplies, train personnel, establish monitoring protocols).

### Fall/Winter Disease Patterns

**Rabies:** Peaks fall/winter (animals seeking shelter near human habitation, hunting season high exposure). **Avian influenza:** Migratory birds arrive fall (H5N1 introduction risk), peaks fall/early winter. **Foodborne infections:** Winter storage of meat/dairy increases risks (improper preservation). **Protective measures:** Fall: educate hunters on rabies prevention, implement wildlife exclusion from buildings. Winter: maintain food safety protocols, monitor stored animal products (consume safest first).

### Year-Round Considerations

**Brucellosis:** Peaks spring (birthing season for livestock = placental tissue exposure risk). **Q fever:** Also peaks spring (birthing season aerosol risk). **Endemic diseases:** Regions with constant vector presence (ticks, mosquitoes) maintain year-round risk (adjust precautions accordingly). **Community calendar:** Survival groups should establish annual disease risk calendar (identify high-risk seasons for each disease in their region), plan resource allocation, ensure preparedness peaks align with disease risk.

<section id="common-mistakes">

## Common Mistakes and Complications

### Mistake 1: Delaying Rabies PEP for Confirmation

**Error:** Waiting for test results or animal observation before starting rabies PEP. **Consequence:** Rabies incubation progresses; PEP loses efficacy after 10-14 days. Once symptomatic = 100% fatal. **Prevention:** Treat immediately on exposure suspicion (especially animal bite from unknown/wild animal). Cost of over-treating: minor vaccine side effects. Cost of under-treating: death. Asymmetric risk favors immediate treatment.

### Mistake 2: Assuming Tick-Borne Disease Has Only One Pathogen

**Error:** Patient with Lyme disease gets doxycycline, but co-infected with Babesiosis (requires atovaquone-proguanil or clindamycin-quinine). **Consequence:** Persistent hemolytic anemia, jaundice despite apparent Lyme treatment. **Prevention:** In endemic tick areas, assume co-infections possible. If patient not improving on doxycycline alone despite adequate dose/duration, investigate for co-infection (especially babesiosis, anaplasmosis, ehrlichiosis).

### Mistake 3: Incomplete Wound Care for Rabies Exposure

**Error:** Wound washed, but not aggressively scrubbed. Tissues not irrigated thoroughly. **Consequence:** Reduced virus neutralization; PEP less effective. **Prevention:** Vigorous scrubbing with soap/water for 1-2 minutes minimum; direct irrigation of deep wounds (use syringe if available). This simple step reduces rabies risk 50%+ on its own.

### Mistake 4: Confusing Brucellosis with Leptospirosis

**Error:** Both cause fever + malaise. Brucellosis treated with doxycycline alone (inadequate); requires doxycycline + rifampin 6 weeks. Leptospirosis overtreated with 6-week doxycycline (unnecessary if mild). **Prevention:** History is key—occupational exposure (dairy/herding) = brucellosis; water exposure = leptospirosis. Undulating fever = brucellosis; biphasic = leptospirosis.

### Mistake 5: Insufficient Rabies Immunoglobulin Dosing

**Error:** Only systemic RIG given (no infiltration into wound). Dose calculated incorrectly (less than 20 IU/kg). **Consequence:** Inadequate neutralization of virus at bite site; PEP failure despite vaccination. **Prevention:** Calculate dose carefully (20 IU/kg), infiltrate as much as possible directly into wound (especially around puncture tracks), give remainder IM at distant site.

### Mistake 6: Continuing Unsafe Animal Contact During Quarantine

**Error:** Animal in quarantine for rabies observation; staff continue handling without PPE assuming observation means safety. **Consequence:** Exposure to early rabies virus shedding (virus may appear in saliva 7-10 days before clinical symptoms). **Prevention:** Treat ALL quarantined animals as potentially infectious. Full PPE required for all handling until 10-day observation complete with ZERO symptoms.

:::danger
Complications in zoonotic disease treatment are often due to delayed diagnosis or misdiagnosis. Most severe complications (organ failure, septic shock) are preventable with early treatment. Recognition and rapid empiric therapy (when diagnostic uncertainty exists but exposure history supports zoonotic disease) saves lives in resource-limited settings.
:::

## Failure Modes & Troubleshooting

### Rabies Exposure Missed/Delayed Treatment

**Problem:** Patient bitten by animal, didn't seek treatment, returns days/weeks later with neurologic symptoms or recognizes exposure late. **Causes:** Delay in recognizing risk, limited medical access, assumption of safety (animal seemed healthy). **Solutions:** 1) If any neurologic signs: likely too late for PEP (supportive care only, palliative focus). 2) If no symptoms and <10-14 days: start PEP immediately (better late than never). 3) Isolation protocols: assume patient potentially infectious (saliva risk) until proven otherwise.

### Adverse Vaccine Reactions

**Problem:** Anaphylaxis or severe reaction to rabies vaccine. **Solutions:** 1) Epinephrine if anaphylaxis (0.3-0.5mg IM for adults). 2) Antihistamines (diphenhydramine 25-50mg). 3) Continue vaccine series if possible (skip subsequent doses at reaction site if local reaction only; change injection site for remaining doses). 4) Alternative: immunoglobulin-only strategy if vaccine inaccessible (lower efficacy but better than nothing).

### Tick-Borne Disease Identified Late

**Problem:** Patient presents with arthritis/carditis months after tick bite (late Lyme disease or other tick-borne illness). **Causes:** Missed early rash, delayed medical access, asymptomatic early phase. **Solutions:** 1) Late Lyme arthritis: doxycycline 100mg BID x 28 days (or IV ceftriaxone if neurologic involvement). 2) Carditis: IV ceftriaxone (higher CNS penetration if meningitis suspected). 3) Expectation: late treatment less effective than early, but still worthwhile.

### Quarantine Breach and Escape

**Problem:** Infected animal escapes quarantine zone. **Causes:** Inadequate fence/barriers, human error (left gate open), structural failure. **Solutions:** 1) Immediate community alert (assume animal infectious, public health emergency). 2) Identify animal for recapture if possible (use nets, traps, tranquilizers if available). 3) Implement precautions for exposed populations (rabies PEP for anyone with bite/contact risk, prophylactic treatment for tick-borne diseases if tick escape). 4) Post-escape review: inspect facility, reinforce barriers, discipline/retrain responsible staff.

### Supply Shortage (Antibiotics/Vaccines)

**Problem:** Antibiotic/vaccine supply depleted during epidemic. **Causes:** Underestimated demand, distribution failures, patient hoarding. **Solutions:** 1) Ration critically: prioritize rabies PEP (near 100% mortality if untreated) over less-critical infections. 2) Use alternative antibiotics (substitute doxycycline with available fluoroquinolone or cephalosporin). 3) Extend treatment intervals if supply extremely limited (risk reduced efficacy; consult clinical guidance if available). 4) Prevention focus: reduce new infections through rigorous prevention (reduces antibiotic demand). 5) Herbal alternatives: initiate supportive therapy (fever management, hydration) while pharmaceutical supplies exhausted.

<section id="recovery">

## Recovery & Aftercare

### Post-Treatment Follow-Up

**Rabies PEP:** After vaccine series complete (Day 28), monitor for any neurologic symptoms (encephalitis signs = PEP failure, extremely rare). Expected: full recovery with no sequelae if treated promptly.

**Leptospirosis:** Mild cases resolve within 1-2 weeks post-treatment. Monitor for kidney/liver function recovery (bilirubin normalization, creatinine decline). Severe cases with renal failure may require months for full recovery; chronic renal impairment possible in ~10% of severe cases. Return to work/normal activity only after fever resolves and energy returns (typically 2-3 weeks post-treatment).

**Lyme Disease:** Early-stage treated with doxycycline: expected full recovery. Late-stage Lyme arthritis: recovery slower (weeks to months for joint pain resolution even with IV cephalosporin). Residual Lyme arthritis (chronic inflammation) affects ~10% of treated patients; ongoing NSAIDs or physical therapy may be needed.

**Brucellosis:** Treatment course is 6 weeks. After antibiotics complete, monitor for relapse (fever return = likely treatment failure, requires longer course). Full recovery expected if completed full 6-week regimen. Chronic brucellosis (residual fatigue, joint pain) persists in ~5-10% despite treatment; supportive care (rest, NSAIDs, psychological support) helpful.

:::tip
Post-treatment monitoring is key to assessing treatment success and identifying complications early. In survival setting without lab access, clinical improvement (fever resolution, energy return, symptom resolution) is your best indicator. If symptoms persist beyond expected duration (e.g., fever ongoing Week 4 of doxycycline), consider: 1) Drug resistance/treatment failure (extend treatment, change antibiotic if possible), 2) Complications (organ involvement not initially recognized), 3) Wrong diagnosis entirely.
:::

### Psychological Recovery from High-Stress Exposures

Rabies exposure is psychologically traumatic (high mortality fear). Provide counseling/reassurance: if PEP started, survival >99%. Maintain contact with patient post-vaccine series (especially if initial anxiety high) to provide reassurance and address fears. For patients with confirmed symptomatic rabies: palliative care team involvement, family support, realistic expectations about prognosis.

### Return to Normal Activity

**After mild zoonotic infection (Lyme disease, mild leptospirosis):** Return to full activity once fever resolves and energy restored (typically 2-3 weeks). **After severe infections (anthrax pulmonary, severe leptospirosis with organ failure):** Recovery is prolonged; gradual return to activity over weeks-months, with physical/occupational therapy if significant weakness/organ damage.

:::warning
Post-infection complications are possible even after apparent recovery. Lyme arthritis can develop months later; brucellosis relapse can occur if treatment incomplete. If patient develops new symptoms weeks-months after recovery, consider relapse and restart treatment rather than assuming unrelated new illness.
:::

<section id="field-adaptations">

## Field Adaptations & Limited-Resource Scenarios

### Scenario 1: No Rabies Vaccine Available

**Challenge:** Animal bite, no rabies vaccine/RIG in supply. **Options:** 1) Aggressive wound care + isolation (if confirmed non-rabid animal or zero suspicion, can avoid prophylaxis). 2) High-dose immunoglobulin alone if available (lower efficacy than vaccine but ~30-50% effective alone). 3) Interferon-alpha if available (theoretical benefit, unproven in humans). **Best strategy:** Prevent exposure through education; stockpile vaccine proactively if possible (highest mortality disease).

### Scenario 2: Only Oral Antibiotics Available (No IV)

**Challenge:** Severe leptospirosis/anthrax requiring IV therapy; only oral doxycycline available. **Adaptation:** High-dose oral doxycycline (100mg TID instead of BID) if tolerating PO intake. Monitor closely for clinical deterioration (oral absorption unpredictable if vomiting/diarrhea present). Prognosis: reduced vs. IV therapy, but better than no treatment. Consider: if available, substitute fluoroquinolone (can achieve higher tissue concentrations, some penetration to meninges).

### Scenario 3: Tick-Borne Disease Suspected, No Doxycycline

**Challenge:** Lyme/Rocky Mtn Spotted Fever suspected; doxycycline unavailable. **Options:** 1) Amoxicillin 500mg TID x 21 days (effective for early Lyme if pregnant or doxycycline unavailable). 2) Fluoroquinolone if available (less ideal but covers some rickettsia). 3) Cephalosporin (third-generation like ceftriaxone if IV available, crosses BBB for CNS involvement). **Best outcome:** Prioritize doxycycline procurement for tick-borne disease season.

### Scenario 4: Quarantine Facility Unavailable

**Challenge:** Suspected rabid animal, no secure enclosure for 10-day observation. **Options:** 1) Euthanize animal immediately (ensure rabies PEP administered to anyone with contact). 2) Secure with strong chains/restraints in isolation area with minimal human contact. 3) Distance quarantine (farm building away from community, fed/watered with long-handled tools, zero hand contact). **Key:** Minimize human exposure; assume infectious.

:::info-box
**Field Medicine Principle for Zoonotic Disease:** In resource-limited settings, prevention is infinitely cheaper than treatment. Invest in: 1) Community education (prevention behaviors), 2) PPE stockpiles (gloves, masks, disinfectants), 3) Wound care supplies, 4) Rabies vaccine if ANY possible, 5) Doxycycline (covers multiple diseases). Treatment options are improvised if supply limited, but prevention measures are universally accessible.
:::

## Quick Reference Card: Zoonotic Disease Response Protocol

**EXPOSED PERSON - IMMEDIATE ACTIONS:**
1. Wash wound aggressively (soap + water, 1-2 min minimum)
2. Identify animal (species, behavior, availability for observation)
3. Determine exposure type: bite (high risk), scratch (moderate), mucous membrane (lower)
4. If animal bite or significant exposure: ASSUME RABIES RISK → start PEP within 24 hours
5. Consider other zoonotic disease risk based on geography + animal type

**RABIES PEP PROTOCOL:**
- Day 0: Wound care + rabies immunoglobulin (20 IU/kg infiltrated into wound + IM at distant site)
- Vaccine: Days 0, 3, 7, 14, 28 (5 doses) OR Days 0, 7, 21, 28 (4-dose if supplies limited)
- Monitor: Report any neurologic symptoms (fever, hallucinations, hypersalivation) immediately
- Follow-up: Ensure compliance with vaccine schedule; PEP only effective if completed

**TICK-BORNE DISEASE PROTOCOL:**
- Rocky Mtn Spotted Fever: Doxycycline 100mg BID START IMMEDIATELY if fever + headache + rash suspected (don't wait for test)
- Lyme Disease (early): Doxycycline 100mg BID x 14-21 days if rash/exposure within 30 days
- Prevention: DEET repellent, long clothing, daily checks, prompt removal if found

**WATER-EXPOSURE FEVER PROTOCOL (Post-Flooding):**
- Leptospirosis likely if: fever + myalgia + water exposure within 2 weeks
- Treatment: Doxycycline 100mg BID x 7 days (early stage highly effective)
- Severe signs (bleeding, jaundice, oliguria): IV ceftriaxone, fluid support, monitor renal function

**OCCUPATIONAL EXPOSURE PROTOCOL (Farming/Slaughterhouse):**
- Brucellosis risk: Fever + night sweats + occupational exposure = treat with doxycycline 100mg BID + rifampin 600mg x 6 weeks
- Dairy safety: Pasteurize all milk (63°C x 30 min minimum), no raw dairy in endemic areas
- Birthing season precaution: Respiratory protection (N95) during livestock births in endemic brucellosis area

**ANIMAL MANAGEMENT PROTOCOL:**
- Suspect rabid animal: Quarantine 10 days, zero contact without PPE, observe for symptoms
- Dead animal disposal: Bury 6+ feet deep with lime powder, >100 feet from water, mark site
- Sick animal handling: Full PPE (gloves, respiratory protection, apron), dedicated cleaning area, decontamination post-handling

## Community One-Health Program Design

**Integrated Approach:** Recognize zoonotic diseases at intersection of animal, human, environmental health. Components: 1) Animal surveillance (monitor livestock/wildlife signs), 2) Human health tracking (illness clusters, risk education), 3) Environmental management (water safety, waste, habitat control). Establish communication between animal health worker, clinician, environmental manager for coordinated response.

**Community Training:** Target hunters/wildlife handlers (rabies prevention), farmers (brucellosis, dairy safety), general population (water/food safety, animal contact precautions). Use community meetings, demonstrations (tick removal, wound care), refresher training annually before high-risk seasons.

**Resource Planning:** Prioritize rabies vaccine/immunoglobulin, doxycycline, N95 respirators. Stockpile: Rabies PEP for 10% population, doxycycline for 5%, PPE for handlers. Focus on prevention (education, basic PPE) over expensive treatments. Share resources between communities, maintain emergency supplies cache. Monitor effectiveness through case tracking (animal/human illness), monthly review meetings, annual assessment of prevention strategy effectiveness. Adapt protocols based on experience and resource constraints.

<section id="scaling">

## Scaling Guidance: Individual vs Community Scale

### Individual Level Exposure

Animal bite/exposure at home: wash wound, assess animal/exposure, initiate rabies PEP if high-risk (don't delay for veterinary consultation), seek medical care if available. Expected outcome: high survival if PEP administered <24 hours.

### Community Scale Response

Animal bite clinic: educate community on rabies/zoonotic disease risks, establish protocols for bite assessment, maintain PEP supplies (rabies vaccine/immunoglobulin if available), coordinate with veterinary surveillance for rabid animals in area. Expected: <1% of bites = rabies risk if proper assessment done, but 100% of high-risk exposures receive PEP (prevent deaths).

### Outbreak Response

Multiple animal exposures/illness cluster: establish investigation team, identify common source (contaminated food/water, sick animal), issue community alerts, implement control measures (quarantine infected animals, improve sanitation, animal control to reduce wildlife contact). Expected: contain spread through early identification + treatment initiation, prevent cascade of community infections.
