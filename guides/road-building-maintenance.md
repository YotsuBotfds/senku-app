## 1. Road Fundamentals

Roads are critical infrastructure that enable trade, communication, and resource movement. In post-collapse scenarios, the ability to build and maintain functional roads determines the viability of larger communities.

### Why Roads Matter

- **Trade and economy:** Accessible roads allow goods to move; increased trade supports specialization and economic growth
- **Communication:** Physical roads enable messenger networks, information flow, and news transmission
- **Emergency response:** Ambulances, fire response, and disaster recovery depend on passable roads
- **Military/defense:** Communities require routes for mutual defense and coordination
- **Agriculture:** Farmers need roads to transport harvest to storage or market

### Road Classification

**Major routes (4-6 ft wide, maintained for carts/teams):** Connect settlements, support trade, require regular maintenance

**Local roads (3-4 ft wide):** Connect villages to fields, water sources, and woods; lighter maintenance

**Footpaths (2-3 ft wide):** Connect nearby fields and structures; minimal maintenance except clearing

### Planning & Surveying

Before construction, the route must be surveyed to identify optimal path, resource requirements, and obstacles.

**Surveying tools:**

- **Surveying staff or rod:** 10-foot tall marked pole (homemade from a straight tree branch with 1-foot marks)
- **Transit or string level:** Determines grade (slope); can be improvised from a weighted string
- **Measuring chain:** 100-foot chain or rope with knots at intervals for distance measurement
- **Compass:** Establishes direction and navigable bearings

**Route selection criteria:**

1. **Minimize grade:** Roads steeper than 10% (1 foot of vertical rise per 10 feet of horizontal distance) are difficult for loaded animals; grades steeper than 20% are unsuitable except in mountains
2. **Avoid swampy/wet ground:** Water-logged soil is expensive to improve and fails seasonally
3. **Use natural features:** Ridgelines, creek crossings, and valley routes minimize excavation
4. **Balance distance with safety:** Shorter routes are preferred but should not traverse areas prone to flooding, rockfall, or impassable conditions
5. **Secondary benefits:** Identify access to water, timber, quarry sites, or historical crossing points

**Survey procedure:**

1. Mark the intended route with blazes (painted marks) on trees
2. Drive stakes at intervals (every 100 feet) to mark the centerline
3. Use the surveying staff to check grade: from a given point, sight through the staff on the next point; if the next point's mark is higher or lower, the grade is not level (rise/run ratio indicates percentage grade)
4. Calculate total elevation change across the full route; excessive grades require re-routing or switchbacks
5. Identify obstacles (large trees, outcrops, boggy areas) and route around them where practical

## 2. Surface Construction

Road surface type depends on available materials, expected traffic, climate, and maintenance capacity.

### Gravel Roads

Gravel roads are the most practical for low-resource communities. Properly constructed gravel roads last 3-5 years with routine maintenance.

**Construction steps:**

1. **Prepare the subgrade:** Clear vegetation, remove topsoil (3-4 inches), and level the ground. Create a slight crown (center higher than edges) for drainage; 1 inch of rise across an 8-foot-wide road is typical.
2. **Add base layer:** Place 4-6 inches of large gravel or crushed stone (fist-sized or smaller). Use a sledge or wooden rammer to compact; compact in 2-inch lifts for best results.
3. **Add middle layer:** Place 2-3 inches of smaller gravel (pea-sized). Compact as above.
4. **Add surface layer:** Place 1-2 inches of fine gravel or sand-gravel mix. This is the wearing layer and will be compacted by traffic.
5. **Initial rolling:** If available, use a heavy roller or have multiple loaded teams traverse the road repeatedly to compact the surface

**Advantages:** Minimal equipment, locally available materials, easily repaired

**Disadvantages:** Requires annual re-grading (raking, re-compacting), loses material in heavy use, becomes muddy when wet

### Compacted Earth Roads

Compacted earth requires no added material but demands excellent drainage and regular maintenance.

**Construction:**

1. Grade the base as above (remove topsoil, crown the surface)
2. Compact the exposed soil in 3-inch lifts using a rammer or by driving teams over the surface multiple times
3. Water the surface lightly to aid compaction; do not saturate (water makes compaction impossible)
4. Continue compacting until the soil is hard-packed (driving tests show no ruts or give)

**Advantages:** No material cost, low environmental impact, no loose stones

**Disadvantages:** Becomes impassable in heavy rain, requires frequent maintenance (raking, re-compacting), develops ruts from wheel tracks

### Corduroy Roads (Temporary Rough Terrain)

In wetland or soft-ground scenarios where better roads cannot be built, corduroy roads (logs laid perpendicular to the direction of travel) provide temporary passage.

**Construction:**

1. Select logs 4-8 inches in diameter and 8-10 feet long
2. Lay them perpendicular to the direction of travel, spaced 2-3 feet apart
3. Secure logs with stakes or cross-members to prevent shifting
4. Cover logs with branches or brush (provides traction and smooths the ride)

**Use:** Temporary; suitable for short crossings of wet areas. Requires constant maintenance as logs rot (every 2-3 years).

### Stone Paving (Long-Term)

Stone paving creates durable roads lasting 50+ years. Requires significant labor but minimal maintenance.

**Materials:**

- **Large foundation stones:** 6-12 inches diameter, placed as the base
- **Medium stones:** 3-6 inches, placed in the middle layer
- **Small stones/gravel:** 1-3 inches, as the wearing surface and filler

**Construction:**

1. Prepare the subgrade as above
2. Place large foundation stones in a single layer, fitting them snugly with minimal gaps
3. Fill gaps between foundation stones with smaller stones
4. Lay medium stones on top, offset from the gaps below (like brickwork)
5. Fill gaps with small stones and gravel, working this material into all voids
6. Compact by driving teams or using a rammer; water lightly and compact again

**Advantages:** Extremely durable, drains well, minimal maintenance

**Disadvantages:** Extremely labor-intensive (requires 500-1000 hours per mile), suitable only for high-traffic routes

## 3. Drainage Systems

Proper drainage is the most critical factor in road longevity. Water causes surface rutting, subsurface failures, and complete loss of bearing capacity in weak soils.

### Crown & Slope

The road surface should slope away from the centerline toward the sides (crown) to shed water quickly.

**Appropriate crowns:**

- **Gravel roads:** 1.5-2 inch rise across an 8-foot width
- **Earth roads:** 1-1.5 inch rise across an 8-foot width
- **Stone roads:** 2-3 inch rise for better drainage

A steeper crown sheds water faster but creates ruts as wheels track the low edges. A gentler crown reduces rutting but retains water longer.

### Drainage Ditches

Ditches on both sides of the road collect water and prevent it from accumulating on the surface.

**Ditch dimensions:**

- **Width:** 2-4 feet
- **Depth:** 1-2 feet (deeper in high-rainfall areas)
- **Slope:** The ditch bottom should slope 1% toward an outfall (1 foot of drop per 100 feet)

**Construction:**

1. Use a spade or plow to excavate the ditch on both sides of the road
2. The ditch lip should be level with or slightly above the road surface so water flows in
3. Angle the ditch side (not vertical; a 45-degree slope is typical) to prevent collapse
4. Direct the ditch toward streams, wetlands, or other natural drainage areas

**Maintenance:** Ditches accumulate leaves and sediment. Clean annually (especially in fall) to prevent blockage.

### Culverts & Cross-Drains

Where roads cross streams or gullies, water flow must be maintained. Culverts (pipes or tunnels) prevent erosion and maintain passage.

**Culvert materials:**

- **Wood:** Large hollow logs (most traditional); split logs, hollowed out or formed into channels
- **Stone:** Arched stone passages (extremely labor-intensive, seen in historical Roman roads)
- **Pipe:** Bamboo (in tropical regions), PVC (modern), or iron (if available)

**Installation:**

1. Choose a culvert diameter that accommodates expected water flow (wider than the existing stream width)
2. Set the culvert to maintain the natural slope of the watercourse
3. Support the culvert with stone or earth banks on both sides
4. Cover the culvert with road material (gravel or compacted earth)
5. Ensure the downstream end is not blocked by sediment or debris

**Maintenance:** Inspect after heavy rains; remove blockages immediately. Replace wooden culverts every 10-20 years as they decay.

### French Drains

In poorly drained areas, subsurface drainage removes water before it softens the road.

**Construction:**

1. Excavate a trench below the road surface (6-12 inches deep)
2. Fill with coarse gravel or stone (acts as a filter)
3. Lay a perforated pipe (bamboo, wood, or PVC with holes) in the center
4. Backfill with sand and gravel
5. Direct the drain toward an outfall (stream or drainage ditch)

**Use:** In clay-heavy soils or areas with high water tables; improves road longevity in wet climates.

## 4. Bridge Approaches & Fords

Roads crossing water require special treatment to prevent erosion and maintain traffic flow.

### Bridge Approaches

The transition from road to bridge requires careful design to prevent rutting and water accumulation.

**Approach design:**

1. **Grade transition:** If the bridge is higher than the road (to allow water flow), the approach must rise gradually (no steeper than 10% grade)
2. **Stability:** The banks on both sides must be stabilized to prevent collapse during water flow
3. **Abutments:** Stone or timber structures support the bridge and prevent washout; see <a href="../simple-bridge-engineering.html">Simple Bridge Engineering</a> for detail
4. **Scour protection:** Install riprap (large rocks) both upstream and downstream of the bridge to prevent water erosion

**Rock Abutments (Low Bridges):**

For bridges crossing shallow streams, abutments can be simple stone stacks:

1. Clear the stream banks down to bedrock or stable soil
2. Stack large stones (1-2 feet diameter) in a wedge shape pointing upstream
3. Fill gaps with smaller stones
4. Backfill behind the abutment with gravel and compacted earth

### Fords (Water Crossings Without Bridges)

In low-resource scenarios, fords are common for shallow water crossings. Properly constructed fords remain passable except during flood.

**Ford construction:**

1. **Select location:** Choose areas where the stream is widest and shallowest (reduced depth and flow velocity)
2. **Clear the ford:** Remove rocks, logs, and obstacles that would catch cart wheels
3. **Establish a firm bottom:** If the ford has mud, lay logs or stones across the stream bed perpendicular to flow; this creates a more stable base
4. **Grade approach banks:** Angle the banks so carts and animals can descend and ascend without excessive grade (max 20% for loaded carts)
5. **Mark the safe route:** In seasons of variable water, mark the shallowest, safest path with stones or stakes on the banks

**Seasonal management:**

- In dry season, clear weeds from the ford and ensure smooth passage
- Before heavy rain, inspect for flood damage; re-level any eroded areas
- In flood season, mark the ford as impassable; post warning signs or barriers

**Safe passage:**

- Only attempt fords when water is below the cart's axle height
- Animal teams may balk at deep water; familiarize them through shallow crossings first
- Use ropes on loaded carts to help recovery if the cart gets stuck

:::warning
**Flash Flooding:** Fords are deadly during flash flood. A 2-foot wall of water moves at high speed and carries enormous force. Never cross a swollen ford during rain or after heavy upstream rainfall. Establish alternative routes or wait for water to recede.
:::

## 5. Hill Construction & Switchbacks

Steep grades are dangerous for loaded animals and carts. In mountainous terrain, switchbacks reduce grade and allow passage where direct routes are impossible.

### Grade Limits

- **0-5%:** Comfortable for all animals and carts
- **5-10%:** Manageable with unloaded or lightly loaded carts
- **10-20%:** Difficult; unloaded carts recommended, heavily loaded teams struggle
- **>20%:** Unsafe for carts; animals risk falling backward; only walkable by people

### Switchback Design

Switchbacks are sharp turns that reverse the direction of travel, reducing grade over a longer distance.

**Example:** A 500-foot elevation gain directly (100% grade — impossible) can be made with switchbacks at 10% grade and 5000 feet of road distance.

**Switchback construction:**

1. **Layout:** Mark the route with switchback locations every 500-1000 feet of elevation gain
2. **Grade calculation:** For a 10% grade road, each switchback rises 100 vertical feet for every 1000 feet of horizontal distance
3. **Turning radius:** The switchback must have a tight turn (minimum 20-30 feet of turning radius); animals need space to make the turn loaded
4. **Grade at turns:** Maintain consistent grade through the turn; a turning cart on a grade >8% risks tipping
5. **Bank the turn:** Elevate the inner edge slightly (1-2 inches) to help carts navigate the tight turn without tipping

**Switchback failures:**

- Too steep: Animals strain, carts are difficult to control
- Too tight: Loaded carts catch on the inner bank; full turn requires unhitching and repositioning
- Grade at turn: Carts tip on the inside wheel during the turn

### Fill & Cut

Road construction often requires moving soil to create level surfaces.

**Cut sections:** Where the road descends an existing slope, excavate (cut) soil from the hillside and move it downslope.

**Fill sections:** Where the road climbs, the excavated soil can be placed as fill to support the higher road grade.

**Erosion control:** Cut sections expose soil; they erode rapidly in rain. Stabilize cuts with:

- Planted vegetation (trees, shrubs) on the slope
- Terracing (horizontal steps) to slow runoff
- Stone walls (expensive; for important routes)

## 6. Seasonal Maintenance

Road longevity depends on consistent maintenance. A road maintained annually lasts 2-3x longer than one maintained haphazardly.

### Spring Maintenance

After winter, inspect for damage:

1. **Frost heave:** Winter freezing causes soil to expand and displace the road surface. Fill depressions with gravel.
2. **Pothole repair:**
   - Remove broken material from the pothole (create a clean edge)
   - Fill with compacted gravel (in 2-inch lifts)
   - Compress with a rammer; test for firmness
   - Replace with finer gravel for the top layer
3. **Ditch clearing:** Remove winter debris from ditches; ensure water flows freely
4. **Culvert inspection:** Clear blockages from culverts; ensure downstream flow

### Summer Maintenance

During dry season, focus on compaction and erosion prevention:

1. **Re-grading:** Grade the road surface to restore crown and eliminate ruts. This is the most important summer task; a graded road drains water and prevents pothole formation.
2. **Dust control:** In dry climates, water lightly when grading to aid compaction
3. **Vegetation control:** Clear brush, overhanging branches, and weeds; they trap moisture and shade the road
4. **Vegetation strip maintenance:** The 3-foot strip on each side of the road should be cleared of saplings and dense brush; this improves drainage and prevents shade

### Fall Maintenance

1. **Ditch cleaning:** Clear leaves and sediment from drainage ditches before heavy rain
2. **Culvert clearing:** Ensure all drainage structures are unobstructed
3. **Surface inspection:** Fill any developing ruts or depressions

### Winter Maintenance

In snow climates:

1. **Snow clearing:** Keep the road open by removing snow with shovels or plow teams
2. **Salt/sand:** Do not use salt (damages roads and vegetation); use sand for traction
3. **Thaw damage:** Spring inspection will reveal winter damage (erosion, rutting); repair immediately

### Long-Term Maintenance Schedule

| Task | Frequency | Labor (person-days/mile) |
|------|-----------|------------------------|
| Grading (re-compacting surface) | Annual | 2-5 |
| Pothole repair | As needed (quarterly) | 1-3 |
| Ditch cleaning | Spring, fall | 1-2 |
| Culvert maintenance | Annual + after floods | 0.5-1 |
| Major surface replacement | Every 3-5 years | 5-20 |

## 7. Community Labor Organization

Road building and maintenance require significant coordinated labor. Organizing community work parties ensures shared burden and builds social capital.

### Labor Requirements

| Task | Crew Size | Duration | Output |
|------|-----------|----------|--------|
| Road grading (1 mile) | 5-10 people | 4-6 hours | 1 mile graded |
| Ditch digging (2 miles) | 10-15 people | 8 hours | 2 miles ditch |
| Gravel placement (1 mile) | 8-12 people | 6-8 hours | 1 mile surface |
| Pothole repair (per road length) | 2-5 people | 1-2 hours | 10-15 potholes |

### Work Party Organization

**Scheduling:**

1. Announce work dates well in advance (1-2 weeks)
2. Schedule for dry weather (roads are impassable and muddy after rain)
3. Early morning start (6-7 AM) to avoid peak heat
4. Typical duration: 4-6 hours with a lunch break

**Task assignment:**

- **Experienced crew:** Lead the work, supervise quality
- **Skilled labor:** Grading, drainage systems, technical work
- **General labor:** Hauling, spreading, compacting
- **Support:** Food preparation, water provision, animal handlers (if teams are used)

**Motivation & fairness:**

- Rotate tasks to reduce monotony
- Recognize significant contributors (first road grader, ditch lead, etc.)
- Ensure work is fairly distributed (heavy/light, dangerous/safe tasks rotated)
- Provide substantial meals and water
- Plan celebration (meal, gathering) after completion

### Equipment & Tools

**Required tools:** Shovels, spades, rakes, picks, sledges, wheelbarrows, levels, measuring chains

**Animal power:** Teams (horses, oxen) for hauling gravel, spreading material, or pulling a grader

**Simple equipment:**

- **Road grader:** A heavy plank (10 feet long, 2 feet wide) dragged by a team, levels and compacts the surface
- **Roller:** Heavy cylindrical object (log or stone-filled barrel) pulled by a team to compact material
- **Sledge:** Flat drag for transporting materials

### Maintenance Schedule Template

Communities should maintain a calendar:

| Month | Task | Responsible Party | Labor Needed |
|-------|------|-------------------|--------------|
| March | Spring inspection, pothole repair | Road committee | 2-3 people |
| April | Ditch cleaning | Work party | 10-15 people |
| May-August | Quarterly spot repairs | Road master | 1-2 people |
| September | Fall ditch cleaning | Work party | 10-15 people |
| October | Pre-winter inspection | Road committee | 2-3 people |
| Every 2 years | Major surface re-grading | Work party | 15-20 people |
